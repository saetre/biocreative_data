###begin article-title 0
###xml 50 55 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Cross-Talk with Myeloid Accessory Cells Regulates Human Natural Killer Cell Interferon-gamma Responses to Malaria
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 388 409 384 405 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">Plasmodium falciparum</named-content>
###xml 831 832 827 828 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 837 843 833 839 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bright</sup>
###xml 315 320 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 388 409 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
###xml 1062 1067 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1772 1777 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Data from a variety of experimental models suggest that natural killer (NK) cells require signals from accessory cells in order to respond optimally to pathogens, but the precise identity of the cells able to provide such signals depends upon the nature of the infectious organism. Here we show that the ability of human NK cells to produce interferon-gamma in response to stimulation by Plasmodium falciparum-infected red blood cells (iRBCs) is strictly dependent upon multiple, contact-dependent and cytokine-mediated signals derived from both monocytes and myeloid dendritic cells (mDCs). Contrary to some previous reports, we find that both monocytes and mDCs express an activated phenotype following short-term incubation with iRBCs and secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines. The magnitude of the NK cell response (and of the KIR- CD56bright NK cell population in particular) is tightly correlated with resting levels of accessory cell maturation, indicating that heterogeneity of the NK response to malaria is a reflection of deep-rooted heterogeneity in the human innate immune system. Moreover, we show that NK cells are required to maintain the maturation status of resting mDCs and monocytes, providing additional evidence for reciprocal regulation of NK cells and accessory cells. However, NK cell-derived signals are not required for activation of accessory cells by either iRBCs or bacterial lipolysaccharide. Together, these data suggest that there may be differences in the sequence of events required for activation of NK cells by non-viral pathogens compared to the classical model of NK activation by virus-infected or major histocompatibility complex-deficient cells. These findings have far-reaching implications for the study of immunity to infection in human populations.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Synopsis
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 608 629 604 625 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">Plasmodium falciparum</named-content>
###xml 917 930 905 918 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">P. falciparum</named-content>
###xml 377 383 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 388 392 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 590 595 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 608 629 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
###xml 881 886 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 917 930 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
The outcome of infection is determined both by the ability to limit the initial phase of pathogen colonisation and by the ability to mount an effective adaptive immune response. Both of these processes are influenced by innate immune responses, of which a crucial component can be the ability of natural killer (NK) cells to secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines. Studies in both humans and mice indicate that the magnitude of the early (innate) interferon (IFN)-gamma response is a crucial determinant of the outcome of malaria infection. In this study, Newman et al. show that activation of human NK cells by Plasmodium falciparum-infected red blood cells to produce IFN-gamma is strictly dependent upon, and regulated by, contact-mediated and soluble (cytokine) signals from two accessory cell populations (myeloid dendritic cells and monocytes). Furthermore, the magnitude of the human NK cell IFN-gamma response to P. falciparum-infected red blood cells is highly correlated with levels of expression of co-stimulatory molecules on resting accessory cells. These findings suggest that it might be possible to predict the magnitude of the innate cytokine response, and possibly even susceptibility to malarial disease, from the phenotype of resting monocytes. In addition, these data contribute to the development of a new model of NK activation by non-viral pathogens in which activation of accessory cells precedes, rather than follows, NK activation.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Introduction
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 652 653 652 653 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b001">1</xref>
###xml 863 864 859 860 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b002">2</xref>
###xml 865 866 861 862 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b006">6</xref>
###xml 998 1020 990 1012 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Plasmodium falciparum&#8211;</italic>
###xml 1054 1055 1046 1047 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b007">7</xref>
###xml 1056 1057 1048 1049 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b009">9</xref>
###xml 1111 1112 1103 1104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b008">8</xref>
###xml 1113 1114 1105 1106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b009">9</xref>
###xml 888 893 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 998 1019 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
After years of neglect, in which the adaptive immune response to malaria was investigated in great depth with a view to rational development of antimalarial vaccines, the innate immune response to malaria has recently come under scrutiny. In part, this is driven by the realisation that innate immune responses initiate and mould the subsequent adaptive response and that understanding the innate immune response may aid the design of novel adjuvants and novel vaccine delivery systems. Furthermore, data from experiments in rodents and from clinical studies indicate that innate immune responses play a crucial role in controlling malaria infections [1] and can significantly alter the outcome of infection, especially in non-immune individuals. The key element in the cell-mediated effector response to malaria is the rapid induction of interferon (IFN)-gamma [2-6]. We have shown that human natural killer (NK) cells can be an important early source of IFN-gamma after exposure of leukocytes to Plasmodium falciparum-infected red blood cells (iRBCs) [7-9], although this response is extremely heterogeneous [8,9].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 195 197 195 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b010">10</xref>
###xml 198 200 198 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b012">12</xref>
###xml 382 383 382 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b008">8</xref>
###xml 783 785 783 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b013">13</xref>
###xml 786 788 786 788 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b014">14</xref>
###xml 886 888 886 888 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b013">13</xref>
###xml 889 891 889 891 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b015">15</xref>
###xml 892 894 892 894 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b017">17</xref>
###xml 943 945 943 945 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b013">13</xref>
###xml 1063 1065 1063 1065 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b018">18</xref>
###xml 696 701 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 967 975 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 1018 1023 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Emerging data suggest that signals from accessory cells such as macrophages, monocytes, and dendritic cells (DCs) are absolutely required for optimal NK cell responses to many infectious agents [10-12]. Consistent with this, we have previously demonstrated that cloned NK cells are unable to respond to iRBCs in the absence of autologous peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) [8]. However, an essential role for accessory cells in promoting robust pro-inflammatory responses to malaria is somewhat controversial given previous observations suggesting that accessory cell function is seriously impaired by exposure to malarial parasite ligands. For example, following exposure to iRBCs, both human DCs and monocytes secrete the immunosuppressive cytokine interleukin-10 (IL-10) [13,14], have an impaired ability to mature in response to lipopolysaccharides (LPS) and other ligands [13,15-17], and are unable to present antigen to T cells [13]. Moreover, DCs from children with acute malaria infection have reduced human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-DR expression [18].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
Here we have assessed the precise contribution of various subsets of accessory cells to NK cell activation by iRBCs. We demonstrate that cells of the myeloid lineage are not only activated by iRBCs, but also play a crucial role in controlling the magnitude of the early burst of NK cell-derived IFN-gamma via both cytokine- and contact-dependent signals. Furthermore, we provide evidence that reciprocal interactions between NK cells and myeloid accessory cells regulate levels of activation of both cell populations, with major implications for the ability of the innate immune system to recognise and respond to infection.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Materials and Methods
###end title 8
###begin title 9
Blood Donors
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 478 479 474 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b007">7</xref>
###xml 480 481 476 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b009">9</xref>
Adult blood donors were recruited at the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine through an anonymous blood donation system. All donors were malaria naive and healthy and gave fully informed consent for their blood to be used in this study. Ethical approval was given by the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine Ethics Committee, application number 805. As we have previously shown that NK cells from only some donors make a strong IFN-gamma response to iRBCs [7-9], cells from known high-responding donors were used for the majority of experiments described here.

P. falciparum Culture and Antigen Preparation
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 0 13 0 13 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">P. falciparum</named-content>
###xml 52 55 52 55 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Rh&#8722;</sup>
###xml 333 334 332 333 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 341 342 340 341 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 353 354 352 353 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 587 597 580 590 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycoplasma</italic>
###xml 0 13 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
###xml 56 61 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 262 267 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
P. falciparum parasites (strain 3D7) were grown in ORh- human erythrocytes (National Blood Service, ) in RPMI 1640 (Gibco, ) supplemented with 25 mM HEPES (Sigma-Aldrich, ), 28 mM sodium bicarbonate (BDH, ), 20 mug/l hypoxanthine (Sigma-Aldrich), and 10% normal human AB serum (National Blood Service). Cultures were gassed with 3% O2, 4% CO2, and 93% N2 and incubated at 37 degreesC. The culture medium was changed daily and the parasitaemia was determined by examination of Giemsa-stained thin blood smears. Parasite cultures were routinely shown by PCR (Stratagene, ) to be free from Mycoplasma contamination. Mature schizonts were harvested from cultures of 5%-8% parasitaemia by centrifugation through a 60% Percoll gradient (Sigma-Aldrich).
###end p 12
###begin title 13
PBMC Preparation and Culture
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 202 203 202 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b009">9</xref>
###xml 253 254 253 254 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
Venous blood was collected into sodium heparin (10 IU/ml blood; CP Pharmaceuticals, ) and PBMCs were isolated by Histopaque 1077 (Sigma-Aldrich) density gradient centrifugation as described previously [9]. Cells were resuspended at a concentration of 106 cells/ml and cultured in flat-bottomed 24-well plates for 24 h. Schizont-infected (iRBCs) or uninfected red blood cells (uRBCs) were added at a ratio of three red blood cells per mononuclear cell.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Cell Surface and Intracellular Staining for Flow Cytometry
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 74 75 74 75 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b009">9</xref>
Surface and intracellular staining was performed as described previously [9]. The antibodies used were anti-CD3 PerCP, IgG1 PerCP, and anti-HLA-DR PerCP (all from BD Biosciences, ); anti-CD11c AlexaFluor-647, IgG1 AlexaFluor-647, anti-CD56 AlexaFluor-647, IgG2a AlexaFluor-647, anti-IFN-gamma FITC, anti-CD14 FITC, IgG1 FITC, anti-CD40 R-PE, anti-CD69 R-PE, IgG2a R-PE, anti-CD80 R-PE-Cy5, and IgG1 R-PE-Cy5 (all from Serotec, ). Flow cytometric analyses were performed using a Becton Dickinson FACSCalibur flow cytometer (BD Biosciences) and FlowJo analysis software (TreeStar, ).
###end p 16
###begin title 17
NK Cell Purification
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 4 5 4 5 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 9 10 9 10 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
CD56+ CD3- NK cells were enriched from PBMCs by magnetic cell separation (NK Cell Enrichment kit; StemCell Technologies, ) according to the manufacturer's instructions and using LS separation columns (Miltenyi Biotec, ). B cells, T cells, monocytes, and erythrocytes were retained in the column, and the effluent containing unlabelled NK cells was collected. NK cells were counted, tested for viability by trypan blue exclusion, and routinely checked by flow cytometry for a purity of greater than 95%.

Transwell Cultures
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 13 14 13 14 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>

Purified NK cells (1 x 105) in a volume of 200 mul were placed in a Transwell with a 0.4-mum microporous polycarbonate membrane (Corning, ) and lowered into the culture well so that all cells were submerged in culture medium. iRBCs (3 x 106, bottom well, or 0.5 x 106, top well) were added as indicated. Plates were cultured for 24 h, and NK cells from the top well and/or PBMCs from the bottom well were harvested, stained, and analysed by flow cytometry as previously described.

###begin title 37
Optimal NK Cell Response to iRBCs Is Accessory Cell-Dependent
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 146 147 146 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b008">8</xref>
###xml 541 549 537 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 592 593 584 585 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 598 599 590 591 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 604 605 596 597 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 740 748 732 740 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 1043 1051 1027 1035 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 1156 1164 1136 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 1237 1245 1213 1221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 1251 1252 1227 1228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g001">1</xref>
We have previously shown that cloned NK cell lines are unable to respond to iRBCs in the absence of accessory cells that are present among PBMCs [8]. To confirm and extend this observation, NK cells were purified from PBMCs of seven different donors by magnetic cell sorting (MACS) and stimulated for 24 h with IL-12 and IL-18, uRBCs, or iRBCs or left unstimulated and analysed for surface expression of CD3, CD56, CD69, and intracellular IFN-gamma; unsorted PBMCs from the same donors were stimulated and analysed in an equivalent fashion (Figure 1). In PBMC cultures, as expected, IFN-gamma+ (CD3- CD56+) NK cells were detected in both cytokine-stimulated and iRBC-stimulated cultures but not in unstimulated or uRBC-stimulated cultures (Figure 1A). We deliberately selected donors who were known, from previous studies, to be relatively high responders in this assay; nevertheless, the magnitude of the IFN-gamma response to iRBCs varied between donors, with between 5% and 50% of NK cells making IFN-gamma in response to iRBCs after 24 h (Figure 1C). In complete contrast, although purified NK cells made IFN-gamma in response to cytokine stimulation (Figure 1B), no IFN-gamma was produced by purified NK cells in response to iRBCs (Figure 1B and 1C).
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 115 116 115 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b009">9</xref>
###xml 242 250 242 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 674 682 674 682 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g001">Figure 1</xref>
We have shown that up-regulation of surface CD69 expression is a marker of partial (early) activation of NK cells [9], and thus, as expected, up-regulation of CD69 was consistently seen on NK cells within the iRBC-stimulated PBMC population (Figure 1D). Surface CD69 expression tended to be higher on unstimulated purified NK cells than on unstimulated NK cells within PBMCs, presumably because of partial activation during cell separation. However, CD69 surface expression was not significantly up-regulated on purified NK cells after stimulation with iRBCs, and NK CD69 expression was significantly higher among iRBC-stimulated PBMC NK cells than among purified NK cells (Figure 1D).
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 307 315 307 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g001">Figure 1</xref>
Importantly, the inability of purified NK cells to respond to iRBCs is not an artefact of the NK cell purification process as MACS-purified NK cells labelled with CellTracker Green (CTG) and added back to autologous PBMC cultures respond as well to iRBC stimulation as do "untouched" NK cells within PBMCs (Figure 1E).
###end p 40
###begin p 41
Thus, these data indicate that accessory cells within PBMCs are required for both IFN-gamma and CD69 expression among iRBC-stimulated NK cells.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
###xml 17 18 17 18 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 27 28 27 28 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
Depletion of CD1c+ and CD14+ Accessory Cells Inhibits the NK Cell Response
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 231 239 231 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 555 564 547 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="ppat-0020118-sg001">Figure S1</xref>
###xml 586 587 578 579 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 693 694 681 682 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 801 802 785 786 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 897 898 877 878 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 925 926 905 906 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1006 1007 982 983 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 1014 1015 990 991 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 1024 1025 1000 1001 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 1048 1049 1024 1025 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 1056 1057 1032 1033 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 1066 1067 1042 1043 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
To identify the accessory cell population(s) required for NK cell activation by iRBCs, PBMCs of known high-responding donors were depleted (by MACS) of cells expressing specific cell surface markers prior to incubation with iRBCs (Figure 2). PBMCs of all the donors tested in these experiments responded to iRBC stimulation, with 5%-40% of all NK cells producing IFN-gamma after 24 h. Depletion of accessory cell populations did not affect the ability of NK cells to produce IFN-gamma following stimulation with positive control stimuli (IL-12 and IL-18; Figure S1). Depletion of HLA-DR+ cells completely abolished the NK cell IFN-gamma response to iRBCs in all donors, but depletion of BDCA-4+ plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs) had no consistent effect on the NK IFN-gamma response. Likewise, depletion of CD19+ B cells did not reduce the NK cell IFN-gamma response. However, depleting PBMCs of either CD1c+ myeloid DCs (mDCs) or CD14+ monocytes significantly reduced NK cell IFN-gamma responses (monocytes, paired t test, t = 3.63, p = 0.036; mDCs, paired t test, t = 3.82, p = 0.032). In the one donor where depletion using either CD14 or CD1c did not substantially reduce the IFN-gamma response (relative response, monocyte-depleted = 0.84, mDC-depleted = 0.90), a double depletion using both antibodies together was performed. In this case the IFN-gamma response was substantially reduced (relative response = 0.32).
###end p 43
###begin p 44
These data indicate that the NK cell response to iRBCs is not dependent on a single population of accessory cells; rather, both mDCs and monocytes can provide activating signals for NK cells, and, in most donors, optimal NK activation requires both of these cell types to be present.
###end p 44
###begin title 45
Monocytes and mDCs Are Activated by iRBCs
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 113 115 113 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b013">13</xref>
###xml 116 118 116 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b014">14</xref>
###xml 339 346 339 346 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">E. coli</named-content>
###xml 527 535 527 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 655 664 655 664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="ppat-0020118-sg002">Figure S2</xref>
###xml 339 346 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Given the numerous published reports indicating that monocyte and DC function is inhibited by exposure to iRBCs [13,14], it was important to determine whether the accessory cells required for NK cell activation were themselves activated by exposure to iRBCs. PBMCs from ten different donors were therefore stimulated with uRBCs, iRBCs, or E. coli LPS (or left unstimulated, growth medium alone [gm]) for 24 h, and then mDC and monocyte populations were analysed for expression of the activation markers CD40, HLA-DR, and CD80 (Figure 3; isotype control data to exclude non-specific binding of labelled antibodies by activated accessory cells are shown in Figure S2).
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 210 211 210 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 218 219 218 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 228 229 228 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 287 288 287 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 295 296 295 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 305 306 305 306 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 362 363 362 363 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 370 371 370 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 380 381 380 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 539 540 539 540 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 547 548 547 548 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>

###xml 616 617 616 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 624 625 624 625 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 634 635 634 635 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 795 796 795 796 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 803 804 803 804 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>

Significant up-regulation of CD40, CD80, and HLA-DR was observed on monocytes from all donors following 24 h incubation with iRBCs (CD40, mean fold increase of median fluorescence intensity [MFI] = 6.3, paired t test, t = 3.59, p = 0.006; HLA-DR, mean fold increase of MFI = 1.7, paired t test, t = 3.42, p = 0.008; CD80, mean fold increase of MFI = 3.0, paired t test, t = 7.56, p < 0.0001). Activation of mDCs was also observed, with significant up-regulation of CD40 and HLA-DR expression (CD40, mean fold increase of MFI = 2.3, paired t test, t = 3.60, p = 0.006; HLA-DR, mean fold increase of MFI = 2.4, paired t test, t = 3.16, p = 0.012). ###begin p 48
These data indicate that both mDCs and monocytes are activated after 24 h co-incubation with iRBCs but that the nature of activation differs between the two accessory cell populations. Up-regulation of CD40 is more pronounced in monocytes and up-regulation of HLA-DR is more pronounced in mDCs, whilst up-regulation of CD80 occurs exclusively in monocytes.
###end p 48
###begin title 49
Accessory Cells Express Both Pro- and Anti-Inflammatory Cytokines in Response to Activation by iRBCs
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 278 286 278 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g004">Figure 4</xref>
To characterise further the activation of accessory cells by iRBCs, we analysed the cytokine response profile after culturing PBMCs for 24 h with iRBCs by quantitative real-time PCR analysis of adherent cells and by Luminex cytokine bead array analysis of culture supernatants (Figure 4).
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 349 357 341 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g004">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 561 569 545 553 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g004">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 871 879 847 855 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g004">Figure 4</xref>
Unstimulated adherent cells expressed detectable levels of mRNA for IL-15, IL-18, and, at somewhat lower levels, IL-10, IL-12, and IL-23, indicating either constitutive expression of these cytokines or their induction during the cell separation process. Levels of IFN-alpha, IL-2, and IFN-gamma mRNA were below the limits of detection of the assay (Figure 4A). However, after iRBC stimulation there was very marked up-regulation of mRNA for IFN-alpha and IL-2 and, to a somewhat lesser extent, for IL-10 and IFN-gamma but not for IL-12, IL-15, IL-18, or IL-23 (Figure 4B). Taken together, these data indicate constitutive or induced expression of IL-12, IL-15, IL-18, IL-23, IFN-alpha, IL-2, IL-10, and IFN-gamma by adherent mononuclear cells stimulated with iRBCs. Production of IL-2, IL-10, and IL-12 in response to iRBC stimulation was confirmed at the protein level (Figure 4C); the discrepancy between mRNA and protein levels for IL-12 is most likely explained by the timing of the assays (all of which were conducted after 24 h).
###end p 51
###begin title 52
Type 1 IFN and IL-2, but Not IL-15, Are Required for NK Cell Activation by iRBCs whilst TGF-beta Suppresses This Response
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 93 95 93 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b019">19</xref>
###xml 96 98 96 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b022">22</xref>
###xml 218 219 218 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b007">7</xref>
###xml 220 221 220 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b008">8</xref>
###xml 716 718 709 711 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b023">23</xref>
###xml 878 880 864 866 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b024">24</xref>
###xml 881 883 867 869 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b025">25</xref>
###xml 1266 1275 1238 1247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="ppat-0020118-sg003">Figure S3</xref>
###xml 788 793 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
NK cell activation by pathogens is typically dependent on accessory cell-secreted cytokines [19-22], and we have shown that IL-12 and, to a somewhat lesser extent, IL-18 are required for optimal NK responses to iRBCs [7,8]. To determine whether other accessory cell-derived cytokines are also required for NK activation, the response to iRBCs was analysed in the presence or absence of neutralising antibodies to individual cytokines or their receptors. Of particular interest were those cytokines that we had found (as described above) to be specifically and strongly upregulated by iRBCs, namely IFN-alpha and IL-2. Also, since TGF-beta has recently been reported to be a powerful inhibitor of NK cell activation [23], and since we have shown that induction of TGF-beta in vivo in both human and rodent malaria infections is associated with lack of early IFN-gamma responses [24,25], it was of considerable interest to determine whether TGF-beta might regulate the NK cell IFN-gamma response to iRBCs in our in vitro assay system where platelets and mononuclear cells provide an abundant source of latent TGF-beta. Representative fluorescence activated cell sorting (FACS) plots showing the effect of blocking cytokines on IFN-gamma response to iRBCs are shown in Figure S3.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 198 199 187 188 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 206 207 195 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>

On the other hand, neutralisation of IL-15 had only a minimal effect on NK cell IFN-gamma responses (highest antibody dose, paired t test, t = 1.83, p = 0.209) (Figure 5C) or CD69 expression (highest antibody dose, paired t test, t = 3.36, p = 0.078) (Figure S4).

When PBMCs were incubated with iRBCs for 24 h in the presence or absence of increasing doses of neutralising antibody to TGF-beta, there was a highly consistent, dose-dependent increase in the proportion of NK cells synthesising IFN-gamma, with up to a 3-fold increase in IFN-gamma+ NK cells in the presence of 100 mug/ml antibody (paired t test, t = 7.98, p = 0.004) (Figure 5D) and a slight, but statistically significant, increase in CD69 expression (highest antibody dose, paired t test, t = 4.00, p = 0.028) (Figure S4). Conversely, addition of recombinant human TGF-beta led to a very marked, dose-dependent suppression of the NK cell IFN-gamma response to iRBCs (highest dose, 1 ng/ml; paired t test, t = 12.8, p = 0.001; unpublished data).

###begin title 56
Optimal NK Cell Response to iRBCs Requires Contact between NK Cells and Accessory Cells
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 119 121 119 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b012">12</xref>
###xml 122 124 122 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b022">22</xref>
###xml 125 127 125 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b026">26</xref>
###xml 128 130 128 130 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b027">27</xref>
###xml 762 771 761 770 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="ppat-0020118-sg005">Figure S5</xref>
###xml 877 885 872 880 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 924 932 919 927 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g006">Figure 6</xref>
For some microbial stimuli it has been reported that optimal NK cell activation requires contact with accessory cells [12,22,26,27]. We thus sought to determine whether such contact-dependent signals were also required for NK activation by iRBCs. Purified NK cells (in the upper chamber of a Transwell culture system) were separated from PBMCs (in the lower chamber) by a microporous membrane (0.4-mum diameter), which allowed diffusion of small molecules including cytokines, but not migration of leukocytes, iRBCs, or extracellular malarial merozoites. When a soluble positive control stimulus (phytohaemagglutinin) was added to the lower well, NK cells in the inner (top) well responded normally, indicating that diffusion of soluble signals was not impeded (Figure S5). When iRBCs were added to the lower chamber, NK cells within the PBMC population synthesised IFN-gamma (Figure 6A, PBMC control) and upregulated CD69 (Figure 6B, PBMC control). However, addition of iRBCs to the upper well, the lower well, or both the upper and lower wells together was insufficient to induce optimal IFN-gamma production among the NK cells in the upper well, suggesting that contact-mediated interactions between NK cells and iRBCs and/or between accessory cells and iRBCs are insufficient for full NK activation and that direct cell-cell contact between the NK cells and the accessory cells is required.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 342 350 342 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 403 411 403 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g001">Figure 1</xref>
Contact between NK cells and accessory cells was also required for optimal up-regulation of CD69, although partial up-regulation of CD69 was observed in some cases when purified NK cells were in contact with iRBCs and/or when accessory cells were in contact with iRBCs. The up-regulation of CD69 by purified NK cells in the Transwell system (Figure 6), but not in regular cultures of purified NK cells (Figure 1), is presumably due to the presence of soluble, cytokine-mediated signals derived from PBMCs in the lower well.
###end p 58
###begin title 59
NK Cell-Derived Signals Maintain the Maturation Status of Accessory Cells but Are Not Required for Accessory Cell Responses to iRBCs or LPS
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 316 318 316 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b028">28</xref>
###xml 319 321 319 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b030">30</xref>
In recent years it has become apparent that interactions between NK cells and DCs can be bidirectional. NK cells, especially those activated by prior exposure to IL-2, enhance DC maturation, inflammatory cytokine secretion, and antigen presentation in allogeneic situations and when exposed to virus-infected cells [28-30]. On the other hand, the degree of reciprocity between NK cells and monocytes, and the potential for NK cells to augment accessory cell responses to non-viral pathogens, has not to our knowledge been investigated. We therefore examined whether the interactions that we had observed to be crucial for the activation of NK cells by iRBCs led to reciprocal activation of either monocytes or mDCs.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 209 217 209 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 379 387 379 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 389 390 389 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g007">7</xref>
###xml 474 476 474 476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b029">29</xref>
###xml 747 755 747 755 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 757 758 757 758 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g007">7</xref>
Whole PBMCs or PBMCs depleted of NK cells were cultured for 24 h without stimulation (gm) or with LPS or iRBCs. After 24 h, cells were stained for HLA-DR, CD14, either CD40 or CD80, and either CD11c or CD11b (Figure 7A). Both mDCs and monocytes expressed significantly lower levels of activation markers when cultured (without additional stimulation) in the absence of NK cells (Figure 7B-7D), confirming a previous report of a role for NK cells in promoting DC maturation [29] and extending this observation to monocytes. On the other hand, when cells were cultured with either LPS or iRBCs, there was no significant effect of the presence or absence of NK cells on expression levels of any of the activation markers in either mDCs or monocytes (Figure 7B-7D). Thus, while NK cells play a role in maturation of both monocytes and mDCs in resting conditions, the response of either of these cell types to LPS or iRBC stimulation is largely NK cell-independent. These data suggest that, at this early time point, the major inducers of myeloid cell activation are the pathogens themselves, rather than NK-derived IFN-gamma.
###end p 61
###begin title 62
Degree of Monocyte Activation Correlates with Magnitude of NK Cell IFN-gamma Response
###end title 62
###begin p 63
###xml 82 83 82 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b008">8</xref>
###xml 84 85 84 85 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b009">9</xref>
###xml 189 197 189 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 228 236 228 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g004">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 571 577 567 573 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bright</sup>
###xml 586 589 582 585 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dim</sup>
###xml 757 760 753 756 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dim</sup>
###xml 773 779 769 775 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bright</sup>
###xml 30 35 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We have previously shown that human NK cell responses to iRBCs are heterogeneous [8,9]. Here, we noticed a considerable degree of heterogeneity of accessory cell surface marker expression (Figure 3D) and of cytokine production (Figure 4) among donors, in terms of both resting levels and responses to iRBCs. Given the absolute dependence of the NK cell response to iRBCs on accessory cells, we sought to determine whether heterogeneity of NK cell responses correlated with heterogeneity of accessory cell responses. Since NK cell IFN-gamma responses can vary between CD56bright and CD56dim NK cells-presumably because of differential expression of NK cell receptors including the killer cell immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIRs), which are expressed by CD56dim but not CD56bright NK cells-we analysed the correlation between accessory cell and NK cell responses for both of these NK cell populations.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 92 100 92 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g008">Figure 8</xref>
###xml 113 121 113 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g008">Figure 8</xref>
###xml 283 284 275 276 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 289 295 281 287 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bright</sup>
###xml 300 301 292 293 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 352 360 344 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g008">Figure 8</xref>
###xml 425 428 417 420 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dim</sup>
###xml 634 641 622 629 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">resting</italic>
###xml 748 756 732 740 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g008">Figure 8</xref>
###xml 762 763 746 747 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g008">8</xref>
###xml 882 888 862 868 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bright</sup>
###xml 920 923 900 903 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dim</sup>
###xml 1106 1109 1082 1085 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dim</sup>
Highly significant correlations were observed between iRBC-induced concentrations of IL-12 (Figure 8A) and IL-2 (Figure 8B) and the magnitude of the NK cell IFN-gamma response. A particularly close correlation was observed between IL-12 concentrations and the proportion of IFN-gamma+ CD56bright (KIR-) cells, whilst, for several donors (see arrows in Figure 8), a significant discrepancy was seen in the response of the CD56dim population. Somewhat surprisingly, there was no correlation between iRBC-induced up-regulation of HLA-DR, CD40, or CD80 on either monocytes or mDCs and NK IFN-gamma production (unpublished data). However, resting levels of expression of both CD40 and HLA-DR were tightly correlated with the NK cell IFN-gamma response (Figure 8C and 8D). For resting levels of CD40 expression there was again a much closer correlation with the IFN-gamma response of CD56bright NK cells than with that of CD56dim NK cells. One plausible interpretation of these data is that the NK cell IFN-gamma response is very sensitive to accessory cell CD40 expression and IL-12 production, but that in CD56dim cells this response is moderated by KIR/major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I-mediated signals.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 88 96 81 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 756 764 722 730 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020118-g008">Figure 8</xref>
###xml 810 813 776 779 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dim</sup>
###xml 853 859 819 825 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bright</sup>
Our observation that TGF-beta is able to markedly suppress NK cell IFN-gamma responses (Figure 5) led us to consider whether the heterogeneity of NK cell responses was due to differential production and/or activation of TGF-beta. There was no consistent increase or decrease in the concentration of bioactive TGF-beta in the supernatants of iRBC-stimulated PBMCs; a modest increase in bioactive TGF-beta concentration was observed for two donors, but these were, in fact, the donors with the highest NK cell IFN-gamma responses to iRBCs, suggesting that suppression by TGF-beta does not explain the heterogeneity of NK cell IFN-gamma response. Rather, regulation of bioactive TGF-beta appears to be positively correlated with NK cell IFN-gamma expression (Figure 8E), and furthermore, the correlation with CD56dim NK cells was higher than that with CD56bright NK cells. This suggests that TGF-beta is not causal in NK cell activation, but rather that the total amount of IFN-gamma produced determines the amount of TGF-beta. Thus, activation may lead to induction of negative feedback mechanisms in order to prevent excessive pro-inflammatory responses.
###end p 65
###begin title 66
Discussion
###end title 66
###begin p 67
###xml 59 64 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 894 899 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
By carefully dissecting the requirements for activation of human NK cells by iRBCs, we have revealed that the early NK cell-derived IFN-gamma response is absolutely dependent upon contact with, and cytokine production by, accessory cells. We have identified the requisite accessory cells as monocytes and mDCs and have shown that both cell types become highly activated during the first 24 h of exposure to iRBCs. Furthermore, we have demonstrated that whilst NK cells provide essential signals that maintain the maturation status of resting monocytes and mDCs, NK cells are not required for these same cells to respond fully to iRBCs. Finally, we have shown that the capacity of NK cells to secrete IFN-gamma is highly correlated with both the resting phenotype of the accessory cells and the concentration of iRBC-induced monokines. Thus, functional heterogeneity of monocytes and mDCs among human populations appears to play a crucial role in determining the magnitude of the innate immune response to iRBCs.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 216 218 216 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b013">13</xref>
###xml 219 221 219 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b017">17</xref>
###xml 222 224 222 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b031">31</xref>
###xml 532 542 532 542 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Plasmodium</italic>
###xml 560 562 560 562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b032">32</xref>
###xml 563 565 563 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b034">34</xref>
###xml 947 949 943 945 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b035">35</xref>
###xml 1068 1070 1064 1066 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b025">25</xref>
###xml 1091 1096 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
A wealth of evidence suggests that, after interaction with malarial parasites, both monocytes and mDCs secrete immunosuppressive cytokines and have an impaired ability to respond to a variety of stimulatory ligands [13-17,31]. These data have led some to conclude that monocyte and DC function is universally impaired by contact with malarial parasites and that this underlies an apparent suppression of immune responses seen during malaria infections. However, there have been several reports of activation of monocytes and DCs by Plasmodium-derived ligands [32-34]. Studies of rodent malaria infections have begun to resolve this debate, showing that in the first 24 h of infection, splenic DCs are activated, secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines including IL-12 and tumour necrosis factor-alpha, and present antigen to T cells, but that they subsequently become refractory to re-stimulation and secrete anti-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-10 [35]. This sequence of events allows infection to be controlled whilst simultaneously avoiding immune-mediated pathology [25]. Here we show that human monocytes and mDCs are highly activated after 24 h of co-incubation with iRBCs. The myeloid cell response occurred in the presence or absence of NK cells, indicating that direct interaction with parasites-as opposed to NK-derived IFN-gamma-drives the initial activation of these cells. Furthermore, this activation initiates and regulates the crucial early burst of IFN-gamma from NK cells. However, our observation that NK activation goes hand in hand with IL-10 and TGF-beta production, and that TGF-beta can suppress NK IFN-gamma production, is consistent with the notion that pro-inflammatory responses to malaria are carefully regulated by homeostatic mechanisms to prevent immune-mediated pathology.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 199 201 199 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b036">36</xref>
###xml 650 652 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b037">37</xref>
###xml 716 718 716 718 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b038">38</xref>
###xml 719 721 719 721 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b039">39</xref>
###xml 850 852 850 852 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b040">40</xref>
###xml 853 855 853 855 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b041">41</xref>
###xml 1042 1044 1034 1036 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b042">42</xref>
###xml 1045 1047 1037 1039 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b043">43</xref>
Our observations regarding the accessory cell requirements for NK cell activation by malaria parasites are in partial agreement with a previous report invoking a role for monocytes in this response [36]; however, we show that mDCs play an equally important role. The fact that NK activation is suboptimal but not absent in the presence of only one or the other accessory cell population suggests that although there is some redundancy in their roles, they also provide complementary signals. Our data are consistent with reports of leukocyte activation by malarial glycosylphosphatidylinositol signalling through toll-like receptor (TLR) 2 and TLR4 [37], which are both expressed on monocytes and mDCs but not pDCs [38,39], although a role for other receptors cannot be ruled out. The reported activation of pDCs by a malarial hemozoin-TLR9 pathway [40,41] does not appear to be required for NK IFN-gamma production, consistent with previous reports that NK cytotoxic activity but not IFN-gamma secretion is dependent on pDC-derived signals [42,43]. In fact, depletion of pDCs increased the IFN-gamma response of NK cells in two out of three donors, suggesting that pDCs may have a net anti-inflammatory effect in this system, perhaps mediated by either IL-10 or TGF-beta. Further experiments are required to test this possibility.
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 202 204 198 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b044">44</xref>
###xml 205 207 201 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b046">46</xref>
###xml 273 275 269 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b012">12</xref>
###xml 276 278 272 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b022">22</xref>
###xml 279 281 275 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b026">26</xref>
###xml 282 284 278 280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b027">27</xref>
###xml 381 383 377 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b047">47</xref>
###xml 575 577 571 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b012">12</xref>
###xml 631 633 627 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b048">48</xref>
###xml 691 693 687 689 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b049">49</xref>
###xml 1016 1017 1012 1013 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b009">9</xref>
###xml 1018 1020 1014 1016 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b036">36</xref>
###xml 1118 1119 1114 1115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b009">9</xref>
###xml 360 365 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The data presented here reveal that accessory cell-dependent activation of NK cells by iRBCs is multifactorial and, as in other systems, involves multiple cytokines (IL-2, IL-12, IL-18, and IFN-alpha) [44-46] as well as direct contact between NK cells and accessory cells [12,22,26,27]. Whether accessory cells are the source of IL-2, as recently reported for human pDCs and mDCs [47], is not clear, and we cannot rule out that IL-2 is produced by a small contaminating population of rapidly responding T cells. Contact-dependent signals might include CD40-CD40L signalling [12], delivery of IL-12 from mDCs via an immune synapse [48], or binding of MHC-peptide complexes by activating KIR [49]. We believe that indirect, accessory cell-mediated signals are likely to be the major mechanism of NK cell activation by iRBCs, but we cannot rule out that the NK cell receives additional signals via direct interaction with iRBCs. Stable contacts between iRBCs and NK cells have been reported by ourselves and by others [9,36], and we have observed reorganisation of the actin cytoskeleton at the point of iRBC-NK contact [9]; the functional consequence of these interactions are, however, not yet fully understood.
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 261 263 261 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b028">28</xref>
###xml 264 266 264 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b030">30</xref>
###xml 765 767 765 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b050">50</xref>
Our observation that NK cells are not required for optimal activation of monocytes and mDCs by pathogen-derived ligands (iRBCs and LPS) runs somewhat counter to previous reports that NK cells augment DC maturation, cytokine secretion, and antigen presentation [28-30]. One plausible explanation for this discrepancy is that the sequence of events leading to activation of NK cells and accessory cells depends on the nature of the stimulus. In the case of virally infected or neoplastic cells, altered expression of MHC or MHC-like ligands on the target cell will lead to direct activation of NK cells either through engagement of natural cytotoxicity receptors or changes in the balance of activating and inhibitory signals delivered by KIR receptors (reviewed in [50]). In this case, amplification of the innate response and induction of adaptive immunity would be facilitated if NK cells were able to provide activating signals to DCs and other antigen-presenting cells, which are much less sensitive to changes in MHC expression. On the other hand, in the case of bacterial and protozoal pathogens, which do not grossly alter MHC class I expression or induce MHC-related stress ligands on host cells, direct activation of NK cells is unlikely. Rather, DCs and monocytes are likely to be directly activated by binding of pathogen-associated molecular patterns to TLRs and other pattern recognition receptors and may not require additional signals from NK cells.
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 107 108 107 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b001">1</xref>
###xml 464 465 460 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b008">8</xref>
###xml 466 467 462 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b009">9</xref>
###xml 971 973 967 969 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b051">51</xref>
###xml 974 976 970 972 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b053">53</xref>
###xml 1067 1073 1063 1069 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bright</sup>
###xml 1078 1079 1074 1075 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1118 1121 1114 1117 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dim</sup>
###xml 1126 1127 1122 1123 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1368 1370 1364 1366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b054">54</xref>
###xml 1485 1488 1481 1484 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dim</sup>
###xml 1564 1566 1560 1562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b055">55</xref>
###xml 2240 2242 2232 2234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020118-b056">56</xref>
###xml 162 167 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 547 552 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 624 629 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1354 1359 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1422 1427 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Given the importance of the innate response to malaria in determining the outcome of disease (reviewed in [1]), dissecting the basis for the heterogeneity of the human NK cell response to iRBCs could be crucial in understanding disease susceptibility. The close correlations shown here between accessory cell maturation, accessory cell responses to iRBCs, and induction of IFN-gamma production by NK cells suggest that heterogeneity of NK cell responses to iRBCs [8,9] is a reflection of deeply rooted heterogeneity of innate responses within the human population. Ongoing studies of the immunogenetics of susceptibility to human malaria should begin to identify the underlying basis for this heterogeneity, but linking genotype to immunological phenotype may not be straightforward. Multiple genes, including various IFN regulatory factor proteins and components of the colony stimulating factor 1 signalling pathway, influence the maturation status of accessory cells [51-53]. Moreover, the much stronger correlation of accessory cell status with activation of CD56bright (KIR-) NK cells than with activation of CD56dim (KIR+) NK cells suggests that NK cell activation by pathogen-induced accessory cell signals may be modulated by NK cell receptor expression. Since KIR genes, along with MHC genes, are some of the most highly polymorphic loci in the human genome [54], dissecting the genetic basis of diversity in the human innate response is a daunting task. Nevertheless, as CD56dim cells are the major population of NK cells in peripheral blood and spleen [55], our data suggest that NK cell receptor expression is likely to have a significant influence on overall NK cell responsiveness and thus potentially affect the severity of malarial disease and other systemic infections. Despite the difficulties associated with carrying them out, population-based studies of disease risk and diversity in innate response genes could have far-reaching implications, and we have recently begun such studies in malaria-endemic populations. Since the antigen-presenting and co-stimulatory functions of accessory cells are pivotal for induction of adaptive immunity, and the magnitude of the early IFN-gamma response affects adaptive responses [56], heterogeneity of expression of CD40, HLA-DR, and NK cell IFN-gamma is likely to have a profound impact on the generation of acquired immune responses.
