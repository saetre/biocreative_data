Potential for luxS related signalling in marine bacteria and production of autoinducer-2 in the genus Shewanella
###end article-title 0

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 294 299 294 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 604 609 604 609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
The autoinducer-2 (AI-2) group of signalling molecules are produced by both Gram positive and Gram negative bacteria as the by-product of a metabolic transformation carried out by the LuxS enzyme. They are the only non species-specific quorum sensing compounds presently known in bacteria. The luxS gene coding for the AI-2 synthase enzyme was found in many important pathogens. Here, we surveyed its occurrence in a collection of 165 marine isolates belonging to abundant marine phyla using conserved degenerated PCR primers and sequencing of selected positive bands to determine if the presence of the luxS gene is phylogenetically conserved or dependent on the habitat.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 4 9 4 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 44 64 44 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 77 91 77 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacteroidetes </italic>
###xml 158 163 158 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 228 229 228 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</italic>
###xml 299 319 299 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 329 334 329 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 351 370 351 370 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella, Vibrio </italic>
###xml 374 386 374 386 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alteromonas </italic>
###xml 404 422 404 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudoalteromonas </italic>
###xml 426 436 426 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Halomonas </italic>
###xml 451 456 451 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 472 486 472 486 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psychrobacter </italic>
###xml 507 527 507 527 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 565 570 565 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 578 583 578 583 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 641 652 641 652 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 662 677 662 677 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio harveyi </italic>
###xml 706 717 706 717 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 889 900 889 900 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 662 676 <span type="species:ncbi:669">Vibrio harveyi</span>
The luxS gene was not present in any of the Alphaproteobacteria (n = 71) and Bacteroidetes strains (n = 29) tested; by contrast, these bacteria harboured the sahH gene, coding for an alternative enzyme for the detoxification of S-adenosylhomocysteine (SAH) in the activated methyl cycle. Within the Gammaproteobacteria (n = 76), luxS was found in all Shewanella, Vibrio and Alteromonas isolates and some Pseudoalteromonas and Halomonas species, while sahH was detected in Psychrobacter strains. A number of Gammaproteobacteria (n = 27) appeared to have neither the luxS nor the sahH gene. We then studied the production of AI-2 in the genus Shewanella using the Vibrio harveyi bioassay. All ten species of Shewanella tested produced a pronounced peak of AI-2 towards the end of the exponential growth phase in several media investigated. The maximum of AI-2 activity was different in each Shewanella species, ranging from 4% to 46% of the positive control.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 104 106 104 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lu</italic>
###xml 234 254 234 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 328 339 328 339 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
The data are consistent with those of fully sequenced bacterial genomes and show that the potential for luxS related signalling is dependent on phylogenetic affiliation rather than ecological niche and is largest in certain groups of Gammaproteobacteria in the marine environment. This is the first report on AI-2 production in Shewanella species; its signalling role in these organisms remains to be elucidated.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 438 439 438 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 490 505 490 505 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio harveyi </italic>
###xml 490 504 <span type="species:ncbi:669">Vibrio harveyi</span>
Cell to cell communication - also called quorum sensing - by self-produced small signalling molecules (autoinducers) is a mechanism by which bacteria coordinate gene expression within their population and in such a way obtain optimum timing and efficiency, for example when infecting a eukaryotic host, degrading polymers, or colonizing surfaces. While most quorum sensing mechanisms studied to date are species specific, Bassler et al. [1] discovered an autoinducer in the marine pathogen Vibrio harveyi which is the byproduct of a reaction carried out by a metabolic enzyme and thus has the potential to act as a universal bacterial signal.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 204 205 204 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</italic>
###xml 270 271 270 271 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</italic>
###xml 303 304 303 304 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</italic>
###xml 388 399 388 399 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 400 401 400 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 410 411 410 411 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</italic>
###xml 488 511 488 511 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella typhimurium </italic>
###xml 512 513 512 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 531 548 531 548 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 549 550 549 550 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 564 574 564 574 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi</italic>
###xml 602 603 602 603 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</italic>
###xml 613 614 613 614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</italic>
###xml 768 769 768 769 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 770 771 770 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 388 398 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
###xml 488 510 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">Salmonella typhimurium</span>
###xml 531 547 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 564 574 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
Autoinducer-2 (AI-2) refers to a group of compounds resulting from spontaneous cyclisation and hydration of dihydroxypentanedione (DPD), which yields (2S,4S)-2-methyl-2,3,3,4-tetrahydroxytetrahydrofuran (S-THMF) and (2R,4S)-2-methyl-2,3,3,4-tetrahydroxytetrahydrofuran (R-THMF) in chemical equilibrium. S-THMF spontaneously reacts with borate ions to form the active compound detected by V. harveyi [2], while R-THMF, the other stereoisomere, is unable to complex borate and is sensed by Salmonella typhimurium [3] and probably by Escherichia coli [4], but not by V. harveyi. The receptor proteins for S-THMF and R-THMF are not homologous, and their three dimensional structures determine the specificity of their interaction with the respective signalling molecules [2,3].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 156 157 156 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</italic>
###xml 250 251 250 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</italic>
###xml 702 703 702 703 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 704 705 704 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
DPD is produced only in the domain Bacteria as a by-product of the activated methyl cycle. The pathway leading to DPD involves a two-step detoxification of S-adenosylhomocysteine (SAH), namely by a MTA/SAH nucleosidase (also called Pfs enzyme) and a S-ribosylhomocysteinase. The latter enzyme is also known as LuxS enzyme or AI-2 synthase. The products of the reaction carried out by the LuxS enzyme are homocysteine and DPD, which spontaneously forms the AI-2 group of compounds. However, all Eukarya, Archaea, and some phyla within the domain Bacteria employ an alternative one-step detoxification of SAH using only a SAH hydrolase (SahH) enzyme. These bacteria are therefore unable to produce AI-2 [5,6].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 20 25 20 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 90 95 90 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 194 195 194 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 196 197 196 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 210 215 210 215 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 238 243 238 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gamma</italic>
###xml 249 267 249 267 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Betaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 273 294 273 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Epsilonproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 308 327 308 327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 341 355 341 355 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Actinobacteria</italic>
###xml 361 372 361 372 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Firmicutes </italic>
###xml 381 393 381 393 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Spirochaetes</italic>
###xml 610 615 610 615 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
The presence of the luxS gene (two step detoxification of SAH leading to AI-2) versus the sahH gene (one step detoxification) was previously surveyed in the genomes of fully sequenced bacteria [6,7]. Thus, the luxS gene was found in some Gamma- and Betaproteobacteria, all Epsilonproteobacteria, none of the Alphaproteobacteria, none of the Actinobacteria, all Firmicutes and some Spirochaetes. Since the majority of the fully sequenced bacteria at the time were pathogens, and the number of genomes within the various phylogenetic groups was sometimes very small, it had to remain open if the presence of the luxS gene is phylogenetically conserved or if it has been acquired through lateral gene transfer by pathogens.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 88 107 88 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 109 128 109 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 134 148 134 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacteroidetes </italic>
###xml 149 150 149 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 151 153 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 257 259 257 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 260 262 260 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 288 293 288 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 416 421 416 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 544 549 544 549 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 646 651 646 651 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 658 663 658 663 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 716 736 716 736 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia blattae </italic>
###xml 787 792 787 792 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 796 801 796 801 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 802 803 802 803 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 804 805 804 805 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 716 735 <span type="species:ncbi:563">Escherichia blattae</span>
In the sea, the three most abundant groups of heterotrophic bacteria are members of the Alphaproteobacteria, Gammaproteobacteria, and Bacteroidetes [8-10]. Therefore, here we studied a collection of 165 marine isolates from these three phylogenetic groups [11,12] for the presence of the luxS gene using PCR amplification with conserved degenerated primers and sequencing of selected bands. Since the absence of the luxS gene could theoretically be due to lack of primer specificity, we confirmed our results by determining the presence of the sahH gene, representing the alternative pathway for SAH detoxification. Most bacteria have either the luxS or the sahH gene in monocopy. There are several exceptions, e.g. Escherichia blattae carries both genes, and some bacteria have neither luxS nor sahH [6,7].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 11 30 11 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 36 41 36 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 66 77 66 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 109 120 109 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 149 151 149 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 238 253 238 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. putrefaciens</italic>
###xml 297 305 297 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. algae</italic>
###xml 308 310 308 310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 311 313 311 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 366 377 366 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 633 647 633 647 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. oneidensis </italic>
###xml 680 682 680 682 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 683 685 683 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 701 706 701 706 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 729 743 729 743 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. oneidensis </italic>
###xml 775 786 775 786 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 993 1004 993 1004 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 1036 1047 1036 1047 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 1110 1121 1110 1121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 238 253 <span type="species:ncbi:24">S. putrefaciens</span>
###xml 280 285 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 297 305 <span type="species:ncbi:38313">S. algae</span>
###xml 633 651 <span type="species:ncbi:211586">S. oneidensis MR-1</span>
###xml 729 742 <span type="species:ncbi:70863">S. oneidensis</span>
###xml 1110 1120 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
Within the Gammaproteobacteria, the luxS gene was detected in all Shewanella strains consistently. The genus Shewanella was first described in 1985 [13] and includes organisms that have been associated with spoilage of proteinacous food (S. putrefaciens) as well as opportunistic human pathogens (S. algae) [14,15]. During the past two decades, members of the genus Shewanella have mostly been studied for their involvement in anaerobic transformations important for biogeochemical cycling of elements. The coupling of growth and energy generation to the dissimilatory reduction of iron and manganese oxide have been demonstrated in S. oneidensis MR-1 for the first time in 1988 [16-18]. Although the luxS gene has been found in S. oneidensis and in several draft genomes of Shewanella strains which are currently sequenced, AI-2 production has not yet been investigated in this genus, which comprises both pathogenic and free living species. Therefore, here we determined the ability of nine Shewanella type strains and one new marine Shewanella isolate to stimulate luminescence of the AI-2 biosensor strain V. harveyi BB170 and indeed confirmed the production of AI-2 in all of them for the first time.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Results
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 247 255 247 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in situ </italic>
###xml 293 295 293 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 296 298 296 298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 385 387 385 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 530 549 530 549 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lphaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 564 578 564 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacteroidetes </italic>
###xml 588 608 588 608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 680 691 680 691 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 733 744 733 744 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 752 774 752 774 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alishewanella fetalis </italic>
###xml 830 831 830 831 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 752 773 <span type="species:ncbi:111143">Alishewanella fetalis</span>
In this study, 148 strains were investigated which have been isolated from various habitats of the North Sea near the island of Helgoland, e.g. the surface of microoalgae (diatoms, dinoflagellates) and macroalgae (laminaria), suspended particles, in situ grown biofilms, and the picoplankton [11,19]. In addition, 8 strains from the hypersaline Antarctic Ekho Lake were also included [12]. The marine isolates were identified on the basis of near complete 16S rRNA gene sequences and belonged to 3 different phylogenetic groups: Alphaproteobacteria (71 isolates), Bacteroidetes (29), and Gammaproteobacteria (64). In order to confirm the results which were obtained on the marine Shewanella isolates, eight type strains of the genus Shewanella and one Alishewanella fetalis type strain were also investigated (see Additional file 1 for a complete list of strains tested).
###end p 16
###begin title 17
###xml 22 27 22 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
Identification of the luxS gene
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 86 105 86 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 163 164 163 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>

###xml 223 243 223 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 247 261 247 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacteroidetes </italic>
###xml 293 294 293 294 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>

All marine isolates were screened with primers LuxS_degfor3 and LuxS_degrev4; for the Gammaproteobacteria, the primer set luxS_gamma_for and luxS_gamma_rev (Table 1) was also used. The luxS gene was not found in any of the Alphaproteobacteria and Bacteroidetes strains tested (Additional file 1), consistent with the survey of fully sequenced microbial genomes published previously [6,7].

###xml 826 841 <span type="species:ncbi:228">P. haloplanktis</span>
###xml 846 858 <span type="species:ncbi:434234|species:ncbi:406074|species:ncbi:524167|species:ncbi:356481|species:ncbi:426657|species:ncbi:288">P. atlantica</span>
###xml 1448 1458 <span type="species:ncbi:111143">A. fetalis</span>
In the Gammaproteobacteria, the fully sequenced genomes available through NCBI in January 2007 revealed that some representatives use the two step detoxification of SAH, hence contain the pfs gene and the luxS gene, while others use the one-step detoxification of SAH, thus harbour the sahH gene. There are several parasitic and also some free living species which have neither luxS nor sahH, but only the pfs gene. We screened 64 marine Gammaproteobacteria for the presence of the luxS gene (Additional file 1). It was found in all strains of Alteromonas, consistent with the full genome sequence of Alteromonas hydrophila. It was also found in seven of 13 strains of Halomonas tested; no complete genome sequence is available in this genus yet. In the genus Pseudoalteromonas, two genome sequences have been completed, i.e. P. haloplanktis and P. atlantica. Both species have neither a luxS nor a sahH homologue, but contain only the pfs gene. We tested 28 Pseudoalteromonas isolates, and found a PCR band of the expected size in 12 of them. In one strain the band could be confirmed by sequencing to represent a luxS gene fragment. Finally, the luxS gene was identified in the marine Shewanella (three strains) and Vibrio (three strains) isolates, as expected from the completed genomes in these genera. In order to determine if the luxS gene was consistently present in the Shewanellaceae family, nine type strains were investigated (including A. fetalis). In all of them the luxS gene could be amplified and verified by sequencing.

Identification of the sahH gene

###xml 838 859 <span type="species:ncbi:135577">Idiomarina loihiensis</span>
###xml 864 888 <span type="species:ncbi:86304">Saccharophagus degradans</span>
The sahH gene is responsible for the S-adenosylhomocysteinase enzyme, which is able to detoxify SAH in one step without production of AI-2. In bacteria, usually either sahH or luxS is present as a single copy. All strains were tested with both sahH primers (Table 1). The sahH gene was identified in 66 out of 71 Alphaproteobacteria and in all 29 Bacteroidetes strains (Additional file 1), confirming that the strains from these phyla do not have the ability to produce AI-2 through the LuxS enzyme. Interestingly, the sahH gene was also found in some Gammaproteobacteria, namely in three strains of Psychrobacter sp. (HEL-6, HEL-32 and HEL-44). Two Psychrobacter species have been fully sequenced and also have the sahH gene (Additional file 1). Full genome sequences of representatives of two additional species of Gammaproteobacteria (Idiomarina loihiensis and Saccharophagus degradans) also revealed the presence of sahH (Additional file 1).

Lack of both the sahH and the luxS gene
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 18 38 18 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 72 92 72 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 118 123 118 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>

###xml 384 403 384 403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 418 423 418 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 427 432 427 432 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 479 490 479 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ricksettia </italic>
###xml 499 500 499 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 528 541 528 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Neoricksettia</italic>
###xml 543 552 543 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Wolbachia</italic>

###xml 591 610 591 610 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 740 741 740 741 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 780 785 780 785 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 789 794 789 794 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 837 847 837 847 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Glaciecola</italic>
###xml 849 859 849 859 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Halomonas </italic>
###xml 862 874 862 874 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Marinobacter</italic>
###xml 966 983 966 983 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudoalteromonas</italic>
###xml 1016 1032 1016 1032 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. haloplanktis </italic>
###xml 1036 1049 1036 1049 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. atlantica </italic>
###xml 1067 1072 1067 1072 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 1076 1080 1076 1080 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH</italic>
###xml 1412 1417 1412 1417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 1016 1031 <span type="species:ncbi:228">P. haloplanktis</span>
###xml 1036 1048 <span type="species:ncbi:434234|species:ncbi:406074|species:ncbi:524167|species:ncbi:356481|species:ncbi:426657|species:ncbi:288">P. atlantica</span>
In five of the 31 Alphaproteobacteria investigated, and in 27 of the 64 Gammaproteobacteria investigated, neither the luxS nor the sahH gene was detected. Available whole genome sequences of the phylogenetically closest organisms in the NCBI database were checked for comparison, since it can never be excluded that a negative result in PCR is caused by a primer mismatch. Within the Alphaproteobacteria, lack of both luxS and sahH has previously only been seen in the parasitic Ricksettia species [7], as well as in the genera Neoricksettia, Wolbachia, Anaplasma, and Ehrlichia [6]. In the Gammaproteobacteria, the lack of both genes has previously been observed, and SAH recycling presumably occurs through the MTA/SAH nucleoidase alone [7]. Ten of the 27 strains where neither luxS nor sahH could be identified belonged to the genera Glaciecola, Halomonas or Marinobacter, where genome sequences are not yet available. However, 17 strains belonged into the genus Pseudoalteromonas. The fully sequenced genomes of P. haloplanktis and P. atlantica also have neither luxS nor sahH, suggesting that this result might not be caused by primer mismatch but is actually correct and indicates SAH detoxification by the Pfs enzyme in these strains. However, confirmation of band identity proved difficult due to the simultaneous amplification of another gene of similar size. It can therefore not be excluded that more luxS genes might have been detected using different primers.

Several of the Gammaproteobacteria species which did not have the luxS gene were tested for AI-2 activity using the V. harveyi bioassay. AI-2 could not be detected, confirming the PCR result (data not shown). All Shewanella and Alishewanella strains contained the luxS gene and were tested for AI-2 activity, which was detected in all of them. Table 2 shows the summary of all 73 bioassays conducted; for each species, the maximum A1-2 activity is averaged for the indicated number of bioassays. Average maximum fold induction values were between 4% (e.g. S. hafniensis, S. marinintestina) and 46% (S. japonica). Figure 1 shows a subset of these data; for each species, two different cultures are shown, each of which was tested in two different bioassays independently. Variability between cultures and between replicate bioassays was sometimes very high. Independent of the level of AI-2 activity detected, the same growth phase dependent pattern of production was observed in all investigated strains. Fig. 2A shows this for two representative species, A. fetalis with low, and S. japonica with high maximum AI-2 activity. In all Shewanella strains AI-2 activity reached its maximum towards the end of the exponential growth phase and decreased during stationary phase. In species with high AI-2 activity, this decrease occurred gradually. By contrast, if the AI-2 activity was low, it disappeared rapidly at the beginning of the stationary phase within 2 - 4 hours. At the late stationary phase (after 24 h of growth), no AI-2 activity was detected in any of the strains tested. The same pattern was observed in all cultivation media used. As an example, Fig. 2B shows AI-2 production of S. hafniensis DT-1 in two different media. The strain produced similar AI-2 activities at the same growth phase in both of them. S. oneidensis was also cultivated anaerobically using iron as an electron acceptor (with 10 and 20 mM ferric citrate, respectively). Under these conditions, growth was very slow and AI-2 activity was not found (data not shown).

###begin title 30
Discussion
###end title 30
###begin title 31
###xml 33 38 33 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 42 47 42 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
Phylogenetic distribution of the sahH and luxS genes
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 7 21 7 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacteroidetes </italic>
###xml 70 84 70 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacteroidetes </italic>
###xml 145 169 145 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Porphyromonas gingivalis</italic>
###xml 204 206 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 404 406 404 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 407 409 407 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 547 548 547 548 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 549 551 549 551 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 605 617 605 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacteroides </italic>
###xml 640 645 640 645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 675 677 675 677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 145 169 <span type="species:ncbi:837">Porphyromonas gingivalis</span>
In all Bacteroidetes isolates tested here, and in all fully sequenced Bacteroidetes strains, the SahH pathway was present, with the exception of Porphyromonas gingivalis, which uses the Pfs/LuxS pathway [20,21]. This may indicate that horizontal gene transfer occurred in this species. The main habitat of this bacterium is the oral cavity, where many of the cultivated species use the Pfs/LuxS pathway [22,23] and gene transfer might have a high probability. Several events of gene transfer have been demonstrated for the LuxS enzyme previously [7,24]. A recent report demonstrated AI-2 activity in some Bacteroides isolates, although the luxS gene could not be identified [25].
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 28 48 28 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 56 61 56 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 103 108 103 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 296 297 296 297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 298 299 298 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
Isolates belonging into the Alphaproteobacteria had the sahH gene consistently and did not contain the luxS gene. Therefore, they use the one-step detoxification of SAH and do not have the potential for communication by AI-2. These results confirm previous work based on fully sequenced genomes [5,7].
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 11 30 11 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 36 41 36 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 85 109 85 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alteromonas, Shewanella </italic>
###xml 113 120 113 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio </italic>
###xml 251 268 251 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudoalteromonas</italic>
###xml 383 388 383 388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 396 401 396 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
Within the Gammaproteobacteria, the luxS gene was found in all strains of the genera Alteromonas, Shewanella and Vibrio consistent with results from fully sequenced genomes in these genera. It was for the first time found in some strains of the genus Pseudoalteromonas. In this genus, whole genome sequencing and PCR amplification indicate that many representatives have neither the luxS nor the sahH gene and thus use only the Pfs enzyme for removal of SAH.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 31 40 31 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Halomonas</italic>
###xml 52 57 52 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 162 182 162 182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 183 195 183 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Marinobacter</italic>
###xml 197 207 197 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Glaciecola</italic>
###xml 209 218 209 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Colwellia</italic>
###xml 247 252 247 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 260 265 260 265 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 297 311 297 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psychrobacter </italic>
###xml 328 333 328 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
The same is true for the genus Halomonas, where the luxS gene was found in some but not all representatives. Species from several other genera of fully sequenced Gammaproteobacteria (Marinobacter, Glaciecola, Colwellia) also contained neither the luxS nor the sahH gene. In addition, in the genus Psychrobacter (3 isolates) the sahH gene was found consistently, in accordance with fully sequenced genomes in this genus.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 70 75 70 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 316 321 316 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 328 333 328 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 340 360 340 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 497 499 497 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 500 502 500 502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
The data strongly support the conclusion that the distribution of the luxS gene is dependent on the phylogenetic position of an organism rather than on the ecological niche which it has occupied, with rare exceptions which may have been caused by horizontal gene transfer. The complex pattern of presence/absence of luxS versus sahH in the Gammaproteobacteria requires a thorough analysis of the organismal phylogeny and possible gene transfer events in this group, which may not be monophyletic [26,27].
###end p 36

###xml 10 21 10 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
The genus Shewanella and the production of autoinducer-2
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 4 15 4 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 94 107 94 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. oneidensis</italic>
###xml 110 112 110 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 149 166 149 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. marinintestina</italic>
###xml 168 177 168 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. sairae</italic>
###xml 179 194 179 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. schlegeliana</italic>
###xml 196 198 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 220 236 220 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. frigidimarina</italic>
###xml 239 241 239 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 259 269 259 269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. fetalis</italic>
###xml 272 274 272 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 317 328 317 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. japonica</italic>
###xml 331 333 331 333 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>

###xml 480 494 480 494 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. hafniensis </italic>
###xml 495 497 495 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 626 628 626 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 94 107 <span type="species:ncbi:70863">S. oneidensis</span>
###xml 149 166 <span type="species:ncbi:190305">S. marinintestina</span>
###xml 168 177 <span type="species:ncbi:190310">S. sairae</span>
###xml 179 194 <span type="species:ncbi:190308">S. schlegeliana</span>
###xml 220 236 <span type="species:ncbi:56812">S. frigidimarina</span>
###xml 246 251 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 259 269 <span type="species:ncbi:111143">A. fetalis</span>
###xml 317 328 <span type="species:ncbi:93973">S. japonica</span>
###xml 336 344 <span type="species:ncbi:38313">S. algae</span>
###xml 447 462 <span type="species:ncbi:24">S. putrefaciens</span>
###xml 480 493 <span type="species:ncbi:365590">S. hafniensis</span>
The Shewanella type strains investigated here were isolated from the sediment of Lake Oneida (S. oneidensis) [17], the intestines of marine animals (S. marinintestina, S. sairae, S. schlegeliana)[28], Antarctic sea ice (S. frigidimarina) [29], a human fetus (A. fetalis) [30], and marine seawater and mussel samples (S. japonica) [31]. S. algae was isolated from red algae [32] and was differentiated from the first described member of the genus, S. putrefaciens, only later[33]. S. hafniensis [34] was isolated from fish in the Baltic Sea, and is 98% similar to the marine isolate DT-1 obtained from the surface of a diatom [11].
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 4 14 4 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella</italic>
###xml 15 29 15 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alishewanella </italic>
###xml 77 88 77 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 160 161 160 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</italic>
###xml 190 201 190 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 318 329 318 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 412 423 412 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 517 526 517 526 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. algae </italic>
###xml 530 544 530 544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. oneidensis </italic>
###xml 565 576 565 576 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 697 707 697 707 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella</italic>
###xml 745 746 745 746 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</italic>
###xml 756 757 756 757 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</italic>
###xml 792 793 792 793 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</italic>
###xml 836 842 836 842 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio</italic>
###xml 77 87 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
###xml 190 200 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
###xml 412 422 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
###xml 517 525 <span type="species:ncbi:38313">S. algae</span>
###xml 530 543 <span type="species:ncbi:70863">S. oneidensis</span>
###xml 565 575 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
All Shewanella/Alishewanella strains were able to induce luminescence in the V. harveyi BB170 reporter strain, indicating that they produced the DPD derivative S-THMF which can be sensed by V. harveyi and which is usually referred to as AI-2. The large differences in the levels of AI-2 seen in the various species of Shewanella are partly due to the variability of the bioassay; however compounds inhibitory to V. harveyi might also be produced by some strains. For example, stationary phase culture supernatants of S. algae and S. oneidensis were able to inhibit V. harveyi luminescence (data not shown). Alternatively, the highly unstable DPD might be converted to compounds other than AI-2 in Shewanella, or the chemical equilibrium between S-THMF and R-THMF might be shifted towards the R-THMF molecule which cannot be detected by Vibrio.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
Production of AI-2 during growth
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 7 18 7 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 321 344 321 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella typhimurium </italic>
###xml 348 365 348 365 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 366 367 366 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 368 370 368 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 555 556 555 556 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</italic>
###xml 589 600 589 600 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella </italic>
###xml 683 694 683 694 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella </italic>
###xml 698 705 698 705 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 907 918 907 918 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 1005 1018 1005 1018 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. oneidensis</italic>
###xml 1055 1059 1055 1059 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lsr </italic>
###xml 321 343 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">Salmonella typhimurium</span>
###xml 348 364 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 698 705 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1005 1018 <span type="species:ncbi:70863">S. oneidensis</span>
In all Shewanella strains, the pattern of AI-2 production during growth was the same. The amount of AI-2 detected in the culture supernatant had a maximum towards the end of the logarithmic growth phase. Subsequently, it decreased and disappeared completely in the late stationary phase. A similar pattern is observed in Salmonella typhimurium and Escherichia coli [4,35]. In these bacteria, AI-2 triggers its own uptake through induction of an ABC transporter in the late logarithmic growth phase, followed by intracellular degradation. AI-2, or rather, R-THMF, is specifically sensed by Salmonella through the LsrR protein which bears remote resemblance to ribose transporters. In Salmonella and E. coli, there is little evidence of cell-cell communication through AI-2. However, nothing is known at the moment about the mechanism responsible for the disappearance of AI-2 from the culture supernatant in Shewanella species. A protein with weak homology (27% identity) to the LsrB protein is present in S. oneidensis, but most of the other genes in the lsr operon are missing.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
Conclusion
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 198 203 198 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 207 212 207 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 462 467 462 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 488 493 488 493 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 499 533 499 533 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria, Bacteroidetes</italic>
###xml 598 617 598 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 694 695 694 695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 696 697 696 697 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 761 766 761 766 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 782 787 782 787 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 916 936 916 936 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria </italic>
In this study, a phylogenetically and ecologically well characterized collection of 165 marine isolates was examined for their potential for communication by AI-2. For this aim, the presence of the luxS and sahH genes - representing the two pathways for SAH recycling - was determined by PCR with degenerate primers and sequencing. The majority of the isolates tested here belonged to phyla where whole genome sequence analysis had indicated the presence of the sahH gene rather than the luxS gene (Alphaproteobacteria, Bacteroidetes), and this was clearly confirmed in our work. Within the marine Gammaproteobacteria, the more complex picture which was already seen in earlier investigations [5-7] was confirmed in this study, with some strains harbouring the luxS gene, while the sahH gene was present in others, and many strains containing neither of those genes, raising questions regarding the evolution of the Gammaproteobacteria and of the Pfs/LuxS and SahH pathways, respectively.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 56 66 56 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella</italic>
###xml 72 77 72 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 119 130 119 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 147 158 147 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 221 232 221 232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 292 302 292 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella</italic>
###xml 414 425 414 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 636 646 636 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella</italic>
###xml 221 231 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
In the second part of our work, we focused on the genus Shewanella. The luxS gene was consistently found in all marine Shewanella isolates and all Shewanella type strains tested. The ability to induce luminescence in the V. harveyi reporter strain was demonstrated in 10 species of the genus Shewanella, both opportunistic pathogens and free living species. The kinetics of AI-2 production was very similar in all Shewanella species, showing a peak in the late logarithmic growth phase, and the disappearance of AI-2 from the culture medium during stationary phase. It will be the aim of future studies to elucidate the role of AI-2 in Shewanella.
###end p 44

###begin title 46
Strains, Media and Cultivation Conditions
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 124 126 124 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 127 129 127 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 169 180 169 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>

###xml 500 501 478 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 0 10 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
###xml 47 57 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
###xml 138 148 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>

V. harveyi BB152 ATCC(R) BAA1119trade mark and V. harveyi BB170 ATCC(R) BAA-1117trade mark [1] were used for the bioassay to detect AI-2. V. harveyi BB170 carries a transposon insertion in the luxN gene, coding for the sensor for AI-1, and is therefore sensitive to AI-2 only. It is used as the sensor strain. V. harveyi BB152 carries a transposon insertion in the luxM gene responsible for AI-1 synthesis and thus produces AI-2, but not AI-1. Its culture supernatant was used as a positive control [1].

###xml 0 11 0 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>

###xml 218 228 206 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. sairae </italic>
###xml 402 413 384 395 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella </italic>
###xml 429 431 411 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 553 554 535 536 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 568 569 550 551 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 148 161 <span type="species:ncbi:70863">S. oneidensis</span>
###xml 176 186 <span type="species:ncbi:111143">A. fetalis</span>
###xml 191 199 <span type="species:ncbi:38313">S. algae</span>
###xml 218 227 <span type="species:ncbi:190310">S. sairae</span>
###xml 477 482 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Shewanella strains were cultivated under aerobic conditions at room temperature with shaking at 160 rpm in Erlenmeyer flasks, with the exception of S. oneidensis (30degreesC), A. fetalis and S. algae (37degreesC), and S. sairae (20degreesC). Media used were Luria broth (LB, Sigma), LBSS (LB with sea salts, composed of LB (Sigma) and 7 g/L sea salts (Sigma)), MB (marine broth, MB 2216, Difco) or SM (Shewanella Marine Medium) [36]. SM medium contained 5 g bacto peptone, 1 g yeast extract, 1x salt solution (5x salt solution: 2.58 M NaCl, 0.125 M MgCl2, 0.125 M MgSO4, 0.1 M KCl) per litre.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 0 9 0 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. algae </italic>
###xml 126 129 120 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D,L</italic>
###xml 241 242 235 236 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 0 8 <span type="species:ncbi:38313">S. algae</span>
S. algae was also cultivated under anaerobic conditions without shaking at 30degreesC in LB medium supplemented with 20 mM Na-D,L-lactate (Fluka) and 10 mM or 20 mM ferric citrate (Sigma). Oxygen was removed from the medium by purging with N2. The bacterium was inoculated from an aerobic LB plate into anaerobic liquid medium (first transfer). After the third transfer growth was monitored and culture supernatants were collected for AI-2 determination.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 0 11 0 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 21 32 21 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 100 102 100 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 163 164 163 164 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 0 10 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
###xml 21 31 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
V. harveyi BB152 and V. harveyi BB170 were cultivated in modified autoinducer bioassay (AB) medium [37]. The AB basic solution contained 0.3 M NaCl and 0.05 M MgSO4, and was autoclaved at 121degreesC for 20 min. After cooling down, the following components were added from sterile stocks: 20 ml casamino acids (10%), 20 ml glycerol (50%), 10 ml potassium phosphate buffer (1 M, pH = 7) and 10 ml L-arginine (0.1 M).
###end p 51
###begin p 52
Growth was monitored by measuring optical density (OD) at 600 nm in a Ultraspec Plus Spectrophotometer (Pharmacia).
###end p 52
###begin title 53
###xml 26 31 26 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 35 40 35 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
Design of primers for the luxS and sahH genes
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 75 80 75 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 84 89 84 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
Degenerate primers were designed targeting highly conserved regions of the luxS and sahH gene, respectively, based on multiple alignments (ClustalW) of LuxS and SahH amino acid sequences. The alignment of the corresponding nucleic acid sequences was used to decide on a nucleotide in the primer sequence in case of degeneracy of the code.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 10 15 10 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 132 134 132 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 196 197 196 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 833 838 833 838 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 894 899 894 899 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 1004 1005 1004 1005 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Since the luxS gene is only moderately conserved, the consensus-degenerate hybrid oligonucleotide primer design strategy (CoDeHOP) [38] was chosen for primers LuxS_degfor3 and LuxS_degrev4 (Table 1). According to this approach the primers are composed of two parts: a highly degenerate 5' end and a specific 3' end. The function of the degenerate 5'end is to bind to all possible templates in the first PCR cycles. Therefore, it targeted a region of 3-4 highly conserved amino acids, and covered all possible variations present in the nucleic acid sequence alignment. The role of the specific 3'end is to stabilize the binding of the primers in the later PCR cycles. Its sequence was identical to the most frequent nucleic acid sequence in the alignment and was about 20 bp long. At the time of primer design (July 2004) 24 complete luxS sequences of Gram negative bacteria were available. The luxS gene is about 500 bp long; and the primer pair LuxS_degfor3 and LuxS_degrev4 amplified ca. 400 bp (Table 1).
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 81 86 81 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 94 114 94 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gammaproteobacteria </italic>
###xml 311 316 311 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
In addition, a second, more specific, primer set was designed especially for the luxS gene in Gammaproteobacteria without using the CoDeHOP approach. The LuxS_gammafor1 primer targeted the same region as LuxS_degfor3. The LuxS_gammarev2 primer targeted the highly conserved mGC*TG*y region in the middle of the luxS gene. A fragment of about 250 bp was amplified with this primer set.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 4 9 4 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 59 64 59 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 222 227 222 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 332 333 332 333 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
The sahH gene is highly conserved and much longer than the luxS gene (about 1400 bp). Therefore, longer conserved amino acid sequences could be identified and it was not necessary to use the CoDeHOP strategy. 60 bacterial sahH sequences were used for the alignment and a universal primer set (SahH_degfor and SahH_degrev; see Table 1) was designed which worked from 40degreesC till 60degreesC and amplified about 470 bp.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 8 27 8 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 63 68 63 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 137 138 137 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 237 242 237 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 251 270 251 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria</italic>
###xml 428 430 428 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 496 501 496 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 521 541 521 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alphaproteobacteria </italic>
In some Alphaproteobacteria, these primers did not amplify the sahH gene. Therefore primers specific for this group were designed (Table 1). Primer SahH_alphafor targeted the AETEMPGL region that is completely identical in all sequenced sahH genes in Alphaproteobacteria. The primer SahH_alpharev targeted the G*G*VGKGS conserved region around the middle of the gene which is part of the NAD binding domain reported previously [39]. This primer set amplified about 700 bp and was able to amplify sahH genes in additional Alphaproteobacteria at an annealing temperature of 60degreesC.
###end p 58
###begin title 59
Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) and Sequencing
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 105 106 105 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 173 175 173 175 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 192 196 192 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Taq </italic>
###xml 551 552 535 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 595 600 573 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luxS </italic>
###xml 620 625 598 603 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sahH </italic>
###xml 746 747 718 719 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
DNA was isolated using the Nucleospin Tissue kit (Macherey & Nagel). The PCR primers are listed in Table 1. The PCR reaction mix contained: 1x PCR buffer (Qiagen); 3 mM MgCl2 (Qiagen); 1.25 U Taq polymerase (Qiagen); 1 muM forward and reverse primer each (Gibco); 200 muM dNTPs (Fermentas); 1 mul DNA and MilliQ water (Fluka) up to an end volume of 50 mul. The PCR protocol was as follows: Predenaturation at 94degreesC for 1 min, followed by 30 cycles of denaturation at 94degreesC for 10 sec, annealing for 30 sec at the temperatures given in Table 1, and elongation at 68degreesC for 40 sec (luxS gene) resp. 50 sec (sahH gene); finally postelongation was carried out at 68degreesC for 1 min 30 sec. The annealing temperatures listed in Table 1 were determined by PCR with a temperature gradient from 40 to 60degreesC. All of the PCRs were done in an Eppendorf Mastercycler.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
The PCR products were visualised on 1.5% agarose gels (agarose NEEO ultra-quality, Roth). Bands that were chosen for sequencing were excised from the gel and purified using the Qiagen Gel Extraction Kit. Excised bands were sequenced using the BigDye Terminator v3.1 Cycle Sequencing Kit on an ABI 3730 x l DNA analyser.
###end p 61
###begin title 62
###xml 32 47 32 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio harveyi </italic>
###xml 32 46 <span type="species:ncbi:669">Vibrio harveyi</span>
Determination of AI-2 using the Vibrio harveyi bioassay
###end title 62
###begin p 63
###xml 654 656 640 642 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 855 866 841 852 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 855 865 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
AI-2 activity was always determined throughout the complete growth curve for at least 24 h. Samples were taken every 30 - 60 min until stationary phase was reached, then larger sampling intervals were used. Sterile culture supernatants were prepared from 1 ml of culture fluid by centrifugation (13.000 rpm, 2 min) and filtration (0.22 mum) and were stored at -20degreesC until use. The assay was conducted in white micro titre plates (Nunc) which were prepared before the assay by pipetting 20 mul of samples, reference and control in at least four replicas into the wells. Plates were stored at -20degreesC until use. DPD was synthesized as described [40]. The concentration of DPD (M = 132 g/mol) was 0.7 mg/ml, e.g. ca. 5.3 mM. This solution was diluted 1:1000 in AB medium and served as a positive control. In some bioassays culture supernatant from V. harveyi BB152 served as a positive control.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 32 34 32 34 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 59 61 59 61 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 91 102 91 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. harveyi </italic>
###xml 310 316 304 310 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600nm </sub>
###xml 91 101 <span type="species:ncbi:669">V. harveyi</span>
The assay described by Bassler [41] was slightly modified [42]. Briefly, the sensor strain V. harveyi BB170 was inoculated from a freshly prepared AB plate with a well luminescent culture into 20 ml AB liquid medium in an Erlenmeyer flask with baffles and incubated (160 rpm, 30degreesC) until it reached an OD600nm = 1.0 and showed strong luminescence in the dark room. At this growth stage the sensor strain was diluted 1:5000 in fresh AB medium. 180 mul of the diluted culture were immediately added to the previously prepared, warmed-up micro titre plates with the samples. Plates were incubated at 30degreesC with shaking at 600 rpm on a Heidolph Titramax -1000 micro titre plate shaker and luminescence was measured every hour for up to 8 hours in a Wallac Victor 1420 Multilabel Counter (Perkin Elmer). AI-2 activity was calculated as the fold change between luminescence of the sample and luminescence of the reference. The reference for the positive control was AB medium, and the reference for the test samples was the growth medium in which the respective test strain had been cultivated. The percentage of AI-2 activity was calculated as the fold induction of test sample divided by that of the positive control.
###end p 64
