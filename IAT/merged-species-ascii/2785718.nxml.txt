###begin article-title 0
###xml 72 78 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Structure/function studies of dogfish alpha-crystallin, comparison with bovine alpha-crystallin
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
All authors contributed equally to the work
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Purpose
###end title 2
###begin p 3
alpha-Crystallin is the major protein of the mammalian lens where it contributes to the refractive properties needed for vision and possibly to the stability of the tissue. The aim of this study was to determine whether the properties of alpha-crystallin have changed during the course of evolution.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 94 100 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Dogfish alpha-crystallin, which appeared over 420 million years ago, has been contrasted with bovine alpha-crystallin, which emerged around 160 million years later, by comparing their sizes, the microenvironments of their cysteine and tryptophan residues, their chaperone-like activities and the flexibility of their COOH-terminal extensions.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 609 610 586 587 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">L</sub>
###xml 739 740 716 717 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sup>
###xml 147 153 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 533 539 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Dogfish alpha-crystallin consists of alphaA- and alphaB-polypeptides, in a 1:5 ratio, and has a molecular mass of around 400 kDa. By contrast, the bovine protein is around 600-800 kDa in mass and has a 3:1 subunit ratio. Cysteine residues in the proteins were equally accessible to reaction with 5,5'-dithiobis-(2-nitrobenzoic acid). Quenching of fluorescence with acrylamide indicated tryptophan residues in the two proteins were in similar environments. The chaperone activity of dogfish alpha-crystallin was comparable to that of bovine alpha-crystallin in preventing the heat-induced precipitation of betaL-crystallin but the dogfish protein was three times more effective at preventing insulin precipitation after reduction at 37  C. 1H nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopic studies showed that the last 17 amino acids of the dogfish alphaB polypeptide (V162-K178) have great conformational flexibility, are highly exposed to solvent and adopt little ordered conformation. This is comparable to, but slightly longer in length, than the COOH-terminal extension observed in mammalian alpha-crystallins.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusions
###end title 8
###begin p 9
The structure and properties of alpha-crystallin have changed relatively little during the evolutionary period from the emergence of sharks and mammals.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Introduction
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 80 81 76 77 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 386 387 374 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 388 389 376 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 213 219 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 376 384 <span type="species:ncbi:9322">kangaroo</span>
alpha-Crystallin is the major protein component (up to 50%) of most eye lenses [1]. It is generally isolated as a polydisperse population of hetero-oligomers, with most averaging 600-800 kDa in mass for the young bovine protein and consisting of two polypeptides, alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin. These are present in molar ratios varying from 1:5 in the dogfish to 10:1 in the kangaroo [1-3] (unpublished observations). The sequences of the two polypeptides are closely related and have changed relatively little during evolution.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 76 77 72 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 212 213 208 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 304 305 300 301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 439 440 431 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 541 542 533 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 738 739 726 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 761 767 749 755 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cloche</italic>
###xml 898 899 878 879 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 751 760 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
The alpha-crystallins belong to the small heat-shock protein (sHsp) family [4] and, under stress conditions, can act as molecular chaperones by preventing the precipitation of partially unfolded target proteins [5] via the formation of a stable, soluble, high molecular-mass sHsp-target protein complex [6]. Unlike other molecular chaperones that are involved in protein folding, alpha-crystallin has no ability to refold target proteins [6] although it has been reported that it can enhance the recovery of activity from denatured enzymes [7]. It has been proposed that the chaperone activity of alpha-crystallin, i.e. its ability to prevent crystallin protein aggregation, may be important in maintaining an optically transparent lens [5]. Thus, in zebrafish cloche mutants, over-expression of alphaA-crystallin prevents cataract formation arising from the insolubilization of gamma-crystallins [8].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 413 414 405 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 575 577 567 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 1202 1204 1182 1184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 1205 1207 1185 1187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 94 99 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 104 110 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 1090 1099 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Most studies on the structure and properties of alpha-crystallin have been conducted with the human and bovine proteins. Comparisons of the mammalian proteins with evolutionary older proteins could yield valuable information on structure/function relationships in the sHsp family. The amino acid sequences of dogfish alpha-crystallin polypeptides display around 67% homology with those of the mammalian proteins [9]. It is therefore probable that they have homologous structural domains and comparable biological properties and functions. However, as with thermal stability [10], there may be differences in some properties necessary for the ectothermic dogfish proteins to exist at low and variable temperatures, very different conditions from their endothermic mammalian counterparts. Recent studies on other fish alpha-crystallins have revealed significant differences in the thermal stabilities of these proteins from endothermic and ectothermic species. The structure and function of the alphaA-crystallin appear to have been conserved but an alphaB-crystallin gene duplication in the zebrafish has led to a possible divergence of the structural and functional properties in the two polypeptides [11-14].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 203 204 195 196 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sup>
###xml 142 148 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 497 503 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
In order to examine whether the properties of alpha-crystallin may have changed during the course of evolution, the structures of dogfish and bovine alpha-crystallin have been compared by acquisition of 1H nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectra and by assessing tryptophan and cysteine accessibilities. In addition, the ability of the proteins to act as molecular chaperones in suppressing the aggregation of stressed target proteins was compared. Our observations indicate that the dogfish and bovine proteins have similar structural properties and that there has been little change in alpha-crystallin since the divergence of the fish and mammalian lines.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Methods
###end title 15
###begin title 16
Isolation of protein
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 258 260 251 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 12 18 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 41 47 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Dogfish and bovine alpha-crystallins and bovine beta-crystallin were prepared by gel filtration of lens extracts, at room temperature, on Sephacryl S300 in phosphate buffered saline containing sodium azide (PBSA) at pH 7.2, as described by Augusteyn et al. [15]. Only the central 50% of the alpha-crystallin peak was retained and, after concentrating, was rechromatographed under the same conditions. The process was repeated in order to remove any remaining traces of the adjacent high molecular weight (HMW) proteins and beta-crystallin peaks. SDS PAGE was used to check that the preparations contained only alpha-crystallin polypeptides.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Protein size analysis
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 112 114 112 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
The size of the proteins was examined by sedimentation velocity analysis as described by Thomson and Augusteyn [16] and by comparison with known proteins on gel permeation chromatography.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Amino acid microenvironments
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 165 167 165 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
The reaction of cysteine residues with 5'-dithio-bis (2-nitrobenzoic acid; DTNB) under pseudo first order conditions was performed as described by Augusteyn et al. [17].
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 131 133 131 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
Accessibility of tryptophan to the solvent was examined by quenching of its fluorescence with acrylamide, as described previously [18].
###end p 22
###begin title 23
###xml 0 1 0 1 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sup>
1H NMR spectroscopy
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 124 125 120 121 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 139 140 135 136 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 197 198 193 194 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 256 258 252 254 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3,</sub>
Dogfish alpha-crystallin was concentrated by ultrafiltration and dialyzed against 11 mM phosphate buffer, pH 6.5. 0.033% NaN3. Buffer and D2O were then added to generate a solution containing 10% D2O and 20mg/ml protein in 10 mM phosphate buffer, 0.03% NaN3, pH 6.5.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 16 17 16 17 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sup>
###xml 213 215 203 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 224 225 214 215 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sup>
###xml 293 294 283 284 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sup>
###xml 296 297 286 287 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sup>
###xml 349 351 339 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 440 442 430 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 571 573 557 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 642 643 628 629 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 691 692 677 678 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
Two-dimensional 1H NMR experiments on dogfish alpha-crystallin were conducted at 500 MHz on a Varian Inova 500 NMR spectrometer (Varian Pty Ltd, Palo Alto, CA) at 25 degreesC. The sequential assignment procedure [19] of the 1H NMR resonances was undertaken using through-bond correlations via 1H-1H WATERGATE Total Correlation spectroscopy (TOCSY) [20] and through-space correlations via WET Nuclear Overhauser Effect Spectroscopy (NOESY) [21]. A spin-lock period of 65 ms was employed in the TOCSY experiment with dogfish alpha-crystallin as described by Carver et al. [22]. In TOCSY experiments, 32 scans were acquired for each of the 256 t1 increments. The free induction decay (FID) in t2 consisted of 4,096 data points over a spectral width of 6,000 Hz. A mixing time of 100 ms was used in the NOESY spectra with 80 scans for each increment. Other parameters were the same as those for the TOCSY experiments. Prior to the Fourier transformation, all spectra were processed using a shifted Gaussian function in both dimensions. The processing of NMR spectra was performed on a Sun Microsystems Sunblade 150 workstation (Sun Microsystems, San Francisco, CA) with Varian NMR software.
###end p 25
###begin title 26
Chaperone activity
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 152 154 148 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r23">23</xref>
###xml 36 42 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
The chaperone action of dogfish and bovine alpha-crystallins was assessed with two assays using different stresses, as described by Farahbakhsh et al. [23]. Assays were performed in duplicate.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
3rd-Insulin reduction assay
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 72 73 72 73 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 256 257 252 253 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">o</sup>
###xml 149 155 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Insulin at 0.43 mg/ml in 50 mM sodium phosphate, pH 7.4, 0.05% (w/v) NaN3, was reduced with 20 mM dithiothreitol (DTT) in the presence of dogfish or bovine alpha-crystallin at various concentrations. The subsequent aggregation of the insulin B chain at 37 oC was monitored as an increase in light scattered at 360 nm using a Spectramax 250 multiwell plate reader spectrophotometer (Molecular Devices, Sunnyvale, CA) with temperature control.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
3rd-Heat-stress assay
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 11 12 8 9 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">L</sub>
###xml 86 88 83 85 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3,</sub>
###xml 109 116 106 107 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#176;</sup>
###xml 0 6 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">Bovine</span>
###xml 148 154 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Bovine betaL-crystallin at 0.3 mg/ml in 50 mM sodium phosphate pH 7.4, 0.05% (w/v) NaN3, was incubated at 60 degreesC in the presence of dogfish or bovine alpha-crystallin at various concentrations. The time-dependent aggregation of the protein was monitored by measuring light scattered at 360 nm using a Cary 500 Scan UV-VIS-NIR spectrophotometer (Varian Inc, Palo Alto, CA).

###begin title 33
Protein purification
###end title 33
###begin p 34

###xml 1222 1223 1194 1195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>

The size-exclusion elution profile for dogfish lens extract is shown in Figure 1. SDS-PAGE indicated that the peak eluting at 490 ml contained alpha-crystallin. It represented around 20%, by weight, of the soluble proteins. Use of Sephacryl S-300 column media, only utilizing the central portion of the alpha-crystallin peak, and further rechromatography of this portion, again taking only the central 50%, ensured that the isolated protein contained only alpha-crystallin. Under the same conditions, bovine alpha-crystallin eluted at around 450 ml, indicative of a smaller Stoke's radius for the dogfish protein. By comparison with proteins of known size, it was estimated that the dogfish protein aggregate was around 400 kDa in mass and the bovine protein, approximately 600 kDa in mass. Sedimentation velocity analysis indicated that dogfish and bovine alpha-crystallins sedimented at 14.0 and 19.2 S, respectively. These data also imply that the dogfish protein aggregate is smaller in mass than the bovine protein. SDS-PAGE (not shown) revealed that the dogfish protein consisted of two subunits, alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin, in a ratio of approximately 1:5. This is similar to the observations of de Jong et al. [3].

Analysis of the reaction of the alpha-crystallins with DTNB provided information on the accessibility of cysteine residues. The time courses for the reaction of bovine and dogfish proteins are presented in Figure 2A. The maximum SH content of the proteins, determined in the presence of 0.1% SDS, was 0.66 and 1.22 moles/mole for the bovine and dogfish proteins, respectively. First order transformation of these data (Figure 2B,C) indicated there were two classes of SH in each protein. In the bovine protein, 0.6 moles/mole subunit were in a slowly reacting, buried class (k=0.0034 min-1) and 0.06 in a more rapidly reacting, exposed class, (k=0.21 min-1) in agreement with a previous report [17]. In dogfish alpha-crystallin (Figure 2B), 1.01 moles SH/mole were in a slowly reacting class (k =0.0048 min-1) and 0.12 moles/mole were in a more rapidly reacting, exposed class (k =0.26 min-1). The small amount (0.08 moles/mole) of very rapidly reacting (k = 4 min-1) thiol was probably residual DTT which had not been completely removed during dialysis of the protein.

###xml 873 876 861 864 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dyn</sub>
###xml 964 967 952 955 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dyn</sub>
###xml 232 238 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 562 568 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Both proteins yield SV plots with upwards curvature, indicative of static quenching. Correction for this, as described previously [18], reveals that tryptophan fluorescence is more easily quenched in the dogfish protein than in the bovine protein. This can be attributed to the dogfish alphaB-crystallin polypeptide which contains three tryptophan residues (at positions 9, 49, and 63) and accounts for >80% of the total protein. The alphaA-crystallin chain contains tryptophan at positions 9 and 154. Comparison with the previously described accessibilities in bovine alphaB-crystallin [18], which contains tryptophan residues equivalent to those in positions 9 and 63 of the dogfish protein, permitted estimation of the quenching constant (Kdyn, 2.6 M-1) and quantum yield (0.18) for W49 and leads to the conclusion that this residue is in a similar environment to W63 (Kdyn=2.6; quantum yield=0.18), i.e. more accessible to the solvent and acrylamide than W9 (Kdyn=2.11: quantum yield=0.14). It was not possible to identify fluorescence originating from W154 in the alphaA chain because of its low abundance (<6% of total fluorescence).

###end title 48
###begin p 49

###xml 185 187 181 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 188 190 184 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
###xml 191 193 187 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 425 426 417 418 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sup>
###xml 696 697 688 689 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sup>
Two-dimensional (2D) 1H-1H NMR spectra were acquired on dogfish alpha-crystallin to determine if the protein contained a flexible COOH-terminal extension as is observed in other sHsps [22,24,25]. Since dogfish alpha-crystallin exists as large heterogeneous aggregates, it would be expected to give rise to poorly-resolved, broad resonances in the NMR spectrum due to its long tumbling time. However, the one-dimensional (1D) 1H NMR spectrum (not shown) exhibited a series of well-resolved resonances superimposed on a broad component, implying that the protein had a region or regions of flexibility present, which may arise from the extreme COOH-terminus. No differences were observed in the 1D 1H NMR spectra of before and after 2D data acquisition, indicating that the conformation of the protein was stable for the duration of the NMR experiments (24 h at 25 degreesC).
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 171 180 167 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6A</xref>
###xml 483 485 467 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 808 811 792 795 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i+1</sub>
###xml 823 824 803 804 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 1177 1178 1153 1154 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1236 1238 1208 1210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
Intra-residue cross-peaks from the NH protons in the 2D TOCSY spectra, arising from residues in the extreme COOH-terminal region of dogfish alphaB-crystallin are shown in Figure 6A. Very few cross-peaks from the alphaA-crystallin subunit were observed in the spectra due to its low concentration relative to the alphaB-crystallin subunit in dogfish alpha-crystallin. The identification of the resolved resonances in the spectrum was achieved via the sequential assignment procedure [19] utilizing through-bond, intra-residue TOCSY spectra and through-space, inter-residue connectivities via NOESY spectra. From these spectra, it was apparent that the well-resolved resonances arise from a 17-residue flexible COOH-terminal extension encompassing V162 to K178. The resonances gave rise mainly to sequential NHi+1 to alpha-CHi cross-peaks in the NOESY spectrum which is consistent with the COOH-terminal extension adopting an extended conformation. Proline residues only give cross peaks in the aliphatic region of the spectrum since they have no NH proton. Thus, for the three resolved proline amino acid resonances, sequential assignment was achieved via nOes from the delta-CH2 proline resonances to the preceding alpha-CH amino acid [19].

The alpha-CH chemical shift of an amino acid in a protein is very sensitive to its local secondary structure [26,27]. ###begin title 56
Discussion
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 145 147 145 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
###xml 539 540 527 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 541 543 529 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
###xml 1057 1059 1039 1041 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r30">30</xref>
###xml 4 17 <span type="species:ncbi:7797">spiny dogfish</span>
###xml 964 970 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
The spiny dogfish is very old in evolutionary terms, having arisen 420-430 million years ago, about 160 million years before the mammalian line [28]. The bony fish diverged around 30 million years later. Thus, the genes (paralogs) for the dogfish alpha-crystallins and those which ultimately gave rise to the modern mammalian proteins evolved independently for around 400 million years. This is reflected in the amino acid sequences, which show dogfish alphaA- and alphaB-crystallins differ by about 33% from their mammalian counterparts [9,29]. Given the variation in their amino acid sequences, it is possible that dogfish and mammalian proteins would exhibit differences in their structures and functions. Furthermore, the dogfish and mammalian proteins exist and function in very different environments. The ectothermic dogfish is found in temperate waters of 10-18 degreesC. Its lens is very hard and contains >60% protein. Most mammalian lenses, such as the bovine used in the present work, are relatively soft, with protein concentrations of 30-40% [30] and are maintained at a constant 36-37 degreesC. The ready availability of dogfish lenses provided an opportunity to determine whether the structure and function of alpha-crystallins have changed during evolution and/or as a result of adaptation to different environments.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 120 121 109 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 122 124 111 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 125 127 114 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 649 653 634 638 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">20,w</sub>
###xml 776 777 757 758 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 823 827 800 804 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">20,w</sub>
###xml 847 849 824 826 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 1196 1197 1165 1166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 1198 1200 1167 1169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r31">31</xref>
###xml 1370 1372 1335 1337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 685 691 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 742 746 <span type="species:ncbi:7962">carp</span>
###xml 795 802 <span type="species:ncbi:71179">catfish</span>
###xml 1075 1081 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 1130 1136 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 1261 1267 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
In previous studies on fish alpha-crystallins, difficulties were encountered in separating alpha- and beta-crystallins [2,11,13]. This was not the case in the present work and may be related to the chromatography medium (S300) and the buffer (PBSA) used, or that the separations were performed at room temperature. Each of these factors might affect the interactions between the crystallins and influence their chromatographic separation. Furthermore, it should be noted that the protein used in this work had been purified by twice rechromatographing the central portion of the peaks. Dogfish alpha-crystallin was isolated as a smaller aggregate (S20,w=14 S;~400kDa in mass) than the bovine protein (~600kDa) but was very similar in size to carp alpha-crystallin (~410 kDa) [2] and recombinant catfish alphaB-crystallin (S20,w=14.5 S; ~450 kDa) [14]. It is not known what the size of the protein is in the intact lens. However, previous studies have shown that aggregate size has no effect on amino acid microenvironments, immunological reactivity, or chaperone activity in bovine alpha-crystallins and it is possible to isolate bovine alpha-crystallins of sizes varying from 160 kDa to >2 MDa [1,31]. Furthermore, construction of heteropolymers from purified bovine subunits yields lower molecular mass aggregates when the proportion of alphaB-crystallin exceeds 50% [16]. Thus, the observed smaller size of dogfish alpha-crystallin may reflect its significantly higher alphaB-crystallin subunit content.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 319 321 311 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
###xml 921 923 893 895 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 147 153 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 208 214 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 387 393 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 887 893 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Probing of the microenvironments of cysteine and tryptophan residues revealed similarities between the proteins. Both dogfish polypeptides and the bovine alphaA chain have a cysteine residue in position 132 (bovine numbering). Dogfish alphaA-crystallin chains have an additional two cysteines at positions 142 and 143 [29]. Based on the alphaA/alphaB-crystallin ratios of 3:1 and 1:5 in bovine and dogfish alpha-crystallins, respectively, averages of 0.66 and 1.32 SH/mole subunit may be calculated for the two proteins, in good agreement with the observed values of 0.66 and 1.22 SH/mole of subunit as determined from the reaction with DTNB. From the kinetic analysis of the SH reactivities, it can be concluded that Cys-132 is in a buried location in the dogfish subunits in the conserved alpha-crystallin domain, shielded by the COOH-terminus, as has been previously reported for the bovine alphaA-crystallin subunit [17]. The two additional cysteines in the dogfish alphaA-crystallin chain react more rapidly, indicative of groups exposed on the surface of the protein. These residues are near the invariant Pro-145, which marks the end of the alpha-crystallin domain and the beginning of the largely unstructured COOH-terminus of the protein. Our NMR observations indicate that much of this region is highly exposed to the solvent.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 127 129 127 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 251 252 247 248 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 324 326 320 322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r32">32</xref>
###xml 400 406 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
All of the proteins have a tryptophan in position 9. It has been shown previously that this residue is in a shielded position [18]. The tryptophans at positions 49 and 63 of the dogfish alphaB-crystallin are within the second sequence repeat of the NH2-terminal domain, which is responsible for the aggregation of subunits [32]. They are more accessible to quenchers than W9, as is residue 63 in the bovine alphaB-crystallin polypeptide, but are still well shielded from the solvent.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 371 378 360 361 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#176;</sup>
###xml 497 498 473 474 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">L</sub>
###xml 613 620 579 580 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#176;</sup>
###xml 667 669 627 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
###xml 670 672 630 632 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r36">36</xref>
###xml 861 868 817 818 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#176;</sup>
###xml 29 35 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 290 296 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 800 806 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
The similarities between the bovine and dogfish proteins extend to the chaperone activities. Dogfish alpha-crystallin is also able to prevent the precipitation of reduced insulin and heat-denatured beta-crystallins. However, the dogfish protein appeared to be 2-3 times more effective than bovine alpha-crystallin in preventing the precipitation of reduced insulin at 37 degreesC. Dogfish alpha-crystallin was slightly less effective in its ability to suppress precipitation of heat denatured betaL-crystallin at 60 degreesC. This may be related to the structural change that alpha-crystallin undergoes around 45 degreesC leading to alterations in chaperone ability [33-36] and to the instability of the dogfish protein at elevated temperatures. The variation in the chaperone ability of dogfish and bovine alpha-crystallin in the insulin reduction assay at 37 degreesC does not arise from differences in the flexibility of the COOH-terminal flexibility of the two proteins or from alterations in the environment of cysteine and tryptophan residues. Instead, this difference must be ascribed to subtle structural alterations between the two proteins.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 461 463 449 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r37">37</xref>
###xml 464 466 452 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r38">38</xref>
###xml 1374 1376 1334 1336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 1454 1455 1406 1407 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1535 1537 1483 1485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 367 372 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 719 723 <span type="species:ncbi:8233">tuna</span>
###xml 935 939 <span type="species:ncbi:8233">tuna</span>
###xml 1066 1075 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">Zebrafish</span>
###xml 1154 1163 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1180 1185 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1219 1226 <span type="species:ncbi:71179">catfish</span>
###xml 1411 1420 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1510 1515 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
It is difficult to compare the chaperone ability of the dogfish protein with that of other ectothermic alpha-crystallins because of the wide variety of target proteins, stresses and solvent conditions employed in the assays, as well as the endothermic alpha-crystallins that have been used for comparisons. It has been noted that the chaperone ability of recombinant human alphaB-crystallin is highly dependent on the solvent conditions and the type of stress [37,38]. As a result of these factors, there is no obvious pattern to the chaperone properties of the fish crystallins, i.e. there are substantial differences between the ectothermic alpha-crystallins, as well as between their isolated subunits. For example, tuna and toothfish alpha-crystallins are equally effective in preventing the precipitation of the (same species of) heated gamma-crystallins. However, the toothfish protein is about two times more effective than the tuna protein in preventing the precipitation of reduced lysozyme. Differences are also apparent in the activities of the subunits. Zebrafish alphaB1-crystallin is less stable and a very poor chaperone compared with the zebrafish alphaA- and the human alphaB-crystallins. By contrast, catfish alphaB-crystallin is more stable and is better able to suppress precipitation of heated target proteins than even the endothermic porcine protein [14]. However, there is also a second zebrafish alphaB-crystallin subunit (alphaB2), which is about two times better as a chaperone than human alphaB-crystallin [11]. Dogfish alpha-crystallin appears to lie in between the two extremes.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 214 216 210 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r39">39</xref>
###xml 699 701 679 681 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 722 724 702 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r40">40</xref>
###xml 997 999 977 979 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
###xml 93 99 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 275 278 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cow</span>
###xml 280 285 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 287 293 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 295 298 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 304 317 <span type="species:ncbi:7797">spiny dogfish</span>
###xml 363 369 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
It would appear from our various observations, presented in this paper, that the dogfish and bovine alpha-crystallins have similar subunit structures. This conclusion is supported by previous reports. Puri et al. [39] found that alpha-crystallins from many vertebrates, e.g. cow, horse, rabbit, rat, and spiny dogfish, are recognized by antibodies raised against bovine alpha-crystallin. They concluded that the regions of the alpha-crystallin protein responsible for antibody binding have been conserved and did not change during evolution. This would be consistent with the observation that dogfish alpha-crystallin polypeptides can form hetero-oligomers with the polypeptides from other species [16]. Leunissen et al. [40] proposed that eye lens crystallins have avoided extreme changes in their charge during evolution. In the lens environment, this could be necessary since changes in the distribution of charged amino acid residues could affect the correct close packing of the crystallins [29].
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 231 239 227 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f7">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 402 404 386 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
###xml 405 407 389 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 483 485 467 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r41">41</xref>
###xml 486 488 470 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r45">45</xref>
###xml 1016 1024 976 984 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f7">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 1088 1112 1048 1072 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Methanococcus jannaschii</italic>
###xml 1122 1124 1082 1084 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r46">46</xref>
###xml 705 710 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 745 751 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 1070 1075 <span type="species:ncbi:4565">wheat</span>
###xml 1088 1112 <span type="species:ncbi:2190">Methanococcus jannaschii</span>
Our NMR observations revealed that the last 17 amino acids of the B subunit of dogfish alpha-crystallin (V162-K178) have great conformational flexibility and are highly exposed to the solvent and adopt little ordered conformation (Figure 7). Dogfish alphaB-crystallin is therefore like the mammalian sHsps (alphaA- and alphaB-crystallins, Hsp25 and Hsp20) in having a flexible COOH-terminal extension [24,25]. This region is important in the chaperone action of all mammalian sHsps [41-45]. Removal or alterations of amino acids in this region lead to variations in chaperone activity. Based on sequence alignment, the COOH-terminal extension in dogfish alpha-crystallin correlates with that observed for human alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin. In bovine alphaB-crystallin, the NMR-observable, flexible extension encompasses the last 12 amino acids whereas it is five amino acids longer in dogfish alphaB-crystallin and includes the highly conserved IXI/V motif in sHsps (V162, P163, I164 in dogfish alphaB-crystallin, Figure 7). The X-ray crystal structures of the sHsps, wheat Hsp16.9 and Methanococcus jannaschii Hsp16.5 [46], reveal that the IXI/V motif is important in regulating, via subunit-subunit interactions, the oligomeric structure of sHsps.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 212 220 204 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f7">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 518 520 498 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r45">45</xref>
###xml 521 523 501 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r47">47</xref>
###xml 32 38 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 52 57 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 447 452 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The COOH-terminal extensions in bovine, dogfish and human alphaB-crystallins exhibit significant sequence conservation, far more than for the alphaA-crystallins from these three species, particularly after K169 (Figure 7). The two aspartic/glutamic acid residues, at positions 167 and 168 in dogfish, are entirely conserved in all alphaA- and alphaB-crystallins. This is consistent with their importance in the stability and chaperone function of human alphaB-crystallin and the closely related mammalian sHsp, Hsp25 [45,47].
###end p 65
###begin p 66
Thus, the NMR and other observations presented here indicate that dogfish and mammalian alpha-crystallins adopt a similar structure, which has been maintained over 400 million years of evolution. In particular, a highly flexible COOH-terminal extension is a characteristic feature of heterogeneous sHsps.
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 200 202 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
We conclude that there has been little or no change in the properties of alpha-crystallin during the accessible evolutionary period. Given the slow rate of amino acid substitution in the sHsp family [29], perhaps, this is not surprising.
