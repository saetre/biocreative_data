Plastid chaperonin proteins Cpn60alpha and Cpn60beta are required for plastid division in Arabidopsis thaliana
###end article-title 0

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 772 792 772 792 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana</italic>
###xml 772 792 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
Plastids arose from a free-living cyanobacterial endosymbiont and multiply by binary division as do cyanobacteria. Plastid division involves nucleus-encoded homologs of cyanobacterial division proteins such as FtsZ, MinD, MinE, and ARC6. However, homologs of many other cyanobacterial division genes are missing in plant genomes and proteins of host eukaryotic origin, such as a dynamin-related protein, PDV1 and PDV2 are involved in the division process. Recent identification of plastid division proteins has started to elucidate the similarities and differences between plastid division and cyanobacterial cell division. To further identify new proteins that are required for plastid division, we characterized previously and newly isolated plastid division mutants of Arabidopsis thaliana.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 27 32 27 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">br04 </italic>
###xml 36 40 36 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arc2</italic>
###xml 116 121 116 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ARC2 </italic>
###xml 125 130 125 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BR04 </italic>
###xml 405 417 391 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946; </italic>
###xml 571 583 554 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana </italic>
###xml 719 732 695 704 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptCpn60&#945; </italic>
###xml 571 582 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
Leaf cells of two mutants, br04 and arc2, contain fewer, larger chloroplasts than those of wild type. We found that ARC2 and BR04 are identical to nuclear genes encoding the plastid chaperonin 60alpha (ptCpn60alpha) and chaperonin 60beta (ptCpn60beta) proteins, respectively. In both mutants, plastid division FtsZ ring formation was partially perturbed though the level of FtsZ2-1 protein in plastids of ptcpn60beta mutants was similar to that in wild type. Phylogenetic analyses showed that both ptCpn60 proteins are derived from ancestral cyanobacterial proteins. The A. thaliana genome encodes two members of ptCpn60alpha family and four members of ptCpn60beta family respectively. We found that a null mutation in ptCpn60alpha abolished greening of plastids and resulted in an albino phenotype while a weaker mutation impairs plastid division and reduced chlorophyll levels. The functions of at least two ptCpn60beta proteins are redundant and the appearance of chloroplast division defects is dependent on the number of mutant alleles.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Our results suggest that both ptCpn60alpha and ptCpn60beta are required for the formation of a normal plastid division apparatus, as the prokaryotic counterparts are required for assembly of the cell division apparatus. Since moderate reduction of ptCpn60 levels impaired normal FtsZ ring formation but not import of FtsZ into plastids, it is suggested that the proper levels of ptCpn60 are required for folding of stromal plastid division proteins and/or regulation of FtsZ polymer dynamics.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 417 418 417 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 419 420 419 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 485 493 485 493 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo </italic>
###xml 682 683 682 683 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 684 685 684 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
All plastids trace their origins to a primary endosymbiotic event in which a previously nonphotosynthetic protist engulfed and enslaved a cyanobacterium. Over time, most of the genes once present in the endosymbiont have been lost or transferred to the host nuclear genome; those nuclear-encoded proteins used by the plastid are translated by the host and targeted back into the organelle to express their functions [1,2]. Consistent with this scenario, plastids are never synthesized de novo and they cannot multiply independently. Their continuity is maintained by the division of preexisting plastids, which is performed and controlled by proteins encoded in the nuclear genome [3-6].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 83 95 83 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana </italic>
###xml 246 247 246 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 248 250 248 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 416 418 416 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 419 421 419 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 510 511 510 511 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 512 514 512 514 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 583 585 583 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 652 672 652 672 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Giant Chloroplast 1 </italic>
###xml 673 675 673 675 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 676 678 676 678 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 684 698 684 698 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Crumpled Leaf </italic>
###xml 699 701 699 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 83 94 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
Consistent with the endosymbiotic origin of plastids, molecular genetic studies in A. thaliana have defined several nucleus-encoded homologs of cyanobacterial cell division proteins that function in plastid division in photosynthetic eukaryotes [7-13]. Plastid division requires assembly of FtsZ1 and FtsZ2, homologs of the tubulin-like bacterial protein FtsZ, into a ring structure at the midplastid division site [14-16]. The FtsZ ring is localized to the midplastid through the activities of MinD and MinE [9-12] and is thought to be stabilized by the J-domain-like protein ARC6 [13]. Mutations in several other cyanobacteria-derived genes, such as Giant Chloroplast 1 [17,18] and Crumpled Leaf [19], also cause defects in plastid division, although their roles in the division process are still not known.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 108 110 108 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 111 113 111 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 476 478 476 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 660 662 660 662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 722 726 722 726 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arc </italic>
###xml 727 728 727 728 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">a</underline>
###xml 744 745 744 745 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">r</underline>
###xml 759 760 759 760 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</underline>
###xml 814 826 814 826 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana </italic>
###xml 827 829 827 829 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 814 825 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
Plant-specific proteins (dynamin-related GTPase protein, PDV1 and PDV2) also regulate chloroplast division [20-22]. Division involves the assembly and constriction of the endosymbiont-derived FtsZ ring on the stromal surface of the inner envelope membrane and the plant-specific dynamin ring on the cytosolic surface of the outer envelope membrane. This coordination is mediated by the outer envelope spanning proteins PDV1 and PDV2, and inner envelope spanning protein ARC6 [23]. As above, recent studies identified several additional components of the plastid division machinery. However, several other proteins that are involved in bacterial cell division [24] are not found in plants, and there are still unidentified arc (accumulation and replication of chloroplasts) loci that impair chloroplast division in A. thaliana [25], suggesting that there are still unidentified components of the plastid division machinery. In order to identify new plastid division proteins, we are using forward genetics approaches.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 177 188 170 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana</italic>
###xml 194 206 187 199 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana </italic>
###xml 689 691 666 668 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 692 694 669 671 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 177 188 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
###xml 194 205 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
By characterizing plastid division mutants, we found that the cyanobacteria-derived chaperonin proteins ptCpn60alpha and ptCpn60beta are required for proper plastid division in A. thaliana. The A. thaliana genome encodes several members of the ptCpn60alpha and ptCpn60beta families and our analyses suggest that at least two ptCpn60beta proteins have redundant functions. Moderate reduction of ptCpn60beta protein levels impaired plastid division while severe loss abolished greening of plastids, suggesting that the level of ptCpn60beta is important for proper plastid division. Since chaperonin proteins have been shown to be required for assembly of the division apparatus in bacteria [26,27], their activities in the division machinery are conserved between bacteria and plastids.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Results
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Mutations in ptCpn60alpha and ptCpn60beta impair plastid division
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 80 92 80 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana </italic>
###xml 118 120 118 120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 341 343 341 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 377 381 377 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">br04</italic>
###xml 455 469 455 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946;1-1</italic>
###xml 478 480 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 497 502 494 499 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">br04 </italic>
###xml 596 598 593 595 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 622 627 619 624 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">br04 </italic>
###xml 688 690 685 687 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 785 789 782 786 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">br04</italic>
###xml 859 864 856 861 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">br04 </italic>
###xml 80 91 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
In order to find new proteins required for plastid division, we screened 22,650 A. thaliana Activation Tagging Lines [28]. By microscopic observation of leaf cell chloroplasts, we found twenty-five mutant lines with chloroplasts that were significantly altered in number and size within single cells as compared with those in the wild type [29]. Among these mutants, one line, br04, which contained enlarged chloroplasts, was characterized in this study (ptcpn60beta1-1; Figure 1B). The growth of br04 was slightly slower than that of the wild type while the mutant plants were fertile. All the F1 progeny, after crossing br04 with wild type, displayed normal chloroplast morphology. In F2 progeny, the chloroplast-division phenotype segregated in approximately a 3:1 ratio (wild type:br04). These results indicated that the chloroplast-division phenotype of br04 is recessive and that the phenotype is caused by a mutation in a single genomic locus.

Because the T-DNA insertion in br04 did not co-segregate with the mutant phenotype in the F2 population, we identified the mutation by map-based cloning. br04 bears a single nucleotide insertion in At1g55490, which encodes a plastid chaperonin 60beta (ptCpn60beta1) (Figure 1G). The nucleotide insertion produced a premature stop codon in the second exon (Figure 1G). To confirm that the mutation in At1g55490 is linked to the chloroplast-division phenotype, we observed another T-DNA insertion mutant in the same gene (SAIL_852_B03; ptcpn60beta1-2; Figure 1G). The mutant displayed a chloroplast-division defect similar to that of br04 (Figure 1C). Because two independent mutant alleles of At1g55490 showed chloroplast division defects, we conclude that At1g55490 is identical to br04 and is required for plastid division.
###end p 17

###xml 245 250 238 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arc2 </italic>
###xml 328 330 321 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 340 342 333 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1E</xref>
###xml 356 360 349 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">br04</italic>

###xml 471 473 464 466 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1H</xref>
###xml 565 570 558 563 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arc2 </italic>
###xml 578 580 571 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1F</xref>
###xml 599 604 592 597 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ARC2 </italic>
Supporting the above relationship between ptCpn60beta and plastid division, map-based cloning of the previously isolated chloroplast division mutant arc2 [30] revealed a mutation in At2g28000, which encodes ptCpn60alpha. Leaf mesophyll cells in arc2 mutants contain fewer and larger chloroplasts than those in wild type cells ([30], Figure 1E), similar to br04. The arc2 mutation bears a single nucleotide substitution, which converts Ala-342 to Val in At2g28000 (Figure 1H). In addition, a genomic copy of At2g28000 complemented the chloroplast-division defect in arc2 (Figure 1F), indicating that ARC2 is identical to At2g28000. These results indicate that ptCpn60alpha as well as ptCpn60beta proteins are required for normal plastid division.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
Moderate reduction of ptCpn60alpha or ptCpn60beta activity causes defects in chloroplast division while severe reduction abolishes greening
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 62 75 62 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#945; </italic>
###xml 76 89 72 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">schlepperless</italic>
###xml 163 165 159 161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 181 185 177 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arc2</italic>
###xml 284 289 280 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">br04 </italic>
###xml 402 422 398 418 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lesion initiation 1 </italic>
###xml 423 427 419 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">len1</italic>
###xml 430 432 426 428 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 454 459 450 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">len1 </italic>
###xml 714 719 710 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">br04 </italic>
###xml 720 722 716 718 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 835 840 827 832 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arc2 </italic>
###xml 890 894 879 883 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">br04</italic>
A previous study showed that a T-DNA insertion null mutant of ptcpn60alpha (schlepperless, At2g28000) had a defect in embryo development and greening of plastids [31]. In contrast, arc2, a missense allele, germinated normally, though it showed a dwarf phenotype later in development. br04 (At1g55490) also germinated normally, consistent with previous observations of another null allele of At1g55490, lesion initiation 1 (len1) [32]. Although leaves of len1 had wrinkled irregular surfaces and displayed lesion formation under short-day conditions, under long-day conditions similar to those used throughout our study, these phenotypes were not observed and the plants showed a dwarf phenotype similar to that of br04 [32]. We hypothesized that the differences in phenotypes resulted from remnant ptCpn60alpha activity in the case of arc2 and redundant ptCpn60beta proteins in the case of br04.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 74 86 74 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana </italic>
###xml 148 160 148 160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana </italic>
###xml 220 221 220 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 513 515 492 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 766 767 738 739 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 74 85 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
###xml 148 159 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
To address the above possibilities, we first analyzed ptCpn60 proteins in A. thaliana by phylogenetic analyses. The results showed monophyly of six A. thaliana proteins with cyanobacterial chaperonin 60 proteins (Figure 2). Of these, two proteins, including At2g28000, were grouped as ptCpn60alpha (we named them ptCpn60alpha1 and ptCpn60alpha2), and four proteins, including At1g55490, were grouped as ptCpn60beta (ptCpn60beta1 through ptCpn60beta4). These results are consistent with a previous classification [33]. Further, our phylogenetic analyses indicated that there are two types of cyanobacterial chaperonin 60 proteins, GroEL-1 and GroEL-2, and that only one group, GroEL-1, gave rise to ptCpn60alpha and ptCpn60beta in land plants and green algae (Figure 2).

Next, we compared the phenotypes of arc2 (a missense allele, ptcpn60alpha1-1) and a T-DNA insertion mutant (ptcpn60alpha1-2, SALK_006606, Figure 1H) of ptCpn60alpha1 (At2g28000) (Figures 3A to 3C). In contrast to arc2, seedlings of the insertion mutant exhibit an albino phenotype and the growth of this mutant was severely suppressed (Figure 3C), similar to that of schlepperless, another T-DNA insertion null allele [31]. By microscopy, we observed small and colourless plastid-like organelles in leaf cells, but no developed chloroplasts (Figure 3C). When a genomic fragment bearing the ptCpn60alpha1 (At2g28000) gene was introduced into the mutant, the phenotype was complemented (Figure 3D). These results indicate that loss of ptCpn60alpha1 abolishes greening of plastids. In support of this conclusion, the amount of chlorophyll extracted from true leaves of arc2 was less than that of wild type (Figure 3E), although the arc2 cells contained green chloroplasts that showed defects in division (Figure 3B). The above observations of two ptcpn60alpha1 mutants suggest that complete loss of ptCpn60alpha1 activity fully abolishes greening of plastids while the weaker arc2 allele, though probably retaining residual activity of ptCpn60alpha1, still confers chloroplast division defects.

We addressed possible functional redundancy among ptCpn60beta proteins. The phylogenetic analyses showed that ptCpn60beta1 (br04, At1g55490) has the closest evolutionary relationship with ptCpn60beta2 (At3g13470) (Figure 2) and a BLAST search showed 92% identity between the two amino acid sequences. In order to assess whether ptCpn60beta2 protein is also required for plastid division and/or plastid development and whether the functions of ptCpn60beta1 and ptCpn60beta2 proteins are redundant, we observed a T-DNA insertion mutant of ptCpn60beta2 (SALK_014547, ptcpn60beta2). Although the mutant did not exhibit plastid division or embryo development defects (Figures 3F7 and 3G7), the ptcpn60beta1-1 (br04) ptcpn60beta2 (SALK_014547) double mutant exhibited small, albino seedlings (Figures 3F9 and 3G9), similar to the ptcpn60alpha1 T-DNA mutant (ptcpn60alpha1-2, Figure 3C). Since the ptcpn60beta1-1 (br04) and ptcpn60beta2 (SALK_014547) single mutants did not show the albino phenotype (Figures 3F3 and 3F7), the above results indicate that ptCpn60beta1 and ptCpn60beta2 are redundant.

Similar to the chlorophyll content, the plastid division defect was dependent on the number of mutant alleles (Figure 3G). Other than the double homozygous mutant, all combinations containing the ptcpn60beta1-1 homozygous mutation (Figures 3G3 and 3G6) and the combination of the ptcpn60beta1-1 heterozygous mutation and ptcpn60beta2 homozygous mutation (Figure 3G8) showed a large-chloroplast phenotype, while the size and number of chloroplasts were normal in other combinations (Figures 3G1, G2, G4, G5 and 3G7). To confirm the reduction of ptCpn60beta proteins in ptcpn60beta mutants and further examine localization of the proteins, we prepared antibodies using recombinant ptCpn60beta1. On immunoblots, the antibodies detected a single band of ~60 kDa, close to the predicted size of the ptCpn60beta proteins (the predicted transit peptide was omitted for calculation of the molecular mass, Figure 4A). When the same amount of total protein extracted from whole plants was examined, the intensity of the band was reduced in the ptcpn60beta1-1 mutant (br04) relative to that in the wild type. However, a residual band was detected, similar to a previous report that anti-spinach ptCpn60beta antibodies recognized residual ptCpn60beta in the ptcpn60beta1-1 mutant (len1, null mutant) [32]. Since the antibodies still detected a faint band at the same position on the gel in the double mutant (Figure 4A), they may also recognize ptCpn60beta3 and/or ptCpn60beta4. In the immunoblot analysis, there was little difference in the intensity of the band between wild type and ptcpn60beta2. This is probably because the ptCpn60beta2 protein level is lower than the levels of the other ptCpn60beta proteins, as suggested by RT-PCR analyses showing that the ptCpn60beta2 transcript level is lower than that of ptCpn60beta1 (Figure 4B).

###begin title 34
Discussion
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 281 289 274 282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptCpn60 </italic>
###xml 347 349 340 342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 350 352 343 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 362 363 355 356 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 406 420 399 413 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">crumpled leaf </italic>
###xml 421 424 414 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">crl</italic>
###xml 508 510 501 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 543 545 536 538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
In this study, we showed that both ptCpn60alpha and ptCpn60beta are required for plastid division as well as for greening of plastids. The results also indicate that the mutant phenotypes vary depending on the severity of the mutations. In addition to defects in plastid division, ptCpn60 mutants exhibited dwarfed or other developmental defects [31,32] (Figure 3). A similar situation was observed in the crumpled leaf (crl) mutant, which also showed both plastid division defects and abnormal development [19]. Because both ptCpn60 and CRL [19] function in chloroplasts and, in our screening, several mutant plants that showed abnormal morphology contained chloroplasts of normal size, it is unlikely that the developmental defects are the cause of the observed chloroplast division defects.
###end p 35

###xml 550 552 550 552 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 680 687 680 687 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 811 813 811 813 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 879 902 879 902 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caulobacter crescentus </italic>
###xml 906 926 906 926 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Streptococcus mutans</italic>
###xml 1002 1004 1002 1004 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 1005 1007 1005 1007 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 227 243 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 541 548 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 680 687 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 879 901 <span type="species:ncbi:155892">Caulobacter crescentus</span>
###xml 906 926 <span type="species:ncbi:1309">Streptococcus mutans</span>
Chaperonins are evolutionarily conserved molecular chaperones found in bacteria (named GroE), mitochondria and plastids. The structure and mechanisms of chaperonin function have been well studied mainly using the chaperonin of Escherichia coli, GroE [34]. The GroE chaperonin functions as a large complex consisting of multiple 60-kD GroEL and 10-kD GroES subunits [34]. Although in vitro studies have clarified the mechanism of GroEL as a molecular chaperone, the in vivo roles are poorly understood. GroE is essential for the viability of E. coli [35] and this is partly because GroE is required for cell wall synthesis. In addition to the cell lysis phenotype of GroE-depleted E. coli, it has been reported that cells with impaired GroE exhibit filamentous cell morphology owing to defects in cell division [27]. The filamentous phenotypes were also observed in GroE-depleted Caulobacter crescentus and Streptococcus mutans, suggesting that GroE plays a universal role in cell division in bacteria [36,37].
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 188 190 188 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 200 201 200 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 266 278 266 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana </italic>
###xml 335 337 335 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 381 383 381 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 415 423 415 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptCpn60 </italic>
###xml 896 904 896 904 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1031 1033 1024 1026 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 1034 1036 1027 1029 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 266 277 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
###xml 896 903 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Plastid Cpn60 proteins are homologs of bacterial GroEL and phylogenetic studies indicate that plant Cpn60 proteins evolved from GroEL proteins in the cyanobacterial ancestor of plastids ([33], Figure 2). Previous studies showed that depletion of ptCpn60 proteins in A. thaliana results in abnormal development of embryos and plastids [31] and cell death in some growth conditions [32]. Although severe mutations in ptCpn60 genes resulted in albino and dwarf seedlings, we found that weaker mutations confer defects in plastid division. Even though plastid chaperonins are expected to be involved in several processes occurring in plastids as are bacterial chaperonins, our results suggest that one of the roles of the chaperonins is related to plastid division and that the role in division is conserved between bacteria and plastids. It is also known that plastid chaperonins are different from E. coli GroE in that plastids contain two distinct proteins, ptCpn60alpha and ptCpn60beta, both of which are expressed in all tissues [31,38]. Despite the difference, our results showed that both ptCpn60alpha and ptCpn60beta are required for plastid division.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 267 275 267 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 276 278 276 278 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 284 296 284 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana </italic>
###xml 317 319 317 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4B</xref>
###xml 449 451 449 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4A</xref>
###xml 733 734 733 734 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 735 737 735 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 738 740 738 740 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 891 899 891 899 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60 </italic>
###xml 963 969 963 969 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ftsZ1 </italic>
###xml 982 984 982 984 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 990 996 990 996 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ftsZ1 </italic>
###xml 1012 1014 1012 1014 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 1046 1050 1046 1050 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ARC6</italic>
###xml 1104 1106 1104 1106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1113 1121 1113 1121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60 </italic>
###xml 1175 1180 1175 1180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">minD </italic>
###xml 1181 1183 1181 1183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 1242 1247 1242 1247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">minE </italic>
###xml 1248 1250 1248 1250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 1253 1258 1253 1258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arc6 </italic>
###xml 1259 1261 1259 1261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1303 1314 1303 1314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pdv1, pdv2 </italic>
###xml 1318 1323 1318 1323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arc5 </italic>
###xml 1324 1326 1324 1326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 1876 1883 1876 1883 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 267 274 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 284 295 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
In our analyses, depletion of ptCpn60 proteins did not alter the level of plastid FtsZ. The size of the FtsZ protein in the mutants was the same as that in wild type, indicating that the transit peptide was cleaved and the protein was imported into plastids. Both in E. coli [26] and A. thaliana chloroplasts (Figure 4B), normal FtsZ ring formation is impaired in the respective chaperonin mutants even though FtsZ protein levels are normal (Figure 4A). These results suggest that ptCpn60 proteins are not required for import of plastid division proteins into plastids. Rather, it is suggested that ptCpn60s are required for assembly and/or maintenance of the plastid division apparatus after import of the components into plastids [8-10,13]. However, the mechanistic basis of the chloroplast division defect remains unclear. The abnormally long, disorganized FtsZ filaments observed in the ptcpn60 mutants resemble the reported FtsZ2 localization patterns in an ftsZ1 null mutant [39], an ftsZ1 antisense line [14], and in a line overexpressing ARC6, which functions in part to stabilize FtsZ polymers [13]. The ptcpn60 FtsZ morphologies are distinct from those observed in minD [40] (multiple closed FtsZ rings with multiple constriction), minE [40], arc6 [13] (many fragmented short FtsZ filaments), pdv1, pdv2 and arc5 [22] (multiple rings or spirals at the constriction site) mutants. The mutant phenotypes suggest that reduced ptCpn60 levels result in excessively stable FtsZ filaments, though whether this is through a direct effect on FtsZ or a regulator of FtsZ assembly, and whether the effects result from misfolding of some proteins in the mutant backgrounds or loss of another activity of ptCpn60, remain to be determined. Whatever the mechanism, the results provided evidence of a role for the ptCpn60 chaperone system in the regulation of FtsZ polymer dynamics in vivo.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 51 63 51 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptCpn60&#946; </italic>
###xml 171 182 168 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946;</italic>
###xml 232 247 226 238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946;1-1 </italic>
###xml 264 277 255 265 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946;2 </italic>
###xml 330 345 318 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946;1-1 </italic>
###xml 358 371 343 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946;2 </italic>
###xml 428 431 410 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3G8</xref>
###xml 470 483 452 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946;2 </italic>
###xml 574 576 550 552 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4C</xref>
###xml 704 716 674 683 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946; </italic>
###xml 893 894 857 858 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 895 897 859 861 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 898 900 862 864 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 996 998 960 962 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 1090 1092 1054 1056 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 1093 1095 1057 1059 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 1096 1098 1060 1062 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 1290 1302 1254 1263 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946; </italic>
###xml 1344 1356 1305 1314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptCpn60&#946; </italic>
###xml 1372 1374 1330 1332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3G</xref>
We compared the effects of several combinations of ptCpn60beta alleles. The appearance of the chloroplast division phenotype depends on the number of disrupted alleles of ptcpn60beta. For example, chloroplast size and number in the ptcpn60beta1-1 heterozygote and ptcpn60beta2 homozygote were normal, but combining these alleles (ptcpn60beta1-1 heterozygous ptcpn60beta2 homozygous mutant) impaired chloroplast division (Figure 3G8). The lack of an obvious phenotype in ptcpn60beta2 is probably because the level of total ptCpn60beta decreased little in this mutant (Figure 4C). The results suggest that ptCpn60beta1 and ptCpn60beta2 have redundant functions and that the plastid division defects in the ptcpn60beta mutants are due to decreased ptCpn60beta dosage. Thus far, several plastid division proteins of cyanobacterial origin, such as FtsZ, MinD, MinE, ARC6, and GC1, were identified [7-13,17]. Studies showing that the stoichiometry among these proteins is tightly maintained in plants [41] and that moderate loss or overexpression of FtsZ, MinD and MinE impairs plastid division [11,42,43] suggest that normal plastid division requires the presence of the proper stoichiometric relationship among plastid division proteins. The observed defects in plastid division in a series of ptcpn60beta mutants even in the presence of wild type ptCpn60beta alleles (Figure 3G) may reflect disruption of the stoichiometric relationship of functional plastid division proteins due to misfolding in the mutants after import from the cytosol.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 14 22 14 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 131 133 131 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 247 259 244 256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. thaliana </italic>
###xml 744 752 741 749 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 843 845 840 842 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 905 907 902 904 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 1029 1037 1026 1034 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1152 1154 1149 1151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 1155 1157 1152 1154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 14 21 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 247 258 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A. thaliana</span>
###xml 744 751 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1029 1036 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Studies using E. coli showed preferential localization of a population of GroEL at division sites by immunofluorescence labelling [26]. In our immunofluorescence analyses, however, ptCpn60beta proteins are spattered throughout the chloroplasts of A. thaliana and we could not observe predominant localization of the protein at the division site. This observation is perhaps because of the existence of several chloroplast proteins which require Cpn60 proteins for their folding. In fact, many proteins other than division proteins have been identified as possible targets of bacterial GroEL as below. Despite of this observation, it is still possible that a portion of the ptCpn60 pool interacts with the plastid division machinery. A study in E. coli further suggested that the division protein FtsE is a target substrate of the GroE system [27]. In contrast, FtsE is missing in plant and algal genomes [44], suggesting that the plastid Cpn60 system targets a different plastid division substrate(s). Proteome-based analyses in E. coli identified ~300 proteins that interact with GroE, including the cell division proteins FtsE, FtsA, FtsI, and FtsZ [45-47] although GroE-dependent folding of FtsA, FtsI, and FtsZ has not been examined. Of these, only FtsZ is conserved in plant genomes, raising the possibility that FtsZ might be a target of Cpn60 in the plastid.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 100 102 100 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 115 117 115 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 177 179 177 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 334 342 334 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 364 366 364 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 448 455 448 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
Several other molecular chaperone proteins have been shown to function in plastids, such as HSP100 [48] and HSP70 [49], but there is limited information about their substrates [50]. Although it is known that functional specificity of Hsp70 is mediated by specialized co-chaperones, how and what kinds of proteins GroE/Cpn60 recognize in vivo is little understood [51]. Further studies on the interaction between GroEL and plastid division proteins in vivo, such as co-immunoprecipitation and FRET analyses, would shed light on the role of ptCpn60 in the assembly and/or maintenance of the plastid division machinery.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
Conclusion
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 209 218 202 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60a </italic>
###xml 222 234 215 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptcpn60&#946; </italic>
Our results show that cyanobacteria-derived ptCpn60alpha and ptCpn60beta proteins are required for plastid division. FtsZ ring formation in plastids, but not import of FtsZ into the plastids, was perturbed in ptcpn60a and ptcpn60beta mutants, suggesting that ptCpn60 proteins are required for assembly of the cyanobacteria-derived part of the plastid division machinery subsequent to import of plastid division proteins, all of which are encoded in the nucleus. Although plants have several members of the ptCpn60alpha and ptCpn60beta family, we found that moderate reduction of ptCpn60 level results in impaired plastid division and reduction of chlorophylls. The results suggest the existence of mechanisms that regulate the levels of the ptCpn60 family of proteins in plastids.
###end p 43

###begin title 59
Phylogenetic analyses
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 47 53 47 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GroEL </italic>
###xml 63 69 63 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">genes </italic>
###xml 118 119 118 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 140 141 140 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 173 175 173 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 373 375 373 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 575 577 575 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
Deduced amino acid sequences encoded by the 82 GroEL and Cpn60 genes (gi numbers or locus IDs are indicated in Figure 2 and Additional file 1) were aligned using CLUSTAL W [55] and the alignment was refined manually. Gaps were deleted and 490 conserved sites were used for the phylogenetic analyses. Bayesian inference was performed with the program MrBayes version 3.1.2 [56] with WAG+I+G4 model. For the MrBayes consensus trees, 1,000,000 generations were completed with trees sampled each 1,000 cycles. Maximum likelihood trees were constructed using RaxML version 7.0.4 [57] with the WAG matrix of amino acid replacements assuming a proportion of invariant positions and four gamma-distributed rates (WAG+I+G4 model). The local bootstrap probability of each branch was calculated by 100 replications.
