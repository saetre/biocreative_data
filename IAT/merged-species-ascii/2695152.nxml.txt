###xml 99 102 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Role of protein kinase C and mu-opioid receptor (MOPr) desensitization in tolerance to morphine in rat locus coeruleus neurons

###begin p 3
###xml 363 371 362 370 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 420 427 419 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 903 911 898 906 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 916 923 911 918 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1136 1137 1127 1128 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 264 267 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
In morphine tolerance a key question that remains to be answered is whether mu-opioid receptor (MOPr) desensitization contributes to morphine tolerance, and if so by what cellular mechanisms. Here we demonstrate that MOPr desensitization can be observed in single rat brainstem locus coeruleus (LC) neurons following either prolonged (> 4 h) exposure to morphine in vitro or following treatment of animals with morphine in vivo for 3 days. Analysis of receptor function by an operational model indicated that with either treatment morphine could induce a profound degree (70-80%) of loss of receptor function. Ongoing PKC activity in the MOPr-expressing neurons themselves, primarily by PKCalpha, was required to maintain morphine-induced MOPr desensitization, because exposure to PKC inhibitors for only the last 30-50 min of exposure to morphine reduced the MOPr desensitization that was induced both in vitro and in vivo. The presence of morphine was also required for maintenance of desensitization, as washout of morphine for > 2 h reversed MOPr desensitization. MOPr desensitization was homologous, as there was no change in alpha2-adrenoceptor or ORL1 receptor function. These results demonstrate that prolonged morphine treatment induces extensive homologous desensitization of MOPrs in mature neurons, that this desensitization has a significant PKC-dependent component and that this desensitization underlies the maintenance of morphine tolerance.

###xml 687 693 686 692 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 679 699 678 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1">Alvarez <italic>et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>
###xml 798 819 797 818 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b12">Finn &amp; Whistler, 2001</xref>
###xml 928 934 927 933 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 921 940 920 939 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2">Bailey <italic>et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>
###xml 942 946 941 945 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">2004</xref>
###xml 948 969 947 968 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10">Dang &amp; Williams, 2005</xref>
###xml 971 992 970 991 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b48">Virk &amp; Williams, 2008</xref>
The analgesic, respiratory depressant and rewarding effects of morphine occur through activation of mu-opioid receptors (MOPrs; Matthes et al., 1996; Romberg et al., 2003). Tolerance to these in vivo responses develops on prolonged exposure (Roerig et al., 1987; Shippenberg et al., 1989; Smith et al., 1999). For many G-protein-coupled receptors prolonged exposure to an agonist results in rapid receptor desensitization through a variety of mechanisms that may involve specific kinases (GRKs) or second messenger kinases, such as protein kinase C (PKC) and A (Kelly et al., 2008). Initial studies suggested that morphine does not induce MOPr desensitization in mature neurons (Alvarez et al., 2002), leading to the suggestion that receptor desensitization was not involved in morphine tolerance (Finn & Whistler, 2001). More recently, however, morphine-induced MOPr desensitization has been observed in mature neurons (Bailey et al., 2003, 2004; Dang & Williams, 2005; Virk & Williams, 2008), although the extent of the desensitization is less than that observed with other opioids.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 199 205 199 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 192 211 192 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">Bailey <italic>et al.</italic>, 2004</xref>

###xml 763 781 763 781 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b51">Zeitz <italic>et al.</italic>, 2001</xref>

###xml 783 800 783 800 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6">Bohn <italic>et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>
###xml 806 812 806 812 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 802 818 802 818 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b18">Hua <italic>et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>
###xml 826 832 826 832 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 820 838 820 838 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b42">Smith <italic>et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>
###xml 840 844 840 844 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b43">2007</xref>
###xml 861 868 861 868 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 499 503 <span type="species:ncbi:10090|species:ncbi:10095">mice</span>
Our work studying acute MOPr desensitization of recombinant MOPrs and endogenous MOPrs in mature brain neurons has revealed a PKC-dependent component of morphine-induced MOPr desensitization (Bailey et al., 2004, 2006; Johnson et al., 2006), whereas for the opioid peptide, DAMGO, desensitization was almost entirely through a GRK-dependent mechanism (Johnson et al., 2006). There is also evidence to suggest that in vivo different opioids induce tolerance by different mechanisms. In GRK3 knockout mice the antinociceptive tolerance that develops to fentanyl was markedly reduced, but that which develops to morphine was not (Terman et al., 2004). A number of studies have reported that PKC is involved in morphine-induced tolerance in vivo (Inoue & Ueda, 2000; Zeitz et al., 2001; Bohn et al., 2002; Hua et al., 2002; Smith et al., 2003, 2007). However, such in vivo studies do not delineate where in the sequence of intracellular events PKC acts to induce such tolerance.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 176 184 176 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 189 196 189 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 521 529 521 529 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 580 588 580 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 686 693 686 693 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ex vivo</italic>
###xml 720 728 720 728 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 733 740 733 740 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
In this study we have determined the role PKC-induced MOPr desensitization plays in the tolerance to morphine that develops in mature neurons on prolonged exposure to the drug in vitro and in vivo. To do this we have developed a protocol with which the level of MOPr desensitization can be determined in adult mammalian neurons in the continued presence of the drug. This approach has allowed us to study the time-course of development of MOPr desensitization at the single cell level in brain slices exposed to morphine in vitro, and to compare the MOPr desensitization observed in vitro with that observed in brain slices prepared from animals chronically treated with morphine (i.e. ex vivo). We found that with both in vitro and in vivo morphine pre-treatment the major component of MOPr desensitization requires ongoing PKC activity to be maintained, whereas incubation of slices with DAMGO induced MOPr desensitization that was not PKC dependent. Analysis of the data using an operational model of drug agonism has allowed us to assess and compare the actual loss of MOPr function that underlies the tolerance that developed to both agonists.

###begin title 8
Materials and methods
###end title 8
###begin title 9
Electrophysiological recordings
###end title 9
###begin title 10
Brain slice preparation
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 185 191 184 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 178 197 177 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2">Bailey <italic>et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>
###xml 5 16 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Wistar rats</span>
Male Wistar rats (130-170 g) were killed by cervical dislocation, and horizontal brain slices (200-250 mum thick) containing the locus coeruleus (LC) were prepared as described (Bailey et al., 2003). All experiments were performed in accordance with the UK Animals (Scientific Procedures) Act 1986, the European Communities Council Directive 1986 (86/609/EEC) and the University of Bristol ethical review document.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Whole-cell patch-clamp recordings
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 172 173 172 173 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 201 202 201 202 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 213 214 213 214 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 224 225 224 225 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 227 228 227 228 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 235 236 235 236 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">d</sc>
###xml 257 258 257 258 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 306 307 306 307 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 313 314 313 314 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 542 543 536 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V</italic>
###xml 543 544 537 538 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">h</sub>
###xml 612 613 602 603 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 659 660 649 650 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 687 688 677 678 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
Slices were submerged in a slice chamber (0.5 mL) mounted on the microscope stage and superfused (2.5-3 mL/min) with artificial cerebrospinal fluid (aCSF) composed of (in mm): NaCl, 126; KCl, 2.5; MgCl2, 1.2; CaCl2, 2.4; NaH2PO4, 1.2; d-glucose, 11.1; NaHCO3, 21.4; ascorbic acid, 0.1; saturated with 95% O2/5% CO2 at 33-34degreesC. For patch-clamp recording LC neurons were visualized by Nomarski optics using infrared light and individual cell somata were cleaned by gentle flow of aCSF from a pipette. Whole-cell voltage-clamp recordings (Vh = -60 mV) were made using electrodes (3-6 MOmega) filled with (in mm): K-gluconate, 115; HEPES, 10; EGTA, 11; MgCl2, 2; NaCl, 10; MgATP, 2; Na2GTP, 0.25 (pH 7.3, osmolarity 270 mOsm). Recordings of whole-cell current were filtered at 2 kHz using an Axopatch 200B amplifier and analysed off-line using pClamp.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 44 45 44 45 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 276 277 272 273 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 319 320 315 316 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 345 367 341 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b28">North &amp; Williams, 1985</xref>
###xml 524 525 519 520 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 762 763 753 754 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 863 864 853 854 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 883 884 872 873 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
Activation of MOPrs evoked a transmembrane K+ current, and by performing whole-cell patch-clamp recordings a real-time index of MOPr activation could be continually recorded. The opioid-evoked current was continuously recorded at a holding potential of -60 mV. MOPrs and alpha2-adrenoceptors couple to the same set of K+ channels in LC neurons (North & Williams, 1985). To reduce variation between cells, the amplitudes of all opioid-evoked currents were normalized to the maximum current evoked by noradrenaline (NA; 100 mum) in the same cell. NA responses were unchanged by any of the drug treatments used in this study. Therefore, any desensitization of MOPrs observed must be homologous to the MOPr. To ensure that responses to NA were mediated through alpha2-adrenoceptors and not attenuated by uptake, NA was always applied in the presence of prazosin (1 mum) and cocaine (3 mum).
###end p 14
###begin p 15
All drugs were applied in the superfusing solution at known concentrations. Drugs and chemicals used were from Sigma (Gillingham, UK), except Met-Enkephalin (Bachem, Bubendorf, Switzerland) and Go6976 (Tocris, Bristol, UK). RACK inhibitors were from Kai Pharmaceuticals, San Francisco, CA, USA. The specific RACK inhibitors used were KIG31-1 (a PKCgamma inhibitor), KIBI31-1 (a PKCbetaI inhibitor), KIBII31-1 (a PKCbetaII inhibitor) and KIC1-1 (a classical PKC isoform inhibitor).

###begin title 16
Induction of morphine tolerance
###end title 16
###begin title 17
In vitro
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 272 273 265 266 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
Slices were placed on a nylon mesh platform in a pre-incubation chamber containing approximately 250 mL of aCSF at 33-34degreesC. Given adequate oxygenation, slices incubated in this manner remained viable for up to 12 h. To induce morphine tolerance, morphine (1 or 30 mum) was added to the aCSF bathing the slices for periods of up to 6 h prior to mounting the slices in the recording chamber. The aCSF bathing the slices in the recording chamber also contained morphine at the same concentration as used in the pre-incubation treatment. Whole-cell recordings were then obtained and the slices challenged with opioids up to 9 h after the start of the morphine treatment.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
In vivo
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 603 609 603 609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 592 615 592 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b38">Santamarta <italic>et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>
###xml 29 33 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
To induce morphine tolerance rats were injected subcutaneously with 200 mg/kg morphine base contained in a slow-release formulation that contained 200 mg/mL morphine base suspended in an emulsion containing 0.9% NaCl, liquid paraffin oil and mannide monooleate (Arlacel A) in a ratio of 0.5 : 0.42 : 0.08 (v : v : v). Three days after injection of the slow-release morphine the animals were killed and brain slices prepared as described above. This method of morphine treatment has previously been shown to induce significant tolerance to the cellular and analgesic effects of MOPr agonists (Santamarta et al., 2005).
###end p 20

###begin title 24
Operational model of agonism and loss of receptor function
###end title 24
###begin p 25

The operational model of antagonism (Black et al., 1985) states that where the response E is expressed in terms of the concentration of agonist A, the theoretical maximal effect Em (greater than that which can be functionally attained; Black et al., 1985), the dissociation constant Ka, the transducer ratio tau and n is the slope of the curve (NB n in this equation is not the Hill slope as suggested in Graph Pad Prism). Em and n are intrinsic properties of the receptor/cell and are independent of the agonist used, whereas tau depends on the cell, receptor function and agonist used. For the MOPrs in LC neurons, values of Em and n were determined by constructing concentration-response curves to Met-Enkephalin in the absence and presence of the irreversible antagonist beta-funaltrexamine (beta-FNA; 30 nm for 30 min), and subjecting the curves to simultaneous non-linear regression analysis (see Fig. 5A). Having obtained values for Em and n in this way, then the values of tau and Ka were calculated for each agonist by fitting concentration-response curves by non-linear regression.

###begin title 33
Measuring MOPr desensitization following prolonged drug exposure
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 550 563 550 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Fig. 1A and B</xref>
###xml 582 588 582 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 574 594 574 594 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1">Alvarez <italic>et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>
###xml 603 609 603 609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 596 615 596 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2">Bailey <italic>et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>
###xml 1128 1129 1127 1128 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
To determine the extent of MOPr desensitization resulting from prolonged morphine exposure, we developed a protocol that would enable us to assess the extent of receptor desensitization following prolonged morphine exposure without washing out the morphine as washout of morphine would induce withdrawal and initiate reversal of any desensitization that had been induced. To do this we made use of the fact that morphine is a partial agonist at MOPrs in LC neurons having a lower maximum response than other opioids such as Met-Enkephalin and DAMGO (Fig. 1A and B; see also Alvarez et al., 2002; Bailey et al., 2003). A basic tenet of receptor theory is that for a partial agonist the maximum response is produced only when all of the available receptors are occupied, and therefore any loss of MOPr function as would occur if receptors were desensitized would result in a decrease in the maximum response to the partial agonist. Therefore, any deficit in receptor function caused by drug treatments would be seen as a decrease in the maximum response to morphine (measured by applying a receptor-saturating concentration, 30 mum, of morphine).
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 123 124 122 123 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 136 137 135 136 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 224 231 221 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Fig. 1B</xref>
###xml 337 338 333 334 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 362 363 354 355 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 477 478 465 466 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 548 570 536 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b28">North &amp; Williams, 1985</xref>
###xml 702 703 689 690 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
In preliminary experiments on drug-naive slices we demonstrated that a receptor-saturating concentration of morphine (30 mum) evoked a K+ current that was 94 +/- 13% of the maximum response evoked by NA in the same neurons (Fig. 1B). In all our experiments the opioid responses have been normalized to the maximum NA response (NA; 100 mum) mediated through alpha2-adrenoceptors in the same neuron to control for variations in current amplitudes between neurons. MOPrs and alpha2-adrenoceptors couple to the same set of GIRK channels in LC neurons (North & Williams, 1985). Following washout of the morphine for 3 min, application of a receptor-saturating concentration of the full agonist, DAMGO (10 mum), evoked a response of 149 +/- 15% of the maximum response to NA. These values for the maximum responses evoked by morphine and DAMGO administered sequentially to the same neurons are not different from those in slices where neurons were exposed to only one of the opioids. This demonstrates that it is possible in the same neuron to obtain maximum response amplitudes for morphine and DAMGO.

###xml 1792 1793 1779 1780 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 1806 1807 1792 1793 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
Figure 1C and D shows sample traces from LC neurons in slices that had been incubated for 6-9 h in 1 mum or 30 mum morphine, and then maintained in the continued presence of morphine whilst the recording was initiated. Morphine (30 mum) and DAMGO (10 mum) were then applied sequentially to obtain the maximum responses to each drug, and naloxone (1 mum) applied to return the holding current to the true baseline level from which opioid-evoked current amplitudes could be measured. Pooled data (Fig. 1E) show that 6-9 h pre-incubation in 1 mum morphine alone caused no statistically significant decrease in the maximum response to morphine, compared with data from slices incubated in control aCSF for 6-9 h. In contrast, when LC slices were incubated with 30 mum morphine for periods up to 6-9 h there was a gradual decrease in the maximum response to morphine over time that first became statistically significant at the 4-6 h time-point (Fig. 1E); after 6-9 h pre-incubation the maximum response to morphine had decreased by 63 +/- 7%. Note, unlike rapid MOPr desensitization (Bailey et al., 2004), that produced by prolonged exposure to 30 mum morphine was observed without the need to activate PKC with oxotremorine-M (oxo-M) or phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (PMA). To ensure that the desensitization induced by the prolonged exposure to 30 mum morphine had not in some non-specific way interfered with G-protein coupling or potassium channel function, we also measured the amplitude of the maximum NA-evoked current. There was no change in the amplitude of the maximum NA-evoked current following exposure of LC slices to either 1 or 30 mum morphine for up to 9 h (Fig. 1F and Supporting Information Fig. S1, A). It is concluded therefore that exposure of LC neurons in vitro to 30 mum but not 1 mum morphine, alone for 6-9 h, induces significant homologous MOPr desensitization.

###xml 1134 1144 1123 1133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SD1">Fig. S1, C</xref>

###xml 1290 1297 1278 1285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig02">Fig. 2C</xref>
###xml 1349 1350 1336 1337 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
Because 1 mum morphine alone did not induce MOPr desensitization, we next examined the effect of PKC activation on the development of MOPr desensitization during prolonged exposure to 1 mum morphine. Slices were incubated for 6-9 h with morphine (1 mum) as well as oxo-M (10 mum) to activate PKC. In control experiments 6-9 h incubation with oxo-M alone had no effect on the maximum response to morphine, whereas following exposure to morphine plus oxo-M the maximum response to morphine was decreased to 46 +/- 8% (Fig. 2A and B). The effect of oxo-M could be mimicked by activating PKC with the phorbol ester, PMA. When slices were incubated with both morphine (1 mum) and PMA (1 mum), the maximum response to morphine was reduced by 52 +/- 10% (Fig. 2B). Like oxo-M, PMA alone (6-9 h) had no effect. Thus, MOPr desensitization can be induced by prolonged exposure to 1 mum morphine in vitro, but only when the PKC activity in LC neurons is enhanced above the basal level in isolated neurons. The decrease in responsiveness was selective to the MOPr, as the NA responses were unaffected by any pre-treatment (Supporting Information Fig. S1, C). In acute exposure (7 min) experiments, 1 mum morphine did not induce MOPr desensitization in the absence or presence of PKC activation by PMA (Fig. 2C), indicating that, even when PKC is activated, 1 mum morphine causes MOPr desensitization only after prolonged exposure to the drug.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 43 44 42 43 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 68 69 66 67 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 182 183 179 180 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 304 320 301 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SD1">Fig. S1, B and D</xref>
###xml 413 437 410 434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b29">Osborne &amp; Williams, 1995</xref>
Whilst prolonged exposure to morphine 30 mum alone, or morphine 1 mum+ oxo-M or PMA, reduced the maximum response to morphine and thus revealed a loss of functional MOPrs, only 30 mum morphine pre-treatment resulted in a decrease in the maximum response to the full agonist DAMGO (Supporting Information Fig. S1, B and D). As a high-efficacy agonist, DAMGO has a significant spare receptor reserve in LC neurons (Osborne & Williams, 1995), such that, in comparison to the partial agonist morphine, a greater amount of MOPr desensitization is required to produce a decrease in the maximum DAMGO response.

###begin title 41
###xml 65 73 65 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
Role of PKC in maintaining MOPr desensitization during prolonged in vitro exposure to morphine
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 263 264 262 263 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 281 282 279 280 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 360 361 357 358 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 638 653 635 650 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Figure 3A and B</xref>
###xml 878 885 875 882 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3B</xref>
To examine whether ongoing PKC activity was required to maintain MOPr desensitization, we investigated whether PKC inhibitors could reverse morphine-induced MOPr desensitization after desensitization had already been induced. Slices were first incubated with 1 mum morphine + 10 mum oxo-M for 6-9 h, and then the conventional PKC isoform inhibitor Go6976 (1 mum) was added for 15-30 min prior to initiation of whole-cell recording, and for a further 15-20 min prior to determining the maximum responses to morphine (i.e. the neurons were only exposed to the PKC inhibitor for the last 30-50 min of the 6-9-h prolonged morphine exposure). Figure 3A and B shows that the presence of Go6976 for the final 30-50 min of incubation with morphine + -M significantly reversed the MOPr desensitization induced by morphine. Go6976 alone had no effect on the maximum response to morphine (Fig. 3B).
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 46 47 45 46 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 153 154 151 152 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 337 338 334 335 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 395 396 391 392 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 559 560 554 555 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 577 584 572 579 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3B</xref>
Having shown that MOPr desensitization to 1 mum morphine required PKC activation, we next tested the hypothesis that the desensitization induced by 30 mum morphine alone was because there was sufficient basal PKC activity to cause MOPr desensitization by this receptor-saturating concentration of morphine. We incubated slices with 30 mum morphine for 6-9 h, followed by addition of Go6976 (1 mum) in the continued presence of morphine for 30-50 min (i.e. prior to and during the recording period). Go6976 significantly reversed the tolerance induced by 30 mum morphine alone (Fig. 3B).
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 130 131 129 130 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 175 181 174 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 170 187 169 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b7">Chen <italic>et al.</italic>, 2001</xref>
###xml 545 551 495 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 541 557 491 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b37">Ron <italic>et al.</italic>, 1995</xref>
###xml 686 687 635 636 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 855 861 794 800 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 847 867 786 806 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b21">Johnson <italic>et al.</italic>, 1996</xref>
###xml 869 898 808 837 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b44">Stebbins &amp; Mochly-Rosen, 2001</xref>
###xml 909 916 848 855 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3C</xref>
We next examined which PKC isoform(s) could be involved in the MOPr desensitization induced by prolonged exposure (6-9 h) to 30 mum morphine alone using RACK inhibitors (Chen et al., 2001). We first used a RACK inhibitor that blocks all conventional PKC isoforms (i.e. PKCalpha, betaI, betaII and gamma), but does not block novel (i.e. PKCdelta, epsilon, theta and mu) or atypical PKC isoforms (i.e. PKCxi and lambda). Exposure of slices to a RACK inhibitor that blocks all conventional PKC isoforms (i.e. PKCalpha, betaI, betaII and gamma; Ron et al., 1995) for 30-50 min at the end of the prolonged exposure to morphine significantly reversed the MOPr desensitization induced by 30 mum morphine (similar to when conventional PKC isoforms were inhibited by Go6976), whereas a combination of RACK inhibitors that block PKCbetaI, betaII and gamma (Johnson et al., 1996; Stebbins & Mochly-Rosen, 2001) did not (Fig. 3C). These data indicate that PKCalpha is probably the isoform required to maintain prolonged morphine-induced MOPr desensitization.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 13 14 12 13 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 41 42 39 40 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
For both 1 mum morphine + oxo-M and 30 mum morphine alone, the MOPr desensitization was reversed when the PKC inhibitors were added after 6-9 h of morphine exposure had already occurred and thus MOPr desensitization had already been initiated. This demonstrates that ongoing PKC activity is essential to maintain MOPr desensitization.

###xml 26 30 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 240 244 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
Slices were prepared from rats pre-treated in vivo with morphine for 3 days (see Materials and methods). During all stages of slice preparation the fluid bathing the brain contained morphine (1 mum). In LC neurons from morphine-pre-treated rats the maximum response to morphine (30 mum) was significantly lower than that observed in parallel experiments on LC slices from untreated animals and not incubated in morphine (Fig. 3D). In neurons from morphine-pre-treated animals the maximum response to morphine was decreased by 48%. In these slices the maximum response to NA (156 +/- 15 pA) was not significantly different from the maximum NA response evoked in slices from non-morphine-pre-treated animals (Supporting Information Fig. S1, A), suggesting that the MOPr tolerance observed was homologous. To test this further, we examined whether chronic morphine treatment caused ORL1 desensitization, a receptor that has been shown to desensitize in a PKC-dependent manner (Pu et al., 1999). We saw no effect on ORL1 function following in vivo morphine treatment, further suggesting that the MOPr desensitization was homologous (Fig. 3E).

To examine whether ongoing PKC activity was required to maintain the MOPr desensitization induced by in vivo exposure to morphine, slices were prepared as above and incubated in morphine (1 mum). PKC is not involved in the MOPr desensitization following prolonged in vitro exposure to DAMGO
###end title 49

To examine the MOPr desensitization induced by DAMGO, we used two concentrations of the drug, 30 nm and 10 mum. We have used the operational model of agonism [Eq. (1) above;Black et al., 1985; ] to determine the actual loss of MOPr function that is responsible for the levels of desensitization observed. This is important because the actual loss of receptor function required to reduce the maximum response to a full agonist may be much greater than that required to cause a similar reduction in the maximum response to a partial agonist as a full agonist will have a receptor reserve that must be removed before any loss of maximum response is observed (Connor et al., 2004).

Treatment for 6-9 h with 1 mum morphine alone caused no significant decrease in MOPr function; however, when neurons were incubated with 1 mum morphine and either oxo-M (10 mum) or PMA (1 mum), receptor function was decreased by 70% and 74%, respectively (Fig. 5G). It appears therefore that exposure to DAMGO induces MOPr desensitization that is both rapid in onset and extensive, whereas morphine-induced MOPr desensitization develops more slowly, requires ongoing PKC activation, but both in vitro and in vivo morphine treatment can induce a similar level of loss of receptor function.

###begin title 61
Discussion
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 363 384 363 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b12">Finn &amp; Whistler, 2001</xref>
###xml 500 508 500 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 512 519 512 519 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
A central finding of this study is that prolonged exposure of mature neurons to morphine causes profound MOPr desensitization. It had been suggested that morphine produces tolerance because it does not induce rapid MOPr desensitization and that morphine tolerance occurs following subsequent, as yet unknown, adaptive changes caused by prolonged MOPr signalling (Finn & Whistler, 2001). Our data would refute that hypothesis. Morphine does induce homologous MOPr desensitization, following prolonged in vitro or in vivo morphine treatment. This finding re-confirms the view that MOPr desensitization contributes to morphine tolerance.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 112 118 112 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 105 124 105 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9">Connor <italic>et al.</italic>, 2004</xref>
###xml 266 274 266 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 278 285 278 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 403 411 403 411 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 567 573 567 573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 558 580 558 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8">Christie <italic>et al.</italic> (1987)</xref>
###xml 677 685 677 685 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1009 1015 1009 1015 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 997 1021 997 1021 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b46">Terwilliger <italic>et al.</italic>, 1994</xref>
###xml 626 629 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
To circumvent problems in the interpretation of opioid agonist-induced desensitization data (reviewed in Connor et al., 2004), we specifically designed our experiments so that we could investigate agonist coupling and desensitization immediately following long-term in vitro or in vivo morphine treatments, in the absence of complications due to drug withdrawal. Indeed, after 2-4 h morphine withdrawal in vitro, MOPr desensitization was reversed. This suggests that the MOPr desensitization we observed was somewhat transient, in contrast with that seen by Christie et al. (1987) where MOPr desensitization was still seen in rat LC neurons up to 6 h after morphine withdrawal in vitro. In that study the chronic morphine treatment used higher doses than in this study (> 2700 mg/kg morphine pellets over 7 days vs 200 mg/kg emulsion over 3 days), and possibly in their experiments the level of chronic treatment induced additional adaptive mechanisms, such as enhanced GRK and arrestin activity (Terwilliger et al., 1994) or receptor downregulation that may result in longer-lasting MOPr desensitization.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 108 116 108 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 217 238 217 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10">Dang &amp; Williams, 2005</xref>
###xml 247 253 247 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 240 259 240 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19">Ingram <italic>et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>
###xml 464 470 464 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 452 476 452 476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b46">Terwilliger <italic>et al.</italic>, 1994</xref>
###xml 483 489 483 489 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 478 495 478 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14">Gold <italic>et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>
Further, there have been recent studies in LC and periaqueductal grey neurons demonstrating that, following in vitro withdrawal from chronic morphine treatment, MOPrs exhibit enhanced agonist-induced desensitization (Dang & Williams, 2005; Ingram et al., 2008). These studies suggest the possibility that morphine withdrawal and/or higher levels of morphine tolerance may induce additional adaptive mechanisms, such as effects on GRKs or RGS proteins (Terwilliger et al., 1994; Gold et al., 2003).
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 110 131 110 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10">Dang &amp; Williams, 2005</xref>
###xml 365 371 365 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 358 377 358 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">Bailey <italic>et al.</italic>, 2004</xref>
Our data with PKC inhibitors reversing morphine-induced desensitization appear somewhat at odds with those of Dang & Williams, 2005. In their paper, using sharp electrode recording, they observed that a relatively high concentration of chelerythrine enhanced the desensitization induced by morphine-6-glucuronide. We have shown in this paper and previously (Bailey et al., 2004) that a range of structurally different PKC inhibitors reverse morphine-induced desensitization.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 111 117 111 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 105 123 105 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b5">Black <italic>et al.</italic>, 1985</xref>
###xml 238 246 238 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 251 258 251 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 316 322 316 322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 307 328 307 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8">Christie <italic>et al.</italic>, 1987</xref>
###xml 702 703 702 703 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 753 754 749 750 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 806 807 802 803 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 832 854 828 850 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b28">North &amp; Williams, 1985</xref>
To obtain a quantitative measure of functional MOPr loss, we subjected the data to operational analysis (Black et al., 1985) to obtain a quantitative measure of functional MOPr loss. This revealed that prolonged exposure to morphine both in vitro and in vivo resulted in up to an 82% loss in MOPr function (Christie et al., 1987). Although the desensitization induced by morphine was slightly less than that induced by DAMGO and developed over a longer time period, it is evident that prolonged morphine exposure can induce robust loss of MOPr function in adult mammalian neurons. Importantly, MOPr desensitization occurs at the level of the MOPr and not downstream of the receptor, as the NA-induced K+-current in the same neurons was unaffected. alpha2-Adrenoceptors and MOPrs couple to the same set of K+ channels in LC neurons (North & Williams, 1985).
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 156 163 156 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 193 201 193 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 385 392 385 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 525 531 525 531 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 519 537 519 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b41">Smith <italic>et al.</italic>, 1999</xref>
###xml 553 560 553 560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 785 791 785 791 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 779 797 779 797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b13">Gabra <italic>et al.</italic>, 2007</xref>
Inhibitors of PKC significantly reversed MOPr desensitization even after the desensitization had been allowed to develop (over 3 days of morphine treatment in vivo and 6-9 h morphine treatment in vitro). This shows that for morphine desensitization to persist, ongoing PKC activity is required. Crucially, this finding is paralleled in previous studies of morphine analgesia tolerance in vivo where PKC inhibitors were able to reverse tolerance even when they were first administered after 3 days of morphine exposure (Smith et al., 1999). Furthermore, in vivo morphine analgesia tolerance can be potentiated by a PP2A phosphatase inhibitor, indicating that the level of tolerance is dependent upon the balance between ongoing MOPr phosphorylation and dephosphorylation by PP2A (Gabra et al., 2007).
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 37 43 37 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 33 49 33 49 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b50">Way <italic>et al.</italic>, 2000</xref>

###xml 608 614 586 592 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 602 620 580 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b51">Zeitz <italic>et al.</italic>, 2001</xref>
###xml 626 632 604 610 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 622 638 600 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b18">Hua <italic>et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>
###xml 647 653 625 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 640 659 618 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b27">Newton <italic>et al.</italic>, 2007</xref>
###xml 667 673 645 651 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 661 679 639 657 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b43">Smith <italic>et al.</italic>, 2007</xref>
PKC exists as multiple isoforms (Way et al., 2000), many of which are expressed in CNS neurons. In LC neurons a single conventional PKC isoform, PKCalpha, appears to be responsible for the PKC component of morphine-induced MOPr desensitization. However, PKCalpha is not the only isoform that may be involved in morphine-induced MOPr desensitization, and it is entirely possible that in different neuronal populations other isoforms of PKC may be involved. Indeed, in vivo, there is evidence from analgesia studies that morphine tolerance may involve not only PKCalpha but also PKCepsilon and PKCgamma (Zeitz et al., 2001; Hua et al., 2002; Newton et al., 2007; Smith et al., 2007).
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 35 36 35 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 36 39 36 39 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1/2</sub>
###xml 69 92 69 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b17">Harris &amp; Williams, 1991</xref>
###xml 101 107 101 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 94 113 94 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2">Bailey <italic>et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>
###xml 296 302 296 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 288 308 288 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b22">Johnson <italic>et al.</italic>, 2006</xref>
In LC neurons DAMGO induces rapid (t1/2=150 s) MOPr desensitization (Harris & Williams, 1991; Bailey et al., 2003). We have reported that in HEK-293 cells rapid DAMGO-induced MOPr desensitization is GRK-mediated because it is blocked by overexpression of a GRK2 dominant negative mutant (Johnson et al., 2006). In the present experiments we sought to determine whether, on prolonged exposure to DAMGO, a PKC component of MOPr desensitization was revealed. This does not appear to be the case because treatment with a PKC inhibitor that blocked morphine-induced MOPr desensitization failed to alter the DAMGO-induced desensitization following 6-9 h pre-treatment with DAMGO. Furthermore, it might have been expected that DAMGO would induce less MOPr desensitization than morphine given that DAMGO induces not only MOPr desensitization but also MOPr internalization that would result in MOPr resensitization by dephosphorylation and then re-insertion of reactivated receptors into the plasma membrane, whereas morphine induces much less MOPr internalization. However, we found that a receptor-saturating concentration of DAMGO induced an almost complete loss of MOPr function.
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 14 22 14 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 137 143 137 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 130 149 130 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">Bailey <italic>et al.</italic>, 2004</xref>
###xml 151 172 151 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10">Dang &amp; Williams, 2005</xref>
###xml 174 195 174 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b48">Virk &amp; Williams, 2008</xref>
###xml 373 380 373 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ex vivo</italic>
###xml 633 639 633 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 625 645 625 645 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b22">Johnson <italic>et al.</italic>, 2006</xref>
###xml 686 692 686 692 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 669 698 669 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b16">Haberstock-Debic <italic>et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>
In LC neurons in vitro under conditions of low PKC activity, a component of morphine-induced rapid MOPr desensitization persists (Bailey et al., 2004; Dang & Williams, 2005; Virk & Williams, 2008). This could be because there is, in addition to PKC-mediated desensitization, a component of morphine desensitization that is GRK-mediated. This might also explain why, in our ex vivo experiments where neurons had been exposed to morphine for 3 days, the PKC inhibitor did not completely reverse MOPr desensitization, and why a small, but significant amount of receptor internalization was induced by morphine in HEK-293 cells (Johnson et al., 2006) and striatal neurons (Haberstock-Debic et al., 2005).
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 61 67 61 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 54 73 54 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">Bailey <italic>et al.</italic>, 2004</xref>
###xml 83 89 83 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 75 95 75 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b22">Johnson <italic>et al.</italic>, 2006</xref>
###xml 464 471 464 471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 476 483 476 483 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 564 567 564 567 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2 +</sup>
###xml 725 726 724 725 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 757 765 756 764 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1105 1107 1104 1106 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">35</sup>
###xml 1262 1268 1257 1263 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1258 1274 1253 1269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b40">Sim <italic>et al.</italic>, 1996</xref>
###xml 868 872 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 1180 1184 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
The present results, together with our previous work (Bailey et al., 2004; Johnson et al., 2006), reveal that there are four main factors that determine the development of MOPr desensitization: the nature of the agonist ligand; the degree of receptor occupancy; the duration of morphine treatment; and the level of PKC activation. LC neurons in the slice preparation can be considered to be in a 'basal state', where PKC activity is lower than in the same neurons in vivo, as in vivo ongoing synaptic activity will activate Gq-coupled metabotropic receptors and Ca2 +-permeable ionotropic receptors that would elevate PKC activity. Prolonged exposure to a sub-maximal, therapeutically relevant concentration of morphine (1 mum) induced MOPr desensitization in vitro only when PKC activity was elevated. In contrast, when slices were prepared from morphine-pre-treated rats and maintained in this concentration of morphine, profound MOPr desensitization was observed that could be reversed by inhibiting PKC. Comparable levels of MOPr desensitization in the brain have been reported using agonist-induced [35S]GTPgammaS autoradiography following chronic morphine administration to rats, although the molecular mechanism of desensitization was not identified (Sim et al., 1996).
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 114 121 114 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 245 251 245 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 231 257 231 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b15">Granados-Soto <italic>et al.</italic>, 2000</xref>
###xml 259 277 259 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b20">Inoue &amp; Ueda, 2000</xref>
###xml 284 290 284 290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 279 296 279 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6">Bohn <italic>et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>
###xml 302 308 302 308 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 298 314 298 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b18">Hua <italic>et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>
###xml 322 328 322 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 316 334 316 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b42">Smith <italic>et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>
###xml 392 398 392 398 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 384 404 384 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b49">Watkins <italic>et al.</italic>, 1984</xref>
###xml 414 420 414 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 406 426 406 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b11">Dourish <italic>et al.</italic>, 1990</xref>
###xml 437 443 437 443 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 428 449 428 449 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b26">Mitchell <italic>et al.</italic>, 2000</xref>
###xml 457 463 457 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 451 469 451 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b31">Popik <italic>et al.</italic>, 2000</xref>
###xml 478 484 478 484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 471 490 471 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b32">Powell <italic>et al.</italic>, 2000</xref>
###xml 492 496 492 496 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b33">2003</xref>
###xml 508 514 508 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 498 520 498 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b47">Tortorici <italic>et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>
###xml 532 540 532 540 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 545 552 545 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ex vivo</italic>
There is now also good evidence to indicate that endogenous levels of PKC play a major role in morphine tolerance in vivo. Tolerance to the antinociceptive effects of morphine can be reduced by co-administration of PKC inhibitors (Granados-Soto et al., 2000; Inoue & Ueda, 2000; Bohn et al., 2002; Hua et al., 2002; Smith et al., 2003) or by blockade of various Gq-coupled receptors (Watkins et al., 1984; Dourish et al., 1990; Mitchell et al., 2000; Popik et al., 2000; Powell et al., 2000, 2003; Tortorici et al., 2003). What our in vitro and ex vivo experiments on brain slices demonstrate is that the site of action of PKC is within the MOPr-expressing neurons themselves, and that PKC activation enhances morphine-induced MOPr desensitization.
###end p 72
###begin p 73
###xml 126 134 126 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 139 146 139 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
In conclusion, we show that morphine can induce robust desensitization of endogenously expressed MOPrs in mature neurons both in vitro and in vivo, and that this desensitization requires ongoing activation of PKC. Our data support the view that MOPr desensitization by a PKC-dependent mechanism underlies the maintenance of morphine tolerance.
