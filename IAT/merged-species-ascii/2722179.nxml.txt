###begin article-title 0
Hepatocyte-specific NEMO deletion promotes NK/NKT cell- and TRAIL-dependent liver damage
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
N. Beraza's present address is CICbioGUNE, Technology Park of Bizkaia, Derio 48160, Spain.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 280 289 268 273 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 392 401 376 381 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 699 708 679 684 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 754 755 730 731 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 862 871 838 843 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 1048 1057 1020 1025 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 1180 1183 1148 1151 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1408 1417 1376 1381 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 402 406 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 872 876 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1382 1387 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1418 1422 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Nuclear factor kappaB (NF-kappaB) is one of the main transcription factors involved in regulating apoptosis, inflammation, chronic liver disease, and cancer progression. The IKK complex mediates NF-kappaB activation and deletion of its regulatory subunit NEMO in hepatocytes (NEMODeltahepa) triggers chronic inflammation and spontaneous hepatocellular carcinoma development. We show that NEMODeltahepa mice were resistant to Fas-mediated apoptosis but hypersensitive to tumor necrosis factor-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL) as the result of a strong up-regulation of its receptor DR5 on hepatocytes. Additionally, natural killer (NK) cells, the main source of TRAIL, were activated in NEMODeltahepa livers. Interestingly, depletion of the NK1.1+ cells promoted a significant reduction of liver inflammation and an improvement of liver histology in NEMODeltahepa mice. Furthermore, hepatocyte-specific NEMO deletion strongly sensitized the liver to concanavalin A (ConA)-mediated injury. The critical role of the NK cell/TRAIL axis in NEMODeltahepa livers during ConA hepatitis was further confirmed by selective NK cell depletion and adoptive transfer of TRAIL-deficient-/- mononuclear cells. Our results uncover an essential mechanism of NEMO-mediated protection of the liver by preventing NK cell tissue damage via TRAIL/DR5 signaling. As this mechanism is important in human liver diseases, NEMODeltahepa mice are an interesting tool to give insight into liver pathophysiology and to develop future therapeutic strategies.
###end p 2
###begin p 3
###xml 152 168 148 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Luo et al., 2005</xref>
The transcription factor NF-kappaB is essential for liver homeostasis, as it is involved in controlling the balance between survival and death signals (Luo et al., 2005). Upon ligand binding, distinct receptors stimulate the IKK complex (IKK1/IKKalpha, IKK2/IKKbeta, and IKKgamma/NEMO), which initiates NF-kappaB activation. The regulatory subunit IKKgamma/NEMO is crucial during this process, as NEMO deletion results in complete NF-kappaB inhibition and embryonic lethality.
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 239 258 235 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Luedde et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 347 366 343 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Beraza et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 567 576 563 568 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 578 582 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Hepatocyte-specific deletion of NEMO and its consequent NF-kappaB inhibition has detrimental effects on liver homeostasis, as it promotes spontaneous steatohepatitis, fibrosis progression, and development of hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC; Luedde et al., 2007). Additionally, NEMO deletion in hepatocytes promotes severe TNF-mediated liver damage (Beraza et al., 2007). However, the impact of other TNF-related cytokines on acute liver injury and their contribution to the development of the strong parenchymal damage observed in hepatocyte-specific NEMO-deleted (NEMODeltahepa) mice remains unknown.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 174 194 174 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">Schutze et al., 2008</xref>
###xml 294 316 294 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib19">Ogasawara et al., 1993</xref>
###xml 502 518 502 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib30">Zou et al., 2007</xref>
FasL and TNF-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL) belong to the TNF family of cytokines, as they signal through death receptors which contain cytoplasmic death domains (Schutze et al., 2008). Activation of the FasL/Fas system leads to massive apoptotic cell death and fulminant hepatitis (Ogasawara et al., 1993). More recently, liver steatosis has been shown to hypersensitize hepatocytes to Fas-mediated apoptosis, contributing to the progression of steatohepatitis and end-stage liver disease (Zou et al., 2007).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 179 195 175 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Luo et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 425 445 421 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Higuchi et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 447 465 443 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Malhi et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 584 602 580 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Malhi et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 754 780 750 776 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib3">Guebre-Xabier et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 981 999 969 987 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Smyth et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 574 582 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
TRAIL, via its receptor DR5, selectively induces apoptosis of transformed and virally infected hepatocytes, and NF-kappaB-dependent signaling counteracts its detrimental effects (Luo et al., 2004). The cellular responses to TRAIL have been related to the inflammatory status of the liver, and DR5 up-regulation can be mediated by free fatty acids and bile acids, indicating a link between fat metabolism and TRAIL signaling (Higuchi et al., 2004; Malhi et al., 2007). This is supported by findings showing high levels of liver DR5 expression in nonalcoholic steatohepatitis patients (Malhi et al., 2007). Additionally, fatty liver disease activates the innate immune system by triggering NKT cells, promoting an unbalanced Th1/Th2 response in the liver (Guebre-Xabier et al., 2000). This contributes to create a proinflammatory environment where high IFN-gamma expression is found. Furthermore, IFN-gamma produced by activated NKT cells strongly induces NK cells to express TRAIL (Smyth et al., 2001).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 123 132 123 128 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 344 353 340 345 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 133 137 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Together, these data suggest that FasL and TRAIL could be involved in mediating the severe liver phenotype observed in NEMODeltahepa mice. In the present study, we aimed to characterize the implication of these cytokines and to uncover the pathophysiological mechanisms contributing to liver inflammation and further disease progression in NEMODeltahepa animals.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 24 33 24 29 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 271 280 267 272 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 423 432 415 420 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 34 38 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 281 285 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 433 437 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We demonstrate that NEMODeltahepa mice are resistant to Fas-mediated apoptosis, whereas they are hypersensitive to TRAIL-mediated cell death. Moreover, we highlight an essential role for NK cells, the main producers of TRAIL, in the spontaneous phenotype observed in NEMODeltahepa mice, as interfering with this cascade significantly improved liver homeostasis and attenuated the impact of T cell-mediated hepatitis in NEMODeltahepa mice.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 148 151 148 151 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">f/f</sup>
###xml 165 174 165 170 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 337 356 329 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Luedde et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 411 430 403 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Luedde et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 437 446 429 434 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 695 704 683 688 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 747 756 731 736 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 1090 1099 1070 1075 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 79 83 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 180 184 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 705 709 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 757 761 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1100 1104 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To better characterize the role of NEMO in hepatocytes, we generated NEMO loxP mice and crossed them with alfp-Cre transgenic animals to create NEMOf/f (WT) and NEMODeltahepa (KO) mice (). Hepatocyte-specific NEMO deletion led to a complete lack of NF-kappaB activation after TNF stimulation, confirming our previous results (Fig. S1 C; Luedde et al., 2007). Additionally, in consonance with our previous data (Luedde et al., 2007), NEMODeltahepa animals exhibited spontaneously elevated TNF and TNFR1 levels, although no significant differences were found in TNFR2 expression (Fig. S1 D). Immunohistochemistry (IHC) confirmed higher TNF expression in the nonparenchymal cell compartment in NEMODeltahepa mice (Fig. S1 D). Moreover, 12-wk-old NEMODeltahepa mice displayed high ALT levels (Fig. S1 E), accompanied by signs of nonalcoholic steatohepatitis, activation of progenitor cells, profuse inflammation, and features of chronic liver damage such as hepatocyte dysplasia and anisokaryosis (Fig. S1 F, top). The combination of these events contributed to HCC development in 1-yr-old NEMODeltahepa mice, as indicated by liver tumors and higher liver weight/body weight ratio (Fig. S1, F [bottom] and G).
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 139 158 139 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Beraza et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 350 359 350 355 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 360 364 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Hepatocyte-specific NEMO deletion promotes hypersensitivity to exogenously administered TNF; however, the animals survived this challenge (Beraza et al., 2007). As NEMO deletion may also contribute to amplifying liver damage triggered by other TNF-related cytokines, we sought to investigate their implication in mediating the liver phenotype of NEMODeltahepa mice.
###end p 11

###xml 165 168 161 164 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">f/f</sup>

Fas activation promotes acute hepatic failure (Ogasawara et al., 1993). Hence, stimulation with an anti-Fas mAb (Jo2, 0.5 microg/g) resulted in the death of all NEMOf/f mice within 10 h (Fig. 1 A). Unexpectedly, NEMODeltahepa animals were less sensitive to Jo2, as 90% of the mice survived (Fig. 1 A). Macroscopic view confirmed that NEMODeltahepa livers remained unaffected while NEMOf/f showed significant blood accumulation and a higher liver weight/body weight ratio (Fig. 1 B). Accordingly, NEMOf/f mice showed clear signs of fulminant hepatitis, as high ALT levels, infiltrating PMN and RBC, necrosis, and apoptotic bodies were observed (Fig. 1, C and D). In contrast, we failed to detect any significant effects of Jo2 in NEMODeltahepa mice, as only the preexisting elevated ALT levels and parenchymal damage, caused by the spontaneous phenotype of these mice, was observed (Fig. 1, C and D). TUNEL assay, caspase 8, and caspase 3 activity confirmed resistance to Fas-induced apoptosis in NEMODeltahepa mice (Fig. 1, E and F).

These data may seem contradictory to reports showing that NF-kappaB inhibition sensitizes hepatocytes to Fas apoptosis (Hatano et al., 2000). However, as we previously described, NF-kappaB is one of the main factors responsible for activating Fas transcription (Kuhnel et al., 2000). Thus, we hypothesized that NEMODeltahepa mice might have a defective turnover of this receptor, thereby promoting resistance to Jo2 apoptosis. In agreement with this, NEMODeltahepa mice exhibited reduced levels of Fas (25% less) and impaired up-regulation of Fas after Jo2 (Fig. 1 G). IHC confirmed these results, as Fas staining was almost negative on NEMODeltahepa mice after Jo2 (Fig. 1 G). Messenger RNA (mRNA) analysis on isolated primary hepatocytes corroborated the inability of NEMODeltahepa mice to express Fas (Fig. 1 H), which led to impaired NF-kappaB activation on these cells (not depicted).

Thus, we next investigated the potential implication of TRAIL in the initiation and progression of chronic liver damage in our NEMO-deficient mice. As expected, low doses of TRAIL had no effect on NEMOf/f mice, as ALT levels and liver architecture remained unaffected (Fig. 2, A and B). In clear contrast, TRAIL had a strong impact on NEMODeltahepa mice, as a significant rise in serum ALT and massive liver damage, indicated by profuse PMN infiltration, necrosis, and apoptosis, were observed (Fig. 2, A and B). TUNEL assay and caspase 3 activity confirmed the severity of TRAIL-mediated apoptosis on NEMODeltahepa mice (Fig. 2, C and D). Previous work indicated that TRAIL induced JNK activation, leading to cellular apoptosis (Herr et al., 1999). Accordingly, we found that TRAIL promoted strong JNK activation in NEMODeltahepa livers, whereas it remained inactive in NEMOf/f mice (Fig. 2 E). Moreover, TRAIL administration promoted a significant induction of TNF in NEMODeltahepa mice that was confirmed by IHC, uncovering the Kupffer cells as its main source (Fig. 2 F). These results suggest that hepatocyte-NEMO deletion promotes hypersensitivity to TRAIL-mediated apoptosis; hence, we next attempted to elucidate the potential mechanisms underlying this effect.

Interestingly, DR5 was highly expressed in livers of untreated NEMODeltahepa mice, where it was mainly present in hepatocytes (Fig. 2 G). This was confirmed in vitro, where NEMODeltahepa hepatocytes showed an eightfold increase in DR5 expression and a complete inability to activate NF-kappaB in response to TRAIL in contrast to WT cells (). NEMODeltahepa livers had significantly reduced numbers of NK and NKT cells compared with NEMOf/f littermates (Fig. 3 A). Hence, NK1.1+ cells from NEMODeltahepa mice exhibited significantly higher levels of annexin V when compared with NEMOf/f mice (Fig. 3 B). Activation of NK cells involves strong production of proinflammatory cytokines, like IFN-gamma and IL-12, promoting their cytotoxic activity and apoptosis (Vivier et al., 2008). ###xml 231 240 231 236 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 273 279 269 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 349 358 345 350 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 452 460 444 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig5">Fig. 5 I</xref>
###xml 523 526 515 518 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/+</sup>
###xml 539 548 531 536 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 635 638 623 626 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 653 656 641 644 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 662 663 650 651 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 718 727 706 711 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 766 774 750 758 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig5">Fig. 5 I</xref>
###xml 241 245 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 359 363 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 728 732 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
During ConA-mediated hepatitis, JNK activation is related to the degree of apoptosis and the severity of the cellular stress (Trautwein et al., 1998). Additionally, we showed the detrimental effects of the TRAIL-JNK cascade in NEMODeltahepa mice both in vivo and in vitro (Fig. 2 and Fig. S2). Accordingly, strong JNK activation was observed in NEMODeltahepa mice after ConA, whereas it was clearly blunted after NK1.1 mAb despite ConA administration (Fig. 5 I). JNK activation was restored after adoptive transfer of TRAIL+/+ MNCs in NEMODeltahepa livers after ConA despite NK1.1 mAb treatment. On the contrary, transfer of both TRAIL-/- MNCs and TRAIL-/- NK1.1+ cells maintained the resistance of NK1.1-depleted NEMODeltahepa mice to ConA-mediated JNK activation (Fig. 5 I).
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 233 252 233 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Toyabe et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 412 421 412 417 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 597 605 593 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig5">Fig. 5 A</xref>
###xml 829 838 825 830 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 1215 1233 1203 1221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Smyth et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 1235 1254 1223 1242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Takeda et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 1380 1389 1368 1373 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 422 426 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 839 843 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1390 1394 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Previous studies using a NK1.1 mAb described that NKT cells are an essential cell subset responsible for ConA-mediated liver injury as selective depletion of NK cells with an anti-asialo GM1 antibody had no impact on ConA hepatitis (Toyabe et al., 1997). However, our present data strongly support the hypothesis that NK cell-derived TRAIL plays a key role in the progression of ConA-induced liver damage in NEMODeltahepa mice. Accordingly, we observed that NK cell depletion with asialo-GM1 mAb significantly reduced ALT levels (2.17-fold; ), a comparable effect to that observed with NK1.1 mAb (Fig. 5 A; 2.56-fold reduction). Accordingly, hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) and TUNEL assay revealed significant protection against ConA after NK cell depletion (Fig. S4, B and C), accompanied by reduced CCL5 in asialo-GM1-treated NEMODeltahepa mice. Interestingly, despite the clear protection elicited by NK cell depletion, we found high IL-4 mRNA expression in asialo-GM1 mAb-treated KOs (Fig. S4 D) suggesting remnant activation of NKT cells. Moreover, downregulation of serum IFN-gamma levels supports the implication of this cytokine in the activation and production of TRAIL from NK cells in an autocrine fashion (Smyth et al., 2001; Takeda et al., 2001). This data strongly supports the essential role of TRAIL-producing NK cells in mediating the dramatic impact of ConA on NEMODeltahepa mice.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 588 606 588 606 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Malhi et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 681 690 681 686 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 751 771 747 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Higuchi et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 918 933 914 929 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib10">Li et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 970 979 966 971 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 691 695 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 980 984 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Our present study uncovers a new mechanism by which hepatocyte-specific NEMO deletion contributes to spontaneous liver inflammation and injury. We show that NEMO deletion sensitizes the liver to TRAIL-mediated apoptosis and this contributes to disease progression. We propose a dual mechanism consisting of up-regulation of DR5 on hepatocytes and the activation of TRAIL-producing NK cells to cause the detrimental effects of NEMO deletion in the liver. The first mechanism is supported by studies showing that steatosis and free fatty acids promote up-regulation of DR5 mRNA expression (Malhi et al., 2007). Additionally, the profuse bile acid accumulation found in livers of NEMODeltahepa mice (unpublished data) may further promote DR5 expression (Higuchi et al., 2004). In addition to increased DR5 expression, fatty liver disease also triggers NKT cell activation, leading to a proinflammatory cytokine response (Li et al., 2005), which we also observed in our NEMODeltahepa mice.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 125 143 121 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Smyth et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 488 508 480 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib1">Dobashi et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 510 536 502 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib3">Guebre-Xabier et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 616 625 608 613 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 626 630 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Additionally, it has been shown that NKT cells promote NK cell activation and TRAIL production through IFN-gamma expression (Smyth et al., 2001). At present, the relevance of intestinal bacterial products transported into the liver through the blood stream and its role in NK and NKT cell activation is unclear in this model. However, this mechanism could also be relevant, as LPS induces macrophages to produce IL-12, triggering IFN-gamma production by NKT cells in the absence of IL-4 (Dobashi et al., 1999; Guebre-Xabier et al., 2000). This scenario closely resembles the liver environment found in untreated NEMODeltahepa mice.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 322 331 318 323 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;hepa</sup>
###xml 332 336 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 393 399 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Our present work strengthens the essential role of hepatocyte NEMO/NF-kappaB to maintain liver homeostasis because absence of NEMO in these cells activates the innate immune system, thus promoting liver damage. The cascade of molecular events leading from steatohepatitis to fibrosis and tumor development observed in NEMODeltahepa mice reflects the pathogenesis of liver cirrhosis and HCC in humans. Further work in this model could yield novel therapeutic strategies targeting the NK cell/TRAIL axis to modulate chronic liver disease progression.
###end p 37
