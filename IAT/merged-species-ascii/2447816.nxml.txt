A nonsense mutation in CRYGC associated with autosomal dominant congenital nuclear cataract in a Chinese family

###begin title 2
Purpose
###end title 2
###begin p 3
To identify the genetic defect associated with autosomal dominant congenital nuclear cataract in a Chinese family.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 224 229 224 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 234 239 234 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGD</italic>
Family history and phenotypic data were recorded, and the phenotypes were documented by slit lamp photography. The genomic DNA was extracted from peripheral blood leukocytes. All the exons and flanking intronic sequences of CRYGC and CRYGD were amplified by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) and screened for mutation by direct DNA sequencing. Structural models of the wild type and mutant gammaC-crystallin were generated and analyzed by .
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 36 41 36 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 46 51 46 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGD</italic>
###xml 152 157 152 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
Sequencing of the coding regions of CRYGC and CRYGD showed the presence of a heterozygous C>A transversion at c.327 of the coding sequence in exon 3 of CRYGC (c.327C>A), which results in the substitution of a wild type cysteine to a nonsense codon (C109X). One and a half Greek key motifs at the COOH-terminus were found to be absent in the structural model of the mutant truncated gammaC-crystallin.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusions
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 29 34 29 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
A novel nonsense mutation in CRYGC was detected in a Chinese family with consistent autosomal dominant congenital nuclear cataract, providing clear evidence of a relationship between the genotype and the corresponding cataract phenotype.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Introduction
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 386 387 386 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 388 389 388 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 577 578 577 578 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 579 580 579 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 731 732 731 732 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 366 374 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 474 482 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Hereditary congenital cataract (OMIM ) is an opacification of the eye lens that frequently results in visual impairment or even blindness during infancy or early childhood. Despite the great advances in the clinical management of cataracts as well as a better understanding of lens structure and function, congenital cataract remains a leading cause of blindness in children worldwide [1,2]. Irreversible visual loss can result if prompt treatment is not performed on these patients. Congenital cataracts are considered to be both phenotypically and genetically heterogeneous [3-5]. The water-soluble lens crystallins account for nearly 90% of the total lens proteins and play essential roles in maintaining the lens transparency [6]. Therefore, crystallins are good candidate genes for congenital cataract.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 135 140 120 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGA</italic>
###xml 141 146 126 131 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGF</italic>
###xml 153 158 138 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 171 176 156 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGD</italic>
###xml 248 249 229 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 250 251 231 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 393 394 370 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 555 556 528 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 557 559 530 532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 588 593 561 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 598 603 571 576 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGD</italic>
###xml 684 686 657 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 687 689 660 662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 778 780 747 749 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 781 783 750 752 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 855 860 824 829 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGD</italic>
###xml 935 937 904 906 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 240 246 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 517 522 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 543 548 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Crystallins are subdivided into alpha-, beta-, and gamma-crystallins with the gamma-crystallin gene cluster subdivided into six genes, CRYGA-CRYGF. Only CRYGC (OMIM ) and CRYGD (OMIM ) are known to encode abundant lens gamma-crystallins in humans [7,8]. The gamma-crystallins have two domains with each domain composed of two exceptionally stable protein structures called "Greek key" motifs [9]. The gamma-crystallins are monomeric with a molecular mass of 21 kDa and comprise about 40% of the total proteins in the mouse lens and 25% in the human lens [6,10]. As reported, mutations in CRYGC and CRYGD have been identified to cause isolated autosomal dominant congenital cataracts [11,12] as a result of altered stability, association, and/or solubility of gamma-crystallins [13-16]. Indeed, in our previous study, we reported heterozygous mutations in CRYGD in a four-generation Chinese family with distinct fasciculiform cataract [17].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 164 169 164 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
In the present study, we investigated a large Chinese family with autosomal dominant congenital nuclear cataract and detected a novel chain-termination mutation in CRYGC that cosegregated with the disease in the family.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Methods
###end title 14
###begin title 15
###xml 0 8 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Patients</span>
Patients and clinical data
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 465 473 465 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 198 209 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participant</span>
A family of three generations was ascertained through the Eye Center of the 2nd Affiliated Hospital (Medical College of Zhejiang University, Hangzhou, China). Appropriate informed consent from each participant was obtained in accordance with the Zhejiang Institutional Review Board, and the study protocol adhered to the guidelines of the Declaration of Helsinki. Thirteen individuals (seven affected and six unaffected) from the family were enrolled in the study (Figure 1). Affected status was determined by a history of cataract extraction or ophthalmologic examination on presentation including visual function, slit lamp examination, and fundus examination with the dilated pupil. The phenotype was documented by slit lamp photography. Fifty subjects without diagnostic features of congenital cataract were recruited from the Chinese Han population in our medical examination center to serve as normal controls.

###begin title 18
Genomic DNA preparation and molecular analysis
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 148 150 148 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 242 246 242 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYG</italic>
###xml 261 266 261 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 271 276 271 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGD</italic>
###xml 375 380 375 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 385 390 385 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGD</italic>
###xml 476 483 476 483 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 36 44 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 236 241 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 394 402 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 800 808 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Blood specimens (5 ml) from all the patients and available family members were collected in EDTA. Genomic DNA was isolated as previously described [18]. Since the number of mutations leading to dominant cataracts was fairly high in the human CRYG gene cluster, CRYGC and CRYGD were taken as a priority to be screened as the candidate genes. The exons and flanking regions of CRYGC and CRYGD in patients II:6 and III:4 were amplified and sequenced using the primers listed in (Table 1). The cycling conditions for PCR were 38 cycles of 95 degreesC for 25 s, 55 degreesC for 25 s and 72 degreesC for 35 s, preceded by 5 min at 95 degreesC and followed by a final elongation step at 72 degreesC for 10 min. Any interesting sequence variation of a mutation suspect was later confirmed in the rest of the patients and unaffected family members by bidirectional sequencing of the particular exon.

###begin title 23
Results
###end title 23
###begin title 24
Clinical evaluation
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 308 316 308 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 517 525 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
We identified isolated autosomal dominant congenital nuclear cataract in a three-generation Chinese family. Opacification of the lens was bilateral and consistent in all of the affected individuals. All embryonal, fetal, and infantile nuclei of the lens were opacified while the cortex remained transparent (Figure 2). Visual acuity ranged from light perception to 0.15 in the unoperated eyes and from 0.20 to 0.02 in the eyes that had undergone iridectomy during childhood. Obvious nystagmus was observed in all the patients except the 10-month-old proband who received phacoemulsification surgery in both eyes on presentation. There was no history of other ocular or related systemic abnormalities in the family aside from age-related changes.
###end p 25

###begin title 27
Mutation analysis
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 161 166 161 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 244 252 244 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f3">Figure 3</xref>
Direct sequencing was performed to cover exons and flanking intron-exon boundary sequences. A heterozygous C>A transversion was identified at c.327 in exon 3 of CRYGC in all the affected members but not in any of the unaffected family members (Figure 3). This mutation resulted in the substitution of a wild type cysteine to a nonsense codon (C109X). The variant was completely absent in 100 chromosomes of 50 unrelated controls.

###begin title 34
Discussion
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 75 80 75 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 624 626 616 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r23">23</xref>
###xml 627 629 619 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
###xml 502 507 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In the present study, we detected a novel mutation (c.327C>A) in exon 3 of CRYGC in a Chinese family with autosomal dominant congenital nuclear cataract. The cataract phenotype was consistent among all the affected family members, providing a clear relationship between the genotype and the corresponding cataract phenotype. The opacification in the nuclei but not in the cortex could be explained by the fact that monomeric gammaC-crystallin, the major type of gamma-crystallin expressed in the young human lens, is synthesized in the early life span and localized only in the central regions of the mature/aging eye lens [23,24].
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 36 41 36 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 90 97 90 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t2">Table 2</xref>
###xml 100 102 100 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 103 105 103 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 106 108 106 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r27">27</xref>
###xml 432 434 428 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
###xml 681 689 652 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f4">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 766 774 733 741 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6</xref>
To our knowledge, four mutations in CRYGC have been reported in the literature (listed in Table 2) [11,25-27]. The mutation detected in our present study, c.327C>A, creates a premature stop codon (C109X) and results in an in-frame stop codon at nucleotide 75 of exon 3 that may cause a truncation of 66 amino acids from the COOH-terminus of gammaC-crystallin. The secondary structure predicted by the Protein Prediction program () [28] shows that there are 16 beta-strands (beta1-beta16) in gammaC-crystallin. The Cys109 residue located between the beta10-strand and beta11-strand is replaced by a nonsense codon, resulting in the loss of six beta-strands after the beta10-strand (Figure 4). Consequently the highly symmetric structure of gammaC-crystallin is lost (Figure 6).

###begin p 40
###xml 606 608 594 596 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
###xml 20 25 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 123 128 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 210 215 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Thus far, wild type human gammaC-crystallin has not been crystallized. Therefore, homology models for wild type and mutant human gammaC-crystallin are usually built based on the X-ray determined coordinates of mouse gammaC-crystallin chain A. The C109X mutation interferes with the formation of two COOH-terminal Greek key motifs. Although the function of the Greek key motifs has not been elaborated in detail, computer-based analysis suggests that it may be responsible for particular protein-protein interactions in the lens, and it is postulated to be critical in the maintenance of lens transparency [29].
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 192 194 188 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r30">30</xref>
###xml 195 197 191 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r31">31</xref>
###xml 414 415 402 403 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 470 472 454 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r32">32</xref>
###xml 647 649 627 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 807 809 783 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 810 812 786 788 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
###xml 435 440 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
It is reported that self-aggregation or quaternary structural alteration of gamma-crystallin is responsible for the phenotypic association with lens opacification as well as cataractogenesis [30,31]. The truncated gammaC-crystallin may change the folding properties of gammaC-crystallin as it has been shown in a previous investigation that the COOH-terminal domain folds before and nucleates the folding of the NH2-terminal domain in human gammaD-crystallin refolding [32]. The relatively loose or partially unfolded structure of mutant gammaC-crystallin may be susceptible to aggregation and insolubilization, which leads to cataract formation [13]. Another possible consequence of the C109X mutation may be related to the disturbances of the interactions between gammaC-crystallin and other crystallins [16,33]. The truncated gammaC-crystallin in the present study may cause a decrease or even complete loss of the ability to interact with other crystallins and may result in congenital cataract.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 57 62 57 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
In conclusion, the novel nonsense mutation (c.327C>A) in CRYGC in this Chinese family is associated with isolated autosomal dominant congenital nuclear cataract, giving evidence of a clear relationship between the genotype and the corresponding cataract phenotype. The possible influence of the mutation on the structure as well as the function of gammaC-crystallin will require further investigation.
###end p 42
