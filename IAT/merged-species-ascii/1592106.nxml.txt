M. leprae inhibits apoptosis in THP-1 cells by downregulation of Bad and Bak and upregulation of Mcl-1 gene expression

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 9 30 9 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium leprae </italic>
###xml 277 287 277 287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 386 396 386 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 528 538 528 538 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 557 566 557 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 9 29 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">Mycobacterium leprae</span>
###xml 277 286 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 386 395 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 528 537 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 557 569 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
Virulent Mycobacterium leprae interfere with host defense mechanisms such as cytokine activation and apoptosis. The mitochondrial pathway of apoptosis is regulated by the Bcl-2 family of proteins. Expression of Fas ligand and apoptotic proteins is found in leprosy lesions and M. leprae has been shown to activate pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 genes, Bak and Bax. However, the mechanism by which M. leprae modulates apoptosis is as yet unclear. We investigated expression of apoptotic genes in THP-1 monocytes in response to infection by M. leprae and non-pathogenic M. bovis BCG.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 0 10 0 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 433 443 433 443 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 534 547 534 547 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium</italic>
###xml 644 654 644 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 864 874 864 874 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 1212 1222 1212 1222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 433 442 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 644 653 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 864 873 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 1212 1221 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
M. leprae did not induce apoptosis in THP-1 cells, while BCG induced a significant loss of cell viability by 18 h post-infection at both (multiplicity of infection) MOI-10 and 20, with an increase by 48 h. BCG-induced cell death was accompanied by characteristic apoptotic DNA laddering in cells. Non-viable BCG had a limited effect on host cell death suggesting that BCG-induced apoptosis was a function of mycobacterial viability. M. leprae also activated lower levels of TNF-alpha secretion and TNF-alpha mRNA expression than BCG. Mycobacterium-induced activation of apoptotic gene expression was determined over a time course of infection. M. leprae reduced Bad and Bak mRNA expression by 18 h post-stimulation, with a further decrease at 48 h. Outcome of cell viability is determined by the ratio between pro- and anti-apoptotic proteins present in the cell. M. leprae infection resulted in downregulation of gene expression ratios, Bad/Bcl-2 mRNA by 39% and Bak/Bcl-2 mRNA by 23%. In contrast, live BCG increased Bad/Bcl-2 mRNA (29 %) but had a negligible effect on Bak/Bcl-2 mRNA. Heat killed BCG induced only a negligible (1-4 %) change in mRNA expression of either Bak/Bcl-2 or Bad/Bcl-2. Additionally, M. leprae upregulated the expression of anti-apoptotic gene Mcl-1 while, BCG downregulated Mcl-1 mRNA.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 138 148 138 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 138 147 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
This study proposes an association between mycobacterium-induced apoptosis in THP-1 cells and the regulation of Bcl-2 family of proteins. M. leprae restricts apoptosis in THP-1 cells by downregulation of Bad and Bak and upregulation of Mcl-1 mRNA expression.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 30 51 30 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium leprae </italic>
###xml 55 71 55 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 402 403 402 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 404 405 404 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 483 484 475 476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 485 486 477 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 565 581 557 573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 585 594 577 586 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 651 652 643 644 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 685 695 677 687 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 804 813 792 801 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 825 826 813 814 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 917 918 905 906 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 997 998 981 982 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1053 1055 1037 1039 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 30 50 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">Mycobacterium leprae</span>
###xml 55 70 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 565 580 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 585 593 <span type="species:ncbi:29425|species:ncbi:476|species:ncbi:28903|species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 685 694 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 804 816 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
Virulent mycobacteria such as Mycobacterium leprae and M. tuberculosis manipulate host cells to persist within them. Apoptosis, or programmed cell death is essential for the homeostatic regulation of cells, restriction of intracellular pathogens and also for stimulation of the host adaptive immune response. Virulent mycobacteria modulate host cell apoptosis to create a protected niche within cells [1-3], and downregulate protective host cytokines such as, TNFalpha and IFNgamma [4,5] to reduce effector T cell and macrophage responses to the pathogen. Virulent M. tuberculosis and M. bovis induce lower levels of apoptosis than avirulent strains [6]. Virulent mycobacteria such as M. leprae induce reduced activation of pro-inflammatory cytokine such as TNFalpha, as compared with the non-pathogenic M. bovis BCG strain [7]. The apoptotic response to mycobacteria is found to be dependent on cytokine activation [8] whereby, TNFalpha has been shown to activate mycobacterium-induced apoptosis[9], while IL-10 downregulates apoptosis in macrophages [10].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 0 10 0 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 441 443 441 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 570 572 566 568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 655 657 651 653 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 812 822 800 810 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 904 906 888 890 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 937 939 917 919 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 645 653 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 812 821 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
M. leprae causes a disease spectrum ranging from disseminated multibacillary (MB) leprosy to restricted paucibacillary (PB) disease. T cell and macrophage responses are less affected in PB leprosy than in MB, where effector T cell and macrophage responses are severely compromised. Multibacillary leprosy granulomas show increased expression of Fas ligand which may protect these sites from attack by Fas-expression cytotoxic T lymphocytes [11]. Production of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNFalpha is found to be greater in PB disease as compared with MB disease [12]. Although apoptotic proteins are activated in leprosy lesions from all patients [13], reports indicate that apoptosis is greater in PB disease, correlating with increased TNFalpha secretion. A role for TNFalpha is further corroborated as M. leprae induced apoptosis is reduced by treatment with TNFalpha inhibitor pentoxyfylline [14] or anti-TNFalpha antibodies [15].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 361 363 361 363 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L </sub>
###xml 522 524 522 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 836 838 836 838 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L </sub>
###xml 945 946 945 946 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L</sub>
Regulation of programmed cell death occurs via either the intrinsic pathway, involving mitochondrial release of cytochrome C and activation of caspase 9, or the extrinsic pathway, involving the stimulation of death receptors expressed on the cell surface and activation of caspase 8. The Bcl 2-family of proteins comprise of anti-apoptotic members; Bcl-2, Bcl-xL and Mcl-1, and pro-apoptotic members; Bad, Bid, Bax and Bak. Pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 proteins located in the cytosol act as sensors of cellular stress and damage [16]. Upon activation, they relocate to the mitochondrial surface where anti-apoptotic proteins are located. Activation of Bax or Bad and blockade of anti-apoptotic Bcl-2 proteins are pivotal steps in the mitochondrial pathway of apoptosis. Bad positively regulates cell apoptosis by forming heterodimers with Bcl-xL and Bcl-2 and reversing their death repressor activity. Pro-apoptotic Bak is sequestered by Mcl-1 and Bcl-xL; but not Bcl-2 until displaced by Bid, Bad or Bak [17]. When there is an excess of pro apoptotic proteins, cells are more sensitive to apoptosis.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 95 111 95 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 173 175 173 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 188 190 188 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 221 223 221 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 234 235 234 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 250 260 250 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 344 346 344 346 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 381 391 381 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 95 110 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 250 259 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 381 390 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
Mycobacteria interfere with host apoptosis by modifying expression of Bcl-2 proteins. Virulent M. tuberculosis has been shown to repress apoptosis by upregulation of Bcl-2 [18] and Mcl-1 [19], and by deactivation of Bad [20] and Bax [2]. Previously, M. leprae has been shown to induce apoptosis in monocytes via induction of Bak and Bax genes [14]. However, the mechanism by which M. leprae influences host cell apoptosis is as yet unclear.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 130 132 130 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 182 184 182 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 185 187 185 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 248 258 248 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 339 348 339 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 248 257 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 287 292 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 339 351 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
The human acute monocytic leukemia cell line THP-1 has been established as a model to study mycobacterial growth and persistence [21] in addition to mycobacterium-induced apoptosis [22-24]. Here we have investigated the mechanism by which virulent M. leprae regulates apoptosis in THP-1 human monocytes as compared with the non-pathogenic M. bovis BCG vaccine strain.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
###xml 0 21 0 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium leprae </italic>
###xml 62 71 62 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 0 20 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">Mycobacterium leprae</span>
###xml 62 74 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
Mycobacterium leprae induces less cell death as compared with M. bovis BCG
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 0 10 0 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 221 235 221 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium </italic>
###xml 307 317 307 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 453 463 453 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 603 607 603 607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B&#8211;C</xref>
###xml 626 628 626 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 630 640 630 640 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 929 933 929 933 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1D&#8211;E</xref>
###xml 993 997 993 997 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2.A2</xref>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 307 316 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 453 462 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 630 639 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
M. leprae and BCG-induced death in THP-1 cells was evaluated by fluorescent viability staining. A time course of infection was carried out at 6, 18 and 48 h post-stimulation. Viability was determined after infection with Mycobacterium at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 10 and 20 in each case. Neither M. leprae nor BCG induced any significant loss in cell viability at the earlier 6 h time interval studied (data not shown). After 18 h of culture M. leprae did not cause any significant loss of viability in THP-1 cells at MOI-10 however, cell death was evident in cells infected at MOI-20 (Figs. 1B-C. As shown in Fig. 2A, M. leprae induced significant cell death induction at MOI-20 (P < 0.01) at 18 h but not at either MOI 10 or MOI-20 (P = 0.052) by the later 48 h time interval. In contrast, BCG infection of THP-1 cells resulted in a significant increase in cell death at both MOIs 10 and 20 at 18 h (P < 0.01) (Fig. 1D-E) and this effect was also evident at 48 h (P < 0.01) (Fig. 2.A2).
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 333 335 333 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 411 421 411 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 411 420 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
The pattern of genomic DNA fragmentation in mycobacterium infected cells was determined in order to investigate the cause of loss in cell viability observed at 18 h post-infection. BCG-infection of cells resulted in DNA laddering characteristic of apoptotic cells, with an increase in apoptosis with dose from MOI-10 to MOI-20 (Fig. 2B). In contrast, apoptotic laddering was not observed in cells infected with M. leprae (data not shown).
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 29 39 29 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 137 147 137 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 216 218 216 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 219 221 219 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 228 237 228 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 29 38 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 137 146 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 206 214 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 228 240 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
We employed gamma-irradiated M. leprae in our assays as this was the only strain available to us and we have previously used it to model M. leprae infections in cells from both healthy controls and leprosy patients [25,26]. The M. bovis BCG strain served as a comparison of a non-pathogenic species, while heat-killed BCG was employed as a control for BCG viability in the assays. Heat-killed BCG at the lower infection dose of MOI-10 did not cause any significant loss in THP-1 viability but did so at MOI-20 (P < 0.05) at 18 h. However, cell death was not induced by heat-killed BCG at 48 h post-infection (data not shown).
###end p 18
###begin title 19
###xml 0 10 0 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 47 56 43 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 47 59 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
M. leprae is a poorer inducer of TNFalpha than M. bovis BCG
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 69 78 65 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae</italic>
###xml 175 177 167 169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 267 269 259 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 350 352 338 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 353 355 341 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 358 368 346 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 455 457 439 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 476 486 460 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 674 684 650 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 1045 1055 1017 1027 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 1243 1253 1211 1221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 1362 1363 1326 1327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 69 78 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 358 367 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 476 485 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 674 683 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 1045 1054 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 1243 1252 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
A role for TNFalpha is indicated in mycobacterium-induced apoptosis. M. leprae-induced apoptosis in primary macrophages has been shown to be dependent on TNFalpha activation [14], while BCG-induced apoptosis in THP-1 cells is dependent on an endogenous TNF response [24]. Virulent mycobacteria are poorer inducers of TNFalpha than avirulent strains [27,28]. M. leprae has previously been shown to induce only negligible TNFalpha secretion in THP-1 cells [29]. We investigated M. leprae induced TNFalpha secretion and mRNA expression in THP-1 cells to determine whether the low level of apoptosis observed could be associated with a lack of TNFalpha activation in the assay. M. leprae and BCG -induced cytokine secretion was measured at 6, 18 and 48 h post-stimulation. BCG infection of cells induced TNFalpha secretion within 6 h of stimulation (mean values: BCG10, 40 pg/ml; BCG20, 80 pg/ml) with peak secretion at 18 h (mean values: BCG10, 238 pg/ml; BCG20, 299 pg/ml) and a decrease at 48 h (mean values: BCG10, 165 pg/ml; BCG20, 204 pg/ml). M. leprae did not induce any detectable TNFalpha secretion in THP-1 cells at either MOI 10 or 20 at 6 h, with negligible secretion (ML10, 0 pg/ml; ML20, 4 pg/ml) at the 18 h peak interval. However, M. leprae induced TNFalpha mRNA expression upon infection of cells but at a lower level than as compared with BCG (Fig 3).
###end p 20
###begin title 21
###xml 49 59 49 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 49 58 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
Bak and Bad gene expression is down-regulated in M. leprae infected THP-1 cells
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 0 10 0 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 153 155 153 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 230 239 230 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae</italic>
###xml 372 385 372 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium</italic>
###xml 609 610 606 607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 678 688 675 685 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>

###xml 901 903 898 900 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3A</xref>
###xml 906 916 903 913 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 1163 1165 1160 1162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3B</xref>

###xml 1723 1733 1717 1727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 1822 1832 1816 1826 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 1931 1936 1925 1930 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4A, D</xref>
###xml 2053 2058 2047 2052 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4B, E</xref>
###xml 2179 2184 2173 2178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4C, F</xref>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 230 239 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 678 687 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 737 746 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 906 915 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 1723 1732 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 1822 1831 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
M. leprae infection of peripheral blood monocytes has been shown to result in the activation of the pro-apoptotic protein Bad and induction of Bak mRNA [14]. In this study we did not observe apoptosis in THP-1 cells infected with M. leprae, while cells infected with BCG showed increased apoptosis. To investigate the mechanism responsible for this difference we measured Mycobacterium-induced mRNA expression of pro-apoptotic (Bad and Bak) and anti-apoptotic (Bcl-2) genes at 6, 18 and 48 h post infection. Gene expression was compared relative to that of the beta-actin housekeeping gene in each case. Fig. 3 illustrates the changes in gene expression observed in response to M. leprae and BCG infection of cells at MOIs of 10 and 20. M. leprae induced negligible effect on Bcl-2 mRNA at both infection doses, while Bad mRNA expression appeared to be reduced at MOI-10 but increased at MOI-20 (Fig. 3A). M. leprae downregulated Bak expression at both infection doses studied. In contrast, BCG infection of monocytes downregulated Bcl-2 mRNA but upregulated Bad mRNA expression at both MOIs 10 and 20, while it had negligible effect on Bak mRNA expression (Fig. 3B). The data shown is of one representative experiment and some variation is evident between background levels of mRNA expression in different experiments. To account for this, in each case mRNA levels for each gene were normalized to baseline levels of beta-actin within the same experiment. Further time course kinetics of apoptotic mRNA expression based on data from 3 independent experiments is presented in Fig. 4. The effect of heat-killed BCG on THP-1 cells is also included. Negligible change was observed in Bad or Bak gene expression in response to M. leprae or BCG (live and heat-killed) at the earlier 6 and 18 h time intervals. However by 48 h, M. leprae induced downregulation of mRNA expression both pro-apoptotic genes Bad (30 %) and Bak (26 %) (Fig. 4A, D). In contrast, live BCG induced negligible change in either Bad or Bak mRNA expression at 48 h post-infection (Fig. 4B, E). Killed BCG induced downregulation of Bad mRNA (34 %) but upregulation of Bak mRNA (46 %) at 48 h post-infection (Fig. 4C, F).
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 225 238 225 238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium</italic>
###xml 486 496 486 496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 518 519 518 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 522 532 522 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 690 694 690 694 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5A&#8211;B</xref>
###xml 702 712 702 712 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 486 495 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 522 531 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 702 711 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
Outcome of cellular viability/apoptosis is determined not only by the expression of specific pro- and anti-apoptotic genes, but is dependent on the ratio between pro and anti apoptotic genes. Therefore, to further understand Mycobacterium-induced change in host apoptotic gene expression we determined Bad/Bcl-2 and Bak/Bcl-2 expression ratios. We chose to evaluate gene expression at 18 h post-infection, the time interval at which differential cell death induction is evident between M. leprae and BCG (MOI-10 (Fig. 1). M. leprae infection of THP-1 cells resulted in a 39 % decrease in Bad/Bcl-2 and a 23 % reduction in Bak/Bcl-2 gene expression as compared with unstimulated cells (Fig. 5A-B) while M. leprae at MOI 20 caused a 54 % decrease in Bad/Bcl-2 and 15 % decrease in Bak/Bcl-2 mRNA expression (data not shown). BCG infection of cells at MOI-10 increased Bad/Bcl-2 by 29 % and reduced Bak/Bcl-2 mRNA expression by 4 %, with a further increase in Bad/Bcl-2 mRNA at MOI-20 and similar change in Bak/Bcl-2 mRNA. Killed BCG had a limited effect on Bad/Bcl-2 (1-13 %) and Bak/Bcl-2 (4-6 %) mRNA expression at MOIs 10 and 20 (data not shown).
###end p 23
###begin title 24
###xml 0 10 0 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
M. leprae upregulates Mcl-1 mRNA expression
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 12 28 12 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 134 136 134 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 168 178 168 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 245 255 245 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 326 340 326 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium-</italic>
###xml 595 597 595 597 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 638 648 638 648 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 775 785 775 785 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 986 987 986 987 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 12 27 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 168 177 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 245 254 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 460 465 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 582 587 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 638 647 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 775 784 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
Survival of M. tuberculosis infection in THP-1 cells has been linked to the upregulation of anti-apoptotic Bcl-2 family member Mcl-1 [19]. However, a role for Mcl-1 in M. leprae or BCG infection of cells is not known. Therefore, we investigated M. leprae and BCG induced Mcl-1 gene expression in THP-1 cells. A time course of Mycobacterium-induced Mcl-1 expression was determined at 6, 18 and 48h. Mcl-1 was evaluated relative to that of the housekeeping gene human ribosomal protein (HuPO), which has been shown to be optimal for real-time PCR quantification of gene expression in human cells [30]. At the earlier 6 h time point neither M. leprae nor BCG infection of THP-1 cells had any effect on Mcl-1 mRNA expression as compared with unstimulated cells (data not shown). M. leprae induced an increase in Mcl-1 expression by 2.9 % at 18 h post-infection with a further (12 %) increase at 48 h. In contrast, BCG induced a decrease in Mcl-1 mRNA by 15 % at 18 h and 12 % by 48 h (Fig. 6).
###end p 25
###begin title 26
Discussion
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 21 31 21 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 21 30 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
Our study shows that M. leprae inhibits apoptosis in the THP-1 monocytic cell line by downregulation of proapoptotic genes Bak and Bad and concomitant upregulation of anti-apoptotic Mcl-1 mRNA. In contrast, the increased apoptosis evidenced by BCG infection of THP-1 cells is coordinated by an increase in Bad mRNA expression relative to Bcl-2 and a downregulation of Mcl-1 mRNA.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 132 133 132 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 155 165 155 165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae-</italic>
###xml 289 299 289 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 696 706 696 706 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 817 827 817 827 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 155 164 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 289 298 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 696 705 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 817 826 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
Our data correlates with previous reports that virulent mycobacteria induce lower levels of cell death than avirulent mycobacteria [6]. We did not observe M. leprae-induced loss in cell viability in cells infected at MOI-10 at 18 post-infection. While, some THP-1 cell death was caused by M. leprae at MOI 20 by 18 h post-infection, this was not accompanied by the characteristic DNA laddering pattern found in apoptotic cells. Therefore, it is possible that the loss of viability observed here could be due to an alternate mechanism of cell death such as, necrosis, and could also be attributed to the increased mycobacterial load in the THP-1 cells. However, at the later time interval of 48 h M. leprae -induced cell death was not observed at either infection dose. This may be attributed to the time required for M. leprae -induced modification of host gene expression which facilitates persistence of the pathogen within cells. This is further evidenced by the time course changes in mRNA expression of the Bcl-2 family of proteins observed in this study.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 66 68 66 68 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 746 748 746 748 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
Cell death induced by BCG was dose dependent as shown previously [24]. BCG-induced apoptosis (as shown by DNA laddering of cells) increased with time from 18 to 48 h post-infection. Loss in cell viability was observed in response to heat-killed BCG at 18 h but was no longer apparent by 48 h. The earlier response to non-viable BCG observed at 18 h may be the result of non-specific binding and entry of the mycobacterium into the host cell which may trigger host defenses. Initial cell attachment and uptake of BCG into host macrophages has shown to be common amongst live and dead mycobacterium, with subsequent differences occurring later due to differential trafficking of live and dead organisms within host the endosomal/lysosomal pathway [31]. As BCG-induced apoptosis is most probably dependent on bacterial viability it is therefore only sustained in the case of live BCG.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 0 10 0 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae-</italic>
###xml 165 167 157 159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 183 192 175 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae</italic>
###xml 273 275 261 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 442 444 422 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 473 483 453 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 183 192 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 473 482 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
M. leprae-induced less TNFalpha secretion and gene expression than BCG did. A role for TNFalpha has been defined previously in BCG-induced apoptosis in THP-1 cells [24]. In addition, M. leprae-induced apoptosis in primary monocytes has been shown to be TNFalpha dependent [14], and inhibition of TNFalpha via treatment with anti-TNFalpha inhibitors or pentoxyfylline is found to reduce host inflammation and cell death in reactional leprosy [32]. Therefore, in the case of M. leprae infection, the reduced levels of TNFalpha activation coordinate with the downregulation of pro-apoptotic gene expression would contribute to apoptosis inhibition in the cells.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 9 19 9 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 304 306 301 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 452 467 449 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 577 579 574 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 632 635 629 632 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et </italic>
###xml 707 717 704 714 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 790 792 787 789 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 975 991 972 988 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 1051 1053 1048 1050 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 1086 1088 1083 1085 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 1102 1112 1099 1109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 1300 1302 1297 1299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 1303 1305 1300 1302 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">. </bold>
###xml 9 18 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 283 289 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 452 467 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 707 716 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 975 990 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1102 1111 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 1176 1184 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1220 1228 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1373 1381 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
We found M. leprae to downregulate expression of pro-apoptotic genes Bak and Bad relative to both beta-actin and anti-apoptotic Bcl-2. This correlates with previous studies where reduced Bax and increased Bcl-2 mRNA expression has been shown to be responsible for slowly progressive murine tuberculosis [33]. The role of Bad gene expression in determining outcome of cellular viability is further indicated where cell wall lipoarabinomannan (LAM) from M. tuberculosis-induces survival in monocytes by phosphorylation of the protein Bad, hence preventing it from binding Bcl-2 [22]. However, our work differs from that by Hernandez, et al. who demonstrate that infection of primary monocytes with irradiated M. leprae resulted in apoptosis due to activation of apoptotic genes, Bax and Bak [14]. This apparent discrepancy can be attributed to differential outcome of mycobacterial infection dependent on host cell type, as evidenced by the variation in apoptosis induction by M. tuberculosis observed in alveolar epithelial cells and U937 macrophages [20]. In addition, Hernandez et al [14] showed that M. leprae increases apoptosis in monocytes-derived macrophages of leprosy patients. However, macrophages from leprosy patients are more prone to apoptotic death than those from healthy individuals [15]. Therefore, the spontaneous activation of apoptotic genes in leprosy patients may make the cells more prone to extracellular triggers such as infection with mycobacteria.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 35 45 35 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 229 231 229 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 250 252 250 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 264 280 264 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 397 399 397 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 35 44 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
###xml 198 203 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 264 285 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
We demonstrate a role for Mcl-1 in M. leprae mediated inhibition of apoptosis in the THP-1 cells. The anti-apoptotic Bcl-2 family member gene Mcl-1 has been shown to play a role in cell survival of human polymorphonuclear cells [34] and neutrophils [35]. Virulent M. tuberculosis H37Rv has been shown to persist in THP-1 cells by upregulation of the anti-apoptotic Bcl-2 family member gene Mcl-1 [19]. Pro-apoptotic Bak is sequestered by Mcl-1 [17] therefore upregulation of Mcl-1 expression would result in a coordinate decrease in available Bak leading to further downregulation of apoptosis.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 198 199 198 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 237 239 237 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 415 425 415 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 415 424 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
We found BCG-induced apoptosis to be accompanied with increased expression of Bad/Bcl-2 mRNA. Previously, BCG-induced apoptosis has been shown to be a consequence of downregulated Bcl-2 expression [1] and also by activation of caspases [24]. We show for the first time in our study that BCG induces downregulation of anti-apoptotic Mcl-1 mRNA. Overall, our results indicate that the differential effect of virulent M. leprae and non-pathogenic BCG on host cellular survival is determined by the combined gene expression of pro- and anti-apoptotic genes Bad, Bak, Bcl-2 and Mcl-1.
###end p 33
###begin title 34
Conclusion
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 0 10 0 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae-</italic>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
M. leprae-induced downregulation of Bad and Bak genes coordinate with a reduced TNFalpha response and upregulation of Mcl-1 results in the inhibition of apoptosis in THP-1 monocytes. Therefore, leading to a more efficient mechanism for dissemination of leprosy infection.

###begin title 39
###xml 0 14 0 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium </italic>
Mycobacterium infection assays
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 371 373 368 370 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sup>
###xml 575 585 572 582 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. leprae </italic>
###xml 12 17 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 295 299 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 575 584 <span type="species:ncbi:1769">M. leprae</span>
The THP - 1 human macrophage-like cell line was acquired from the American Type Culture Collection, USA (TIB-202) and cultured in RPMI-1640 medium containing 2 mM L-glutamine, 10 mM HEPES, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, 4.5 g/L glucose, 1.5 g/L bicarbonate, supplemented with 10 % heat inactivated fetal calf serum and 0.05 mM beta-mercaptoethanol. Monocytes were seeded at 2 x 105 cells/well in 24 well plates and differentiated by stimulation with 20 ng/ml phorbol myristate acetate (PMA) for 18-20 h. Cells were subsequently washed with sterile PBS and infected with either BCG or M. leprae at a dose of 10 or 20 bacteria per cell. Supernatants were harvested at 6, 18 and 48 h as per experiment and stored at -70degreesC until measured.
