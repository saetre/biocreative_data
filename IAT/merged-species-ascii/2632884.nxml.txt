###begin article-title 0
Deletion of the Mitochondrial Flavoprotein Apoptosis Inducing Factor (AIF) Induces beta-Cell Apoptosis and Impairs beta-Cell Mass
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: FTS SK AB KM. Performed the experiments: FTS SK AA HK SG DB. Analyzed the data: FTS SK AA HK. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: DB AB KM. Wrote the paper: FTS AA KM.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 422 424 413 415 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 411 420 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Harlequin</span>
###xml 422 424 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 433 437 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Apoptosis is a hallmark of beta-cell death in both type 1 and type 2 diabetes mellitus. Understanding how apoptosis contributes to beta-cell turnover may lead to strategies to prevent progression of diabetes. A key mediator of apoptosis, mitochondrial function, and cell survival is apoptosis inducing factor (AIF). In the present study, we investigated the role of AIF on beta-cell mass and survival using the Harlequin (Hq) mutant mice, which are hypomorphic for AIF.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 49 51 49 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 579 581 564 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 685 687 667 669 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 49 51 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 59 63 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 116 120 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 122 124 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">WT</span>
###xml 163 167 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 579 581 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 589 593 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 637 639 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">WT</span>
###xml 640 644 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 685 687 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 695 699 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 790 792 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 800 804 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 898 903 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Immunohistochemical evaluation of pancreata from Hq mutant mice displayed much smaller islets compared to wild-type mice (WT). Analysis of beta-cell mass in these mice revealed a greater than 4-fold reduction in beta-cell mass together with an 8-fold increase in beta-cell apoptosis. Analysis of cell cycle dynamics, using BrdU pulse as a marker for cells in S-phase, did not detect significant differences in the frequency of beta-cells in S-phase. In contrast, double staining for phosphorylated Histone H3 and insulin showed a 3-fold increase in beta-cells in the G2 phase in Hq mutant mice, but no differences in M-phase compared to WT mice. This suggests that the beta-cells from Hq mutant mice are arrested in the G2 phase and are unlikely to complete the cell cycle. beta-cells from Hq mutant mice display increased sensitivity to hydrogen peroxide-induced apoptosis, which was confirmed in human islets in which AIF was depleted by siRNA. AIF deficiency had no effect on glucose stimulated insulin secretion, but the impaired effect of hydrogen peroxide on beta-cell function was potentiated.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Our results indicate that AIF is essential for maintaining beta-cell mass and for oxidative stress response. A decrease in the oxidative phosphorylation capacity may counteract the development of diabetes, despite its deleterious effects on beta-cell survival.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 132 135 126 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Donath1">[1]</xref>
Apoptosis of the insulin producing beta-cells and the decline in beta-cell mass is a major mechanism of the progression of diabetes [1]. In addition, the rapid loss of beta-cell function and the relative discrepancy between insulin demand and insulin secretion worsen the ability of the organism to maintain normoglycemia.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 88 91 88 91 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Wiederkehr1">[2]</xref>
###xml 93 96 93 96 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Wiederkehr2">[3]</xref>
###xml 651 654 648 651 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Krauss1">[4]</xref>
Important metabolites that regulate insulin secretion are generated in the mitochondria [2], [3]. The beta-cell mitochondria are key regulators of glucose stimulated insulin secretion, and several mitochondrial pathways are disabled in type 2 diabetes (T2DM), e.g. the glucose induced hyperpolarization of the mitochondrial membrane and the raise in the ATP/ADP ratio at high glucose. Diabetic islets also have increased UCP-2 expression, which uncouples mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, such that energy is wasted through heat, and cellular ATP synthesis is decreased, probably through an increased formation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) [4].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 321 324 318 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Maedler1">[5]</xref>
###xml 326 329 323 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Sakurai1">[6]</xref>
###xml 393 396 387 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Maechler1">[7]</xref>
###xml 594 597 585 588 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Maechler2">[8]</xref>
Besides regulating factors of insulin secretion, mitochondria play a prominent role in apoptosis. In beta-cells, elevated free fatty acids and production of ROS have been shown to induce apoptosis through the release of cytochrome c from the inner mitochondrial membrane to the cytosol, which triggers caspase activation [5], [6]. Mitochondria are the principal source of ROS in the beta-cell [7], which accumulates when the respiratory chain function is defective. The low expression of an antioxidant enzyme defense system makes the beta-cells particularly susceptible to an oxidative insult [8].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 143 146 143 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Susin1">[9]</xref>
###xml 224 227 224 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Susin1">[9]</xref>
###xml 229 233 229 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Cande1">[10]</xref>
###xml 319 323 319 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Liu1">[11]</xref>
###xml 422 426 422 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Porter1">[12]</xref>
###xml 637 639 637 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 962 966 962 966 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Klein1">[13]</xref>
###xml 393 398 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 626 635 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Harlequin</span>
###xml 637 639 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 648 653 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 661 665 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The apoptosis-inducing factor (AIF) is a mitochondrial intermembrane protein, which translocates to the cytoplasm and nucleus during apoptosis [9], where it binds to DNA triggering DNA fragmentation and nuclear condensation [9], [10]. In contrast to cytochrome c, AIF induces apoptosis in a caspase-independent fashion [11]. AIF is essential for mammalian development, knocking out AIF in the mouse is lethal before birth [12]. Therefore, the role of AIF as a death executor or as an oxidative stress scavenger is controversial. Evidence that AIF serves as free radical scavenger to prevent apoptosis came from studies on the Harlequin (Hq) mutant mouse. These mice have an 80% reduction in AIF protein due to a proviral insertion in the first intron of the AIF gene. They exhibit a reduced oxidative phosphorylation (OxPHOS) in the retina and in the brain, which entails increased oxidative stress and neuronal cell death through unscheduled cell cycle reentry [13].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 123 127 123 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Lowell1">[14]</xref>
###xml 129 133 129 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Pospisilik1">[15]</xref>
###xml 156 160 156 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Rabol1">[16]</xref>
###xml 257 261 257 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-DiMauro1">[17]</xref>
###xml 263 267 263 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Wallace1">[18]</xref>
###xml 78 83 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Defects in OxPHOS and mitochondrial energy metabolism have been implicated in human diseases, including insulin resistance [14], [15] and diabetes mellitus [16], neurodegenerative disorders such as Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, as well as aging [17], [18]. Given the different roles of AIF in maintaining a functional respiratory chain, protecting cells from oxidative stress, but also contributing to stress- induced cell-death and that important mitochondrial pathways control beta-cell pathophysiology, we asked the question what role does AIF play in the regulation of beta-cell survival, mass and insulin secretion?
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Materials and Methods
###end title 14
###begin title 15
Animal breeding and genotyping
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 78 81 78 81 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">w-J</sup>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Harlequin</span>
###xml 11 13 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 108 110 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 111 115 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 186 188 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">WT</span>
###xml 223 225 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
Harlequin (Hq) arose on a CF1 outbred stock and was transferred to a BGCBACa-Aw-J/A (B6CBA) background. The Hq mice were obtained from Jackson Laboratory (Bar Harbor, ME). For controls, WT littermates which do not have the Hq mutation (+/Y) were used. Animals were housed at 22degreesC with a 12-h light-dark cycle (lights on at 07:00) and allowed free access to water and chow according to the protocol approved by the UCLA Chancellor's Animal Research Committee in agreement to NIH animal care guidelines.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Islet isolation and culture
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 257 260 257 260 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Maedler1">[5]</xref>
###xml 776 777 768 769 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 778 779 770 771 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 161 166 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
###xml 317 322 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 394 399 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Human islets from six different donors were obtained from the Islet cell resource center (ICR-ABCC) or the European Consortium for Islet Transplantation (ECIT). Mouse islets were isolated by bile duct perfusion and collagenase digestion as described before [5]. The islets were cultured in suspension culture dishes. Human islets were cultured in CMRL 1066 medium containing 5.5 mM glucose and mouse islets in RPMI 1640 medium containing 11.1 mM glucose, both supplemented with 100 U/ml penicillin, 100 microg/ml streptomycin and 10% FCS (Invitrogen Ltd., Carlsbad, CA), hereafter referred to as culture medium. Islets were pre-cultured for 48 h after the isolation before the experiment, in some experiments, islets were transfected with siRNA and/or treated with 50 microM H2O2 (Sigma) for 2 h.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
beta-cell mass analysis and immunohistochemistry
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 517 518 511 512 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 36 40 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 931 941 <span type="species:ncbi:10141">guinea pig</span>
###xml 985 990 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1044 1050 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 1138 1144 <span type="species:ncbi:9793">donkey</span>
###xml 1149 1153 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
At the age of 2 and 9 weeks of age, mice were sacrificed and pancreata dissected, weighed and fixed in formalin followed by paraffin embedding, orienting pancreata such that sections were cut along the head-tail axis. Sections for beta-cell mass were at least 50 microns apart. Five representative sections from each pancreas (spanning the width of the pancreas) were used in the analysis of beta-cell mass. Pancreatic and islet sections were deparaffinized in toluene, rehydrated in grades of alcohol and washed in H2O. Slides were exposed to antigen-retrieval using antigen unmasking buffer according to the manufacturer's instructions (Vector Laboratories, Inc. Burlingame, CA). After antigen unmasking, the sections were cooled to room temperature, permabilized in 0.4% Triton X-100/TBS for 30 min, and blocked with 0.2% Tween 20/3% IgG-free BSA/2% Triton X-100/TBS. Primary antibodies were diluted at the following dilutions: guinea pig anti-insulin 1:50 (Dako, Carpinteria, CA), mouse anti-glucagon 1:1000 (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO), rabbit anti-phosphohistone H3 1:200 (Upstate, Charlottesville, VA) followed by detection with donkey-and goat-derived secondary antibodies conjugated to FITC and Cy3 (Jackson ImmunoResearch Laboratories, West Grove, PA). A montage of the whole pancreatic section was created using OpenlabTM and ImageJ software (Improvisison, MA) on a Leica DM6000. The relative area of beta-cells (green fluorescence) was determined by quantification of the cross-sectional beta-cell area divided by the cross-sectional area of total tissue. beta-cell mass per pancreas was estimated as the product of the relative cross-sectional area of beta-cells per total tissue and the weight of the pancreas.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
To obtain sections from isolated islets, islets were washed with PBS, fixed in Bouin's solution for 15 min. and resuspended in 2% melted agarose in PBS, followed by short centrifugation and paraffin embedding. 4 microM islet sections were cut and re-hydrated. For detection of beta-cell apoptosis, sections were incubated with 20 microg/ml proteinase K (Roche Diagnostics, Indianapolis, IN) for 12 min at 37degreesC and blocked with 0.2% Tween 20/3% IgG-free BSA/2% Triton X-100/TBS. Apoptosis was analyzed by the terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase-mediated dUTP nick-end labeling (TUNEL) technique according to the manufacturer's instructions (In Situ Cell Death Detection Kit, TMR red; Roche). Then, sections were double-stained for insulin as described above.
###end p 21

###begin title 28
RNA interference
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 180 199 180 199 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="gene">GCAACCTAGTGTACTTCTT</named-content>

Western blot analysis

###begin p 31
###xml 607 613 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>

###xml 716 722 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>

Islets were washed in PBS and lysed for 40 min on ice in 40 microl lysis buffer containing 20 mM Tris acetate, 0.27 M sucrose, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM EGTA, 50 mM NaF, 1% Triton X-100, 5 mM sodium pyrophosphate and 10 mM beta-glycerophosphate. ###begin title 36
Results
###end title 36
###begin title 37
AIF depletion decreases beta-cell mass
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 358 365 355 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004394-g001">Fig. 1A</xref>
###xml 508 510 505 507 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 724 731 721 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004394-g001">Fig. 1A</xref>
###xml 818 820 815 817 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 834 841 831 838 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004394-g001">Fig. 1B</xref>
###xml 942 944 936 938 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 1005 1012 999 1006 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004394-g001">Fig. 1C</xref>
###xml 143 148 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 153 158 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 236 241 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 246 251 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 334 339 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 344 349 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 496 498 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">WT</span>
###xml 499 503 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 508 510 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 518 522 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 542 547 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 818 820 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 828 832 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 942 944 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 952 956 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 989 991 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">WT</span>
To understand if AIF plays a role in the regulation of survival in beta-cells, we analyzed both mRNA and protein expression of AIF in isolated mouse and human pancreatic islets. RT-PCR analysis showed the mRNA transcript of AIF in both human and mouse islets. Western blot analysis confirmed the expression of AIF protein in isolated human and mouse islets (Fig. 1A). To elucidate if AIF played a role in the establishment of the endocrine pancreas, we compared the islet cellular composition of WT mice and Hq mutant mice, a hypomorphic AIF mouse model in which the expression of AIF is reduced to 10-20% of wild type littermates. This depletion of AIF was confirmed in isolated islets by RT-PCR and Western Blot analysis (Fig. 1A). Immunohistochemical evaluation of pancreata revealed normal islet morphology in the Hq mutant mice (Fig. 1B). Islet beta-cell mass was 2.7-fold reduced in 2-week (p<0.001) and 4.6-fold in 9-week-old (p<0.01) Hq mutant mice, respectively compared to their WT littermates (Fig. 1C).

Possible reasons for the decreased beta-cell mass could be increased beta-cell apoptosis and/or reduced proliferation. Previous studies show that neurons from Hq mutant mice display an increased propensity to re-enter the cell cycle, which results in aberrant apoptotic destruction of the neurons [13]. To determine if cell cycle dynamics were misregulated in the Hq beta-cells, we analyzed the incorporation/expression of cell cycle markers of beta-cells in Hq mutant mice. A BrdU pulse was administered to 4 week old Hq and WT littermates 2 h before sacrifice to mark cells that were currently in S-phase. BrdU+insulin+ double-positive cells were counted as beta-cells in S phase. Immunohistochemistry for phosphorylated Histone H3 (pHH3) staining allows one to discriminate cells in G2 or M phases of cell cycle [19], [20]. pHH3+insulin+ (with punctuated pattern) cells were counted as beta-cells at G2 phase. pHH3+insulin+ (with a homogeneously condensed pattern of strong nuclear expression) cells were counted as beta-cells at M phase (Fig. 1F). Analysis of BrdU incorporation did not reveal any significant difference in proliferating cells between WT and Hq mutant mice (Fig. 1E). However, pHH3 staining showed a 3-fold increase in beta-cells in the G2 phase in Hq mutant mice but no differences in M-phase (Fig. 1E, p<0.05), an example of such punctuated staining pattern in the Hq mice is shown in Fig. 1F. This suggests that beta-cells from Hq mutant mice either stop or prolong the G2 phase and some do not further re-enter the cell cycle. Previous studies show that such cells are highly prone to apoptosis [21], [22]. To test this possibility, we analyzed beta-cell apoptosis in pancreatic sections from WT and Hq mutant mice at different ages. beta-cell apoptosis in Hq mutant mice was increased at all ages compared to WT (Neonates P0: 0.4% in Hq mutant mice versus 0.2% in WT. No apoptosis was observed in the islets from WT mice at all further ages analyzed but the number of apoptotic beta-cell in the Hq mutant mice were at P7: 0.3%, P14: 0.1%, 4-weeks-old: 0.2% and 9-weeks-old: 0.2%.)

Based on our in vivo results, we studied beta-cell apoptosis from isolated islets in order to further investigate the nature of the beta-cell loss in Hq mutant mice. Neurons [13] as well as cardiomycytes [23] from Hq mutant mice are highly susceptible to cell death induced by oxidative stress. Isolated mouse islets from mutant and WT animals were treated with 50 microM H2O2 for 2 h and beta-cell apoptosis was analyzed by double staining for TUNEL and insulin. In accordance with our in vivo observations, beta-cell apoptosis in the Hq mutant mice islets was 8-fold increased in the untreated islets (*p<0.05). When islets were exposed to H2O2, there was a 16.5-fold increase in beta-cell apoptosis in the WT mice (**p<0,05), and a 14-fold increase in the Hq mutant mice islets (p<0.05). When compared to the WT mice, there was a 6.8-fold increase in the Hq mutant mice islets (p<0.05), when islets were exposed to H2O2 (Fig. 2A&B). Because apoptosis results in decreased beta-cell mass, we hypothesized that Hq animals would be hyperglycemic. Contrary to our predictions, Hq mutant mice showed significantly lower fasting blood glucose levels as well as improved glucose tolerance during an ipGTT (Fig. 2C). Our data confirm previously published data that AIF depletion in the liver and/or skeletal muscle leads to increased insulin sensitivity [15]. Because Hq mice are global hypomorphs, we can only assume that minimal insulin secretion from the reduced beta-cell mass is sufficient to maintain glucose homeostasis in insulin hyper-sensitive mice. This indicates that hepatic and muscular defects in the oxidative phosphorylation may counteract the development of diabetes, despite its deleterious effects on beta-cell survival.

###begin title 47
###xml 56 61 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
AIF depletion leads to increased beta-cell apoptosis in human islets without affecting insulin secretion
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 325 332 325 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004394-g003">Fig. 3A</xref>
###xml 380 382 380 382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 495 496 492 493 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 555 562 552 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004394-g003">Fig. 3B</xref>
###xml 63 68 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 227 232 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 380 382 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 390 394 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 417 422 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
To investigate the significance of AIF and oxidative stress in human islets, AIF was knocked down by small interfering RNAs (siRNA). Western blot analysis of AIF expression shows 76% downregulation of AIF protein expression in human islets exposed for 3 days to siRNA directed to AIF (siAIF) versus scrambled control (siScr, Fig. 3A). In line with our observations in islets from Hq mutant mice, exposure of isolated human islets to siAIF resulted in a 8.8-fold increase in beta-cell apoptosis (p<0,05), analyzed by double staining for TUNEL and insulin (Fig. 3B).

To assess the effect of AIF depletion on insulin secretion, we have performed a glucose stimulated insulin secretion assay (GSIS) in siAIF and siScr transfected human islets. Basal and stimulated insulin secretion (Fig. 3C) as well as the stimulatory index (Fig. 3D) was not significantly changed in response to AIF depletion. Despite its deleterious role in inducing beta-cell apoptosis, AIF depletion did not impair beta-cell function, neither basal nor glucose stimulated insulin secretion. Then we compared response to H2O2. As expected, H2O2 decreased glucose stimulated insulin secretion in control as well as in siAIF treated islets. Stimulatory index was decreased by 23% by H2O2 in siScr islets and by 40% in siAIF islets (p<0.05), assuming that AIF plays a role to protect the islets from oxidative stress, but is not involved in insulin secretion itself.

###begin title 53
Discussion
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 15 17 15 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 146 150 146 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Klein1">[13]</xref>
###xml 15 17 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 25 29 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Recent work in Hq mutant mice, which have an 80% decrease in AIF expression, suggests that AIF depletion results in increased neuronal cell death [13]. Under physiologic conditions, AIF acts as a free radical scavenger in the mitochondrial respiratory chain to prevent apoptosis. Considering the high correlation of mitochondrial dysfunction and oxidative stress with T2DM, we hypothesized that AIF may play an important role in the regulation of beta-cell mass and survival.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 326 328 317 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 422 424 413 415 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 466 470 454 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Donath2">[21]</xref>
###xml 472 476 460 464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Ritzel1">[22]</xref>
###xml 280 289 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Harlequin</span>
###xml 290 294 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 326 328 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 329 333 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 422 424 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
In the present study, we report that AIF depletion results in a 3-fold decrease in beta-cell mass at 2-weeks of age which was further decreased with age. This was due to the missregulation of cell cycle control. Although there was no difference in proliferating beta-cells in the Harlequin mice, 3-fold more beta-cells in the Hq mice retained in G2 phase of the cell cycle. In accordance to previous studies we found that Hq beta-cells are highly prone to apoptosis [21], [22].
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 375 379 375 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Shackelford1">[24]</xref>
###xml 757 759 751 753 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 1044 1047 1035 1038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Maechler1">[7]</xref>
###xml 751 755 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 757 759 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 767 771 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 777 782 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
A proposed mechanism for the induction of apoptosis could be oxidative stress, which is associated with abnormal cell cycle checkpoint function. Under normal circumstances the cell cycle proceeds without interruptions. However, if cycling cells are damaged by agents which induce oxidative stress, a G2 check point response is triggered and cells pause and repair the damage [24]. In the case of a severe damage, cells may alternatively undergo apoptosis. Given the role of AIF in mitochondrial respiration as a hydrogen peroxide scavenger, loss of AIF could result in an accumulation of ROS, which would then generate a misregulation of beta-cell cycle and apoptosis. This is further supported by the finding, that beta-cells from both AIF-deficient mice (Hq mutant mice) and human islets (AIF-siRNA-treated) displayed increased sensitive to cell death induced by hydrogen peroxide. beta-cells are in general very sensitive to oxidative stress, in particular with hydrogen peroxide, due to their low expression of a peroxide scavenging system [7].
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 121 123 118 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 234 236 231 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 283 285 280 282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 427 429 424 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 509 513 506 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Pospisilik1">[15]</xref>
###xml 645 649 642 646 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Lowell1">[14]</xref>
###xml 651 655 648 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Rabol1">[16]</xref>
###xml 657 661 654 658 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Befroy1">[25]</xref>
###xml 750 752 747 749 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 915 919 912 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Pospisilik1">[15]</xref>
###xml 969 971 966 968 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 1154 1156 1151 1153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 1382 1384 1376 1378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 1552 1553 1546 1547 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1554 1555 1548 1549 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 121 123 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 124 128 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 234 236 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 244 248 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 283 285 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 286 290 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 427 429 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 437 441 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 741 745 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 750 752 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 753 757 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 969 971 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 972 976 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1154 1156 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 1157 1161 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1294 1299 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1371 1373 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">WT</span>
###xml 1382 1384 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
Given the role of beta-cell mitochondria as key regulators of glucose stimulated insulin secretion, we hypothesized that Hq mice show an impaired glucose tolerance. Surprisingly, and in accordance to a recent study, AIF deficiency in Hq mutant mice did not impair glucose tolerance. Hq mice were rather hypoglycemic compared to wt. This could be due to impaired glucose sensing as a result of selective neuron loss reported in Hq mutant mice, but is more likely to be a result of improved insulin sensitivity [15]. Many studies have linked changes in mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation to the development of insulin resistance and diabetes [14], [16], [25]: Importantly, a recent study in tissue specific muscle- and liver AIF knockout mice and Hq mice with a global AIF deficiency shows that a primary OxPhos defect alone does not cause diabetes, but rather increases insulin sensitivity and reduces fat mass [15]. In line with our results, this study found that Hq mice displayed improved glucose tolerance associated with reduced fasting and glucose-induced insulin secretion, possibly due to the improved insulin sensitivity. This explains, why Hq mice still maintain normoglycemia, despite their loss of beta-cell mass. Interestingly, in the absence of oxidative stress, AIF depleted human islets did not show any changes in insulin secretion. When compared to WT islets, Hq islets showed a similar response to glucose, confirming the hypothesis that AIF does not directly act on insulin secretion. Nevertheless, when islets were exposed to H2O2, insulin secretion was further impaired in the AIF depleted islets, assuming that AIF plays a role in protecting the islets from oxidative stress, but is not involved in insulin secretion itself.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 118 122 118 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Pospisilik1">[15]</xref>
###xml 158 160 158 160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hq</italic>
###xml 241 245 241 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004394-Vahsen1">[26]</xref>
###xml 158 160 <span type="species:ncbi:124350">Hq</span>
###xml 161 165 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The proposed compensation mechanism involves a shift to anaerobic glucose metabolism and increase in fuel utilization [15]. It is important to note; that the Hq mice, despite their OxPhos deficiency, had no indication of elevated ROS levels [26]. However, our finding that AIF is necessary for maintaining beta-cell mass is in favor of a critical role of ROS signaling and oxidative stress in the beta-cells and supports the role of AIF as a free radical scavenger in the mitochondrial respiratory chain to prevent apoptosis.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
Our data provide new insights into a protective effect of AIF on beta-cell turnover. Loss of AIF could therefore be one of the mechanisms of beta-cell apoptosis.
