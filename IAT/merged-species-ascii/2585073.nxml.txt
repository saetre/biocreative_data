Interleukin-1beta and tumor necrosis factor-alpha are expressed by different subsets of microglia and macrophages after ischemic stroke in mice
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
This is an Open Access article distributed under the terms of the Creative Commons Attribution License (), which permits unrestricted use, distribution, and reproduction in any medium, provided the original work is properly cited.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 417 421 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Interleukin-1beta (IL-1beta) and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) are expressed by microglia and infiltrating macrophages following ischemic stroke. Whereas IL-1beta is primarily neurotoxic in ischemic stroke, TNF-alpha may have neurotoxic and/or neuroprotective effects. We investigated whether IL-1beta and TNF-alpha are synthesized by overlapping or segregated populations of cells after ischemic stroke in mice.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 182 186 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 246 250 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We used flow cytometry and immunohistochemistry to examine cellular co-expression of IL-1beta and TNF-alpha at 6, 12 and 24 hours after permanent middle cerebral artery occlusion in mice, validating the results by the use of bone marrow chimeric mice.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 94 95 87 88 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 99 103 92 96 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dim </sup>
###xml 122 123 115 116 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 127 132 120 125 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">high </sup>
###xml 212 214 205 207 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
We found that IL-1beta and TNF-alpha were expressed in largely segregated populations of CD11b+CD45dim microglia and CD11b+CD45high macrophages, with cells expressing both cytokines only rarely. The number of Gr1+ granulocytes producing IL-1beta or TNF-alpha was very low, and we observed no IL-1beta- or TNF-alpha-expressing T cells or astrocytes.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusion
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 162 166 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Taken together, the results show that IL-1beta and TNF-alpha are produced by largely segregated populations of microglia and macrophages after ischemic stroke in mice. Our findings provide evidence of a functional diversity among different subsets of microglia and macrophages that is potentially relevant to future design of anti-inflammatory therapies in stroke.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Background
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 158 159 144 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 160 161 146 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 290 291 273 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 292 293 275 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 365 366 344 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 367 368 346 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 391 392 370 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 393 395 372 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 591 592 570 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 593 595 572 574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 428 432 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 440 444 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The proinflammatory cytokines interleukin-1beta (IL-1beta) and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) play key roles in the pathogenesis of ischemic stroke [1-3]. IL-1beta exerts neurotoxic effects in ischemic stroke and blocking its action has been shown to reduce ischemic brain damage [4,5]. In comparison, there is evidence that TNF-alpha has both neurotoxic [6,7] and neuroprotective [8-10] roles after ischemic stroke in rats and in mice. Increasing evidence implicates both cytokines in the early inflammatory response that precedes and accompanies ischemia-induced neuronal damage [6,11]. However, detailed knowledge about the contribution of different cell types to the production of IL-1beta and TNF-alpha is still not available.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 305 307 291 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 308 310 294 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 401 403 384 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 404 406 387 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 423 425 402 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 426 428 405 407 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 636 638 615 617 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 639 641 618 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
The relative physiological outcome of increased IL-1beta and TNF-alpha signaling in ischemic stroke may depend on the kinetics and location of cytokine producing cells. There is compelling evidence that IL-1beta and TNF-alpha are primarily synthesized by activated microglia and infiltrating macrophages [12-14], although granulocytes and astrocytes have also been suggested to produce both IL-1beta [15-17] and TNF-alpha [18,19]. Precise identification of cell source has, however, been compromised by the lack of microglial and macrophage specific markers, which prevents discrimination of these cell types at the histological level [12,14]. Furthermore, it is presently unknown whether IL-1beta and TNF-alpha are expressed to the same extent by the same or different subsets of microglia and macrophages following ischemic stroke.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 107 109 96 98 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 131 133 120 122 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 344 346 333 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 347 349 336 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 350 352 339 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 565 567 547 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 938 940 920 922 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 941 943 923 925 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 338 342 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 559 563 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 764 769 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We have previously shown that IL-1beta mRNA and TNF-alpha mRNA, and TNF-alpha protein are produced by CD11b+ microglia and by CD11b+ macrophages at the edge of and within areas of infarction, and that this production reaches maximum levels of expression between 12 and 24 hours after permanent middle cerebral artery occlusion (pMCAO) in mice [12,14,20]. The objective of the present study was to provide additional insight into the cell types and cell subpopulations that produce IL-1beta and TNF-alpha within the first 24 hours following ischemic stroke in mice [20]. To distinguish microglia from infiltrating macrophages after pMCAO, we used flow cytometry with CD45 and CD11b as myeloid-lineage specific markers, we used a radiated, bone marrow (BM) chimeric mouse model; and we used intracellular cytokine-staining, and double immunofluorescence staining. In addition, since CD11b is expressed by both macrophages and granulocytes [21,22], we also analyzed cytokine production by granulocytes using the granulocyte specific marker Gr1. Our results show that IL-1beta and TNF-alpha are produced by largely segregated subsets of microglia and macrophages, and that very few cells express both cytokines.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Methods
###end title 14
###begin title 15
Animals
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 24 26 24 26 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 39 41 39 41 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">a </sup>
###xml 45 46 45 46 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">b</sup>
###xml 68 70 68 70 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 108 110 108 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 359 361 359 361 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 447 449 447 449 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 599 601 599 601 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 611 612 611 612 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 691 693 691 693 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 53 57 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 112 116 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 329 333 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 417 421 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 507 511 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 777 781 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 832 836 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
Breeding pairs of CD45.1+ (B6.SJL-Ptprca Pepcb/BoyJ) mice and CD45.2+ C57BL/6-Tg(UBC-GFP)30Scha/J (GFP-Tg) [23] mice were purchased from the Jackson Laboratory (Bar Harbour, Maine, USA) and transferred to the Department of Medical Microbiology and Immunology, University of Aarhus, where they were maintained as a colony. GFP-Tg mice, which express the CD45.2+ allotype, were used as BM donors and congenic BoyJ male mice, which express the CD45.1+ allotype, were used as BM recipients. This combination of mice was chosen so that infiltrating cells could be identified by two different markers (GFP+ and CD45.2+), however the GFP signal alone proved to be sufficiently strong and reliable [24] to identify infiltrating cells. Peritoneal macrophages were obtained from C57BL/6 mice, which were purchased from Taconic (Ry, Denmark). Mice were housed under diurnal lightning conditions with free access to food and water. The experiments were approved by the Danish Animal Inspectorate (J. no. 2005/561-1068).
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Generation of bone marrow chimeras
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 104 106 104 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 625 628 625 628 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">137</sup>
###xml 794 796 794 796 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">7 </sup>
###xml 21 25 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 64 68 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 441 447 <span type="species:ncbi:3635">cotton</span>
###xml 540 544 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 772 776 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 802 807 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 837 841 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1012 1016 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1049 1054 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
BM cells from GFP-Tg mice were grafted into lethally irradiated mice as described by Wirenfeldt et al. [24]. For donor BM recovery, the proximal and distal ends of tibia and femur were removed and BM was flushed from the medullary channel into sterile 50 mL polypropylene tubes using cold RPMI 1640 medium (Gibco, Paisley, UK). The BM cells were rinsed in RPMI medium and centrifuged at 1,000 rpm for 10 min, then filtered through a sterile cotton sieve. After 3 rounds of rinsing and filtering, cells were kept on ice until use. Recipient mice (6-8 weeks of age) were lethally irradiated with a single dose of 9.5 Gy from a 137Cs source (Riso National Laboratory, Roskilde, Denmark). Within 2 hours of irradiation, BM cells were injected into the tail veins of recipient mice (approximately 107 cells/mouse). For 3 days after grafting, mice received oxytetracycline (2 g/L Terramycin vet. 20%; Pfizer, Amoise, France) in autoclaved drinking water. Thereafter they were supplied with acidified water (pH 3). The mice were fed irradiation-sterilized mouse chow and maintained under pathogen-free conditions for 6 weeks until they were subjected to surgery.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
Permanent middle cerebral artery occlusion
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 177 179 177 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 897 899 877 879 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 19 23 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 198 202 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 550 554 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 640 644 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 738 742 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Under anaesthesia, mice were subjected to focal cerebral ischemia by permanent occlusion of the distal part of the left middle cerebral artery (pMCAO), as previously described [20]. During surgery, mice were placed on a 37degreesC +/- 0.5degreesC warm heating pad. An incision was made from eye to ear, the parotid gland and the upper part of the temporal muscle were split, and a small hole was drilled over the distal part of the MCA. The MCA was occluded by electrocoagulation and the incision was stitched with a 4.0 nylon-suture. After surgery, mice were injected with 1 ml of isotonic saline and their eyes were coated with ointment. Mice were kept at 28degreesC until sacrificed, 24 hours post-surgery. For post-surgical analgesia mice were treated with Temgesic (0.001 mg/20 g buprenorphinum, Reckitt & Colman, Hull, UK) three times at 8-hour intervals starting immediately after surgery [20].
###end p 20
###begin title 21
Tissue preparation
###end title 21
###begin title 22
Histology
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 161 162 161 162 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 164 166 164 166 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4 </sub>
###xml 179 180 179 180 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 183 184 183 184 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 489 493 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Mice were anesthetized i.p. with an overdose of pentobarbital and perfused through the left ventricle using 10 ml of Soerensen's phosphate buffer (SB) (0.03 M KH2PO4 and 0.12 M Na2HPO4, pH 7.4) followed by 20 ml of cold 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA) in 0.15 M SB, pH 7.4. The brains were post-fixed in 4% PFA for 1 hour followed by immersion in 20% sucrose in 0.15 M SB overnight. Next, the brains were frozen and cut into 16-mum frontal sections. For verification of chimerism in BM-chimeric mice, samples of spleen, heart, kidney and liver were frozen and processed to obtain 16-mum cryostat sections.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Cell culture preparation
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 625 627 619 621 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 127 131 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 165 169 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Thioglycolate-elicited peritoneal macrophages were obtained after i.p. injection of 1 ml 3% thioglycolate (Sigma) into C57BL/6 mice. Three days after injection, the mice were killed by cervical dislocation and 10 ml Dulbecco's modified Eagle Medium (Invitrogen) was injected into the peritoneum. Subsequently, the peritoneal fluid was withdrawn. After two washes in phosphate buffered saline (PBS, pH 7.4) the cells were counted and plated in T25 flasks in DMEM Medium containing 5% FBS, 100 units/ml of penicillin, and 100 mg/ml streptomycin (Sigma). The macrophages were kept in a humidified 37degreesC incubator with 5% CO2 for 2 days and then stimulated for 24 hours with 100 ng/ml LPS (Sigma).
###end p 25

Flow cytometry

###xml 382 383 382 383 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>

Immunohistochemistry for GFP+ cells
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 26 28 26 28 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 192 193 192 193 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 194 195 194 195 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>

###xml 676 678 670 672 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>

###xml 349 353 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 457 463 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 1076 1082 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
To detect infiltrating GFP+ cells, sections were rinsed 3 x 15 min in 0.05 M Tris-buffered saline (TBS, pH 7.4), and endogenous peroxidase activity was blocked by rinsing the sections in 30% H2O2, methanol and TBS (1:1:8) for 30 min at room temperature (RT). After rinsing 2 x 10 min in TBS + 0.5% Triton, the sections were incubated with 10% fetal calf serum (FCS) in TBS for 30 min at RT to reduce non-specific staining. Sections were then incubated with rabbit anti-GFP (Table 1, Abcam) diluted 1:1,000 in TBS containing 10% FCS overnight at 4degreesC. Next, sections were rinsed 3 x 15 min in TBS + 0.5% Triton and incubated with peroxidase-labeled "ready-to-use" EnVision+ polymer (DakoCytomation) overnight at 4degreesC. Sections were then rinsed 3 x 15 min in TBS and developed for 7 min in TBS containing 0.05% diaminobenzidine and 0.033% H2O2. Finally, sections were rinsed in TBS, dehydrated in a graded series of alcohol, cleared in xylene and mounted in Depex. Antibody specificity was assessed by negative controls using substitution of the primary antibody with rabbit IgG (Table 1, DakoCytomation) or omission of the primary antibody. These sections were devoid of signal.

###begin title 35
Double immunofluorescence staining for cell specific markers and IL-1beta and/or TNF-alpha
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 178 179 178 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 320 321 314 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 828 829 822 823 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 854 858 848 850 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174; </sup>
###xml 1008 1009 1000 1001 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Sections were air dried, rinsed in TBS for 10 min, and blocked with 10% FCS in TBS + 0.5% Triton for 30 min at RT. Next, the sections were incubated with primary antibody (Table 1) overnight at 4degreesC, followed by rinsing for 10 min in TBS and incubation with a species-specific secondary fluorescent antibody (Table 1) diluted 1:200 for 1-2 hours the next day. From this point forward, the sections were protected from light. After 10 min in TBS, sections were incubated for 2 hours with the second primary antibody diluted in TBS + 0.5% Triton containing 10% FCS, rinsed in TBS for 10 min at RT, and then incubated with the secondary antibody diluted 1:200 in TBS + 0.5% Triton containing 10% FCS for 1-2 hours at room temperature (RT). Finally, sections were rinsed 2 x 10 min in TBS, followed by 2 x 10 min in distilled H2O, and mounted in ProLong(R) Gold antifade reagent with DAPI (Invitrogen, Taastrup, Denmark). Negative controls, where primary antibodies were replaced by isotype controls (Table 1) or omitted from the protocol, were devoid of signal.

Estimation of numbers of infiltrating GFP+ cells
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 17 19 17 19 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 120 125 120 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 128 130 128 130 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 136 138 136 138 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 420 422 417 419 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 674 676 667 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 796 798 789 791 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
The number of GFP+ cells in the ipsilateral cortex was estimated using the cell counting method described in Lambertsen et al. [25]. GFP+ cells with an identifiable nucleus were counted in approximately 16 sections from each animal, 384 mum apart. This was done using a 100x objective and a 25% frame area, stepping 300 mum/300 mum in the x-y position using the CAST Grid System from Olympus. The total number (N) of GFP+ cells in each animal was estimated using the formula: Estimate of N = SigmaQ x (1/ssf) x (1/asf) x (1/tsf), where Q was the number of cells counted, 1/ssf the sampling section fraction (1/ssf = 24), and 1/asf the area sampling fraction (1/asf = 36.4) [26]. The thickness sampling fraction (1/tsf) was set to 1, since cells were counted in the entire height of the sections [25].

Staining procedures
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 423 424 408 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>

###xml 962 966 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 972 978 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
Cells were incubated for 30 min in staining-buffer (staining-buffer: Hank's Buffered Salt Solution with 2% FCS and 0.1% sodium azide), containing 50 mug/ml Syrian hamster Ig (Jackson Immunoresearch, West Grove, PA, USA) and 1 mug/ml anti-FcgammaIII/II receptor (BD FcBlocktrade mark, BD Biosciences), to block non-specific staining. After rinsing, cells were stained for surface antigens (or their isotype controls) (Table 1) for 30 min at RT, and then rinsed in staining-buffer [24,27]. Following staining of surface markers (CD11b, CD45, Gr1, TCRbeta), the cells were fixed and permeabilized in Cytofix/Cytopermtrade mark (BD Biosciences) for 20 min at 4degreesC, washed in 1 ml 1x PermWashtrade mark buffer (BD Biosciences), and incubated with antibodies recognizing intracellular antigens (IL-1beta, TNF-alpha or isotype controls) (Table 1) for 30 min at RT. Cells stained with unconjugated antibodies were rinsed, then incubated with fluorescently labelled goat anti-rabbit secondary antibodies for 30 min at RT. After the final rinse, cells were resuspended in staining-buffer.

The distribution of infiltrating GFP+ cells was visualized using immunohistochemistry. The time profile for TNF-alpha-expressing CD11b+CD45dim microglia showed a significant increase in numbers of TNF-alpha+ cells at 12 hours, which peaked 24 hours after pMCAO compared to unmanipulated control mice. Similarly there was a injury-induced increase in numbers of CD11b+CD45high macrophages/granulocytes that expressed TNF-alpha at 24 hours after pMCAO (Figure 5F). Calculation of the proportion of TNF-alpha-expressing CD11b+CD45dim microglia and CD11b+CD45high macrophages/granulocytes showed that the proportion of TNF-alpha+ microglia peaked with 2% of the CD11b+CD45high population at 12-24 hours after pMCAO, while the proportion of TNF-alpha+ macrophages/granulocytes was 17% of the CD11b+CD45high population in unmanipulated controls and 4% at 24 hours after pMCAO. This most of all reflected that a relatively large proportion of the very few CD11b+CD45high cells present in the unmanipulated mouse cortex expressed TNF-alpha (Figure 5G). In contrast, the level of TNF-alpha expression by CD11b+CD45high macrophages/granulocytes seemed unaffected by ischemic injury (Figure 5H).

We additionally identified a subpopulation of CD11b+CD45dim microglia and CD11b+CD45high macrophages/granulocytes that co-expressed IL-1beta and TNF-alpha (Figure 5I). The proportion of IL-1beta/TNF-alpha co-expressing microglia increased from 0.05% in unmanipulated controls to 1.2% after 24 hours of pMCAO, whereas co-expressing macrophages doubled from 2% in unmanipulated controls to 4% after 12 hours of pMCAO, before declining to 2% after 24 hours (Figure 5J).

However, since the number of CD11b+CD45dim microglia and CD11b+CD45high macrophages/granulocytes did not differ between unmanipulated controls and sham-operated mice (Figure 4E), the overall number of cytokine expressing cells was much lower in sham-operated versus pMCAO operated mice (Figure 5C, F, I).

###begin p 70
###xml 168 172 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Taken together, these results clearly show that largely non-overlapping subsets of microglia and macrophages/granulocytes produce IL-1beta and TNF-alpha after pMCAO in mice.

Finally, since both macrophages and granulocytes express CD11b and high levels of CD45 [21], we used Gr1 to determine which cell population was the predominant CD45high source of IL-1beta and TNF-alpha. We found a small but significant increase in both IL-1beta- and TNF-alpha expressing CD45highGr1+ granulocytes at 24 hours after pMCAO compared to unmanipulated control mice. The flow cytometry data presented above show that microglia and macrophages, but not granulocytes, are major producers of IL-1beta and TNF-alpha, and that co-expression of IL-1beta and TNF-alpha is limited to a small subset of microglia and macrophages. We then validated these results using double-immunofluorescence of tissue sections, to distinguish resident microglia from infiltrating cells in BM-chimeric mice. Because a subpopulation of the infiltrating GFP+ cells were identified as granulocytes (Figure 7A), we first investigated if Gr1+ granulocytes express IL-1beta and TNF-alpha by using double immunofluorescence. We found that neither IL-1beta nor TNF-alpha was expressed by Gr1+ granulocytes (Figure 7B). This might reflect the lower sensitivity of immunohistochemistry compared to flow cytometry, or may indicate that granulocytes express smaller amounts of protein (IL-1beta MFI: 58 +/- 18; TNF-alpha MFI: 27 +/- 2) than do macrophages (IL-1beta MFI: 104 +/- 25; TNF-alpha MFI: 40 +/- 2) (P < 0.004). Double-immunofluorescence staining identified both resident and infiltrating cells expressing either IL-1beta (Figure 7C) or TNF-alpha (Figure 7D), but only rarely identified cells co-expressing IL-1beta and TNF-alpha 24 hours after pMCAO (Figure 7E), confirming the flow cytometric data. There was no co-localization of IL-1beta or TNF-alpha staining with the astroglial marker GFAP (data not shown) or with the endothelial markers vWF/CD31 (data not shown).

###begin title 78
Discussion
###end title 78
###begin p 79
###xml 562 563 541 542 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 567 572 546 551 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">high </sup>
###xml 645 647 624 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 727 728 699 700 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 732 737 704 709 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">high </sup>
###xml 176 180 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 254 258 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 875 879 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We have shown for the first time that IL-1beta and TNF-alpha are produced by largely non-overlapping subsets of microglia and macrophages after induction of ischemic stroke in mice. Using flow cytometry and histology, validated by the use of BM-chimeric mice, we showed that microglia and macrophages are the major producers of IL-1beta and TNF-alpha after pMCAO, and that at maximum 1.2% of microglia and 4.5% of infiltrating macrophages co-express IL-1beta and TNF-alpha within the first 24 hours after pMCAO. Granulocytes, which accounted for 50% of the CD11b+CD45high cell population and which are known to exacerbate ischemic brain damage [30], accounted for only 2.4% of IL-1beta and 0.8% of TNF-alpha expression by CD11b+CD45high cells after 24 hours of pMCAO, indicating that the contribution of these cells to the production of IL-1beta and TNF-alpha after pMCAO in mice is negligible. A scenario therefore emerges wherein different subsets of microglia and macrophages may have different roles in ischemic stroke, and may thus either improve or reduce the chance of survival of ischemic neurons.
###end p 79
###begin p 80
###xml 315 317 297 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 318 320 300 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 321 323 303 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 407 408 389 390 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 412 416 394 398 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dim </sup>
###xml 448 449 430 431 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 453 458 435 440 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">high </sup>
###xml 284 288 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Our observation of a peak in the total number of IL-1beta- and TNF-alpha-expressing cells 24 hours after pMCAO is in line with previous demonstrations of a time-dependent peak in the number of IL-1beta mRNA-, TNF-alpha mRNA-, and TNF-alpha protein-expressing cells in SJL and C57BL/6 mice, 12-24 hours after pMCAO [12,14,25]. By taking advantage of the ability of flow cytometry to distinguish between CD11b+CD45dim microglia and infiltrating CD11b+CD45high macrophages, we showed that the number of IL-1beta- and TNF-alpha-expressing microglia by far exceeded the number of cytokine-expressing macrophages 12 hours after pMCAO. This result was expected based on the known steady increase in the number of infiltrating macrophages over the first 24 hours of pMCAO, but this result has not been previously demonstrated.
###end p 80
###begin p 81
###xml 709 711 693 695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 712 714 696 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 897 898 881 882 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 902 907 886 891 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">high </sup>
###xml 138 142 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 312 316 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 560 564 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 597 601 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 840 844 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 944 948 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
It was striking to observe that a relatively large proportion of the small number of macrophages present in the cortex from unmanipulated mice express TNF-alpha, and that the MFI levels of TNF-alpha expression in these cells are comparable to mean cellular TNF-alpha expression levels in pMCAO and sham-operated mice. This indicates that TNF-alpha expression by macrophages is relatively constant no matter whether the cells have infiltrated the cortex prior to or after pMCAO. Interestingly, the proportion of cytokine-expressing macrophages in sham-operated mice was at least as high as that in mice subjected to pMCAO. This likely reflects that sham surgery in itself induces a focal lesion in the cortex [12,14]. It is important to note, however, that overall numbers of cytokine-expressing macrophages are not increased versus control mice, since there was no significant recruitment of CD11b+CD45high cells to the cortex of sham-operated mice.
###end p 81
###begin p 82
###xml 204 206 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 302 304 302 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 305 307 305 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 308 310 308 310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 389 390 389 390 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 394 398 394 398 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dim </sup>
###xml 583 585 583 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 586 588 586 588 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 589 591 589 591 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 592 594 592 594 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 611 613 611 613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 614 616 614 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 880 882 880 882 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 969 973 969 973 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dim </sup>
###xml 991 993 991 993 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 1198 1200 1198 1200 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 1767 1769 1767 1769 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 1854 1856 1854 1856 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 1857 1859 1857 1859 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 1860 1862 1860 1862 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 1173 1177 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1322 1326 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1359 1363 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Permanent MCAO results in formation of a pan-necrotic infarct, with loss of all cell types including microglial cells. Although neurons and microglia can still be clearly detected after 6 hours of pMCAO [12,14], the developing infarct is usually characterised by severe cell loss 12 hours after pMCAO [12,14,20] We were therefore surprised not to observe a reduction in the number of CD11b+CD45dim microglia 12 and 24 hours after pMCAO. We wondered whether determination of upregulation of CD45, which is widely used to detect both resting and activated microglia in flow cytometry [24,27-29,31] and histology [32,33], might lead to increased detection. Indeed, MFI analysis showed that CD45 levels are upregulated in microglia 12-24 hours after pMCAO. However, microglial expression of CD45 was far below that expressed by macrophages/granulocytes, confirming results by others [34]. Using a bone marrow chimeric approach, we observed that approximately 7% of the CD45dim microglia were GFP+ after 24 hours of pMCAO, suggesting that BM-derived microglial precursors could also contribute to the expansion of the microglial population after ischemic stroke in non-chimeric mice. Infiltration of GFP+ cells was specific to the infarcted cortex, since no increase was observed in the contralateral hemisphere of BM-chimeric mice or in unmanipulated BM-chimeric mice. Other factors contributing to the expansion of the microglial population 12 and 24 hours after pMCAO might be immigration of microglia from regions of the brain not included in the preparation used for flow cytometry, or microglial proliferation. However, microglial proliferation is not prominent at 12 and 24 hours, but is first evident at 48 and 72 hours after induction of focal cerebral ischemia [34], a finding that is similar to observations in other models of acute neural injury [32,36,37].
###end p 82
###begin p 83
###xml 124 126 124 126 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 198 199 198 199 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 203 208 203 208 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">high </sup>
###xml 322 324 322 324 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 467 469 467 469 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 474 475 474 475 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 479 483 479 483 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dim </sup>
###xml 548 550 548 550 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 551 553 551 553 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 842 844 842 844 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 845 847 845 847 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 938 940 938 940 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 941 943 941 943 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 954 959 954 959 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">high </sup>
###xml 1046 1048 1046 1048 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 1049 1051 1049 1051 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 1121 1123 1121 1123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 1124 1126 1124 1126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 1217 1219 1217 1219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 1220 1222 1220 1222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 1259 1261 1259 1261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 1289 1291 1289 1291 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 1417 1418 1417 1418 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1422 1427 1422 1427 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">high </sup>
###xml 1382 1386 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1541 1545 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1565 1569 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The use of a GFP BM-chimeric approach in our study of pMCAO served two purposes: 1) direct visualization of infiltrating GFP+ cells in tissue sections and 2) validation of the BM origin of the CD11b+CD45high macrophages/granulocytes identified by flow cytometry in our model. In addition to the massive infiltration of GFP+ macrophages/granulocytes 24 hours after pMCAO, we found that a small proportion of microglia in the infarcted cortex could be classified as GFP+ CD11b+CD45dim microglial cells. These observations confirm findings by others [38-42], showing a lesion-induced recruitment of microglial progenitors of bone marrow origin into the infarcted cortex. Recently it has been suggested that these microglial progenitors would not enter into the bloodstream or cross the blood-brain barrier under normal physiological conditions [43,44]. However, BM-derived cells have been reported to infiltrate non-irradiated normal brain [45-47], and CD45high leukocytes are routinely detected in unmanipulated, perfused brains by flow cytometry [24,27]. Although focal cerebral ischemia disrupts the blood brain barrier [33,39], and irradiation in itself preconditions the brain for cells to infiltrate the neuropil [43,44], we in line with earlier findings [44] observed only sporadic GFP+ cells in contralateral, non-ischemic cortex and in the brains of unmanipulated BM-chimeric mice. Furthermore, numbers of CD11b+CD45high macrophages/granulocytes recruited to ischemic cortex 24 hours after pMCAO were not different between BM-chimeric mice and non-irradiated mice. This suggests that any damage induced by irradiation alone was insufficient to trigger excessive entry of BM-derived cells into the CNS.
###end p 83
###begin p 84
###xml 293 295 293 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 570 572 570 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 111 115 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 155 159 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 453 457 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 564 568 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 648 652 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Our observation of a significant reduction in microglial population in the cortex of unmanipulated BM-chimeric mice compared to unmanipulated non-chimeric mice indicates that irradiation might impair microglial turnover in normal brain. This is supported by observations by Wirenfeldt et al. [24], who reported that microglial numbers were reduced by approximately 30% in unmanipulated contralateral hippocampi of perforant pathway-lesioned BM-chimeric mice. That study also reported a lesion-induced impairment of the mitotic capacity of microglia in BM-chimeric mice [24]. Taken together, we ascribe the reduced microglial numbers in BM-chimeric mice to irradiation damage.
###end p 84
###begin p 85
###xml 36 38 36 38 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 179 181 179 181 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 276 278 276 278 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 689 691 689 691 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 692 694 692 694 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 847 849 847 849 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 850 852 850 852 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 853 855 853 855 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 954 956 954 956 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 957 959 957 959 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 960 962 960 962 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 963 965 963 965 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 1142 1144 1142 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 1145 1147 1145 1147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 1360 1362 1360 1362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 1363 1365 1363 1365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 743 747 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 993 997 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The quantification of BM-derived GFP+ cells was done by use of an approximated stereological approach, and by use of flow cytometry. The results show that approximately 2/3 of GFP+ cells are lost during tissue processing procedures prior to flow cytometric analysis. Since GFP+ cells located in perivascular aggregates were not included in the approximated stereological analysis, cell loss using flow cytometry might be larger than 2/3. However, the consistency in the data shows that flow cytometry is a reliable and robust tool with which to obtain quantitative data on microglia and infiltrating macrophages/granulocytes in brain pathology, as also has been shown in previous studies [24,27]. Observations made by others using BM-chimeric mice have shown that BM cells have the capacity to differentiate into microglia and perivascular cells [24,39,48], as well as non-myeloid cell types such as endothelial cells, pericytes, astrocytes and neurons [38,42,47,49]. However, in our study of mice with 24-hour survival after pMCAO, we observed no evidence of co-expression of GFP with the astroglial marker GFAP, confirming earlier studies [38,49]. Similarly, we find no clear evidence of co-expression of GFP and vWF/CD31, although such co-expression has been previously reported at later times of observation (3 days - 1 month) after induction of ischemia [38,50].
###end p 85
###begin title 86
Conclusion
###end title 86
###begin p 87
###xml 128 132 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We show that IL-1beta and TNF-alpha are produced by largely non-overlapping subsets of microglia and macrophages after pMCAO in mice. This observation is indicative of activation of distinct signalling pathways in different subpopulations of microglia and macrophages after ischemic stroke. Resolution of these pathways may further the development of cytokine-based therapies in stroke.
###end p 87
