Time-dependent Autoinactivation of Phospho-Thr286-alphaCa2+/Calmodulin-dependent Protein Kinase II*
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 2 4 2 4 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>

###xml 1296 1299 1292 1295 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286</sub>
###xml 1400 1402 1392 1394 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 1461 1463 1453 1455 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 1484 1487 1476 1479 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286</sup>
###xml 1588 1591 1576 1579 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286</sup>
###xml 1605 1606 1589 1590 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
Ca2+/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II (alphaCaMKII) is thought to exert its role in memory formation by autonomous Ca2+-independent persistent activity conferred by Thr286 autophosphorylation, allowing the enzyme to remain active even when intracellular [Ca2+] has returned to resting levels. Ca2+ sequestration-induced inhibition, caused by a burst of Thr305/306 autophosphorylation via calmodulin (CaM) dissociation from the Thr305/306 sites, is in conflict with this view. The processes of CaM binding, autophosphorylation, and inactivation are dissected to resolve this conflict. Upon Ca2+ withdrawal, CaM sequential domain dissociation is observed, starting with the rapid release of the first (presumed N-terminal) CaM lobe, thought to be bound at the Thr305/306 sites. The time courses of Thr305/306 autophosphorylation and inactivation, however, correlate with the slow dissociation of the second (presumed C-terminal) CaM lobe. Exposure of the Thr305/306 sites is thus not sufficient for their autophosphorylation. Moreover, Thr305/306 autophosphorylation and autoinactivation are shown to occur in the continuous presence of Ca2+ and bound Ca2+/CaM by time courses similar to those seen following Ca2+ sequestration. Our investigation of the activity and mechanisms of phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII thus shows time-dependent autoinactivation, irrespective of the continued presence of Ca2+ and CaM, allowing a very short, if any, time window for Ca2+/CaM-free phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII activity. Physiologically, the time-dependent autoinactivation mechanisms of phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII (t(1/2) of approximately50 s at 37 degreesC) suggest a transient kinase activity of approximately1 min duration in the induction of long term potentiation and thus memory formation.

###xml 100 101 96 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">N</italic>
###xml 109 110 105 106 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">d</sc>
###xml 193 194 189 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 196 197 192 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
Ca2+/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II (alphaCaMKII)2 is essential in hippocampal learning and N-methyl-d-aspartate receptor-dependent synaptic plasticity, causing long term potentiation (1, 2). The exact mechanisms of alphaCaMKII in memory functions have not yet been identified.
###end p 2
###begin p 3
###xml 138 146 134 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 148 149 144 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 503 504 499 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 608 609 600 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 706 707 694 695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 844 845 832 833 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
alphaCaMKII is a broad specificity Ser/Thr protein kinase, which catalyzes the phosphorylation of over 100 protein and peptide substrates in vitro (3). Uniquely, the CaMKII family possesses two distinct kinase mechanisms. The first mechanism is a "canonical" intrasubunit phosphorylation, commonly found in monomeric kinases, in which the phosphorylatable residue of the substrate bound to the helical subdomain of the catalytic domain at the active site is lined up with the terminal phosphate of ATP (4). Although there is a large number of potential "canonical" substrates for alphaCaMKII at the synapse (5), so far AMPA receptors have been shown to be possible physiological substrates of alphaCaMKII (6). For the purpose of this study, syntide 2, a commonly used peptide substrate derived from phosphorylation site 2 of glycogen synthase (7), was chosen.
###end p 3

###xml 741 742 733 734 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">d</sc>

###xml 885 887 873 875 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1009 1012 997 1000 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286</sup>
###xml 1195 1197 1183 1185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 1199 1201 1187 1189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
The second mechanism, intersubunit autophosphorylation, takes advantage of the oligomeric organization of CaMKII (8). The most important autophosphorylation site in the alpha isoform is Thr286, which resides in the vicinity of the autoinhibitory domain (9). Peptide substrates with homologous sequences to this region have been reported to be phosphorylated by alphaCaMKII. This, however, occurs with a low Vmax, and these substrates show properties of a non-competitive inhibitor with respect to phosphorylation of "canonical" substrates (10) and of Thr286 autophosphorylation itself (11). Examples of such substrates include autocamtide, a peptide substrate derived from the autoinhibitory region (12) and the NR2B subunit of the N-methyl-d-aspartate receptor, which has been identified as a potential physiological target of phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII at the postsynaptic membrane (13). The possible physiological significance of NR2B phosphorylation is not yet known. There is evidence to suggest that Thr286 autophosphorylation is required to achieve full activity of the enzyme, since the unphosphorylatable T286A mutant enzyme has much diminished activity compared with wild type enzyme (14, 15).
###end p 4

###xml 539 541 527 529 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>

###xml 654 656 642 644 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>

###xml 700 703 688 691 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286</sup>
###xml 752 754 736 738 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 790 792 774 776 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 808 810 792 794 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
Thr286 autophosphorylation causes CaM "trapping," a >104-fold increase in the affinity of alphaCaMKII for Ca2+/CaM (16-18). At the same time, Thr286 autophosphorylation is also attributed to confer Ca2+- and CaM-independent persistent "autonomous" kinase activity to alphaCaMKII. However, due to the extremely high affinity of phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII for Ca2+/CaM, [Ca2+] of <10 nm is required to achieve full dissociation of Ca2+/CaM, since CaM trapping occurs by virtue of Ca2+ trapping (19). Partial activity measured upon partial Ca2+ withdrawal therefore may not always reflect Ca2+/CaM-free enzyme (9). Furthermore, the physiological resting [Ca2+] range is 50-100 nm; therefore, phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII is likely always to have residual Ca2+/CaM bound. This may be partially Ca2+-saturated CaM (19).

###xml 607 609 599 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 869 871 857 859 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 893 900 881 888 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">305/306</sup>
###xml 953 956 941 944 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286</sup>
###xml 1019 1021 1003 1005 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
Persistent autonomous activity conferred by Thr286 autophosphorylation is thought to enable alphaCaMKII to function as a memory molecule (20, 21). In contrast, however, following the development of chemical long term potentiation, rapid inactivation has also been reported (22). The extent of an autonomous activity is further obscured by the finding that Ca2+ sequestration induces a burst of autophosphorylation at residues Thr305/306, followed by a loss of activity (23). Moreover, when examined across a broad range of [Ca2+], the Ca2+/CaM dependence of phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII activity is apparent (19). It is thus vital to establish the mechanisms of activation and inactivation of alphaCaMKII at the molecular level in order to understand how it may function physiologically in learning and memory. To this end, it is necessary to dissect the mechanisms of Ca2+/CaM dissociation, Thr305/306 autophosphorylation, and inactivation of phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII and to establish the time window for autonomous Ca2+/CaM-independent activity.

The average density reading for each band on the Western blot and a reading for the background was taken. The following formula was used to calculate relative density on an inverted scale: (Dmax - Dt)/(Dmax - DB), where Dmax represents the average density of the darkest band, Dt is the average density at a particular time point, and DB is the average density of the background. Thr305/306 autophosphorylation was also detected during the period of inactivation. Since the Thr305/306 sites are located in the CaM binding domain, it was expected that Ca2+/CaM may be displaced by Thr305/306 autophosphorylation. We thus checked if Ca2+/CaM remained bound or dissociated during inactivation, using DA-CaM (see above). The fluorescence of DA-CaM is strongly quenched when bound to a target in a compact conformation, due to the donor and acceptor labels on the N and C lobes of CaM being in a closer proximity than the critical distance between them for resonance energy transfer. Unbound CaM is more extended, and less energy transfer is shown by the greater, unquenched fluorescence. A complex of Ca2+/DA-CaM.alphaCaMKII also has high fluorescence (relative fluorescence 1), since CaM is bound in an extended conformation (17). Fig. 7 shows that upon the addition of ATP, which results in immediate Thr286 autophosphorylation and Ca2+/CaM trapping, the fluorescence of DA-CaM becomes quenched, indicating that Ca2+/DA-CaM is bound in a compact conformation. It was expected that at least one CaM lobe, assumed to be the N lobe covering the Thr305/306 sites, dissociates, allowing CaM to adopt the extended conformation during Thr305/306 autophosphorylation. It is clearly seen however, that DA-CaM fluorescence remained quenched in the autoinhibited and presumed Thr286/305/306-phosphoenzyme complex, as monitored for 4000 s at 21 degreesC (not shown) and 1600 s at 37 degreesC (Fig. 7). In the latter conditions, as shown above, autoinactivation occurred with a t(1/2) of 50 s. Ca2+/DA-CaM supports both Thr286 and Thr305/306 autophosphorylation (data not shown). These data show that Thr305/306 autophosphorylation of phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII proceeds in the presence of bound Ca2+/CaM, contrary to previous reports suggesting that CaM dissociation is a prerequisite for it to occur.

###xml 824 826 794 796 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 841 842 811 812 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</italic>
###xml 960 962 930 932 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 1138 1141 1108 1111 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286</sup>
###xml 1339 1342 1309 1312 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286</sup>
###xml 1392 1394 1362 1364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
First, the conditions of phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII formation were studied. In our observation, Thr286 autophosphorylation and phosphorylation of an exogenous peptide substrate have similar Ca2+ dependences with Km values of approximately500 nm for Ca2+. This value is typical for the [Ca2+] responsible for activation. It is, however, at variance with the previously reported Km or EC50 values for Ca2+ for Thr286 autophosphorylation of 1.6 mum (26) and 2 mum (28), obtained by the measurement of "autonomous" activity and Thr286 autophosphorylation by Western blotting, respectively. An n value of approximately8 for Ca2+ activation was measured, suggesting that the Ca2+ sites from two CaM molecules are simultaneously involved, supporting the previously determined intersubunit mechanism for Thr286 autophosphorylation (29). Such a high n value has previously been reported for the measurement of "autonomous" activity in the presence of phosphatase only (26). These discrepancies are probably related to "autonomous" activity measurements being greatly dependent on experimental conditions. Our data furthermore clearly show that Thr286 autophosphorylation occurs immediately upon activation and that it proceeds regardless of the presence of saturating exogenous peptide substrate, demonstrating the highly preferential nature of Thr286 autophosphorylation over an exogenous substrate (27).

Autoinactivation of phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII was investigated in two different conditions. Ca2+ sequestration and Ca2+-induced autoinactivation had common and distinct features; both were characterized by Thr305/306 autophosphorylation, but the fate of CaM was distinct. Upon Ca2+ sequestration, Ca2+/CaM sequential lobe dissociation occurred, whereas in the presence of Ca2+, Ca2+/CaM remained trapped in phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII during Thr305/306 autophosphorylation and autoinactivation.

###begin p 70
###xml 40 42 40 42 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>

###xml 703 710 699 706 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">305/306</sup>
###xml 769 776 765 772 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">305/306</sup>
###xml 822 824 818 820 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 858 865 854 861 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">305/306</sup>
###xml 967 975 963 971 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="S1">Scheme 1</xref>
###xml 1022 1029 1018 1025 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">305/306</sup>
In the mechanism of inactivation upon Ca2+ sequestration following initial activation by Ca2+/CaM, one CaM lobe was shown to dissociate rapidly, but the second lobe remained attached, probably by virtue of a high affinity Ca2+ binding site in trapped CaM (19). The structures of the Ca2+/CaM.peptide complexes of peptides derived from the CaM binding domain of alphaCaMKII show anti-parallel binding (30), suggesting that the N lobe of CaM is attached to the C-terminal region of the CaM binding domain in the proximity of or over the Thr305/306 sites. Thus, according to previous models, dissociation of the N lobe, which is likely to correspond to the rapid phase of CaM dissociation, should allow Thr305/306 autophosphorylation. If N lobe dissociation controlled Thr305/306 autophosphorylation, it would thus be rapid (31). However, this was not seen. Thr305/306 autophosphorylation correlated with the slow phase of dissociation, assumed to represent the C lobe (Scheme 1). Thus, our data show that exposure of the Thr305/306 residues is not sufficient to allow their autophosphorylation.

Mechanisms of auto-inactivation of Ca2+/CaM.phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII. Two alternative pathways of autoinactivation leading to Thr305/306 autophosphorylation are illustrated from the perspective of a single kinase domain. The top and bottom ellipses represent the beta-sheeted propeller domain and the alpha-helical substrate binding domain connected by a hinge and with the ATP binding site in between (4). The pathway in the left column includes initial rapid dissociation of the CaM N lobe followed by a slow conformational change accompanying the dissociation of the CaM C lobe, allowing a conformational change permissive for Thr305/306 autophosphorylation to occur. This path results in an inactivated enzyme complex free of CaM. 1, active Ca2+/CaM.phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII; the autoinhibitory domain is detached from the kinase domain and is Ca2+/CaM-bound. 2, upon Ca2+ chelation by EGTA, rapid dissociation of the N lobe of CaM is seen. 2b, slow dissociation of CaM C lobe; slow conformational change positions Thr305/306 as a "canonical" substrate, adjacent to the ATP binding pocket. 2c, Thr305/306 autophosphorylation of Ca2+/CaM-free kinase. In the right-hand column, Thr305/306 autophosphorylation of the Ca2+/CaM-bound phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII is shown. 3, slow conformational change leads to the conformation permissible for Thr305/306-autophosphorylation.

###xml 813 815 795 797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
Is complete dissociation of CaM required for Thr305/306 autophosphorylation? Thr305/306 autophosphorylation is shown here to proceed in the continued presence of Ca2+ with rates similar to those in its absence. Thr305/306 autophosphorylation observed in the presence of Ca2+ was expected to occur with the concomitant dissociation of at least the CaM lobe that binds at or near residues Thr305/306. Our DA-CaM experiments, however, showed no detectable change in the compact structure of Ca2+/CaM, indicating that Ca2+/CaM remained bound throughout the experiment, approximately30 min at 37 degreesC. Despite predictions, Ca2+/CaM dissociation was not required for Thr305/306 autophosphorylation to occur. Interestingly, the rate of Thr305/306 autophosphorylation resembled that of Ca2+/CaM dissociation in Ca2+ (17), suggesting that CaM binding dynamics may play a role.
###end p 72
###begin p 73

###xml 669 671 657 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
Since autoinactivation is contemporaneous with the slow dissociation of the second CaM lobe, it is further evident that rapid dissociation of the presumed N lobe of CaM does not cause inhibition of phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII. It is important to consider whether the state in which phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII has CaM attached by one lobe only (presumed to be the C lobe) has any special properties in terms of activity and if it limits the rate of Thr305/306 autophosphorylation. Although the existence of stable, partially Ca2+-saturated CaM-bound phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII has been demonstrated at Ca2+ concentrations that correspond to resting intracellular [Ca2+] (19), further work is required to establish their functional significance.
###end p 73

###xml 117 119 117 119 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>

###xml 382 384 382 384 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 423 425 423 425 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
How does autonomous activity fit into the observed processes? Interestingly, whether autonomy is considered during Ca2+ sequestration or in the continuous presence of Ca2+, it is difficult to find a time window for such a process. The enzyme is not inactive until both CaM lobes have dissociated; thus, C lobe-attached partially Ca2+-saturated CaM may be the active species. Upon Ca2+ dissociation from the high affinity Ca2+ site and the subsequent dissociation of the second CaM lobe, inactivation occurs, and thus there appears to be no time window for autonomous activity.
###end p 74
###begin p 75
###xml 18 20 18 20 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>

###xml 321 323 313 315 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 431 432 422 423 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 449 451 440 442 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 488 489 479 480 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
The activity of Ca2+/CaM-bound phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII also declines in time. It is unclear at present by what mechanism phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII becomes inactivated upon the continued presence of Ca2+/CaM. Inactivation in the presence of Ca2+ has been shown to occur by self-association (32). Autoinactivation in Ca2+ observed here, however, does not appear to be the same process as self-association, which was seen at 10 mum ATP and pH 6.3 (32). Our experimental conditions of 1 mm ATP and pH 7.0 would not be expected to give rise to self-association.
###end p 75
###begin p 76
###xml 95 102 95 102 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">305/306</sup>
###xml 198 205 198 205 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">305/306</sup>
###xml 298 300 298 300 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 358 369 358 369 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286/305/306</sup>
###xml 625 636 617 628 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286/305/306</sup>
###xml 705 712 693 700 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">305/306</sup>
###xml 720 722 708 710 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 801 803 789 791 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 805 807 793 795 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
As seen in the Arrhenius plots, the rates of autoinactivation correlated well with those of Thr305/306 autophosphorylation. This suggests that similar intermediates and mechanisms may lead up to Thr305/306 autophosphorylation and autoinactivation irrespective of the presence or absence of bound Ca2+/CaM. It is worth noting that the existence of phospho-Thr286/305/306-alphaCaMKII itself has not been directly confirmed, since detection of autophosphorylation by immunotechniques does not allow definitive determination of whether the same subunit of alphaCaMKII becomes autophosphorylated in all three sites. If phospho-Thr286/305/306-alphaCaMKII can be reactivated by selective dephosphorylation at Thr305/306, the Ca2+/CaM-bound and free forms may have different susceptibilities to phosphatases (33, 34).
###end p 76
###begin p 77
###xml 95 97 91 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 215 217 211 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 305 307 297 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 342 349 334 341 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">305/306</sup>
###xml 371 373 363 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
The NR2B subunit of the NMDA receptor has been reported to fix alphaCaMKII in an active state (35). It has, however, also been shown that the resulting activity is very low compared with that with other substrates (11), that NR2B in fact is an effective non-competitive inhibitor of alphaCaMKII activity (10), and that it does not prevent Thr305/306 autophosphorylation (10) and is thus unlikely to prevent the above described autoinhibition.
###end p 77

###xml 507 514 503 510 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">305/306</sup>
It is concluded that Ca2+/CaM binding does not determine the rate of Thr305/306 autophosphorylation but that slow conformational changes in the phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII enzyme result in a conformation permissible to Thr305/306-autophosphorylation with or without Ca2+ and CaM present. Thr305/306 autophosphorylation may be the common process that is responsible for autoinhibition both in the presence and absence of Ca2+ (Scheme 1). Further questions do, however, still remain as to by what mechanism Thr305/306 autophosphorylation may cause inactivation.
###end p 78
###begin p 79
###xml 26 29 26 29 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">286</sup>
###xml 277 279 257 259 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 461 463 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 689 696 649 656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 698 700 658 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
In conclusion, phospho-Thr286-alphaCaMKII, universally the functionally essential form of alphaCaMKII, appears to be programmed for autoinactivation, having a brief time window of approximately1 min to exert its activity. After this, new enzyme is required to respond to new Ca2+ stimuli. These observations suggest a transient activity by alphaCaMKII at the synapse and may explain why new protein synthesis is required from local mRNA in synaptic plasticity (36). Coincidentally, transient activation of alphaCaMKII in single dendritic spines lasting for approximately1 min has been reported during long term potentiation; however, the mechanism of inactivation has yet to be determined in vivo (34).
