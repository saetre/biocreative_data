###begin title 3
Background
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 4 5 4 5 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</underline>
###xml 11 12 11 12 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</underline>
###xml 22 23 22 23 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</underline>
###xml 28 29 28 29 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</underline>
###xml 249 255 245 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Commd1</italic>
###xml 318 322 314 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">VEGF</italic>
###xml 324 327 320 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PGK</italic>
###xml 332 337 328 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Bnip3</italic>
###xml 221 226 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
The Copper Metabolism MURR1 Domain containing 1 protein COMMD1 has been associated with copper homeostasis, NF-kappaB signaling, and sodium transport. Recently, we identified COMMD1 as a novel protein in HIF-1 signaling. Mouse embryos deficient for Commd1 have increased expression of hypoxia/HIF-regulated genes i.e. VEGF, PGK and Bnip3. Hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs) are master regulators of oxygen homeostasis, which control angiogenesis, erythropoiesis, glycolysis and cell survival/proliferation under normal and pathologic conditions. Although HIF activity is mainly controlled by ubiquitination and protein degradation by the von Hippel Lindau (pVHL) tumor suppressor gene other mechanisms have recently been identified that regulate HIF signaling independently of pVHL.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Principal Findings
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 192 193 185 186 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
Here we characterized the mechanism by which COMMD1 regulates HIF-1alpha protein degradation. We show that COMMD1 competes with the chaperone heat shock protein HSP90beta for binding to the NH2-terminal DNA-binding and heterodimerization domain of HIF-1alpha to regulate HIF-1alpha stability together with HSP70. Inhibition of HSP90 activity with 17-Allylamino-17-demethoxygeldanamycin (17-AAG) increased COMMD1-mediated HIF-1alpha degradation independent of ubiquitin and pVHL.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Conclusion/Significance
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 100 101 97 98 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
These data reveal a novel role for COMMD1 in conjunction with HSP90beta/HSP70 in the ubiquitin and O2-independent regulation of HIF-1alpha.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Introduction
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 0 2 0 2 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Co</underline>
###xml 7 8 7 8 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</underline>
###xml 18 19 18 19 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</underline>
###xml 24 25 24 25 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</underline>
###xml 95 98 95 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Burstein1">[1]</xref>
###xml 214 217 214 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Burstein1">[1]</xref>
###xml 219 222 219 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Maine1">[2]</xref>
###xml 508 511 508 511 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Maine2">[3]</xref>
###xml 512 515 512 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Maine3">[5]</xref>
Copper Metabolism MURR1 Domain 1 protein (COMMD1) is the prototype of the COMMD protein family [1]. This family of proteins is highly conserved between eukaryotes, and some members have been identified in protozoa [1], [2]. The COMM proteins are ubiquitously expressed and characterized by their conserved and distinctive domain (COMM domain) located at the carboxy-terminus. The COMMD domain is important for protein-protein interactions, nuclear export and postranslational modifications of COMMD proteins [3]-[5].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 182 185 182 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Klomp1">[6]</xref>
###xml 187 190 187 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-vanDeSluis1">[7]</xref>
###xml 395 398 395 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-deBie1">[8]</xref>
###xml 400 403 400 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Tao1">[9]</xref>
###xml 519 522 519 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-deBie1">[8]</xref>
###xml 118 122 <span type="species:ncbi:9615">dogs</span>
###xml 336 344 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
To date the best-characterized COMMD protein is COMMD1. We identified COMMD1 (previous called MURR1) to be mutated in dogs with the hepatic copper storage disorder, copper toxicosis [6], [7]. Recent observations suggest that COMMD1 regulates the biliary copper excretion via ATP7B, a P-type ATPase copper transporter that is mutated in patients with the copper storage disorder Wilson's disease [8], [9]. It appears that COMMD1 participates in the protein quality control of ATP7B by mediating the proteolysis of ATP7B [8].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 135 138 131 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Burstein1">[1]</xref>
###xml 140 143 136 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Maine3">[5]</xref>
###xml 145 149 141 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Mao1">[10]</xref>
###xml 169 176 165 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 205 211 201 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Commd1</italic>
###xml 227 231 223 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-vandeSluis1">[11]</xref>
###xml 245 251 241 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Commd1</italic>
###xml 390 396 386 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Commd1</italic>
###xml 536 541 532 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Vegfa</italic>
###xml 543 547 539 543 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Pgk1</italic>
###xml 549 554 545 550 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AldoA</italic>
###xml 556 561 552 557 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Bnip3</italic>
###xml 656 660 652 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-vandeSluis1">[11]</xref>
###xml 222 226 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 261 265 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
COMMD1 also inhibits basal and cytokine induced NF-kappaB activity by promoting the ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation of RelA [1], [5], [10]. To understand the in vivo role of COMMD1 we generated Commd1 deficient mice [11]. Remarkably, Commd1 knockout mice are embryonically lethal, and show abnormal embryonic development, including placental defects. Gene expression analysis in Commd1 deficient embryos revealed deregulation of the hypoxia inducible factor (HIF) pathway by upregulation of canonical HIF-1 target genes like Vegfa, Pgk1, AldoA, Bnip3 and others. Conversely, overexpression of COMMD1 resulted in downregulation of HIF-1 activity [11].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 260 264 260 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Semenza1">[12]</xref>
###xml 406 410 399 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Wang1">[13]</xref>
###xml 519 523 505 509 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Wang2">[14]</xref>
###xml 806 810 784 788 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Bruick1">[15]</xref>
###xml 811 815 789 793 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Maxwell1">[18]</xref>
###xml 1298 1302 1264 1268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Wang2">[14]</xref>
###xml 1304 1308 1270 1274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Pugh1">[19]</xref>
HIF-1 is a key factor in oxygen homeostasis. Under conditions of chronic or acute oxygen deprivation (hypoxia), HIF-1 regulates the transcriptional activation of target genes involved in angiogenesis, erythropoiesis, glycolysis and cell survival/proliferation [12]. HIF-1alpha is a basic-helix-loop-helix (bHLH)-PAS (Per, ARNT, SIM) protein and forms together with HIF-1beta the transcription factor HIF-1 [13]. The N-terminal bHLH and PAS domain of HIF-1alpha and HIF-1beta mediates heterodimerization and DNA binding [14]. The cellular HIF-1alpha protein level is tightly regulated by oxygen. During normoxia HIF-1alpha is continuously hydroxylated by prolyl hydroxylases (PHD1-3), which, in turn recruits the VCB-Cul2 (von Hippel-Lindau protein (pVHL), Elongin B/C, Cullin2) E3 ubiquitin ligase complex [15]-[18]. VCB-Cul2 ubiquitinates HIF-1alpha and thereby targets it for 26S proteasomal degradation. HIF-1alpha is hydroxylated at conserved proline residues (402 and 564) located within the oxygen-dependent degradation domain (ODDD). Under hypoxic conditions, hydroxylase activities of PHDs are attenuated, resulting in HIF-1alpha stabilization and eventually the formation of active HIF-1 that regulate gene expression of HIF-1 target genes by binding to hypoxia responsive elements (HREs) [14], [19].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 202 206 198 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Liu1">[20]</xref>
###xml 208 212 204 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-vandeSluis2">[21]</xref>
###xml 590 594 569 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Picard1">[22]</xref>
###xml 788 792 763 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Fath1">[23]</xref>
###xml 793 797 768 772 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Minet1">[28]</xref>
Although HIF-1 is generally regulated by pVHL, it has become apparent that more pathways are involved in the regulation of basal HIF-1alpha levels in an oxygen- and pVHL-independent manner (reviewed in [20], [21]). Recent studies showed that the heatshock protein 90beta (HSP90beta) binds HIF-1alpha and is necessary for proper maturation and stabilization of HIF-1alpha under normoxic and hypoxic conditions. HSP90beta is a molecular chaperone that is involved in many different cellular processes and contributes to post-translation maturation and folding of client proteins (reviewed by [22]). HSP90 inhibitors, e.g. geldanamycin or 17-Allylamino-17-demethoxygeldanamycin (17-AAG), both induce ubiquitin-independent and ubiquitin-dependent degradation of HIF-alpha independent of pVHL [23]-[28].
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 156 157 148 149 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 447 454 425 432 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
In this study, we further characterized the role of COMMD1 in HIF-1alpha proteolysis and demonstrated that COMMD1 regulates HIF-1alpha stability in a pVHL/O2 and ubiquitin-independent manner by competing with HSP90beta for binding to HIF-1alpha. Our findings suggest that COMMD1 is associated with the HSP90beta/HSP70 chaperone regulatory axis in regulating HIF-1 pathway activity, and explain why loss of COMMD1 attenuates HIF-1alpha proteolysis in vivo.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Results
###end title 16
###begin title 17
COMMD1 mediates HIF-1alpha protein stability
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 158 165 150 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 170 178 162 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 349 353 337 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-vandeSluis1">[11]</xref>
###xml 429 430 417 418 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 514 523 498 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g001">Figure 1A</xref>
###xml 555 556 539 540 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1283 1287 1243 1247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-vandeSluis1">[11]</xref>
We previously demonstrated that COMMD1 physically interacts with HIF-1alpha and that loss of COMMD1 is associated with increased HIF-1alpha protein stability in vivo and in vitro. To further define the role of COMMD1 in HIF-1alpha regulation, we generated stable HeLa COMMD1 knock down cells by transfection of pSuper-shRNA, as described previously [11]. This cell line and a control HeLa cell line were cultured for 18 h at 1% O2, which resulted in comparable HIF-1alpha protein stabilization in both cell lines (Figure 1A). Upon reoxygenation (ambient O2), HIF-1alpha levels decreased in both cell lines but in COMMD1 knockdown cells the rate of HIF-1alpha degradation was markedly reduced. After 5 min of normoxic exposure, high HIF-1alpha level was still seen in COMMD1 knockdown cells, whereas HIF-1alpha was almost undetectable in the control cell line. In both cells lines, higher molecular weight species of HIF-1alpha were detected by immunoblotting that appeared more abundant in reoxygenated COMMD1-silenced cells. The identity of these higher molecular weight band is unknown at present but may represent posttranslational modification such as ubiquitination. These findings are in agreement with a role for COMMD1 in HIF-1alpha degradation as observed in HEK 293T cells [11]
###end p 18

###begin p 21
###xml 118 121 118 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-deBie1">[8]</xref>
###xml 398 399 390 391 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 431 432 423 424 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 617 626 605 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g001">Figure 1B</xref>
###xml 744 753 728 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g001">Figure 1B</xref>
Recently we suggested that COMMD1 promotes the proteolysis of newly synthesized and incorrectly folded ATP7B proteins [8]. To investigate whether COMMD1 is also involved in the stability of newly synthesized HIF-1alpha, we studied the dynamics of HIF-1alpha maturation at several time-points after oxygen deprivation in control or COMMD1 knockdown cells. These cells, cultured under normoxia (21% O2), were exposed to hypoxia (1% O2) after removal of the culture medium to obtain rapid deoxygenation. Upon oxygen deprivation, the levels of HIF-1alpha rapidly increased in both cell lines in a time-dependent fashion (Figure 1B). However, HIF-1alpha level in COMMD1 silenced cells were markedly higher at all time-points, than in control cells (Figure 1B). Similar results were obtained with silenced COMMD1 HEK 293T cells (data not shown). Altogether, these data indicate that COMMD1 controls the protein degradation and maturation of HIF-1alpha.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
COMMD1 competes with HSP90beta for binding to HIF-1alpha
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 318 322 296 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Kong1">[25]</xref>
###xml 743 752 696 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g002">Figure 2A</xref>
###xml 1194 1203 1137 1146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g002">Figure 2B</xref>
###xml 1325 1334 1261 1270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g002">Figure 2C</xref>
###xml 1691 1700 1612 1621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g002">Figure 2D</xref>
We recently showed that COMMD1 directly binds to the N-terminal region of HIF-1alpha and competes with HIF-1beta for binding to HIF-1alpha (van de Sluis et al., submitted). The heat shock protein HSP90beta binds also to this region of HIF-1alpha and is essential for the stability and maturation of HIF-1alpha protein [25]. Since both proteins bind to same region, we examined the interaction between HIF-1alpha and COMMD1 in relation to HSP90beta levels. COMMD1-Ha and Flag-HIF-1alpha were transfected in HEK 293T cells in the presence of increasing amounts of Ha-HSP90beta. COMMD1-Ha co-immunoprecipitated with HIF-1alpha under all conditions, but the level of COMMD1-HIF-1alpha binding gradually decreased with increasing HSP90beta levels (Figure 2A). The competition between HSP90beta and COMMD1 for HIF binding could be reversed when culturing in the presence of an HSP90 specific inhibitor, 17-Allylamino-17-demethoxygeldanamycin (17-AAG). Moreover, the interaction between COMMD1 and HIF-1alpha was enhanced after 17-AAG treatment compared to mock treated cells or cells not transfected with HSP90beta. This interaction was even more pronounced when proteasomal degradation was blocked (Figure 2B). Consistent with a competition model, we found that overexpression of COMMD1 decreased HSP90beta binding to HIF-1alpha (Figure 2C). To address whether COMMD1/HSP90 mediated HIF-1alpha degradation was dependent on pVHL we used a hydroxy-prolyl HIF-1alpha mutant (P402A/P564A), which is not targeted for pVHL-mediated proteasomal degradation. HSP90beta also decreased the binding of COMMD1 with the hydroxy-prolyl HIF-1alpha mutant and was completely abrogated in the presence of 17-AAG (Figure 2D) indicating that the effect of COMMD1 was independent of proly-hydroxylation and pVHL binding to HIF-1alpha. These results show that the binding of COMMD1 to HIF-1alpha is in competition with active HSP90beta, in a dose-dependent manner.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
HSP90beta competes with COMMD1 for binding to HIF-1alpha.

###begin title 26
COMMD1 promotes HIF-1alpha degradation in an ubiquitin independent manner
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 225 234 214 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g003">Figure 3A</xref>
###xml 406 415 395 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g003">Figure 3A</xref>
###xml 469 472 458 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Maine2">[3]</xref>
###xml 1002 1011 975 984 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g003">Figure 3B</xref>
###xml 1157 1166 1123 1132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g003">Figure 3B</xref>
###xml 1437 1441 1395 1399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Kulka1">[29]</xref>
###xml 1596 1605 1544 1553 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g003">Figure 3C</xref>
###xml 1866 1875 1803 1812 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g003">Figure 3C</xref>
###xml 1332 1347 <span type="species:ncbi:10029">Chinese hamster</span>
Since HSP90beta regulates the HIF-1alpha protein stability in a pVHL-independent manner we investigated the stability of the hydroxy-prolyl HIF-1alpha mutant (P402A/P564A) in HEK 293T cells stably overexpressing COMMD1-Flag (Figure 3A). HEK-293T-COMMD1 cells expressed approximately 3.5-fold higher COMMD1 levels than control cells. The endogenous COMMD1 protein was repressed in HEK293-COMMD1-Flag cells (Figure 3A), a phenomenon also observed and described by others [3]. We expressed Flag-HIF-1alpha (P402A/P564A) in both cell lines in the presence or absence of 17-AAG. After adding cyclohexamide (CHX), to block new protein synthesis, we assessed whether COMMD1 overexpression resulted in a decrease of HIF-1alpha stability. No clear difference in HIF-1alpha stability could be detected between COMMD1 overexpression and control HEK 293T cells in the absence of 17-AAG, whereas the half-life of HIF-1alpha was markedly reduced in control cells and COMMD1 overexpression cells treated with 17-AAG (Figure 3B). However, inhibition of HSP90beta in COMMD1 overexpressing cells significantly decreased the half-life of HIF-1alpha compared to control cells (Figure 3B). Next, we studied whether COMMD1 promotes the degradation of HIF-1alpha in an ubiquitin-independent manner by studying the expression of endogenous HIF-1alpha in a Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cell line (Ts20), which contains thermolabile ubiquitin activation enzyme E1 [29]. At non-permissive temperature (39degreesC) the ubiquitin conjugation is dysfunctional and leads to HIF-1alpha stability in an oxygen-independent manner (Figure 3C). Under these conditions HIF-1alpha levels were clearly reduced when HSP90beta activity was inhibited. Interestingly, overexpression of COMMD1 together with 17-AAG treatment considerably decreased endogenous HIF-1alpha levels in the absence of ubiquitination (Figure 3C). These data further demonstrate that COMMD1 mediates HIF-1alpha stability in concert with HSP90beta activity in a manner independent pVHL-mediated polyubiquitination.

###begin title 30
COMMD1 forms a tripartite complex with HSP70 and HIF-1alpha
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 152 156 141 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Kong1">[25]</xref>
###xml 194 205 183 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g002">Figure 2B,D</xref>
###xml 533 542 510 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g004">Figure 4A</xref>
###xml 1024 1033 986 995 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g004">Figure 4B</xref>
###xml 1310 1319 1268 1277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g004">Figure 4C</xref>
Heat shock protein HSP70 also binds to HIF-1alpha in an HSP90beta-dependent manner and facilitates the proteolysis of misfolded and immature HIF-1alpha [25] in a manner similar seen for COMMD1 (Figure 2B,D). We therefore hypothesized that COMMD1 may act as a 'scaffold' protein involved degradation of HIF-1alpha together with HSP70. First we studied the interaction between HSP70 and HIF-1alpha under normal conditions and after attenuating HSP90 activity. Blocking HSP90 clearly increased the binding between HSP70 and HIF-1alpha (Figure 4A). Second, we investigated whether HSP70 and COMMD1 form a protein complex with HIF-1alpha. To address this we sequentially precipitated Flag-HIF-1alpha from cells, in which we co-expressed Flag-HIF-1alpha, HSP70 and COMMD1-Ha. Transfected cells were treated with 17-AAG to block HSP90beta activity prior to cell lysis. Flag-precipitates were eluted with Flag-peptide and subsequently re-precipitated with anti-Ha to enrich for COMMD1 containing immunocomplexes. As demonstrated in Figure 4B, HSP70 could be found in complex with COMMD1 in cells expressing HIF-1alpha. To delineate whether COMMD1 is associated with HSP70, HSP70 was immunoprecipitated from HEK 293T cell lysates expressing HSP70-V5 and COMMD1-Ha. COMMD1 was clearly present in HSP70-immunoprecipates (Figure 4C). These data indicate that COMMD1 can form with HSP70 a tripartite complex with HIF-1alpha.

###begin title 34
Discussion
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 191 195 187 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-vandeSluis2">[21]</xref>
###xml 299 305 295 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Commd1</italic>
###xml 407 411 399 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-vandeSluis1">[11]</xref>
###xml 723 724 696 697 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 759 767 732 740 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 965 969 934 938 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Kong1">[25]</xref>
###xml 971 975 940 944 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Zhou1">[30]</xref>
###xml 977 981 946 950 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Kong2">[31]</xref>
COMMD1 has been recognized as a protein that plays an important role in wide range of biological processes, including copper metabolism, NF-kappaB signaling and sodium transport (reviewed in [21]). We recently identified COMMD1 as a novel protein regulating HIF-1 signaling as embryos deficient for Commd1 display upregulation of HIF-1 target genes with corresponding increased HIF-1alpha protein stability [11]. In this study, we further characterized the mechanism of COMMD1 in HIF-1alpha regulation and demonstrated that COMMD1 competes with HSP90beta for binding to HIF-1alpha to facilitate HIF-1alpha degradation. Overexpression of COMMD1 enhances the protein degradation of HIF-1alpha in an HSP90-dependent but pVHL/O2 and ubiquitin-independent manner (Figure 3). Previous studies reported that upon HSP90 inhibition, a protein complex consisting of HSP70 and HIF-1alpha is formed to facilitate 20S proteasomal degradation in an ubiquitin-independent fashion [25], [30], [31]. Here we showed that COMMD1 is in complex with HSP70/HIF-1alpha suggesting that COMMD1 acts in concert with HSP70 to facilitate proteolysis of HIF-1alpha in an HSP90-dependent manner.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 238 247 230 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g001">Figure 1A</xref>
###xml 666 670 642 646 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-vandeSluis1">[11]</xref>
###xml 1069 1078 1033 1042 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0007332.s001">Figure S1</xref>
###xml 1526 1530 1478 1482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Kong2">[31]</xref>
###xml 1701 1705 1645 1649 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Cho1">[32]</xref>
###xml 1836 1837 1776 1777 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
The effect of COMMD1 on HIF-1alpha stability is also evident during the early transitions between hypoxia and normoxia and vice versa. In cells reoxygenation in the absence COMMD1 results in a delay in the rate of HIF-1alpha degradation (Figure 1A). It is well established that under normal oxygen conditions, HIF-1alpha levels are downregulated by polyubiquitination and proteosomal degradation (18). We therefore hypothesize that the higher molecular weight protein, detected by the HIF-1alpha antibody, represents polyubiquitinated-HIF-1alpha species. We have previously observed the same HIF-1alpha pattern in HEK 293T COMMD1 deficient cells upon reoxygenation ([11]). The persistence of polyubiquitinated HIF-1alpha in COMMD1 deficient cells may suggest that COMMD1 does not affect HIF-1alpha polyubiquitination per se but assists the proteasomal degradation of HIF-1alpha. This could also explain why HIF-1 transcriptional activity is higher in the absence of COMMD1 (11). Together with our observation that COMMD1 interacts with the 20S proteasome (supplemental Figure S1) we hypothesize that COMMD1 may escort HIF-1alpha protein to the proteasome. Furthermore, the increased interaction between COMMD1 and 20S after blocking proteasomal degradation, supports a more general role for COMMD1 in the 20S proteasome. Ubiquitin-independent degradation of HIF-1alpha directly by the 20S proteosome has also been reported by others and seems to be involved in regulating constitutive degradation of HIF-1alpha during hypoxia [31]. While HIF-1alpha can interact with the 20S proteasome subunit PMSA7 (alpha-4 subunit), it is unknown yet whether this is a direct process or regulated by other proteins [32]. Although more studies are needed, these data shed light on a novel pathway of HIF-1alpha regulation by the 20S proteasome in an O2 and ubiquitin-independent manner.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 127 131 120 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Liu2">[26]</xref>
###xml 261 262 250 251 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 516 525 497 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0007332.s002">Figure S2</xref>
A competition with HSP90beta for binding to HIF-1alpha was also reported for Receptor for activated protein kinase C 1 (RACK1) [26]. Similar as for COMMD1, RACK1 reduces HIF-1 transcriptional activity and mediates HIF-1alpha protein degradation independent of O2-and pVHL. However, in constrast to COMMD1, RACK1 recruits the Elongin-C/B ubiquitin ligase complex and regulates HIF-1alpha stability in an ubiquitin-dependent fashion. Importantly, we found no effect of COMMD1 on RACK1-HIF-1alpha binding (supplemental Figure S2) suggesting that COMMD1 does not compete with RACK1 for binding to HIF-1alpha and regulates HIF in a distinct manner.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 99 102 99 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-deBie1">[8]</xref>
Interestingly, COMMD1 also promotes the proteolysis of the copper transporting p-type ATPase ATP7B [8]. This study established a stronger binding between COMMD1 and ATP7B mutants that are associated with mis-localization and protein instability compared to wild-type ATP7B. It has been postulated that COMMD1 is engaged in the quality control and protein folding of newly synthesized ATP7B. Although recent data demonstrated interaction between ATP7B and the molecular chaperones HSP70 and HSP90beta (unpublished data, P. van den Berghe and L.W. Klomp) the mechanism of COMMD1 in regulating ATP7B degradation is largely unknown. Further studies are needed to reveal whether COMMD1 also cooperates with HSP70 and HSP90beta to regulate the structural integrity of ATP7B by assisting the proteolysis of misfolded ATP7B proteins
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 186 194 182 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007332-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 446 450 442 446 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Bos1">[33]</xref>
###xml 452 456 448 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Pouyssegur1">[34]</xref>
###xml 569 573 565 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007332-Semenza2">[35]</xref>
###xml 748 753 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Taken together, this study demonstrates that COMMD1, in complex with HSP70, facilitates the proteasomal degradation of HIF-1alpha in an HSP90-dependent and ubiquitin independent manner (Figure 5). In addition, these findings highlight a potentially novel role of COMMD1 protein in escorting client proteins to the proteasome. Importantly, hypoxia is a hallmark of solid cancers and HIF-1 activity correlates with a poor prognosis in many cancers [33], [34]. Currently, HSP90 inhibitiors (e.g. 17-AAG) are being tested in clinical trials as potential anti-cancer agents [35]. Therefore, further elucidation of the molecular mechanism of HSP90-dependent proteolysis of proteins may reveal additional opportunities for modulation of HIF-1 activity in human disease, including cancer.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
Schematic representation of the mechanism of COMMD1-associated HIF-1alpha degradation.
###end title 40
###begin p 41
Under normoxic conditions, newly synthesized HIF-1alpha binds to HSP70 and HSP90 for proper maturation, but it is immediately degraded by the 26S proteasome after it is hydroxylated and ubiquitinated in a pVHL dependent process. Under hypoxic conditions, HIF-1alpha is not subjected to hydroxyl- and ubiquitin-dependent degradation and heterodimerizes with HIF-1beta, and binds to hypoxia response element (HRE) containing promoters in target genes. Upon reoxygenation HIF-1alpha is ubiquitinated by pVHL, and COMMD1 is bound to HIF-1alpha to facilitate proteasomal degradation. Inactivation of HSP90 (e.g. using 17-AAG or Geldanamycin) leads to incorrect folding of HIF-1alpha and the formation of a heterocomplex with HSP70 and COMMD1, and subsequently degradation by the 20S proteasome. Altogether, in this study we suggest that COMMD1 facilitates the proteasomal degradation of HIF-1alpha after reoxygenation or HSP90 inhibition independent of posttranslational modification by ubiquitin.
###end p 41
