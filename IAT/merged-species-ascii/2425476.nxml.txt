Conserved residues in the delta subunit help the E. coli clamp loader, gamma complex, target primer-template DNA for clamp assembly

The Escherichia coli clamp loader, gamma complex (gamma3deltadelta'lambdapsi), catalyzes ATP-driven assembly of beta clamps onto primer-template DNA (p/tDNA), enabling processive replication. The mechanism by which gamma complex targets p/tDNA for clamp assembly is not resolved. According to previous studies, charged/polar amino acids inside the clamp loader chamber interact with the double-stranded (ds) portion of p/tDNA. We find that dsDNA, not ssDNA, can trigger a burst of ATP hydrolysis by gamma complex and clamp assembly, but only at far higher concentrations than p/tDNA. Thus, contact between gamma complex and dsDNA is necessary and sufficient, but not optimal, for the reaction, and additional contacts with p/tDNA likely facilitate its selection as the optimal substrate for clamp assembly. We investigated whether a conserved sequence-HRVW279QNRR-in delta subunit contributes to such interactions, since Tryptophan-279 specifically cross-links to the primer-template junction. Mutation of delta-W279 weakens gamma complex binding to p/tDNA, hampering its ability to load clamps and promote proccessive DNA replication, and additional mutations in the sequence (delta-R277, delta-R283) worsen the interaction. These data reveal a novel location in the C-terminal domain of the E. coli clamp loader that contributes to DNA binding and helps define p/tDNA as the preferred substrate for the reaction.

INTRODUCTION

###xml 1066 1085 1059 1078 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Pyrococcus furiosus</italic>
###xml 991 1007 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 1040 1064 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 1066 1085 <span type="species:ncbi:2261">Pyrococcus furiosus</span>
Clamps and clamp loaders enhance the ability of DNA polymerase to extend a primer from a few nucleotides to several thousand nucleotides per primer-template binding event, and therefore serve as essential accessory proteins in DNA replication. Clamps are ring-shaped, two or three subunit-containing proteins that encircle the double helix and can move along its length, forming mobile tethers for the polymerase and enabling processive DNA synthesis (1). Clamp loaders are claw-shaped, multi-subunit protein complexes that use ATP to catalyze assembly of clamps onto primed DNA templates for use by the polymerase (2-4). In addition to their role in DNA replication, clamps and clamp loaders play important roles in other processes of DNA metabolism, including DNA repair and recombination, as well as in mechanisms of cell cycle control (5,6). Not surprisingly, these proteins are found in all cells. The structures and mechanisms of action of these proteins from model organisms, such as Escherichia coli (gamma complex and beta clamp), Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Pyrococcus furiosus (RFC complex and PCNA clamp) and the T4 bacteriophage (gp44/62 complex and gp45 clamp), are under active investigation.

In E. coli, the beta clamp comprises two identical subunits that bind head-to-tail to fashion a ring (7). The clamp loader comprises three gamma/tau subunits and one each of the delta, delta', chi and psi subunits; tau is a longer version of gamma, and its extra domain serves to hold core polymerases in a holoenzyme at the replication fork (8); however, a complex containing three gamma subunits is a fully functional clamp loader as well (9-12). The gamma/tau, delta, and delta' proteins form a minimal, functional clamp loader (13); the chi subunit helps coordinate the actions of the clamp loader, primase and single-stranded DNA-binding protein at the replication fork (14,15); the psi subunit, whose function has been rather inscrutable until lately, facilitates productive interactions of the clamp loader with both clamp and DNA during clamp assembly (16). gamma/tau, delta and delta' are arranged in a circle that is closed completely at the C-terminus and open at the N-terminus (17). These proteins are members of the large AAA+ family (ATPases Associated with diverse cellular Activities), which contain two signature N-terminal domains that catalyze ATP binding and hydrolysis (18,19), although only gamma and tau are functional ATPases in the E. coli clamp loader (20-22). In addition to the Walker A and B motifs, each gamma/tau and delta' subunit also contains the conserved SRC motif, which has an 'arginine finger' that participates in the ATPase active site of the neighboring subunit. This arginine finger is necessary for catalysis, and also helps mediate ATP binding and hydrolysis-coupled conformational changes in the clamp loader that drive clamp assembly on primer-template DNA (p/tDNA) (23,24).

The clamp assembly reaction, as currently understood, involves an ordered series of events (Figure 1): (i) the clamp loader binds ATP; (ii) consequently, changes in the clamp loader conformation facilitate binding to the clamp and clamp opening, followed by binding to p/tDNA and positioning it within the open clamp; (iii) p/tDNA binding triggers rapid ATP hydrolysis by the gamma/tau subunits; and (iv) the resultant change in clamp loader conformation facilitates release of the clamp and DNA, linked topologically to each other. While the clamp loader can interact with and act on each of its three substrates-ATP, beta clamp, DNA-individually, one of the more intriguing features of the reaction mechanism is how each substrate modulates clamp loader interactions/actions with the other substrates in a coordinated fashion, resulting in catalysis of clamp assembly on DNA (25). Past studies of gamma complex in the presence of ATP, beta, and/or DNA have clarified both its intrinsic and substrate-mediated structure-function capabilities. For example, in the absence of ATP, gamma complex can bind the beta clamp, but with very low affinity, whereas in the presence of ATP, the interaction occurs with a tight KD approximately 20 nM (16,26,27). This observation is consistent with ATP stabilizing a gamma complex conformation that binds optimally to beta. Also, gamma complex has a basal ATPase rate that increases dramatically following DNA binding (26,28). This occurs both in the absence and presence of beta. In the absence of beta, however, the reaction is inefficient in that only a fraction of gamma complex appears to achieve a conformation in which the gamma subunits catalyze rapid ATP hydrolysis, whereas in the presence of beta, all the gamma subunits catalyze rapid ATP hydrolysis upon DNA binding (23,29,30). This observation is consistent with beta stabilizing a gamma complex conformation that binds optimally to DNA, which in turn leads to maximal stimulation of its ATPase activity, and release of the clamp assembled on DNA. ###xml 1102 1104 1070 1072 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
All the evidence thus far indicates that p/tDNA with a 5' single-stranded overhang-the appropriate target for clamp assembly in DNA replication-specifically stimulates the maximal ATPase activity of gamma complex (30). When p/tDNA enters the central chamber of the clamp loader, gamma and delta' subunits present conserved lysine, arginine and polar residues for interaction with the double-stranded (ds) portion of the DNA (31,32). Mutation of these residues impacts both DNA binding and DNA-dependent ATPase activities of gamma complex, affirming their important role in recognizing the dsDNA and coupling this step with the ATP hydrolysis and clamp/DNA release steps in the reaction (33). The single-stranded (ss) template is thought to exit the clamp loader via a gap between the delta and delta' subunits (Figure 1) (31,33,34), but it is not clear whether there are sites on the clamp loader that specifically recognize this portion of p/tDNA. In an earlier study, we discovered a Tryptophan residue in the delta subunit (delta-W279) that cross-links specifically at the ss/ds junction of p/tDNA (35). Although the binding site for dsDNA on the clamp loader has been well defined, thus far delta-W279 is the only lead to a site that might interact with the ss/dsDNA junction or ssDNA template. If so, this residue should facilitate binding and selection of p/tDNA as the substrate for clamp assembly.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 380 383 368 371 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">279</sub>
###xml 389 397 377 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
In order to investigate the hypothesis that delta-W279 is involved in gamma complex binding to p/tDNA, and subsequent steps in the reaction, we examined the effects of mutating this residue on clamp loader function. Furthermore, delta-W279 is part of a highly conserved sequence in bacterial clamp loaders with several charged and polar residues that might interact with DNA (HRVW279QNRR; Figure 1). We therefore examined the effects of mutating additional residues in the vicinity of delta-W279, in order to determine whether this previously undefined motif plays a role in binding p/tDNA. The data provide useful insights into the clamp assembly mechanism of gamma complex and related clamp loaders.

Escherichia coli Pol III core proteins (alpha,epsilon,theta) (36) and the minimal (gammadeltadelta') and complete (gamma,delta,delta', chi, psi) gamma complexes (10) were reconstituted from pure subunits and then purified from unassembled subunits as described. The beta clamp was purified as described (7). Protein concentrations were determined by Bradford assay or absorbance at 280 nm in 6 M guanidinium-HCl, 20 mM potassium phosphate, pH 6.5. Phosphate-binding protein (PBP) was purified and labeled with 7-diethylamino-3-((((2-maleimidyl)ethyl)amino)carbonyl) coumarin (MDCC) as described previously, except the final Mono-Q column chromatography step was omitted (37).

The p/tDNA triggers rapid ATP hydrolysis by all three gamma subunits in the complex, most efficiently in the presence of beta (30), and this hydrolysis step is coupled to completion of the clamp assembly reaction with release of the clamp.p/tDNA complex (25). Next, in order to assess the influence of these DNAs on gamma complex function, we measured their ability to stimulate its ATPase activity. We used a reporter assay for ATP hydrolysis that is based on the change in fluorescence of MDCC-labeled PBP when it binds Pi. This assay has been used effectively to measure the kinetics of Pi release following ATP hydrolysis in the millisecond time scale (37), and it reveals the rate of ATP hydrolysis when Pi release is a fast step in the reaction. As reported previously, when gamma complex is preincubated with beta and ATP for 1 s prior to addition of p/tDNA (30 nt/105 nt), DNA binding triggers a rapid burst of ATP hydrolysis and Pi release by the three gamma subunits in the first catalytic turnover, which is followed by slower step(s) and steady state (Figure 2B; final concentrations: 0.25 muM gamma complex and 1.4 muM p/tDNA) (29). The burst of ATP hydrolysis is associated with release of the clamp on p/tDNA (29) (in the absence of beta and DNA, or in the presence of beta only, gamma complex has very low basal ATPase activity; Figure 2B). When p/tDNA was replaced by dsDNA (37/37 nt), we found that at 1.4-muM concentration it stimulates gamma complex ATPase activity over the basal level, but a burst phase is barely detectable. Only at much higher dsDNA concentrations did we detect a significant burst of ATP hydrolysis, although even at 7-muM dsDNA the amplitude and the rate were still lower than with p/tDNA (Figure 2C). A 37-nt ssDNA, however, does not stimulate rapid ATP hydrolysis even at 7-muM concentration (Figure 2D). Together, the data suggest that the dsDNA portion of p/tDNA is an essential feature for its productive interaction with gamma complex. And, while the ssDNA portion itself appears to be ineffective, the combination of dsDNA and ssDNA, and possibly the ss/dsDNA junction, favors selection of p/tDNA as the substrate for clamp assembly. In order to understand the basis for this selectivity, we decided to find out if there are sites on gamma complex, other than the known dsDNA binding sites, which might contribute to its high-affinity interaction with p/tDNA.

In an earlier study, a conserved Tryptophan residue (W279) in the delta subunit of gamma complex was found to cross-link p/tDNA, specifically to a 5-bromo-2-deoxyuridine base located on the template strand adjacent to the recessed 3'-OH primer-template junction (35). This finding presented delta-W279, possibly along with conserved neighboring cationic residues, as a promising p/tDNA-binding site in gamma complex; therefore, we mutated it to alanine and tyrosine to assess its role in the clamp loader DNA binding and clamp assembly mechanism. The mutant proteins, deltaW279A and deltaW279Y, were overexpressed and purified in the same manner as wild-type delta, and reconstituted into full (deltaW279A-gamma complex, deltaW279Y-gamma complex) and minimal (deltaW279A-gammadeltadelta', deltaW279Y-gammadeltadelta') clamp loader complexes. During the reconstitution and purification process, the mutant complexes exhibited the same properties as wild-type complexes (). Moreover, treatment of deltaW279A and deltaW279Y with Elastase enzyme yielded the same partial proteolysis pattern as wild-type delta (), indicating that these mutations do not cause gross changes in protein structure.

We also observed that at higher NaCl concentration (100 mM), wild-type gamma complex binding to p/tDNA is weakened (KD approximately 500 nM; Figure 3B). It is likely that the salt interferes with electrostatic contacts between charged/polar residues on the clamp loader and DNA, thereby weakening the interaction. This result is consistent with our hypothesis that the duplex portion is essential for high-affinity binding of p/tDNA to gamma complex, which in turn leads to rapid ATP hydrolysis and clamp assembly. Under these conditions, deltaW279A-gamma complex binding to p/tDNA is barely detectable (a KD value cannot be determined from the data; Figure 3B), while deltaW279Y-gamma complex binding is weakened to a similar extent as wild-type gamma complex (KD approximately 800 nM; Figure 3B). Thus, in addition to contacts between the clamp loader and dsDNA, the presence of delta-W279 (or at least an aromatic residue) near the primer-template junction appears important for gamma complex-p/tDNA interaction. A similar result is obtained with the minimal gammadeltadelta' complex, except that even at 60 mM NaCl wild-type gammadeltadelta' binds p/tDNA with a KD of 160 nM and deltaW279A-gammadeltadelta' binding to p/tDNA is barely detectable ().

Next, we examined whether delta-W279 influences the p/tDNA-stimulated rapid ATP hydrolysis activity of gamma complex. Pre-steady-state ATPase experiments measuring Pi release were performed initially under stringent conditions, at 100 mM NaCl and low DNA concentration (0.4 muM p/tDNA). Wild-type gamma complex catalyzes a rapid burst of ATP hydrolysis when mixed with p/tDNA, following preincubation with beta and ATP for one second (Figure 4B), similar to that observed at 60 mM NaCl and with 1.4 muM DNA (Figure 2B). In case of deltaW279A-gamma complex, however, burst activity is barely detectable (Figure 4B). The deltaW279Y-gamma complex still catalyzes rapid ATP hydrolysis, although the burst rate and amplitude are lower compared with wild-type gamma complex (Figure 4B), which may reflect the fact that this mutant has intermediate affinity for p/tDNA (Figure 3). Data from experiments performed with the minimal clamp loader are also consistent with the above findings. The DNA-dependent steady-state ATPase rate of deltaW279A-gammadeltadelta' is lower and more sensitive to NaCl concentration than that of wild-type gammadeltadelta' and deltaW279Y-gammadeltadelta' (). Also, while the latter complexes catalyze rapid ATP hydrolysis in the presence of p/tDNA and beta, deltaW279A-gammadeltadelta' does not do so under the same conditions (). Note: the lower amplitude of the burst phase for wild-type gammadeltadelta' relative to gamma complex (<3 ATP hydrolyzed per clamp loader) is thought to reflect some fraction of 'inactive' gammadeltadelta' in the reaction or perhaps, intriguingly, asymmetry among gamma subunits in the complex such that all three are activated for ATP hydrolysis only when the complex achieves a conformation enabled by the psi subunit (16).

These results confirm that delta-W279 has a significant role in the gamma complex-DNA interaction. More specifically, our hypothesis is that delta-W279 is important for gamma complex binding to the primer-template junction and/or template strand; therefore, it follows that the delta-W279A mutation should not affect the dsDNA-dependent ATPase activity of the clamp loader. Indeed, as shown in Figure 4C, in the presence of excess dsDNA (7 muM), deltaW279A-gamma complex catalyzes a burst of ATP hydrolysis comparable to wild-type gamma complex. Interestingly, the ssDNA-stimulated ATPase rate of the mutant is lower than that of wild-type complex (albeit only by 2-fold), which is also consistent with a role for delta-W279 in gamma complex interactions with the ss/dsDNA junction and/or ssDNA. Additionally, it is possible to restore the p/tDNA-dependent ATPase activity of deltaW279A-gamma complex to wild-type levels under conditions that favor clamp loader binding to the ds portion of p/tDNA. As shown in Figure 4D, lowering the NaCl concentration to 60 mM NaCl (0.4 muM p/tDNA) or increasing p/tDNA concentration to 1.4 muM in the reaction (100 mM NaCl) stimulates deltaW279A-gamma complex activity. The burst ATPase kinetics of the mutant now mirror those of wild-type gamma complex at 0.4 muM p/tDNA (100 mM NaCl); deltaW279Y-gamma complex activity also recovers to wild-type levels at lower NaCl and/or higher DNA concentrations (data not shown). Together, these findings suggest that delta-W279 does not participate directly in gamma complex binding to dsDNA, but rather complements this interaction to facilitate high-affinity binding to p/tDNA, which in turn triggers ATP hydrolysis and leads to clamp assembly.

Several of the experiments described above were performed also in the presence of sodium acetate instead of sodium chloride (). As is the case with NaCl, the p/tDNA-stimulated ATPase activity of deltaW279A-gamma complex is more sensitive to increasing NaOAc concentration than wild-type gamma complex (Figure S3A). We performed an additional set of experiments to confirm that the effects of delta-W279 mutation reflect specifically its role in the p/tDNA-binding activity of gamma complex. Thus far, all experiments demonstrating defects in the mutant clamp loaders have been performed in the presence of beta; i.e. DNA binding (Figure 3) and ATPase (Figure 4) assays. It is known that beta influences both the DNA binding and ATPase activities of the clamp loader, thus the question remains whether substitution of delta-W279 with alanine adversely affects gamma complex interaction with beta, which in turn could impact its DNA binding, ATPase and, ultimately, its clamp loading activity (26,43,44). We consider this an unlikely possibility given that delta-W279 is located virtually at the other end of delta from the beta binding site (43) (Figure 1); also, all the experiments have been performed with several-fold excess beta over gamma complex. We can, however, address the caveat directly because p/tDNA can trigger rapid ATP hydrolysis by gamma complex even in the absence of beta, although the burst rate and amplitude are not optimal (29). The data in Figure 4E show that when it is mixed with ATP and p/tDNA in the absence of beta, gamma complex hydrolyzes ATP with burst kinetics, at both 60 mM and 100 mM NaCl concentrations. deltaW279A-gamma complex activity is similar to that of the wild-type complex at 60 mM NaCl, but no burst of ATP hydrolysis is detectable at the higher salt concentration (Figure 4E); the steady-state rate is slightly faster, which may reflect more rapid release of products. Thus, delta-W279 does directly affect gamma complex binding to p/tDNA and, as a result, subsequent steps in the clamp assembly reaction.

The significance of delta-W279 for clamp loader function was tested in DNA replication assays, by measuring extension of a 32P-labeled primer by E. coli Pol III core DNA polymerase. In the absence of clamp and clamp loader proteins, the core polymerase exhibits distributive activity on a 30-nt/105-nt p/tDNA substrate and is unable to extend the primer to the end of the template (80 nt product) (Figure 5, lane 1). The presence of wild type gamma complex and beta facilitates processive core polymerase activity, and now it can synthesize the full-length product (Figure 5, lane 2). At 50 mM NaCl concentration, both deltaW279A-gamma complex and deltaW279Y-gamma complex can support processive DNA synthesis (Figure 5, lanes 3 and 4). However, reactions containing deltaW279A-gamma complex display salt sensitivity, as revealed by the decrease in full-length product formation at 100 mM NaCl (Figure 5, lanes 5-7); the activity of deltaW279Y-gamma complex appears comparable to that of the wild-type clamp loader (Figure 5, lane 7). A similar result is obtained with gammadeltadelta' complexes, except the absence of chi/psi subunits makes the clamp loaders more sensitive to salt, and the difference between wild-type gammadeltadelta' and deltaW279A-gammadeltadelta' function is detectable even at 80 mM NaCl concentration (). These data confirm the hypothesis that Tryptophan 279 of the delta subunit contributes toward optimal functioning of the clamp loader in DNA replication. ###begin title 41
Additional residues in the conserved motif neighboring delta-W279 are involved in gamma complex-p/tDNA interaction
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 44 47 44 47 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">279</sub>
###xml 227 235 223 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 826 828 818 820 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 1052 1060 1040 1048 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 1072 1080 1060 1068 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">Figure 4</xref>
According to the crystal structure, the HRVW279QNRR sequence in delta forms a loop-like structure with charged and polar residues that appear to be in a favorable position for interaction with DNA as it exits the clamp loader (Figure 1). In order to determine whether this motif contributes to the clamp loader mechanism beyond the function(s) associated with delta-W279, we examined the effects of mutating the three positively charged residues (Arginine) in the motif to Alanine. R277 and R283 were chosen because their cationic side chains are exposed and accessible for interaction with DNA, and their interesting bookend positions in the motif, while R282 was chosen as a potential negative control, since according to the crystal structure its side chain is buried and should not be accessible for interaction with DNA (17). We made these changes individually and in conjunction with the already well-characterized delta-W279A mutation, as this allowed us to assess the role of the Arginine residues under two different conditions that are more (Figure 4B) or less (Figure 4D) stringent for clamp loader activity.

###xml 578 586 537 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 655 666 610 621 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">W279A/R283A</sub>
Under less stringent conditions, higher DNA (1.4 muM) and lower NaCl (60 mM), the activities of the single mutants, deltaR277A-gamma complex and deltaR283A-gamma complex, are indistinguishable from wild-type gamma complex (Figure 6C and D). We also detect recovery of deltaW279A/R277A-gamma complex ATPase activity, although the burst rate and amplitude are still lower compared with the single mutants (Figure 6C). The activity of deltaW279A/R283A-gamma complex also increases slightly under these conditions (Figure 6D). As is the case with wild-type gamma complex and dsDNA (Figure 2C), the burst amplitude and rate of ATP hydrolysis catalyzed by deltaW279A/R283A-gamma complex rise further with increasing DNA concentrations, but the full burst is not achieved even at 7-muM p/tDNA (data not shown), suggesting that this double mutant suffers a severe loss in p/tDNA-binding ability. Together, these data affirm that a conserved sequence in the C-terminal domain of delta contributes to the p/tDNA-binding activity of gamma complex, and indicate that multiple residues in the motif participate in the interaction and are important for subsequent steps in the clamp loading reaction.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
DISCUSSION
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 476 477 436 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 619 626 571 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 645 658 593 606 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 799 804 747 752 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31 B32 B33">31&#8211;33</xref>
###xml 974 976 914 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 1286 1288 1222 1224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 1527 1535 1459 1467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 1538 1540 1470 1472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 619 626 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 645 658 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
In the clamp.clamp loader complex, beta is positioned underneath gamma complex, docked on the N-terminal surfaces of the gamma, delta and delta' subunits. Of these proteins, delta is primarily responsible for binding and opening beta clamp for assembly on primed DNA templates. The three gamma subunits provide the ATPase activity, and along with delta', also contribute to beta binding and coordination of ATP binding and hydrolysis with beta opening and closing around DNA (3). In addition, both gamma and delta' subunits are known to play a prominent role in binding DNA. Earlier structure-function analyses of both E. coli gamma complex and S. cerevisiae RFC revealed conserved charged and polar amino acids on the inner surface of the clamp loader that interact with the minor groove of dsDNA (31-33). Mutations of these residues in the gamma and delta' subunits cause defects in the DNA binding, DNA-dependent ATPase, and clamp release activities of the clamp loader (33). Thus far, no specific site has been identified on clamp loaders that can interact with the single-stranded template or the primer-template junction. One possible location is a cleft in the C-terminal portion of the delta subunit that has a width of about 12 A, enough to allow entry of ssDNA but not dsDNA (17). A loop-like structure extends out over the top of the cleft, and a Tryptophan residue (delta-W279) in this loop specifically forms a zero-length cross-link with the first or second template base from the 3'-OH primer-template junction (Figure 1) (35). This finding suggests that in addition to binding and opening the clamp, the delta subunit participates in binding to DNA-perhaps the ss/dsDNA junction and/or ssDNA template of p/tDNA.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 327 329 313 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 810 812 761 763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 954 963 905 914 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">Figures 4</xref>
###xml 968 969 919 920 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 1139 1147 1082 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">Figure 3</xref>
In the clamp assembly reaction, gamma complex binds ATP and beta and then, upon interaction with DNA, catalyzes a burst of ATP hydrolysis and releases the beta.DNA complex. Until now, only p/tDNA was known to elicit this rapid ATP hydrolysis response, and given our finding that delta-W279 lies close to the ss/dsDNA junction (35) and is a highly conserved residue in bacterial clamp loaders (approximately95% W, approximately3% Y in aligned sequences), we decided to investigate its possible function in selective interaction of the clamp loader with p/tDNA. The results of our study reveal that while delta-W279 is not essential for gamma complex.beta binding to p/tDNA, it is necessary for high-affinity interaction between the two, at salt concentrations considered comparable to physiological conditions (45), which in turn is necessary for the clamp loader to achieve a conformation that is 'activated' for rapid ATP hydrolysis and clamp assembly (Figures 4 and 5). Consistent with the sequence conservation data, mutation of delta-W279 to Tyrosine has much lower impact on gamma complex activity compared with the Alanine mutation (Figure 3), suggesting that an aromatic side chain is important at this location. The data reveal that delta-W279 does not participate directly in the interactions between gamma complex and the duplex portion of p/tDNA, rather it complements these contacts by interacting with the ss/dsDNA junction and/or ssDNA template (possible stacking with bases), and thus facilitates selection of p/tDNA as the appropriate substrate for clamp assembly.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 622 624 564 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 737 739 660 662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
A function that can be considered analogous to that of delta-W279 has been discovered recently for psi subunit of gamma complex. The minimal complex of gammadeltadelta' is deficient in p/tDNA binding, which is likely an underlying reason why it is not as active a clamp loader as gamma complex (gammadeltadelta'chipsi), especially under high NaCl concentrations. Addition of psi to gammadeltadelta' restores full DNA binding and corresponding burst ATPase activity (even though psi is not known to bind DNA directly), suggesting that the presence of this subunit also favors the 'active' conformation of the clamp loader (16). Addition of the beta clamp to gammadeltadelta' also brings its activity closer to that of full gamma complex (16). The subtle effects of so many factors on gamma complex function are consistent with the idea that the clamp loader exists in different conformations in equilibrium, and that several events in the reaction, including appropriate interactions with ATP, beta and DNA, shift the equilibrium toward a conformation that is optimal for catalysis of clamp assembly on DNA.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 106 108 102 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 395 398 383 386 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">279</sub>
###xml 404 412 392 400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 1088 1093 1040 1045 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">R277A</sub>
###xml 1117 1122 1061 1066 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">R283A</sub>
###xml 1258 1263 1190 1195 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">W279A</sub>
###xml 1304 1315 1228 1239 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">W279A/R277A</sub>
###xml 1339 1350 1255 1266 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">W279A/R283A</sub>
###xml 1421 1426 1329 1334 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">W279A</sub>
The C-terminal sequence of the delta subunit is not conserved among clamp loaders from diverse organisms (46). Among bacterial clamp loaders, however, the sequence neighboring delta-W279 is highly conserved, with cationic and polar residues projecting out from a loop-like structure that could participate in electrostatic and hydrogen bonding interactions between gamma complex and p/tDNA (HRVW279QNRR; Figure 1). We tested this hypothesis by mutation of two exposed cationic residues in the loop, delta-R277 and delta-R283, to alanine, individually and in conjunction with delta-W279A mutation, followed by analysis of the corresponding gamma complexes. We also mutated delta-R282 to alanine, hoping it could serve as a negative control as its side chain appears buried in the C-terminal domain unlike the other arginines. It turned out that the mutation alters the protein structure, suggesting that delta-R282 plays a role in maintaining the structural integrity of delta (perhaps R282 helps anchor the otherwise exposed loop in the delta C-terminal domain). The single mutants, deltaR277A-gamma complex and deltaR283A-gamma complex, have slightly lower p/tDNA-stimulated ATPase activity than gamma complex, but their defects are mild compared with deltaW279A-gamma complex. The double mutants, deltaW279A/R277A-gamma complex and deltaW279A/R283A-gamma complex, have lower p/tDNA-stimulated ATPase activity than deltaW279A-gamma complex, indicating that the mutations have an additive effect, consistent with their contributing to the same aspect of gamma complex structure-function. In the absence of any structural data on a clamp loader.DNA complex, it is not clear whether residues in the loop make direct contact with ssDNA or affect the interaction in an indirect manner, although the former is entirely possible given the positive electrostatic potential of this conserved motif in the delta subunit.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 343 351 336 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 708 710 683 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 847 850 811 814 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ATP</sub>
###xml 951 954 908 911 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ATP</sub>
It is also noteworthy that while gamma complex.beta does not exhibit a burst of ATP hydrolysis in the presence of low concentrations of dsDNA or ssDNA, in both cases its ATPase activity is still substantially greater than in the absence of DNA. Also, we found that higher concentrations of dsDNA, but not ssDNA, restore burst ATPase kinetics (Figure 2). Thus, it appears that a duplex DNA structure is necessary and sufficient for DNA-mediated activation of gamma complex.beta for rapid ATP hydrolysis; our finding that blunt-ended dsDNA can trigger rapid ATP hydrolysis by gamma complex is consistent with a previous report that blunt-ended dsDNA can stimulate release of the beta clamp from gamma complex (33). Together, these data support the existence of more than one gamma complex.beta conformation in the reaction, depicted below with gammaATP.beta as the species whose ATPase activity is stimulated to some extent on binding DNA (N) and gamma*ATP.beta as the species that catalyzes a burst of ATP hydrolysis on binding DNA.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 0 0 0 0 <inline-graphic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xlink:href="gkn157i1.jpg"/>

###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 48 51 44 47 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ATP</sub>
###xml 186 194 179 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 335 338 324 327 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ATP</sub>
###xml 353 356 335 338 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ATP</sub>
###xml 402 410 381 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 444 447 419 422 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ATP</sub>
###xml 562 570 534 542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 685 693 656 664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 815 818 782 785 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ATP</sub>
According to this scheme, if p/tDNA binds gamma*ATP.beta with high affinity, low concentrations of the DNA would stabilize this conformation and lead to rapid ATP hydrolysis, as seen in Figure 2B. Given mutations that lower p/tDNA-binding affinity, higher concentrations of the DNA would be required to shift the equilibrium from gammaATP.beta to gamma*ATP.beta and produce the same effect, as seen in Figure 6. Similarly, if dsDNA binds gamma*ATP.beta with low affinity, high concentrations of this DNA would be necessary to produce the same effect, as seen in Figure 2C. In the case of ssDNA, we do not observe any burst of ATP hydrolysis at the highest concentration tested (7 muM; Figure 2D), which suggests that ssDNA is severely lacking in structural features necessary for high-affinity binding to the gamma*ATP.beta conformation.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 514 517 493 496 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ATP</sub>
###xml 920 929 884 893 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bona fide</italic>
In summary, we began this investigation in order to understand more clearly the basis of clamp loader selectivity for primed DNA templates as substrates for clamp assembly. We found that the dsDNA portion of p/tDNA appears to be essential for productive interaction between DNA and gamma complex.beta, which leads to ATP hydrolysis and beta assembly on DNA. However, dsDNA alone is a poor substrate for clamp assembly, because of its low binding affinity for the 'active' conformation of gamma complex.beta (gamma*ATP.beta in the scheme above). Other features of p/tDNA, such as the 3'-OH primer/template junction and the ssDNA template enhance the interaction, likely via contacts with additional sites on gamma complex beyond those that bind dsDNA. Results of our mutational analysis make a strong case that a conserved motif in the C-terminal domain of delta is one such site helping gamma complex select p/tDNA as a bona fide substrate for clamp assembly.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 267 280 263 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 394 396 386 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 653 655 645 647 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 846 859 834 847 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 862 864 850 852 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 865 867 853 855 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 881 883 869 871 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 884 886 872 874 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 902 904 890 892 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 1052 1054 1040 1042 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 267 280 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 846 859 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
Since the C-terminal region of delta is not conserved among analogous clamp loader subunits in other organisms, it is not clear whether aromatic and charged/polar residues help impose DNA substrate selectivity in these proteins as well. Thus far, it appears that the S. cerevisiae RFC clamp loader is more promiscuous than gamma complex both in its ability to bind a variety of DNA structures (47) and the related rapid ATP hydrolysis activity (Chen,S., Hingorani,M.M., unpublished data). Such promiscuity may be necessary for RFC to function on DNA substrates in DNA replication as well as in other processes such as bidirectional DNA mismatch repair (48); therefore, it is possible that a gamma complex-like mechanism for highly selective p/tDNA binding does not exist in RFC. Alternative eukaryotic clamp loaders, e.g. Rad17-RFC (Rad24-RFC in S. cerevisiae) (49,50), Ctf18-RFC (51,52) and Elg1-RFC (53), that function in DNA damage checkpoints and sister chromatid cohesion, among other processes, apparently bind yet other types of DNA structures (54). We expect that comparative structure-function analyses will likely reveal customized features in these clamp loaders that enable their function on distinct DNA substrates.
