###begin article-title 0
Lineage Diversion of T Cell Receptor Transgenic Thymocytes Revealed by Lineage Fate Mapping
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: DL Hv TE. Performed the experiments: TE TK. Analyzed the data: Hv TE TK. Wrote the paper: DL Hv TE.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 301 316 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 479 494 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 547 562 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
The binding of the T cell receptor (TCR) to major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules in the thymus determines fates of TCRalphabeta lymphocytes that subsequently home to secondary lymphoid tissue. TCR transgenic models have been used to study thymic selection and lineage commitment. Most TCR transgenic mice express the rearranged TCRalphabeta prematurely at the double negative stage and abnormal TCRalphabeta populations of T cells that are not easily detected in non-transgenic mice have been found in secondary lymphoid tissue of TCR transgenic mice.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology and Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 386 387 375 376 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 388 389 377 378 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 445 446 434 435 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 447 448 436 437 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 485 486 470 471 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 341 345 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 590 594 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To determine developmental pathways of TCR-transgenic thymocytes, we used Cre-LoxP-mediated fate mapping and show here that premature expression of a transgenic TCRalphabeta diverts some developing thymocytes to a developmental pathway which resembles that of gamma delta cells. We found that most peripheral T cells with the HY-TCR in male mice have bypassed the RORgammat-positive CD4+8+ (double positive, DP) stage to accumulate either as CD4-8- (double negative, DN) or as CD8alpha+ T cells in lymph nodes or gut epithelium. Likewise, DN TCRalphabeta cells in lymphoid tissue of female mice were not derived from DP thymocytes.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
The results further support the hypothesis that the premature expression of the TCRalphabeta can divert DN thymocytes into gamma delta lineage cells.
###end p 7

Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9

###xml 309 310 309 310 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>

###xml 939 942 932 935 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Teh1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1016 1024 1009 1017 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1168 1171 1153 1156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Yamagata1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1424 1428 1386 1390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Rorc</italic>
###xml 1434 1437 1396 1399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Eberl1">[6]</xref>
###xml 1646 1647 1592 1593 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1962 1965 1894 1897 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-GuyGrand1">[7]</xref>
###xml 16 20 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 170 185 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 527 542 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 662 666 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1085 1100 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 1257 1261 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1627 1642 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
"HY transgenic" mice express a transgenic T cell receptor (TCR) specific for HY antigen, i.e. a peptide of the Dby protein presented by class I Db MHC molecules. In male transgenic mice, CD4+8+ double positive (DP) cells undergo negative selection by TCR agonist ligands and die by apoptosis [1]. However, CD8+ cells expressing mostly CD8alpha and low levels of CD8beta as well as double negative (DN) cells with the transgenic TCR are present among extrathymic lymphocytes and intraepithelial gut lymphocytes (IEL) in male HY transgenic mice [2]. In fact, CD8+ cells with the transgenic TCR represent the most abundant population of T cells in the gut of these mice [3]. Two competing hypotheses with regard to the origin of these extrathymic cells have been put forward: one suggested that these cells survived the negative selection process at the DP stage of thymocyte development by down-regulating CD8 and CD4 co-receptor expression [4]. This hypothesis appeared consistent with later experiments showing that in vitro fetal thymic organ culture some DP cells from female HY-TCR transgenic mice could become CD8alphaalpha T cells when confronted with HY antigen [5]. Furthermore, our previous fate mapping experiments indicated that in wild-type (WT) mice CD8alphaalpha IEL with TCRalphabeta are derived from precursors expressing RORgammat, which is an isoform of an orphan nuclear receptor, RORgamma, encoded by the Rorc gene [6]. RORgammat is specifically expressed in DP thymocytes. IEL with TCRgammadelta expression are not derived from RORgammat expressing precursors. This led to the assumption that in WT and TCR transgenic mice CD8+ IEL are derived from DP thymocytes and that this pathway of differentiation represents induction of a lineage away from conventional TCRalphabeta cells that home to secondary lymphoid organs and towards intestinal intraepithelial TCRalphabeta lymphocytes that may have an important role in regulating gut immunity [7].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 41 42 41 42 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 488 491 453 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-vonBoehmer1">[2]</xref>
###xml 634 635 599 600 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 636 637 601 602 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 720 723 677 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Baldwin1">[8]</xref>
###xml 847 848 804 805 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1037 1040 994 997 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Kisielow1">[1]</xref>
###xml 97 101 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 559 563 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 697 701 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 864 879 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 957 961 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The competing hypothesis was that the CD8+ and DN cells expressing the HY transgenic TCR in male mice represent an abnormal subset of gammadelta lineage cells misguided by the premature expression of the transgenic TCRalphabeta that would mimic signals delivered normally by the TCRgammadelta. In addition, it was postulated that the confrontation of DN cells with the HY agonist ligand led to expression of the CD8alpha co-receptor as is observed with some activated TCRgammadelta cells [2]. Consistent with the second hypothesis was the observation that in mice exhibiting timely expression of the HY transgenic TCR, i.e. at the CD4+8+ stage of development, CD8alphaalpha IEL in the gut of male mice were much reduced [8] arguing that abnormally early expression of this particular transgenic TCR was indeed responsible for the generation of CD8+ IEL in male HY transgenic mice. Also, DN cells with the HY-TCR could be observed in the periphery of female mice indicating that a TCR agonist ligand was not required for their generation [1].
###end p 10

###xml 506 509 494 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-He1">[9]</xref>
###xml 511 515 499 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Villey1">[10]</xref>

###xml 994 1000 955 961 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Rosa26</italic>

###xml 1088 1092 1045 1049 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Srinivas1">[12]</xref>
###xml 1387 1388 1303 1304 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>

###xml 1405 1409 1321 1325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Egawa1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1738 1739 1635 1636 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1937 1938 1795 1796 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 215 230 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 582 597 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 873 877 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 881 885 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1171 1175 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1539 1554 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 1649 1664 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>

###xml 1807 1811 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1986 2001 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 2061 2076 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 2180 2184 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The former, but not the latter, hypothesis postulates that extrathymic lymphocytes with the HY receptor are derived from RORgammat-positive DP precursors. By using RORgammat-dependent lineage fate mapping in HY-TCR transgenic mice, it is therefore possible to determine which of the models provides an adequate explanation for how these cells are derived. RORgammat is an orphan nuclear receptor, whose expression during T cell development begins in late DN cells and is mostly restricted to DP thymocytes [9], [10]. Lineage fate mapping using bacterial artificial chromosome (BAC) transgenic mice in which cre-recombinase is controlled by RORgammat regulatory elements irreversibly labels cells developing through the conventional CD4+8+ TCRalphabeta, but not the DN TCRgammadelta, pathway. Moreover, IEL do not express RORgammat [11]. Thus, breeding of the RORgammat-cre mice to mice bearing a loxP flanked stop cassette in front of enhanced yellow fluorescent protein (EYFP) embedded in the Rosa26 locus results in fluorescent labeling of all RORgammat+ precursor-derived T cells [6], [12]. We showed that all TCRalphabeta cells in the secondary lymphoid organs of WT mice were labeled by RORgammat-cre and that non-conventional TCRalphabeta populations of CD8alphaalphaTCRalphabeta IEL and Valpha14i natural killer T (NKT) cells were likewise selected from a common pool of RORgammat+ precursors [6], [13]. Utilizing this approach we have found that both of the original hypotheses concerning non-conventional TCRalphabeta cells in HY transgenic mice may be correct: the majority of intraepithelial CD8alphaalpha cells in the gut of male HY-TCR transgenic mice was derived from RORgammat-negative precursors while a minority of HY-TCR+ CD8alphaalpha cells in male mice and all CD8alphaalpha cells in WT mice are derived from RORgammat-positive precursor cells. Thus induction of the CD8alphaalphaTCRalphabeta lineage at the RORgammat+ stage of development occurs in both WT and TCR transgenic mice whereas the precocious expression of a TCRalphabeta in TCR transgenic mice generates subsets of misguided TCRalphabeta-expressing T cells that are only rarely observed in normal mice.

Results
###end title 12
###begin title 13
###xml 89 104 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
Origin of non-transgenic T cells with diverse alphabeta and gammadeltaTCRs in WT and TCR transgenic mice
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 146 147 139 140 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>

In RORgammat-cre;ROSA26-stop-EYFP mice on a non-TCR transgenic background, practically all extrathymic cells with surface TCRbeta chains were EYFP+ and thus derived from RORgammat+ cells such as DP thymocytes (Figure 1). The same EYFP expression pattern was observed in extrathymic T cells from lymph nodes of male and female HY-TCR transgenic mice that did not express the transgenic TCR because of replacement of transgenic TCRalpha by endogenous TCRalpha chains (Figure 1) [14], [15]. This indicates that, after endogenous TCRalpha rearrangement in TCR transgenic mice, cells with new TCRs composed of transgenic TCRbeta chain and endogenous TCRalpha chains develop in a similar pathway as they do in normal mice. Cells with the phenotype of extrathymic T cells were also found intrathymically (Figure S1) making it likely that they are all derived from RORgammat+ DP thymocytes. Thus, in spite of the thymic cortex being largely devoid of DP thymocytes in male mice, some cells develop normally if they escape deletion by the early expression of endogenous TCRalpha chains.

The pattern of reporter expression observed in IEL bearing endogenous TCRalpha chains was very similar to that obtained in lymph node T cells, with the exception that IEL contained a high proportion of CD8alphaalpha cells with TCRalphabeta that, like CD8alphabeta IEL, were EYFP+ (Figure 2). These results confirm earlier data on IEL in WT mice and in addition indicate that in TCR transgenic mice IEL with receptors other than the transgenic TCR are derived from RORgammat+ precursors, presumably DP thymocytes. In contrast, the vast majority of IEL with TCRgammadelta was EYFP-negative in WT as well as in TCR transgenic mice (Figures 2 and S3). Thus, T cells with receptors other than the HY transgenic TCR, while they are relatively rare in TCR transgenic mice, develop along the same pathway as T cells in WT mice, indicating that normal pathways of T cell differentiation are operative in TCR transgenic mice.

In female HY TCR transgenic mice, single positive thymocytes and lymph node T cells that express the transgenic TCR and either CD4 or CD8 co-receptor were all EYFP-positive and thus derived from DP thymocytes (Figures 3 and S2). Initial studies suggested that the abnormal DN and CD8+ cells with the HY transgenic TCR have similarities with gamma delta lineage cells because, in contrast to conventional TCRalphabeta cells, they had retained the germline TCRdelta (Tcrd) locus [17]. To further determine whether premature TCRalphabeta signals could induce a signature gene for the TCRgammadelta lineage, we purified thymic subpopulations from HY-TCR transgenic mice or WT mice and examined expression of Sox13, which has been shown to contribute to the development of TCRgammadelta cells [18] (Figure 5). We could observe upregulation of Sox13 in TCRgammadelta+CD24+CD25+ thymocytes as compared to DN2 thymocytes or TCRgammadelta- DN3 thymocytes, and Sox13 was further upregulated in TCRgammadelta+CD24+CD25- thymocytes (Figure 5A). However, Sox13 expression was then downregulated in TCRgammadelta+CD24lo thymocytes and it became almost undetectable in TCRgammadelta+ IEL, while Sox13 expression was barely detected in TCRgammadelta- DN4 or DP thymocytes which are selected from DN3 precursors by pre-TCR signals (Figure 5A and 5B; data not shown). These results suggest that Sox13 is transiently upregulated following signals through TCRgammadelta, but not pre-TCR, at the DN thymocyte stage and may play some role in specification of the TCRgammadelta lineage [18]. In thymocytes from male HY mice, we could observe significant upregulation of Sox13 in HY+DN thymocytes. Similar to WT TCRgammadelta lineage cells, Sox13 upregulation was detected at the CD25+CD24+ stage and peaked at the CD25-CD24+ stage followed by Sox13 downregulation as cells matured. These findings strongly support our hypothesis that premature TCRalphabeta signals divert a proportion of TCRalphabeta+ DN thymocytes into the TCRgammadelta lineage.

Most TCR transgenic mice express the transgenic TCRalphabeta at the DN stage of T cell development and thus earlier than the endogenous TCR in non-transgenic mice. This is the case even when normal regulatory elements of the Tcra and Tcrb genes have been used, such as in HY transgenic mice; in these mice, cosmids containing rearranged TCR genes were introduced and TCRbeta and TCRalpha transgenes were integrated in tandem such that expression of the TCRalpha transgenes can be regulated by Tcrb control elements. Because of the premature TCRalphabeta expression, the validity of conclusions obtained in TCR transgenic mice has sometimes been questioned. A good example is the deletion of DP thymocytes in HY transgenic mice, whose physiological relevance was questioned by either assuming that the virtual absence of DP thymocytes in male HY-TCR transgenic mice was not caused by deletion but by a developmental arrest at the DN stage [19] or by arguing that the too early TCR expression in the DP compartment was responsible for deletion that normally would only occur in the medulla [8]. One of us has previously explained why these assumptions are unlikely to be true: DP cells are observed in the thymus of male HY-TCR transgenic mice and are increased in mice that lack CD8beta co-receptor chains [20], [21], arguing against a developmental arrest at the DN stage as the sole explanation for the low proportion of DP thymocytes in these mice. These studies, together with another study using transgenic mice expressing a mutant Db molecule [21], suggest that interaction of MHC class I and the CD8alphabeta/TCRalphabeta complex is important for efficient depletion of DP thymocytes in male HY mice. DP thymocyte deletion also occurs in male HY-TCR transgenic mice with timely onset of TCR transgene expression at the DP stage [8]. In addition, studies in WT and TCR transgenic mice show that ligation of the TCR on DP thymocytes results in their deletion [22]. Thus, there is convincing evidence that DP thymocytes are generally susceptible to deletion depending on the availability of appropriately presented antigen in the thymic cortex, especially class I MHC presented peptides derived from proteins such as HY antigen [23], and that deletion of DP thymocytes in TCR transgenic mice is representative of this physiological process.

###xml 1103 1106 1085 1088 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Teh1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1153 1154 1135 1136 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1340 1343 1303 1306 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-vonBoehmer1">[2]</xref>
###xml 455 470 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 623 638 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 711 726 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
The above considerations are important for the results presented here since it is believed that confrontation of DP thymocytes with cognate TCR ligands can result not only in deletion, but also in induction of development of alternative lineages, thereby generating T cells that can migrate from the thymus and regulate immunity in the gut as CD8alphaalpha T cells [7]. Since these conclusions were reached from studies that included experiments with TCR transgenic mice [5], careful consideration has to be given to the possibility that results may have been influenced by precocious expression of the TCRalphabeta in TCR transgenic mice. With regard to this issue, it is important to consider that in male HY transgenic mice CD8+ T cells with the transgenic TCR are present in peripheral lymphoid tissues and in IEL, in spite of the fact that the cortex is largely devoid of DP thymocytes. Two different scenarios were proposed to account for these observations: in one scenario, down-modulation of CD8beta co-receptor at the DP stage was held responsible for the escape of T cells into the periphery [4] while in the other it was assumed that the CD8+ cells were generated through activation of a differentiation program for DN gammadelta lineage cells that express the transgenic TCRalphabeta, resulting in increased levels of CD8alpha [2].
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 146 147 142 143 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 367 370 351 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Eberl1">[6]</xref>
###xml 372 376 356 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Egawa1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1003 1007 980 984 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Crooks1">[20]</xref>
###xml 1009 1013 986 990 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Killeen1">[21]</xref>
###xml 1147 1148 1117 1118 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1242 1243 1209 1210 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">b</sup>
###xml 1379 1380 1346 1347 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1512 1513 1472 1473 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1884 1888 1829 1833 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Aifantis1">[24]</xref>
###xml 428 443 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 955 959 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 963 967 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1197 1201 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1208 1223 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 1633 1637 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1646 1661 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 1879 1883 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The different hypotheses can be tested by using lineage fate mapping that can distinguish whether subsets of T cells develop through the RORgammat+ stage or are derived directly from RORgammat-negative precursors. The lineage fate mapping method utilized here had previously been employed to trace the origin of CD8alphaalpha cells in the gut and invariant NKT cells [6], [13]. Using the same approach in female and male HY-TCR transgenic mice, we have now delineated the origin of various peripheral T cells in lymph node and gut epithelium. The results do in fact show that the vast majority of lymph node T cells and IEL with the HY transgenic TCR and low level or no expression of CD8 co-receptors are lineage diverted most likely at the DN stage of thymocyte development before expression of RORgammat sets in, and are thus independent of the DP thymocyte differentiation pathway. This hypothesis is also supported by studies using CD8beta deficient mice or mice with a mutant MHC class I molecule [20], [21]. Although effective depletion of DP thymocytes relies on interaction of CD8alphabeta and the MHC class I molecule, accumulation of HY+ DN thymocytes was observed in CD8beta deficient mice or in transgenic mice bearing a mutant Db molecule, in which substantially large numbers of DP cells escape from deletion. These results suggest that the lineage diversion of HY+ DN cells does not require CD8alphabeta/MHC interaction and may take place independently of the DP stage in which the majority of HY+ cells are deleted. This conclusion applies to most, but not all, of the CD8alphaalpha cells in IEL, especially in male mice. In TCR transgenic mice, these cells are dependent on the premature expression of the transgenic TCRalphabeta in DN thymocytes and hence represent an artificially exaggerated population of cells that exists at a very low frequency in normal mice [24].
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 199 202 176 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Yamagata1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1088 1089 1037 1038 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1103 1104 1052 1053 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1219 1223 1152 1156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Ivanov1">[11]</xref>
###xml 76 91 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 465 480 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 797 801 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The few EYFP-labeled CD8alphaalpha cells with the transgenic TCR in male HY transgenic mice may be generated through the DP thymocyte stage, similarly to non-transgenic CD8alphaalphaTCRalphabeta IEL [5]. There is, however, a caveat to the assumption that these cells develop through the confrontation with TCR agonist ligands at the DP stage: EYFP-positive CD8alphaalpha cells with relatively high levels of the HY transgenic TCR are also found among IEL in female transgenic mice where the HY ligand is not present. However, it cannot be excluded that these cells also express endogenous TCR chains and hence may have encountered TCR agonist ligands intrathymically. The expression of endogenous TCRalpha chains cannot, however, explain the existence of EYFP-negative CD8alphaalpha IEL in female mice, since endogenous TCRalpha expression at the DN thymocyte stage is a rather rare event. These cells may express CD8alpha independently of cognate TCR ligands, perhaps facilitated by the gut microenvironment. We also do not completely rule out the possibility that a few EYFP-positive HY+ cells or EYFP+TCRgammadelta cells might turn on RORgammat expression in the lamina propria, where some T cells express RORgammat [11], and then migrate to the epithelium.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 62 77 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 185 189 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
It is of interest to note that all extrathymic T cells in TCR transgenic mice that do not express the transgenic TCR exhibit the same EYFP labeling pattern as extrathymic T cells in WT mice, i.e. the TCR transgenes do not lead to major changes in lymphoid organ structure that prevent normal lymphocyte development.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 336 337 314 315 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 367 370 345 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-vonBoehmer1">[2]</xref>
###xml 539 540 517 518 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 838 842 794 798 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Melichar1">[18]</xref>
###xml 844 848 800 804 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001512-Terrence1">[25]</xref>
In summary these studies suggest that consequences of premature TCRalphabeta expression have to be considered when analyzing pathways of lymphocyte subset generation. It had previously been argued that signals from the prematurely expressed TCRalphabeta may "fool" DN cells to develop into gammadelta lineage cells, including DN and CD8+ cells in lymph nodes and gut [2]. The lineage fate mapping studies reported here support this scenario. Our conclusion is further strengthened by our data demonstrating that Sox13 was upregulated in HY+ DN thymocytes following premature signals through TCRalphabeta instead of pre-TCR, as is by other reports showing that these abnormal cells exhibit profiles of surface marker and cytokine expression, which resemble those of TCRgammadelta cells rather than those of conventional TCRalphabeta cells [18], [25].
###end p 36
