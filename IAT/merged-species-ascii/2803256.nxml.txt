###begin article-title 0
MICB0106 gene polymorphism is associated with ulcerative colitis in central China
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Background
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 77 81 77 81 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MICA</italic>
###xml 86 90 86 90 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MICB</italic>
The highly polymorphic nonclassical MHC class I chain-related genes A and B (MICA and MICB) encode stress-inducible glycoproteins expressed on various epithelial cells including intestinal epithelial cells. MICA and MICB gene polymorphisms and expressions are associated with autoimmune diseases but not known in ulcerative colitis (UC).
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Aims
###end title 3
###begin p 4
To investigate the association of MICB exon 2-4 polymorphisms and soluble MICA (sMICA) expression with the susceptibility of UC in central China.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Materials and methods
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 113 121 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 215 223 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Genomic DNA was isolated from peripheral blood. The allele frequencies of MICB exon 2-4 were genotyped in 105 UC patients and 213 healthy controls by PCR single-stranded conformation polymorphism method. Thirty-two patients and 32 controls were selected for determining serum sMICA expression by ELISA.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Results
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 121 122 121 122 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 124 126 124 126 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pc</italic>
###xml 202 204 202 204 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pc</italic>
###xml 261 263 261 263 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pc</italic>
###xml 323 325 323 325 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pc</italic>
###xml 367 369 367 369 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pc</italic>
###xml 435 437 435 437 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pc</italic>
###xml 574 575 570 571 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 60 68 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 153 161 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 341 349 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 384 392 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Allele frequency of MICB0106 was significantly higher in UC patients than in healthy controls (19.0% vs. 8.9%, corrected P (Pc) = 0.0006), especially in patients with extensive colitis (24.4% vs. 8.9%, Pc = 0.0006), moderate and severe disease (24.1% vs. 8.9%, Pc = 0.0006), extraintestinal manifestations (20.5% vs. 8.9%, Pc = 0.012), male patients (22.1% vs. 8.0%, Pc = 0.006), and patients over the age of 40 years (28.8% vs. 8.3%, Pc = 0.0006). The sMICA level was significantly higher in UC than in healthy controls (604.41 +/- 480.43 pg/ml vs. 175.37 +/- 28.31 pg/ml, P = 0.0001) but not associated with the MICB0106 genotypes.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Conclusions
###end title 9
###begin p 10
Overall, MICB0106 allele was positively associated with UC in the Han Chinese in central China. sMICA was highly expressed in UC but not associated with the MICB0106 genotype.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
Keywords
###end title 11
###begin title 12
Introduction
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 315 316 315 316 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">1</xref>
###xml 318 319 318 319 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2</xref>
###xml 474 475 474 475 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">3</xref>
###xml 477 478 477 478 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">4</xref>
Ulcerative colitis (UC) is a subtype of chronic nonspecific inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) with unknown etiology. Epidemiological studies in the European and American countries have demonstrated strong evidences of genetic susceptibility to IBD, such as concordance rates in twins and multiply-affected families [1, 2]. Moreover, several genetic studies have found that the HLA genes, which are located on chromosome 6p (IBD3), is one group of the susceptible loci of UC [3, 4]. Therefore, major histocompatibility complex (MHC) genes have been studied as candidate genes for the susceptibility of UC.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 31 34 31 34 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MIC</italic>
###xml 98 99 98 99 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">5</xref>
###xml 346 347 343 344 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 837 838 815 816 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>
The MHC class I chain-related (MIC) gene is located 47-kilo base pairs (kb) centromeric to HLA-B [5]. Mapping studies identified seven MIC loci (MICA-MICG), of which only MICA and MICB encode transcripts, while MICC, MICD, MICE, MICF, and MICG are pseudogenes. MIC molecules show homology with classical HLA molecules but do not combine with beta2 microglobulin or recognize antigen. The MIC molecules have three external domains (alpha1-3), one transmembrane, and one cytoplasmic domain. The highly polymorphic MICA and MICB encode stress-inducible glycoproteins expressed on a variety of epithelial cells including intestinal epithelial cells. Interaction with the receptor NKG2D is likely to provide an important costimulatory signal for the activation of natural killer (NK) cells, macrophages, CD8+alphabeta, and gammadeltaT cells [6].
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 111 112 111 112 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>
###xml 160 164 160 164 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MICA</italic>
###xml 350 351 350 351 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">8</xref>
###xml 516 520 516 520 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MICB</italic>
###xml 537 538 537 538 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>
###xml 758 760 758 760 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">10</xref>
###xml 809 811 809 811 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">11</xref>
###xml 833 835 833 835 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">12</xref>
###xml 837 839 837 839 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">13</xref>
###xml 858 860 858 860 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">14</xref>
###xml 862 864 862 864 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR15">15</xref>
###xml 889 891 889 891 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">16</xref>
###xml 893 895 893 895 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">17</xref>
###xml 916 918 916 918 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">18</xref>
###xml 920 922 920 922 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">19</xref>
###xml 934 936 934 936 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">20</xref>
###xml 937 939 937 939 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">23</xref>
###xml 24 29 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
There are 65 recognized human MICA alleles and 30 different MICB alleles which have been identified so far () [7]. The microsatellite polymorphism in exon 5 of MICA gene consists of five alleles based on the number of GCT/AGC triplet repeat units (alleles A4, A5, A6, and A9) and the presence of an additional nucleotide G/C insertion (allele A5.1) [8]. MICB is the closest neighbor related to MICA, which has strict polymorphism. The (CA/TG)n microsatellite, consisting of 15 alleles, is present in intron 1 of the MICB gene (CA14-28) [9]. However, numerous high polymorphisms are more likely within the extracellular domain encoding exons 2, 3, and 4 of MICB. Recently, MICA and MICB gene polymorphisms have been found associated with autoimmune diseases [10] including insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus [11], Addison's disease [12, 13], celiac disease [14, 15], rheumatoid arthritis [16, 17], Behcet's disease [18, 19], and IBD [20-23], although little is known about the association between exons 2, 3, and 4 of MICB gene polymorphisms and UC.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 130 132 130 132 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">24</xref>
###xml 133 135 133 135 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">28</xref>
###xml 202 204 202 204 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">27</xref>
###xml 222 224 222 224 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">28</xref>
###xml 267 275 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
In our previous studies, we found ethnic differences in the distribution of MICA (exon 5) and MICB (intron 1) gene polymorphisms [24-28] in central China. MICA-A5.1/A5.1 homozygous genotype, MICA-A5.1 [27], and MICB-CA18 [28] allele are associated with UC in Chinese patients. Since MICA and MICB play important roles in immune regulation in autoimmune diseases and very few studies have observed an association of MICB polymorphism with the hereditary susceptibility to UC, therefore, we provided the exon 2-4 of MICB gene polymorphism and the soluble MICA (sMICA) expression for the susceptibility to UC in the Chinese Han population.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Materials and methods
###end title 17
###begin title 18
###xml 0 8 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Patients</span>
Patients and healthy controls
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 272 274 272 274 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR29">29</xref>
###xml 312 314 312 314 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">30</xref>
###xml 18 26 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 704 712 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 775 783 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 946 954 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1093 1101 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
A total of 105 UC patients with no family history who registered at our hospital from 2002 to 2006, were randomly collected and investigated. The diagnosis of UC was based on conventional clinical, endoscopic, and pathohistological criteria as described by Lennard-Jones [29]. Using the Montreal classification [30], the extent of the disease was classified as extensive colitis (involvement extends proximal to the splenic flexure) and left side colitis (involvement limited to a proportion of the colorectum distal to the splenic flexure) on endoscopy examination. The severity of UC was defined as mild, moderate, and severe colitis. We combined moderate and severe colitis since there were only five patients in the severe group. Extraintestinal manifestations (EIMs) of patients, including abnormal manifestations on joint, skin, eyes, and primary sclerosing cholangitis, were reviewed by records of the case history and interviews with the patients. A group of 213 unrelated ethnically matched blood donors who matched with gender and age were selected as healthy controls (HC). All the patients and controls were unrelated Chinese with Han nationality. The study was approved by the ethics committee of Wuhan University Zhongnan Hospital, and all the subjects gave informed consent.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
DNA isolation
###end title 20
###begin p 21
Genomic DNA was isolated from EDTA anti-coagulated peripheral venous blood by a conventional proteinase K digestion/phenol-chloroform extraction method.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Allele genotyping for exon 2, 3, and 4 of MICB
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 573 575 555 557 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR31">31</xref>
###xml 725 726 706 707 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1211 1213 1166 1168 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR32">32</xref>
###xml 1525 1527 1480 1482 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR33">33</xref>
MICB alleles were determined by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification on exons 2, 3, and 4 and followed by single-stranded conformation polymorphism (SSCP) analysis. The primers and annealing temperatures were forward 5'-CCAGGAAGAAGTTTCACCTG-3', reward 5'-CTGCTCTCTGTCTCCAACTT-3', 54degreesC; forward 5'-AAGGTGATGGGTTCGGGAAT-3', reward 5'-AGCAGAATTGCGGGAACAGT-3', 56degreesC; forward 5'-GTGAGAACAGTGGAGAGGAG-3', reward 5'-CACAGCCGTCCCTGCTGTT-3', 58degreesC, respectively, for amplifying exon 2, 3, and 4, which were designed using the genomic DNA sequence of MICB [31]. The concentration of primers used was 0.4 mM. PCR amplification was carried out in a 30-muL volume containing 100 ng of genomic DNA, 2.5 mM of MgCl2, 3 muL 10xGeneAmp PCR Buffer II, 200 muM of each dNTP, and 0.5 units of AmpliTaq Gold DNA Polymerase (PE Biosystems). The PCR conditions (in BIO-RAD PCR apparatus) were as follows: heating at 95degreesC for 5 min, followed by 30 cycles of denaturation at 95degreesC for 30 s, annealing temperature for 45 s, extension at 72degreesC for 45 s, and a final extension at 72degreesC for 10 min. The amplified DNA fragments were analyzed by a previously described modified PCR-SSCP method [32]. Exons 2-4 were applied to an 8% polyacrylamide gel (without glycerol) and carried on electrophoresis at 200 V for 2 h. Denatured single-stranded DNA fragments separated in the gel were visualized by silver staining. Genomic DNAs from HLA homozygous typing cell lines with MICB alleles were used as standards [33].

###begin title 26
sMICA analysis
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 81 83 81 83 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">34</xref>
###xml 414 415 414 415 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y</italic>
###xml 454 455 454 455 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">X</italic>
###xml 476 477 476 477 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">X</italic>
cMICA in serum was tested by sandwich enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) [34]. The ELISA development kit contains the basic components required, including capture antibody, detection antibody, standard, and Streptavidin-HRP (R&D Systems, USA). After being tested in microplate reader (Tecan, Australia) set at 450 nm, we constructed a standard curve by plotting the mean absorbance for each standard on the Y axis against the concentration on the X axis, calculated as X. The data were fitted by plotting the log of the MICA levels (picograms per milliliter) versus the log of the O.D based on linear regression analysis.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
Statistical analysis
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 159 162 159 160 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#967;</italic>
###xml 162 163 160 161 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 435 436 433 434 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 438 440 436 438 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pc</italic>
###xml 461 462 459 460 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 548 549 542 543 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 554 556 548 550 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pc</italic>
SPSS 11.5 was used for statistical calculations. The allelic frequencies were calculated by direct counting, and statistical comparisons were performed by the chi2 test with Fisher's exact test. sMICA levels were analyzed by independent samples nonparametric test. Odds ratios (OR) and 95% confidence intervals (CI) were calculated for the carriers of the specific genotype. Bonferroni multiple corrections were used for the corrected P (Pc) by multiplying the P value by the number of the statistical tests in order to avoid the alpha1 error. The P and Pc below 0.05 were considered to be statistically significant.

Furthermore, the frequency of MICB0106 allele was significantly higher in the male patients (27/122; 22.1% vs. 8.0%; P = 0.001; Pc = 0.006; OR = 3.276; 95%CI, 1.737-6.178) and in those patients over the age of 40 years (34/118; 28.8% vs. 8.3%; P = 0.0001; Pc = 0.0006; OR = 4.500; 95%CI, 2.381-8.504) compared with healthy controls (13/88 and 17/206, respectively). Therefore, MICB0106 allele was associated with extensive colitis, moderate and severe disease, EIMs, male UC patients, and the patients over the age of 40 years.

###begin title 50
Discussion
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 41 44 41 44 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MIC</italic>
Our study focused on the polymorphism of MIC gene which has strong linkage disequilibrium with MHC class I gene. MICA and MICB exclusively encode transcripts and respond to protein expression among all MIC members, while the association of MICB exon 2-4 polymorphisms and soluble MICA (sMICA) expression with the susceptibility of UC has still not been clarified.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 60 62 60 62 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">23</xref>
###xml 90 92 90 92 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">36</xref>
###xml 218 220 218 220 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">27</xref>
###xml 237 239 237 239 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">28</xref>
###xml 511 519 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 702 710 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 715 723 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 820 826 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">people</span>
###xml 1061 1067 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">people</span>
In recent studies on MIC genes susceptible to UC, MICA*007 [23], MICA*00801, and MICA-A6 [36] were shown to be associated with UC onset in Japan. Previously, we found that MICA-A5.1/A5.1 homozygous genotype MICA-A5.1 [27] and MICB-CA18 [28] alleles were associated with UC in the Chinese Han population. In this study, we included a limited population in central China which may not be generalized; however, we found that the frequency of MICB0106 allele was significantly higher in the UC group, especially in patients with extensive colitis, moderate and severe disease, and in those with extraintestinal manifestations. In addition, the frequency of MICB0106 was significantly higher in the male UC patients and patients over the age of 40 years than in healthy controls. These positive associations suggest that the people carrying MICB0106 allele are at higher risk of developing UC and might be affected more severely and more extensively throughout the colon or develop more extraintestinal manifestations. In terms of UC susceptibility, males and older people are more affected. We did not analyze the association of UC with MICB genotype because of the small size of each subgroup.
###end p 52

###xml 416 417 414 415 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 428 429 426 427 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 461 464 459 460 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#967;</italic>
###xml 464 465 460 461 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 476 477 472 473 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 639 641 635 637 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">28</xref>
###xml 753 757 749 753 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MICB</italic>
So far, very few studies have observed a relationship between MICB gene polymorphism and UC. Based on genotyping, we selected several publications [22, 35] from a number of different ethnic populations. The comparison of MICB alleles in our study (Hubei Han population, China) with those in England and Spain showed significant differences in the distribution of the exon2-4 of MICB alleles (Chinese vs. English, chi2 = 50.233, P = 0.0001; Chinese vs. Spanish, chi2 = 13.700, P = 0.018). Furthermore, significant differences in the distribution of the intron 1 of MICB alleles were also observed between the Chinese and other populations [28]. These findings suggest that the differences in ethnic and regional living environment would influence on the MICB gene distribution. The association of MICB with UC might be specific to the Chinese population or even to the Han population in central China.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 338 345 338 340 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#955;s</italic>
###xml 366 373 361 363 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#955;s</italic>
###xml 396 398 386 388 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR37">37</xref>
###xml 424 426 414 416 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">27</xref>
###xml 535 536 525 526 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
The neighbors MICA and MICB have big similarities in both molecular structure and encoding function. The analysis in available Spanish families indicated that MICB0106 was part of the B8-DR3-DQ2 haplotype (HLA-B*0802-MICA-A5.1-MICB0106-DRB1-*0301-DQA1-*0501-DQB1-*0201). Accordingly, a strong linkage disequilibrium of MICB0106 with DQ2 (lambdas = 1) and MICA-A5.1 (lambdas = 0.55) was observed [37]. In our previous study [27], we also observed that some MICA-MICB haplotypes occurred at frequencies of strong linkage disequilibrium (P < 0.01). Therefore, we hypothesize that sMICA expression in serum might also be associated with UC.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 220 228 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 742 750 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 895 903 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 916 923 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patient</span>
By ELISA analysis, we found sMICA are positively associated with UC compared with healthy controls but not associated with MICB gene polymorphism. These observations indicate that sMICA plays a very important role in UC patients, while it is still not clear whether sMICA protein expression is related to the MICB0106 and MICA-A5.1 genotype. The significantly higher MICB0106 gene expression may not be able to fully affect sMICA protein expression, which means that it might affect the function of MIC genes in ways other than genotype or the relatively small sample we studied. A bias of this study might also be that the serum samples were not taken at the time point of diagnosis, so that the rate of disease development may vary between patients. Therefore, a prospective study to analyze serum samples procured at the time of diagnosis will help to clarify whether sMICA can identify risk patients or optimize patient treatment.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 132 133 132 133 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>
###xml 152 154 152 154 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR38">38</xref>
###xml 181 183 181 183 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR39">39</xref>
###xml 519 521 504 506 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">22</xref>
###xml 655 657 640 642 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR40">40</xref>
###xml 801 803 786 788 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR41">41</xref>
###xml 831 833 816 818 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">34</xref>
###xml 271 274 <span type="species:ncbi:12305?0.03296277145811789|species:ncbi:36410?3.8779731127197516E-4">CMV</span>
As previously reported, MIC expression is under the control of heat shock elements and is increased in response to cellular stress [6] including viral [38] and bacterial infection [39]. In recent observations, MIC-NKG2D engagement augments both the cytotoxic response of CMV-infected CD8+alphabetaT cells and TCR-dependent activation of Vgamma2Vdelta2 T cells by bacterial antigens, which suggest that this interaction might provide an important costimulatory signal for cytokine production and cellular proliferation [22]. sMICA molecules impair the functions of NKG2D+ T and NK cells, which may facilitate tumor cells to escape from immunosurveillance [40]. Serum levels of sMICA have been shown to be of diagnostic significance in malignant diseases of various origins, including multiple myeloma [41] and colon adenocarcinoma [34]. sMICA are elevated and responsible for down-modulation of NKG2D and other important activating chemokine receptors to deactivate NK immunity. These immune reactions may also present in UC disease which confirms our findings.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
In conclusion, our study strongly suggests that MICB gene polymorphism is positively associated with UC in the Han Chinese in central China; sMICA was highly expressed in UC but not associated with the MICB0106 genotype. Further investigation on the function of MIC genes may be interesting in future.
