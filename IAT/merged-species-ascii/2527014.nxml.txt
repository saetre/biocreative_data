Plant-like substitutions in the large-subunit carboxy terminus of Chlamydomonas Rubisco increase CO2/O2 Specificity
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 132 158 132 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas reinhardtii </italic>
###xml 478 492 478 492 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 132 157 <span type="species:ncbi:3055">Chlamydomonas reinhardtii</span>
Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate is the rate-limiting enzyme in photosynthesis. The catalytic large subunit of the green-algal enzyme from Chlamydomonas reinhardtii is ~90% identical to the flowering-plant sequences, although they confer diverse kinetic properties. To identify the regions that may account for species variation in kinetic properties, directed mutagenesis and chloroplast transformation were used to create four amino-acid substitutions in the carboxy terminus of the Chlamydomonas large subunit to mimic the sequence of higher-specificity plant enzymes.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 52 53 52 53 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 55 57 55 57 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
The quadruple-mutant enzyme has a 10% increase in CO2/O2 specificity and a lower carboxylation catalytic efficiency. The mutations do not seem to influence the protein expression, structural stability or the function in vivo.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Owing to the decreased carboxylation catalytic efficiency, the quadruple-mutant is not a "better" enzyme. Nonetheless, because of its positive influence on specificity, the carboxy terminus, relatively far from the active site, may serve as a target for enzyme improvement via combinatorial approaches.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 232 233 232 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 242 243 242 243 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 245 247 245 247 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 327 328 323 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">k</italic>
###xml 328 331 324 327 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cat</sub>
###xml 332 333 328 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 333 334 329 330 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</sub>
###xml 355 356 351 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 356 357 352 353 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</sub>
###xml 358 359 354 355 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 359 360 355 356 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</sub>
###xml 377 378 373 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 378 379 374 375 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 380 381 376 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 381 383 377 379 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o </sub>
###xml 384 385 380 381 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 409 411 405 407 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 499 501 495 497 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 502 503 498 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 504 505 500 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 573 574 569 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase (Rubisco) (EC 4.1.1.39) is limited by a low carboxylation rate and competing oxygenase activity that initiates a wasteful photorespiratory pathway leading to the loss of fixed carbon [1]. The CO2/O2 specificity (Omega) of Rubisco is equal to the ratio of catalytic efficiencies (kcat/Km) for carboxylation (Vc/Kc) to oxygenation Vo/Ko [2]. Net photosynthetic CO2 fixation would be increased if Rubisco could work faster with greater specificity for CO2 [1,2]. Because there is natural variation in Rubisco kinetic constants [3], it may be possible to develop genetic engineering strategies aimed at improving the enzyme by focusing on regions responsible for this variation.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 15 41 15 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas reinhardtii </italic>
###xml 172 177 172 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL </italic>
###xml 247 252 247 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcS </italic>
###xml 260 261 260 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 352 357 352 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcS </italic>
###xml 373 378 373 378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL </italic>
###xml 416 417 416 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 418 419 418 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 430 444 430 444 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 548 549 544 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 720 721 716 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 722 723 718 719 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 760 774 756 770 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 845 846 841 842 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 990 1004 986 1000 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 1086 1099 1082 1095 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas</italic>
###xml 1139 1140 1131 1132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 15 40 <span type="species:ncbi:3055">Chlamydomonas reinhardtii</span>
###xml 951 958 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
The green alga Chlamydomonas reinhardtii is an excellent model for studying plant-like Rubisco enzymes comprised of eight large subunits (~55 kDa, coded by the chloroplast rbcL gene) and eight small subunits (~15 kDa, coded by a family of nuclear rbcS genes) [1]. Mutants that lack Rubisco function can be maintained with acetate, and both the nuclear rbcS and chloroplast rbcL genes are amenable to transformation [4,5]. Because Chlamydomonas Rubisco has a faster carboxylation rate and lower Omega value than Rubisco enzymes of flowering plants [3], but shares ~90% sequence identity with flowering-plant enzymes, a phylogenetic approach has been initiated to define the structural basis for differences in catalysis [6,7]. There are only 34 residues in the Chlamydomonas large subunit that differ from those of 500 flowering-plant sequences [6]. Changing five of these "phylogenetic" residues to those common to flowering plants, and introducing a spinach small-subunit loop, produced a Chlamydomonas Rubisco enzyme with catalytic properties more like those of flowering plants than Chlamydomonas, including a 12-17% increase in Omega [7]. However, because the Omega value was not identical to that of flowering-plant Rubisco, there must be other residues that also contribute to the differences in Rubisco catalytic properties.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 170 171 156 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 179 180 165 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 301 302 287 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 305 306 291 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 307 309 293 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 315 329 301 315 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 426 428 412 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 521 523 503 505 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 583 597 565 579 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Synechococcus </italic>
###xml 775 777 749 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 869 871 843 845 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 950 952 920 922 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
The loop between beta-strand 6 and alpha-helix 6 of the large-subunit alpha/beta barrel folds over the transition-state analog carboxyarabinitol 1,5-bisphosphate (CABP) [8] (Fig. 1). In this closed conformation, the large-subunit carboxy terminus (residues Trp-462 to Leu-475) packs over loop 6 (Fig. 1) [9,10]. In Chlamydomonas Rubisco, substitution of conserved Asp-473, which was proposed to be an essential latch residue [11], with either Ala or Glu did not eliminate catalysis but caused a 14-17% decrease in Omega [12]. Deletion of 10 residues from the carboxy terminus of the Synechococcus (cyanobacterium) large subunit caused a 38% decrease in Omega, and a group of four plant-like substitutions E470P/T471A/K474T/L475V was reported to cause a 9% increase in Omega [13]. However, this small increase was close to the experimental error of the assays employed [13]. Lengthening the carboxy terminus beyond residue 475 had no effect on Omega [14].
###end p 11

###begin p 13
###xml 4 18 4 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 153 155 153 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 158 159 158 159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 288 301 288 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Synechococcus</italic>
###xml 323 325 323 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 512 526 512 526 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 627 629 627 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 605 612 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
The Chlamydomonas carboxy-terminal residues Asp-470, Thr-471, Ile-472, and Lys-474 comprise a set of previously identified "phylogenetic" residues (Fig. 1A) [6] that differ from the set common to flowering plants (Glu-470, Ala-471, Met-472, and Thr-474). The carboxy terminal residues of Synechococcus, studied previously [13], comprise a somewhat different set of residues (Thr-471, Lys-474, and Leu-475) relative to those of flowering plants (Ala-471, Thr-474, and Val-475). Therefore, the carboxy terminus of Chlamydomonas was changed via directed mutagenesis and chloroplast transformation to that of spinach Rubisco (Fig. 1B) to see whether the catalytic properties common to Rubisco of flowering plants might be obtained.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
Recovery and phenotype of the mutant strain
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 27 41 27 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 157 158 157 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 245 258 245 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas</italic>
###xml 315 317 315 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 354 355 354 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 483 497 483 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 189 196 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 288 295 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 458 465 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 594 601 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
The residues equivalent to Chlamydomonas Asp-470, Thr-471, Ile-472, and Lys-474 are Glu, Ala, Met, and Thr, respectively, in most flowering-plant sequences [6]. The carboxy terminus of the spinach large subunit is identical in length to that of Chlamydomonas, and the availability of the spinach crystal structure [10] allows structural comparison (Fig. 1). However, whereas most plant Rubisco enzymes contain a Glu at position 470, this residue is a Pro in spinach Rubisco. Thus, a Chlamydomonas D470P/T471A/I472M/K474T mutant enzyme was created to mimic the large-subunit carboxy terminus of spinach Rubisco.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 9 13 9 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 62 66 62 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 459 461 453 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
When the rbcL-deletion mutant MX3312 was transformed with the rbcL-D470P/T471A/I472M/K474T gene, photosynthesis-competent colonies were recovered. Thus, the substituted residues are not essential for Rubisco expression, assembly, or function in vivo. Furthermore, the mutant strain was indistinguishable from wild type with respect to growth at either 25 or 35degreesC, eliminating the possibility that the mutant is temperature conditional (data not shown) [15].
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Mutant Rubisco holoenzyme stability
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 165 166 165 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 564 565 564 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
To assess the effect of the mutant substitutions on holoenzyme levels in vivo, protein extract was subjected to SDS-PAGE and western-blot analysis. As shown in Fig. 2, the D470P/T471A/I472M/K474T quadruple-mutant strain contains wild-type levels of Rubisco subunits. A wild-type level of holoenzyme could also be purified from the quadruple mutant when cell extract was fractionated on a sucrose-density gradient (data not shown). No difference was detected between the mutant and wild-type enzymes when thermal stability experiments were performed in vitro (Fig. 3). Thus, analysis of the catalytic properties of the quadruple-mutant enzyme would not be expected to be influenced by structural instability during the assays.

To determine whether the spinach-like substitutions can confer catalytic properties mimicking those of the spinach enzyme, kinetic constants were determined for the purified mutant enzyme (Table 1). The Omega value of the D470P/T471A/I472M/K474T quadruple-mutant Rubisco enzyme is 10% higher than that of the wild-type Chlamydomonas enzyme. Nonetheless, this value is still 17% lower than that of spinach Rubisco (Table 1). Although the Omega value appears to be altered in the correct direction, the other kinetic constants remain quite different from those of spinach Rubisco (Table 1). The increase in Omega is likely the consequence of an increase in Ko/Kc (Table 1). Due to an apparent decrease in Vc/Kc, the D470P/T471A/I472M/K474T quadruple-mutant enzyme would not be a "better" Rubisco [1].

###begin title 26
Discussion
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 20 34 20 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 256 257 252 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 308 309 304 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 381 395 377 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 519 521 515 517 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c </sub>
###xml 526 528 522 524 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o </sub>
###xml 535 536 531 532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 559 573 555 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 743 744 739 740 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 965 966 961 962 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 996 1015 992 1011 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chromatium vinosum </italic>
###xml 1085 1087 1081 1083 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 74 81 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 233 240 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 399 406 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 463 470 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 881 888 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 996 1014 <span type="species:ncbi:1049">Chromatium vinosum</span>
Substitution of the Chlamydomonas carboxy-terminal residues with those of spinach, to produce the D470P/T471A/I472M/K474T quadruple-mutant enzyme, causes a 10% increase in Omega, but the other kinetic constants are not like those of spinach Rubisco (Table 1). Thus, these engineered "phylogenetic" residues [6] do not by themselves account for the differences in catalysis between Chlamydomonas and spinach Rubisco. However, there should be other residues in the spinach enzyme that complement for the lower values of Kc and Ko (Table 1). Residue 341 (Val in Chlamydomonas and Ile in flowering-plant enzymes) is a "phylogenetic" residue in the loop 6, which is in van der Waals contact with the conserved Asp-473 of the carboxy terminus (Fig. 1). Another divergent residue, Arg-305, is also involved in van der Waals contact with the carboxy terminus. The analogous Lys-305 of the spinach enzyme interacts with the carboxy terminus via a single hydrogen-bond (Fig. 1) and a P305K substitution in Chromatium vinosum Rubisco causes an 80% increase in carboxylation catalytic efficiency [16]. It is thus likely that the addition of V341I and R305K substitutions to the D470P/T471A/I472M/K474T quadruple mutant would confer kinetic constants that are more like the flowering-plant enzymes.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 15 29 15 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Synechococcus </italic>
###xml 205 207 201 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 245 259 237 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Synechococcus </italic>
###xml 314 328 302 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 567 581 555 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Synechococcus </italic>
###xml 590 592 578 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 638 652 626 640 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 661 662 649 650 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 670 671 658 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 739 741 723 725 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 96 103 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
Engineering of Synechococcus Rubisco to produce an E470P/T471A/K474T/L475V mutant enzyme with a spinach-like large-subunit carboxy terminus may also have resulted in a similar, relative increase in Omega [13], but the Omega value of that mutant Synechococcus enzyme is still ~10% lower than the Omega value of the Chlamydomonas quadruple-mutant enzyme analyzed in the present study. Variation in amino-acid identities at residues 472 and 475 may indicate that these residues are less important than the others. The loss of the E470-R131 and E470-K474 salt bridges in Synechococcus Rubisco [17] and D470-R131 and D470-K474 salt bridges in Chlamydomonas Rubisco [9] (Fig. 1) may be primarily responsible for the observed increases in Omega [18].
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 70 84 70 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 157 158 153 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 341 342 334 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 343 345 336 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 476 477 465 466 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 680 681 665 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 845 859 826 840 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 958 959 939 940 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 318 325 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 460 467 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 619 626 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
###xml 941 948 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
In a recent study, a set of three "phylogenetic" substitutions in the Chlamydomonas large subunit (C256F/K258R/I265V), which causes a 10% decrease in Omega [6], was complemented by the addition of two large-subunit phylogenetic substitutions (V221C/V235I) and the loop that resides between beta-strands A and B of the spinach small subunit [7,19]. The resultant penta/ABSO enzyme has a ~15% higher Omega value and all other catalytic properties similar to the spinach enzyme [7]. Although the D470P/T471A/I472M/K474T quadruple-mutant enzyme, analyzed in the present study, does not have kinetic constants like those of spinach Rubisco, it does have a 10% increase in Omega (Table 1). If these carboxy-terminal substitutions could increase the Omega value of the penta/ABSO enzyme by 10% without substantially altering other kinetic constants, a Chlamydomonas enzyme would be generated with kinetic properties indistinguishable from those of spinach Rubisco [7].
###end p 29
###begin title 30
Conclusion
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 136 150 136 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas </italic>
###xml 288 290 288 290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 154 161 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
Such combinatorial approaches may identify only a small number of regions responsible for the differences in kinetic properties between Chlamydomonas and spinach Rubisco. These regions may then serve as suitable targets for DNA shuffling and genetic selection aimed at improving Rubisco [20].

###begin title 35
Directed mutagenesis and transformation
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 21 25 21 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 42 44 42 44 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 51 70 51 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlamydomonas rbcL </italic>
###xml 491 496 491 496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL </italic>
###xml 530 533 530 533 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pac</italic>
###xml 608 611 608 611 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bsp</italic>
###xml 618 621 618 621 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pac</italic>
###xml 706 709 706 709 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pac</italic>
###xml 720 724 720 724 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 726 729 726 729 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pac</italic>
###xml 780 782 780 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 783 785 783 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 881 882 880 881 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
Using the plasmid pUCrbcLP as a template [21], the Chlamydomonas rbcL gene was PCR-amplified, with the first base of the coding region numbered 1, using forward (bases -169 to -144) and reverse (bases 1404 to 1438) primers. The reverse primer was designed to change codons GAT to CCT, ACT to GCT, ATT to ATG, and AAA to ACA, thereby introducing amino-acid substitutions D470P, T471A, I472M, and K474T, respectively. The reverse primer also contained a 2-base change (TT to AA) following the rbcL stop codon that would introduce a PacI recognition site. After PCR amplification, the product was digested with BspEI and PacI, and cloned into the corresponding sites of a modified plasmid containing the same PacI site (pUCrbcLP-PacI). Chloroplast-gene transformation was performed [22,23], and photosynthesis-competent colonies were selected on minimal medium with 80 mumol photons/m2/s. Single-colony isolation, followed by PCR and restriction-enzyme analysis, was performed to ensure homoplasmicity of the mutant gene, which was sequenced completely to confirm the mutations.

###xml 372 374 372 374 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">14</sup>
###xml 384 386 384 386 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">14</sup>
###xml 388 390 388 390 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sub>
###xml 391 393 391 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 544 545 538 539 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 578 580 572 574 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">14</sup>
###xml 582 584 576 578 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sub>

###xml 72 79 <span type="species:ncbi:3562">spinach</span>
