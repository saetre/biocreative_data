Norepinephrine Controls Both Torpor Initiation and Emergence via Distinct Mechanisms in the Mouse
###end article-title 0

###begin p 2
###xml 197 200 194 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh</italic>

###xml 664 668 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>

###xml 966 970 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1141 1145 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Some mammals, including laboratory mice, enter torpor in response to food deprivation, and leptin can attenuate these bouts of torpor. We previously showed that dopamine beta-hydroxylase knockout (Dbh -/-) mice, which lack norepinephrine (NE), do not reduce circulating leptin upon fasting nor do they enter torpor. To test whether the onset of torpor in mice during a fast requires a NE-mediated reduction in circulating leptin, double mutant mice deficient in both leptin (ob/ob) and DBH (DBL MUT) were generated. Upon fasting, control and ob/ob mice entered torpor as assessed by telemetric core Tb acquisition. While fasting failed to induce torpor in Dbh -/- mice, leptin deficiency bypassed the requirement for NE, as DBL MUT mice readily entered torpor upon fasting. These data indicate that sympathetic activation of white fat and suppression of leptin is required for the onset of torpor in the mouse. Emergence from torpor was severely retarded in DBL MUT mice, revealing a novel, leptin-independent role for NE in torpor recovery. This phenotype was mimicked by administration of a beta3 adrenergic receptor antagonist to control mice during a torpor bout. Hence, NE signaling via beta3 adrenergic receptors presumably in brown fat is the first neurotransmitter-receptor system identified that is required for normal recovery from torpor.

###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 60 61 60 61 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">a</sub>
###xml 242 245 236 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Bouthegourd1">[1]</xref>
###xml 246 249 240 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Williams1">[6]</xref>
###xml 420 423 414 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Geiser1">[7]</xref>
###xml 938 941 932 935 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Cannon1">[8]</xref>
###xml 1193 1196 1187 1190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Thomas1">[9]</xref>
###xml 75 79 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 468 473 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 779 783 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1143 1147 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Under acute food deprivation and cool ambient temperature (Ta), laboratory mice allow their body temperature to fall to near ambient temperature, with a minimum body temperature around 20degreesC and a concomitant reduction in metabolic rate [1]-[6]. This heterothermic response contrasts with true hibernators, like ground squirrels, that can achieve a minimum body temperature at or below freezing for weeks at a time [7]. Common to both daily heterotherms like the mouse and hibernators is the endogenous production of heat to facilitate exit from torpor. Heat can be generated via general activity (i.e. voluntary muscle contractions), shivering (i.e. involuntary muscle contractions), and through futile cycling of ions, such as protons or calcium. In small mammals such as mice, an important source of rapid heat production comes from brown fat thermogenesis, which involves uncoupling the synthesis of ATP from the proton gradient [8]. In general, heat production, including brown fat thermogenesis, is primarily under the control of the sympathetic nervous system (SNS) in mammals. The SNS is also critical for preventing heat loss in mice via vasoconstriction of the tail vasculature [9].
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 131 138 128 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 385 392 382 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 447 451 444 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Swoap1">[10]</xref>
###xml 542 546 539 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Bartness1">[11]</xref>
###xml 547 551 544 548 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Lowell1">[13]</xref>
###xml 751 758 745 752 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 785 789 779 783 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Swoap1">[10]</xref>
###xml 870 874 864 868 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Doring1">[14]</xref>
###xml 876 880 870 874 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Geiser2">[15]</xref>
###xml 962 966 956 960 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Gavrilova1">[16]</xref>
###xml 140 144 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 393 397 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 759 763 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Epinephrine (Epi) and norepinephrine (NE) are the primary neurotransmitters for the SNS. Using dopamine beta-hydroxylase knockout (Dbh -/-) mice that lack both Epi and NE, we wished to test the hypothesis that these neurotransmitters are required for the thermogenic events required for exit from torpor. However, we could not test this hypothesis when we were surprised to learn that Dbh -/- mice failed to enter torpor under inducing conditions [10]. Both brown adipose tissue (BAT) and white adipose tissue (WAT) are innervated by the SNS [11]-[13]. Pharmacological evidence suggested that the lack of sympathetic activation of WAT, mediated by the beta3-adrenergic receptor, and subsequent failure to lower circulating leptin levels prevented the Dbh -/- mice from entering torpor [10]. Indeed, administration of leptin attenuates torpor bouts in daily heterotherms [14], [15], although low circulating leptin levels are not the only signal for torpor entry [16].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 16 23 16 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 134 141 134 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 172 177 172 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ob/ob</italic>
###xml 275 282 275 282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 24 28 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 142 146 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 227 231 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 283 287 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 333 337 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 405 409 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 475 479 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 759 764 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
To test whether Dbh -/- mice do not enter torpor upon fasting because of their failure to suppress circulating leptin levels, we bred Dbh -/- mice onto a leptin-deficient (ob/ob) background to produce double knockout (DBL MUT) mice. We reasoned that if the lack of torpor in Dbh -/- mice was a result of hyperleptinemia, the DBL MUT mice would enter torpor bouts upon fasting. Our results show that these mice do in fact enter daily torpor upon fasting. Hence, we used these mice to test our original hypothesis that exit from a torpor bout requires sympathetic release of NE for endogenous heat production. Our data identify NE and beta3-adrenergic receptors as the first neurotransmitter-receptor system required for the normal emergence from torpor in the mouse.

Dbh -/- males were bred to ob/+ heterozygous females to produce Dbh+/-, ob/+ offspring. These double heterozygotes were bred to each other to produce the final breeder males (Dbh -/-, ob/+) and females (Dbh +/-, ob/+). These breeders were crossed to generate the 4 genotypes tested in the experiments: control (Dbh +/-, ob/+ or +/+), NE-deficient (Dbh -/-, ob/+ or +/+), leptin-deficient (Dbh +/-, ob/ob), or both NE- and leptin-deficient (Dbh -/-, ob/ob), named DBL MUT. Pregnant dams carrying potential Dbh -/- fetuses were given the adrenergic receptor agonists isoproterenol and phenylephrine from E9.5-E14.5 and the synthetic NE precursor L-3,4-dihydroxyphenylserine (DOPS) from E14.5-birth in their drinking water to rescue the embryonic lethality associated with the homozygous Dbh -/- mutation, as described [17]. Dbh -/-, ob/+ breeder males were given daily DOPS injections to rescue the male fertility deficit associated with the Dbh -/- mutation [17]. All genotypes were confirmed by PCR. Dbh +/- mice were used as controls because they have normal catecholamine levels and are indistinguishable from wild-type mice for all previously tested phenotypes, including torpor [10], [17]. No differences were observed between ob/+ and +/+ mice, and results were combined. All mice were born and reared in a specific pathogen-free facility at Emory University with a 12 h light/dark cycle (lights on - 7 am; lights off - 7 pm). Food and water were available ad libitum (except during torpor experiments, as described below). All mice were shipped to Williams College for physiological assessment. Studies were approved by each of the local IACUCs and guidelines for animal husbandry of both institutions were followed.

###begin title 12
Implantation of temperature telemeters
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 162 167 162 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ob/ob</italic>

###xml 126 130 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 284 288 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Male and female mice aged 6 months were used in all experiments. No gender differences were found, and results were combined. Mice (n = 6 for control, n = 16 for ob/ob, n = 5 for Dbh -/-, n = 6 for DBL MUT) were implanted with temperature telemeters as described previously [10]. The mice were maintained on a heating pad for 48 hours following the surgery, and then housed individually at 28-30degreesC for 10 days to allow time for recovery.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Temperature data collection and analysis
###end title 14
###begin p 15
Data from the temperature telemeters were recorded at 500 Hz, for one second, once per minute for 23 hours using Data Sciences Int. acquisition software. To calculate maximum rates of temperature gain and temperature loss, a 30 minute sliding window was used to calculate the rate of temperature change. The maximums and minimums of this data series were then calculated.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Experimental protocol
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 407 411 390 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Sato1">[18]</xref>
###xml 716 717 699 700 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">b</sub>
###xml 47 51 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 173 177 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 229 233 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 327 331 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 679 683 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
After the 10 day recovery period from surgery, mice were moved to a temperature-controlled cage (+/-0.25degreesC) at 21degreesC and held there for two days. On day one, the mice had free access to food and water. On day two, the mice were fasted at the onset of the dark cycle, but had free access to water. In one experiment, mice were injected with SR59230A, a potent beta3 adrenergic receptor antagonist [18]. SR59230A was dissolved in DMSO, and diluted in sterile phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) to a concentration of 2.5 mg/ml. DMSO was at a final concentration of 3%. 0.2 ml of either SR59230A or vehicle (3% DMSO in PBS) was injected subcutaneously between the scapula in mice in fasting-induced torpor at a Tb of approximately25degreesC.

###begin title 18
Statistics
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 400 401 395 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
GraphPad Prism 4.0c for Macintosh was used for all statistical tests. All results are reported as means+/-SE. Mice that are deficient in both leptin and DBH are viable following prenatal NE and Epi replacement. These DBL MUT mice are hyperphagic and obese to the same extent as ob/ob mice (Fig. 1). If NE activation of the beta3 adrenergic receptor on brown fat is required for rapid thermogenesis and recovery from torpor, then administration of a beta3 antagonist should blunt the rate of recovery from a bout of torpor. To test this prediction, control mice (n = 16) were fasted to induce a bout of torpor. When their Tb was approximately25degreesC, the mice were administered either vehicle (n = 6) or the beta3 antagonist SR59230A (n = 10) via a subcutaneous injection in the vicinity of the interscapular brown fat pads. Handling of the mice for subcutaneous injection caused immediate arousal from the torpid state, but the rate of arousal following beta3 antagonist administration was blunted (70% of the rate following vehicle administration) (Fig. 4). These data, combined with the slow recovery of torpor in DBL MUT mice, demonstrate the requirement for activation of the beta3 adrenergic receptor by NE for normal emergence from torpor.

###end title 45
###begin p 46

###xml 571 578 559 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 1014 1021 999 1006 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 227 231 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 285 289 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 406 410 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 492 496 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 579 583 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 816 820 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1036 1040 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The breeding of DBH deficiency onto an ob/ob background has allowed for the testing of two separate hypotheses concerning noradrenergic control of entrance and emergence from torpor. First, we had found previously that Dbh -/- mice do not enter torpor under conditions where wild-type mice do [10], namely a 24 hour fast at a typical housing temperature (21degreesC). This phenotype was rescued in Dbh -/- mice by administration of a beta3-adrenergic receptor agonist and mimicked in control mice by a beta3 antagonist [10]. The correlation between the lack of torpor in Dbh -/- mice and the failure to suppress leptin levels during a fast led us to postulate that it was the inability to engage the SNS, activate beta3 receptors and suppress leptin secretion from white adipose tissue during a fast that kept these mice from entering torpor. Our second hypothesis was that emergence from torpor requires thermogenesis driven by sympathetic innervation of brown adipose tissue, an idea that could not be tested in Dbh -/- single mutant mice due to their inability to enter torpor.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 94 101 94 101 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 276 283 276 283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 517 524 517 524 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 771 793 768 790 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004038-g005">Figure 5, left pathway</xref>
###xml 102 107 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 284 288 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 298 302 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 460 465 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The data presented here support the first hypothesis, that elevated leptin levels prevent the Dbh -/- mouse from entering fasting-induced torpor. We found that leptin deficiency was epistatic to NE deficiency; in contrast to the slow and modest hypothermia observed in fasted Dbh -/- mice, DBL MUT mice were able to respond to a fast with a true torpor bout. Further, we have found in a preliminary experiment that exogenous replacement of leptin in a DBL MUT mouse prevents torpor and causes a reversion back to the Dbh -/- single mutant phenotype (unpublished observations). These results suggest that entrance into torpor requires the sequential SNS release of NE, activation of beta3-adrenergic receptors on white adipocytes, and the suppression of leptin secretion (Figure 5, left pathway).
###end p 47
###begin title 48
###xml 60 65 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Hypothetical model of torpor induction and emergence in the mouse.
###end title 48
###begin p 49
During an acute food shortage, sympathetic discharge to white adipose tissue increases. Activation of the beta3 adrenergic receptor on white adipose tissue results in a fall in circulating leptin which is required for entrance into torpor. At some point during the torpor bout, sympathetic discharge to beta3 adrenergic receptors on brown fat activates existing UCP1 proteins resulting in heat production and ultimately exit from torpor. Decreased peripheral blood flow is also required to retain heat generated by brown fat, as well as shivering.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 259 260 247 248 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">b</sub>
###xml 883 886 868 871 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Cannon1">[8]</xref>
###xml 888 892 873 877 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Mory1">[19]</xref>
###xml 1045 1049 1030 1034 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Rasmussen1">[20]</xref>
###xml 1259 1262 1244 1247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-HimmsHagen1">[2]</xref>
###xml 1264 1268 1249 1253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Horwitz1">[21]</xref>
###xml 1269 1273 1254 1258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Smith1">[23]</xref>
###xml 1275 1282 1260 1267 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 1358 1361 1343 1346 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Thomas1">[9]</xref>
###xml 1772 1778 1754 1760 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004038-g004">Fig. 4</xref>
###xml 1909 1916 1888 1895 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 20 24 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 159 163 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 335 340 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 445 449 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 691 695 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1283 1287 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1453 1457 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1552 1556 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1766 1770 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1917 1921 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 2076 2080 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 2113 2117 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Because the DBL MUT mice were able to enter torpor, we could test our second hypothesis, that NE is required for exit from torpor. We found that while DBL MUT mice initiated torpor recovery at an appropriate time (approximately5 hours after reaching minimum Tb), the rate of temperature gain was severely retarded. The rate at which a mouse recovers from torpor will be dependent upon both heat production and heat loss. It appears that DBL MUT mice have a deficit in both heat production and heat retention, which would explain their slow arousal from torpor. The two primary sources of heat production available to small mammals are non-shivering thermogenesis (NST) and shivering. NST in mice is achieved predominantly through activation of uncoupling protein 1 (UCP1), which requires sympathetic release of NE and activation of beta3-adrenergic receptors in brown adipose tissue [8], [19]. The thermogenic role of brown adipose tissue in hibernators has been well established; indeed, this tissue was initially named the "hibernation gland" [20]. Activity of UCP1 within brown adipose is blocked both during hibernation and during daily torpor and unblocked during arousal from both states with the resultant elevation of temperature within brown adipose [2], [21]-[23]. Dbh -/- mice have low basal UCP1 levels and cannot induce UCP1 in response to cold [9], and the data presented here indicate that the slow emergence from daily torpor in DBL MUT mice may be partially explained by their inability to activate UCP1 within brown fat. When control mice were treated with a beta3 antagonist during torpor in an attempt to inhibit NE-induced thermogenesis from brown fat, rates of arousal from torpor were significantly slowed, although not as slow as the DBL MUT mice (Fig. 4). There are several possible explanations for this disparity. For example, there is a total genetic lack of NE/beta3 signaling in Dbh -/- mice, whereas antagonist treatment results in partial pharmacological blockade in the presence of competition from the endogenous ligand. In addition, DBL MUT mice were undisturbed, while control mice were injected with antagonist, and handling itself precipitates rapid recovery from torpor. Finally, SR59230A may have impacted lipolysis from white fat to influence the recovery rate from torpor, or had other effects in addition to beta3 receptor antagonism that influenced torpor recovery.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 82 89 82 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 179 182 179 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Thomas1">[9]</xref>
###xml 189 194 189 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ob/ob</italic>
###xml 229 233 229 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-HimmsHagen2">[24]</xref>
###xml 435 439 429 433 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Coleman1">[25]</xref>
###xml 441 445 435 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Golozoubova1">[26]</xref>
###xml 455 460 449 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ob/ob</italic>
###xml 571 575 565 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Hayward1">[27]</xref>
###xml 576 580 570 574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Seydoux1">[29]</xref>
###xml 723 730 717 724 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 90 94 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 195 199 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 248 252 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 461 465 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 632 636 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 816 820 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Shivering is also a major component of heat generation in small mammals. Although Dbh -/- mice are cold sensitive due to lack of brown fat thermogenesis, they can shiver normally [9]. Both ob/ob mice, which have very little UCP1 [24], and UCP1 -/- mice are cold sensitive if immediately exposed to 4degreesC, but can tolerate this temperature if they undergo a cold adaptation protocol which allows time to increase shivering capacity [25], [26]. Indeed, ob/ob mice as well as many small marsupials exhibit rapid recovery from torpor despite having very little brown fat [27]-[29]. Although it remains to be determined, the DBL MUT mice likely have the ability to shiver. Thus, the lack of brown fat thermogenesis from the Dbh -/- mutation likely contributes to a severe impairment in heat generation in the DBL MUT mice.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 134 141 134 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 342 349 338 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 423 426 419 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Thomas1">[9]</xref>
###xml 448 455 444 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 593 599 589 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004038-g001">Fig. 1</xref>
###xml 629 636 625 632 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dbh &#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 681 686 677 682 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ob/ob</italic>
###xml 725 730 721 726 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0004038-t001">Table</xref>
###xml 154 158 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 350 354 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 456 460 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 511 515 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 559 563 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 781 785 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 941 946 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1076 1080 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The other side of the heat balance equation that allows for rapid arousal from a low body temperature is retention of heat generated. Dbh -/- and DBL MUT mice lack NE, the neurotransmitter not only required for sympathetic activation of fat, but also for alpha1-adrenergic receptor-mediated vasoconstriction of smooth muscle beds. Therefore, Dbh -/- mice are impaired in their ability to reduce blood flow to the periphery [9]. The hypothesis that Dbh -/- mice lose more heat through the periphery than control mice is supported by the observation that these mice eat similar amounts of food (Fig. 1) relative to their controls (Dbh -/- compared to control and DBL MUT compared to ob/ob), yet have a cooler body temperature (Table). However, the cooler body temperature of DBL MUT mice may be a reflection of energy storage at the expense of heat generation through metabolism. On the whole, it appears that heat generated by shivering in a mouse deficient in DBH is only partially retained, which combined with the deficit in UCP1 induction, further explains why the DBL MUT mice emerge from torpor so slowly.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 144 150 144 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004038-g005">Fig. 5</xref>
###xml 458 462 458 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Brito1">[30]</xref>
###xml 464 468 464 468 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Brito2">[31]</xref>
###xml 605 609 602 606 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Mantzoros1">[32]</xref>
###xml 610 614 607 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Young1">[34]</xref>
###xml 682 686 679 683 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004038-Myers1">[35]</xref>
We propose a model of fasting-induced torpor in a daily heterotherm that has differential sympathetic activation of adipose tissue at its core (Fig. 5). Brito and colleagues have recently shown differential activation of white and brown fat in response to fasting, glucoprivation, exposure to a cold environment, as well as a result of activation of the melanocortin 4 receptor, a hypothalamic signaling pathway that partially mediates the actions of leptin [30], [31]. Fasting and cool temperatures induce beta3-mediated sympathetic activation of white adipose, resulting in a fall in circulating leptin [32]-[34]. Suppression of leptin then leads to altered hypothalamic activity [35], with the outcome of a much reduced metabolic rate and body temperature. At some point during the torpor bout, arousal is initiated and the SNS activates beta3 receptors on brown fat, leading to rapid heat production. Additionally, the SNS activates (or continues to activate) the smooth muscle surrounding peripheral blood vessels, causing vasoconstriction and decreased peripheral blood flow. The generation of heat, and its retention, allow for rapid emergence from torpor, although the timing trigger for torpor recovery is still unknown. Thus, the coordination of sympathetic outflow to these two adipose tissues helps orchestrate the metabolic response to and ultimately survival from acute food shortages.
