###begin title 3
Background
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 0 19 0 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Schistosoma mansoni</italic>
###xml 0 19 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">Schistosoma mansoni</span>
###xml 149 154 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 206 211 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Schistosoma mansoni is the major causative agent of schistosomiasis. The parasite takes advantage of host signals to complete its development in the human body. Tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) is a human cytokine involved in skin inflammatory responses, and although its effect on the adult parasite's metabolism and egg-laying process has been previously described, a comprehensive assessment of the TNF-alpha pathway and its downstream molecular effects is lacking.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 85 95 81 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 97 99 93 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 106 108 102 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 293 295 289 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 467 476 457 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico</italic>
###xml 732 740 714 722 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 85 95 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 421 426 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 597 602 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 674 679 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In the present work we describe a possible TNF-alpha receptor (TNFR) homolog gene in S. mansoni (SmTNFR). SmTNFR encodes a complete receptor sequence composed of 599 amino acids, and contains four cysteine-rich domains as described for TNFR members. Real-time RT-PCR experiments revealed that SmTNFR highest expression level is in cercariae, 3.5 (+/-0.7) times higher than in adult worms. Downstream members of the known human TNF-alpha pathway were identified by an in silico analysis, revealing a possible TNF-alpha signaling pathway in the parasite. In order to simulate parasite's exposure to human cytokine during penetration of the skin, schistosomula were exposed to human TNF-alpha just 3 h after cercariae-to-schistosomula in vitro transformation, and large-scale gene expression measurements were performed with microarrays. A total of 548 genes with significantly altered expression were detected, when compared to control parasites. In addition, treatment of adult worms with TNF-alpha caused a significantly altered expression of 1857 genes. Interestingly, the set of genes altered in adults is different from that of schistosomula, with 58 genes in common, representing 3% of altered genes in adults and 11% in 3 h-old early schistosomula.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 94 104 90 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 68 73 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 94 104 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
We describe the possible molecular elements and targets involved in human TNF-alpha effect on S. mansoni, highlighting the mechanism by which recently transformed schistosomula may sense and respond to this host mediator at the site of cercarial penetration into the skin.

###begin title 9
Author Summary
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 0 19 0 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Schistosoma mansoni</italic>
###xml 534 544 522 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 744 752 732 740 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 0 19 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">Schistosoma mansoni</span>
###xml 394 399 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 534 544 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 787 792 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 854 859 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 960 965 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Schistosoma mansoni is the major causative agent of schistosomiasis in the Americas. This parasite takes advantage of host signaling molecules such as cytokines and hormones to complete its development inside the host. Tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) is one of the most important host cytokines involved in the inflammatory response. When cercariae, the infective stage, penetrates the human skin the release of TNF-alpha is started. In this work the authors describe the complete sequence of a possible TNF-alpha receptor in S. mansoni and detect that the receptor is most highly expressed in cercariae among all life cycle stages. Aiming to mimic the situation at the site of skin penetration, cercariae were mechanically transformed in vitro into schistosomula and exposed to human TNF-alpha. Exposure of early-developing schistosomula to the human hormone caused a large-scale change in the expression of parasite genes. Exposure of adult worms to human TNF-alpha caused gene expression changes as well, and the set of parasite altered genes in the adult parasite was different from that of schistosomula. This work increases the number of known signaling pathways of the parasite, and opens new perspectives into understanding the molecular components of TNF-alpha response as well as into possibly interfering with parasite-host interaction.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
Introduction
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 77 96 77 96 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Schistosoma mansoni</italic>
###xml 350 353 350 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-WHOGeneve1">[1]</xref>
###xml 77 96 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">Schistosoma mansoni</span>
###xml 194 200 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">people</span>
Schistosomiasis is a public health problem in many developing countries, and Schistosoma mansoni is one of the most widespread species of the causative trematode parasite, affecting 200 million people in the world. Parasite eggs laid in the hepatic portal vasculature are the principal cause of morbidity, and the resulting pathology may prove fatal [1].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 351 354 351 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Loverde1">[2]</xref>
###xml 645 648 634 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Pfeffer1">[3]</xref>
###xml 649 652 638 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-He1">[4]</xref>
###xml 91 96 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 390 395 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The complex parasite life cycle in the vertebrate host begins when cercariae penetrate the human host skin, transforms into schistosomula and begin their life as endoparasites. To continue their development schistosomula take advantage of signals from the host; co-evolution has allowed the schistosome parasite to sense and respond to host mediators [2]. One of the important responses of human host at the site of cercariae penetration in the skin is TNF-alpha (Tumor necrosis factor alpha) cytokine production in the early steps of the inflammatory process, along with the synthesis of other cytokines such as IL-1, IL-8, TGF-beta, IFN-gamma [3],[4].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 162 165 159 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Loverde1">[2]</xref>
###xml 166 169 163 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Wolowczuk1">[5]</xref>
###xml 199 209 192 202 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 293 296 286 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Amiri1">[6]</xref>
###xml 297 300 290 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Cheever1">[8]</xref>
###xml 308 313 301 306 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 391 398 380 387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 398 401 387 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Amiri1">[6]</xref>
###xml 425 430 414 419 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 430 433 419 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Cheever1">[8]</xref>
###xml 668 671 653 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Cheever1">[8]</xref>
###xml 806 809 787 790 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Cheever1">[8]</xref>
###xml 830 835 811 816 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 851 861 832 842 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 969 972 946 949 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Haseeb1">[7]</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 199 209 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 534 538 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 663 667 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 794 799 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 851 861 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
Human cytokines such as IL-7 and TGF-beta have been described as host signals that interfere with the metabolism, gene expression and development of schistosomes [2],[5]. The effects of TNF-alpha on S. mansoni metabolism, development and egg-laying process have been described a long time ago [6]-[8]; Amiri et al reported that TNF-alpha induces liver granulomas and egg-laying of parasites in vivo[6]; additionally, Cheever et al[8] observed that egg laying and fecundity were delayed when SCID immuno deficient schistosome-infected mice were studied, suggesting the possibility that the delay in fecundity is due to a delay in tissue produced TNF-alpha in SCID mice [8]; nevertheless, their data provided little evidence that TNF-alpha alone can reconstitute early fecundity in this infected-mouse model [8]. Conversely, Haseeb et al showed that in S. mansoni females the egg-laying process is decreased and tyrosine up-take is increased in the presence of TNF-alpha [7]. There is controversial evidence of TNF-alpha influence on the parasite's metabolism, and the molecular mechanisms of its action have never been explored.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 218 221 218 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Locksley1">[9]</xref>
###xml 704 707 704 707 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Locksley1">[9]</xref>
###xml 708 712 708 712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Bodmer1">[11]</xref>
###xml 1198 1202 1198 1202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Chan1">[12]</xref>
###xml 1284 1294 1284 1294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 211 217 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 1284 1294 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
The TNF receptor (TNFR) superfamily comprises membrane bound or soluble receptors which interact with one or more specific ligands. Currently more than 40 members of the TNFR superfamily have been identified in humans [9]. The TNF-like receptors are transmembrane proteins characterized by extra-cellular Cysteine-Rich domains (CRD) that are the hallmark of the TNFR superfamily. These pseudo-repeats are defined by intrachain disulphides, typically having highly conserved cysteine residues within the receptor chains. Significant variation in the number of CRDs exists among the receptor family members, with the most common structure bearing four CRDs and other members having from one up to six CRDs [9]-[11]. TNFR superfamily can be divided into three groups: (i) the death receptors, which mediate cell death through their cytoplasmic Death Domain, with TNFR1 as the typical member; (ii) the non-death receptors, which signal mostly through one or more of the TNF receptor-associated factors (TRAFs), TNFR2 being the typical member; and (iii) decoy receptors which bind TNF-related apoptosis-inducing ligands but may prevent ligand-mediated apoptosis through a number of different mechanisms [12]. Until this moment, no member of the TNFR superfamily has ever been described in S. mansoni.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 235 245 235 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 250 262 250 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. japonicum</italic>
###xml 408 412 408 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-VerjovskiAlmeida1">[13]</xref>
###xml 413 417 413 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Hu1">[14]</xref>
###xml 462 472 462 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 493 497 493 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Wilson1">[15]</xref>
###xml 498 502 498 502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Haas1">[16]</xref>
###xml 769 773 769 773 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Gobert1">[17]</xref>
###xml 774 778 774 778 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Jolly1">[20]</xref>
###xml 821 825 821 825 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Fitzpatrick1">[21]</xref>
###xml 826 830 826 830 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Waisberg2">[25]</xref>
###xml 235 245 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 250 262 <span type="species:ncbi:6182">S. japonicum</span>
###xml 462 472 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
Recent advances on schistosome genomics have led to considerable progress in understanding of the complex molecular mechanisms controlling the life cycle of this helminth parasite. Publication of large-scale sequence databases of both S. mansoni and S. japonicum transcriptomes in 2003 provided the first large repository of schistosome genes and brought insights into several aspects of schistosome biology [13],[14]. A large-scale joint effort to sequence the S. mansoni genome is under way [15],[16] and a draft of the assembled sequences is accessible at the project website. One of the post-genomic approaches that have been explored in schistosomes is the use of microarrays to perform large-scale studies of gene expression at different stages of the life cycle [17]-[20] and gender associated expression profiles [21]-[25]. However, no studies have looked at the effect of hormones or cytokines on large-scale gene expression in the parasite.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 140 150 132 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 242 252 230 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 367 375 351 359 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 47 52 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 140 150 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 192 197 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 242 252 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
In the present work we describe the homolog to human TNF-alpha receptor and the possible TNF-alpha downstream signaling pathway elements in S. mansoni; we also evaluate the in vitro effect of human TNF-alpha on the gene expression profile of S. mansoni adult parasites along with the effect of TNF-alpha on schistosomula just 3 hours after cercariae-to-schistosomula in vitro transformation.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Materials and Methods
###end title 18
###begin title 19
###xml 17 19 17 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
Amplification of SmTNFR transcript by PCR and RACE experiments
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 823 831 785 793 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s003">Table S1</xref>
###xml 823 831 785 793 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s003">Table S1</xref></bold>
###xml 833 839 795 801 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">part A</bold>
Total RNA (1 microg) was extracted from BH strain adult worms using Trizol reagent (Invitrogen, Life Technologies Inc., Carlsbad, CA, USA) and then treated with DNAse I (QIAGEN, Hilden, Germany). Total RNA was used for reverse transcription with SuperScript III First Strand Synthesis SuperMix (Invitrogen), using 50 ng of random hexamers according to manufacturer's instructions. The PCR step was performed with Advantage II polymerase (Clontech, Mountain View, CA, USA) with buffer supplied by the manufacturer, 2 microl of reverse transcription reaction and 200 nM of each primer using the following program: 95degreesC (1min); 40 cycles of 95degreesC (30 s), 49 to 57degreesC (30 s), depending on the primer pair combination used, and 68degreesC (5min); final extension of 68degreesC (5min). Primers used are listed in Table S1, part A.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 296 303 296 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 436 440 436 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Ewing1">[26]</xref>
###xml 456 458 456 458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 296 303 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Rapid Amplification of cDNA Ends (RACE) 3'kit (Invitrogen) and RACE 5'kit (Invitrogen) were used according to manufacturer's instructions. The PCR step was performed as described above. Products from all PCR experiments were cloned into pGem-T vector (Promega, Madison, WI, USA) transformed into E. coli and stocked in TB-ampicillin. Selected clones were sequenced and the sequences were analyzed and assembled using PhredPhrap program [26]. The resulting SmTNFR sequence has been deposited in GenBank with accession number GQ222226.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Real-time RT-PCR experiments
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 1249 1257 1209 1217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s003">Table S1</xref>
###xml 1249 1265 1209 1225 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s003">Table S1</xref>, part A</bold>
###xml 1318 1322 1278 1282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Livak1">[27]</xref>
###xml 1462 1466 1422 1426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Dawson1">[28]</xref>
Total RNA (3 microg) from each sample was treated with DNAse I (QIAGEN) and purified using RNeasy Mini kit (QIAGEN) according to the manufacturer's instructions. 1.5 microg of total RNA were used as template to perform reverse transcription with random hexamer primers, 200 units of SuperScript III (Invitrogen) reverse transcriptase for 10 min at 25degreesC, 50 min at 50degreesC followed by 5 min at 85degreesC. 1 microl of Rnase H was added to the reaction and incubated for 20 min at 37degreesC. A parallel negative control reaction was carried out with addition of all components except reverse transcriptase. Each resulting cDNA sample was assayed by real-time PCR in triplicate reactions using gene-specific primers that were designed with the Primer Express program (V2.0) with default parameters (Applied Biosystems, Life Technologies Inc., Carlsbad, CA, USA). Reactions were carried out with SybrGreen PCR core reagent (Applied Biosystems) for 40 cycles in a volume of 20 microl and according to the manufacturer's instructions using the GenAmp5700 sequence detector (Applied Biosystems). Tubulin was used as internal standard gene in expression measurements among parasite's life cycle stages. Gene specific primers used are described in Table S1, part A. The results were analyzed by comparative CT method [27], and the statistical significance among expression changes in the expression level was calculated using ANOVA followed by Tukey Range Test [28].

###begin title 36
Microarray data analysis
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 301 305 301 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Bolstad1">[39]</xref>
###xml 552 556 552 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Tusher1">[40]</xref>
###xml 648 652 648 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Cheadle1">[41]</xref>
###xml 758 765 758 765 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">q-value</italic>
###xml 1298 1306 1292 1300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">q-value </italic>
Low intensity data points were filtered out according to Feature Extraction software criteria, which essentially determine those points that are significantly below the average background signal of the array. Total intensity data from each experiment were normalized by Quantiles Normalization Method [39], excluding positive and negative external controls. For both experiments (treated schistosomula and treated adult paired worms) Significance Analysis of Microarray (SAM) was used as the statistical test to identify differentially expressed genes [40]. For schistosomula, we performed a z-score transformation of normalized data was performed [41] and SAM two class tests were applied; genes were considered as significantly differentially expressed at q-value </=0.05. For adult worms, we calculated a virtual Log2 ratio between intensities of TNF-alpha Treated/Control, for each gene. With these ratios, we used two different approaches for SAM statistical analyses. SAM one-class approach was used to identify genes with sustained changes in their expression levels along the entire time period of observation (1 and 24 h); SAM two-class approach was used to identify genes with transient changes in their expression levels. In both cases, genes were considered differentially expressed at q-value </=0.05. Hierarchical clustering of selected genes was generated using Spotfire Decision Site software (TIBCO Software Inc., Palo Alto, CA, USA). For a gene that was represented in the array by multiple probes, we picked a single representative probe by selecting the probe with the highest absolute value of the Log2 ratio between intensities of TNF-alpha Treated/Control.

###begin p 48
Curiously, the genomic sequence between coordinates 63139 and 63202, corresponding to a region within exon 7, is composed of 11 copies of a three-base simple sequence repeat (with sequence AAT). Upon aligning the assembled cDNA to genome scaffold Smp_scaff000357 a gap was detected from 63139 to 63150, which in the genome sequence corresponds to 4 out of the 11 AAT repeats. Consequently, we concluded that our cDNA sequence lacks 4 of the 11 AAT repeats, being characteristic of the parasite BH strain used in the laboratory, which is different from the Puerto Rican strain used for genome sequencing (). In addition, cDNA bases 1954 and 1958 at the very 3'-end of our cloned cDNA are the only two bases that disagree with the genomic sequence: in the cDNA they are A's that come from the poly-dT-adaptor reverse primer used for the 3'-RACE and in the genome we found T and G in the respective positions. As discussed later, an internal priming of the message has probably occurred during reverse-transcription at the cDNA synthesis step.

Human TNFR2-activated pathway involves a conserved signaling cascade composed of protein kinases (Figure 4). The human receptor interacts with TRAF2 that will activate MAPKKK, which in turn activates JNKK; the latter activates JNK that will phosphorylate and activate c-JUN transcription factor in the nucleus. Homologs of all these elements were found in S. mansoni (Figure 4). Phosphorylated c-JUN promotes transcription of target genes in the TNF-alpha signaling pathway. We also found a possible ortholog to A20-like protein (A20-L, Traf-Binding Domain-Containing Protein; TRABID), a protein that interacts with TRAF, along with orthologs to c-IAP (cellular inhibitor of apoptosis protein 1/2), and to caspase-3 and caspase-8, which are related to apoptosis in human cells, especially when activated by signaling through TNFR1.

We note that human MAPKKK is the most difficult element in the pathway for which to predict an ortholog in S. mansoni because it refers to two similar kinases, namely MEKK1 (also known as MAPKKK1) and MEKK5 (also known as ASK1 or MAPKKK5), and there are still other very similar MAP kinases [46]. Human MAPKKK5 best hit against S. mansoni gene prediction is Smp_162800.1, annotated as "protein kinase", with E-value = 3x10-156, 36% identity, 56% similarity, and 64% coverage of the human MAPKKK5. The best human hit of Smp_162800.1 against GenBank is MAPKKK15, with E-value = 1x10-160, 39% identity, 59% similarity, and 64% coverage, while MAPKKK5 is only the second best human hit against GenBank. BLAST search of GenBank using contig C911313.1 gives the same MAPKKK15 and MAPKKK5 hits, although with lower scores than for the full-length predicted S. mansoni protein (Table S2). We propose that the MAPKKK homolog found in S. mansoni could activate JNKK because of the overall context of other signaling elements.

###begin title 95
Discussion
###end title 95
###begin p 96
###xml 30 32 30 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 82 92 82 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 205 214 201 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico</italic>
###xml 229 239 225 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 82 92 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 104 109 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 229 239 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
Our results indicate that the SmTNFR identified in the present work is a possible S. mansoni homolog to human TNFR. The possible conserved elements of a complete TNF-alpha signal transduction pathway were in silico identified in S. mansoni. In addition, our microarray results show different sets of target genes that are transcriptionally activated by TNF-alpha signaling in the two different developmental stages studied.
###end p 96
###begin p 97
###xml 0 2 0 2 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 107 117 107 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 167 169 167 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 241 243 241 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 485 488 485 488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Locksley1">[9]</xref>
###xml 107 117 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
SmTNFR identified in the present work is the only detectable member of the TNF-receptor superfamily in the S. mansoni genome. A phylogeny tree analysis suggested that SmTNFR is orthologous to vertebrate TNFRs. Considering the tree topology, SmTNFR represents an ancestral protein that diverged before the formation of TNFR1, TNFR2 and CD40 families in vertebrates, but after the divergence to NGFRs and other TNF-receptors. Interestingly, due to its dissimilar distribution of introns [9] it has been previously hypothesized that the NGFR gene has been formed early in the evolution of TNFR superfamily.
###end p 97

###xml 9 15 9 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>

###xml 1271 1293 1271 1293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Nematostella vectensis</italic>

###xml 474 479 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 660 665 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 799 821 <span type="species:ncbi:7739">Branchiostoma floridae</span>
###xml 891 901 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 937 960 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 1011 1033 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
###xml 1271 1293 <span type="species:ncbi:45351">Nematostella vectensis</span>
###xml 1685 1697 <span type="species:ncbi:45351">N. vectensis</span>
Naismith et al.[49] developed a classification method for TNFR-family extracellular cysteine-reach domains (CRDs) based on the cysteine repeats: each CRD domain contains modules named according to their type (A, B or C module) and the number of disulphide bridges (1, 2 or 3). The four CRD domains of NGFR contain four A1 modules and four B2 modules that are combined in a manner similar to the first two CRD domains of TNFR2 (Figure 8). Regarding the intracellular region, human NGFR and TNFR1have a C-terminal Death-Domain motif, whereas SmTNFR intracellular region lacks the conserved Death-Domain, indicating that SmTNFR is a non-death receptor similar to human TNFR2 (Figure 8). The presence of Death-Domain motif in NGFR orthologs is also observed in invertebrate protein sequences such as in Branchiostoma floridae (XP_002224391.1). The absence of NGFR homolog with a Death-Domain in S. mansoni and in other invertebrates such as Drosophila melanogaster (accession numbers: BAC01264.1 and AAM484141) and Caenorhabditis elegans (accession numbers: AAP82639, AAB36865, NP_001024870 and NP_001024869), but its presence in invertebrates of the Deuterostomia groups suggests that this family of receptors may have been lost in Protostomes. Interestingly, the cnidarian Nematostella vectensis displays a TNFR with a Death-Domain (XP_001629570.1); the available sequence is partial, lacking a portion of the N-terminal extracellular region of the protein containing the TNFR conserved domain (cd00185), which prevented us from adding this sequence to our multiple alignment. However, analysis of the domain fragment suggests that this protein forms a monophyletic group with the other N. vectensis TNFR and is not closely related to any NGFR (data not shown). Overall, data suggests that multiple events of acquisition or loss of Death-Domains by TNFR/NGFR receptors must have occurred during their evolution.

###xml 458 462 458 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Banner1">[51]</xref>
###xml 504 506 504 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 538 547 538 547 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bona fide</italic>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Human NGFR does not interact with bona fide TNFR ligands and its only known interactions are to dimeric ligands of the neurotrophin family [50]. In contrast, the CD40, TNFR1 and TNFR2 receptors included in our phylogenetic analysis are able to bind bona fide trimeric TNF ligands. The protein region chosen for our evolutionary analysis includes the A1-B2-A1 modules previously shown in this family of receptors to be responsible for ligand interaction [49],[51]. Therefore, it is possible to infer that SmTNFR must be capable of binding bona fide TNF ligands.

###xml 82 92 82 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>

###xml 394 396 386 388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 562 572 550 560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 768 776 752 760 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure 7</bold>
###xml 768 776 752 760 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pntd-0000556-g007"><bold>Figure 7</bold></xref>
###xml 953 955 933 935 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 82 92 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 154 164 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 200 205 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 532 537 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 562 572 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 739 744 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We were unable to find any obvious gene homologs encoding TNF-like ligands in the S. mansoni genome. The presence of a single TNF-superfamily receptor in S. mansoni, along with the observed effect of human TNF-alpha on the expression of a very broad range of parasite genes with a stage-specific pattern, is a strong suggestion that host TNF-alpha as well as other TNF-like ligands may bind to SmTNFR and activate a canonical signal transduction pathway in the parasite. This hypothesis is further supported by the observation that human TNF- alpha treatment of S. mansoni adult worms has induced a sustained change in expression of genes that encode proteins belonging to a significantly enriched network that comprises homologs to known human TNF-alpha interactors (Figure 7). In this respect, we postulate that the minimal downstream elements of the TNF-alpha signaling pathway are present in the parasite (see below). Further experiments, involving SmTNFR heterologous expression and direct ligand binding assays are warranted to confirm this hypothesis.
###end p 102
###begin p 103
###xml 32 34 32 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 253 255 253 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 611 619 611 619 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure 1</bold>
###xml 611 619 611 619 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pntd-0000556-g001"><bold>Figure 1</bold></xref>
###xml 782 784 782 784 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
The 5'- and 3'-end sequences of SmTNFR differ from the predicted Smp_168070 gene. The 5'-end sequence was obtained by a combination of 5'-RACE experiment and PCRs with cDNA template and primers based on upstream genomic sequence. This approach revealed SmTNFR first exon that contains the start codon and a signal peptide, typical of this type of membrane proteins. The 3'-end sequence was obtained through 3'-RACE experiments; the resulting sequence is different from gene prediction Smp_168070 because in our cDNA the second to last exon is longer than the predicted and we found that this was the last exon (Figure 1). Therefore, the last exon of Smp_168070 (that matches the same genomic scaffold but in a downstream region: 64497 to 64519) had been wrongly predicted. Probably SmTNFR cDNA 3'-end is longer than we have obtained here, because the poly-dT-Adaptor primer used in the cDNA synthesis step of the 3'-RACE experiment has annealed to an A-rich region of the message. This can be determined by observing the genomic sequence at the segment to which the cDNA 3'-end is aligned. This genomic sequence contains 12 A's out of 14 bases from 63453 to 63467.
###end p 103
###begin p 104
###xml 500 504 500 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Kovtun1">[52]</xref>
###xml 596 614 596 614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Trypanosoma brucei</italic>
###xml 697 701 697 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Barry1">[53]</xref>
###xml 739 749 739 749 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 531 536 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 596 614 <span type="species:ncbi:5691">Trypanosoma brucei</span>
###xml 739 749 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
The eleven copies of the simple sequence repeat (SSR) detected in genome scaffold Smp_scaff000357 were not observed in the cDNA sequence; rather, only 7 repeats were present in the cDNA. The seven AAT repeats occur inside the protein-coding region of the message and will encode asparagine residues in the protein. Strand slippage at trinucleotide repeat sequences and expansion in the number of repeats has been proposed to occur when DNA polymerase encounters difficulty while replicating a repeat [52] and may result in diverse human diseases. In bacteria, fungi and in kinetoplastids such as Trypanosoma brucei the SSR expansion is described as a mechanism that generates functional diversity [53]. The observed difference between the S. mansoni strain sequenced by the genome project and the strain used in this work, with the resulting change in the number of asparagine residues at the intracellular protein domain, could alter the receptor's affinity to downstream signaling elements.
###end p 104
###begin p 105
###xml 48 57 48 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico</italic>
###xml 126 136 122 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 234 242 230 238 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure 4</bold>
###xml 234 242 230 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pntd-0000556-g004"><bold>Figure 4</bold></xref>
###xml 244 248 240 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Baud1">[54]</xref>
###xml 448 458 444 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 460 468 456 464 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure 4</bold>
###xml 460 468 456 464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pntd-0000556-g004"><bold>Figure 4</bold></xref>
###xml 532 534 528 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 581 589 577 585 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure 4</bold>
###xml 581 589 577 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pntd-0000556-g004"><bold>Figure 4</bold></xref>
###xml 641 656 637 652 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D. melonagaster</italic>
###xml 693 697 689 693 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Igaki1">[55]</xref>
###xml 735 745 731 741 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 841 851 837 847 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 126 136 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 448 458 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 735 745 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
An interesting point of the present work is the in silico identification of TNF-alpha signal transduction pathway elements in S. mansoni. We were able to identify nine conserved elements comprising a complete TNFR2-activated pathway (Figure 4) [54]. Noteworthy is the fact that none of the downstream elements that interact with the Death-Domain in the canonical vertebrate TNFR1-activated or Neurotrophin/NGF-activated pathways were identified in S. mansoni (Figure 4). Thus, similarly to the vertebrate TNFR2, it is possible that SmTNFR intracellular region interacts with TRAF (Figure 4). Interestingly, the JNK pathway has been shown in D. melonagaster to be triggered by Eiger TNF ligand [55]; JNK pathway elements are present in S. mansoni, suggesting that Schistosomes may have a response to TNF ligands analogous to that observed in Drosophila. The c-JUN transcription factor (also known as AP1) is likely to be the downstream effector of the pathway, acting on promoter regions of target genes to activate their transcription. Other unknown signaling elements may integrate the signaling pathway, such as different protein kinases and transcription factors. Further experiments are necessary to confirm the postulated interaction of each element with upstream and downstream proteins.
###end p 105
###begin p 106
###xml 39 49 39 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 402 412 394 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 39 49 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 402 412 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
An important point to be considered in S. mansoni TNF-alpha signaling pathway is the presence of Caspases 3 and 8 and C-IAP protein. Classically, caspases 3 and 8 are involved in the apoptosis process induced by TNF-alpha signaling and are inhibited by c-IAP. No direct evidence exists so far in the literature for the apoptosis process in schistosomes. The present findings open a new perspective for S. mansoni biology that warrants further investigation.
###end p 106
###begin p 107
###xml 94 97 90 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-He1">[4]</xref>
###xml 147 149 143 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 424 429 416 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 429 433 421 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Davies1">[56]</xref>
###xml 29 34 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
When cercariae penetrate the human skin, macrophages start secreting TNF-alpha in that tissue [4]. We propose that the highest expression level of SmTNFR observed in cercariae reflects an evolutionary adaptation; during the penetration process, cercariae will be exposed to host TNF-alpha in the skin and this could act to promote parasite survival and development, in a similar manner as described in adult worms by Davies et al[56].
###end p 107
###begin p 108
###xml 125 135 121 131 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 193 197 189 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-VerjovskiAlmeida2">[37]</xref>
###xml 231 241 227 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 63 68 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 125 135 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 231 241 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
Finally, this is the first work that evaluated the effect of a human cytokine such as TNF-alpha on the expression profile of S. mansoni using oligonucleotide arrays comprised of 44000 elements [37], probing the entire set of known S. mansoni gene fragments.
###end p 108
###begin p 109
###xml 313 316 305 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-He1">[4]</xref>
###xml 650 658 638 646 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s006">Table S4</xref>
###xml 650 658 638 646 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s006">Table S4</xref></bold>
###xml 868 876 852 860 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s006">Table S4</xref>
###xml 868 876 852 860 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s006">Table S4</xref></bold>
###xml 1018 1022 998 1002 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Baud1">[54]</xref>
###xml 82 87 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 297 302 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 753 758 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 882 888 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
We reasoned that the exposure of 3-hour old recently transformed schistosomula to human TNF-alpha would somewhat mimic the conditions at the skin during penetration. In fact, about 3-4 h after exposure to the host skin, schistosomula start invading the epidermis where they will have contact with human TNF-alpha [4]. In this respect, it is noteworthy that genes involved in mRNA translation and protein synthesis were found among the enriched GO categories in TNF-alpha treated schistosomula. High metabolic rates are probably present, as the expression of pyruvate dehydrogenase complex and the glucose transporter gene (C800471.1) were increased (Table S4). Two proteins related to cell cycle control were induced in early schistosomula treated with human TNF-alpha: cell division cycle 23 (C800690.1) and cell-cycle and apoptosis regulatory protein 1 (C917134.1) (Table S4). In humans, TNF-alpha signaling resulting from TNFR2 activation is described to induce cell proliferation through anti-apoptotic mechanisms [54].
###end p 109
###begin p 110
###xml 319 327 315 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s006">Table S4</xref>
###xml 319 327 315 323 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s006">Table S4</xref></bold>
###xml 83 88 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
It is noteworthy that a number of receptors were induced in early schistosomula by human TNF-alpha, such as Activin receptor (C811502.1), Olfactory receptor (C903953.1), Retinoic acid receptor RXR (Contig C910708.1), a putative seven-transmembrane receptor (C809780.1) and nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (C812523.1) (Table S4). These TNF-alpha induced receptors may increase parasite sensitivity to host stimuli.
###end p 110
###begin p 111
###xml 131 139 127 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s006">Table S4</xref>
###xml 131 139 127 135 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s006">Table S4</xref></bold>
###xml 179 181 175 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 352 354 344 346 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 114 119 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
A most interesting finding was the up-regulation of TRAF expression (C905513.1) induced in early schistosomula by human TNF-alpha (Table S4). Cercariae have the highest levels of SmTNFR mRNA; cercariae-to-schistosomula early transformed parasites exposed to TNF-alpha apparently signal a positive feed-back regulation of expression of TRAF, a putative SmTNFR partner.
###end p 111
###begin p 112
###xml 575 583 563 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s010">Table S8</xref>
###xml 575 583 563 571 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s010">Table S8</xref></bold>
###xml 690 700 678 688 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 752 756 740 744 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Juhasz1">[57]</xref>
###xml 758 766 746 754 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s009">Table S7</xref>
###xml 758 766 746 754 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s009">Table S7</xref></bold>
###xml 30 35 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The target genes activated by human TNF-alpha in paired adult worms have shown two distinct expression patterns: genes with transient changes (that are induced in 1h and then repressed in 24hs or the opposite pattern) and genes with sustained changes in their expression levels (induced or repressed by TNF-alpha, independently of parasite's exposure time). Among the genes induced in 1 h by TNF-alpha, we highlight those that are categorized in the "RNA binding" and "RNA processing" GO terms; they encode proteins present in ribonucleoprotein complexes, such as Ribosomes (Table S8). AUT1 gene (C918331.1) had a significantly induced expression in 1 h of treatment. AUT 1 is described in Drosophila as essential for development and autophagy process [57] (Table S7).
###end p 112
###begin p 113
###xml 116 124 112 120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s009">Table S7</xref>
###xml 116 124 112 120 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s009">Table S7</xref></bold>
###xml 535 540 527 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 540 543 532 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Amiri1">[6]</xref>
###xml 622 627 610 615 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 627 630 615 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pntd.0000556-Haseeb1">[7]</xref>
###xml 590 595 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Among TNF-alpha target genes with transient expression it is worth mentioning a set of genes related to egg laying (Table S7). Curiously, Egg shell protein (C910370.1) and P40 egg shell protein (C806383.1) had an induced expression at 1 h treatment and were repressed at 24 h. On the contrary, p48 egg shell protein (contig JAP04349.S) and Egg protein (C804676.1) along with gynecophoral canal protein (C810707.1) had an opposite transient expression change, being repressed at 1 h and induced at 24 h treatment with TNF- alpha. Amiri et al[6] reported an increase in egg laying induced by human TNF-alpha, whereas Haseeb et al[7] described a decrease in egg production upon short time exposure to TNF-alpha (1, 3 and 6 h). Our results revealed that there is a complex pattern of regulation of the egg laying related genes upon TNF-alpha treatment that deserves additional characterization.
###end p 113
###begin p 114
###xml 218 227 214 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s012">Table S10</xref>
###xml 218 227 214 223 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pntd.0000556.s012">Table S10</xref></bold>
A particularly interesting finding is the sustained increase of expression in adults (1 h and 24 h treatment) of MAPKKK5 (contig C902728, probe Q2_P09086), a kinase member of the TNF-alpha signal transduction pathway (Table S10). MAPKKK5 kinase protein acts at the initial steps of the signaling process by phosphorylation and activation of other target kinases; the increase of MAPKKK5 expression suggests that as soon as 1 h after TNF-alpha exposure the signaling pathway is activated not only by phosphorylation but also by an increase in the number of MAPKKK5 molecules present in the cell.
###end p 114
###begin p 115
###xml 95 97 95 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sm</italic>
###xml 176 186 172 182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 445 455 433 443 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. mansoni</italic>
###xml 157 162 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 176 186 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
###xml 445 455 <span type="species:ncbi:6183">S. mansoni</span>
In conclusion, the present work reports an important signaling element in schistosomes, namely SmTNFR, thus giving a molecular perspective to the effects of human TNF-alpha on S. mansoni. The possible receptor is described along with all the elements of a conserved TNF-alpha signaling pathway together with a set of probable target genes activated by TNF-alpha in the parasite. The work extends the complexity of signal transduction biology of S. mansoni.
