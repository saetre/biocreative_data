Phenotypic and genetic characterization of a novel phenotype in pigs characterized by juvenile hairlessness and age dependent emphysema
###end article-title 0

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 291 293 288 290 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 387 388 380 381 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 392 393 382 383 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sub>
###xml 400 406 390 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 410 415 400 405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV</italic>
###xml 2 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 131 134 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 324 328 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
A pig phenotype characterized by juvenile hairlessness, thin skin and age dependent lung emphysema has been discovered in a Danish pig herd. The trait shows autosomal co-dominant inheritance with all three genotypes distinguishable. Since the phenotype shows resemblance to the integrin beta6 -/- knockout phenotype seen in mice, the two genes encoding the two subunits of integrin alphavbeta6, i.e. ITGB6 and ITGAV, were considered candidate genes for this trait.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 123 146 123 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">musculi arrectores pili</italic>
###xml 271 277 271 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 280 286 280 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 493 499 493 499 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 503 509 503 509 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 610 616 610 616 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 620 626 620 626 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 751 757 751 757 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 789 795 789 795 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 799 805 799 805 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 1156 1157 1152 1153 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 1161 1163 1154 1156 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 1255 1257 1245 1247 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 12 15 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 668 672 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 1129 1133 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 1316 1320 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
The mutated pig phenotype is characterized by hairlessness until puberty, thin skin with few hair follicles and absence of musculi arrectores pili, and at puberty or later localized areas of emphysema are seen in the lungs. Comparative mapping predicted that the porcine ITGB6 andITGAV orthologs map to SSC15. In an experimental family (n = 113), showing segregation of the trait, the candidate region was confirmed by linkage analysis with four microsatellite markers. Mapping of the porcine ITGB6 and ITGAV in the IMpRH radiation hybrid panel confirmed the comparative mapping information. Sequencing of the ITGB6 and ITGAV coding sequences from affected and normal pigs revealed no evidence of a causative mutation, but alternative splicing of the ITGB6 pre-mRNA was detected. For both ITGB6 and ITGAV quantitative PCR revealed no significant difference in the expression levels in normal and affected animals. In a western blot, ITGB6 was detected in lung protein samples of all three genotypes. This result was supported by flow cytometric analyses which showed comparable reactions of kidney cells from affected and normal pigs with an integrin alphavbeta6 monoclonal antibody. Also, immunohistochemical staining of lung tissue with an integrin beta6 antibody showed immunoreaction in both normal and affected pigs.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 40 42 37 39 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 73 77 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 108 111 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
A phenotype resembling the integrin beta6 -/- knockout phenotype seen in mice has been characterized in the pig. The candidate region on SSC15 has been confirmed by linkage analysis but molecular and functional analyses have excluded that the mutated phenotype is caused by structural mutations in or ablation of any of the two candidate genes.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 408 414 408 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 420 425 420 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6</italic>
###xml 456 457 453 454 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sub>
###xml 705 706 702 703 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 757 758 754 755 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 130 133 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 386 390 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 473 477 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
A phenotype, characterized by juvenile hairlessness, thin skin and age dependent lung emphysema, has been encountered in a Danish pig herd. All piglets showing the trait are descendants of a specific boar and the trait shows autosomal co-dominant inheritance. At the age of six months hair growth is increased but not fully restored. A similar phenotype has been described in knock-out mice deficient of the ITGB6 gene (ITGB6-/-) encoding the integrin beta6-subunit. These mice display juvenile hairlessness mainly on their neck, head and inner thighs, macrophage infiltration of the dermis and lymphocyte infiltration of the conductive airways of the lung. At the age of puberty, hair growth is resumed [1] and later, age dependent lung emphysema appears [2].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 234 235 227 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 246 247 236 237 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sub>
###xml 281 282 267 268 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 318 319 300 301 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 323 325 302 304 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 326 327 305 306 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 339 340 314 315 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 400 401 372 373 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 499 505 471 477 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 568 569 540 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 508 512 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Integrins are a family of cell surface heterodimers, each consisting of an alpha- and a beta-subunit. The integrin molecules are anchored in the cell membrane where they mediate a wide range of cell-cell and cell-matrix interactions [3]. The beta6-subunit pairs only with the alphav-subunit constituting integrin alphavbeta6 [4]. The alphav-subunit, however, pairs with five different beta-subunits [5] and plays a central role with respect to normal embryonic development and survival. Ablation of ITGAV in mice causes malformations and embryonic or postnatal death [6].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 23 24 19 20 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 28 30 21 23 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 129 130 122 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 131 132 124 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 238 239 231 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 240 241 233 234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 259 261 252 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 323 324 316 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
The expression of alphavbeta6 is restricted to epithelia and seems predominant in various developing organs of the embryo/fetus [7,8]. Besides, various patho-biological events induce a neo-expression of this integrin, e.g. tumor genesis [7,9], wound healing [10], and general cases of clinical or subclinical inflammation [7].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 84 89 84 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6</italic>
###xml 107 113 107 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 54 58 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 93 97 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 149 152 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
The resemblance between the phenotype detected in the pigs and the phenotype of the ITGB6-/- mice suggests ITGB6 as a candidate gene for the mutated pig phenotype. However, since the ITGB6 polypeptide dimerizes with ITGAV the gene encoding ITGAV is also considered a possible candidate.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 179 185 179 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 189 195 189 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 26 29 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
In this paper the mutated pig phenotype is characterized macroscopically and histologically. Furthermore, taking the outset in linkage studies, sequencing and functional studies, ITGAV and ITGB6 are evaluated as candidates for the phenotype.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
Phenotype 0-6 months
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 558 559 558 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 284 288 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 357 361 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 517 521 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 541 545 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
Based on the founder boar and his offspring an experimental population (n = 113) was established. A clear difference in amount of hair was observed in the pedigree, i.e. animals could be scored as normal (hh), heterozygous (Hh) and homozygous (HH) according to these observations. Hh pigs displayed hairless areas primarily on the back and rear, whereas HH pigs were almost entirely hairless. The degree of hairlessness varied somewhat within each genotype group. The skin appeared thin and reddish, mainly in the HH pigs but also in the Hh pigs (see Figure 1).
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 0 29 0 29 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Juvenile hairlessness in pigs</bold>
###xml 25 29 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
Juvenile hairlessness in pigs. All three genotypes (HH, Hh and hh) represented in one litter.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 57 61 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
Shoulder and thigh skin samples from nine two months old pigs (three of each genotype) were evaluated histologically. The thickness of the dermis was measured and the hair follicles were counted. No lesions were observed apart from a few small areas with erosions and mild hyperkeratosis, which are normally present in the skin of piglets. However, in skin sections from both shoulder and thigh the dermis was significantly thinner in the HH and Hh groups compared to the hh group (p = 0.0001). There was no significant difference between the HH and Hh groups. The HH group had significantly fewer hairs/hair follicles than the Hh group (p = 0.0001), and the Hh group had significantly fewer hairs/hair follicles than the hh group (p = 0.01).
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 0 24 0 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Musculi arrectores pili </italic>
###xml 409 410 409 410 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
Musculi arrectores pili were totally absent in skin from HH animals and only rarely detected in skin from Hh animals. Another morphologic difference was the tissue surrounding the sweat glands in the skin. In all skin sections these glands were found within the dermis close to the subcutis, but in HH skin they were embedded in connective tissue and in hh skin they were surrounded by fat tissue (see Figure 2).
###end p 19
###begin p 20
###xml 0 23 0 23 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HH and hh skin sections</bold>
###xml 159 183 159 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">musculi arrectores pili </italic>
###xml 68 71 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
HH and hh skin sections. Skin from one HH (left) and one hh (right) pig, both two months old. The HH skin is characterized by a lack of hair/hairfollicles and musculi arrectores pili (arrow), thin dermis and sweat glands (arrowhead) embedded directly in the connective tissue (blue). Trichrome staining, bar = 200 mum.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 67 71 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
Histological examination of lung tissue samples from the same nine pigs showed signs of interstitial pneumonia to a variable extend characterized by thickening of the alveolar walls due to septal infiltrates of mononuclear cells and hyperplasia of type II pneumocytes. Within some of the interstitial infiltrates syncytial cells were seen. Also hyperplasia of the bronchial associated lymphoid tissue (BALT) was present to a variable extend in all animals.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Phenotype of adults
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 349 373 349 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Musculi arrectores pili </italic>
###xml 75 79 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
Around the age of six months, hair growth was resumed but even in adult HH pigs the hair coat was very thin and frizzy. Hh animals grew slightly more hair; however they did not recover normal hair growth. The skin of both HH and Hh adults became very scaly and histological examination of skin sections revealed noticeable hyperkeratotic epidermis. Musculi arrectores pili were still absent and the sweat glands were also surrounded by connective tissue in the skin of adult HH animals.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 241 242 241 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
Lung tissue of the HH animals (n = 5, ages from 8 to 21 months) and older Hh animals (n = 3, ages from 29 to 42 months) also showed interstitial pneumonia as well as areas with alveolar enlargement (localized areas of emphysema) (see Figure 3).
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 0 28 0 28 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Age dependent lung emphysema</bold>
Age dependent lung emphysema. Lung from a HH boar of nine months, emphysema area (left) and normal area (right). Haematoxylin and eosin staining, bar = 200 mum.
###end p 25
###begin p 26
Lung tissues from one yonger adult Hh animal (n = 1, age of 12 months) and the hh animals (n = 4, ages of 8 months) showed normal, diffusely distended alveolar lumina. Some septal enlargement was observed in the lung tissue from the Hh and one hh.
###end p 26
###begin title 27
Candidate region
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 0 6 0 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 10 16 10 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 177 179 177 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 180 182 180 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 230 236 230 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 240 246 240 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 288 290 288 290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 196 201 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
ITGB6 and ITGAV have been shown to be mapped to MMU2 at positions 34 and 46 cM respectively and to the orthologous region on HSAP2 at positions 160.7 and 187.2 Mb respectively [11,12]. Based on a human-porcine comparative map the ITGB6 and ITGAV candidate region was identified on SSC15 [13].
###end p 28
###begin title 29
Radiation hybrid (RH) mapping
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 12 18 12 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 22 28 22 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 61 67 61 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 137 139 137 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 201 203 201 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 206 212 206 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 341 343 341 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 421 427 421 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 431 436 431 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV</italic>
###xml 415 420 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The porcine ITGB6 and ITGAV genes were mapped by RH mapping. ITGB6 was mapped to the interval between the markers CL327053 and CL378417 [14] on SSC15 corresponding to an approximate position of 65 cM [13]. ITGAV was mapped to the interval between the markers CL356064 and CL360872 corresponding to an approximate position of 71 cM on SSC15 [13]. These results are in agreement with the orthologous positions of the human ITGB6 and ITGAV.
###end p 30
###begin title 31
Linkage study
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 233 235 233 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 558 560 558 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 611 612 611 612 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
A linkage study was performed with seven microsatellite markers selected from the candidate region on SSC15. The locus underlying the mutated phenotype was linked to four of the seven microsatellites: S0149, SW1945, KS155 and SW906 [15] with LOD scores from 8.4 to12.1. The mutated locus could be genetically mapped between markers S0149 and SW906 (~25 cM) with high confidence (LOD > 3), however finer mapping with SW1945 and KS155 could not be supported by a LOD score higher than 3. Keeping the linear order of the markers from the published linkage map [15] the result can be illustrated as shown in Figure 4.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 0 23 0 23 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Linkage region on SSC15</bold>
Linkage region on SSC15. The mutated locus has been mapped to a position between the markers S0149 and SW906 on SSC15.
###end p 33
###begin title 34
###xml 0 6 0 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
ITGB6 coding sequence
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 0 6 0 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 185 191 185 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 241 247 241 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 712 714 709 711 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 479 484 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 504 509 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
ITGB6 specific PCR amplification from skin cDNA samples gave either little or no PCR products, whereas the transcript was easily amplified in lung cDNA. PCR products covering the whole ITGB6 transcript were cloned and sequenced. The porcine ITGB6 coding sequence [GenBank: ] consists of 2367 nucleotides covering 15 exons. A short 3'UTR of only 16 nucleotides precedes the polyA tail. The amino acid sequence [Swiss-Prot: ] has a similarity of 98.5 and 95.4% respectively to the human [Swiss-Prot: ] and mouse [Swiss-Prot: ] ITGB6 amino acid sequences. It contains beta6 specific features like the 2 Arg-Gly-Asp (RGD) sequences in the extracellular part and the 11 amino acid extension of the carboxyl terminus [16].
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 38 40 38 40 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 562 568 562 568 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 633 639 633 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 600 604 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
As observed for many other integrins [17] sequence analysis revealed alternative splice forms of the pre-mRNA. The following alternative splicing events were observed: skipping of exon 2 leading to frameshift and generation of a stop codon in exon 3; skipping of exon 6 (in frame); skipping of exon 12 through 15 with the concomitant inclusion of an additional sequence [GenBank:] that does not show homology to any of the splice variants reported in other species. In addition, there was evidence of concomitant skipping of exons 13 and 14. All splice forms of ITGB6 were detected in both hh and HH pigs. Alignment of the HH and hh ITGB6 coding sequences revealed one synonymous SNP but no allelic variants of this SNP showed cosegregation with the phenotype.
###end p 36
###begin title 37
###xml 0 6 0 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
ITGAV coding sequence
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 12 18 12 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 62 68 62 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 126 132 126 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 458 464 458 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 327 332 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 352 357 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The porcine ITGAV coding sequence was generated by sequencing ITGAV specific PCR products amplified in lung cDNA. The porcine ITGAV coding sequence [GenBank: ] consists of 3140 nucleotides and the 3' UTR consists of 831 nucleotides. The amino acid sequence [Swiss-Prot: ] has a similarity of 95.8 and 94.2% respectively to the human [Swiss-Prot: ] and mouse [Swiss-Prot: ] ITGAV amino acid sequences. No SNP's were detected in the alignment of the HH and hh ITGAV sequences.
###end p 38
###begin title 39
Quantitative PCR
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 66 72 66 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 76 82 76 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 138 143 138 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6</italic>
###xml 426 432 426 432 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 496 502 496 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 506 512 506 512 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 537 539 537 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 775 776 775 776 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Real time quantitative PCR (qPCR) was used to study expression of ITGB6 and ITGAV in animals with the hh and HH genotype respectively. In ITGB6, two different target sequences were included: one within exon 3 and one spanning exon 9 to 10. These exons seem to be present in all detected splice variants and the two target sequences provide an opportunity to compare the expression of two different parts of the transcript. In ITGAV the target sequence was spanning exon 1 to 3. The expression of ITGB6 and ITGAV was analyzed using REST [18] and the pair wise fixed reallocation randomisation test included in the programme. All three target sequences showed a slight upregulation in the HH group but the differences did not reach the significance level (p > 0.05) (see Table 1).
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 0 6 0 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 10 16 10 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
ITGB6 and ITGAV qPCR
###end p 41

###begin title 52
Discussion
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 221 226 221 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6</italic>
###xml 636 638 636 638 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 12 15 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 239 243 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 428 432 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 507 511 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 549 567 <span type="species:ncbi:46221">porcine circovirus</span>
###xml 571 574 <span type="species:ncbi:46221?0.06469298245614036">PCV</span>
###xml 630 634 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
The mutated pig phenotype was originally detected in a boar and among his offspring. It is most probably caused by a spontaneous mutation in the founder boar and the corresponding phenotype has resemblance to that of the ITGB6-/- knockout mice. The only not coinciding features are the distribution of the hairlessness and the macrophage and lymphocyte infiltrations observed in the skin and lungs respectively of the knock-out mice. The interstitial pneumonia detected in the lungs of both young and adult pigs is most likely due to infection with porcine circovirus-2 (PCV-2) which is widespread within the Danish population of pigs [19] including our experimental population.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 381 404 381 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">musculi arrectores pili</italic>
###xml 51 55 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
The fact that the hairlessness of the heterozygous pigs is intermediate indicates, that one functional copy of the gene in question is not enough to maintain a normal skin phenotype (or one dysfunctional copy can impair the "normal" pathway). The mutation seems to affect secondary structures of the skin in general. We have observed aplasia not only of hair follicles but also of musculi arrectores pili. Furthermore, the sweat glands are embedded directly in the connective tissue without any surrounding fat tissue. Also, the thickness of the dermis is affected.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 33 37 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 311 315 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 376 380 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
Both heterozygous and homozygous pigs develop age dependent lung emphysema. Our animal material has not been sufficient to provide an "age of onset" of emphysema within the two genotype groups but our data suggest that emphysema appears around the age of puberty in homozygous animals and that the heterozygous pigs maintain a normal lung phenotype longer than the homozygous pigs.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 92 98 92 98 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 201 207 201 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 271 277 271 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 393 399 393 399 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 574 580 574 580 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 636 637 636 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 638 639 638 639 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 640 642 640 642 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 708 711 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
Although the candidate region on SSC15 was confirmed, our results so far have not supported ITGB6 as a candidate gene. First, we did not detect any non-synonymous mutations in the coding region of the ITGB6 gene in the HH animals. A difference in the expression level of ITGB6 in the two genotype groups could have pointed towards a mutation in the regulatory region of this gene. Even if the ITGB6 expression was upregulated in the HH group, the difference was not significant. A reason for the upregulation could be the interstitial pneumonia observed in the HH group, as ITGB6 is known to be upregulated in response to inflammation [2,7,20]. In the hh group we observed interstitial pneumonia in only one pig. The western blot showed an apparently equal expression of the ITGB6 protein in the three groups.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 44 46 40 42 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v </sub>
###xml 55 60 51 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV</italic>
###xml 139 145 135 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 213 215 205 207 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v </sub>
###xml 264 265 253 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 307 313 296 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 350 352 339 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 376 382 365 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 524 530 510 516 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 669 675 655 661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 271 275 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 699 703 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
The porcine gene encoding the integrin alphav subunit (ITGAV) was mapped to a position on SSC15 within the candidate region. A mutation in ITGAV would be expected to have far more severe consequences, as the alphav subunit pairs with five different beta subunits [5]. In mice, for instance, the ablation of ITGAV causes embryonic or postnatal death [21]. Still, a mutation in ITGAV could not be entirely excluded as such a mutation might reside in a region interacting specifically with the beta6 subunit. Sequencing of the ITGAV HH and hh coding sequences, however, did not reveal any potential causative mutation, and also, quantitative PCR results revealed an equal ITGAV expression in HH and hh pigs.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 98 99 94 95 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 103 105 96 98 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 252 256 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 330 333 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
These observations were supported by the flow cytometric demonstration of high levels of the alphavbeta6 receptor on kidney cells from HH animals and immunohistochemical detection of the receptor in HH lung sections. The presence of the receptor in HH pigs was furthermore confirmed by immunocytochemical staining of monolayer HH pig kidney cells (data not shown).
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 91 98 91 98 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al. </italic>
###xml 99 100 99 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 234 239 234 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6</italic>
###xml 334 340 334 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 420 421 416 417 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 425 427 418 420 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 484 485 477 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 486 487 479 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 488 490 481 483 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 499 504 492 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 507 508 500 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 547 548 536 537 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 552 554 538 540 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 557 562 543 548 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6</italic>
###xml 1046 1069 1032 1055 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">musculi arrectores pili</italic>
###xml 61 65 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 243 247 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 566 570 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 804 807 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
The pathogenesis underlying the hairlessness in the knockout mice is so far unknown. Huang et al. [1] observe fewer hair follicles and numerous degenerating hair follicles surrounded by mononuclear cells in the hairless dermis of the ITGB6-/- mice, indicating an inflammatory response. It has been demonstrated that the expression of ITGB6 is upregulated in response to injury and/or inflammation and that integrin alphavbeta6 exerts a modulating effect on the inflammatory response [2,7,20]. Huang et al. [1] argue that the lack of integrin alphavbeta6 in ITGB6-/- mice allows for minor skin irritations to cause an escalated inflammatory response resulting in hairlessness, for instance in the neck skin where the mother lifts her pups. Hair loss due to skin irritation can however, be excluded in the pig since the phenotype is inborn and without any sign of inflammatory response. Interestingly, our more detailed histological characterization of the skin show that hair follicles are present at a low frequency, however, without presence of musculi arrectores pili. This indicates that the mechanism underlying the phenotype has a subtle influence on cell migration/cellular development during embryonal development.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 103 108 103 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6</italic>
###xml 496 502 493 499 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MMP12 </italic>
###xml 503 504 500 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 544 545 537 538 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 549 551 539 541 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 694 695 674 675 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 699 701 676 678 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 795 797 766 768 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 807 814 778 785 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al. </italic>
###xml 815 816 786 787 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 852 858 823 829 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MMP12 </italic>
###xml 872 878 843 849 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 112 116 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 887 891 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 914 918 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In contrast to the hairlessness the pathogenesis regarding the lung emphysema is well described in the ITGB6-/- mice. The condition is caused by an elevated expression of matrix metalloproteinase 12 (Mmp12), a macrophage expressed protease that degrades elastin causing a loss of lung elasticity and enlargement of alveolar airspaces. The elevated Mmp12 level, again, is caused by a lack of active transforming growth factor beta-1 (TGF-beta1), which has a regulatory effect on the expression of MMP12 [2]. It has been shown that integrin alphavbeta6 is central in activating TGF-beta1. TGF-beta1 is secreted in an inactive form bound to a latency associated peptide (LAP), but binding of alphavbeta6 to the LAP part of the LAP-TGF-beta1 complex, allows TGF-beta1 to interact with its receptor [20]. Morris et al. [2] report a 200-fold increase in the MMP12 expression in ITGB6 knockout mice compared to wild-type mice.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 35 41 35 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 45 51 45 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 167 172 167 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6</italic>
###xml 335 336 331 332 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 340 342 333 335 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 434 436 424 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 437 439 427 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 569 571 559 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1162 1163 1148 1149 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 1167 1169 1150 1152 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sub>
###xml 127 131 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
###xml 198 202 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 273 276 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 314 320 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 611 614 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 644 649 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our results have supported neither ITGB6 nor ITGAV as candidate genes for juvenile hairlessness and age dependent emphysema in pigs. However, the resemblance with the ITGB6-/- knockout phenotype in mice is striking and it is obvious to expect the causative mutation in the pig to affect a pathway analogous to the murine integrin alphavbeta6 - TGF-beta1 - Mmp12 - pathway. This pathway is, however, complex and elaborately regulated [22,23] and involves several indispensable key elements in terms of maintaining full functionality. According to the database PigQTLdb [13] the region between S0149 and KS155 on pig chromosome 15 is syntenic to human chromosome 2 in the region from approximately 170 Mb to 220 Mb. According to Ensembl release 49, March 2008, this region comprises 350 protein coding genes many of which are poorly annotated. Thus, it will be necessary to narrow down the candidate region further, before good predictions of candidate genes possibly involved in the biochemical pathway leading to the phenotype in question can be made. It is, however, also important to keep in mind that we can not yet completely rule out that the integrin alphavbeta6 is implicated in the phenotype since for instance a mutation in the promoter region or in a transcription factor/transcription factor target site could influence expression of either of the genes temporally or spatially.
###end p 61
###begin title 62
Conclusion
###end title 62
###begin p 63
###xml 161 185 161 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">musculi arrectores pili </italic>
###xml 464 470 464 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGB6 </italic>
###xml 474 480 474 480 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ITGAV </italic>
###xml 45 48 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 608 612 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
A mutated phenotype has been detected in the pig. The phenotype is characterized by hairlessness until puberty, thin skin with few hair follicles and absence of musculi arrectores pili and development of localized areas of emphysema in the lungs from the age of puberty. Linkage studies have unequivocally pointed out the region harbouring the mutation responsible for the phenotype. However, sequencing and functional studies of the most obvious candidate genes, ITGB6 and ITGAV have so far not supported either of them as the causative gene behind juvenile hairlessness and age dependent lung emphysema in pigs. Future work will therefore be directed at narrowing down the candidate region through linkage analysis in an extended experimental family. We are in the process of breeding additional animals for this purpose. Once the candidate region spans only a few megabases, re-sequencing will be performed in HH and hh animals with the purpose of identifying the mutation explaining the phenotype in question. Further characterization of the mutated gene or locus and the effect of the mutation will provide novel information about the biochemical pathway involved in the development of lung emphysema.
###end p 63
###begin title 64
Methods
###end title 64
###begin title 65
Experimental population and DNA
###end title 65
###begin p 66
###xml 202 204 202 204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 60 64 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
Based on the founder boar an experimental population of 113 pigs was established and an EDTA stabilized blood sample was collected from each animal. DNA was extracted using a salt precipitation method [24].
###end p 66
###begin title 67
Histopathology
###end title 67
###begin p 68
###xml 750 752 749 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 131 135 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
From a two months old litter representing all three genotypes, three piglets of each phenotype were euthanized. Besides, 13 mature pigs (ages from eight to 42 months) were euthanized. Tissue samples from all lung lobes as well as skin samples from two locations on the lateral part of the left shoulder and thigh, respectively, were collected for histological evaluation. Following fixation for 24 hours in 10% neutral buffered formalin, the tissues were transformed to 70% ethanol for 48 hours. Tissues were processed through graded concentrations of ethanol and xylene, and embedded in paraffin blocks. The procedure of paraffin-embedment ensured that the 4-5 mum thick tissue sections, which were stained with haematoxylin and eosin or trichrome [25], were cut perpendicular to the tissue surface.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 514 518 514 516 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174; </sup>
All tissue sections were examined histologically using an Olympus BX60 microscope. Besides, the thickness of the dermis and epidermis was measured (mm) (18 observations pr. animal) and the number of hairs (encompassing hair roots and hair follicles) was counted (4 counts pr. animal) from randomly obtained standard microphotos of each skin section. A Leica DC500 camera was used for this purpose and for all histological pictures presented. The data were analyzed using the generalized linear procedure (GLM), SAS(R) System version 8.2 and a significance level of p < 0.05.
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 91 95 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pigs</span>
For immunohistochemistry, 3- to 4-mum sections of paraffin-embedded lung tissue from seven pigs representing young and adult animals of the three genotypes (hh, Hh and HH) were mounted on adhesive slides (Superfrost R Plus; Menzel-Glaser, Germany) and kept at 4degreesC until processed.
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 791 792 778 779 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 793 795 780 782 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 37 41 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 47 52 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 340 346 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 851 857 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 1192 1198 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 1204 1208 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 1318 1322 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
For the primary antibody (polyclonal goat anti-mouse integrin alphavbeta6 antibody) to work on porcine lung tissue, immunohistochemical stainings were initially performed with different procedures according to antigen retrieval methods and dilution of the primary antibody: a comparable positive reaction was obtained in sections of normal murine and normal porcine lung tissue when the concentration of primary antibody was 0.02 mg/ml and the antigen retrieval was performed with microwave oven boiling in Tris-EDTA buffer, pH 9 for 2 x 5 min. Before the antigen retrieval, the sections were heated at 70degreesC for 15 min. and then processed through xylene and rehydrated in graded concentrations of ethanol. After antigen retrieval and quenching of endogenous peroxidase activity (0.6% H2O2 for 15 min.) the sections were incubated with 5% normal rabbit serum (#X0902 DakoCytomation Norden A/S, Glostrup, Denmark) in TBS for 30 min. to reduce non-specific positive reaction. The sections were incubated with the primary antibody (#AF2389, R&D Systems Minneapolis, MN, USA) in 5% normal porcine serum in TBS, pH 7.6. for 18 h at 4degreesC, followed by washes in TBS. A secondary antibody (rabbit anti-goat #Z0454 DakoCytomation Norden A/S) was applied followed by wash in TBS. The sections were then incubated with goat peroxidase anti-peroxidase (#B0157 DakoCytomation Norden A/S) and positive reaction was visualized using the chromogenic substrate diaminobenzidine (#4170 Kem-En-Tec Diagnostics A/S, Taastrup, Denmark).
###end p 71

Sections of murine lung tissue served as positive controls for specific staining of integrin beta6. The tissues were screened for reactivity following the optimal antigen retrieval procedure and dilution of the primary antibody.
