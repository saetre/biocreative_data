The Francisella pathogenicity island protein IglA localizes to the bacterial cytoplasm and is needed for intracellular growth

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 0 23 0 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Francisella tularensis </italic>
###xml 123 135 123 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 157 171 157 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis </italic>
###xml 303 311 303 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglABCD </italic>
###xml 333 345 333 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Francisella </italic>
###xml 0 22 <span type="species:ncbi:263">Francisella tularensis</span>
###xml 123 134 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 157 170 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 197 203 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 235 239 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Francisella tularensis is a gram negative, facultative intracellular bacterium that is the etiological agent of tularemia. F. novicida is closely related to F. tularensis but has low virulence for humans while being highly virulent in mice. IglA is a 21 kDa protein encoded by a gene that is part of an iglABCD operon located on the Francisella pathogenicity island (FPI).
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 506 511 506 511 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglB </italic>
###xml 577 588 577 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida</italic>
###xml 695 706 695 706 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida</italic>
###xml 720 725 720 725 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 802 809 802 809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in cis </italic>
###xml 577 588 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 695 706 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 769 774 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Bioinformatics analysis of the FPI suggests that IglA and IglB are components of a newly described type VI secretion system. In this study, we showed that IglA regulation is controlled by the global regulators MglA and MglB. During intracellular growth IglA production reaches a maximum at about 10 hours post infection. Biochemical fractionation showed that IglA is a soluble cytoplasmic protein and immunoprecipitation experiments demonstrate that it interacts with the downstream-encoded IglB. When the iglB gene was disrupted IglA could not be detected in cell extracts of F. novicida, although IglC could be detected. We further demonstrated that IglA is needed for intracellular growth of F. novicida. A non-polar iglA deletion mutant was defective for growth in mouse macrophage-like cells, and in cis complementation largely restored the wild type macrophage growth phenotype.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
The results of this study demonstrate that IglA and IglB are interacting cytoplasmic proteins that are required for intramacrophage growth. The significance of the interaction may be to secrete effector molecules that affect host cell processes.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 0 23 0 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Francisella tularensis </italic>
###xml 132 146 132 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis </italic>
###xml 364 365 364 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 498 512 498 512 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis </italic>
###xml 616 628 616 628 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 666 679 666 679 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis</italic>
###xml 0 22 <span type="species:ncbi:263">Francisella tularensis</span>
###xml 132 145 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 333 340 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbits</span>
###xml 498 511 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 616 627 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 666 679 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 741 745 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 771 777 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Francisella tularensis is the etiological agent of the severe, febrile disease tularemia. Although there have been rare isolates of F. tularensis in Australia, tularemia is mainly a disease of the Northern hemisphere that is spread by blood-sucking mosquitoes, flies, and ticks or acquired from contact with infected animals such as rabbits, rodents, and beavers [1]. Occasionally, local outbreaks of tularemia are associated with contact or consumption of contaminated natural water. In addition, F. tularensis is potentially a threat as a bioterrorist agent due to its high infectivity and lethality when inhaled. F. novicida is highly related at the DNA level to F. tularensis, and serves as a model organism since it is very virulent in mice while being avirulent in humans.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 0 14 0 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis </italic>
###xml 122 123 122 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 266 289 266 289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Legionella pneumophila </italic>
###xml 444 445 444 445 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 446 447 446 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 450 470 450 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella enterica </italic>
###xml 576 577 576 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 578 579 578 579 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 630 631 630 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 687 688 687 688 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 730 752 730 752 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Listeria monocytogenes</italic>
###xml 837 838 837 838 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 841 855 841 855 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis </italic>
###xml 962 976 962 976 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis </italic>
###xml 1031 1032 1031 1032 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 1033 1034 1033 1034 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1035 1037 1035 1037 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 1059 1064 1059 1064 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglC </italic>
###xml 1083 1097 1083 1097 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis </italic>
###xml 0 13 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 266 288 <span type="species:ncbi:446">Legionella pneumophila</span>
###xml 450 469 <span type="species:ncbi:28901">Salmonella enterica</span>
###xml 730 752 <span type="species:ncbi:1639">Listeria monocytogenes</span>
###xml 841 854 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 962 975 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 1083 1096 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
F. tularensis is a gram-negative, facultative intracellular bacterium capable of survival and replication in macrophages [2]. A common virulence strategy of intracellular pathogens is to favorably modulate the intracellular milieu of hosts for their own benefit. In Legionella pneumophila a type IV secretion system (T4SS) delivers effectors that allow the pathogen to replicate in ribosome-studded phagosomes that fail to fuse with lysosomes [3,4]. Salmonella enterica relies on a pathogenicity island-encoded type III secretion system (TTSS) to modify phagosome biogenesis [5,6], including inhibition of phago-lysosomal fusion [7] and the NADPH oxidase-mediated killing by host cells [5]. Other intracellular pathogens, such as Listeria monocytogenes, degrade the phagosomal membrane and escape into the cytoplasm to replicate freely [8]. F. tularensis initially resides in a phagosome which accumulates some late endosome markers. After about four hours most F. tularensis cells escape the phagosome and grow in the cytoplasm. [2,9-11]. Although an intact iglC gene is needed for F. tularensis to escape phagosomes, the role of IglC is unknown.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 24 36 24 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Francisella </italic>
###xml 139 146 139 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglABCD</italic>
###xml 185 187 185 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 248 250 248 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 318 331 318 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis</italic>
###xml 353 370 353 370 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 493 495 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 512 517 512 517 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mglA </italic>
###xml 520 525 520 525 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mglB </italic>
###xml 588 590 588 590 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 725 727 725 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 728 730 728 730 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 787 792 787 792 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglC </italic>
###xml 796 801 796 801 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mglA </italic>
###xml 871 873 871 873 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 874 876 874 876 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 891 896 891 896 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 1107 1112 1107 1112 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglC </italic>
###xml 1113 1115 1113 1115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 318 331 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 353 369 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
We recently described a Francisella pathogenicity island (FPI) harboring several genes necessary for intracellular growth. Four FPI genes, iglABCD, are organized in an apparent operon [12]. The production of IglC mRNA is in part dependent on MglA [13] which is thought to be a global regulator of virulence factors in F. tularensis. By analogy with its Escherichia coli homologue, SspA, MglA likely interacts with RNA polymerase to directly or indirectly alter transcription of several genes [14]. Disruption of mglA or mglB results in mutants that are severely attenuated for virulence [15]. IglC has been shown to be induced about four-fold during intracellular growth relative to broth growth and necessary for virulence [16-18], and it was recently demonstrated that inactivation of iglC and mglA result in mutants that remain in phagosomes that fuse with lysosomes [19,20]. Although an iglA transposon insertion mutant has been shown to be defective for intracellular growth, it could not be ruled out that the observed phenotype was due to interruption of transcription of downstream genes, including iglC [17].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 22 34 22 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 88 100 88 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 122 134 122 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 189 202 189 202 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis</italic>
###xml 315 327 315 327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 389 403 389 403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis </italic>
###xml 412 413 412 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 414 416 414 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 428 440 428 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 520 532 520 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Francisella </italic>
###xml 22 33 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 88 99 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 122 133 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 189 202 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 315 326 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 337 342 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 389 402 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 428 439 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
In this study, we use F. novicida to investigate the properties of IglA and its role in F. novicida intracellular growth. F. novicida is particularly suited for these studies since, unlike F. tularensis, it contains only one copy of the FPI, and this simplifies the construction of mutants. Further, the biology of F. novicida growth in human macrophages is indistinguishable from that of F. tularensis strains [9,11], and thus F. novicida serves as a valid surrogate for virulent strains when studying basic aspects of Francisella intracellular growth. In this work we supply evidence that IglA is a cytoplasmic protein that interacts with IglB, and is required for intramacrophage growth.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Results
###end title 13
###begin title 14
IglAB homologues in diverse bacteria are organized in a conserved gene cluster
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 14 19 14 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 23 28 23 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglB </italic>
###xml 125 127 125 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 166 171 166 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglC </italic>
###xml 174 178 174 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglD</italic>
###xml 200 261 200 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio cholerae, Salmonella enterica, Rhizobium leguminosarum</italic>
###xml 386 388 386 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 389 391 389 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 515 531 515 531 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio cholerae </italic>
###xml 532 534 532 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 200 215 <span type="species:ncbi:666">Vibrio cholerae</span>
###xml 217 236 <span type="species:ncbi:28901">Salmonella enterica</span>
###xml 238 261 <span type="species:ncbi:384">Rhizobium leguminosarum</span>
###xml 515 530 <span type="species:ncbi:666">Vibrio cholerae</span>
Homologues of iglA and iglB exist in several bacterial species that are either animal or plant pathogens or plant symbionts [12] but there are no known homologues of iglC or iglD. IglAB homologues in Vibrio cholerae, Salmonella enterica, Rhizobium leguminosarum, and other bacteria are found in a cluster of genes encoding proteins known as IcmF-associated homologous proteins (IAHPs) [21-23]. Recently, it was demonstrated that this gene cluster encodes components of a proposed type VI secretion system (T6SS) in Vibrio cholerae [24].
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 290 295 290 295 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 299 303 299 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglB</italic>
###xml 543 545 543 545 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-9</sup>
###xml 685 688 685 688 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-20</sup>
###xml 750 754 750 754 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-102</sup>
###xml 792 798 792 798 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglAB </italic>
###xml 892 898 892 898 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglAB </italic>
###xml 921 925 921 925 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">icmF</italic>
###xml 1120 1125 1120 1125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pdpB </italic>
###xml 1136 1139 1136 1139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">vgr</italic>
###xml 1149 1150 1149 1150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 1208 1211 1208 1211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">vgr</italic>
###xml 1244 1246 1244 1246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 1279 1290 1279 1290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. cholerae</italic>
###xml 1343 1344 1343 1344 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</underline>
###xml 1350 1351 1350 1351 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g</underline>
###xml 1358 1359 1358 1359 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">r</underline>
###xml 1456 1461 1456 1461 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pdpB </italic>
###xml 1584 1590 1584 1590 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglAB </italic>
###xml 1598 1602 1598 1602 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">icmF</italic>
###xml 1614 1619 1614 1619 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pdpB </italic>
###xml 1279 1290 <span type="species:ncbi:666">V. cholerae</span>
In light of the emerging role of IAHP/T6SS in the secretion of proteins we re-examined the ORFs in the FPI to determine if components of a type VI secretion system may be present. Three essential components of a T6SS are a protein with an IcmF-motif and two linked genes that correspond to iglA and iglB. A BLASTP search revealed that an IcmF region was found as part of the C-terminal third of PdpB which aligned with the corresponding regions of proteins belonging to the IcmF conserved orthologous group (COG3523.2 with an E-value of 7 x 10-9). The identification of IglA and IglB as members of COGs is much clearer. IglA has strong identity to members of COG3516 (E-value of 2 x 10-20) and IglB has strong identity with COG3517 (E-value of 2 x 10-102). Remarkably all of the relatives of iglAB are organized in the same order, and are always adjacent to each other on the chromosome. The iglAB genes together with an icmF-containing gene form the core set of genes that suggest the presence of a type VI secretion system. We also found through BLASTP analysis that the deduced product of an ORF 380 bp downstream of pdpB (shown as "vgr" in Fig. 1) shows a weak similarity (E-value 0.15) to the family of vgr-encoded proteins, such as VgrG [24] which is secreted by a T6SS in V. cholerae. Vgr proteins are hydrophilic proteins that contain valine-glycine repeats, and are found in a number of gram negative pathogens. Another ORF, 4587 bp downstream of pdpB show similarity (E-value, 0.0005) to proteins in COG3455 that includes the IAHP-associated protein DotU. The clustering of iglAB and the icmF-containing pdpB gene, together with two other IAHP-associated genes strongly suggests that the FPI carries a type VI secretion system.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
###xml 22 28 22 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mglAB </italic>
IglA expression in an mglAB background
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 43 47 43 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA</italic>
###xml 49 54 49 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglC </italic>
###xml 58 63 58 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglD </italic>
###xml 131 139 131 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglABCD </italic>
###xml 163 165 163 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 244 249 244 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mglA </italic>
###xml 253 258 253 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mglB </italic>
###xml 313 318 313 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mglA </italic>
###xml 332 337 332 337 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mglB </italic>
###xml 428 429 428 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
Previously RT-PCR analysis of the level of iglA, iglC and iglD transcripts revealed a role of MglA in regulating expression of the iglABCD operon mRNA production [13]. We wished to test if IglA protein expression levels are depressed in mutant mglA and mglB backgrounds. Western immunoblot analysis of IglA in an mglA mutant and an mglB background revealed that IglA is not expressed at detectable levels in these strains (Fig. 2).
###end p 18
###begin title 19
IglA expression during intramacrophage growth
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 110 112 110 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 194 196 194 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 247 259 247 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 563 564 563 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 247 258 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
Previous studies provide evidence that MglA expression peaks at about 5 hours after infection of macrophages [25], and that IglC expression is maximal at between 6 and 24 hours after infection [16]. To access the pattern of IglA expression during F. novicida infection of macrophages, we lysed J774 macrophages at various time points after infection with the wild type strain U112 and examined the lysates for IglA using immunoblotting. In our assays IglA was first detectable at 8 hours post-infection, peaked at 10 hours, and showed a decline by 12 hours (Fig. 3). In broth grown cultures IglA appeared to be maximally expressed at the late logarithmic phase of growth.
###end p 20
###begin title 21
IglA is cytoplasmically located
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 171 183 171 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 397 398 397 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 586 598 586 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 882 902 882 902 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella enterica </italic>
###xml 955 957 955 957 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 171 187 <span type="species:ncbi:401614">F. novicida U112</span>
###xml 586 597 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 882 901 <span type="species:ncbi:28901">Salmonella enterica</span>
Knowing the cellular localization of a protein can help lead to a hypothesis as to its biological role. To investigate the subcellular localization of IglA, we fractioned F. novicida U112 into soluble and membrane-associated fractions and determined the amount of IglA in each fraction by immunoblot analysis. The data from this experiment revealed that IglA is exclusively a soluble protein (Fig 4). Although IglA lacks a signal peptide sequence, it could not be ruled out that IglA localizes to the periplasm by a novel mechanism. Therefore, we isolated the periplasmic contents from F. novicida and determined by immunoblotting that IglA does not localize to this compartment. We also failed to detect IglA in culture supernatant (data not shown). The data from these experiments strongly suggest that IglA is a cytoplasmic protein. In agreement with this, the IglA homologue in Salmonella enterica has been predicted to be localized to the cytoplasm [23].
###end p 22
###begin title 23
IglA interacts with IglB in vivo
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 47 59 47 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5A</xref>
###xml 529 541 529 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 547 549 547 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5B</xref>
###xml 716 721 716 721 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 930 941 930 941 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida</italic>
###xml 47 58 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 529 540 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 930 941 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
To investigate interactions of IglA with other F. novicida proteins we performed immunoprecipitations with anti-IglA antibody on soluble proteins. A co-precipitating protein with a relative molecular mass of approximately 60 was detected (Fig 5A). This protein band was excised and subjected to MALDI-TOF analysis, and the resulting peptide fragment masses were submitted to searches against predicted peptide fragments of prokaryotes in the MASCOT data bank. This analysis revealed that the only significant match was IglB from F. novicida (Fig. 5B). The relative molecular mass of the co-precipitated protein is consistent with this result as IglB is predicted to be 58 kDa. Immunoprecipitations performed with an iglA null strain did not result in the appearance of the 60 kDa band, nor did immunoprecipitations of U112 done with pre-immune serum. These results strongly suggest that IglA and IglB interact in the cytoplasm of F. novicida.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 148 149 148 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
Supporting the hypothesis that IglA interacts with IglB is the finding that IglB mutants but not IglC mutants lack detectable IglA (see below, Fig. 8). Presumably a lack of association of IglA with IglB makes the former susceptible to degradation.
###end p 25
###begin title 26
###xml 24 29 24 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
Deletion mutagenesis of iglA and complementation of the mutant strain
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 3 8 3 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 98 100 98 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6A</xref>
###xml 153 158 153 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 185 190 185 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglB </italic>
###xml 306 311 306 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sacB </italic>
###xml 378 390 378 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 567 572 567 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sacB </italic>
###xml 803 808 803 808 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sacB </italic>
###xml 866 871 866 871 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 884 886 884 886 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6B</xref>
###xml 933 938 929 934 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 962 967 958 963 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 974 988 970 984 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis </italic>
###xml 1004 1006 1000 1002 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 1077 1088 1073 1084 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida</italic>
###xml 1100 1107 1096 1103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in cis </italic>
###xml 1154 1159 1150 1155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 1244 1246 1240 1242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7A</xref>
###xml 1251 1253 1247 1249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7B</xref>
###xml 1260 1265 1256 1261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 1350 1351 1346 1347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
###xml 1372 1377 1364 1369 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 1439 1446 1431 1438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In cis </italic>
###xml 1474 1479 1462 1467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 1572 1577 1560 1565 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglB </italic>
###xml 1915 1920 1903 1908 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglC </italic>
###xml 1979 1980 1967 1968 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
###xml 378 389 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 974 987 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 1077 1088 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
An iglA deletion mutant, ODB2, was constructed using a two-step integration-excision method (Fig. 6A). First, the PCR-amplified 1.5 kbp regions flanking iglA were joined so as to leave iglB intact, including its ribosome binding region. This recombinant construct was ligated to an erythromycin resistance-sacB cassette and the ligation mixture was used to chemically transform F. novicida JL0 to erythromycin resistance. The JL0 strain is a derivative of U112 that has a deletion in one of its putative sucrose hydrolase genes, and is thus sensitive to sucrose when sacB is expressed. This strain behaves like wild type in our virulence assays (data not shown). An erythromycin resistant colony was grown and plated on agar media containing 10% sucrose which acts as a counter selective marker for the sacB gene. Sucrose sensitive strains were examined for loss of iglA by PCR (Fig. 6B). Attempts to genetically complement the DeltaiglA strain by incorporating iglA into a F. tularensis plasmid pFNLT1 [26] failed, presumably because the over-expression of IglA was lethal to F. novicida. Hence, an in cis complementation approach was devised, allowing iglA to be incorporated into the chromosome linked to a kanamycin resistance marker (Fig. 7A and 7B). The iglA deletion strain failed to produce IglA as determined by Western immunoblotting (Fig. 8). However, the DeltaiglA strain retained expression of IglC at parental strain levels. In cis complementation of the DeltaiglA strain resulted in a strain that regained partial expression of IglA. An insertion mutant of iglB gave a reduction in the amount of IglC that was made, and this is not surprising since many insertion mutation decrease the expression of downstream genes. Surprisingly, this same mutant lacked expression of IglA, suggesting that the co-expression of IglB is needed for expression of IglA or to prevent degradation of IglA. Disruption of iglC however, does not affect the amount of IglA detected (Fig. 8).
###end p 27
###begin title 28
IglA is required for growth in the J774 macrophage cell line
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 54 66 54 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 379 380 379 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F9">9</xref>
###xml 407 412 403 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 469 474 465 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglC </italic>
###xml 506 511 498 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 755 760 743 748 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 54 65 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
Previous work has suggested that IglA is required for F. novicida intramacrophage growth and virulence; however, its role has never been unequivocally demonstrated. In order to assess the requirement for IglA expression in intramacrophage growth we used our defined deletion and complemented strains to infect a culture of the J774 macrophage cell line. The data shown in figure 9 illustrates that the DeltaiglA strain is incapable of intramacrophage growth, as is the iglC negative strain, CG62. The DeltaiglA strain that was complemented for IglA production partially regained its ability to grow in macrophages. The residual defect in intracellular growth is not unexpected since we showed that the expression of IglA was not at wild type levels. DeltaiglA replicated as the parental strain in broth (data not shown).
###end p 29
###begin title 30
###xml 46 53 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
The DeltaiglA strain has lowered virulence in chicken embryos
###end title 30

###xml 130 132 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F10">10</xref>
###xml 159 171 155 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 267 272 259 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 356 358 348 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F10">10</xref>
###xml 45 52 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 112 123 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 159 170 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
When the DeltaiglA strain was used to infect chicken embryos it caused low mortality when compared to wild type F. novicida (Fig. 10). The wild type strain of F. novicida caused 100% mortality at day 5 post infection at an infecting dose of 600 CFU, whereas the DeltaiglA strain caused only 14% mortality at day 6 with an infecting dose of 4,500 CFU (Fig. 10) or 50% mortality at day 6 with an infecting dose of 45,000 CFU (data not shown).

###begin title 32
Discussion
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 35 43 35 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglABCD </italic>
###xml 64 78 64 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. tularensis </italic>
###xml 186 193 186 193 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglABCD</italic>
###xml 447 455 447 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglABCD </italic>
###xml 529 537 529 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 772 774 772 774 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 64 77 <span type="species:ncbi:263">F. tularensis</span>
###xml 529 536 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
There is growing evidence that the iglABCD operon is needed for F. tularensis intracellular growth and virulence and that the MglAB proteins are involved in regulating the expression of iglABCD. However, there is very little genetic and corresponding biochemical data demonstrating the roles of MglAB and IglAB and their corresponding homologues in other bacteria. For example, while it is clear that MglA plays a role in regulating the amount of iglABCD transcript it is unclear if the role precisely corresponds to that of the E. coli SspA protein. The data that exists for the functioning of SspA suggest that much of the regulation of stationery phase proteins occurs indirectly via the repression of H-NS, and that some of the effect of SspA is post-transcriptional [14].
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 190 192 190 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 193 195 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 239 245 239 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglAB </italic>
###xml 532 537 532 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglB </italic>
There is also growing evidence that proteins encoded by IAHP clusters, of which IglAB homologues are important components, are involved in secretion of proteins from gram-negative bacteria [24,27]. There are approximately 30 homologues of iglAB and in every case the two genes are adjacent to each other and arranged in the same gene order. In this work we provided biochemical evidence that the IglAB proteins physically associate with each other and are localized to the cytoplasm. The surprising finding that inactivation of the iglB gene results in the disappearance of the IglA protein suggest that the presence of IglB is required for IglA to be stable.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 94 106 94 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 143 145 143 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 367 372 367 372 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 451 458 451 458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In cis </italic>
###xml 486 491 482 487 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 595 602 591 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in cis </italic>
###xml 639 644 635 640 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 797 802 793 798 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 941 946 937 942 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 962 971 958 967 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in trans </italic>
###xml 985 989 981 985 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA</italic>
###xml 1031 1043 1027 1039 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Francisella </italic>
###xml 1102 1113 1098 1109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida</italic>
###xml 94 105 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 418 423 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1102 1113 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
IglA was first identified as a locus that when inactivated by a transposon insertion rendered F. novicida defective for growth in macrophages [17]. However, it could not be ruled out that the effect was due to interruption of transcription of downstream genes. In this report, we provide strong evidence that IglA is necessary for intracellular growth as a non-polar iglA deletion mutant was defective for growth in a mouse macrophage-like cell line. In cis complementation of the DeltaiglA strain restored intramacrophage growth although the growth was slower than in the wild type strain. The in cis complementation strategy created two iglA promoter regions on the chromosome, one on either side of a kanamycin resistance cassette. It is conceivable that this results in aberrant regulation of iglA expression, which could explain why the growth of the complementation strain lags early during infection. We were unable to complement the iglA deletion mutant in trans with pFNLTP1::iglA, a high copy-derivative of an endogenous Francisella plasmid. Presumably, over-expression of IglA was lethal to F. novicida.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 206 218 206 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Francisella </italic>
###xml 367 382 367 382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia pestis</italic>
###xml 453 455 453 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 497 499 497 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 619 642 619 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Legionella pneumophila </italic>
###xml 664 666 664 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 667 669 667 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 678 685 678 685 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al. </italic>
###xml 728 740 728 740 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 741 743 741 743 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 831 843 831 843 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 856 861 852 857 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 1048 1056 1044 1052 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo-</italic>
###xml 1073 1075 1069 1071 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 367 382 <span type="species:ncbi:632">Yersinia pestis</span>
###xml 619 641 <span type="species:ncbi:446">Legionella pneumophila</span>
###xml 728 739 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
###xml 831 842 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
We hypothesize that IglA and IglB are cytoplasmic, chaperone-like proteins that are involved in secretion of virulence factors. Therefore, the biological significance of IglAB interaction may be to secrete Francisella effector molecules. In other pathogens, secretion of virulence proteins often requires interaction between two cytoplasmic proteins. For example, in Yersinia pestis, a complex composed of SycN and YscB function as chaperones for YopN [28], which is secreted to the cell surface [29]. Also, interaction of IcmS and IcmW is required for translocation of effector proteins via the Dot/Icm complex during Legionella pneumophila intracellular growth [30,31]. Hager et al. recently demonstrated protein secretion by F. novicida [32]. We did not observe any difference in secreted peptides between broth-grown wild type F. novicida and the DeltaiglA strain by SDS-PAGE electrophoresis (data not shown). This observation is not surprising given the fact it has been demonstrated that secretion involving IAHPs is a highly regulated or an in vivo-induced process [27].
###end p 36
###begin p 37
In summary, our results suggest that IglA and IglB are interacting cytoplasmic proteins that are required for intramacrophage growth. The significance of the interaction may be to secrete effector molecules that affect host cell processes.
###end p 37
###begin title 38
Conclusion
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 4 16 4 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Francisella </italic>
###xml 168 173 168 173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iglA </italic>
###xml 645 646 645 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F</italic>
###xml 648 656 648 656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">novicida</italic>
###xml 645 656 <span type="species:ncbi:264">F. novicida</span>
The Francisella Pathogenicity Island harbors uncharacterized genes implicated in virulence. By constructing an in-frame deletion mutant we have shown that the FPI gene iglA is needed for intramacrophage growth. Biochemical characterization of IglA strongly suggests that it is a cytoplasmic protein that interacts physically with IglB. In addition, we provide data that show IglA is induced during infection of macrophages. Bioinformatics analysis of the FPI suggests that it is similar to virulence loci that encode a protein secretion apparatus. We propose that IglA and IglB are chaperone-like proteins that are part of a secretion system in F. novicida.
###end p 39

###begin title 43
Subcellular fractionation
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 21 33 21 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F. novicida </italic>
###xml 321 323 321 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g </italic>
###xml 495 497 489 491 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g </italic>
###xml 811 813 799 801 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g </italic>
###xml 971 973 953 955 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 1516 1518 1498 1500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 1668 1670 1650 1652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 21 37 <span type="species:ncbi:401614">F. novicida U112</span>
1000 ml of overnight F. novicida U112 culture was harvested and resuspended in 50 ml of cold phosphate buffered saline (PBS). Cells were broken by repeated passage through a French Pressure cell (American Instruments Co, Silver Spring, MD) at 1200 PSI. Unbroken cells were removed by 20 min of centrifugation at 10,000 x g at 4degreesC, and a sample was taken as the total protein fraction. The lysate was subjected to ultracentrifugation (Beckman L8-70, rotor Type 45 Ti) for 1 hr at 100,000 x g at 4degreesC to pellet the membranes. The supernatant (soluble protein fraction) was removed, whereas the membrane pellet was resuspended in 2.5 ml of 1% Sarkosyl (Sigma). The sarkosyl soluble (inner membrane) and the sarkosyl insoluble (outer membrane) were separated by ultracentrifugation for 1 hr at 100,000 x g at 4degreesC in a Beckman TLA-100.3 ultramicrocentrifuge. The activity of the inner membrane-associated enzyme NADH oxidase was determined per mg of protein [33] for each of the fractions as a measure of the relative mixing of the different cell compartments. The soluble fraction contained 3%, the sarkosyl soluble membrane fraction 79% and the sarkosyl insoluble membrane fraction 18% of the NADH oxidase activity. In addition, we found that 90% of IglC was found in the soluble fraction (data not shown) and 10% was in the total membrane fraction. IglC could not be detected in the sarkosyl-soluble or sarkosyl-insoluble membrane fractions. As IglC has previously been shown to be a soluble protein [16], this served as another control of our fractionation experiment. Isolation of periplasmic proteins was performed as described by Nossal and Heppel [34].

Beads and bound proteins were removed by centrifugation and the soluble fraction was incubated with 10 mul rabbit anti-IglA serum or nonspecific antibody for 1 h at RT followed by addition of 75 mul protein-G/Agarose beads and incubation 1 h at RT. Complexes were recovered by centrifugation, 6500 rpm, 3 min, and beads were washed three times with 150 mM NaCl, 10 mM Na2H3PO, pH 7.2. After the final wash, complexes were resuspended in 30 mul SDS-PAGE loading buffer and the sample was boiled for 5 min. Beads were removed by centrifugation and released proteins were separated on a 12% Sodium dodecyl sulphate-polyacrlamide electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) gel. The immunoprecipitated material was examined by immunoblotting with anti-IglA to confirm that IglA was present (data not shown).

Similarity of the FPI to other virulence gene clusters. Homologues of Francisella pathogenicity island proteins IglA and IglB are found on a conserved gene clusters known as IcmF associated homologous proteins (IAHP), which, in some cases encode a proposed type VI secretion system. In the FPI the IcmF motif appears at the C-terminus of PdpB. Downstream of the pdpB gene is an ORF designated "vgr" that encodes a protein with similarity the Vgr family, one of which is secreted by the proteins encoded by an IAHP cluster in Vibrio cholerae. Homologues of the Legionella dotU gene are often associated with IAHP clusters. A very weak similarity to dotU is seen in an ORF that is sixth downstream of pdpB.
