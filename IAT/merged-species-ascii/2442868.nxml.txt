###begin article-title 0
Both Functional LTbeta Receptor and TNF Receptor 2 Are Required for the Development of Experimental Cerebral Malaria
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: RM LR JB BR VQ GG DT NH MF VB PL SS AH. Performed the experiments: BD DT MF VB PL LF AH HB. Analyzed the data: RM KP LR JB BR VQ BD GG DT NH MF VB PL LF SS AH HB. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: RM KP LR JB BD NH LF SS HB. Wrote the paper: KP BR VQ GG.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 76 94 68 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Plasmodium berghei</italic>
###xml 76 94 <span type="species:ncbi:5821">Plasmodium berghei</span>
TNF-related lymphotoxin alpha (LTalpha) is essential for the development of Plasmodium berghei ANKA (PbA)-induced experimental cerebral malaria (ECM). The pathway involved has been attributed to TNFR2. Here we show a second arm of LTalpha-signaling essential for ECM development through LTbeta-R, receptor of LTalpha1beta2 heterotrimer.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 528 529 508 509 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 533 534 513 514 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 18 22 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 178 182 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 231 235 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 441 445 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 622 626 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 726 730 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
LTbetaR deficient mice did not develop the neurological signs seen in PbA induced ECM but died at three weeks with high parasitaemia and severe anemia like LTalphabeta deficient mice. Resistance of LTalphabeta or LTbetaR deficient mice correlated with unaltered cerebral microcirculation and absence of ischemia, as documented by magnetic resonance imaging and angiography, associated with lack of microvascular obstruction, while wild-type mice developed distinct microvascular pathology. Recruitment and activation of perforin+ CD8+ T cells, and their ICAM-1 expression were clearly attenuated in the brain of resistant mice. An essential contribution of LIGHT, another LTbetaR ligand, could be excluded, as LIGHT deficient mice rapidly succumbed to ECM.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
LTbetaR expressed on radioresistant resident stromal, probably endothelial cells, rather than hematopoietic cells, are essential for the development of ECM, as assessed by hematopoietic reconstitution experiment. Therefore, the data suggest that both functional LTbetaR and TNFR2 signaling are required and non-redundant for the development of microvascular pathology resulting in fatal ECM.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 111 114 111 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Miller1">[1]</xref>
###xml 214 217 214 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Idro1">[2]</xref>
###xml 219 222 219 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Hunt1">[3]</xref>
###xml 308 311 308 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Schofield1">[4]</xref>
###xml 406 409 406 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Grau1">[5]</xref>
###xml 410 413 410 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Kwiatkowski1">[7]</xref>
###xml 542 545 542 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Grau2">[8]</xref>
###xml 640 643 636 639 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Rudin1">[9]</xref>
###xml 804 808 796 800 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Engwerda1">[10]</xref>
###xml 49 57 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 89 110 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
###xml 316 324 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 431 437 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 547 551 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
Cerebral malaria is a frequent cause of death in children and young adults infected with Plasmodium falciparum [1], which is characterized by the sequestration of parasitized erythrocytes in cerebral blood vessels [2], [3]. TNF has been originally implicated in pathogenesis of the neurological complication [4]. In patients with cerebral malaria disease severity has been correlated with TNF serum levels [5]-[7]. In experimental murine malaria the administration of TNF neutralizing antibodies prevented experimental cerebral malaria (ECM) [8]. Mice with a combined inactivation of TNF and LTalpha were to be resistant to ECM development [9]. Recent data suggest that LTalpha rather than TNF is responsible for the development of microvascular damage resulting in the neurological malaria complication [10].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 217 221 210 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Gommerman1">[11]</xref>
###xml 223 227 216 220 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Fu1">[12]</xref>
###xml 427 431 399 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Ware1">[13]</xref>
###xml 433 437 405 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Hehlgans1">[14]</xref>
Lymphotoxin alpha is a member of TNF superfamily and the closest TNF relative. It exists as a soluble homotrimer (LTalpha3) or forms membrane-bound heterotrimers with the membrane associated lymphotoxin beta (LTbeta) [11], [12]. Both homotrimeric TNF and LTalpha3 interact with two receptors, TNFR1 (CD120alpha) and TNFR2 (CD120beta), whereas membrane bound LTalphabeta heterotrimers function through the engagement of LTbetaR [13], [14]. LTbetaR binds not only LTalphabeta heterotrimers but also LIGHT homotrimers, another TNF family member. Both LIGHT and LTalpha3 bind herpes virus entry mediator (HVEM), a fourth member of TNFR superfamily. The role of soluble LTalpha3, of LTalphabeta and LIGHT in pathogenesis is not fully understood.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 121 125 121 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Flynn1">[15]</xref>
###xml 204 208 204 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Stoelcker1">[16]</xref>
###xml 338 342 328 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Futterer1">[17]</xref>
###xml 424 428 414 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Scheu1">[18]</xref>
###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 166 170 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 333 337 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 419 423 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Mice with complete inactivation of TNF or TNFR1 gene are unable to mount a protective response against various pathogens [15], but develop ECM, while TNFR2 deficient mice are resistant to ECM development [16]. In order to assess the role of membrane LTalphabeta heterotrimer signaling we studied ECM development in LTbetaR deficient mice [17] and further extended the investigation to LIGHT, using LIGHT gene deficient mice [18].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 196 200 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Here we report that absence LTbetaR prevented ECM development. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and angiography (MRA) were used to verify the lack of ischemia and microvascular pathology in these mice. Therefore, the data suggest that both functional LTbetaR and TNFR2 axis are required for the development of microvascular pathology resulting in fatal ECM. The disruption of either axis prevents cerebral microvascular disease.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Results
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Disruption of LTbetaR signaling protects from fatal ECM development
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 214 218 206 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Engwerda1">[10]</xref>
###xml 220 224 212 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Stoelcker1">[16]</xref>
###xml 63 81 <span type="species:ncbi:5821">Plasmodium berghei</span>
###xml 109 113 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
LTalpha and TNFR2 signaling have been shown to be critical for Plasmodium berghei ANKA (PbA) induced ECM, as mice deficient for either LTalpha or TNFR2 are resistant to cerebral malaria, while TNFR1 is dispensable [10], [16]. Since LTalpha exists either as soluble homotrimer or heterotrimer in association with membrane bound LTbeta, we asked whether signaling of membrane LTalphabeta through LTbetaR may be involved in ECM development.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 366 375 363 372 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure 1A</italic>

###xml 625 637 611 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure 1B, C</italic>
###xml 625 637 611 623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002608-g001"><italic>Figure 1B, C</italic></xref>
###xml 746 757 732 743 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure 1D,E</italic>

###xml 810 814 796 800 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Stoelcker1">[16]</xref>
###xml 936 948 922 934 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure 1B, C</italic>
###xml 936 948 922 934 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002608-g001"><italic>Figure 1B, C</italic></xref>
###xml 1176 1188 1155 1167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure 1D, E</italic>
###xml 1176 1188 1155 1167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002608-g001"><italic>Figure 1D, E</italic></xref>
###xml 36 40 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 118 122 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 464 468 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 591 595 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 829 833 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 968 972 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1018 1022 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1170 1174 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We show here that LTbetaR deficient mice did not develop ECM and survived more than 20 days, while C57BL/6J wild type mice developed typical neurological signs of CM, including postural disorders, ataxia, impaired reflexes and loss of grip strength, progressive paralysis and coma, and succumbed within a week after infection with 5-10% of parasitized erythrocytes (Figure 1A). In order to compare directly the requirement for different members of the TNF family, mice deficient for TNF, LTalpha, LTbeta, LIGHT, TNFR1 or TNFR2 were infected with blood stage PbA. LTalpha and TNFR2 deficient mice resisted to ECM development (Figure 1B, C), with no neurological signs up to day 22-25, at which time they succumbed of high parasitaemia and anemia (Figure 1D,E and data not shown), consistent with published data [16]. In contrast, mice deficient for TNF, LIGHT or TNFR1 developed neurological signs and succumbed to ECM within 7-10 days (Figure 1B, C). LTbeta deficient mice were more susceptible than LTalpha deficient mice, although they did not show neurological signs in the first week of infection, and developed slightly higher parasitemia and anemia than wild-type mice (Figure 1D, E). Therefore, our data demonstrate that LTbetaR signaling is necessary for ECM development.
###end p 16

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is a non-invasive tool to investigate neurological disease processes in humans. In the murine experimental cerebral malaria model using MRI allow a semi-quantitative analysis of brain swelling, microvascular pathology due to obstruction of small vessels with parasitized erythrocytes and leukocytes endothelial damage, edema and focal ischemia [10], [16], [19]. Mice deficient for LTbetaR, LTalphabeta-deficient mice used as ECM resistant controls, and wild-type C57BL/6 or TNF-deficient mice used as ECM sensitive controls, were examined by MRI at day 7, when wild-type mice are dying with acute ECM, while LTbetaR and LTalphabeta-deficient mice were all alive without neurological signs.

Since microvascular disease occurring in PbA infected mice presenting with CM is not limited to the brain, we also investigated the pulmonary microvascular beds. While the lungs of wild-type and TNF-deficient mice displayed congested septal capillaries, hemorrhages and interstitial edema, LTalphabeta or LTbetaR deficient mice had no evidence of microvascular pathology, with less erythrocytes in the alveoli (Figure. 3E, G). The total white blood cell counts decreased after 7 days of PbA infection in wild type mice, and this was largely attributed to a decrease in lymphocytes (Figure 5E), while monocyte and neutrophil counts were essentially unaffected (data not shown). LTbetaR deficient mice also experienced a marked decrease in lymphocyte counts (Figure 5F).
###end p 42

Finally, no significant anemia developed in wild-type mice 7 days post-infection, the erythrocyte count was still in the normal range (Figure 5G) as well as the hematocrit (Figure 5I) and hemoglobin concentrations (data not shown). ###begin p 44
###xml 82 86 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 177 181 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In conclusion, the resistance to ECM development of LTalpha and LTbetaR deficient mice was not attributable to decreased parasitaemia or thrombocytopenia, and the ECM resistant mice developed very high parasitaemia and died of severe anemia within 3 weeks.
###end p 44

###end title 45

###xml 861 865 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>

To assess the cellular source of LTbetaR that mediates resistance toward ECM development, we generated bone marrow chimeric mice. Wild-type C57BL/6 and LTbetaR deficient mice were lethally irradiated and engrafted with bone marrow from either C57BL/6 mice or LTbetaR deficient mice [24], [25]. After infection with 106P. berghei ANKA parasitized erythrocytes, wild-type mice reconstituted with LTbetaR deficient bone marrow died as rapidly as wild-type mice reconstituted with wild-type bone marrow (Figure 6A). In contrast, LTbetaR deficient mice reconstituted with wild-type bone marrow displayed protection to ECM comparable to LTbetaR deficient mice reconstituted with LTbetaR deficient bone marrow, and further developed severe fatal anaemia, but no cerebral syndrome. Erythrocyte counts and hematocrit were already decreased on day 7 in LTbetaR deficient mice reconstituted with either LTbetaR deficient or wild-type bone marrow (Figure 6B,C), similar to non reconstituted LTbetaR deficient mice (Figure 5H, J). ###begin title 49
Discussion
###end title 49
###begin p 50
The present data demonstrate that interruption of LTbetaR signaling abrogates the microvascular disease leading to fatal ECM, which sheds a new light on the pathogenesis of cerebral malaria. Indeed, since LTalpha and TNFR2 signaling are also required for ECM development, we propose that both functional TNFR2 and LTbetaR axes are required for the development of microvascular pathology resulting in fatal ECM. The disruption of either axis prevents cerebral microvascular alterations, indicating that the two signaling pathways are non-redundant.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 831 835 776 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Basak1">[26]</xref>
###xml 1038 1042 979 983 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Basak1">[26]</xref>
###xml 1202 1206 1140 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Katakai1">[27]</xref>
Signaling through TNF receptors activate the classic NF-kappaB/RelA pathway mediated by the inhibitors IkappaB-alpha, -beta and -epsilon, leading to the expression of a large number of genes involved in inflammatory responses, cell adhesion or cell proliferation. In addition to the classic NF-kappaB/RelA pathway, LTbetaR signaling activates NF-kappaB through an alternative, non-canonical NIK-IKK1-dependent route that results in the activation of RelB/NF-kappaB2. The alternative pathway NF-kappaB is activated by a limited set of receptors including LTbetaR, CD27, CD40, BAFFR, RANK, but not by TNF receptors. A recent study identified p100 as a fourth IkappaB protein that is required and sufficient for non-canonical NF-kappaB signaling downstream of NIK and IKK1, while it acts also as a signal transducer of NF-kappaB/RelA [26]. P100 was proposed as an integrator, involved in a cross-talk between canonical and non-canonical NF-kappaB inducing signaling that result in qualitative changes in the expression of inflammatory genes [26]. Examples of dual requirement for TNFR and LTbetaR stimulation were described for the in vitro generation of fibroblast reticular cells from lymph node tissue [27].
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 344 348 327 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Scheu1">[18]</xref>
###xml 506 510 476 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Scheu1">[18]</xref>
###xml 27 31 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 71 75 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 238 242 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 287 291 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Although LTalpha deficient mice are resistant to ECM, LTbeta deficient mice were slightly less protected and absence of LIGHT did not prevent ECM. A similar apparent discrepancy, with milder phenotype for either LTbeta or LIGHT deficient mice as compared to LTalpha or LTbetaR deficient mice, was reported previously for lymphoid organogenesis [18]. Evidence for a cooperative role for LIGHT and LTbeta, existence of a weak LTalpha3-LTbetaR interaction or of an additional ligand for LTbetaR were proposed [18]. Here, LIGHT, another LTbetaR ligand appears to be redundant in pathogenesis of ECM to blood stage PbA infection.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 551 555 545 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-vonZurMuhlen1">[28]</xref>
###xml 78 82 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 461 465 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 679 683 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 722 726 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We investigated several pathophysiological aspects of CM in LTbetaR-deficient mice and show that the absence of clinical signs of ECM is associated with normal cerebral perfusion as assessed by MRI. The in vivo imaging data correlated with absence of microvascular disease with accumulation of mononuclear cells, vascular leak, hemorrhage and inflammation in the brain. Further, platelet adherence to cerebrovascular endothelium was absent in LTbetaR-deficient mice (data not shown), which is an important factor in ECM development as reported before [28]. Further pulmonary microvascular damage with alveolitis, plasma and erythrocyte leak in the alveolar space seen in control mice was also reduced in LTbetaR-deficient mice. Therefore, PbA induced microvascular damage in both lungs and brain is LTbetaR signaling dependent.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 88 107 85 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Plasmodium chabaudi</italic>
###xml 107 111 104 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Wunderlich1">[29]</xref>
###xml 383 394 374 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P. chabaudi</italic>
###xml 405 409 396 400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Wunderlich1">[29]</xref>
###xml 69 73 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 88 107 <span type="species:ncbi:5825">Plasmodium chabaudi</span>
###xml 183 187 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 336 340 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 383 394 <span type="species:ncbi:5825">P. chabaudi</span>
A previous report demonstrated a sex dependence of LTbetaR deficient mice infected with Plasmodium chabaudi[29], therefore we investigated whether males and females LTbetaR deficient mice have a differential susceptibility to PbA infection. However, we did not find any difference in susceptibility of male and female LTbetaR deficient mice to PbA infection unlike that reported for P. chabaudi infection [29].
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 445 449 428 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Scheu1">[18]</xref>
###xml 935 939 908 912 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Muller1">[24]</xref>
###xml 984 988 957 961 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Engwerda1">[10]</xref>
###xml 229 233 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 309 313 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 440 444 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 534 538 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 809 813 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 877 881 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Since members of the TNF family are involved in lymphoid organogenesis, we next questioned whether ECM resistance might be related to lymphoid organ structure and function. ECM resistant LTalpha, LTalphabeta or LTbetaR deficient mice lack lymph node (LN) formation, while partially protected LTbeta deficient mice retain mesenteric LNs and in some cases cervical LNs, and intact lymphoid organs develop in the ECM sensitive LIGHT deficient mice [18]. Therefore, although absence of LTbetaR led to a severe defect in LN organogenesis, mice singly deficient for either LTbeta or LIGHT retained mesenteric LN, suggesting a redundancy or partial compensation of the two ligands. Bone marrow reconstitution is classically used to separate the effects on lymphoid organogenesis from the functional defects in adult mice. Wild-type bone marrow reconstitution in TNF/LTalpha deficient mice, that corrects immunodeficiency but not LN formation [24], could not restore their sensitivity to ECM [10]. Although the potential contribution of radiation-resistant brain endothelial cells, microglial cells or astrocytes as sources of LTalpha was proposed, the involvement of immune defects such as LN formation cannot be ruled out. We thus investigated in more details the inflammatory and hematological responses to PbA infections.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 18 22 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 45 49 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 121 125 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 432 436 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 575 579 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
LTbetaR deficient mice, similar to wild-type mice, showed thrombocytopenia and decreased lymphocyte counts at day 7 when mice show signs of incipient CM, together with a slight anemia and slight increase in parasitaemia, that further increased thereafter. Therefore, disruption of LTbetaR signaling while protecting from ECM development, had no strong effect on the typical hematological / inflammatory response to PbA infection in mice. Further, absence of LTalpha or LTbetaR did not interfere with the progression of blood stage infection, and LTalpha or LTbetaR deficient mice succumb within 3 weeks of severe parasitaemia and anaemia.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 93 103 93 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Plasmodium</italic>
###xml 169 173 169 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Hall1">[30]</xref>
###xml 284 288 276 280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Stevenson1">[31]</xref>
###xml 497 501 486 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Pichyangkul1">[32]</xref>
###xml 590 594 579 583 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Coban1">[33]</xref>
###xml 778 782 759 763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Stevenson1">[31]</xref>
###xml 784 788 765 769 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Amani1">[34]</xref>
###xml 881 894 862 875 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P. falciparum</italic>
###xml 1023 1027 1000 1004 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Baratin1">[35]</xref>
###xml 1100 1104 1077 1081 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Baratin1">[35]</xref>
###xml 1303 1307 1280 1284 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Togbe1">[36]</xref>
###xml 411 416 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 421 427 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 574 579 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 754 759 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 881 894 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
Recent investigations demonstrated a potential for innate immune mechanisms directed against Plasmodium parasites to contribute to pathology and protection from malaria [30]. Several cell types such as DC, macrophages, gammadelta T, NK and NKT cells contribute to the innate response [31]. Among them, non lymphoid hematopoietic cells, but also follicular dendritic cells and stroma cells express LTbetaR. Both human and murine DC are activated by blood stage parasites in a TLR9 dependent manner [32], which is possibly mediated by hemozoin derived from infection with the human parasites [33]. NK cell are an important source of IFNgamma and disruption of IFNgamma signaling or NK cell depletion leads to increased parasitaemia and higher mortality in mouse models of malaria [31], [34]. We have recently shown that macrophage derived IL-18 is required for NK cell activation by P. falciparum infected erythrocytes resulting in IFNgamma production, which depends on IL18R/MyD88 signaling on both macrophages and NK cells [35]. However, TLR signaling of NK cells is dispensable for their activation [35]. We extensively investigated the role of TLR and their adaptor proteins, in the control of ECM, but found that TLR signaling does not contribute to the development of ECM induced by blood-stage PbA [36] while others suggested TLR9-MyD88 dependence of ECM development.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 3 4 3 4 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 74 92 74 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Plasmodium berghei</italic>
###xml 173 177 173 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Renia1">[37]</xref>
###xml 182 183 182 183 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 298 302 298 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Chang1">[38]</xref>
###xml 486 487 483 484 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 566 567 560 561 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 782 786 770 774 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Gommerman1">[11]</xref>
###xml 888 889 873 874 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 966 967 951 952 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1096 1097 1078 1079 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1106 1107 1088 1089 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1111 1112 1093 1094 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1224 1225 1199 1200 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1493 1494 1465 1466 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 74 92 <span type="species:ncbi:5821">Plasmodium berghei</span>
###xml 98 103 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 519 523 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 610 614 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1019 1023 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1191 1195 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1537 1541 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
CD8+ T cells were recently shown to be the primary effectors of CM in the Plasmodium berghei ANKA mouse model, although their precise mechanism of action remains unresolved [37]. CD8+ T cells may mediate circulatory shock, vascular permeability changes, and edema in the brain during PbA infection [38]. We therefore investigated whether absence of LTbetaR affects T cell response to PbA induced ECM, and whether the effect is direct. We show a clear reduction in brain-infiltrating CD8+ cells in ECM resistant LTbetaR mice. The low recruitment and activation of CD8+ T cells in the brain of LTbetaR deficient mice may be linked to a defective production of CXCR3 ligands CXCL9, 10, 11, essential for T cell migration to inflamed tissues, that are reduced in the absence of LTbetaR [11]. Absence of LTbetaR seemed to have little incidence on the effector properties of the sequestered CD8+ T cell which expressed perforin, although the overall population of perforin+ CD8 cells was clearly reduced in LTbetaR deficient mice, due to a sharp decrease in CD8+ population recruited in the brain. ICAM+ and CD69+ CD8+ T cells were also largely reduced in the brain of PbA infected LTbR deficient mice. LTalpha/beta expressing CD8+ T cells might participate directly in the microvascular pathology, through an interaction with LTbetaR expressed on endothelial cells, leading to cell activation, expression of adhesion molecules or cytokine secretion. The diminished recruitment and activation of CD8+ T cells in the brain of LTbetaR deficient mice may therefore be responsible for protection from ECM development.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 195 199 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Bone marrow irradiation chimera experiments revealed that LTbetaR expression on radioresistant resident stromal cells is essential for the development of ECM. Reconstitution of LTbetaR deficient mice with wild-type bone marrow did not confer sensitivity to ECM. Therefore, the data suggest that LTbetaR expressed on endothelial cells might be critical, and their absence may prevent the recruitment of CD8 lymphocytes into the brain and microvascular pathology.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 118 122 114 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Flori1">[39]</xref>
###xml 226 230 218 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-McGuire1">[40]</xref>
###xml 231 235 223 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Gimenez1">[43]</xref>
###xml 42 46 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The clinical relevance of the findings in mice are under investigation for gene polymorphism of the TNF/LTalpha locus [39]. Association between TNF/LTalpha polymorphism and increased risk of cerebral malaria has been reported [40]-[43], however more investigations are necessary to fully decipher the molecular bases of the link between disease manifestation and TNF/LTalpha polymorphism.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
In conclusion, the data suggest that both functional LTbetaR and TNFR2 signaling are required and non-redundant for the development of microvascular pathology resulting in fatal ECM. The disruption of either axis prevents cerebral microvascular disease.
###end p 61

Mice deficient for TNF [44], LTalpha [45], LTbeta [46] and LTalphabeta [47], TNFR1 [48], TNFR2 [49], LTbetaR [17] or LIGHT deficient mice [18] were bred in the animal facility of the Transgenose Institute (CNRS, Orleans). ###begin title 65
Experimental malaria infection
###end title 65
###begin p 66
###xml 17 35 17 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Plasmodium berghei</italic>
###xml 106 110 106 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-FrankeFayard1">[50]</xref>
###xml 165 166 165 166 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 212 215 212 215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Rudin1">[9]</xref>
###xml 368 372 368 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Voza1">[22]</xref>
###xml 17 35 <span type="species:ncbi:5821">Plasmodium berghei</span>
###xml 112 116 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 217 221 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 496 500 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
A cloned line of Plasmodium berghei ANKA (PbA) stably transfected with GFP was obtained from Dr. A Waters [50]. Mice were infected by intraperitoneal injection of 106 parasitized erythrocytes as described before [9]. Mice were observed daily for clinical neurological signs of ECM culminating in ataxia, paralysis and coma. Parasitaemia was analyzed by flow cytometry [22] at various time point and hematological parameters were assessed as indicated. Before succumbing to neurological symptoms, mice were killed for histological analysis of the brain, lung and liver.
###end p 66
###begin title 67
Parasitaemia
###end title 67
###begin p 68
###xml 126 144 <span type="species:ncbi:5821">Plasmodium berghei</span>
Parasitaemia was assessed in 2 microl of blood collected from the tail vein at different time points post-infection with EGFP-Plasmodium berghei ANKA. Blood was diluted in 2 ml of PBS containing 0.5% BSA and fluorescent cells analyzed by FACScan flow cytometer LSR (Becton Dickinson, Grenoble, France) using CellQuest software (Becton Dickinson, Grenoble, France).
###end p 68
###begin title 69
Hematology
###end title 69
###begin p 70
Blood was drawn into tubes containing EDTA (Vacutainer; Becton, Grenoble, France) as indicated and hematological parameters determined using 5-part-differential hematology analyzer MS 9.5 (Melet Schloesing Laboratoires, France).
###end p 70
###begin title 71
Histology
###end title 71
###begin p 72
###xml 115 124 115 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P berghei</italic>
###xml 229 232 229 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Rudin1">[9]</xref>
###xml 565 568 561 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Rudin1">[9]</xref>
###xml 31 35 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 139 143 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
For histological analysis, the mice were perfused under anesthesia with saline and the brain, lung, and liver from P berghei ANKA infected mice were fixed in 4% phosphate-buffered-saline formaldehyde for 72 h as described before [9]. Three microm sections were stained with haematoxylin and eosin. CNS microvascular obstruction, erythrocyte accumulation in alveoli, thickening of alveolar septae were quantified using a semi-quantitative score with increasing severity of changes (0-5) by two independent observers including a trained pathologist (BR) as described [9].
###end p 72
###begin title 73
Bone marrow chimera

###xml 127 128 124 125 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 310 314 307 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Muller1">[24]</xref>
###xml 316 320 313 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Ryffel1">[25]</xref>

The reconstituted mice were used at 2 months after bone marrow transplantation [24], [25].

###begin title 75
Assessment of vascular leak
###end title 75
###begin p 76
###xml 392 395 392 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Rudin1">[9]</xref>
###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 136 140 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 158 162 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Mice were injected intravenously (i.v) with 0.2 ml of 1% Evans blue (Sigma-Aldrich) on day 7, shortly before the death of the wild type mice. One hour later, mice were sacrificed and the coloration of brain was assessed. Brain and lung extravasation in formamide (Sigma-Aldrich) was evaluated by absorbance at 610 nm of the tissue extracts as an indicator of increased capillary permeability [9].
###end p 76
###begin title 77
Brain imaging using magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
###end title 77
###begin p 78
MRI experiments were performed in a horizontal 9.4 T/20-ECM Bruker Biospec MR system (Bruker Biospin, Wissembourg, France) equipped with gradients capable of switching 950 mT/m in 50 micros. A 12 elements linear birdcage coil (Bruker Biospin) with inner diameter of 35 mm and length 60 mm has been used to achieve uniform excitation and reception.
###end p 78
###begin p 79
###xml 107 111 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 385 389 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
A custom-built stereotaxic head holder was used to fix the animals into the birdcage coil (see below). The mice were anaesthetized with isoflurane (1.5-2.5%) in an O2/N2O 1:1 mixture applied with a face mask allowing free breathing. Respiration was monitored using a balloon taped to the abdomen and connected to a pressure transducer (SA Instruments, Inc., Stony Brook, NY, USA). The mice body temperature was kept at 37+/-0.5 degreesC throughout the experiment, using warm water circulation
###end p 79
###begin p 80
###xml 162 166 162 166 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">eff.</sub>
###xml 195 196 195 196 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
Preparatory anatomical saggital MR images were performed using a MSME (multi-slices, multi-echoes) sequence, with the following parameters: RARE factor = 8, TR/TEeff. = 4000/46 ms, FOV = 20x20 mm2, matrix = 128x128, 1 slice 1 mm thickness, number of averages (NA) = 1, experimental time = 1 min.
###end p 80
###begin p 81
###xml 76 77 76 77 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 179 183 179 183 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">eff.</sub>
###xml 212 213 212 213 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
Brain lesions and global changes in tissue structure have been accessed by T2 weighted (T2w) MR images using a MSME sequence, with the following parameters: RARE factor = 8, TR/TEeff. = 5000/46 ms, FOV = 17x17 mm2, matrix = 256x192 (zero-filled to 256x256), 37 axial slices 0.5 mm thickness, NA = 8, experimental time = 16 min.
###end p 81
###begin p 82
###xml 174 175 174 175 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
###xml 432 433 428 429 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sup>
Measurements of vascular cerebral blood flow was performed by MR angiography (MRA) using a Fast Low Angle Shot (FLASH) sequence, with the following parameters: FOV = 14x14 mm2, matrix = 128x128, TR/TE = 30/5 ms, 51 axial slices 0.3 mm thickness, NA = 4, experimental time = 13 min. Angiograms were produced by generating maximum intensity projections (MIP) after interpolating raw data to obtain an isotropic resolution (109 microm)3. Image analysis and processing were performed with the public domain software Image J (NIH, ).
###end p 82
###begin title 83
Isolation of brain- sequestered leukocytes
###end title 83
###begin p 84
###xml 208 212 208 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002608-Belnoue1">[20]</xref>
###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
Mice with neurological symptoms of ECM were anaesthetized (at day 7-8) and perfused with 10 ml of PBS/EDTA 0.02% intra-cardiac to remove circulating red blood cells and leukocytes from the brain as described.[20] Brains were removed and homogenized gently (30 sec at 4000 rpm) using the sterile disposable homogenization system Dispomix (Medic Tools AG, 6300-Zug, Switzerland) in RPMI 1640 medium containing 2% FCS. Tissue-cell homogenates were passed through a nylon cell strainer (100 microm; Becton Dickinson Grenoble, France) and centrifuged at 400 g for 10 minutes. The mononuclear cells were then separated over a 30% Percoll gradient (Amersham Biosciences AB, Uppsala, Sweden). Residual red blood cells were lyzed by hypotonic shock in ammonium chloride- potassium lysis buffer.
###end p 84
###begin title 85
Flow cytometry analysis (FACS)
###end title 85
###begin p 86
###xml 66 71 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 304 307 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 313 318 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 411 414 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>

###xml 718 721 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>

Brain-sequestered cells were collected, washed and saturated with mouse serum prior to the staining with fluorescence labelled antibodies for 30 minutes. The cells were then phenotyped by flow cytometry. T cells were identified by their forward side scatter and with the use of the following antibodies: rat anti-mouse CD4 conjugated to fluorescein isothiocyante (FITC) (clone GK1.5; Pharmingen San Diego, CA), rat anti-mouse CD8alpha conjugated to allophycocyanin (APC) (clone 53-6.7, Pharmingen San Diego, CA), rat anti-mouse Perforin conjugated to phycoerytthrin (PE) (clone JAW246, eBioscience San Diego, CA), rat anti-mouse CD69 conjugated to phycoerytthrin (PE) (clone H1,F3, Becton-Dickinson, Grenoble France), rat anti-mouse CD54 conjugated to PE (clone 3E2, Becton-Dickinson, Grenoble France) and isotype-matched control antibodies (Becton-Dickinson, Grenoble France). For each sample, 10000 cells were scored. Data were analyzed using a LSR II-Flow cytometer and CellQuest software (Becton-Dickinson, Grenoble France).

Statistical analysis
###end title 87
###begin p 88
