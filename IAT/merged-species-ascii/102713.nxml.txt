Retrotransposition of the I factor, a non-long terminal repeat retrotransposon of Drosophila, generates tandem repeats at the 3' end
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###begin p 2
###xml 340 341 340 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 352 375 352 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 465 466 465 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 716 717 716 717 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 910 911 910 911 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 1056 1057 1056 1057 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 352 375 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
Non-long terminal repeat (LTR) retrotransposons or LINEs transpose by reverse transcription of an RNA intermediate and are thought to use the 3' hydroxyl of a chromosomal cleavage to initiate synthesis of the first strand of the cDNA. Many of them terminate in a poly(dA) sequence at the 3' end of the coding strand although some, like the I factor of Drosophila melanogaster, have 3' ends formed by repeats of the trinucleotide TAA. We report results showing that I factor transcripts end a few nucleotides downstream of the TAA repeats and that these extra nucleotides are not integrated into chromosomal DNA during retrotransposition. We also show that the TAA repeats are not required for transposition and that I elements containing mutations affecting the TAA sequences generate transposed copies ending with tandem repeats of various types. Our results suggest that during integration the 3' end of the I factor RNA template can pair with nucleotides at the target site and that tandem duplications are generated by the reverse transcriptase of the I factor in a manner that is reminiscent of the activity of the reverse transcriptases of telomerases. Reverse transcriptases of other non-LTR retrotransposons may function in a similar way.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 382 383 382 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c1">1</xref>
###xml 384 385 384 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c2">2</xref>
###xml 414 415 414 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c3">3</xref>
###xml 416 417 416 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c4">4</xref>
###xml 543 545 543 545 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R1</italic>
###xml 550 552 550 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R2</italic>
###xml 610 611 610 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c5">5</xref>
###xml 618 623 618 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HeT-A</italic>
###xml 628 632 628 632 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TART</italic>
###xml 636 659 636 659 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 661 662 661 662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c6">6</xref>
###xml 663 664 663 664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c7">7</xref>
###xml 636 659 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
The mechanism by which the RNA transposition intermediates of non-long terminal repeat (LTR) retrotransposons or LINEs are reverse transcribed is still poorly understood. Non-LTR retrotransposons contain dA-rich sequences at the 3' end of their coding strand which, in many cases, is poly(dA). They usually have two open reading frames (ORFs), the second of which encodes nuclease (1,2) and reverse transcriptase (3,4) activities. Most non-LTR retrotransposons are dispersed in the genome but some insert at specific sites. The latter include R1 and R2 which insert at specific sites in rRNA genes of insects (5), and HeT-A and TART in Drosophila melanogaster (6,7) which insert at chromosome ends and compensate for the absence of standard telomeric repeats.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 148 149 148 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c8">8</xref>
###xml 495 503 495 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 528 532 528 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R2Bm</italic>
###xml 542 543 542 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c9">9</xref>
###xml 544 546 544 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c12">12</xref>
###xml 549 553 549 553 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R2Bm</italic>
###xml 586 597 586 597 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx mori</italic>
###xml 711 715 711 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R2Bm</italic>
###xml 886 888 886 888 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c12">12</xref>
###xml 889 891 889 891 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c13">13</xref>
###xml 586 597 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
Reverse transcription of the RNA transposition intermediate of non-LTR retrotransposons is thought to occur at the site of integration (reviewed in 8). According to current models, the endonuclease encoded by the element makes staggered single strand breaks at the target site and the reverse transcriptase uses the 3' hydroxyl at one of the nicks to prime synthesis of the first DNA strand using the RNA transposition intermediate as template. Some steps of this process have been demonstrated in vitro using the site-specific R2Bm element (9-12). R2Bm is a non-LTR retrotransposon of Bombyx mori with a single ORF which encodes both reverse transcriptase and endonuclease activities. Reverse transcription of R2Bm initiates precisely at the 3' end of the element provided that the RNA template contains additional nucleotides corresponding to DNA downstream of the site of insertion (12,13). The additional nucleotides at the 3' end of the RNA presumably facilitate alignment of the RNA with the site of insertion during reverse transcription.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 170 174 170 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L1Hs</italic>
###xml 399 401 399 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c14">14</xref>
###xml 402 404 402 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c16">16</xref>
###xml 551 552 551 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 563 577 563 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D.melanogaster</italic>
###xml 579 581 579 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c17">17</xref>
###xml 584 588 584 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dong</italic>
###xml 592 598 592 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B.mori</italic>
###xml 600 602 600 602 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c18">18</xref>
###xml 605 608 605 608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LOA</italic>
###xml 612 633 612 633 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila silvestris</italic>
###xml 635 637 635 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c19">19</xref>
###xml 643 644 643 644 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Q</italic>
###xml 648 665 648 665 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anopheles gambiae</italic>
###xml 667 669 667 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c20">20</xref>
###xml 202 207 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 563 577 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D.melanogaster</span>
###xml 592 598 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">B.mori</span>
###xml 612 633 <span type="species:ncbi:47010">Drosophila silvestris</span>
###xml 648 665 <span type="species:ncbi:7165">Anopheles gambiae</span>
Very little is known about the sequences required at the 3' ends of non-LTR retrotransposons that are not sequence specific to allow initiation of reverse transcription. L1Hs elements that occur in the human genome contain a putative polyadenylation signal near their 3' ends and their transcripts contain poly(A) tails that appear to be at least partially reverse transcribed during transposition (14-16). This is not true for all non-LTR retrotransposons, however, since some have other A-rich sequences at their 3' ends like the TAA repeats of the I factor of D.melanogaster (17), Dong of B.mori (18), LOA of Drosophila silvestris (19) and Q of Anopheles gambiae (20).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 0 1 0 1 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 101 115 101 115 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D.melanogaster</italic>
###xml 146 148 146 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c21">21</xref>
###xml 149 151 149 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c22">22</xref>
###xml 183 184 183 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 214 215 214 215 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 410 412 410 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c23">23</xref>
###xml 522 524 522 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c17">17</xref>
###xml 525 527 525 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c24">24</xref>
###xml 535 536 535 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f1">1</xref>
###xml 810 812 810 812 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c25">25</xref>
###xml 101 115 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D.melanogaster</span>
I factors are non-LTR retrotransposons that are not site specific and are present in some strains of D.melanogaster known as inducer (reviewed in 21,22). Strains devoid of functional I factors are called reactive. I factors are stable in inducer strains, but transpose at high frequencies in the germ line of the females, known as SF females, resulting from crosses between reactive females and inducer males (23). They have 4-8 repeats of the trinucleotide TAA at the 3' end of the coding strand which contains two ORFs (17,24) (Fig. 1A). ORF2 putatively encodes nuclease, reverse transcriptase and RNase H activities. A 5.4 kb long RNA containing the complete sequence of the element, including UAA repeats, which is thought to be the transposition intermediate, is synthesized in the ovaries of SF females (25).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 74 75 74 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 260 261 260 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 548 549 548 549 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
In this paper we show that the transposition intermediates of a wild-type I factor extend a few nucleotides beyond the TAA repeats and that these extra nucleotides are not normally integrated into chromosomal DNA during retrotransposition. We have constructed I elements containing mutations affecting the TAA repeats and shown that they are not required for transposition. Transposed copies of these mutated elements have tandem repeats of various types at their 3' ends. These might be produced by an activity of the reverse transcriptase of the I factor related to that of the reverse transcriptases of telomerases.
###end p 8

P-mediated transformation of the reactive strain JA was performed according to Spradling and Rubin (29) using the helper plasmid phs70Delta2-3wc. Flies containing transgenes I97 and I99 were selected on a medium containing neomycin (1 mg/ml) at 23degreesC. Males transformed with pI954Y were [y+] and had a single copy of the full-length I factor. In order to maintain a single copy of I954Y in the genome, transgenic males were crossed with JA reactive females at each generation (28). I factor transposition only occurs in the female germ-line. The structure of all transgenes was checked by Southern blot analyses.

Cloning transposed copies of the I factor
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 110 113 110 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nde</italic>
###xml 119 122 119 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bst</italic>

Inverse PCR experiments were carried out as follows. One microgram of genomic DNA was digested with 4-12 U of NdeII or BstYI overnight at the appropriate temperature. ###begin title 16
RLM-3'-RACE
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 14 15 14 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 149 151 149 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c30">30</xref>
###xml 717 718 711 712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f1">1</xref>
###xml 800 801 794 795 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f1">1</xref>
###xml 991 992 985 986 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
The 3' end of I factor RNA was analyzed by RNA ligase mediated RACE experiments with the following modifications of the method previously described (30). Ten micrograms of total RNA extracted with the RNAble (Eurobio, Les Ulis, France) from 50 flies were incubated for 15 min at room temperature with 10 U of DNase I (Gibco BRL, Cergy, France). Eighty picomoles of the anchor oligonucleotide (5'-CAACGGTGGTATATCCAGTGATTTT-3') were ligated overnight to the RNA with 120 U of RNA ligase (Gibco BRL). The reverse transcription reaction was primed using 40 pmol of anchor primer (5'-AAAATCACTGGATATACCACCGTTG-3') and incubated for 1 h at 37degreesC. Then nested PCR was carried out first using oligonucleotide 6851 (Fig. 1A) and the anchor primer and then second with oligonucleotides 6851, 3 or 1 (Fig. 1A: 5'-GTACATAACAAGCCAGCAATTAG-3') and the anchor primer. PCR products were cloned as described previously. Colony blot screening was performed using a PCR fragment of the last 160 nt of the I factor and labeled with the Megaprime kit (Amersham, Les Ulis, France). Positive clones were sequenced (see above).

###begin title 18
RESULTS
###end title 18
###begin title 19
###xml 19 20 19 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
Transcripts of the I factor terminate within downstream flanking sequences
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 62 63 62 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 107 109 107 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c25">25</xref>
###xml 215 216 215 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 328 330 328 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JA</italic>
###xml 352 356 352 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">21.1</italic>
###xml 358 360 358 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c28">28</xref>
###xml 397 398 397 398 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 407 412 407 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I954Y</italic>
###xml 453 454 453 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f1">1</xref>
###xml 500 501 500 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f1">1</xref>
###xml 593 598 593 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I954Y</italic>
The results of previous experiments indicate that full-length I factor transcripts are not polyadenylated (25). We used RLM-3'-RACE to determine precisely the structure of the 3' end of transcripts from full-length I factors. This was done using RNA extracted from SF females produced by crossing females of the reactive strain JA with males of strain 21.1 (28) which contains a single functional I factor, I954Y, with 5 TAA repeats at its 3' end (Fig. 1B). We observed two types of transcript (Fig. 1B). Both were obtained in two independent experiments. Up to four nucleotides downstream of I954Y were present at the 3' end of the RNAs, suggesting that transcription terminates a few nucleotides downstream of the TAA repeats.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 109 110 109 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 204 209 204 209 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I954Y</italic>
###xml 227 231 227 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">21.1</italic>
###xml 353 357 353 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">21.1</italic>
###xml 368 370 368 370 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JA</italic>
###xml 582 585 582 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bst</italic>
###xml 688 689 688 689 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f1">1</xref>
In order to determine whether or not these additional nucleotides are copied by reverse transcriptase during I factor retrotransposition we sequenced the ends of transposed copies from the single element I954Y contained in the 21.1 transgenic line. These were cloned from DNA extracted from 50 males coming directly from SF females produced by crossing 21.1 males and JA reactive females in such a way that it is very likely that each transposed copy resulted from a single transposition event that occurred in the germ line of the SF females. They were cloned by inverse PCR using BstY1 digested DNA and primers 3 and 5, and sequenced using oligonucleotides 1 and 5 as primers (see Fig. 1A and Materials and Methods).
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 245 247 245 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c31">31</xref>
###xml 319 320 319 320 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 337 338 337 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f1">1</xref>
###xml 576 577 576 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
The sequences of both ends of 14 transposed copies were determined. Two of them were not flanked by target site duplications suggesting that they have been involved in chromosomal rearrangements that frequently occur in the SF female germ line (31). Target site duplications delimit the ends of the other 12 transposed I factors. Figure 1C summarizes the data. The 3' ends of these transposed copies can be assigned to the last nucleotide preceding the target site duplication. None of the insertions contain the extra nucleotides present downstream of the UAA repeats in the I factor transcripts, indicating that these nucleotides are not integrated into chromosomal DNA during retrotransposition.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 21 26 21 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I954Y</italic>
###xml 77 78 77 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f1">1</xref>
Transposed copies of I954Y terminate with 3, 4, 5 or 6 TAA repeats (see Fig. 1C) demonstrating that the number of repeats can vary during retrotransposition.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
###xml 33 34 33 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
The TAA repeats at the 3' end of I elements are not required for transposition
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 75 76 75 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 148 151 148 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I97</italic>
###xml 209 210 209 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f2">2</xref>
###xml 219 222 219 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 264 265 264 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f3">3</xref>
###xml 354 356 354 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JA</italic>
###xml 391 396 391 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">97-52</italic>
###xml 401 404 401 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I97</italic>
###xml 410 415 410 415 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">99-18</italic>
###xml 420 425 420 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">99-59</italic>
###xml 430 433 430 433 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 467 475 467 475 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">en masse</italic>
###xml 530 531 530 531 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 690 693 690 693 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I97</italic>
###xml 698 701 698 701 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 822 823 822 823 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
In order to investigate whether the terminal TAA repeats are essential for I factor transposition we constructed two elements with altered 3' ends. I97 has the TAA repeats replaced by 13 deoxyadenosines (Fig. 2A) while I99 has these repeats deleted entirely (Fig. 3A). Transgenic lines containing these elements were established using the reactive stock JA for transgenesis, and were called 97-52 for I97, and 99-18 and 99-59 for I99. They were maintained by crosses en masse, allowing transposition to occur provided the mutated I elements retained the ability to transpose. After several generations, all lines behaved as typical inducer stocks (data not shown), indicating that elements I97 and I99 can transpose and that the transgenic stocks contain multiple copies resulting from retrotransposition of these mutated I elements.
###end p 25
###begin title 26
###xml 33 34 33 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
The reverse transcriptase of the I factor might generate repeats at the 3' end of the element
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 72 75 72 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I97</italic>
###xml 118 123 118 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">97-52</italic>
###xml 142 145 142 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nde</italic>
###xml 182 185 182 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I97</italic>
###xml 193 196 193 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpA</italic>
###xml 313 314 313 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f1">1</xref>
###xml 467 470 467 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I97</italic>
###xml 564 565 564 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f2">2</xref>
###xml 580 583 580 583 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpA</italic>
###xml 671 674 671 674 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I97</italic>
###xml 760 761 760 761 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
We have determined the structure of the 3' ends of transposed copies of I97. DNA was extracted from 50 flies of stock 97-52 and digested with NdeII. The ends of transposed copies of I97, named IpA elements, were cloned by inverse PCR experiments using oligonucleotides 3 and 7, and sequenced using primer 1 (Fig. 1A). The sequences of the 3' end of 12 of these transposed copies were determined and found to have poly(dA) sequences which are much longer than that of I97, varying in size from 25 to more than 100 deoxyadenosines. The data are summarized in Figure 2B. None of the IpA elements that were sequenced are flanked by sequences identical to the DNA adjacent to I97 confirming that they were generated by transposition. These data demonstrate that an I element terminated by a poly(dA) sequence can transpose and hence that the TAA repeats are not essential for transposition.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 63 66 63 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpA</italic>
###xml 146 150 146 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpA1</italic>
###xml 155 159 155 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpA2</italic>
###xml 220 223 220 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nde</italic>
###xml 242 245 242 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bst</italic>
###xml 256 257 256 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f2">2</xref>
###xml 408 412 408 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpA1</italic>
###xml 447 451 447 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpA2</italic>
###xml 630 633 630 633 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpA</italic>
In order to determine the structure of the sequence into which IpA elements had inserted we sequenced both ends of two further insertions (called IpA1 and IpA2) using the same method as that described above, except that NdeII was replaced by BstYI. Figure 2B shows that they are flanked by target site duplications, indicating that they are the results of normal retrotransposition. The duplication flanking IpA1 is 14 nt long while that flanking IpA2 is either 11 or 13 nt long depending on whether the first two deoxyadenosines at the 3' junction belong to the target site or to the poly(dA) sequence. The poly(dA) sequences of IpA elements were presumably created during the retrotransposition process since they are not part of the target site duplications.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 33 36 33 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 48 53 48 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">99-18</italic>
###xml 58 63 58 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">99-59</italic>
###xml 173 174 173 174 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f3">3</xref>
###xml 219 222 219 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
The ends of transposed copies of I99 in strains 99-18 and 99-59 were similarly analyzed. We sequenced the 3' ends of 13 transposed copies. The data are summarized in Figure 3B and C. Surprisingly, retrotransposition of I99 generated two types of elements with different 3' ends.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 35 39 35 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpdA</italic>
###xml 81 82 81 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f3">3</xref>
###xml 161 164 161 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 241 242 241 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f3">3</xref>
Elements of the first type (called IpdA) terminate with poly(dA) sequences (Fig. 3B). They begin after the deoxyguanosine which is the first nucleotide flanking I99. We sequenced both ends of three transposed copies of this category. Figure 3B shows that they are flanked by target site duplications that are not themselves composed solely of deoxyadenosines, suggesting again that the poly(dA) sequences were added during transposition. These elements have a few deoxyadenosines that could belong to either the poly(dA) sequence or the target site.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 58 63 58 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpdA3</italic>
###xml 102 103 102 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f3">3</xref>
###xml 169 172 169 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 347 349 347 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c32">32</xref>
###xml 413 414 413 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
Three extra nucleotides are present between the 5' end of IpdA3 and the target site duplication (Fig. 3B). These are not present at the 5' junction of the donor element I99, suggesting that they are untemplated nucleotides that were added during the retrotransposition process, possibly during priming of the second strand as previously observed (32). We have made similar observations for a few other transposed I elements that we have analyzed in this study.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 45 48 45 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs</italic>
###xml 79 80 79 80 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f3">3</xref>
###xml 192 195 192 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 293 296 293 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 410 414 410 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs1</italic>
###xml 419 423 419 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs2</italic>
###xml 445 450 445 450 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">99-18</italic>
###xml 456 460 456 460 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs3</italic>
###xml 465 469 465 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs4</italic>
###xml 475 480 475 480 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">99-59</italic>
###xml 638 642 638 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs1</italic>
###xml 727 728 727 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f3">3</xref>
###xml 752 755 752 755 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs</italic>
###xml 833 836 833 836 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 890 893 890 893 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs</italic>
###xml 1047 1050 1047 1050 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
Transposed copies of the second type (called Ifs elements) are shown in Figure 3C. They have no poly(dA) sequences but have at their 3' ends 11 nt that were adjacent to the progenitor element I99. Sequences downstream of these nucleotides are different from each other and from those flanking I99 indicating that each element had inserted at a different site. We sequenced both ends of four of these elements, Ifs1 and Ifs2 from transgenic line 99-18, and Ifs3 and Ifs4 from 99-59. Each insertion is flanked by a target site duplication that is 12 to 14 or 15 nt long depending on whether the deoxyguanosine present at the 3' junction of Ifs1 is the last nucleotide of the transposed copy or the first of the target site (Fig. 3C). This indicates that Ifs elements were generated by transposition and that these transposed copies of I99 have taken with them some 3' flanking sequences. All Ifs elements are flanked at the 3' end by a tandem duplication of the sequence AATTCGT that was present only once in the DNA flanking the progenitor element I99.
###end p 32
###begin title 33
DISCUSSION
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 65 78 65 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R2Bm in vitro</italic>
###xml 132 134 132 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c11">11</xref>
###xml 233 234 233 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 335 336 335 336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 388 389 388 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 508 509 508 509 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 523 537 523 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D.melanogaster</italic>
###xml 542 562 542 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila teissieri</italic>
###xml 609 611 609 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c24">24</xref>
###xml 523 537 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D.melanogaster</span>
###xml 542 562 <span type="species:ncbi:7243">Drosophila teissieri</span>
Initiation of reverse transcription of the site-specific element R2Bm in vitro requires 250 nt of the 3' untranslated region (UTR) (11) suggesting that the transposition complex interacts with this sequence. Since we have found that I elements with different 3' ends can efficiently transpose, the 3' terminal TAA repeats of wild-type I factors cannot be essential for recognition by the I factor reverse transcriptase. Any sequence or structure that it does recognize may lie in the 3' UTR since functional I factors from D.melanogaster and Drosophila teissieri show very strong similarities in this region (24).
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 204 205 204 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 353 360 353 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 527 528 527 528 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 608 612 608 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R2Bm</italic>
###xml 739 741 739 741 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c12">12</xref>
###xml 742 744 742 744 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c13">13</xref>
Synthesis of the first DNA strand of non-LTR retrotransposons is thought to be initiated at the 3' end of the RNA transposition intermediate. We have shown here that the transposition intermediate of the I factor contains a few nucleotides flanking the element at its 3' end and that these extra nucleotides are not integrated during retrotransposition in vivo. This suggests that reverse transcription does not initiate at the last nucleotide of the RNA template but a few nucleotides upstream, precisely at the 3' end of the I element. Generation of precise junctions between the cDNA of the site-specific R2Bm retrotransposon and the insertion site requires RNA templates containing nucleotides from the DNA flanking the donor element (12,13). The presence of flanking sequences in the RNA might increase the precision of reverse transcription primed by a 3' OH at the site of integration.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 94 95 94 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 147 148 147 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 584 586 584 586 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c33">33</xref>
###xml 587 589 587 589 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c34">34</xref>
###xml 652 653 652 653 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 108 113 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Termination of transcription and initiation of reverse transcription seem to be different for I factors and human L1 elements (L1Hs). Although the I factor does not contain any polyadenylation signal, its transcripts end just a few nucleotides downstream of the element indicating that it has strong signals for the end of transcription. The polyadenylation signal near the 3' end of L1Hs appears to be very weak and, in a system to study transposition in cultured cells, transposed copies of this element contain long sequences that flanked the progenitor elements at their 3' ends (33,34). These data indicate that reverse transcription of wild-type I factors initiates in the UAA repeats at the very end of the RNA transposition intermediate whereas that of L1Hs may initiate in flanking sequences at the end of the transcripts far from the end of L1Hs sequences.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 9 10 9 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 244 245 244 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 340 342 340 342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c21">21</xref>
###xml 355 356 355 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 484 485 484 485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f2">2</xref>
###xml 491 492 491 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f3">3</xref>
###xml 550 552 550 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L1</italic>
###xml 570 573 570 573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alu</italic>
###xml 575 577 575 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B1</italic>
###xml 579 581 579 581 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B2</italic>
###xml 586 588 586 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ID</italic>
###xml 627 628 627 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c2">2</xref>
###xml 629 631 629 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c35">35</xref>
###xml 891 892 891 892 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 544 549 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Although I factors do not insert in a sequence-specific manner, association of DNA at the target site and nucleotides at the 3' end of their transposition intermediates may nevertheless facilitate initiation of reverse transcription. Wild-type I factors insert into AT-rich regions and their targets often begin with the trinucleotide TAA (21) while most I elements terminating with a poly(dA) sequence have inserted into target sites beginning with two or more deoxyadenosines (Figs 2B and 3B). Similar observations have been reported for the human L1 elements and the Alu, B1, B2 and ID elements all which end with poly(dA) (2,35). Association of the transposition intermediate and target DNA may stabilize the reverse transcription complex promoting the initiation of the synthesis of the first cDNA strand. With this in mind it may be possible to modify the sequence at the 3' end of an I factor to target it to particular chromosomal sites.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 25 28 25 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 43 44 43 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 132 133 132 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f3">3</xref>
###xml 182 186 182 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpdA</italic>
###xml 250 253 250 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs</italic>
###xml 411 414 411 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I99</italic>
###xml 462 466 462 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpdA</italic>
###xml 507 510 507 510 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs</italic>
###xml 644 645 644 645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 686 689 686 689 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs</italic>
The transposed copies of I99, the modified I factor with no TAA repeats, are particularly informative. These are of two types (Fig. 3B and C): those followed by a poly(dA) sequence (IpdA insertions) and those followed by the sequence GAATTCGTAATTCG (Ifs insertions). The target site duplications flanking insertions of each type start with a few nucleotides related to those which lie immediately downstream of I99, a short run of deoxyadenosines in the case of IpdA insertions and TA or TAA in the case of Ifs insertions. This may reflect base pairing of the target site with RNA transposition intermediates which extend beyond the end of the I factor. Furthermore, the 3' end of each Ifs insertion contains a tandem duplication of the sequence AATTCGT.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 80 81 80 81 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f4">4</xref>
###xml 155 156 155 156 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 289 290 289 290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f4">4</xref>
###xml 440 441 440 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f4">4</xref>
###xml 599 600 599 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f4">4</xref>
###xml 719 720 719 720 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 734 735 734 735 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f4">4</xref>
###xml 777 778 777 778 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 1112 1113 1112 1113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 1221 1222 1221 1222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f4">4</xref>
A mechanism by which these tandem repeats could be generated is shown in Figure 4. The RNA transposition intermediate, which extends beyond the end of the I factor, pairs with a few nucleotides of chromosomal DNA after cleavage of the target site by the endonuclease encoded by ORF2 (Fig. 4A and B). This allows reverse transcriptase to initiate DNA synthesis using as primer the 3' OH at the end of the strand of the chromosomal DNA (Fig. 4C). After polymerization of a few nucleotides the RNA template dissociates and reanneals to a short complementary sequence in the newly synthesized DNA (Fig. 4D). Continued reverse transcription generates a tandem duplication in the flanking DNA before entering the body of the I factor (Fig. 4E). Once synthesis of the first strand of I factor DNA is complete, the RNase H encoded by the element degrades the RNA template in the DNA/RNA hybrid molecule. Then reverse transcriptase can synthesize the second DNA strand using the 3' OH at the other end of the target site as primer and the newly synthesized DNA as template. This process produces a transposed copy of the I factor with a short repeated sequence at its right hand end and flanked by a target site duplication (Fig. 4F).
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 7 8 7 8 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd406f4">4</xref>
###xml 38 42 38 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ifs1</italic>
###xml 47 51 47 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpdA</italic>
###xml 278 282 278 282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IpdA</italic>
###xml 452 453 452 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</italic>
###xml 665 667 665 667 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c21">21</xref>
###xml 822 824 822 824 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c36">36</xref>
###xml 911 913 911 913 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L1</italic>
Figure 4 illustrates this process for Ifs1 but IpdA insertions could be generated in a similar manner with adjacent poly(dA) sequences formed by a series of disassociation and reannealing events which may be favored by a homopolymeric sequence. The poly(dA) sequences following IpdA insertions are unlikely to have resulted from polyadenylation of RNA transposition intermediates since the potential polyadenylation signal nearest to the 3' end of the I factor is 73 bp upstream. This process can account for variation in the number of TAA repeats at the 3' end of wild-type elements since elements of this type generally insert at sites containing a TAA sequence (21). A similar mechanism can also explain the presence of more complex repeats at the 3' end of some other non-LTR retrotransposons as previously suggested (36). Although non-LTR retrotransposons that end with a poly(dA) sequence, like mammalian L1 elements, have potential polyadenylation signals near their 3' ends, the possibility that a poly(A) sequence in an RNA transposition intermediate is amplified during transposition by the process described above cannot be ruled out.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 423 425 423 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c37">37</xref>
###xml 426 428 426 428 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c39">39</xref>
###xml 448 462 448 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D.melanogaster</italic>
###xml 559 564 559 564 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HeT-A</italic>
###xml 566 567 566 567 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c6">6</xref>
###xml 573 577 573 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TART</italic>
###xml 579 580 579 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c7">7</xref>
###xml 926 930 926 930 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TART</italic>
###xml 935 940 935 940 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HeT-A</italic>
###xml 1131 1147 1131 1147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Euplotes crassus</italic>
###xml 1536 1538 1536 1538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd406c40">40</xref>
###xml 448 462 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D.melanogaster</span>
###xml 1131 1147 <span type="species:ncbi:5936">Euplotes crassus</span>
Our results suggest that the first steps of DNA synthesis by reverse transcriptases of non-LTR retrotransposons might be similar to the generation of telomeric repeats. The reverse transcriptase component of telomerase repeatedly copies a short sequence of the RNA component of this enzyme using as primer the 3' OH of the extending chromosome end to which the telomerase RNA can associate by a few base pairs (reviewed in 37-39). The telomeres of D.melanogaster chromosomes do not comprise short tandem repeats but copies of the two non-LTR retrotransposons HeT-A (6) and TART (7) which insert preferentially at chromosome ends. Telomerase may have evolved from a primitive nucleic acid replication system with the reverse transcriptase component subsequently taking on a life of its own as a non-LTR retrotransposon. The ancestors of Diptera may then have lost telomerase by mutation and recruited the related activities of TART and HeT-A to preserve the ends of their chromosomes. It is also interesting that reverse transcription of non-LTR retrotransposons shows similarities to telomere addition to fragmented chromosomes in Euplotes crassus. Ciliates undergo chromosome fragmentation and telomere addition when the genome of the micronucleus is converted to the transcriptionally-active macronucleus. Fragmentation occurs at specific sites that are devoid of telomeric repeats and is followed by telomere addition by telomerase in a way that might be related to reverse transcription and integration of non-LTR retrotransposons (40), providing an additional similarity between these retroelements and telomeres.
