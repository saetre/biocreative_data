###begin article-title 0
Genetic Variation in Glutathione-Related Genes and Body Burden of Methylmercury
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
The authors declare they have no competing financial interests.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
Exposure to toxic methylmercury (MeHg) through fish consumption is a large problem worldwide, and it has led to governmental recommendations of reduced fish consumption and blacklisting of mercury-contaminated fish. The elimination kinetics of MeHg varies greatly among individuals. Knowledge about the reasons for such variation is of importance for improving the risk assessment for MeHg. One possible explanation is hereditary differences in MeHg metabolism. MeHg is eliminated from the body as a glutathione (GSH) conjugate.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Objectives
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 129 133 129 133 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
###xml 187 191 187 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLC</italic>
###xml 230 231 230 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</italic>
###xml 250 255 250 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 264 269 264 269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
We conducted this study to assess the influence of polymorphisms in GSH-synthesizing [glutamyl-cysteine ligase modifier subunit (GCLM-588) and glutamyl-cysteine ligase catalytic subunit (GCLC-129)] or GSH-conjugating [glutathione S-transferase pi 1 (GSTP1-105 and GSTP1-114)] genes on MeHg retention.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Methods
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Based on information obtained from questionnaires, 292 subjects from northern Sweden had a high consumption of fish (lean/fat fish two to three times per week or more). We measured total Hg in erythrocytes (Ery-Hg) and long-chain n-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids in plasma (P-PUFA; an exposure marker for fish intake).
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Results
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 4 9 4 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 58 63 58 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 276 277 276 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 351 355 351 355 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
###xml 393 394 393 394 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 426 430 426 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
The GSTP1 genotype modified Ery-Hg; effects were seen for GSTP1-105 and -114 separately, and combining them resulted in stronger effects. We found evidence of effect modification: individuals with zero or one variant allele demonstrated a steeper regression slope for Ery-Hg (p = 0.038) compared with individuals with two or more variant alleles. The GCLM-588 genotype also influenced Ery-Hg (p = 0.035): Individuals with the GCLM-588 TT genotype demonstrated the highest Ery-Hg, but we saw no evidence of effect modification with increasing P-PUFA.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Conclusions
###end title 10
###begin p 11
These results suggest a role of GSH-related polymorphisms in MeHg metabolism.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 209 239 209 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b22-ehp0116-000734">National Research Council 2000</xref>
###xml 297 344 297 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b15-ehp0116-000734">International Programme on Chemical Safety 1990</xref>
###xml 443 456 443 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b7-ehp0116-000734">Clarkson 2002</xref>
###xml 522 552 522 552 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2-ehp0116-000734">Al-Shahristani and Shihab 1974</xref>
###xml 187 194 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">infants</span>
###xml 199 207 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
Methylmercury (MeHg) is a very reactive organometal compound. Exposure especially affects the nervous system. Hence, low-level exposure during pregnancy may cause impaired development in infants and children (National Research Council 2000). In most populations, fish is the major source of MeHg (International Programme on Chemical Safety 1990). The elimination half-time of MeHg varies greatly among individuals, ranging from 45 to 70 days (Clarkson 2002), but extreme values, up to almost 190 days, have been reported (Al-Shahristani and Shihab 1974). One explanation of this variation might be hereditary differences in MeHg metabolism.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 65 92 65 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3-ehp0116-000734">Ballatori and Clarkson 1985</xref>
###xml 94 120 94 120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10-ehp0116-000734">Dutczak and Ballatori 1994</xref>
###xml 392 467 392 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b25-ehp0116-000734">National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) Unigene Database 2006b</xref>
###xml 708 712 706 710 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLC</italic>
###xml 714 718 712 716 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLC</italic>
###xml 725 742 723 740 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19-ehp0116-000734">Koide et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 800 804 796 800 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
###xml 806 826 802 822 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b21-ehp0116-000734">Nakamura et al. 2002</xref>
MeHg is eliminated in the bile as a glutathione (GSH) conjugate (Ballatori and Clarkson 1985; Dutczak and Ballatori 1994), and polymorphisms in GSH-synthesizing or GSH-conjugating enzymes may thus influence the elimination capacity. The rate-limiting enzyme for GSH synthesis is glutamyl-cysteine ligase (GCL), which is composed of a catalytic subunit [GCLC; Unigene accession no. Hs.654465; National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) Unigene Database 2006b] and a modifier subunit (GCLM; Unigene accession no. Hs.315562). Polymorphisms affecting GSH production (lower promoter activity for the variant alleles) have been found in both subunits: a C-->T nucleotide substitution at position -129 in GCLC (GCLC-129) (Koide et al. 2003) and a C-->T nucleotide substitution at position -588 in GCLM (Nakamura et al. 2002).
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 16 17 16 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</italic>
###xml 105 127 105 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b13-ehp0116-000734">Hayes and Strange 2000</xref>
###xml 129 151 129 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b29-ehp0116-000734">Strange and Fryer 1999</xref>
###xml 287 288 287 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</italic>
###xml 307 312 307 312 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 525 546 525 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1-ehp0116-000734">Ali-Osman et al. 1997</xref>
###xml 548 568 548 568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b31-ehp0116-000734">Sundberg et al. 1998</xref>
###xml 570 589 570 589 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b38-ehp0116-000734">Zimniak et al. 1994</xref>
###xml 267 273 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
The glutathione S-transferases (GSTs), which conjugate GSH to a wide variety of electrophilic compounds (Hayes and Strange 2000; Strange and Fryer 1999), may also affect the metabolism of MeHg. The GST family comprises several genes, many of which are polymorphic in humans. Glutathione S-transferase pi 1 (GSTP1; Unigene accession no. Hs.523836) exhibits a number of variations, of which the Ile105Val and Ala114Val amino acid substitutions both are associated with differences in enzyme activity and substrate preferences (Ali-Osman et al. 1997; Sundberg et al. 1998; Zimniak et al. 1994).
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 32 52 32 52 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9-ehp0116-000734">Custodio et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 102 106 102 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLC</italic>
###xml 114 119 114 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 766 770 766 770 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLC</italic>
###xml 775 780 775 780 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 799 803 799 803 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
###xml 806 826 806 826 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8-ehp0116-000734">Custodio et al. 2004</xref>
In a recent study by our group (Custodio et al. 2005), individuals with one variant allele for either GCLC-129 or GSTP1-114 were found to have a higher retention of mercury in erythrocytes (Ery-Hg) compared with individuals with other genotypes but similar exposure [level of long-chain n-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids in plasma (P-PUFA) was used as a proxy for fish consumption/MeHg exposure]. A limitation of that study was that most subjects had low levels of fish consumption, and thereby low Ery-Hg. The aim for this study was to further investigate the associations between Ery-Hg and GSH-related genotypes at higher MeHg exposures. For this purpose, we chose polymorphisms in the three genes, for which effects on Hg retention have been seen for either MeHg (GCLC and GSTP1) or inorganic Hg (GCLM) (Custodio et al. 2004).
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Materials and Methods
###end title 16
###begin title 17
Study population
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 652 672 646 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b12-ehp0116-000734">Hallmans et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 739 760 733 754 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b16-ehp0116-000734">Johansson et al. 2002</xref>
The study subjects were selected from the Vasterbotten Intervention Program (VIP) cohort. VIP is a long-term project intended for health promotion of the population of Vasterbotten, a county in northern Sweden. Since 1985, all individuals at 40, 50, and 60 years of age in the population of the county were invited for screening, and the cohort included 94,630 sampling occasions from 74,690 unique individuals by June 2006. Blood samples were taken from the VIP cohorts in a uniform way after an overnight fasting period, and the samples were stored as plasma, erythrocytes, or buffy coat at -80degreesC. The survey procedures are detailed elsewhere (Hallmans et al. 2003). The study subjects filled out a health/lifestyle questionnaire (Johansson et al. 2002), and a random sample of 300 individuals was chosen from among individuals consuming fish at least 2.5 times a week (2,648 individuals in total). The individuals were matched to our previous study for age and sex. Among these, we had erythrocytes and DNA samples for 292 individuals. The mean age was 49 years, with a range of 29-61 year. Of this group, 207 individuals (71%) were male and 85 (29%) were female. The samples were collected between 1992 and 2002. The study was approved by the Regional Ethics Committee of Umea University.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 145 157 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Participants</span>
We complied with all applicable requirements of the United States and international regulations (including institutional review board approval). Participants gave written informed consent before the study.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Measurement of Ery-Hg and P-PUFA
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 97 126 97 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b26-ehp0116-000734">Sandborgh-Englund et al. 1998</xref>
###xml 456 461 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We determined Ery-Hg in acid-digested samples using cold vapor atomic fluorescence spectrometry (Sandborgh-Englund et al. 1998). The limit of detection, calculated as 3 x SD for the blanks, was 0.10 mug/L. All samples were prepared in duplicate, and the method imprecision (calculated as the coefficient of variation for duplicate preparations measurements) was 3.7%. The analytical accuracy was checked against reference material, consisting of certified human blood samples (lot M0401 and M0407) obtained from the Centre de Toxicologie du Quebec, International Comparison Program, Quebec, Quebec, Canada). The results obtained were 1.8 +/- 0.10 mug/L (mean +/- SD; lot M0401) and 9.2 +/- 0.26 mug/L (lot M0407) versus certified 2.0 and 9.4 mug/L, respectively.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 169 187 169 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6-ehp0116-000734">Boberg et al. 1985</xref>
###xml 476 496 476 496 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b11-ehp0116-000734">Hallgren et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 498 518 498 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b32-ehp0116-000734">Svensson et al. 1993</xref>
Plasma fatty acids were separated by gas-liquid chromatography after separation of the lipids by thin-layer chromatography and transmethylation, as described elsewhere (Boberg et al. 1985). The relative amounts of the fatty acids were expressed as a percentage of all fatty acids analyzed. We calculated P-PUFA as the fraction of (%) eicosapen-taenoic acid (20:5 n-3) and docosahexaenoic acid (22:6 n-3) in plasma phospholipids, which are closely associated with fish intake (Hallgren et al. 2001; Svensson et al. 1993).
###end p 22
###begin title 23
Genotype analyses
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 172 176 172 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLC</italic>
###xml 182 186 182 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
###xml 192 197 192 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 207 212 207 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 273 293 273 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9-ehp0116-000734">Custodio et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 361 366 361 366 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 690 695 663 668 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 723 726 696 699 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Taq</italic>
###xml 739 742 712 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Taq</italic>
###xml 811 812 784 785 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
We used the Taqman allelic discrimination assay (ABI 7000; Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA) to analyze separately all four single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs): GCLC-129, GCLM-588, GSTP1-105, and GSTP1-114. The primers and probes have been described previously (Custodio et al. 2005). Each real-time polymerase chain reaction (PCR) assay (except for GSTP1-114) was performed with a reaction volume of 25 muL containing 1x Universal Taqman mix (Applied Biosystems), 5 ng DNA, 0.9 muM of each primer, and 0.2 muM of each probe, with the following program: 50degreesC for 2 min and 95degreesC for 10 min, followed by 40 cycles of 95degreesC for 15 sec and 60degreesC for 1 min. The GSTP1-114 reaction contained 1 U Taq polymerase (Taq Platinum; Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA), 1x PCR buffer, 2.5 mM MgCl2, 0.8 mM dNTP (Amersham Biosciences, Piscataway, NJ, USA), 1x ROX (6-carboxyl-X-rhodamine; Invitrogen), 2 ng DNA, 1 muM of each primer, 0.08 muM of each probe, and double-distilled water in a total volume of 25 muL, and followed the program described above. We included controls for each genotype in each run, and genotyping was repeated on 5% of the samples. There was a perfect agreement between the original and repeat genotyping runs in all of the four SNPs analyzed.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Statistical analysis
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 170 171 170 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">r</italic>
###xml 171 172 171 172 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">s</sub>
###xml 171 172 171 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>s</sub></italic>
###xml 643 648 643 648 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 810 815 810 815 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 880 884 880 884 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLC</italic>
We tested deviations from Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium using the Fisher-Freeman-Halton test (StatXact; Cytel Inc., Cambridge, MA, USA). We analyzed Spearman's correlation (rs) to evaluate the "strongest" exposure marker of MeHg (P-PUFA, total fish intake, fat fish intake, or lean fish intake). To explore effects at different levels of exposure, subjects were divided into three groups of equal sizes, according to the strongest exposure marker variable. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) techniques were employed to analyze, within the different exposure groups, the effects of genotype (single gene effects, as well as combined effects of the two GSTP1 polymorphisms) on natural log (ln)-transformed Ery-Hg, as the dependent variable. Because of the low number of individuals with the homozygote variant genotype, GSTP1-114 AlaVal and ValVal were pooled (AlaVal + ValVal), as well as GCLC-129 CT and TT (CT + TT). For the multivariate analyses, we used only the "strongest" exposure marker of MeHg-as in the ANOVAs (the different exposure marker variables were highly correlated)-as a numerical (not categorized) independent variable, with ln Ery-Hg as the dependent variable. We carefully evaluated the form of association between the exposure marker and ln Ery-Hg variables.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 156 157 156 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 268 269 268 269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 299 300 299 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 323 324 323 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
Other potentially influential independent variables (age, sex, alcohol consumption, snuff usage, year of sampling) were included in the model if they had a p-value < 0.2. Variables that did not influence the results were thereafter excluded from the model (i.e., sex, p = 0.21; alcohol consumption, p = 0.31; and snuffing, p = 0.78). We used a multivariate model with an interaction term between exposure and genotype to account for multiplicative effect modification. If no effect modification was present, we used a model without the interaction term.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
We also analyzed effects of dual-gene polymorphisms, where genotype was defined as the total number of variant alleles for both genes. The effects of the genes were first assessed separately for each genotype; genotypes were subsequently grouped based on the effect estimates. We used data from individuals with the most variant alleles as references values. We performed post hoc tests using the least significant difference method to account for comparisonwise error rate. All statistical analyses were performed using SPSS software (version 14; SPSS, Chicago, IL, USA).
###end p 28

###begin title 30
Exposure markers
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 106 113 106 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1-ehp0116-000734">Table 1</xref>
###xml 195 196 195 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">r</italic>

###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 90 94 90 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ALAD</italic>
###xml 246 250 242 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ALAD</italic>
###xml 290 294 286 290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ALAD</italic>
###xml 347 365 343 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b18-ehp0116-000734">Kelada et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 367 392 363 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b28-ehp0116-000734">Scinicariello et al. 2007</xref>
###xml 399 402 395 398 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">VDR</italic>
###xml 497 517 493 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14-ehp0116-000734">Haynes et al. (2003)</xref>
###xml 670 675 666 671 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AS3MT</italic>
###xml 800 816 796 812 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b20-ehp0116-000734">Meza et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 818 849 814 845 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b27-ehp0116-000734">Schl&#228;wicke Engstr&#246;m et al. 2007</xref>
###xml 952 974 948 970 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9-ehp0116-000734">Custodio et al. (2005)</xref>
###xml 1036 1040 1032 1036 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLC</italic>
###xml 1048 1053 1044 1049 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 530 538 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
Genetic influences on metal metabolism have been established for only a few toxic metals. ALAD (delta-aminolevulinic acid dehy-dratase) genotype and lead exposure is the most studied interaction; at high levels of exposure and in comparison with ALAD-1 subjects, heterozygous or homozygous ALAD-2 carriers demonstrate increased blood lead levels (Kelada et al. 2001; Scinicariello et al. 2007). The VDR (vitamin D receptor) genotype has also been shown to influence the level of blood lead. Thus, Haynes et al. (2003) showed that children with the Ff genotype displayed lower blood lead levels than did those with the FF genotype. For exposure to inorganic arsenic, the AS3MT genotype appears to have a strong influence on the fraction of the most toxic metabolite, monomethylated arsenic, in urine (Meza et al. 2005; Schlawicke Engstrom et al. 2007). For most other metals, gene-environment interactions are largely unknown, and MeHg is no exception. Custodio et al. (2005) reported that individuals with one variant allele for either GCLC-129 or GSTP1-114 had higher Ery-Hg compared with individuals with other genotypes but similar exposure. To our knowledge, no other studies regarding gene-exposure interactions for MeHg have been performed.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 765 785 765 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8-ehp0116-000734">Custodio et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 787 807 787 807 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b11-ehp0116-000734">Hallgren et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 1306 1311 1306 1311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 1337 1342 1337 1342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 1384 1389 1384 1389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 1398 1403 1398 1403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
There are several methodologic issues to take into consideration. Obviously, evaluating the modification of the relationship between exposure and retention of MeHg by genes requires accurate estimates of both. Hence, the first methodologic issue is the choice of an appropriate exposure marker for MeHg. The exposure to MeHg depends on both the intake of fish and the MeHg concentration in the fish. Because we could not measure MeHg in fish, we had to rely on a proxy. A way to assess the usefulness of the different options is to look for correlations with Ery-Hg. The association between P-PUFA and Ery-Hg was much stronger than the association between Ery-Hg and the self-reported variables-total, fat, and lean fish intake-in accordance with previous studies (Custodio et al. 2004; Hallgren et al. 2001), which probably reflects the fact that the number of fish meals reported is a poor measure of the amount of fish consumed. Hence, we used P-PUFA as an exposure marker, fully aware of the fact that P-PUFA is a very imprecise marker. However, this would bias toward the null. To achieve linearity, P-PUFA was double ln transformed. However, the difference in results between ln %PUFA and double ln %PUFA is not that large. Some examples on this (ln-transformed PUFA vs. double ln-transformed PUFA): GSTP1-105, 0.090 versus 0.077; GSTP1-114, 0.054 versus 0.061; and combination GSTP1-105 and GSTP1-114, 0.030 vs. 0.038.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 198 218 198 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b37-ehp0116-000734">Wennberg et al. 2006</xref>
###xml 605 624 605 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b34-ehp0116-000734">Voss and Siems 2006</xref>
We found a time trend of decreasing Ery-Hg. Another study from the same geographic area also shows that Ery-Hg is declining, with an average annual decrease of 5.8% between the years 1990 and 1999 (Wennberg et al. 2006). Ery-Hg rose with increasing age in our study. This finding is probably explained by more than just higher fish consumption among elderly; the correlation between age and Ery-Hg remained after adjustments for P-PUFA (data not shown). Increasing age may also be associated with impairment of the metabolism of MeHg. Indeed, both GSH and GST levels have been shown to decrease with age (Voss and Siems 2006).
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 213 233 213 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b5-ehp0116-000734">Berglund et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 235 282 235 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b15-ehp0116-000734">International Programme on Chemical Safety 1990</xref>
###xml 393 413 393 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b4-ehp0116-000734">Bergdahl et al. 1995</xref>
###xml 415 435 415 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b5-ehp0116-000734">Berglund et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 541 586 541 586 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b23-ehp0116-000734">National Swedish Board of Social Welfare 2002</xref>
###xml 588 608 588 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b30-ehp0116-000734">Sundberg et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 644 663 644 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b36-ehp0116-000734">W&#228;nnman et al. 2004</xref>
The second issue is the use of Ery-Hg as a marker for MeHg retention. Inorganic Hg can, to some extent, contribute to the levels of Ery-Hg. However, Ery-Hg is mainly MeHg and is a good marker of MeHg body burden (Berglund et al. 2005; International Programme on Chemical Safety 1990). Part of the inorganic Hg could be attributed to demethylation of MeHg during sample treatment and analysis (Bergdahl et al. 1995; Berglund et al. 2005). Additionally, the major source of inorganic Hg, dental amalgam, has decreased significantly in Sweden (National Swedish Board of Social Welfare 2002; Sundberg et al. 2000), including in the present region (Wannman et al. 2004).
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 58 62 58 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
###xml 195 212 195 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b33-ehp0116-000734">Tosic et al. 2006</xref>
###xml 309 329 309 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9-ehp0116-000734">Custodio et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 480 500 480 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8-ehp0116-000734">Custodio et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 502 521 502 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b17-ehp0116-000734">J&#246;nsson et al. 2007</xref>
The third issue regards Hardy-Weinberg disequilibrium for GCLM-588. The allele frequency reported here is similar to another study on Danish and Swiss populations where equilibrium was reported (Tosic et al. 2006). The preceding gene-environment study for MeHg with individuals from the same geographic area (Custodio et al. 2005) also showed linkage disequilibrium, whereas in other studies by our group, using the same methods but in other populations, we observed equilibrium (Custodio et al. 2004; Jonsson et al. 2007). This may indicate that the linkage disequilibrium is population specific.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 247 268 247 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b35-ehp0116-000734">Wacholder et al. 2004</xref>
The fourth issue concerns multiple testing. Several genotypes have been analyzed, which increases the possibility of false-positive findings. A false-positive report probability depends on the subjective prior belief regarding a true association (Wacholder et al. 2004). We did not adjust for multiple effect estimation, based on multiple models for different genotypes. However, we performed post hoc testing (with adjustments for multiple testing) when comparing multiple subgroups (genotypes) of a given SNP. Further, some results should be cautiously interpreted because they were based on comparisons in groups with small number of individuals (e.g., the ANOVA results, dividing the study subjects in three exposure groups).
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 225 229 225 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
###xml 325 329 325 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
###xml 420 440 420 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b21-ehp0116-000734">Nakamura et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 580 600 580 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8-ehp0116-000734">Custodio et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 834 838 834 838 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
The present study indicates that the individual differences in retention and elimination of MeHg are partly due to hereditary differences in genes conjugating and synthesizing GSH. Individuals with two variant alleles of the GCLM-588 polymorphism (TT) had higher Ery-Hg, but there was no evidence of effect modification. The GCLM-588T allele is associated with lower promoter activity and lower levels of GSH in plasma (Nakamura et al. 2002). An effect of the T allele genotype (CT + TT), giving increased levels of Hg in blood, plasma, and urine, has been seen for inorganic Hg (Custodio et al. 2004), which is also metabolized by conjugation to GSH. However, we saw similar effects only for the TT genotype, whereas individuals with the CT genotype had lower Ery-Hg than did those with either the TT or CC genotype. It appears that GCLM does not exhibit an allele-dose effect. A recessive model appears to be more plausible, where TT individuals have higher MeHg compared with carriers of the other genotypes. Still, this does not explain the lower levels of the CT genotype.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 68 72 68 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
We found no results in the dual gene combinations that included the GCLM-588 polymorphism. This was probably because the individuals with the lowest and those with the highest Ery-Hg levels (i.e., CT and TT genotypes) often were classified in the same group.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 21 26 21 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 756 777 756 777 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1-ehp0116-000734">Ali-Osman et al. 1997</xref>
###xml 779 799 779 799 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b31-ehp0116-000734">Sundberg et al. 1998</xref>
###xml 801 820 801 820 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b38-ehp0116-000734">Zimniak et al. 1994</xref>
The polymorphisms in GSTP1 (Ile105Val and Ala114Val) separately appear to modify the levels of Ery-Hg for different levels of P-PUFA, and the combination of those polymorphisms demonstrated a stronger effect than did each polymorphism alone. Individuals with zero or one variant allele had a steeper regression slope compared with individuals with two variant alleles. This resulted in significantly higher Ery-Hg at high exposures, which can be seen in the ANOVA results. No significant effects were seen at the low-exposure groups for any polymorphism. The functional impact of these variants on MeHg remains to be clarified. However, both amino acid exchanges are in the active site of the enzyme and influence the activity toward different substrates (Ali-Osman et al. 1997; Sundberg et al. 1998; Zimniak et al. 1994).
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 16 38 16 38 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9-ehp0116-000734">Custodio et al. (2005)</xref>
###xml 87 91 87 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLC</italic>
###xml 99 104 99 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 349 350 349 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 458 480 458 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9-ehp0116-000734">Custodio et al. (2005)</xref>
In the study by Custodio et al. (2005), individuals with one variant allele for either GCLC-129 or GSTP1-114 had a steeper regression slope, compared with those with zero variant alleles. In the present study, an effect was seen mainly among carriers with two variant alleles, but carriers with one allele demonstrated a nonsignificantly shallower (p = 0.35) regression slope compared with carriers with zero variant alleles. The individuals in the study of Custodio et al. (2005) had a lower exposure compared with the individuals in our study, which may explain some of the differences between the studies. The effect of the genotypes may differ according to the exposure. Moreover, the genetic effects are difficult to study at low exposure levels, and we cannot rule out the possibility that the finding from the first study may be a false-positive finding.
###end p 60
###begin title 61
Conclusion
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 116 121 116 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 201 205 201 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
###xml 336 340 336 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GCLM</italic>
Data from the present study indicate that hereditary factors influence the metabolism of MeHg. Two polymorphisms in GSTP1 modified the Ery-Hg levels at high MeHg exposures, whereas homozygotes for the GCLM-588 polymorphism had higher Ery-Hg levels but showed no evidence of effect modification. However, the mode of inheritance for the GCLM polymorphism is not clear and needs to be studied further. We would like to stress the importance of information on genetic impact on MeHg metabolism, because it may mean a difference in susceptibility to toxic effects, which may influence the risk assessment and thus the foundation of preventive actions.
