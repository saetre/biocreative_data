###begin article-title 0
BRG1 directly regulates nucleosome structure and chromatin looping of the alpha globin locus to activate transcription
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 508 515 496 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 593 594 577 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</italic>
###xml 643 647 627 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Brg1</italic>
###xml 647 656 631 640 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">null/ENU1</sup>
###xml 852 853 832 833 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</italic>
###xml 919 920 895 896 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</italic>
###xml 945 949 921 925 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Brg1</italic>
###xml 1270 1271 1234 1235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</italic>
###xml 483 489 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 664 668 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
alpha globin expression must be regulated properly to prevent the occurrence of alpha-thalassemias, yet many questions remain unanswered regarding the mechanism of transcriptional activation. Identifying factors that regulate chromatin structure of the endogenous alpha globin locus in developing erythroblasts will provide important mechanistic insight. Here, we demonstrate that the BRG1 catalytic subunit of SWI/SNF-related complexes co-immunoprecipitates with GATA-1 and EKLF in murine fetal liver cells in vivo and is recruited to the far-upstream major-regulatory element (MRE) and alpha2 promoter. Furthermore, based on our analysis of Brg1null/ENU1 mutant mice, BRG1 regulates DNase I sensitivity, H3ac, and H3K4me2 but not CpG methylation at both sites. Most importantly, BRG1 is required for chromatin loop formation between the MRE and alpha2 promoter and for maximal RNA Polymerase II occupancy at the alpha2 promoter. Consequently, Brg1 mutants express alpha globin mRNA at only 5-10% of wild-type levels and die at mid-gestation. These data identify BRG1 as a chromatin-modifying factor required for nucleosome remodeling and transcriptional activation of the alpha globin locus. These data also demonstrate that chromatin looping between the MRE and alpha2 promoter is required as part of the transcriptional activation mechanism.

The alpha and beta globin must be expressed at similar levels in erythrocytes to produce functional hemoglobin consisting of alpha2-beta2 tetramers; a failure to do so results in globin chain imbalances and the occurrence of thalassemias (1). To facilitate their coordinately regulated transcription, the alpha and beta globin loci are structurally similar with a series of evolutionarily conserved regulatory elements that direct tissue-specific transcription of far-downstream globin genes (Figure 1A). Furthermore, the genes are arranged in the order of their developmental expression with primitive (embryonic) globin genes followed by definitive (fetal/adult) globin genes. To account for their similarity in genomic structure, the alpha and beta loci arose by duplication of an ancestoral locus at an early stage of vertebrate evolution approximately500 million years ago and have been constrained by purifying selection (2,3). ###begin p 5
###xml 268 269 265 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>

###xml 652 655 643 646 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5 B6 B7">5&#8211;7</xref>
###xml 747 748 738 739 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 840 844 831 835 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9 B10 B11">9&#8211;11</xref>
###xml 960 965 948 953 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">major</italic>
###xml 1078 1083 1063 1068 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">major</italic>
###xml 1203 1205 1176 1178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
The beta globin locus has been characterized in considerable detail and serves as a model for studying transcriptional regulatory mechanisms in general and the relationship between sequence-specific transcription factors and chromatin-modifying factors in particular (4). For example, the zinc-finger transcription factors GATA-1 and EKLF bind to their consensus sequences [(A/T)GATA(A/G) and CACCC, respectively] in the locus control region (LCR) as well as the beta major and beta minor promoters and recruit several co-regulators including the CBP/P300 histone acetyltransfereases (HATs) and the BRG1 catalytic subunit of SWI/SNF-related complexes (5-7). BRG1 occupancy is presumably reinforced by a bromodomain that binds acetylated histones (8), and it confers ATPase activity that alters the conformation and position of nucleosomes (9-11). As a result, DNase I hypersensitive sites (HSs) indicative of open chromatin are created in the LCR and the beta major promoter. BRG1 also mediates chromatin looping such that GATA-1 and EKLF bound sites in HS2 of the LCR and beta major promoter are brought into close physical proximity while the intervening approximately40-kb DNA segment is looped out (12). This step culminates in maximal RNA Polymerase II recruitment at the promoter and transcriptional activation.
###end p 5

###xml 698 700 683 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 784 792 769 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C16orf35</italic>
###xml 401 406 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 417 422 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The alpha globin locus also contains GATA-1 and EKLF binding sites in upstream regulatory elements as well as the alpha1 and alpha2 promoters. The regulatory elements share several properties with the beta globin LCR including the presence of acetylated histones and HSs in erythroid cells but not other cell types (13-17). The most highly conserved of these regulatory elements, which corresponds to human HS -40 or mouse HS -26, confers robust, erythroid-specific enhancer activity on its own and is referred to as the major regulatory element (MRE) (18-22). The MRE is different from the LCR, however, because it is embedded in an intron of the ubiquitously expressed C16orf35 gene (Figure 1A) (23). Considering that the MRE does not impose erythroid-specific transcription on the C16orf35 promoter and also lacks a CTCF insulator, it is believed that the mechanism of transcriptional regulation is fundamentally different for the alpha and beta globin loci.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 98 99 94 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</italic>
###xml 272 277 253 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">major</italic>
###xml 288 293 269 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24 B25 B26">24&#8211;26</xref>
###xml 335 343 312 320 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C16orf35</italic>
###xml 643 645 613 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
Chromosome conformation capture (3C) experiments have recently demonstrated that the MRE and alpha2 promoter are brought into close physical proximity in erythroid cells and the approximately40 kb of intervening DNA is looped out in a manner analogous to the LCR and beta major promoter (24-26). This finding suggests alpha globin and C16orf35 are recruited to distinct higher order chromatin environments and are regulated differently. Interestingly, EKLF is dispensable for chromatin looping at the alpha globin locus but required at the beta globin locus, which highlights another functional difference in how these two loci are regulated (24). Most importantly, unlike the beta globin locus, it is not known which chromatin-modifying factors are recruited to the alpha globin locus to mediate histone acetylation, nucleosome remodeling, and chromatin looping. These factors must be identified to elucidate the mechanism of alpha globin transcriptional activation.
###end p 7

###xml 114 118 114 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Brg1</italic>

###xml 514 516 511 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 517 519 514 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 85 90 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We previously generated and characterized an ENU-induced hypomorphic mutation in the mouse brahma-related gene 1 (Brg1, also known as Smarca4) gene that results in a single amino-acid substitution (E1083G) in the ATPase domain (27). The mutant protein is stable and assembles into SWI/SNF-related complexes, but Brg1null/ENU1 mutants have markedly reduced nucleosome remodeling, chromatin looping, and transcription of the beta globin locus and die between embryonic day (E) 11.5 and 14.5 due to severe anemia (12,27,28). Based on additional analysis of these mutants, we demonstrate here that BRG1 also remodels nucleosomes, mediates chromatin looping, and activates transcription of the alpha globin locus. These findings indicate that alpha and beta globin transcription are regulated by a common, BRG1-dependent mechanism.

We previously identified reduced transcription of the alpha and beta globin genes in flow-sorted erythroid cells from E12.5 Brg1null/ENU1 fetal livers (FLs) (12,27,28). We subsequently validated the beta globin result and have studied the role of BRG1 in beta globin regulation (12,27,28). To validate the alpha globin result, we performed RT-PCR and demonstrated that expression is markedly reduced in mutants (Figure 1B). RT-qPCR indicated that mutants express alpha globin at 5-10% of wild-type levels (Figure 1C). A similar reduction was observed for beta major previously and in this study (Figure 1C). To determine whether BRG1 directly regulates the alpha globin locus, we performed ChIP assays and localized it to the MRE and alpha2 promoter in FLs (Figure 2A). The ability of GATA-1 and EKLF to bind BRG1 and recruit it to the alpha globin locus was unaffected in mutants (Figure 2A, B). To assess chromatin structure in more detail, we evaluated two covalent histone modifications associated with transcriptionally permissive promoters. In wild-type FLs, the MRE and alpha2 promoter are enriched for H3 acetylation (H3ac) and H3 lysine 4 dimethylation (H3K4me2) in erythroid cells of the FL several fold higher than non-erythroid cells of the head as expected (Figure 4B, C). In contrast, mutant FLs had low-level enrichment of H3ac comparable to head tissue at both sites (Figure 4B). Higher H3ac levels in wild-type compared to mutant is consistent with BRG1 having a bromodomain and functioning in concert with HATs. Mutant FLs also had lower levels of H3K4me2 similar in magnitude to H3ac (Figure 4C). The previous total H3 ChIP experiment (Figure 4A) serves as a control to indicate that decreased H3ac and H3K4me2 in mutants is not due to depletion of H3. The effect of BRG1 on H3ac and H3K4me is presumably indirect and could be a consequence of decreased transcription (39).

###xml 348 350 345 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 566 571 556 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40 B41 B42">40&#8211;42</xref>
Sodium bisulfite mutagenesis assays were also performed to evaluate DNA methylation. Wild-type and mutant samples were hypomethylated to a similar degree (). These results are different than what we previously observed at the beta globin locus where wild-type samples were hypomethylated to an even greater extent and mutants were hypermethylated (27). Previous studies have demonstrated that hypomethylation at the alpha globin locus occurs in erythroid and non-expressing tissues, whereas hypomethylation at the beta globin locus occurs only in erythroid tissues (40-42). Therefore, CpG methylation appears to be less utilized as a regulatory mechanism at the alpha globin locus compared to the beta globin locus.

###xml 435 445 431 441 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>

###xml 435 445 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
Chromatin looping is thought to transfer RNA Polymerase II (Pol II) from upstream regulatory elements to the promoter to facilitate transcription (44). Consistent with this notion, the Brg1 mutation does not affect Pol II occupancy at the MRE but the loss of chromatin looping in Brg1 mutants is associated with loss of high-level occupancy of Pol II and P-Ser5 Pol II at the alpha2 promoter (Figure 5C, D). This finding is similar to Drosophila BRM promoting Pol II association with polytene chromosomes (39) and accounts for the observed decrease in alpha globin transcription in this study.

###begin title 38
DISCUSSION
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 371 372 359 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</italic>
###xml 753 755 741 743 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
Molecular determinants of histone acetylation, nucleosome remodeling and chromatin looping at the alpha globin locus are unknown. Here, we demonstrate that the BRG1 catalytic subunit of SWI/SNF-related complexes is required for each of these inter-related processes and for alpha globin transcription. We also demonstrate that chromatin looping between the MRE2 and alpha2 promoter is an essential part of the mechanism of the transcriptional activation mechanism. In this regard, it is noteworthy that not all chromatin loops will be functionally important. For example, the H enhancer undergoes chromatin looping with numerous olfactory receptor genes but is required for the expression of only a relatively small subset that are most tightly linked (45).
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 133 134 129 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</italic>
###xml 277 279 270 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 280 282 273 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 475 477 461 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 660 662 639 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 816 818 791 793 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 819 821 794 796 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
Other transcription factors and chromatin-modifying factors are likely involved in chromatin-loop formation between the MRE and alpha2 promoter, and GATA-1 and FOG-1 are good candidates because they recruit BRG1 and are required for chromatin looping at the beta globin locus (24,46). It should be noted, however, that EKLF also recruits BRG1 and is essential for chromatin looping at the beta globin locus but is dispensable for chromatin looping at the alpha globin locus (24). CTCF is also required for chromatin looping at the beta globin locus but is not a good candidate because it is not known to bind the alpha globin locus or regulate its expression (47). Conversely, ATRX is a good candidate because it is a putative chromatin-remodeling factor similar to BRG1 and is required for alpha globin expression (48,49).
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 238 240 231 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 635 636 617 618 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</italic>
###xml 663 668 642 647 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">major</italic>
###xml 798 800 777 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
Based on our previous work and data presented here, we propose that BRG1 performs a similar role at the alpha and beta globin loci: 1, it binds to GATA-1, EKLF, and possibly other transcription factors such as p45/NF-E2 in erythroblasts (50), which leads to the recruitment of SWI/SNF-related complexes to the far-upstream regulatory elements (the alphaMRE and betaLCR) and globin promoters; 2, it confers DNA-dependent ATPase activity and harnesses this energy to alter nucleosome conformation and position directly and promotes histone acetylation and H3K4me2 indirectly; 3, it is required for chromatin looping between the MRE-alpha2 promoter and the LCR-beta major promoter, which is thought to transfer Pol II from the upstream regulatory elements to the promoters and initiate transcription (44).
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 27 31 23 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Brg1</italic>
###xml 206 208 194 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 209 211 197 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 613 617 597 601 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Brg1</italic>
###xml 792 794 776 778 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 795 797 779 781 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 1133 1135 1109 1111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 1352 1354 1328 1330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 443 448 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 642 647 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 648 653 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 675 680 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 735 740 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 782 790 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 879 884 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 937 942 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 966 971 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1008 1013 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1114 1119 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
DeltaLCR mutants phenocopy Brg1 mutants, which supports our model, but DeltaMRE mutants have a smaller decrease in alpha globin transcription (reduced to 50% of normal) and exhibit a more subtle phenotype (51,52). To reconcile this difference, the LCR consists of six HSs that function in an additive manner such that deletion of any one HS results in a relatively mild phenotype. Similarly, deletion of the MRE plus the other four HSs at the mouse alpha globin locus, even though they confer less enhancer activity than the MRE when incorporated into transgenes, may result in a more severe phenotype similar to Brg1 mutants. There are also human-mouse differences with the human MRE playing a more important role based on transgenic mouse experiments and deletions in thalassemia patients (19,22). A gene-targeting experiment also has been performed that replaced 85 kb of the mouse alpha globin locus with the corresponding 120 kb of human sequence; although the human alpha globin genes knocked into the mouse were expressed in a tissue- and stage-appropriate manner, mRNA levels were 60% less than endogenous mouse transcripts (53). The MRE might function differently between the two species, despite being highly conserved, because of breaks in the conserved synteny within the locus that occurred after the rodent and primate lineages diverged (54).
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 268 270 261 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 271 273 264 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 274 276 267 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 394 396 384 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 412 415 402 405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cis</italic>
###xml 428 433 418 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">trans</italic>
###xml 925 928 905 908 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">via</italic>
###xml 989 991 969 971 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
The alpha and beta globin loci are known to move from their chromosomal territories at the nuclear periphery, where they are transcribed at low levels, to transcription factories where multiple chromatin loops converge at Pol II foci and transcription is upregulated (25,55,56). The LCR is required for re-localization of beta globin from its repressive compartment to transcription factories (56), but no other cis elements or trans-acting factors have been identified that are required for this process to occur at beta globin or any other locus to our knowledge. BRG1 is an attractive candidate because it is a DNA-dependent ATPase that could provide the necessary energy and it participates in the formation of local chromatin loops, referred to as active chromatin hubs, at the alpha and beta globin loci. Additionally, SWI/SNF-related complexes contain actin and actin-related subunits that could potentially move loci via the nuclear cytoskeleton/matrix to transcription factories (57). It must be determined whether BRG1 functions exclusively in the formation of local chromatin loops (where active chromatin hubs allow upstream enhancers to load Pol II onto promoters) or if it also transports these domains to transcription factories.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 379 387 368 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C16orf35</italic>
In summary, BRG1 plays a fundamental role in the mechanism of transcriptional activation at the alpha globin locus as well as the beta globin locus. Our findings reveal a common regulatory mechanism, notwithstanding certain differences such as the role of EKLF and CpG methylation, which was not anticipated because the alpha globin locus contains a ubiquitously expressed gene (C16orf35) and lacks a CTCF insulator.
###end p 44
