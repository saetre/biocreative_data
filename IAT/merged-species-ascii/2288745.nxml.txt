AGEs and Glucose Levels Modulate Type I and III Procollagen mRNA Synthesis in Dermal Fibroblasts Cells Culture

###begin p 3
###xml 376 377 376 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</italic>
###xml 430 435 430 431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 456 461 452 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 633 638 625 626 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 659 664 647 648 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 791 796 775 776 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 876 881 856 857 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 288 293 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In the dermis, fibroblasts play an important role in the turnover of the dermal extracellular matrix. Collagen I and III, the most important dermal proteins of the extracellular matrix, are progressively altered during ageing and diabetes. For mimicking diabetic conditions, the cultured human dermal fibroblasts were incubated with increasing amounts of AGE-modified BSA and D-glucose for 24 hours. The expression of procollagen alpha2(I) and procollagen alpha1(III) mRNA was analyzed by quantitative real-time PCR. Our data revealed that the treatment of fibroblasts with AGE-modified BSA upregulated the expression of procollagen alpha2(I) and procollagen alpha1(III) mRNA in a dose-dependent manner. High glucose levels mildly induced a profibrogenic pattern, increasing the procollagen alpha2(I) mRNA expression whereas there was a downregulation tendency of procollagen alpha1(III) mRNA.

###begin title 4
1. INTRODUCTION
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 313 317 313 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 400 401 397 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 576 577 573 574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 896 897 893 894 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 1005 1006 1002 1003 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 1385 1386 1382 1383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1531 1532 1528 1529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 1632 1633 1629 1630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
Elevated levels of blood glucose give birth to a vicious cycle of metabolic disturbances within the intracellular and extracellular environment and lead to a broad array of diabetes complications. In the hyperglycemic milieu activation of the aldose reductase pathway (AR), protein kinase C (PKC), especially the beta-isoform, and generation of advanced glycation end products (AGEs) can be noticed [1]. In ageing, the nonenzymatic glycosylation of proteins or Maillard reaction occurs, which is a consequence of the elevated levels of glucose and is accelerated in diabetes [2]. The glycation reaction starts when the amino groups of a protein react nonenzymatically with glucose to forma Schiff base, stabilized through Amadori rearrangement, and it represents an early temporary step in the glycation process. In the advanced stage, complex reactions that lead to the formation of AGEs occur [3]. In ageing and diabetes, the AGEs levels of long life proteins of the extracellular matrix are increased [4]. These products cause cellular dysfunctions by multiple mechanisms, including receptor-independent and receptor-dependent processes, and can directly influence the structural integrity of the vessel wall and underlying basement membranes through excessive cross-linking of matrix molecules such as collagen and through disruption of matrix-matrix and matrix-cell interactions [5]. The formation of AGEs in skin collagen favors cross-linking reactions, resulting in decreased degradability and impaired dermal regeneration [6]. AGEs can bind to fibroblast cell membranes and may contribute to the progression of skin ageing [7].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 254 255 254 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 387 392 387 388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 395 396 391 392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
The receptor for AGE (RAGE) is a multiligand receptor belonging to the immunoglobulin superfamily found in a widerange of cell types, including endothelial cells (EC), mononuclear phagocytes (MP), lymphocytes, vascular smooth muscle (VSMC), and neurons [8]. Recently, it was demonstrated that RAGE is highly expressed in skin and upregulated by AGEs and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) [9].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 228 230 228 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 483 487 483 484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 491 493 488 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
Collagen type I and III are the major structural components of the dermal extracellular matrix (ECM), representing over 70% and, respectively, 15% of skin dry weight and providing the dermis with tensile strength and stability [10]. Collagen metabolism is a complex process requiring a balance between synthesis and degradation through the action of cytokines and matrix metalloproteinase (MMPs). The most important profibrogenic cytokine is the transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-beta1) [11].
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 141 146 141 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 156 161 152 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 127 163 127 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">procollagen 1 <italic>&#945;</italic>2 (PCol 1 <italic>&#945;</italic>2)</italic>
###xml 182 187 174 175 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 197 202 185 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 168 204 160 188 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">procollagen 3 <italic>&#945;</italic>1 (PCol 3 <italic>&#945;</italic>1)</italic>
###xml 234 239 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The aim of this study was to investigate the influence of high glucose concentration and AGEs mimicking diabetic conditions on procollagen 1 alpha2 (PCol 1 alpha2) and procollagen 3 alpha1 (PCol 3 alpha1) genes expression in cultured human skin fibroblasts.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
2. MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 9
###begin title 10
2.1. Preparation and identification of AGE-modified BSA
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 19 20 19 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</italic>
###xml 128 129 128 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V</italic>
###xml 268 269 262 263 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 307 309 301 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 566 568 560 562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 67 73 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Solutions of 1.6 M D-glucose (Sigma-Aldrich company) and 100 mg/mL bovine serum albumin (BSA Sigma-Aldrich, RIA grade, fraction V) have been coincubated insterile PBS 10 mM, pH 7.4 (Gibco) for 6 weeks under aerobic conditions at 37degreesC, in the presence of 3 mM NaN3 (Merck) to prevent bacterial growth [12]. Controls with BSA solution only were simultaneously kept under the same conditions. After 6 weeks, the free sugar was removed by dialysis against 10 mM PBS, pH 7.4 for 48 hours. The protein concentration (70 mg BSA/mL) was determined by Bradford method [13]. The formation of AGE-modified BSA (AGE-BSA) was analyzed by fast protein liquid chromatography (FPLC), SDS-PAGE, and by fluorescence spectroscopy.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Fast protein liquid chromatography
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 26 27 26 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#196;</italic>

An FPLC automated system (AKTA FPLC-Amersham Pharmacia Biotech) with a size exclusion column (Superdex 200 HR 10/30) was used for separation of glycated and unglycated BSA. The samples of 1.55 mug /muL prepared in 10 mM PBS pH 7.4 were eluted in 5% acetonitrile (Merck) and 10 mM PBS pH 7.4, at a constant flow rate of 0.8 mL/min, and their absorption at 280 nm was automatically recorded.

2.2. Cell culture
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 192 194 190 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 403 404 401 402 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sup>
###xml 609 611 607 608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 633 634 630 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</italic>
###xml 692 693 689 690 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 753 754 750 751 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 119 127 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 201 209 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 479 485 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Human dermal fibroblasts were obtained from skin biopsy sampled from the inferior pubian region of young normal female patients (average age 30 +/- 2.3 years) by employing explants technique [16]. All patients gave informed written consent to tissue collection, which was conducted under a protocol approved by the Ethical Commission of National Institute of Endocrinology C.I. Parhon. The cells (2 x 104/mL) were grown in DMEM (Sigma-Aldrich) medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (Sigma-Aldrich), sterile antimycotic solution 1X: penicillin 100 IU/mL, streptomycin 0.1 mg/mL and amphotericin 0.25 mug/mL (Sigma-Aldrich), D-glucose 5.5 mM, 2% glutamine (Sigma-Aldrich), 0.22% NaHCO3 (Sigma-Aldrich), and 0.47% HEPES (Sigma-Aldrich) in a 5% CO2 humidified atmosphere, at 37degreesC.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
2.3. Treatment of cells
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 217 218 217 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</italic>
###xml 353 354 353 354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</italic>
###xml 630 631 630 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</italic>
###xml 641 642 641 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</italic>
###xml 788 789 788 789 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</italic>
###xml 799 800 799 800 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</italic>
###xml 121 127 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
The 70% confluent fibroblasts cultures (passage 3-5) were maintained 24 hours in the growth medium containing 0.5% fetal bovine serum for synchronization of cells cycle. Then they were treated with varying amounts of D-glucose: 5.5 mM (normoglycemic), 11 mM, 22 mM, and 33 mM (hyperglycemic) for 24 hours. The osmolarity of the medium was adjusted with D-mannitol after the addition of glucose, in order to have the same osmolarity in all samples. Other flasks of cells were treated with different amounts of sterile AGE-modified BSA, and BSA was added in order to adjust the total protein concentration to 5 mg/mL. The effect of D-glucose, D-mannitol, or AGE-BSA on cell viability was assessed by estimating the percent of cells excluding Trypan blue. There was no significant effect of D-glucose, D-mannitol, or AGE-BSA on cells viability. Over 95% of the cells excluded the dye.

###begin title 38
4. DISCUSSION
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 369 371 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 771 773 771 773 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 825 829 825 826 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 1527 1529 1524 1526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 1691 1693 1688 1690 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 1766 1770 1763 1764 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 1766 1770 1763 1764 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>&#946;</italic></sup>
###xml 1841 1843 1835 1837 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 1884 1888 1878 1879 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 1884 1888 1878 1879 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>&#946;</italic></sup>
###xml 2053 2057 2044 2045 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 2096 2098 2084 2086 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 2242 2260 2230 2244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">procollagen1&#945;2</italic>
###xml 2365 2369 2349 2350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 2397 2401 2378 2379 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 2397 2401 2378 2379 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>&#946;</italic></sup>
###xml 2501 2502 2479 2480 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 2543 2544 2521 2522 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 2856 2857 2834 2835 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1634 1639 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Tissue remodeling of extracellular matrix (ECM) is an essential and dynamic process associated with physiological responses and it involves the production and deposit of newly synthesized ECM components, as well as the degradation of ECM. The balance of these processes results in either preservation or alteration of the structure and functions of the support tissue [21]. Resorption of the ECM is mediated by MMPs, whereas generation of ECM is predominantly achieved through the production of collagen. Degradation of ECM generally characterizes pathological states such as arthritis or tumor invasion, whereas the increased generation of ECM underlies fibrotic diseases. Both processes are strictly regulated by complex networks of cellular and molecular interaction [22]. Mediators such as the profibrotic cytokines (TGF-beta1, interleukins, and connective tissue growth factor-CTGF) released by resident cells, for examples, skin fibroblasts, or infiltrating leukocytes, monocytes, or macrophages may play a central role in the turnover of the dermal ECM. Expansion of ECM in fibrosis occurs in many tissues, including skin, as part of the end-organ complications in diabetes and chronic hyperglycemia. The formation of AGEs is considered as causative factor in diabetic tissue fibrosis. Elevated levels of blood glucose ignite a vicious cycle of metabolic disturbances with activation of multiple pathways. For example, it was demonstrated that AR is implicatedin the adverse cellular response to high levels of glucose [23]. Multiple studies indicate that expression and activity of AR are increased in experimental models and human tissuesin diabetes, including the diabetic kidney [24]. In addition to AR, isoforms of protein kinase C family, especially PKCbeta isoform, has been associated with enhanced activity in hyperglycemia [25]. In vivo, pharmacologic blockade of PKCbeta has been associated with improved vascular function in diabeticrats, as well as amelioration of accelerated mesangial expansion and expression of genes such as TGF-beta1 and extracellular matrix components [11]. Our results suggest that high levels of glucose may influence the expansion of ECM and the process of skin aging through mild stimulation of procollagen1alpha2 gene expression and possibly of other ECM proteins. A potential mediator for this effect is probablyTGF-beta1, which is regulated by PKCbeta. Increased glucose flux through glycolysis and AR pathway leads to increased intracellular NADH/NAD+ ratio, which causes an inhibition of NAD+-dependent enzyme glyceraldehydes-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH), which in turn will result in an increase in the dihydroxyacetone phosphate and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate levels. These triosephosphates can be converted into PKC activator diacylglycerol, transformation accelerated by the increase in NADH/NAD+ ratio.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 158 160 158 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">26</xref>
###xml 279 283 279 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 279 283 279 280 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>&#946;</italic></sup>
###xml 340 344 337 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 340 344 337 338 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>&#946;</italic></sup>
###xml 430 432 424 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">27</xref>
###xml 582 587 576 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 611 615 601 602 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 626 630 613 614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 660 662 644 646 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">28</xref>
###xml 493 499 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 531 535 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Another consequence of elevated levels of glucose is the Maillard reaction. In the advanced step, complex reactions that lead to the formation of AGEs occur [26]. Numerous studies have suggested a link among glucose-modified proteins, Amadori products, AGE, and activation of PKCbeta isoforms. In cultured mesangial cells, inhibitors of PKCbeta isoforms prevented the glycated albumin-induced increased expression of collagen IV [27]. Some in vivo studies showed that infusion of AGE-modified murine serumal bumin into nondiabetic mice for 4 weeks caused upregulation of glomerular alpha1(IV) collagen, laminin beta1, and TGF-beta1 transcriptionin the kidney [28].
###end p 40
###begin p 41
In our experimental conditions, the AGE-modified BSA concentrations used appeared to be high, but the doses used in the treatment of cells (1, 2, and 4 mg/mL) represent the concentration of glycated protein not of AGEs products. By fluorescence, gel filtration chromatography, and SDS-PAGE assays, we have highlighted the formation of AGEs and the cross-linking of glycated BSA but we did not measure the level of AGEs compounds. On the other hand, fibroblasts can suffer in vivo directly from the effects of AGEs formed during the degradation of matrix proteins that have a long life and important amounts of these compounds can accumulate in time.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 224 229 224 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 211 230 211 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">procollagen 1<italic>&#945;</italic>2</italic>
###xml 235 254 231 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">procollagen 3&#945;1</italic>
###xml 378 383 370 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 402 407 390 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 94 99 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Finally, by means of real-time PCR, we revealed that AGE-modified BSA interacts with cultured human dermal fibroblasts and influence their function by significant upregulation in a dose-dependent manner of both procollagen 1alpha2 and procollagen 3alpha1 genes expression. In addition, at all AGE-BSA doses used for the cultured fibroblasts treatments, the ratio of procollagen alpha1(III)/procollagen alpha2(I) mRNA remained constant to approximately 1.7 on average.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 215 217 215 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">29</xref>
###xml 596 598 596 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">30</xref>
###xml 632 633 632 633 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 911 913 911 913 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">31</xref>
###xml 1171 1175 1171 1172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 1194 1199 1191 1192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 1205 1206 1198 1199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1253 1258 1246 1247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 1319 1321 1308 1310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">32</xref>
###xml 1377 1379 1366 1368 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">31</xref>
###xml 274 279 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 461 466 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 887 890 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1016 1021 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1043 1048 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1279 1284 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1353 1356 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
This could probably alter the turnover of collagenous ECM in the skin and contribute to a decreased tensile strength and mechanical stability of connective tissues and a difficult healing in diabetes. Twigg et al. [29] have reported that in confluent monolayers of cultured human dermal fibroblasts, the connective tissue growth factor (CTGF) is upregulated at the mRNA and protein levels by AGEs. Later, they have shown that CTGF contributes significantly, in human dermal fibroblasts, to AGEs upregulation of fibronectin, another ECM component like collagen, through a PKC-dependent mechanism [30]. In the same year Okano et al. [7] demonstrated that AGEs which are accumulating in elastin, fibronectin, and collagens bind to fibroblast membranes at concentrations of 2.5-40 mg/mL. Our data are in accord with other studies which described AGEs increased collagen production in normal rat kidney fibroblasts [31]. Recently, Lohwasser et al. demonstrated for the first time that RAGE protein is highly expressed in human skin and in cultured human skin fibroblasts and AGE adduct upregulated RAGE expression and induced significantly upregulated expression of CTGF, TGF-beta1, and procollagen alpha1(I) [9]. Also RAGE induction through AGE-BSA and TNF-alpha was shown before in human umbilical vein endothelial cells [32] and through AGE-BSA in normal rat kidney fibroblasts [31].
###end p 43

###xml 65 70 65 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 59 71 59 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Pcol 1<italic>&#945;</italic>2</italic>
###xml 82 87 78 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 76 88 72 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Pcol 3<italic>&#945;</italic>1</italic>
###xml 188 193 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
It seems that the most important ECM-related protein genes Pcol 1alpha2 and Pcol 3alpha1 are upregulated in the presence of AGEs and to a less extent by high levels of glucose in cultured human dermal fibroblast possibly by receptor-independent/dependent pathways.
