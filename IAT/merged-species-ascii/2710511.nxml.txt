###begin article-title 0
TLR2-Dependent Inhibition of Macrophage Responses to IFN-gamma Is Mediated by Distinct, Gene-Specific Mechanisms
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: SB JE. Performed the experiments: SB. Analyzed the data: SB JE. Wrote the paper: SB JE.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 0 26 0 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 125 140 125 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 390 391 386 387 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 394 395 390 391 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 435 436 427 428 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 439 440 431 432 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 0 26 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 125 140 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Mycobacterium tuberculosis uses multiple mechanisms to avoid elimination by the immune system. We have previously shown that M. tuberculosis can inhibit selected macrophage responses to IFN-gamma through TLR2-dependent and -independent mechanisms. To specifically address the role of TLR2 signaling in mediating this inhibition, we stimulated macrophages with the specific TLR2/1 ligand Pam3CSK4 and assayed responses to IFN-gamma. Pam3CSK4 stimulation prior to IFN-gamma inhibited transcription of the unrelated IFN-gamma-inducible genes, CIITA and CXCL11. Surface expression of MHC class II and secretion of CXCL11 were greatly reduced as well, indicating that the reduction in transcripts had downstream effects. Inhibition of both genes required new protein synthesis. Using chromatin immunoprecipitation, we found that TLR2 stimulation inhibited IFN-gamma-induced RNA polymerase II binding to the CIITA and CXCL11 promoters. Furthermore, TATA binding protein was unable to bind the TATA box of the CXCL11 promoter, suggesting that assembly of transcriptional machinery was disrupted. However, TLR2 stimulation affected chromatin modifications differently at each of the inhibited promoters. Histone H3 and H4 acetylation was reduced at the CIITA promoter but unaffected at the CXCL11 promoter. In addition, NF-kappaB signaling was required for inhibition of CXCL11 transcription, but not for inhibition of CIITA. Taken together, these results indicate that TLR2-dependent inhibition of IFN-gamma-induced gene expression is mediated by distinct, gene-specific mechanisms that disrupt binding of the transcriptional machinery to the promoters.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 230 233 230 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Aderem1">[1]</xref>
###xml 527 530 523 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Dunne1">[2]</xref>
###xml 532 535 528 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Foster1">[3]</xref>
###xml 578 581 574 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Lopez1">[4]</xref>
Macrophages are important mediator cells during the immune response to invading pathogens. They are able to recognize a variety of pathogens through cell surface receptors, including members of the Toll-like receptor (TLR) family [1]. Among these receptors, TLR2 and TLR4 specifically recognize bacteria-derived lipopeptides and LPS, respectively. Engagement of TLRs results in activation of MAPK and NF-kappaB signaling pathways, culminating in the expression of proinflammatory cytokines and antimicrobial effector molecules [2], [3], as well as in the induction of apoptosis [4].
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 327 330 323 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Mosser1">[5]</xref>
###xml 374 400 366 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 400 403 392 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Mogues1">[6]</xref>
###xml 404 407 396 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Flynn2">[9]</xref>
###xml 459 463 451 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Murray1">[10]</xref>
###xml 464 468 456 460 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Skerrett1">[13]</xref>
###xml 374 400 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
Macrophages also function as effector cells in the adaptive immune response. While macrophages play an important part in controlling infections as part of the innate immune response, full activation of their antimicrobial capacity and antigen presentation function only occurs after stimulation with the Th1 cytokine IFN-gamma [5]. IFN-gamma is essential for the control of Mycobacterium tuberculosis[6]-[9] and the clearance of other intracellular pathogens [10]-[13].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 230 234 222 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Levy1">[14]</xref>
IFN-gamma acts by binding to the heterodimeric IFN-gamma receptor. Receptor binding and dimerization leads to the recruitment of JAKs 1 and 2 and ultimately to tyrosine and serine phosphorylation of the transcription factor STAT1 [14]. Phospho-STAT1 dimers then drive gene expression by binding gamma-activated sites (GAS) in the promoters of a large number of genes.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 130 134 122 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Kovarik1">[15]</xref>
###xml 176 180 168 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Stoiber1">[16]</xref>
###xml 182 186 174 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Yao1">[17]</xref>
###xml 207 211 199 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Kincaid1">[18]</xref>
###xml 212 216 204 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Ting1">[22]</xref>
###xml 275 279 267 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Banaiee1">[23]</xref>
###xml 312 316 304 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Fortune1">[24]</xref>
###xml 318 322 310 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini1">[25]</xref>
###xml 465 480 453 468 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 610 613 598 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Mogues1">[6]</xref>
###xml 615 619 603 607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Barnes1">[26]</xref>
###xml 621 625 609 613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Gomez1">[27]</xref>
###xml 465 480 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
While exposure to a TLR agonist and IFN-gamma can have synergistic effects and enhance activation of some IFN-gamma-induced genes [15], a number of studies have shown that LPS [16], [17], whole mycobacteria [18]-[22], the mycobacterial lipoglycan phosphatidylinositol mannan [23], and mycobacterial lipoproteins [24], [25] can have inhibitory effects on a subset of IFN-gamma-induced genes. This appears to be of special relevance in the context of infections with M. tuberculosis where, even in the presence of a strong adaptive immune response, clearance of bacteria from the infected tissue is not achieved [6], [26], [27].
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 60 75 56 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 143 147 139 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Fortune1">[24]</xref>
###xml 160 164 156 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Kincaid2">[28]</xref>

###xml 434 438 430 434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Banaiee1">[23]</xref>
###xml 551 552 547 548 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 561 565 557 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Steimle1">[30]</xref>
###xml 595 599 591 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Kincaid1">[18]</xref>
###xml 601 605 597 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pai1">[20]</xref>
###xml 607 611 603 607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Fortune1">[24]</xref>
###xml 613 617 609 613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini1">[25]</xref>
###xml 619 623 615 619 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini2">[31]</xref>
###xml 791 792 783 784 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 795 796 787 788 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 930 931 922 923 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 940 944 932 936 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Loetscher1">[32]</xref>
###xml 1030 1034 1022 1026 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Cole1">[33]</xref>
###xml 1068 1083 1060 1075 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 1094 1098 1086 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Fuller1">[34]</xref>
###xml 60 75 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1068 1083 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Although inhibition of IFN-gamma-induced gene expression by M. tuberculosis occurs by both TLR2-dependent and -independent mechanisms in vitro [24] and in vivo [28], we focused on the contribution of TLR2 signaling to inhibition in the experiments reported here. Pam3CSK4, a synthetic triacylated hexapeptide and specific TLR2/1 ligand [29], has been found to mimic the inhibitory effects of mycobacterial lipoproteins in macrophages [23]. Inhibition of class II transactivator (CIITA), a gene required for antigen presentation via MHC class II to CD4+ T cells [30], has been well characterized [18], [20], [24], [25], [31]. We wanted to extend these findings and compare inhibition of CIITA with that of CXCL11, another IFN-gamma-inducible gene that we found to be strongly inhibited by Pam3CSK4 through microarray analysis. CXCL11 is a member of the CXC chemokine family and ligand for CXCR3, which is expressed on activated CD4+ T cells [32]. CXCL11 acts as a chemoattractant to recruit these cells to the site of inflammation [33]. Although studied during chronic M. tuberculosis infection [34], the role of CXCL11 during early infection is unknown.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
We found that TLR2 inhibition of IFN-gamma-induced transcription of CXCL11 and CIITA required new protein synthesis, but that inhibition of each of these genes involved distinct downstream mechanisms.

###xml 1030 1034 998 1002 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pai2">[21]</xref>
###xml 1036 1040 1004 1008 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Fortune1">[24]</xref>
###xml 1059 1060 1027 1028 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 1063 1064 1031 1032 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 1153 1160 1117 1124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g001">Fig. 1C</xref>
###xml 41 56 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 984 999 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
In the context of initial infection with M. tuberculosis, lung macrophages are likely to encounter the bacteria before being stimulated by IFN-gamma. Therefore, we studied the effects of TLR2 stimulation by Pam3CSK4 prior to stimulation of macrophages with IFN-gamma. We first analyzed the kinetics of TLR2-initiated inhibition of IFN-gamma induction of CXCL11 and CIITA. Bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDM) were treated with Pam3CSK4 for 8 h then stimulated with IFN-gamma for 4, 8, and 12 h. Although CIITA and CXCL11 were both induced by IFN-gamma, the kinetics of their induction differed. CXCL11 mRNA levels peaked after 4 h of IFN-gamma stimulation whereas CIITA mRNA peaked after 12 h (Figs. 1A and B). We found that Pam3CSK4 treatment prior to IFN-gamma stimulation enhanced transcription of NOS2 in macrophages (Fig. 1C). These data show that TLR2 stimulation affects IFN-gamma-induced transcription in a gene-specific manner.

###xml 129 133 125 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Banaiee1">[23]</xref>

We extended these findings and examined the effect of cycloheximide, a pharmacological inhibitor of protein synthesis, on induction of CXCL11, CIITA, and NOS2 mRNA by IFN-gamma in TLR2 stimulated macrophages. ###begin title 40
TLR2 stimulation prevents RNA polymerase II from binding the CXCL11 and CIITA promoters
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 236 237 232 233 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 240 241 236 237 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 741 748 729 736 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g004">Fig. 4A</xref>

###xml 1325 1326 1289 1290 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 1329 1330 1293 1294 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 1384 1391 1348 1355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g004">Fig. 4A</xref>
###xml 1465 1472 1429 1436 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g001">Fig. 1C</xref>
To further characterize the mechanism of inhibition of transcriptional responses to IFN-gamma by prior TLR2 stimulation, we determined whether RNA polymerase II (PolII) binds the promoters of the affected genes. We treated BMDM with Pam3CSK4 for 8 h then stimulated with IFN-gamma for 4 h, followed by chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) to assay binding of PolII to the CXCL11, CIITA, and NOS2 promoters. Primers that specifically amplified regions flanking the transcriptional start site of each gene were designed to detect initial binding of PolII to the promoters. IFN-gamma stimulation induced binding of PolII to the CXCL11 and CIITA promoters, as indicated by a three-fold increase in pulldown of promoter fragments of these genes (Fig. 4A). However, stimulation of TLR2 prior to IFN-gamma inhibited binding of PolII by 66% (CXCL11) and 76% (CIITA) compared with IFN-gamma alone. The effect of TLR2 stimulation was not merely to delay IFN-gamma-stimulated PolII binding at these promoters, as similar inhibition of binding was seen after 8 and 12 h of IFN-gamma stimulation (data not shown). IFN-gamma stimulation alone caused a two-fold increase in binding and prior Pam3CSK4 treatment resulted in a further increase in binding (Fig. 4A). This was similar to the effect observed at the level of transcription (Fig. 1C). These results indicate that TLR2 stimulation prevents PolII from binding to the CXCL11 and CIITA promoters, but does not affect binding to the NOS2 promoter. The effect of TLR2 stimulation on transcription of IFN-gamma-responsive genes correlates with PolII binding at the respective promoters.

###begin title 44
TLR2 stimulation prevents binding of TBP to the CXCL11 promoter
###end title 44
###begin p 45
###xml 310 311 310 311 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 314 315 314 315 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>

Since we found that TLR2 stimulation prevents PolII from binding the CXCL11 and CIITA promoters, we determined whether another member of the general transcriptional machinery was similarly affected. We examined the ability of TATA binding protein (TBP) to bind the TATA box in the CXCL11 promoter following Pam3CSK4 and IFN-gamma stimulation. This suggests that the lack of transcription is not due to a deficiency in PolII binding alone, but that prolonged TLR2 signaling also inhibits binding of other members of the general transcriptional machinery.

TLR2 signaling inhibits histone acetylation at the CIITA promoter, but not at the CXCL11 promoter

###xml 320 324 316 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini1">[25]</xref>
###xml 525 526 517 518 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 529 530 521 522 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 864 878 844 858 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g005">Figs. 5A and D</xref>
###xml 1169 1183 1137 1151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g005">Figs. 5B and E</xref>
###xml 1378 1392 1338 1352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g005">Figs. 5C and F</xref>
Transcriptional activity is usually associated with increased acetylation of histones H3 and H4 in the promoter region of transcribed genes [36], and TLR2 stimulation by the mycobacterial lipoprotein LpqH inhibits acetylation of histones H3 and H4 at the CIITA promoter after 4 h of IFN-gamma stimulation in macrophages [25]. We extended these findings by examining earlier IFN-gamma time points and by determining whether TLR2 stimulation also inhibited histone acetylation at the CXCL11 promoter. BMDM were treated with Pam3CSK4 for 8 h followed by IFN-gamma for 1, 2, 4, and 8 h. ChIPs were then performed using antibodies against either acetylated histone H3 or acetylated histone H4. At the CXCL11 promoter, we observed a rapid increase in acetylation of both histones after 1 h of IFN-gamma stimulation, which persisted for as long as IFN-gamma was present (Figs. 5A and D). TLR2 stimulation prior to IFN-gamma did not inhibit histone acetylation at the CXCL11 promoter at any time point after IFN-gamma stimulation. In contrast, TLR2 stimulation significantly inhibited histone H3 and H4 acetylation at the CIITA promoter by 35-63% at all IFN-gamma time points (Figs. 5B and E). For comparison, we also examined histone acetylation at the NOS2 promoter. TLR2 stimulation prior to IFN-gamma resulted in an increase of histone acetylation over IFN-gamma stimulation alone (Figs. 5C and F). These data indicate that TLR2-mediated inhibition of IFN-gamma induction of CIITA and CXCL11 occurs by at least two distinct mechanisms, one that affects histone acetylation and one that does not.

###xml 1073 1080 1049 1056 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g006">Fig. 6C</xref>

Transcriptional responses to TLR2 activation are largely mediated by the transcription factor NF-kappaB, a heterodimer commonly consisting of the RelA and p50 subunits. RelA deficiency is embryonic lethal but can be rescued by deletion of TNF [37], [38]. BMDM were treated with Pam3CSK4 for 8 h followed by IFN-gamma for 4 h. TLR2 stimulation prior to IFN-gamma inhibited induction of CXCL11 and CIITA in RelA+/+ macrophages (Figs. 6A and B). However, lack of TNF did result in lower NOS2 expression that was further decreased in RelA-/- macrophages (Fig. 6C). These data provide further support that CXCL11 and CIITA are differentially regulated upon TLR2 stimulation. Inhibition of CXCL11 induction requires NF-kappaB whereas inhibition of CIITA does not.

###begin title 54
Discussion
###end title 54
###begin p 55
###xml 0 15 0 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 86 90 82 86 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Douvas1">[39]</xref>
###xml 92 96 88 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Rook1">[40]</xref>
###xml 128 143 124 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 263 267 251 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Kincaid1">[18]</xref>
###xml 269 273 257 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pai1">[20]</xref>
###xml 274 278 262 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Ting1">[22]</xref>
###xml 415 419 403 407 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Fortune1">[24]</xref>
###xml 526 530 514 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Banaiee1">[23]</xref>
###xml 532 536 520 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Fortune1">[24]</xref>
###xml 689 690 677 678 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 693 694 681 682 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 0 15 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 128 143 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
M. tuberculosis survives in macrophages, even when they are stimulated with IFN-gamma [39], [40]. We and others have found that M. tuberculosis blocks selected macrophage responses to IFN-gamma by inhibiting transcription of a subset of IFN-gamma-inducible genes [18], [20]-[22]. At least two proximal mechanisms are involved. One is initiated by mycobacterial peptidoglycan in a TLR2- and MyD88-independent manner [24], while the other requires TLR2 and MyD88 and is initiated by lipoproteins and phosphatidylinositol mannan [23], [24]. In the experiments presented here, we examined the mechanisms downstream of TLR2 in mediating this inhibition, by using the specific TLR2/1 agonist Pam3CSK4.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 86 90 86 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Kincaid1">[18]</xref>
###xml 92 96 92 96 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pai1">[20]</xref>
###xml 98 102 98 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Fortune1">[24]</xref>
###xml 104 108 104 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini1">[25]</xref>
###xml 110 114 110 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini2">[31]</xref>
###xml 300 315 292 307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 315 319 307 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pai2">[21]</xref>
###xml 321 325 313 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Fortune1">[24]</xref>
###xml 483 497 467 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g001">Figs. 1A and B</xref>
###xml 753 759 733 739 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g002">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 826 833 806 813 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g001">Fig. 1C</xref>
###xml 300 315 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
This work expands on previous studies that examined inhibition of CIITA transcription [18], [20], [24], [25], [31], by comparing the kinetics of inhibition of CIITA with that of CXCL11, an unrelated IFN-gamma-inducible gene, as well as with NOS2, an IFN-gamma-inducible gene that is not inhibited by M. tuberculosis[21], [24]. We found that TLR2 stimulation inhibited IFN-gamma-induced transcription of CIITA and CXCL11 to similar levels over a time course of IFN-gamma stimulation (Figs. 1A and B), indicating that macrophages are unable to recover the ability to respond to IFN-gamma, regardless of the length of stimulation. This reduction in transcription resulted in decreased CXCL11 secretion and decreased MHC class II on the macrophage surface (Fig. 2). However, TLR2 stimulation resulted in an increase in NOS2 mRNA (Fig. 1C), indicating that TLR2-mediated transcriptional inhibition is gene specific, and therefore not mediated by inhibition of a proximal signaling step such as STAT1 activation.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 279 283 279 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Lee1">[41]</xref>
One of the initial steps in transcription initiation is acetylation of lysine residues within the N-terminal tails of core histones. This decreases their affinity for DNA, allowing a more permissive chromatin structure for transcription factors and other proteins to bind to DNA [41]. This process is tightly controlled by two counteracting enzymatic activities: the histone deacetylases (HDACs) and the histone acetyltransferases (HATs). HDACs repress transcription by removing acetyl groups whereas HATs acetylate critical lysines.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 99 103 99 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Sengupta1">[42]</xref>
###xml 248 252 248 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Wang1">[43]</xref>
###xml 253 257 253 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Ito1">[47]</xref>
###xml 718 722 718 722 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Hu1">[48]</xref>
###xml 724 728 724 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Lee2">[49]</xref>
###xml 953 957 953 957 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pflum1">[50]</xref>
###xml 959 963 959 963 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Tsai1">[51]</xref>
###xml 1056 1060 1052 1056 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Singh1">[52]</xref>
###xml 87 92 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Mammalian HDACs fall into three classes (I, II, and III) based on sequence homology to yeast HDACs [42]. Class II and III HDACs are expressed in a limited number of tissues. However, HDACs 1 and 2 (of class I) appear to be constitutively expressed [43]-[47]. Therefore, their activity must be tightly regulated. They are the enzymatically active components of multi-protein complexes, which include DNA binding proteins and corepressors. These complexes target the HDACs to the promoters of genes by interactions with sequence-specific transcription factors, leading to transcriptional repression of select genes. HDACs require complex formation for enzymatic activity, as most purified recombinant HDACs are inactive [48], [49]. In addition to regulation by protein-protein interactions, HDACs can be post-translationally modified. Casein kinase 2 (CK2), a ubiquitously expressed protein kinase, has been identified as a key regulator of class I HDACs [50], [51]. CK2 activity is induced by a number of stimuli including IFN-gamma and the TLR4 ligand LPS [52]. CK2 phosphorylation of S421/S422 and S423/S424 at the C-terminal region of HDACs 1 and 2, respectively, is important for complex formation and enzymatic activity. Although more light is being shed on how HDAC activity is regulated, many of the signaling pathways involved remain to be elucidated.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 270 274 266 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Kalkhove1">[53]</xref>
###xml 479 483 467 471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Zhang1">[54]</xref>
###xml 742 746 726 730 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Kalkhove1">[53]</xref>
###xml 748 752 732 736 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Huang1">[55]</xref>
###xml 823 827 803 807 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Yuan1">[56]</xref>
HATs are a diverse group of enzymes that regulate transcription by rendering the chromatin more accessible via acetylation of histone tails. Gene transcription in response to IFN-gamma involves CREB binding protein (CBP) and/or p300, coactivators that have HAT activity [53]. Following IFN-gamma stimulation, phosphorylated STAT1 associates with CBP/p300, which is thought to facilitate contact with transcriptional machinery at the promoter regions of IFN-gamma-inducible genes [54]. Similar to HDACs, the HAT activity of CBP and p300 is tightly regulated via interactions with other proteins as well as by phosphorylation by a number of kinases. Phosphorylation by p42/p44 MAPK, CDK2, protein kinase A, and IKKalpha upregulate HAT activity [53], [55] whereas phosphorylation by protein kinase Cdelta reduces HAT activity [56].
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 206 210 206 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Gaston1">[57]</xref>
Transcriptional repressors also regulate gene expression. General transcriptional repression occurs when a repressor sequesters or modifies a member of the general transcriptional machinery or PolII itself [57]. Expression of all genes transcribed by PolII will then be inhibited. Gene-specific repression occurs when a repressor targets a specific coactivator or interacts in a promoter-specific manner with members of the general transcriptional machinery or PolII. Therefore, only a subset of genes will be inhibited.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 150 154 146 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini2">[31]</xref>
###xml 704 708 679 683 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Gupta1">[58]</xref>
###xml 789 798 761 770 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="gene">TGCCTGAAG</named-content>
###xml 821 830 793 802 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="gene">TCCTCAGAC</named-content>
###xml 1067 1070 1029 1032 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1167 1171 1129 1133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini2">[31]</xref>
###xml 1435 1439 1393 1397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Litvak1">[59]</xref>
###xml 1527 1531 1481 1485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini2">[31]</xref>
Previously published data suggest that gene-specific repressors may contribute to TLR2-initiated inhibition of transcriptional responses to IFN-gamma [31]. The mycobacterial lipoprotein and TLR2 agonist LpqH induces expression of the transcription factor C/EBPbeta, which can act as a transcriptional activator or repressor, depending on the promoter and stimulus. This increased expression correlated with inhibition of IFN-gamma-induced CIITA transcription, and macrophages stimulated with LpqH and IFN-gamma exhibited increased C/EBPbeta binding to the CIITA promoter. The NOS2 promoter also has a C/EBPbeta binding site that is involved in gene induction in response to TLR and IFN-gamma stimulation [58]. The CXCL11 promoter has two potential C/EBPbeta binding sites from -52 to -44 (TGCCTGAAG) and from -24 to -16 (TCCTCAGAC), although the functionality of these sites remains to be determined. It is therefore possible that C/EBPbeta or a related protein may contribute to negative regulation of IFN-gamma-induction of both CIITA and CXCL11. However, C/EBPbeta-/- macrophages were found to remain sensitive to LpqH-mediated transcriptional inhibition of CIITA [31], suggesting that additional factors are involved. One such factor may be C/EBPdelta, whose expression is induced by TLR4 stimulation and has been shown to regulate genes involved in the innate immune response as part of a circuit with other transcription factors [59]. TLR2 stimulation also induces C/EBPdelta expression and binding to the CIITA promoter [31]. However, the potential inhibitory function of C/EBPdelta on IFN-gamma-induced transcription needs to be explored.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 202 203 198 199 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 206 207 202 203 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
We attempted to identify other potential transcription factor binding sites that might be responsible for TLR2-mediated inhibition by comparing the promoter sequences of several IFN-gamma-inducible, Pam3CSK4-inhibited genes. Computer-based comparison of these promoter sequences with those from a control group of unaffected, IFN-gamma-inducible genes did not yield an over-represented transcription factor binding motif, indicating that more than one signaling pathway is involved, or that inhibition is mediated by one or proteins that do not bind promoter elements directly (data not shown).
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 135 145 131 141 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="gene">GGGGAATTCC</named-content>
###xml 273 276 265 268 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 362 368 350 356 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g006">Fig. 6</xref>
###xml 762 769 746 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g003">Fig. 3A</xref>
However, when specifically examining the CXCL11 and CIITA promoters, we found that CXCL11 has an NF-kappaB binding site at -68 to -59 (GGGGAATTCC) that is missing in CIITA. Further investigation of the role of NF-kappaB in TLR2-mediated inhibition of these genes using RelA-/- macrophages showed that inhibition of CXCL11, but not CIITA, is NF-kappaB dependent (Fig. 6). We could not detect binding of p65 or p50 to this site in the CXCL11 promoter (data not shown), suggesting that NF-kappaB most likely has an indirect inhibitory effect, possibly by inducing expression of a protein that blocks CXCL11 transcription upon TLR2 stimulation. The reversal of transcriptional inhibition seen when new protein synthesis is blocked is concordant with this mechanism (Fig. 3A).
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 205 209 201 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini1">[25]</xref>
###xml 211 215 207 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Wang1">[43]</xref>
###xml 424 428 412 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Pennini1">[25]</xref>
###xml 242 248 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
We also examined potential epigenetic mechanisms as TLR2 stimulation has been shown to inhibit IFN-gamma-induced histone acetylation at the promoters of genes involved in MHC class II antigen presentation [25], [43]. ChIP experiments done in murine macrophages stimulated with LpqH followed by IFN-gamma showed that acetylation of histones H3 and H4 was reduced at the CIITA promoter compared to IFN-gamma stimulation alone [25]. This inhibition was abrogated with pharmacological blockade of MAPKs. In addition, this inhibition was partially reversed in the presence of the HDAC inhibitor sodium butyrate, suggesting that inhibition of histone acetylation is one mechanism by which TLR2 stimulation prevents CIITA expression.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 267 280 259 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g005">Fig. 5B and E</xref>
###xml 362 375 354 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g005">Fig. 5A and D</xref>
###xml 494 507 486 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g005">Fig. 5C and F</xref>
We elaborated on those experiments to determine if inhibition of histone acetylation was a common mechanism for TLR2-mediated inhibition of other IFN-gamma-inducible genes. In contrast to the inhibition of IFN-gamma-induced histone acetylation at the CIITA promoter (Fig. 5B and E), histone acetylation at the CXCL11 promoter was unaffected by TLR2 stimulation (Fig. 5A and D). Concordant with the transcriptional data, histone acetylation at the NOS2 promoter increased with TLR2 stimulation (Fig. 5C and F). This indicates that a decrease in modifications that make the chromatin more accessible to transcription factors and coactivators may be involved, but this is not the sole mechanism responsible for TLR2-mediated inhibition of transcriptional responses to IFN-gamma.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 284 291 276 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006329-g004">Fig. 4A</xref>
In contrast to the gene-selective requirement for NF-kappaB and different effects of TLR2 stimulation on histone acetylation, we found that TLR2 stimulation decreased IFN-gamma-induced binding of RNA polymerase II at both the CIITA and CXCL11 promoters, but not at the NOS2 promoter (Fig. 4A). This indicates that while the intermediate signaling steps may vary for distinct genes, TLR2 stimulation interrupts a crucial step in transcription initiation at specific IFN-gamma-responsive genes.
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 15 30 15 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 174 196 174 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Listeria monocytogenes</italic>
###xml 316 320 316 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Hamon1">[60]</xref>
###xml 349 368 349 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium avium</italic>
###xml 541 545 537 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Wang1">[43]</xref>
###xml 15 30 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 174 196 <span type="species:ncbi:1639">Listeria monocytogenes</span>
###xml 349 368 <span type="species:ncbi:1764">Mycobacterium avium</span>
In addition to M. tuberculosis, other pathogens have developed mechanisms to evade immune responses through disruption of host gene transcription. The intracellular bacteria Listeria monocytogenes induces a reduction in total cellular histone acetylation early after infection, mediated partially by listeriolysin O [60]. The opportunistic pathogen Mycobacterium avium inhibits histone acetylation at the HLA-DRalpha promoter, possibly through recruitment of HDAC corepressor mSin3a, which was found to bind the promoter following infection [43]. In those studies, infection also led to a reduction in CBP recruitment to the HLA-DRalpha promoter.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 162 166 162 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Weidman1">[61]</xref>
###xml 229 233 229 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-LeMay1">[62]</xref>
###xml 305 309 305 309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Yuan2">[63]</xref>
###xml 521 525 517 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006329-Popkin1">[64]</xref>
###xml 113 123 <span type="species:ncbi:138953">Poliovirus</span>
###xml 239 265 <span type="species:ncbi:11276">vesicular stomatitis virus</span>
###xml 367 389 <span type="species:ncbi:10366">murine cytomegalovirus</span>
Viruses also disrupt host gene transcription by preventing transcriptional machinery assembly at gene promoters. Poliovirus cleaves general transcription factors [61], Rift Valley Fever virus blocks transcription factor assembly [62], and vesicular stomatitis virus targets TBP using an unknown mechanism [63]. These all prevent general PolII transcription. However, murine cytomegalovirus has been shown to inhibit specific gene transcription by targeting IFN-gamma-inducible genes without affecting JAK/STAT activation [64]. PolII binding to the promoters of the affected genes was significantly reduced, suggesting that disruption of transcriptional machinery assembly was responsible for this inhibition.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 113 139 113 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 309 324 309 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 113 139 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 309 324 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
The findings reported here extend the understanding of the mechanisms that may be used by at least one pathogen, Mycobacterium tuberculosis, to evade elimination by adaptive immune responses. Developing the means to increase the efficacy of adaptive immune responses in order to better control infection with M. tuberculosis will require additional investigation; increasing the efficacy of IFN-gamma by restoring macrophage transcriptional responses to this cytokine may be one effective approach, but will require further study.
###end p 69
