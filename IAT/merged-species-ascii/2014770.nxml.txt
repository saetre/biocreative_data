Postembryonic RNAi in Heterorhabditis bacteriophora: a nematode insect parasite and host for insect pathogenic symbionts

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 0 30 0 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Heterorhabditis bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 212 229 212 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 289 313 289 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Photorhabdus luminescens</italic>
###xml 415 440 415 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Photorhabdus luminescens </italic>
###xml 672 682 672 682 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 710 727 710 727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 742 759 742 759 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 0 29 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">Heterorhabditis bacteriophora</span>
###xml 212 228 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 289 313 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">Photorhabdus luminescens</span>
###xml 415 439 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">Photorhabdus luminescens</span>
###xml 672 682 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 710 726 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 742 758 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
Heterorhabditis bacteriophora is applied throughout the world for the biological control of insects and is an animal model to study interspecies interactions, e.g. mutualism, parasitism and vector-borne disease. H. bacteriophora nematodes are mutually associated with the insect pathogen, Photorhabdus luminescens. The developmentally arrested infective juvenile (IJ) stage nematode (vector) specifically transmits Photorhabdus luminescens bacteria (pathogen) in its gut mucosa to the haemocoel of insects (host). The nematode vector and pathogen alone are not known to cause insect disease. RNA interference is an excellent reverse genetic tool to study gene function in C. elegans, and it would be useful in H. bacteriophora to exploit the H. bacteriophora genome project, currently in progress.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 17 34 17 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 67 78 67 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 334 351 334 351 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 355 366 355 366 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 479 496 479 496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 530 539 530 539 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hba-cct-2</italic>
###xml 541 551 541 551 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hba-daf-21</italic>
###xml 553 562 553 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hba-icd-1</italic>
###xml 564 573 564 573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hba-nol-5</italic>
###xml 579 590 579 590 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hba-W01G7.3</italic>
###xml 636 647 636 647 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hba-rack-1 </italic>
###xml 651 660 651 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hba-arf-1</italic>
###xml 688 699 688 699 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 767 777 767 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 829 845 829 845 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora</italic>
###xml 17 33 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 67 77 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 334 350 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 355 365 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 479 495 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 688 698 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 767 777 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 829 845 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
Soaking L1 stage H. bacteriophora with seven dsRNAs of genes whose C. elegans orthologs had severe RNAi phenotypes resulted in highly penetrant and obvious developmental and reproductive abnormalities. The efficacy of postembryonic double strand RNA interference (RNAi) was evident by abnormal gonad morphology and sterility of adult H. bacteriophora and C. elegans presumable due to defects in germ cell proliferation and gonad development. The penetrance of RNAi phenotypes in H. bacteriophora was high for five genes (87-100%; Hba-cct-2, Hba-daf-21, Hba-icd-1; Hba-nol-5, and Hba-W01G7.3) and moderate for two genes (usually 30-50%; Hba-rack-1 and Hba-arf-1). RNAi of three additional C. elegans orthologs for which RNAi phenotypes were not previously detected in C. elegans, also did not result in any apparent phenotypes in H. bacteriophora. Specific and severe reduction in transcript levels in RNAi treated L1s was determined by quantitative real-time RT-PCR. These results suggest that postembryonic RNAi by soaking is potent and specific.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 143 160 143 160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 354 371 354 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 143 159 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 354 370 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
Although RNAi is conserved in animals and plants, RNAi using long dsRNA is not. These results demonstrate that RNAi can be used effectively in H. bacteriophora and can be applied for analyses of nematode genes involved in symbiosis and parasitism. It is likely that RNAi will be an important tool for functional genomics utilizing the high quality draft H. bacteriophora genome sequence.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 0 30 0 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Heterorhabditis bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 114 138 114 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Photorhabdus luminescens</italic>
###xml 189 190 189 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 499 521 499 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caenorhabditis elegans</italic>
###xml 610 633 610 633 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 657 658 657 658 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 0 29 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">Heterorhabditis bacteriophora</span>
###xml 114 138 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">Photorhabdus luminescens</span>
###xml 499 521 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
###xml 610 633 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
Heterorhabditis bacteriophora is a rhabditid entomopathogenic nematode (EPN) symbiotic with the enteric bacterium Photorhabdus luminescens, a dangerous liaison lethal to many insect hosts [1]. EPNs are applied globally for the biological control of insects. The nematode is also a potentially powerful animal model to study interspecies interactions such as mutualism, parasitism, and vector-borne disease. Genomics and genetics are available for the symbiont, and because it is a close relative to Caenorhabditis elegans, are being developed for the nematode. In addition, well-studied arthropod models (e.g. Drosophila melanogaster) can be used as hosts [2].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 108 123 108 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 237 238 237 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 411 412 411 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 108 122 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
The developmentally arrested infective juvenile (IJ) nematodes exist in soil and transmit an average of 130 P. luminescens bacteria in their gut mucosa, sometimes for several months, before locating and infecting a suitable insect host [3]. The IJs search, locate and penetrate the insect exoskeleton or intestine, sometimes by using a buccal tooth, and then regurgitate the bacteria into the haemocoel (Figure 1). The association is an obligate mutualism since both the nematode and bacterium are required for insect pathogenicity.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 14 44 14 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Heterorhabditis bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 0 62 0 62 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Life-cycle of <italic>Heterorhabditis bacteriophora </italic>(adapted from [1])</bold>
###xml 175 190 175 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 204 219 204 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 551 566 551 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescence</italic>
###xml 655 669 649 663 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens</italic>
###xml 14 43 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">Heterorhabditis bacteriophora</span>
###xml 175 189 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 204 218 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 655 669 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
Life-cycle of Heterorhabditis bacteriophora (adapted from [1]). The non-feeding developmentally arrested dauer or infective juvenile (IJ) transmits a monoculture of symbiotic P. luminescens (GFP-labelled P. luminescens are shown) to the haemocoel of an insect host, where it regurgitates the bacteria. The bacteria rapidly kill the insect (usually <48) and grow to high densities allowing nematode growth and reproduction (The lower panel is an image taken from the bioluminescence of the bacteria). The nematodes grow for 2-3 generations, feeding on P. luminescence, after which (~10 d at 28degreesC) IJs are formed en masse, most transmitting symbiotic P. luminescens.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 193 194 193 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 210 225 210 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 298 301 298 301 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50 </sub>
###xml 469 470 469 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 779 780 779 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 781 782 781 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 210 224 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
The nematode is dependent on symbiotic bacteria for insect pathogenicity and nematode growth and reproduction since axenic IJs do not cause insect mortality nor develop and reproduce normally [4]. In contrast, P. luminescens are highly virulent when injected into the insect haemocoel, having an LD50 <30 cells for many insect hosts, but are dependent on the IJs for transmission and persistence outside of insect hosts. After the bacteria are regurgitated by the IJs [3], insect mortality rapidly ensues (usually <48 h) and the bacteria grow to high cell densities and produce insecticidal toxins, secondary metabolites to inhibit saprophytic microbes, bacteriophagous nematodes and savaging insects. In addition, the bacteria are required for nematode growth and reproduction [4,5].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 16 33 16 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 126 127 126 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 257 271 257 271 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens</italic>
###xml 363 378 363 378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 16 32 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 257 271 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 363 377 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
Reproduction of H. bacteriophora is heterogonic: both automictic hermaphrodites and amphimictic females and males are formed [6]. The IJs are hermaphroditic, but generate automictic and amphimictic progeny that grow and reproduce for several generations on P. luminescens, after which, the hermaphroditic IJ stage is formed. The IJs are specifically colonized by P. luminescens and transmit the bacteria to new insect hosts.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 0 30 0 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Heterorhabditis bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 52 63 52 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 206 221 206 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 294 305 294 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 339 372 339 372 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora, P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 0 29 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">Heterorhabditis bacteriophora</span>
###xml 52 62 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 206 220 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 294 304 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 339 355 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 357 371 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
Heterorhabditis bacteriophora is closely related to C. elegans (Rhabditidae) and can be propagated outside of insect hosts on agar based media and in liquid culture provided that a monoculture of symbiotic P. luminescens is present. We sought to further utilize the techniques and knowledge of C. elegans to study the interactions between H. bacteriophora, P. luminescens and insects.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 125 135 125 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 215 216 215 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 217 218 217 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 263 264 263 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 265 267 265 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 309 311 309 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 339 350 339 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 357 359 357 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 379 381 379 381 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 382 384 382 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 396 398 396 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 415 417 415 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 484 501 484 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 652 668 652 668 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora</italic>
###xml 745 762 745 762 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 772 783 772 783 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 865 875 865 875 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 931 947 931 947 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora</italic>
###xml 1001 1012 1001 1012 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 1071 1073 1071 1073 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 1224 1240 1224 1240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora</italic>
###xml 125 135 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 339 349 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 484 500 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 652 668 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>

One of these techniques is targeted gene silencing by RNAi, a powerful molecular genetic tool to elucidate gene function. In C. elegans, RNAi was originally performed by injecting dsRNA into the body of L4 animals [7,8] and also shown to be effective by soaking [9,10], on lawns of bacteria expressing dsRNA [11] or by expressing dsRNA in C. elegans cells [12]. RNAi by feeding [13,14], soaking [15] and injection [16] are amenable to high throughput methodologies. The dependence of H. bacteriophora for symbiotic bacteria for growth and reproduction makes RNAi by feeding problematic. Therefore, we developed a soaking protocol for efficient RNAi in H. bacteriophora. Using this protocol we detected highly penetrant and obvious phenotypes in H. bacteriophora for seven C. elegans orthologs, previously reported to have highly penetrant and obvious phenotypes in C. elegans. This is the first demonstration of RNAi by soaking in H. bacteriophora, a technique difficult for many nematodes other than C. elegans perhaps due to inefficient uptake of environmental dsRNA [[17], M. Montgomery pers. comm.]. This technique is amenable to high-throughput methodologies and should greatly enhance the analysis of gene function in H. bacteriophora.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Results and discussion
###end title 16
###begin title 17
###xml 37 53 37 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora</italic>
###xml 37 53 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
Optimization of RNAi methodology for H. bacteriophora
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 6 23 6 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 32 47 32 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 118 135 118 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 144 161 144 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 207 221 207 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens</italic>
###xml 301 304 301 304 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cel</italic>
###xml 305 311 305 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pop-1 </italic>
###xml 346 357 346 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 386 413 386 413 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens. C. elegans </italic>
###xml 454 463 454 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cel-pop-1</italic>
###xml 581 596 581 596 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 661 678 661 678 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 753 768 753 768 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 837 851 837 851 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens</italic>
###xml 929 945 929 945 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora</italic>
###xml 6 22 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 32 46 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 118 134 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 144 166 <span type="species:ncbi:634469">Escherichia coli HT115</span>
###xml 168 170 <span type="species:ncbi:634469">DE</span>
###xml 207 221 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 346 356 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 386 400 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 402 412 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 581 595 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 661 682 <span type="species:ncbi:637912">Escherichia coli OP50</span>
###xml 753 767 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 837 851 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 929 945 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
Since H. bacteriophora requires P. luminescens for growth and reproduction, one strategy for RNAi might be to express H. bacteriophora dsRNA in Escherichia coli HT115 (DE) delivered while in co-culture with P. luminescens. We tested the validity of this approach by determining if highly penetrant ds Cel-pop-1 RNA, retains silencing activity in C. elegans when grown in co-cultures of P. luminescens. C. elegans L4 grown on lawns of HT115 expressing ds Cel-pop-1RNA resulted in embryonic lethality (Emb) with 100% penetrance. However, silencing was abolished when as little as 2% P. luminescens were added (0% Emb) and to a lesser extent in the presence of 2% Escherichia coli OP50 (data not shown). Therefore, the strategy to deliver dsRNA as a HT110-P. luminescens co-culture was not feasible, presumably due to nucleases produced by P. luminescens. We then attempted to use a soaking methodology to deliver dsRNA for RNAi in H. bacteriophora.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 11 28 11 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 377 391 377 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens</italic>
###xml 461 493 461 493 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora-P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 11 27 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 377 391 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 461 477 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 478 492 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
Soaking L1 H. bacteriophora in M9 buffer resulted in high lethality (>90%). Survival of L1 larvae was improved in S-basal media and found to be optimal in Ringer's solution buffered with HEPES (pH 6.9). Initial experiments soaking L1 larvae for 24 h in Ringer's solution sometimes resulted in significant adult sterility. However, using L1s harvested from pure monocultures of P. luminescens, the adult nematodes were fertile. Thus, a pure monoxenic culture of H. bacteriophora-P. luminescens is important for survival and development of the nematodes during and after RNAi.
###end p 19

The C. elegans cct-2 (T21B10.1) gene encodes a component of eukaryotic T-complex chaperonin (CCT or TRiC)[18]. CCT is a group II chaperonin of similar structure to the prokaryotic GroEL chaperonin, but found only in Eukarya and Archea [19]. CCT/TRiC is required to fold actin [20], tubulin [21], cyclin E1 [22] and also 10% of newly synthesized cytoplasmic proteins [23], including proteins with WD40 domains [24]. In C. elegans, mec-3 independent expression of cct-2 touch receptors has also been demonstrated, suggesting a role for CCT in touch receptor function, possibly through microtubule assembly [25].

In C. elegans, published RNAi experiments of cct-2 resulted in embryonic lethal (Emb) and sterile (Ste) phenotypes [13,14] and sometimes a protruding vulva (Pvl)(Table 1)[14]. RNAi by feeding L1 C. elegans with bacteria expressing ds Cel-cct-2 RNA resulted in a highly penetrant Ste phenotype with Pvl also observed (Table 2). Soaking L1 H. bacteriophora in ds Hba-cct-2 RNA resulted in a highly penetrant (100% in 4 trials, 96% and 94% in two other trials) Ste phenotype (Table 2). The cause of sterility was likely due to defective gonad development and absence of germ cells (Figure 2E, F). Mature H. bacteriophora hermaphrodites which lack gonads have an empty or transparent appearance due to a void space in the pseudocoelom normally occupied by the gonad (Figure 2E, F). Both C. elegans and H. bacteriophora treated with their respective ds cct-2 RNA were sterile and had abnormal gonads, although only C. elegans had a protruded vulva (Table 2). The comparable penetrances of these phenotypes suggest that RNAi by soaking in H. bacteriophora is nearly as efficient as by feeding in C. elegans.

The C. elegans daf-21(C47E8.5) gene encodes a member of the Hsp90 family of molecular chaperones important for maturation of signal transduction kinases in neurons involved in odorant perception and required for larval development [26]. Hsp90 is also expressed in the gonad and required for germline development [27], but is also expressed somatically during stress as part of the age-1 related aging regulon [28-30]. A published RNAi experiment of daf-21 in C. elegans resulted in Emb and Ste phenotypes [31]. The post-embryonic phenotype of H. bacteriophora treated with ds Hba-daf-21 RNA resulted in sterile animals with abnormal gonad morphology (Figure 2G, H). Like RNAi of Hba-cct-2, RNAi of Hba-daf-21 was highly penetrant (Table 2, 100% for 3 trials and 92%, 82%, 87% each for single trials).

The C. elegans icd-1 (C56C10.8) gene encodes the beta subunit of nascent polypeptide associated complex (betaNAC) associated with the mitochondrial membrane and an inhibitor of apoptosis [32]. Published RNAi experiments of icd-1 in C. elegans resulted in Emb, slow growth and Ste phenotypes (Table 1) [13-16]. We detected highly penetrant Ste, Pvl, and defective gonad morphology phenotypes by feeding C. elegans ds-icd-1 (Table 2, Figure 3). Postembryonic RNAi of H. bacteriophora resulted in Ste phenotype due to an abnormal gonad with little germ cell proliferation (Figure 2I, J). This phenotype was highly penetrant (Table 2, 100% Ste in 3 trials, 98% in 2 trials, and 87% in one trial). We did not observe apoptosis in H. bacteriophora, and we were unable to generate sufficient males to observe defects in male tale rays, phenotypes observed in C. elegans [32].

The C. elegans W01B11.3 gene, recently named nol-5 (Jonathan Hodgkin, personal communication), is predicted to encode an ortholog of the ribosome biogenesis protein Nop58p/Nop5p and is likely essential for information processing. Published RNAi experiments of Cel-nol-5 usually resulted in larval arrest and Emb phenotypes, with multivulva, protruding vulva, slow growth, maternal sterility and sterile progeny phenotypes also observed [14,31]. RNAi by feeding ds Cel-nol-5 RNA resulted in Pvl, Ste with abnormal gonad development phenotypes (Table 2, Figure 3E, F). RNAi of Hba-nol-5 in H. bacteriophora resulted in a Ste phenotype with abnormal gonads (Figure 2K, L), which was highly penetrant (Table 2, 100% two trials, 95%, 93%, 72% and 57% in four other trials).

RNAi of mrp-4 in C. elegans resulted in no observed phenotype [13,16]. To determine the extent of RNAi silencing in H. bacteriophora, quantitative real-time RT-qPCR experiments were performed. One of our goals for developing RNAi in H. bacteriophora is to use this technique along with imminent high quality draft (6x coverage) H. bacteriophora genome sequence (R. Wilson, personal communication), to identify genes involved in symbiotic host-bacterial interactions. Many of the genes silenced have essential functions in the nematode and we sought to determine if these RNAi treated animals have defects in symbiotic host-bacterial interactions. RNAi treated worms for all 10 genes described above were reared on GFP-labeled P. luminescens and observed for bacterial colonization in the adult nematode intestine. No difference was seen in the presence of GFP-labeled P. luminescens in the intestines of RNAi treated and untreated worms (see additional file 2). This observation suggests that specific genes involved in symbiotic host-bacterial interactions can be identified using RNAi.

###begin title 51
Conclusion
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 127 137 127 137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 208 219 208 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 284 293 284 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cel-sid-1</italic>
###xml 336 338 336 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 339 341 339 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 357 367 357 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cel-sid-2 </italic>
###xml 416 427 416 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 438 440 438 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 487 496 487 496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hba-sid-1</italic>
###xml 516 533 516 533 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 534 536 534 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 623 639 623 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora</italic>
###xml 1053 1060 1053 1060 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">unc-22 </italic>
###xml 1101 1112 1101 1112 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 1113 1115 1113 1115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 1164 1175 1164 1175 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hba-unc-22 </italic>
###xml 1199 1210 1199 1210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hba-unc-22 </italic>
###xml 1241 1258 1241 1258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 1300 1317 1300 1317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 127 137 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 208 218 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 416 426 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 516 532 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 623 639 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 1101 1111 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 1241 1257 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 1300 1316 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
Gene silencing by RNAi is a powerful reverse genetic tool to study gene function. Although, RNAi using long dsRNA is robust in C. elegans, it is inefficient in many other nematodes. The robustness of RNAi in C. elegans can be partially attributed to the systemic RNAi defective gene, Cel-sid-1, involved in the transport of long dsRNA [41,42] and recently, Cel-sid-2 required for efficient transport of dsRNA in the C. elegans intestine [17]. Recently, the systemic RNAi defective gene, Hba-sid-1, was identified in H. bacteriophora [43] which might partially explain the obvious phenotypes and high penetrances of RNAi in H. bacteriophora. Because the nematode egg yolk is synthesized by intestinal cells, it is possible that the Ste and gonad defective phenotypes are caused by RNAi of only the nematode intestinal cells. However, the low amounts of transcript levels detected in L1s following RNAi suggests that RNAi is nearly systemic. This hypothesis could be tested, in principle, by performing RNAi on muscle tissue, for example by silencing the unc-22 gene conferring a twitching phenotype to C. elegans [44]. Attempts to use degenerate primers to amplify Hba-unc-22 were not successful and Hba-unc-22 has not been found in existing H. bacteriophora EST datasets. After the completion of the H. bacteriophora genome, this and many other RNAi experiments will be performed.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 95 111 95 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora</italic>
###xml 551 566 551 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. luminescens </italic>
###xml 815 831 815 831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora</italic>
###xml 95 111 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 551 565 <span type="species:ncbi:29488">P. luminescens</span>
###xml 815 831 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
This study demonstrates that RNAi by soaking is an efficient methodology for gene silencing in H. bacteriophora, which can be applied to study many aspects of the unique biology including parasitism and mutualism. One limitation to this methodology is the development of RNAi treated nematodes to adults versus the infective juvenile stage, the later which transmits the symbiotic bacteria and are insect parasitic. The lack of IJ development from RNAi treated larvae is likely due to the large amount of food signal provided by the confluent lawn of P. luminescens on which the L1s are placed. The density of L1 larvae might also influence the development of the L1 larvae to the IJ stage. The severe and highly penetrant phenotypes observed here suggests that RNAi will be a useful tool to study gene function in H. bacteriophora, i.e. related to symbiont transmission, parasitism, sex determination, stress resistance and infective juvenile formation.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 125 127 125 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 320 329 320 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 392 409 392 409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora </italic>
###xml 490 507 490 507 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Globodara padilla</italic>
###xml 576 578 576 578 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 676 692 676 692 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. bacteriophora</italic>
###xml 392 408 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
###xml 469 475 <span type="species:ncbi:4113">potato</span>
###xml 676 692 <span type="species:ncbi:37862">H. bacteriophora</span>
More generally, mixed results concerning the efficiency of RNAi have been reported in several clades of parasitic nematodes [45]. Problems encountered using RNAi in parasitic nematodes includes non-target effects of dsRNA, variable efficiencies of RNAi with regard to the target gene silenced and dsRNA preparation, and in vitro cultivation. These problems appear to be less of a concern for H. bacteriophora due to the high specificity and penetrances of RNAi. In the potato cyst nematode Globodara padilla, RNAi of neuronal FMRF amide-like peptides was surprisingly robust [46]. The results reported here are promising to the applicability of RNAi to study gene function in H. bacteriophora. However, further refinements of the RNAi methodology are expected when targeting diverse genes expressed in different tissues, when applied to nematodes of different developmental stages and when applied to larger gene sets.
