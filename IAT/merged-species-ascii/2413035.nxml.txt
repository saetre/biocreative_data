In vivo equilibrium of proinflammatory IL-17+ and regulatory IL-10+ Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells

###xml 639 643 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The nuclear hormone receptor retinoic acid receptor-related orphan receptor gammat (RORgammat) is required for the generation of T helper 17 cells expressing the proinflammatory cytokine interleukin (IL)-17. In vivo, however, less than half of RORgammat+ T cells express IL-17. We report here that RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells include Foxp3+ cells that coexist with IL-17-producing RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells in all tissues examined. The Foxp3+ RORgammat+ Talphabeta express IL-10 and CCL20, and function as regulatory T cells. Furthermore, the ratio of Foxp3+ to IL-17-producing RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells remains remarkably constant in mice enduring infection and inflammation. This equilibrium is tuned in favor of IL-10 production by Foxp3 and CCL20, and in favor of IL-17 production by IL-6 and IL-23. In the lung and skin, the largest population of RORgammat+ T cells express the gammadelta T cell receptor and produce the highest levels of IL-17 independently of IL-6. Thus, potentially antagonistic proinflammatory IL-17-producing and regulatory Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells coexist and are tightly controlled, suggesting that a perturbed equilibrium in RORgammat+ T cells might lead to decreased immunoreactivity or, in contrast, to pathological inflammation.

###xml 957 958 949 950 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1040 1041 1029 1030 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">8</xref>
###xml 1043 1044 1032 1033 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">9</xref>
T cells adapt immune responses not only to the antigens, but also to the type of pathogen they face. Cell-based pathogens, such as viruses, intracellular bacteria, and tumors evoke a CD4+ Th1 type of response characterized by the production of IFN-gamma. In contrast, extracellular-based pathogens, such as worms and allergens, evoke a Th2 type of response characterized by the production of IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13 (1). Recently, some bacteria and fungi have been found to evoke a Th17 type of response characterized by the production of IL-17 (or IL-17A), IL-17F, IL-21, IL-22, IL-6, and TNF-alpha, as well as IL-23 by APCs (2-6). The proinflammatory Th17 response induces the recruitment of neutrophils and is involved in autoimmune inflammatory diseases. However, the reactivity of T cells is modulated by T regulatory (T reg) cells. These include so-called natural Foxp3+ T reg cells that are induced in the thymus (7) as well as adaptive Foxp3+ or Foxp3- T reg cells that are induced in the periphery and produce TGF-beta and/or IL-10 (8, 9).

###xml 128 132 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 484 488 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 640 644 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1175 1179 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
The generation of these distinct T cell effector types is under control of specific cytokines and transcription factors (2). In mice, TGF-beta is required for the generation of both Th17 and T reg cells. The switch to the Th17 pathway is induced by IL-6 and/or IL-21 (4-6, 10-12), and requires IL-23 for full effector maturation (2, 3). However, TGF-beta together with IL-6 induces CD4+ T cells to produce both IL-17 and IL-10, a population that is not able to induce inflammation in mice (13). In contrast, IL-23 generates proinflammatory cells that produce only IL-17. In accordance with these findings, spontaneous colitis is induced in mice deficient for IL-10 (14), and the inflammatory disease is prevented by concomitant deficiency in IL-23 (15). The immunosuppressive IL-10 can be produced by all types of T cells, including a subset of T reg cells (16), and is induced by IL-27 in Th1 and Th2 cells (17-19). TGF-beta alone induces the T reg cell pathway (20) and is strongly promoted in the gut-associated lymphoid tissues by all-trans retinoic acid (RA) (21, 22). The generation of T reg cells requires the forkhead/winged-helix transcription factor Foxp3 (23-25). Mice deficient in Foxp3 lack natural T reg cells (24) and are highly susceptible to inflammatory disease (26).

###xml 260 266 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 446 450 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 505 513 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The development of inflammatory pathologies, such as experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis (27, 28), an animal model of multiple sclerosis, collagen-induced arthritis (CIA) (29), and inflammatory bowel disease (15, 30), depends on Th17 cells and IL-23. In humans, a coding variant of Il23 confers protection to Crohn's disease (31). The Th17-IL-23 pathway in also involved in resistance to a growing list of bacterial and fungal pathogens in mice (11, 32-35), and Th17 cells are readily isolated from patients with Candida infection (36). However, a comprehensive picture of the class of pathogens targeted by this pathway remains to be drawn. T reg cells, on the other hand, and IL-10 in particular, have been shown to limit inflammation and adaptive immunity to a variety of pathogens (37), as well as to promote long-term T cell memory (38). Collectively, these studies demonstrate the essential role of the Th17 and the T reg cells in immunity, immunoregulation, and immunopathology. They also suggest that a balanced expansion of these Th subsets during immune responses might ensure efficient yet restrained immunity that limits damage to self.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 149 151 141 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">39</xref>
###xml 222 223 210 211 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 271 272 252 253 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 338 339 315 316 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 445 446 415 416 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 639 640 597 598 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 815 816 762 763 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 192 197 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The nuclear hormone receptor RA receptor-related orphan receptor gammat (RORgammat) is a marker for Th17 cells and is required for their generation (39). Using an enhanced GFP (EGFP) reporter mouse, we found that RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells included IL-10-producing Foxp3+ T reg cells. These cells coexisted with IL-17-producing RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells in all tissues examined in an equilibrium regulated by Foxp3 and CCL20 in favor of Foxp3+ cells, and by IL-6 and IL-23 in favor of IL-17-producing cells. Foxp3 was shown to bind to RORgammat, suggesting that it directly regulates RORgammat activity. The equilibrium within RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells was maintained during massive inflammation of the intestine or the lungs, but it was perturbed in cancer. We suggest that the balanced expansion of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cell subsets during inflammation might ensure efficient yet regulated immunity, and that perturbation of this equilibrium might lead to inadequate immune reactivity to tumors, pathogens, or self.

To optimize the visualization of RORgammat+ cells, we have generated transgenic Rorc(gammat)-GfpTG mice expressing EGFP under control of the Rorc(gammat) locus on a bacterial artificial chromosome (BAC) (40). An estimated 5-10 copies of the BAC transgene were integrated, as assessed by quantitative PCR. The expression pattern of EGFP in Rorc(gammat)-GfpTG mice was identical to the one described previously in Rorc(gammat)+/GFP mice carrying one allele of Rorc(gammat) inserted with EGFP (39, 41). Faithful expression of GFP was further confirmed by immunofluorescence histology of GFP and endogenous RORgammat in various organs from Rorc(gammat)-GfpTG mice. In all tissues analyzed, we found a strict correlation between GFP and RORgammat protein expression (not depicted). High expression of GFP was found in fetal lymphoid tissue inducer (LTi) cells, LTi-like cells in the adult intestine, in immature CD4+CD8+ thymocytes (not depicted), and in a subpopulation of peripheral T cells (Fig. 1 A). The highest frequency of RORgammat+ T cells was observed in the intestine, as previously reported (39), which were localized in the lamina propria of the villi, whereas CD3- RORgammat+ cells, i.e., LTi-like cells, were clustered in cryptopatches that are precursor structures to isolated lymphoid follicles (Fig. 1 C) (42). RORgammat+ T cells were also found in all organs or tissues tested, including the LNs, spleen, lung, skin, and bone marrow (Fig. 1 A). Assessing the homogeneity of these populations within different organs, we observed that up to 50% of RORgammat+ T cells in the lung and skin expressed the gammadelta TCR instead of the alphabeta TCR (Fig. 1 B). RORgammat+ Tgammadelta cells expressed the highest levels of EGFP (Fig. 1 A) and none of the CD4 or CD8 coreceptors (Fig. 1 D).

We assessed whether RORgammat was a marker for IL-17-producing cells in both Talphabeta and Tgammadelta populations, and stained RORgammat+ T cells for IL-17, IFN-gamma, and Foxp3. Surprisingly, even though a majority of IL-17-producing cells expressed RORgammat (Fig. 2 A), only 15-50% of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells expressed IL-17, depending on the organ examined, and few but detectable numbers of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells produced both IL-17 and IFN-gamma (Fig. 2 B). No RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells were found to express both IL-17 and Foxp3, and no RORgammat+ Tgammadelta cells expressed Foxp3 (not depicted). The coexpression of RORgammat and Foxp3 protein at the single cell level was confirmed by immunofluorescence histology of the LNs (Fig. 2 D). Furthermore, Foxp3+ RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells were functionally T reg cells, as they suppressed in vitro proliferation of activated CD4+ T cells (Fig. 2 E).

We further assessed how the generation of IL-17-producing or Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells in vivo was affected by the absence of factors shown to be required for the generation of Th17 or T reg cells, such as IL-6 (4-6, 10-12) and Foxp3 (24, 26). In IL-6-deficient mice, the number and proportion of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells, in particular of IL-17-producing RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells, were significantly decreased, whereas the proportion of IL-10-producing Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells was unaffected (Fig. 4, A, C, and D). This confirms the requirement of IL-6 for the generation of Th17 cells, but also shows that IL-6 is not required for the generation of IL-10-producing RORgammat+ T cells, even though in vitro it acts in synergy with TGF-beta to induce IL-10-producing CD4+ T cells (13, 17). In contrast, IL-17-producing RORgammat+ Tgammadelta cells were unaffected by the absence of IL-6 (Fig. 4 B). Furthermore, in IL-12Rbeta1-deficient mice, unresponsive to both IL-12 and IL-23 (2, 3), the proportion of IL-17-producing RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells was slightly decreased (Fig. 4, C and D), in accordance with the important but not essential role of IL-23 in the generation of Th17 cells (2, 3).

Given the prominent role of IL-17-producing cells in inflammation and of IL-10-producing cells in immune regulation, we assessed if and how the relative frequencies of the different RORgammat+ T cell subsets were modified in mice enduring severe intestinal inflammation, virus infection, or cancer. Dextran sodium sulfate (DSS) was administered orally to Rorc(gammat)-GfpTG mice until the development of chronic intestinal inflammation (Fig. 6 A). A high number of T cells was recovered in DSS-treated mice and both the frequency and the number of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells increased, but the ratio of IL-17-producing to Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells remained constant. Similar results were obtained in mice exposed to intranasal infection with influenza A virus, even though their lungs sustained heavy leukocyte infiltration (Fig. 6 B). In contrast, in Rorc(gammat)-GfpTG x Apcmin/+ mice bearing colonic and ileal polyps and tumors (52), no significant infiltration of leukocyte and RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells was observed in the colon and the terminal ileum (Fig. S7, available at ), whereas the mesenteric LNs were largely hyperplastic. The ratio of IL-17-producing to Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cell in these LNs was significantly decreased, even though the frequency and total number of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells were increased (Fig. 6 C). Similar results have recently been obtained comparing Th17 and Foxp3 cells in mice injected with B16 melanoma (53). These data indicate that the relative frequency of IL-17-producing or Foxp3+ RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells can remain constant during severe inflammation or infection. However, chronic tumors can alter this balance in favor of the generation or recruitment of Foxp3+ RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells producing the immunosuppressive IL-10.

###xml 1890 1892 1790 1792 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">17</xref>
###xml 1904 1905 1800 1801 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 2020 2021 1906 1907 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 2031 2032 1913 1914 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 2146 2147 2028 2029 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 2226 2227 2108 2109 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 2237 2238 2115 2116 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 2313 2314 2180 2181 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 2413 2414 2273 2274 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
Many steps in the differentiation pathway of Th17 cells have been inferred from in vitro studies. We therefore assessed if the distinct subsets of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells we have identified in vivo were derived in vitro from naive CD4+ T cells. TGF-beta together with IL-6 or IL-21 induced the generation of Th17 cells from naive CD4+ T cells (4-6, 10-12) after massive up-regulation of RORgammat expression after 1-2 d of culture (Fig. 7) (39). In contrast, TGF-beta alone induces the generation of T reg cells (20). However, we also observed that most cells up-regulated of RORgammat after 1-2 d of culture in the presence of TGF-beta alone, and all Foxp3+ cells coexpressed RORgammat after 2-3 d. Up-regulation of RORgammat was specific to these conditions because stimulation under Th1 conditions did not induce RORgammat+ expression (Fig. S8, available at ). Expression of RORgammat in the culture of naive CD4+ T cells in the presence of TGF-beta has been shown before at the population level (39), but not in individual cells expressing Foxp3. In contrast to their in vivo counterparts, however, few in vitro-derived Foxp3+ RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells expressed IL-10, which was rather expressed by RORgammat- Talphabeta cells. IL-10 was not induced by TGF-beta alone but required the addition of IL-6, as found previously (13, 17), or the addition of IL-21. IL-10 production was inhibited in the presence of RA, even though RA favored the generation of Foxp3+ RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells and decreased the generation of IL-17-producing cells in the presence of TGF-beta and IL-6. A combination of TGF-beta with IL-6 (with or without RA) or IL-21 generated IL-17-producing as well as IL-10-producing or Foxp3+ RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells, showing that the engagement of these pathways is not mutually exclusive at the cell population level, in agreement with recent reports (13, 17). RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells producing both IL-17 and IL-10 could be generated in the presence of TGF-beta and IL-6, but Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells produced neither IL-10 nor IL-17 (not depicted). Collectively, these data show that even though naive CD4+ T cells differentiate in vitro into IL-17-producing, IL-10-producing, or Foxp3+ RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells, substantial differences exist with the in vivo RORgammat+ Talphabeta cell subsets in the phenotype of the cells producing IL-10, and the proportion of Foxp3+ cells expressing RORgammat.
###end p 31

###begin title 33
DISCUSSION
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 43 44 39 40 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 98 99 94 95 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 214 215 206 207 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 374 375 354 355 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 587 591 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In this study, we have shown that RORgammat+ T cells include both IL-17- and IL-10-producing Foxp3+ T reg cells, which are endowed with proinflammatory and regulatory functions, respectively. In addition, RORgammat+ T cells include Tgammadelta cells producing high levels of IL-17 in an IL-6-independent pathway. The relative frequency of IL-17- or IL-10-producing RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells is maintained constantly during infection or inflammation, suggesting that a robust mechanism maintains an equilibrium between these two effector arms. However, this equilibrium is perturbed in mice harboring intestinal tumors. IL-6, Foxp3, and CCR6, and to a lesser extent IL-23, are essential regulators of this equilibrium.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 15 17 15 17 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">39</xref>
###xml 42 43 38 39 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 229 230 221 222 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 363 364 351 352 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 503 504 487 488 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 712 714 696 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">39</xref>
###xml 763 767 747 751 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Egfp</italic>
###xml 800 812 784 792 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rorc(&#947;t)</italic>
###xml 820 822 800 802 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib41">41</xref>
###xml 909 913 889 893 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Egfp</italic>
###xml 1021 1022 997 998 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1250 1252 1226 1228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib27">27</xref>
###xml 1253 1255 1229 1231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib29">29</xref>
###xml 1257 1259 1233 1235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">39</xref>
###xml 565 569 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 671 675 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 732 737 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 854 859 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1186 1190 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Ivanov et al. (39) reported that RORgammat+ T cells or IL-17-producing cells were only present in the intestinal lamina propria. These authors therefore suggested that the intestinal flora induces the differentiation of RORgammat+ IL-17-producing T cells. We show now that although these cells are more abundant in the intestine, sizeable populations of RORgammat+ T cells were found in all organs examined, including the LNs, spleen, lung, skin, and bone marrow. This extended distribution of RORgammat+ T cells was not due to infection, as specific pathogen-free mice were used in all experiments. This discrepancy might be explained by the sensitivity of the reporter mice that have been used. Ivanov et al. (39) used a knock-in mouse encoding one copy of the Egfp reporter gene inserted into the Rorc(gammat) locus (41), whereas we used a transgenic mouse encoding 5-10 copies of a BAC carrying a similar Egfp insertion and therefore induces a brighter reporter expression. The more extended distribution of RORgammat+ IL-17-producing T cells, in particular in the LNs and spleen, might better explain the occurrence of Th17-dependent inflammatory disease in the brain or joints of mice enduring experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis or CIA (27-29, 39).
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 5 6 5 6 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 16 17 12 13 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 74 75 59 60 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 234 235 215 216 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 351 352 328 329 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 383 384 360 361 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 394 395 367 368 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 510 511 479 480 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 570 572 539 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">13</xref>
###xml 596 597 565 566 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 697 702 663 668 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ccl20</italic>
###xml 829 830 795 796 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 840 841 802 803 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 882 883 844 845 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1010 1012 972 974 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib44">44</xref>
###xml 1014 1016 976 978 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib48">48</xref>
###xml 1018 1020 980 982 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib49">49</xref>
###xml 1079 1080 1041 1042 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1125 1129 1087 1091 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Il10</italic>
###xml 1143 1145 1105 1107 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib54">54</xref>
###xml 1181 1182 1139 1140 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1367 1368 1314 1315 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1444 1445 1384 1385 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1455 1456 1391 1392 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1503 1504 1439 1440 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1514 1515 1446 1447 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 922 926 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 978 983 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1295 1299 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1406 1410 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Foxp3+ RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells were an important fraction of RORgammat+ T cells. They produced the immunosuppressive IL-10 and functioned as genuine T reg cells in vitro. Furthermore, these cells, but not IL-17-producing RORgammat+ T cells, also expressed high levels of the transcript coding for CCL20. This transcript was undetected in RORgammat- T cells, suggesting that Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells were the main source of CCL20 in T cells, even though it might be expressed by a small subset of RORgammat- T cells. In accordance with our results, McGeachy et al. (13) have reported that CD4+ T cells cultured in the presence of TGF-beta and IL-6, which produce both IL-17 and IL-10, express Ccl20, in contrast to cells cultured with IL-23 alone, which produce only IL-17. CCL20 is the ligand for CCR6, suggesting that Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells regulate the recruitment of CCR6+ T cells. Th17 cells isolated from SKG mice, which spontaneously develop arthritis, as well as human Th17 cells, express CCR6 (44, 48, 49). CCR6 is also expressed by a subset of "memory-like" CD25+ T reg cells that produce elevated levels of Il10 transcripts (54). We found that 35-50% of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells expressed CCR6, regardless of their expression of CD25 (or Foxp3). Importantly, CCR6-deficient mice generated an increased ratio of IL-17- to IL-10-producing RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells as compared with WT mice. These results suggest that Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells producing IL-10 and CCL20 recruit CCR6+ RORgammat+ T cells and negatively regulate their differentiation into IL-17-producing cells.
###end p 36

###xml 16 17 12 13 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 92 96 88 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Icos</italic>

###xml 600 606 592 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cx3cl1</italic>
###xml 615 616 607 608 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>

###xml 926 927 914 915 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 937 938 921 922 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1178 1179 1162 1163 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1189 1190 1169 1170 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1290 1291 1270 1271 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 247 252 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 382 387 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells also expressed elevated levels of the costimulatory receptor gene Icos, shown to be essential for the induction of CIA and the optimal generation of Th17 cells (43, 55). The expression of ICOS is also induced by IL-23 in human cells (56). Furthermore, Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells specifically expressed the metalloproteinase gene Mmp9, induced by IL-23 in a mouse tumor model (57), by IL-17 in the lung (45) and by IL-17R in inflamed joints (46), as well as elevated levels of the fractalkine gene Cx3cl1. The functional consequences of elevated expression of Icos, Mmp9, and Cx3cl1 by Foxp3+ RORgammat+ remain to be explored, but this expression pattern appears paradoxical as the product of these genes might promote rather than modulate inflammation through the recruitment and activation of effectors, as well as through tissue remodeling through MMP-9. It might be suggested that the role of Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells is not merely to regulate inflammation through IL-10, but also to participate in the recruitment of inflammatory cells such as Th17 cells to an immunosuppressive environment. On the other hand, we cannot formally exclude that Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells contain distinct functional subsets of IL-10-producing and/or CCL20-, ICOS-, MMP-9-, and CX3CL1-expressing cells.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 28 29 24 25 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 342 343 327 328 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 642 643 619 620 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
Of note, 30-50% of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells did not express IL-17, IL-10, or Foxp3. We could not purify these cells from IL-17 producers for transcript analysis, as no markers were found to distinguish these cells before intracellular cytokine staining. It is possible that non-IL-17 and non-IL-10 producers contained uncommitted RORgammat+ T cells, i.e., not yet committed to either the Th17 or the T reg cell pathway. They could also represent a third committed subpopulation that remains to be characterized. In favor of the first hypothesis, only few IFN-gamma- and no IL-4-producing cells (not depicted) could be detected in RORgammat+ T cells.

###xml 955 960 864 869 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Il23r</italic>
###xml 965 969 874 878 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">csf2</italic>
###xml 1142 1144 1047 1049 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib62">62</xref>
###xml 1171 1172 1072 1073 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1216 1217 1106 1107 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1321 1322 1199 1200 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1370 1371 1236 1237 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1472 1473 1327 1328 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 442 468 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 477 496 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">Mycobacterium bovis</span>
###xml 586 602 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 1290 1294 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
RORgammat+ Tgammadelta cells have been reported previously to represent 50% of total Tgammadelta cells in the lamina propria of the small intestine, a majority of them expressing IL-17 (39). In the CIA model, 60-80% of Vgamma4+ Tgammadelta cells and an equivalent number of CD4+ Talphabeta cells produce IL-17 in the draining LNs (58). Furthermore, Tgammadelta cells are a primary source of IL-17 in the lung before and during infection with Mycobacterium tuberculosis (59) or Mycobacterium bovis (60), and Vdelta1+ Tgammadelta cells produce IL-17 during intraperitoneal infection with Escherichia coli (61). In accordance with these data, we found that RORgammat+ Tgammadelta cells were a major source of IL-17 in the lung and skin, as >80% of them produced IL-17, representing half of the total RORgammat+ T cell population in these compartments. RORgammat+ Tgammadelta cells expressed no Foxp3, but produced the highest levels of RORgammat, as well of Il23r and csf2 coding for GM-CSF. The latter induces the differentiation of granulocytes and monocytes, as well as the production of proinflammatory cytokines such as TNF-alpha and IL-6 (62). In contrast to RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells, IL-17-producing RORgammat+ Tgammadelta cells were not affected by the absence of IL-6 in deficient mice, indicating that RORgammat+ Tgammadelta cells and IL-17-producing RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells are generated by distinct pathways. Collectively, these data suggest that RORgammat+ Tgammadelta cells play an important role in inflammation to pathogens through the production of IL-17 and GM-CSF; however, their precise role in vivo remains to be assessed.

###xml 1213 1215 1173 1175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">13</xref>
###xml 1217 1219 1177 1179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">17</xref>
###xml 1240 1241 1196 1197 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 116 121 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
RORgammat is required for the generation of Th17 cells and has been shown to induce Th17 cells when transduced into mouse naive CD4+ T cells (39). Furthermore, naive CD4+ T cells cultured in the presence of TGF-beta and IL-6 or IL-21 generate Th17 cells (4-6, 10-12) and IL-17-producing RORgammat+ T cells (Fig. 4). We find that a large fraction of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells expressed Foxp3, in particular in the LNs, as well as the immunosuppressive IL-10, and functioned as T reg cells. It has been shown previously that culture conditions that favor the generation of Th17 cells from naive CD4+ T cells, i.e., in the presence of TGF-beta and IL-6, also induce the generation of IL-10-producing cells (13, 17). But in contrast to the in vivo situation, IL-10 was produced by Foxp3- rather than Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells, indicating that these culture systems might not faithfully replicate in vivo pathways. Interestingly, RA blocked the generation of IL-10-producing cells, whereas it favored the generation of Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells. Furthermore, cultures of CD4+ T cells in the presence of TGF-beta and IL-6 or IL-21 generated RORgammat+ T cells expressing both IL-17 and IL-10, as observed previously (13, 17). However, RORgammat+ T cells producing both cytokines could not be detected in vivo, exposing potential conflicts between in vivo and in vitro differentiation pathways.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 121 122 114 115 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>

###xml 264 276 250 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rorc(&#947;t)</italic>
###xml 307 311 289 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Il17</italic>

###xml 334 335 316 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">4</xref>
###xml 336 337 318 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib6">6</xref>
###xml 454 455 432 433 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 635 641 606 612 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Fig. 4</xref>
###xml 644 646 615 617 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">39</xref>

Most cells cultured in the presence of TGF-beta alone expressed RORgammat after 1-2 d, but then differentiated into Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells that did not express IL-17 after 2-3 d. It has been previously reported that TGF-beta alone induces significant levels of Rorc(gammat) transcripts but no detectable Il17 or Il23r transcripts (4-6). These data indicate that RORgammat is necessary but not sufficient for the generation of Th17 cells from naive CD4+ T cells, in accordance with the observation that cells cultured in the presence of TGF-beta and IL-6 express RORgammat after 1-2 d, but express IL-17 only after 2-3 d of culture (Fig. 4) (39). Collectively, these data indicate that TGF-beta is opening a RORgammat-dependent differentiation pathway in CD4+ T cells that leads either to Th17 or T reg cells, depending on the presence of maturation and polarizing factors such as IL-6, IL-21, RA, IL-23, and possibly IL-10.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 49 50 45 46 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>

###xml 365 369 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 398 402 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 766 770 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 789 793 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1165 1169 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The ratio of Th17 to T reg cells within RORgammat+ T cells varied between organs, with the highest proportions of Th17 cells in the intestine and lung, and of Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells in the LNs. This ratio was tuned in favor of Th17 cells by IL-6 and IL-23, as the ratio of IL-17-producing to Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells decreased in IL-6- or IL-12Rbeta1-deficient mice. However, in IL-6-deficient mice, the total number of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells was also significantly diminished, indicating that IL-6 is involved more generally in the generation of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells, as observed previously (6, 39). Conversely, the ratio of Th17 to T reg cells within RORgammat+ T cells was tuned in favor of T reg cells by Foxp3 and CCL20, as inferred from scurfy mice or CCR6-deficient mice. Foxp3 might regulate the fate of "uncommitted" RORgammat+ T cells into IL-17- or IL-10-producing cells by directly modifying the molecular function of RORgammat, as Foxp3 was coimmunoprecipitated with RORgammat. Foxp3 might also act through Foxp3+ RORgammat+ T cells or "mainstream" Foxp3+ T cells regulating RORgammat+ T cells. Interestingly, cells derived from scurfy mice generated a decreased proportion of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells and IL-17-producing RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells, indicating a more complex role for Foxp3 in the generation and regulation of RORgammat+ Talphabeta cells that remains to be elucidated.

###xml 88 89 84 85 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>

###xml 1139 1141 1123 1125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib53">53</xref>
###xml 1243 1244 1223 1224 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1396 1398 1376 1378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib63">63</xref>
###xml 1474 1475 1450 1451 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1678 1679 1650 1651 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1873 1874 1837 1838 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 250 267 <span type="species:ncbi:11320">influenza A virus</span>
###xml 597 601 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 796 800 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 985 989 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
A robust mechanism maintains an equilibrium between Th17 to T reg cells within RORgammat+ T cells. During massive inflammation induced by DSS in the intestine, the total number of RORgammat+ T cells increased 10-fold in the intestine and >10-fold in influenza A virus-infected lungs. However, the numbers of IL-17- and IL-10-producing (Foxp3+) RORgammat+ T cells were increased in comparable proportions. Regulating IL-17 versus IL-10 production might promote inflammation while limiting "collateral" damage, a necessary compromise between effective immunity and tissue integrity. IL-10-deficient mice, which lack one of these effectors arms, generate increased proportions of IL-17-producing CD4+ T cells and develop spontaneous colitis in an IL-23-dependent pathway (15). In contrast, Apcmin/+ mice that developed large colon and ileal polyps and tumors by 3-4 mo of age generated an increased proportion of IL-10-producing RORgammat+ T cells. Similar observations have been made in mice injected with the B16 melanoma, which develop an increased ratio of Foxp3+ cells versus IL-17-producing cells as early as 8 d after tumor injection (53). The mechanisms used by tumors to alter the equilibrium between IL-17- and IL-10-producing RORgammat+ T cells remain to be investigated, but this phenomenon is reminiscent of the IL-10-mediated immunosuppression induced by diverse viruses and bacteria (63). In contrast, the equilibrium between IL-17- and IL-10-producing RORgammat+ T cells might be disrupted in favor of Th17 cells in chronic inflammatory diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis, or inflammatory bowel disease. Tools to control the fate of RORgammat+ T cells might therefore prove useful either to boost or to limit IL-17-producing cells. Potential targets include RORgammat as well as any factors that regulate the differentiation of RORgammat+ T cells into IL-17- or IL-10-producing effectors.

Centrifugation for 20 min at 2,000 rpm yielded the mononuclear cells at the 40-80% interface. Cells were finally washed twice with PBS-F (PBS containing 2% FCS). Single cell suspensions were prepared from the thymus, spleen, and LNs by pressing the tissues through a 100-mum mesh and from the bone marrow by flushing the long bones with PBS. All cells were first preincubated with mAb 2.4G2 to block Fcgamma receptors, and then washed and incubated with the indicated mAb conjugates for 40 min in a total volume of 100 mul PBS-F. For intracellular cytokine staining, cells were stimulated for 5 h in complete medium in the presence of 50 ng/ml phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate and 500 ng/ml ionomycin (both from Sigma-Aldrich). For the last 2 h, 10 mug brefeldin A (Sigma-Aldrich) was added to the cultures. After surface staining for CD3, CD4, TCR-beta, and/or TCR-delta, cells were fixed with fresh 2% paraformaldehyde in PBS for 20 min. Intracellular staining was performed in permeabilization-solution (1% saponin in FACS buffer) with anti-IL-17 (BD Biosciences), anti-IFN-gamma, anti-Foxp3, and/or anti-IL-10. Polyclonal anti-GFP was used to increase GFP signal during intracellular staining. Cells were analyzed on a FACSCanto (BD Biosciences), followed by analysis with FlowJo software (Tristar).

###begin title 57

###begin p 58

Tissues were washed and fixed overnight at 4degreesC in a fresh solution of 4% paraformaldehyde (Sigma-Aldrich) in PBS. The samples were then washed for 1 d in PBS, incubated in a solution of 30% sucrose (Sigma-Aldrich) in PBS until the samples sank, embedded in OCT compound 4583 (Sakura Finetek), frozen in a bath of isopentane cooled with liquid nitrogen, and stocked at -80degreesC. Frozen blocs were cut at 8-mum thickness, and sections were collected onto Superfrost/Plus slides (VWR). Slides were dried for 1 h and processed for staining or stocked at -80degreesC. For staining, slides were first hydrated in PBS-XG (PBS containing 0.1% Triton X-100 and 1% normal goat serum; Sigma-Aldrich) for 5 min and blocked with 10% bovine serum in PBS-XG for 1 h at room temperature. Endogenous biotin was blocked with a biotin blocking kit (Vector Laboratories). Slides were then incubated with primary polyclonal antibody or conjugated mAb (in general 1/100) in PBS-XG overnight at 4degreesC, washed three times for 5 min with PBS-XG, incubated with secondary conjugated polyclonal antibody or streptavidin for 1 h at room temperature, washed once, incubated with DAPI (Sigma-Aldrich) for 5 min at room temperature, washed three times for 5 min, and mounted with Fluoromount-G (SouthernBiotech). Slides were examined under an AxioImager M1 fluorescence microscope (Carl Zeiss, Inc.) equipped with a CCD camera, and images were processed with AxioVision software (Carl Zeiss, Inc.).
