Absence of the TAP2 Human Recombination Hotspot in Chimpanzees
###end article-title 0
###begin article-title 1
What's So Hot about Recombination Hotspots?
###end article-title 1
###begin article-title 2
###xml 33 39 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Humans</span>
A DNA Recombination "Hotspot" in Humans Is Missing in Chimps
###end article-title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 747 771 747 771 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">(Pan troglodytes verus).</italic>
###xml 88 93 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 441 447 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 473 484 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 531 537 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 735 746 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 748 769 <span type="species:ncbi:37012">Pan troglodytes verus</span>
###xml 852 857 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 991 1001 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 1136 1142 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 1170 1175 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1228 1239 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 1402 1407 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Recent experiments using sperm typing have demonstrated that, in several regions of the human genome, recombination does not occur uniformly but instead is concentrated in "hotspots" of 1-2 kb. Moreover, the crossover asymmetry observed in a subset of these has led to the suggestion that hotspots may be short-lived on an evolutionary time scale. To test this possibility, we focused on a region known to contain a recombination hotspot in humans, TAP2, and asked whether chimpanzees, the closest living evolutionary relatives of humans, harbor a hotspot in a similar location. Specifically, we used a new statistical approach to estimate recombination rate variation from patterns of linkage disequilibrium in a sample of 24 western chimpanzees (Pan troglodytes verus). This method has been shown to produce reliable results on simulated data and on human data from the TAP2 region. Strikingly, however, it finds very little support for recombination rate variation at TAP2 in the western chimpanzee data. Moreover, simulations suggest that there should be stronger support if there were a hotspot similar to the one characterized in humans. Thus, it appears that the human TAP2 recombination hotspot is not shared by western chimpanzees. These findings demonstrate that fine-scale recombination rates can change between very closely related species and raise the possibility that rates differ among human populations, with important implications for linkage-disequilibrium based association studies.
###end p 3

###begin title 5
Introduction
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 225 237 225 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Nachman1">Nachman 2002</xref>
###xml 239 258 239 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Arnheim1">Arnheim et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 407 409 395 397 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;8</sup>
###xml 468 491 456 479 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">Drosophila melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 500 514 488 502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Ashburner1">Ashburner 1989</xref>
###xml 516 532 504 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Kong1">Kong et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 844 860 832 848 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Kong1">Kong et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 209 214 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 459 464 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 468 491 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 805 811 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Recombination is a fundamental biological feature about which we still know remarkably little, especially in mammals. Understanding recombination is also of practical importance for evolutionary inference and human genetics (Nachman 2002; Arnheim et al. 2003). Unfortunately, the process is difficult to study, because recombination events occur extremely rarely (e.g., with a probability of approximately10-8 per bp per generation in a typical region of the human or Drosophila melanogaster genome; Ashburner 1989; Kong et al. 2002). Thus, direct measurements for closely linked sites often require the examination of a prohibitive number of individuals. As a result, our knowledge of recombination rates stems primarily from estimates for markers that are megabases apart, obtained from crosses or, for humans, obtained from pedigrees (e.g., Kong et al. 2002).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 81 95 81 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Li1">Li et al. 1988</xref>
###xml 97 115 97 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Hubert1">Hubert et al. 1994</xref>
###xml 117 137 117 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys4">Jeffreys et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 170 189 170 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Arnheim1">Arnheim et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 516 527 516 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Hudson1">Hudson 1987</xref>
###xml 529 558 529 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Pritchard1">Pritchard and Przeworski 2001</xref>
###xml 799 815 799 815 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Daly1">Daly et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 817 837 817 837 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys4">Jeffreys et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 839 858 839 858 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Gabriel1">Gabriel et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 860 881 860 881 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Schneider1">Schneider et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 883 906 883 906 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Wall1">Wall and Pritchard 2003</xref>
###xml 1158 1171 1158 1171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-de1">de Massy 2003</xref>
###xml 564 570 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 999 1004 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
One way to learn about finer-scale recombination rates in males is sperm typing (Li et al. 1988; Hubert et al. 1994; Jeffreys et al. 2001). In this approach (reviewed by Arnheim et al. 2003), genetic markers are amplified and typed from a large number of sperm in order to estimate the fraction of recombinant sperm and hence the recombination rate. Fine-scale rates can also be measured indirectly from patterns of allelic associations, or linkage disequilibrium (LD), observed in samples from natural populations (Hudson 1987; Pritchard and Przeworski 2001). In humans, both direct estimates of the recombination rate using sperm typing techniques and indirect approaches based on analyses of LD have suggested the existence of substantial heterogeneity in rates of recombination at small scales (Daly et al. 2001; Jeffreys et al. 2001; Gabriel et al. 2002; Schneider et al. 2002; Wall and Pritchard 2003). In particular, sperm typing experiments have demonstrated that, in several regions of the human genome, crossover resolutions are not uniformly distributed but instead tend to cluster within narrow regions of 1-2 kb termed "recombination hotspots" (de Massy 2003 and references therein).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 292 303 292 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Brooks1">Brooks 1988</xref>
###xml 427 443 427 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Kong1">Kong et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 469 487 469 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Cullen1">Cullen et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 735 750 735 750 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 755 767 755 767 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D. simulans,</italic>
###xml 834 850 834 850 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-True1">True et al. 1996</xref>
###xml 969 985 969 985 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Papio hamadryas;</italic>
###xml 985 1003 985 1003 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Rogers1">Rogers et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 1273 1303 1273 1303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Betancourt1">Betancourt and Presgraves 2002</xref>
###xml 1305 1323 1305 1323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Thomas1">Thomas et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 332 338 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 735 750 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
###xml 755 766 <span type="species:ncbi:7240|species:ncbi:323382|species:ncbi:394305|species:ncbi:264136|species:ncbi:397685">D. simulans</span>
###xml 888 894 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 961 967 <span type="species:ncbi:9557">baboon</span>
###xml 969 984 <span type="species:ncbi:9557">Papio hamadryas</span>
###xml 1082 1088 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
While there has been recent progress characterizing the extent of spatial variation in recombination rates, the time scale over which recombination rates change remains an open question. It has been known for decades that natural populations harbor genetic variation for recombination rates (Brooks 1988 and references therein). In humans, in particular, there are significant differences in recombination rates among females (Kong et al. 2002) as well as among males (Cullen et al. 2002). Thus, there is a clear potential for the evolution of recombination rates. However, there are only a couple of demonstrated cases that help to delimit the time scale on which this might occur: at the megabase scale, the best example is probably D. melanogaster and D. simulans, two sibling species that differ in their recombination landscape (True et al. 1996). Among primates, the genetic map of humans is approximately 28% longer than that of an Old World monkey, the baboon (Papio hamadryas;Rogers et al. 2000), suggesting that-if physical maps are roughly similar-recombination rates in humans may be higher overall. These instances demonstrate that large-scale recombination rates can change between species that differ on average at roughly 6% to 10% of nucleotide positions (Betancourt and Presgraves 2002; Thomas et al. 2003).
###end p 8

###xml 670 674 667 668 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</sub>
###xml 670 674 667 668 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>&#946;</sub></italic>
###xml 692 695 686 689 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mus</italic>
###xml 701 717 695 711 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Yauk1">Yauk et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 875 900 869 894 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys1">Jeffreys and Neumann 2002</xref>
###xml 964 983 958 977 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Boulton1">Boulton et al. 1997</xref>
###xml 1088 1113 1082 1107 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys1">Jeffreys and Neumann 2002</xref>
###xml 145 151 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 238 252 <span type="species:ncbi:9544">rhesus macaque</span>
###xml 254 268 <span type="species:ncbi:9544">Macaca mulatta</span>
###xml 374 379 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 594 600 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 686 690 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
At a finer scale, the only evidence stems from a recent study of the beta-globin gene, where a hotspot had been characterized by sperm typing in humans. Wall et al. (2003) found no evidence of rate variation in LD data collected from the rhesus macaque (Macaca mulatta), another Old World monkey. For more closely related species, nothing is known. However, observations in yeast (e.g., Petes 2001; Steiner et al. 2002) and mammals (Jeffreys and Neumann 2002; Yauk et al. 2003) raise the possibility that local recombination rates could change rapidly. Indeed, at the MS32 and DNA2 hotspots in humans (Jeffreys et al. 1998; Jeffreys and Neumann 2002) as well as at the Ebeta hotspot in mice (Mus sp.; Yauk et al. 2003), some haplotypes were found to lead to higher rates of initiation of crossover events. Such haplotypes tended to be undertransmitted in crossover products (Jeffreys and Neumann 2002), an asymmetry that favors the loss of recombination hotspots (Boulton et al. 1997). If this is a common phenomenon, it may lead hotspots to be short-lived on an evolutionary time scale (Jeffreys and Neumann 2002).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 185 203 185 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">(Pan troglodytes).</italic>
###xml 351 374 351 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Ebersberger1">Ebersberger et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 700 720 700 720 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Li2">Li and Stephens 2003</xref>
###xml 901 922 901 922 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="sec" rid="s4">Materials and Methods</xref>
###xml 1155 1175 1155 1175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Li2">Li and Stephens 2003</xref>
###xml 1424 1444 1412 1432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys3">Jeffreys et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 1533 1553 1521 1541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys3">Jeffreys et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 1663 1683 1651 1671 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Li2">Li and Stephens 2003</xref>
###xml 112 118 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 174 184 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 186 201 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">Pan troglodytes</span>
###xml 433 444 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 487 497 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 1336 1342 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
To evaluate whether fine-scale recombination rates can change rapidly, we were interested in comparing rates in humans with those in their closest evolutionary relative, the chimpanzee (Pan troglodytes). The two species are thought to have had a common ancestor five to six million years ago and differ at approximately 1.2% of base pairs on average (Ebersberger et al. 2002). Since it is difficult to use sperm typing techniques in chimpanzees, not least of all because of the need for chimpanzee sperm, we took an indirect approach and estimated the extent of recombination rate variation from patterns of LD in a population sample. To do so, we modified a recently developed statistical approach (Li and Stephens 2003). The method estimates recombination rates by exploiting the fact that patterns of LD reflect the rate and distribution of recombination events in the ancestors of the sample (see Materials and Methods for more details). Although it is based on simplistic assumptions about population demography, it has been shown to produce reliable estimates of recombination rates for data sets simulated under a range of demographic assumptions (Li and Stephens 2003; D. C. Crawford, T. Bhangale, N. Li, G. Hellenthal, M. J. Rieder, et al., unpublished data). We focused on the TAP2 genic region, where a sperm typing study of humans characterized a approximately1.2 kb recombination hotspot in one of the introns (Jeffreys et al. 2000). Application of the statistical method to polymorphism data collected for this region (Jeffreys et al. 2000) led to estimates similar to those obtained by sperm typing, providing further evidence for its reliability (Li and Stephens 2003).
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 170 199 170 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Pritchard1">Pritchard and Przeworski 2001</xref>
###xml 348 365 348 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Morin1">Morin et al. 1994</xref>
###xml 367 384 367 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Stone1">Stone et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 386 405 386 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Fischer1">Fischer et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 517 536 517 536 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">(P. t. troglodytes)</italic>
###xml 553 566 553 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">(P. t. verus)</italic>
###xml 580 597 580 597 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Gilad1">Gilad et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 599 618 599 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Fischer1">Fischer et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 235 246 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 521 535 <span type="species:ncbi:388886|species:ncbi:36278|species:ncbi:96555|species:ncbi:290682|species:ncbi:549197">t. troglodytes</span>
###xml 567 578 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 682 693 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
Samples that include individuals from diverged populations are expected to harbor high levels of LD that may lead to incorrect estimates of recombination rate variation (Pritchard and Przeworski 2001). This is of particular concern in chimpanzees, for which previous studies have reported high levels of genetic differentiation between subspecies (Morin et al. 1994; Stone et al. 2002; Fischer et al. 2004). In addition, there appears to be a high proportion of less informative, rare alleles in samples from central (P. t. troglodytes) but not western (P. t. verus) chimpanzees (Gilad et al. 2003; Fischer et al. 2004). We therefore collected polymorphism data from a sample of 24 chimpanzees that were all known to be from the western subspecies. Strikingly, we found no evidence for recombination rate variation at TAP2 in these data.

###begin title 12
Results
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 68 90 68 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys3">Jeffreys et al. (2000)</xref>

In humans, LD data for the TAP2 region were previously collected by Jeffreys et al. (2000), who resequenced approximately9.7 kb in a sample of eight individuals from the United Kingdom (UK) and found 46 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), excluding insertion-deletions. The SNPs were then typed in a sample of 30 individuals from the UK, in whom haplotypes were determined experimentally (by allele-specific PCR). We collected genotype data for the same region in western chimpanzees by resequencing 24 individuals (see Materials and Methods for details). This led to the discovery of 57 SNPs. When differences in study design are taken into account, diversity levels in western chimpanzees are very similar to those observed in samples of humans from the UK (thetaW = 0.145% versus thetaW = 0.144% per bp, respectively), consistent with previous findings (e.g., Gilad et al. 2003; Fischer et al. 2004).

###begin p 14

The LD data are summarized in Figure 1; overall, there is much less LD in humans than in chimpanzees. In particular, in humans, strong allelic associations are only seen between pairs of sites in close physical proximity, while in chimpanzees, such associations are also found among more distant pairs. Whether this reflects differences in the underlying recombination landscape or chance variation is unclear from visual inspection of these plots alone. We therefore used a statistical approach to assess the evidence for recombination rate variation in the two species. Specifically, we assumed that there is (at most) one hotspot in the region and, as a first step, specified its location according to the results of the sperm typing study in humans. We then applied our modification of the method of Li and Stephens (2003) to estimate a background population recombination rate, rho, and the relative intensity of recombination in the hotspot segment, lambda (see Materials and Methods). Within this model, a lambda value of 1 corresponds to an absence of recombination rate variation, while values of lambda greater than 1 indicate a hotspot. The approach taken here is Bayesian (see Materials and Methods) so, as a measure of support for a hotspot in the LD data, we report estimates for the probabilities Pr(lambda > 1) and Pr(lambda > 10); these are the posterior probabilities of a hotspot of any kind and of a hotspot of intensity at least ten times the background rate, respectively.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 123 129 123 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
###xml 146 152 141 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>

###xml 343 349 328 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
###xml 502 508 482 483 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
###xml 758 778 733 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys3">Jeffreys et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 34 39 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 208 213 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Application of this method to the human haplotype data led to extremely strong support for rate variation: we estimated Pr(lambda > 1) = 1 and Pr(lambda > 10) = 0.982. When the same method was applied to the human genotype data (i.e., ignoring the information about the phase of multiple heterozygotes), we estimated Pr(lambda > 1) = 1 and Pr(lambda > 10) = 0.992. The results are almost identical, suggesting minimal loss of information with the use of genotypes. Interestingly, the point estimate of lambda using either haplotypes (28.4) or genotypes (32.1) is higher than the corresponding estimate from sperm typing (11). This difference may reflect error in the estimates; alternatively, it may point to a more intense hotspot in females than in males (Jeffreys et al. 2000).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 148 149 146 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">,</italic>

Next, we applied the same method to the genotype data collected from western chimpanzees. The estimate of the background rate of recombination, rhô, is 5.0 x 10-4 per base pair, which is very similar to the estimate from the human genotype data (Figure 2). However, in contrast to what is found in humans, there is no evidence for recombination rate variation: our estimate of lambda is 1, suggesting a uniform rate of recombination throughout the region, and our estimates of Pr(lambda > 1) = 0.200 and Pr(lambda > 10) = 0.006, reflecting tepid support for a hotspot of any kind and almost no support for a hotspot similar to the one observed in humans. Indeed, the latter figure represents very strong evidence against a hotspot of moderate intensity and rules out the possibility that the chimpanzee polymorphism data are simply uninformative, because of, for example, insufficient sample size or diversity.

To assess how likely we would be to obtain such weak support if there were in fact a hotspot in western chimpanzees similar to the one in humans, we generated 200 simulated genotype data sets under a model with a hotspot of intensity lambda = 11 and then tabulated the proportion with posterior probability estimates as low or lower than that observed (see Materials and Methods). We took the lambda value estimated from sperm typing because it is the lowest of the various estimates for humans and hence its use was conservative for our purposes. With the rho value estimated from the data (5.0 x 10-4 per bp), the probability of obtaining Pr(lambda > 1) </= 0.200 is p = 0.010 and the probability of obtaining Pr(lambda > 10) </= 0.006 is p = 0.005. With a lower rho value (2.7 x 10-4 per bp; see Materials and Methods), the probability of obtaining Pr(lambda > 1) </= 0.200 is p = 0.020. In other words, we can reject the null hypothesis that there is a hotspot in western chimpanzees similar to the one in humans, because we would expect to see more support for a hotspot in these data if one were there. It appears that western chimpanzees do not harbor a hotspot in the same location as humans.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 269 272 269 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#961;</italic>
###xml 277 283 275 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
###xml 289 310 282 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="sec" rid="s4">Materials and Methods</xref>
###xml 523 531 516 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020155-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 94 105 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 631 642 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
The possibility remains, however, that there is a hotspot in a slightly different position in chimpanzees. To evaluate this, we used a more general model in which there is at most one hotspot in the region, but where the location is unknown and estimated together with rho and lambda (see Materials and Methods). Again, we found very little evidence for recombination rate variation: across all pairs of consecutive segregating sites, the largest posterior probability of elevated recombination is estimated to be < 0.060 (Figure 3). Thus, the hotspot appears to be entirely absent from the approximately9.4 kb surveyed in western chimpanzees.

###begin title 19
Discussion
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 441 458 441 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Gilad1">Gilad et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 460 479 460 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Fischer1">Fischer et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 622 623 622 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</italic>
###xml 633 634 633 634 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 648 669 648 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="sec" rid="s4">Materials and Methods</xref>
###xml 785 805 785 805 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Li2">Li and Stephens 2003</xref>
###xml 280 291 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 1221 1227 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 1430 1441 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
These estimates of recombination rate parameters are based on assumptions of neutrality, constant population size, and random mating, raising the concern that the hotspot is not absent but instead masked by departures from model assumptions. However, we chose to focus on western chimpanzees precisely because previous studies reported allele frequencies in rough accordance with the assumptions of our model. Consistent with these studies (Gilad et al. 2003; Fischer et al. 2004), the allele frequencies at TAP2 are not significantly different from the expectations of the standard neutral model (as assessed by Tajima's D = 0.848, p = 0.237; see Materials and Methods). Moreover, simulations suggest that the power to detect a hotspot is not strongly affected by population history (Li and Stephens 2003). To some extent, this is expected, as population history tends to affect LD in the entire region, not only in the hotspot, so that estimates of the relative rates of recombination are unlikely to be substantially altered. In summary, there is no evidence for a marked departure from model assumptions in the allele frequencies, and the method is expected to be robust to small departures. Consistent with this, in humans, the approach yields similar results to sperm typing experiments that do not rely on the same assumptions. On this basis, it seems that the hotspot is truly absent from the homologous region in western chimpanzees.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 503 513 503 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Petes1">Petes 2001</xref>
###xml 790 807 790 807 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Smith1">Smith et al. 1998</xref>
###xml 809 826 809 826 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Badge1">Badge et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 50 61 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 75 81 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 931 937 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 952 963 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
This finding implies that the hotspot was lost in chimpanzees or gained in humans, or that it moved in one of the species (over a larger distance than we surveyed). This in turn raises a number of more general questions. Are hotspots frequently born de novo or do they tend to migrate within circumscribed regions of the genome? Are particular sequence motifs sufficient to produce recombination hotspots, or are larger-scale requirements, such as chromatin accessibility, required for their formation (Petes 2001)? The systematic comparison between closely related species with different recombination landscapes may be helpful in addressing these problems. As an illustration, in these data, we found two motifs that were previously implicated in the formation of recombination hotspots (Smith et al. 1998; Badge et al. 2000 and references therein) and that varied between the two species: a Pur binding motif that is present in humans but absent in chimpanzees (because of a single base pair difference) and two scaffold attachment sites that are in different positions in the two species. The significance of these differences cannot be determined on the basis of a single example; however, once a larger sample of hotspot regions has been surveyed, one can begin to test for an association between particular sequence motifs or features and the presence of hotspots.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 127 149 127 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys3">Jeffreys et al. (2000)</xref>

###xml 582 592 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 783 789 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 801 812 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 1096 1101 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1102 1112 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 1235 1240 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Comparative studies of hotspot regions will also increase our understanding of the determinants of mutation rates. As noted by Jeffreys et al. (2000), there is a significant excess of diversity within the hotspot region in humans from the UK (Figure 4): when the hotspot region is compared to the 8,735 other windows of the same size, only 0.3% have as many or more SNPs. In contrast, in western chimpanzees, levels of diversity are not higher than elsewhere in the region (Figure 4): 17.0% of comparable windows harbor at least as many SNPs as the hotspot. Nor are levels of human-chimpanzee divergence unusual in the hotspot region: 67.3% of windows show the same or higher numbers of fixed differences between species (Figure 4). Given the evidence for a recombination hotspot in humans but not in chimpanzees, these observations are consistent with an association between recombination and mutation in primates (Hellmann et al. 2003) and, in particular, with a mutagenic effect of recombination (Rattray et al. 2002). If indeed recombination events introduce mutations, the lack of a peak of human-chimpanzee divergence in the hotspot region (Jeffreys et al. 2000; Figure 4) would suggest that the hotspot arose fairly recently in human evolution.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 215 240 215 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys1">Jeffreys and Neumann 2002</xref>
###xml 309 327 309 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Cullen1">Cullen et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 329 345 329 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Kong1">Kong et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 416 421 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 539 544 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 667 678 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
###xml 683 689 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 706 711 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In conclusion, these analyses demonstrate that fine-scale recombination rates can change between closely related species. Together with the observations that crossover frequencies can depend on specific haplotypes (Jeffreys and Neumann 2002) and that large-scale recombination rates differ among individuals (Cullen et al. 2002; Kong et al. 2002), this finding raises the possibility that local rates can vary among human groups that differ in their allele frequencies. Unfortunately, demonstrating compelling evidence for variation among human populations on the basis of LD data alone promises to be substantially harder than demonstrating such differences between chimpanzees and humans. In particular, human populations share most of their evolutionary history, making differences between extant populations, if they exist, more difficult to detect. Nevertheless, LD studies should be helpful in identifying interesting regions for further study via sperm typing.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 300 319 300 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Johnson1">Johnson et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 485 521 485 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-International1">International Hapmap Consortium 2003</xref>
###xml 57 62 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 334 339 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 408 413 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The extent to which local recombination rates vary among human populations influences the degree of similarity of LD patterns among them, with important consequences for the design of efficient LD-based association studies (including, for example, the choice of appropriate "haplotype tagging SNPs" [Johnson et al. 2001] in different human populations) and for the relevance of data generated by the current human HapMap project to populations not currently represented in that study (International Hapmap Consortium 2003). Perhaps most importantly, if local recombination rates do vary among groups, then the study of regions with the most pronounced differences should lead to further insights into the underlying biological processes that cause fine-scale variation in recombination rates.
###end p 24

###begin title 38
Analyses of LD
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 139 161 139 161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Li2">Li and Stephens (2003)</xref>
To assess the support in the polymorphism data for a recombination hotspot, we used the Product of Approximate Conditionals (PAC) model of Li and Stephens (2003). Assuming haplotypes are known, the method considers each one in turn and attempts to represent it as a mosaic of the previously considered haplotypes. Qualitatively, the larger the regions over which haplotypes tend to resemble one another, the fewer the pieces required in each mosaic, and the lower the estimates of the recombination rates. The method uses simplistic assumptions about population demography to quantify this qualitative relationship and hence to estimate recombination rates across the region.
###end p 39

###xml 2162 2167 2116 2117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#952;</italic>
###xml 2177 2199 2127 2149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Li2">Li and Stephens (2003)</xref>
###xml 443 453 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
More formally, the model of Li and Stephens (2003) defines the probability of observing haplotypes H given the underlying recombination parameters alpha (which in our case may include the background recombination rate and the hotspot location and intensity; see below). This can be used directly to estimate alpha from H in situations where haplotypes have been experimentally determined (e.g., Li and Stephens 2003). However, in our case the chimpanzee haplotypes are not known. Rather, we have genotype data G and we wish to estimate alpha from G. A simple approximate solution to this would be first to use a statistical method (e.g., that of Stephens et al. 2001) to obtain an estimate H for the haplotypes H from the genotypes G, and then to estimate alpha from H. However, a risk of this approach is that overconfident conclusions will be drawn by ignoring uncertainty in the estimated haplotypes. A better solution, and the approach we take here, is to jointly estimate H and alpha from G, or, more specifically, to obtain a sample from the joint posterior distribution, Pr(H, alpha | G). To do so, we start with an initial guess for the haplotypes, and iterate the following steps: (i) estimate a new value for alpha, using the current estimate of H and (ii) estimate a new value for H, using the genotypes G and the current value for alpha. Step (i) is performed using the PAC-B model of Li and Stephens (2003) and the priors on alpha described below. Step (ii) is performed by using the method for haplotype inference described in Stephens and Donnelly (2003), but replacing the conditional distribution that they use (which ignores recombination) with the conditional distribution of Fearnhead and Donnelly (2001) (which takes into account recombination) computed using two quadrature points. (Actually, we modified the Fearnhead and Donnelly conditional distribution slightly, replacing the equation qi = zirho/(j+ zirho) in their Appendix A with qi =1-exp(-zirho/j).) Both the PAC-B model and the Fearnhead and Donnelly conditional require the specification of a mutation parameter, theta, and a mutation process. In each case, we used the value of theta given in Li and Stephens (2003) and a mutation process whereby each mutation event at a biallelic site results in a change from one allele to the other.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 98 99 98 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H</italic>
###xml 101 106 101 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 109 110 105 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">G</italic>
###xml 217 222 213 214 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 289 294 281 282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 297 298 285 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">G</italic>
###xml 316 321 304 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 397 399 381 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">G,</italic>
This iterative scheme defines a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the distribution Pr(H, alpha | G) from which we wish to sample. Provided that the algorithm is run for sufficiently long, the estimates of alpha obtained each iteration provide a sample from the distribution Pr(alpha | G), and thus allow alpha (i.e., the underlying recombination process) to be estimated directly from G, taking full account of the fact that the actual underlying haplotypes are not known. The algorithm is implemented within the software package PHASE version 2.1, which is available online at .
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 58 80 58 80 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Li2">Li and Stephens (2003)</xref>
###xml 145 151 145 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
###xml 241 242 236 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">r</italic>
###xml 300 307 295 297 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;r</italic>
###xml 570 592 560 582 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Li2">Li and Stephens [2003]</xref>
###xml 734 737 724 725 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#961;</italic>
###xml 742 743 730 731 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">N</italic>
###xml 743 744 731 732 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">e</sub>
###xml 744 745 732 733 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">r</italic>
###xml 753 754 741 742 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">N</italic>
###xml 754 755 742 743 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">e</sub>
###xml 794 800 782 783 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
###xml 898 904 881 882 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
###xml 959 965 937 938 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
###xml 1013 1015 986 988 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 1015 1023 988 991 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">(&#955;)</italic>
###xml 1073 1076 1041 1042 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#961;</italic>
###xml 1094 1096 1060 1062 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 1096 1101 1062 1065 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">(&#961;)</italic>
###xml 490 496 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
We considered two versions of the simple hotspot model of Li and Stephens (2003). In this model, there is a single hotspot of constant intensity lambda. Crossovers occur as a Poisson process (i.e., there is no interference) of constant rate r (per base pair) outside the hotspot and of constant rate lambdar inside the hotspot; gene conversion is not explicitly modeled. In the first version, we assumed that, if present, the hotspot is at the same location as estimated by sperm typing in humans (4180-5417). (This location is not precisely the same as the one used by Li and Stephens [2003], which is why our estimates differ from theirs.) There are two parameters to be estimated: the background population recombination parameter rho (= 4Ner, where Ne is the effective population size) and lambda. We assumed a priori that a hotspot exists with probability 0.5 and that, if the hotspot exists, lambda is between one and 100. Specifically, we assumed that lambda = 1 with probability 0.5 and otherwise that log10(lambda) is uniformly distributed on (0, 2). The prior on rho is uniform on log10(rho) in the range (-8, 3), which covers all plausible values.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 120 126 120 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
###xml 131 134 126 127 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#961;</italic>
###xml 648 651 629 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#961;</italic>
###xml 659 665 638 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
In the second version, we assumed that the location and width of the hotspot are unknown and to be estimated along with lambda and rho. In this case, we assumed a priori that the hotspot exists with probability 0.18 (corresponding to an assumption that a hotspot occurs roughly once per 50 kb of sequence), that the center of the hotspot is equally likely to be anywhere along the length of the sequence, and that the width of the hotspot is between 200 and approximately4,000 bp (specifically, we assumed that the width had a normal distribution, with a mean of 0 bp and a standard deviation of 2,000 bp, truncated to lie above 200 bp). Priors on rho and on lambda (conditional on there being a hotspot) are as in the first version.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
To allow for potential problems with convergence of this Markov chain Monte Carlo algorithm, we ran the algorithm ten times for each analysis, using different seeds for the pseudorandom number generator. For each run, we obtained a point estimate of the parameters (using sample posterior medians) and posterior probabilities. The reported estimates are the median of the estimates obtained from the ten runs.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 215 226 215 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Hudson2">Hudson 1990</xref>
###xml 339 345 339 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#955;</italic>
###xml 356 361 351 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#952;</italic>

###xml 742 764 729 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Li2">Li and Stephens [2003]</xref>
###xml 1064 1067 1051 1052 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#961;</italic>
###xml 1074 1075 1057 1058 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">,</italic>
###xml 1190 1193 1173 1174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#961;</italic>
###xml 1199 1200 1180 1181 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">e</sub>
###xml 1213 1215 1194 1196 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;4</sup>
###xml 1232 1233 1213 1214 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">e</sub>
###xml 1324 1343 1305 1324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Fischer1">Fischer et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1435 1457 1416 1438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020155-Jeffreys3">Jeffreys et al. (2000)</xref>
###xml 142 148 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 1131 1141 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 1302 1313 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzees</span>
To test how likely we would be to obtain such weak support for a hotspot in the LD data if there were in fact a hotspot similar to the one in humans, we ran 200 coalescent simulations of the standard neutral model (Hudson 1990) with the same number of base pairs and sample size as the actual data (48 chromosomes), a hotspot of intensity lambda = 11, and theta = thetaW. Haplotypes were randomly paired to form genotypes and phase information was ignored. The data were masked to mimic the actual data structure, i.e., they included a gap of 487 bp in the same position. We then counted the proportion of simulated data sets for which our estimate of the posterior probability was as low as observed or lower (using the first version of the Li and Stephens [2003] model). Since we obtained estimates for the simulated data in the same way as for the actual data, significance values obtained from this analysis are valid independent of the convergence, or even the correctness, of the Markov chain Monte Carlo scheme. In the first set of 200 simulations, we used rho = rhô, the background rate that we estimated from the western chimpanzee data. In the second set of simulations, we used rho = 4N̂er̂ = 2.7 x 10-4 per bp, where N̂e = 17,100 is an estimate of the effective population size of western chimpanzees (based on Fischer et al. 2004) and r̂ = 0.4 cM/Mb is the rough estimate of the background recombination rate reported in Jeffreys et al. (2000).
