UP element-dependent transcription at the Escherichia coli rrnB P1 promoter: positional requirements and role of the RNA polymerase alpha subunit linker
###end article-title 0

###begin p 2
###xml 49 53 49 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 219 223 211 215 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 477 481 465 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 878 882 854 858 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 1045 1049 1021 1025 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
The UP element stimulates transcription from the rrnB P1 promoter through a direct interaction with the C-terminal domain of the RNA polymerase alpha subunit (alphaCTD). We investigated the effect on transcription from rrnB P1 of varying both the location of the UP element and the length of the alpha subunit interdomain linker, separately and in combination. Displacement of the UP element by a single turn of the DNA helix resulted in a large decrease in transcription from rrnB P1, while displacement by half a turn or two turns totally abolished UP element-dependent transcription. Deletions of six or more amino acids from within the alpha subunit linker resulted in a decrease in UP element-dependent stimulation, which correlated with decreased binding of alphaCTD to the UP element. Increasing the alpha linker length was less deleterious to RNA polymerase function at rrnB P1 but did not compensate for the decrease in activation that resulted from displacing the UP element. Our results suggest that the location of the UP element at rrnB P1 is crucial to its function and that the natural length of the alpha subunit linker is optimal for utilisation of the UP element at this promoter.

###xml 0 16 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Escherichia coli RNA polymerase holoenzyme (RNAP) is a multi-subunit complex consisting of an alpha subunit homodimer, single beta, beta' and omega subunits, and one of several sigma subunit species (1,2). During transcription initiation at many promoters, RNAP containing the major sigma factor, sigma70, recognises two hexameric promoter elements located approximately10 and 35 bp upstream of the transcription start point (+1) (3). Recognition of the -10 and -35 promoter elements requires regions 2.3-2.5 and 4.2 of sigma70, respectively (4,5). However, a third promoter element, the UP element, is frequently required for full promoter activity (6,7). UP elements interact directly with the C-terminal domain of the alpha subunit of RNAP (alphaCTD) (8,9) in holoenzymes containing sigma70 or alternative sigma factors (10-12).

###xml 768 770 756 758 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c17">17</xref>
###xml 930 934 906 910 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 950 952 926 928 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c17">17</xref>
###xml 1131 1132 1107 1108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c8">8</xref>
###xml 1133 1135 1109 1111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c13">13</xref>
###xml 1136 1138 1112 1114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c16">16</xref>
 UP elements consist of A+T-rich sequences located upstream of the -35 region at many promoters (6,8,13). At the most well-studied UP element-dependent promoter, rrnB P1, the UP element comprises DNA sequences from approximately positions -40 to -60 and is composed of two subsites centred at about -42 and -52 (termed the proximal and distal subsites, respectively), each of which can bind an alphaCTD (6,14-17). The interaction between alphaCTD and the UP element results in an increase in KB, the initial equilibrium constant for RNAP binding to the promoter, but may also affect later steps in the pathway to open complex formation (16,18). The alphaCTD-UP element interaction is responsible for a 30-70-fold increase in in vivo promoter activity at rrnB P1 (8,16,17). The optimised UP element sequence ('consensus' UP element) contains alternating A- and T-tracts and is approximately5-fold more effective than the wild type rrnB P1 UP element (17). UP elements can also be transposed as a separate module from one promoter to another where they retain their ability to stimulate transcription in a factor-independent manner (8,13,16).
###end p 5

###xml 730 732 710 712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c32">32</xref>
###xml 881 882 853 854 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1022 1024 994 996 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c30">30</xref>
###xml 1025 1027 997 999 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c32">32</xref>
###xml 1028 1030 1000 1002 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c35">35</xref>
 Each alpha subunit consists of 329 amino acids organised in two independently folding domains (9,19). The N-terminal domain (alphaNTD; residues 8-231) contains determinants for dimerisation and assembly into RNAP (20,21) and plays a role in transcription activation at some promoters (22,23). alphaCTD (residues 249-329) (24) plays roles in transcription initiation, elongation and termination (25-27). During transcription initiation, alphaCTD provides a contact site for many transcription activators (25,28,29) and is directly involved in UP element recognition (8,30). alphaCTD folds into a (HhH)2 domain (31) in which key residues interacting with DNA reside within the loop and second helix of HhH1 and HhH2, respectively (32). Based on genetic, biochemical and NMR studies of the alphaCTD-DNA interaction, and on the X-ray structure of the RuvA-DNA complex, the alpha (HhH)2 domain is thought to interact in a sequence-specific way with bases in the minor groove and with the DNA backbone across the minor groove (30,32-35).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 192 198 184 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 206 207 194 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c9">9</xref>
###xml 208 210 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c19">19</xref>
###xml 419 421 403 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c36">36</xref>
###xml 547 549 527 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c21">21</xref>
###xml 740 746 716 722 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 762 764 734 736 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c37">37</xref>
###xml 1048 1050 1016 1018 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c37">37</xref>
###xml 1051 1053 1019 1021 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c38">38</xref>
###xml 1638 1640 1594 1596 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c37">37</xref>
###xml 1641 1643 1597 1599 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c38">38</xref>
###xml 192 198 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 740 746 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
 Several lines of evidence indicate that a flexible linker connects alphaCTD to alphaNTD. First, limited proteolysis studies pointed to an accessible region between amino acids 234 and 249 of E.coli alpha (9,19). Secondly, NMR analysis of an isolated C-terminal fragment of alpha (amino acids 233-329) showed that a region of at least 13 amino acids, extending from D233 to E248, exhibits a high degree of flexibility (36). This is consistent with the crystallographic data for alphaNTD, where the C-terminal limit of helix 3 is assigned to F231 (21). Thirdly, amino acid sequence alignments of alpha subunits from eubacteria and chloroplasts reveal a non-conserved sequence of variable length corresponding to amino acids V237-P251 of the E.coli alpha subunit (37). Consistent with its proposed role as a linker, the introduction of substantial deletions or insertions into this region does not affect the efficiency of assembly of alpha into RNAP, nor do they affect transcription from activator-independent and UP element-independent promoters (37,38). However, deletion of three amino acids from within the linker greatly reduces transcription from a promoter where the activator protein CRP binds to a site centred at -61.5 (Class I), whereas transcription is enhanced from a promoter where CRP binds at -41.5 (Class II). This result, together with the observation that alphaCTD tethered by a shorter linker is still recruited to DNA sequences upstream of the CRP binding site at the Class II promoter, implies that the alpha linker deletion compromises the motional freedom of alphaCTD rather than its inherent ability to bind DNA (37,38).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 202 203 202 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c6">6</xref>
###xml 204 205 204 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c8">8</xref>
###xml 206 208 206 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c13">13</xref>
###xml 209 211 209 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c17">17</xref>
###xml 330 333 326 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 363 365 359 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c39">39</xref>
 Although potential UP elements or UP element subsites have been identified in the -40 to -60 region at many promoters, in most cases such searches have not been extended to locations further upstream (6,8,13,17). The recent demonstration that alphaCTD is able to contact DNA near positions -43, -53, -63, -73, -83 and -93 at the lac promoter, unassisted by CRP (39), suggests that the length of the alpha linker is unlikely to restrict access of alphaCTD to UP elements located upstream of -60. However, the question of whether UP elements are able to stimulate transcription efficiently from distant locations has not been satisfactorily addressed.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 113 115 113 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c15">15</xref>
###xml 270 274 270 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 748 750 748 750 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c12">12</xref>
###xml 751 753 751 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c40">40</xref>
###xml 754 756 754 756 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c41">41</xref>
###xml 959 963 955 959 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 1101 1105 1093 1097 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 1335 1339 1323 1327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 1472 1475 1456 1459 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrn</italic>
 Gourse and colleagues studied effects of UP element location before the UP element subsites were fully defined (15). They observed that upstream displacement of sequences between positions -47 and -87 by a single turn of the helix did not affect transcription from the rrnB P1 promoter, whereas displacement by two or more turns exerted an inhibitory effect on transcription. However, as the proximal UP element subsite was retained in its normal location in these early experiments, conclusions concerning displacement of the full UP element must be re-evaluated. In cases where UP elements have been identified upstream of the -40 to -60 region, DNA bending by accessory DNA binding proteins such as IHF is also required for full activity (e.g. 12,40,41), presumably to bring alpha binding sequences closer to the core promoter. Therefore, in this work, we have examined the effect of upstream displacement of the full UP element on transcription from the rrnB P1 promoter in the absence of accessory factors. We have also investigated the role of alpha linker length in UP element function at the rrnB P1 promoter and the possibility that lengthening the alpha linker might alleviate the negative effect of UP element displacement. Our results demonstrate that the position of the full UP element is crucial to its function at the rrnB P1 promoter and that the length of the alpha subunit linker is optimised for utilisation of UP elements in their normal position at rrn P1 promoters.

The E.coli strains and plasmids used in this work are listed in Table 1. Standard molecular biology techniques for plasmid isolation and DNA manipulation were used throughout (42). All rrnB P1 promoter derivatives used for transcription assays or single copy lacZ fusion analysis were cloned as EcoRI-HindIII fragments into the transcription vector pRLG770 or lambda phage system I (16), respectively, and possess a downstream end point at position +52 with respect to the rrnB P1 transcription initiation site. ###begin title 21
RESULTS
###end title 21
###begin title 22
###xml 60 64 60 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 77 85 77 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 90 97 90 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
Effect of UP element displacement on transcription from the rrnB P1 promoter in vitro and in vivo
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 123 127 123 127 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 153 157 153 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 270 274 270 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 431 432 431 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f1">1</xref>
###xml 522 530 522 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 561 565 561 565 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 668 672 668 672 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 772 774 772 774 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c17">17</xref>
###xml 1072 1073 1060 1061 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f2">2</xref>
###xml 1271 1272 1259 1260 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f2">2</xref>
###xml 1351 1355 1339 1343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 1486 1490 1474 1478 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 1548 1550 1536 1538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c15">15</xref>
To investigate the relationship between UP element location and UP element-dependent stimulation of transcription from the rrnB P1 promoter, a series of rrnB P1 promoter derivatives were constructed in which the full consensus UP element was displaced upstream from the rrnB P1 core promoter by distances corresponding to 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5 and 3.0 turns of the DNA helix, i.e. 5, 11, 16, 22, 27 and 33 bp, respectively (Fig. 1). These promoters were cloned into the transcription vector pRLG770 and their activities in vitro were compared with that of an rrnB P1 promoter derivative containing the consensus UP element in the wild type position, and to the core rrnB P1 promoter, in which the UP element is replaced by a sequence devoid of UP element-like activity (17). The results showed that whereas the presence of the consensus UP element in the wild type location resulted in a 29-fold increase in promoter activity relative to the core promoter, displacement of the UP element by one helical turn (11 bp) resulted in only approximately5-fold activation (Fig. 2A, compare lanes 2 and 4 with lane 1). Upstream displacement of the consensus UP element by 0.5, 1.5, 2, 2.5 or 3 turns of the DNA helix essentially abolished UP element-dependent stimulation (Fig. 2A). These results suggest that the location of the UP element at the wild type rrnB P1 promoter is optimal for UP element-dependent stimulation. The results also confirm the previous observation that activation of rrnB P1 by upstream sequences is face of the helix dependent (15).
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 72 76 72 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 80 87 80 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 93 97 93 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 183 187 183 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ</italic>
###xml 268 269 263 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gke549tb1">1</xref>
###xml 451 452 443 444 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f2">2</xref>
###xml 753 754 745 746 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f2">2</xref>
###xml 816 824 808 816 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 915 919 907 911 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 210 222 <span type="species:ncbi:10710">lambda phage</span>
 To examine the effect of UP element displacement on transcription from rrnB P1 in vivo, the rrnB P1 promoter derivatives possessing displaced UP elements were cloned upstream of the lacZ gene on a recombinant lambda phage and monolysogens were constructed (see Table 1). The results from beta-galactosidase assays showed that UP element-dependent stimulation of transcription is maximal when the UP element is present in the wild type location (Fig. 2B). Displacement of the consensus UP element by 11 bp upstream from the wild type location gave rise to >20-fold decrease in transcriptional activity, whereas displacement of the UP element by any other distance, including displacement by only 5 bp, abolished UP element-dependent transcription (Fig. 2B). These results are fully consistent with the data obtained in vitro and emphasise the crucial importance of the position of the UP element to its function at rrnB P1.

###begin title 25
###xml 68 76 64 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
Effect of alpha linker length on UP element-dependent transcription in vitro
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 78 82 74 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 95 103 91 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 283 285 275 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c37">37</xref>
###xml 596 600 548 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 790 794 742 746 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 810 811 762 763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c6">6</xref>
###xml 812 814 764 766 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c17">17</xref>
###xml 842 847 794 799 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"> rrnB</italic>
###xml 1095 1100 1043 1048 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB </italic>

###xml 1175 1179 1123 1127 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
To test the requirements for the RNAP alpha subunit interdomain linker at the rrnB P1 promoter in vitro, RNAPs were reconstituted using a series of 10 mutant alpha subunits possessing interdomain linkers of different lengths. These mutant derivatives have been described previously (37) and contain deletions of 3, 6, 9 and 12 amino acids (termed Delta3, Delta6, Delta9 and Delta12), and insertions of 3, 6, 10, 13, 16 and 32 amino acids (termed Omega3, Omega6, Omega10, Omega13, Omega16 and Omega32). Multiple round transcription assays were performed with template DNA containing the wild type rrnB P1 promoter, including its natural UP element, or rrnB P1derivatives possessing the consensus full UP element or either the consensus proximal or distal UP element subsites, instead of the rrnB P1 UP element (6,17). As transcription from the rrnB P1 core promoter in comparison to a control promoter, the RNA-I promoter, encoded on the same plasmid, was relatively unaffected by alterations to the length of the alpha interdomain linker, i.e. the ratio of transcripts originating from the core rrnB P1 and RNA-I promoters remained constant (Fig. 3A), transcription from the rrnB P1 core promoter was used to normalise the activities of the RNAP preparations containing interdomain linkers of different lengths.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 18 22 18 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 178 179 174 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f3">3</xref>
###xml 233 237 229 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 292 293 288 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f3">3</xref>
 At the wild type rrnB P1 promoter, a progressive decrease in RNAP activity was observed as the size of the deletion introduced into the alpha subunit linker was increased (Fig. 3B). The effect of linker deletion was similar for the rrnB P1 promoter containing the consensus UP element (Fig. 3C), although the extent of the decrease was less marked. Removal of 12 amino acids from the alpha linker resulted in an 80-90% decrease in transcriptional efficiency from the promoters with the wild type and consensus UP elements, almost as large a decrease as was observed following complete removal of the alphaCTD. Transcription from these promoters was much less sensitive to increases in the length of the alpha linker. Thus, insertion of 16 amino acids resulted in only a 20-25% decrease in transcription from both promoters, and RNAP containing a 32 amino acid insertion in the alpha linker retained approximately half of wild type RNAP activity at these promoters.
###end p 27

###xml 70 74 66 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 244 248 240 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 371 375 363 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 440 444 432 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 466 467 458 459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f3">3</xref>
 The effect of altering alpha linker length on transcription from the rrnB P1 promoter containing only the consensus distal subsite or only the consensus proximal UP subsite was broadly similar to the overall pattern observed for the wild type rrnB P1 promoter, although the effect of deletion of three amino acids from the alpha linker was greater on transcription from rrnB P1 containing the proximal UP element subsite than on the other rrnB P1 derivatives (Fig. 3D and E).

To investigate the effects of altering the length of the alpha interdomain linker on UP element-dependent transcription in vivo, plasmids expressing each of the mutant rpoA alleles were introduced into NK5031 harbouring single copy transcriptional fusions of the lacZ gene to various rrnB P1 promoter derivatives. beta-Galactosidase activities were measured following induction of mutant alpha synthesis for three to four generations. ###xml 1093 1097 1062 1066 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 1118 1119 1087 1088 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f4">4</xref>
###xml 1161 1162 1130 1131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f4">4</xref>
###xml 1455 1462 1412 1419 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1520 1528 1477 1485 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1593 1597 1550 1554 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
 Unlike the results in vitro, deletions of more than three amino acids from within the alpha linker strongly stimulated transcription from the core rrnB P1 promoter in vivo (Fig. 4C). Thus, cells containing RNAP in which the Delta12 alpha subunit was incorporated into RNAP gave rise to an approximately15-fold increase in the beta-galactosidase activity relative to cells expressing only wild type rpoA. Increasing the length of the alpha linker caused a gradual but much less profound increase in transcription from the core promoter. The increase in rrnB P1 core promoter activity in vivo can be interpreted in the context of our understanding of rRNA transcription regulation (58,59); i.e. decreased rRNA transcription (from decreased UP element function) leads to compensating derepression of rrnB P1 core promoter activity to keep total rRNA synthesis constant (see ref. 8 and Discussion). Figure 4D and E illustrates the effects of the altered linkers on UP element function in vivo, adjusted for these feedback effects. The fold increases in transcription resulting from the wild type rrnB P1 UP element (Fig. 4D) or from the consensus UP element (Fig. 4E) in the presence of each of the alpha linker mutants are presented as a percentage of the fold increases in transcription observed in the presence of wild type alpha. In this representation, it is obvious that the alpha linker deletions exert a strong negative effect on UP element function in vivo, consistent with their effects on UP element utilisation in vitro. Likewise, it is apparent that UP element-dependent function at rrnB P1 is also impaired by insertions within the alpha linker, although to a lesser degree.
###end p 31
###begin title 32
###xml 97 101 89 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
Effect of shortening the alpha linker on interactions between alphaCTD and the UP element at the rrnB P1 promoter
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 137 141 133 137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 272 276 264 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 476 478 468 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c14">14</xref>
###xml 486 487 478 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f5">5</xref>
###xml 690 692 678 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c14">14</xref>
###xml 693 695 681 683 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c17">17</xref>
###xml 860 861 840 841 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f5">5</xref>
###xml 1194 1195 1162 1163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f5">5</xref>
###xml 1365 1366 1325 1326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c8">8</xref>
###xml 1455 1456 1411 1412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f5">5</xref>
###xml 1867 1875 1811 1819 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1983 1987 1927 1931 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 2088 2096 2032 2040 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 2101 2108 2045 2052 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 2151 2155 2095 2099 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
To determine whether there is a correlation between the effect of shortening the alpha interdomain linker on the activity of RNAP at the rrnB P1 promoter, and the ability of alphaCTD to bind the UP element, we carried out hydroxyl radical footprinting experiments on RNAP:rrnB P1 binary complexes. As previously observed, wild type RNAP protected a continuous region extending from approximately position -10 to +16 (not shown) and short regions around positions -20 and -30 (14) (Fig. 5A, lane 3, and B). In addition, short protected regions were observed around positions -43 and -53, corresponding to the sites of alphaCTD interaction within the proximal and distal UP element subsites (14,17). RNAP reconstituted with the Delta6 alpha derivative affords a similar degree of protection of the proximal and distal UP element subsites as wild type RNAP (Fig. 5A, lane 4, and B). However, protection of both UP element subsites was greatly diminished following deletion of a further three amino acids from the alpha linker (Delta9 RNAP) and was essentially abolished following deletion of 12 amino acids (Delta12 RNAP), without a concomitant reduction in the protection downstream of -40 (Fig. 5A, lanes 5 and 6, and B). RNAP reconstituted with the Delta235 alpha subunit failed to protect the UP element subsites from hydroxyl radical attack, as shown previously (8), confirming that the protections observed around -43 and -53 are due to alphaCTD (Fig. 5A, lane 7, and B). The pattern of protection produced in the core promoter region by each of the linker modified RNAPs and the Delta235 RNAP derivative was essentially identical to that afforded by wild type RNAP. These results indicate that as many as six amino acids can be deleted from the alpha interdomain linker without significantly compromising the ability of alphaCTD to engage the UP element subsites in vitro. The strong correlation between the transcriptional activity of the different RNAP deletion derivatives at rrnB P1 and their ability to protect the UP element subsites suggests that that the observed decrease in in vitro and in vivo transcriptional activity at the wild type rrnB P1 promoter upon shortening the alpha interdomain linker is due to a decreased ability to interact with the UP element.
###end p 33
###begin title 34
###xml 121 129 117 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 134 141 130 137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
Effect of increasing the length of the alpha interdomain linker on stimulation of transcription by displaced UP elements in vitro and in vivo
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 117 125 113 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 266 270 258 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 441 445 421 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 537 538 517 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f3">3</xref>
###xml 617 618 597 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f6">6</xref>
###xml 923 927 895 899 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 940 948 912 920 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
The footprinting experiments suggest that the 'reach' of alphaCTD is a function of linker length. Therefore, we used in vitro transcription assays to investigate whether tethering alphaCTD by a longer linker could improve the utilisation of displaced UP elements at rrnB P1. We observed no significant difference between the activity of RNAP reconstituted with the Omega13 alpha derivative and RNAP reconstituted with wild type alpha at the rrnB P1 promoter containing the UP element either in the normal position (as also shown in Fig. 3B) or when the UP element was displaced by one or two turns of the helix (Fig. 6A). A similar pattern was observed when RNAPs reconstituted with alpha mutants possessing 10 or 16 additional amino acids were compared with wild type RNAP (results not shown). We conclude that extending the alpha linker does not significantly enhance the response of RNAP to displaced UP elements at the rrnB P1 promoter in vitro.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 116 123 112 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 171 175 167 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ</italic>
###xml 179 183 175 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 292 296 288 292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 324 328 312 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 358 366 346 354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 571 572 556 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f6">6</xref>
 To explore the effect of lengthening the alpha interdomain linker on the response of RNAP to displaced UP elements in vivo, the strains harbouring single copy fusions of lacZ to rrnB P1 promoters possessing displaced consensus UP elements were transformed with plasmids expressing wild type rpoA, or the Omega6 and Omega13 rpoA mutants. Consistent with our in vitro observations, beta-galactosidase assays indicated that possession of an extended linker does not significantly increase the activity of RNAP at any of the promoters containing displaced UP elements (Fig. 6B).
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 314 316 314 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c15">15</xref>
###xml 570 574 563 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ</italic>
###xml 583 587 576 580 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 720 722 713 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c15">15</xref>
 Previous studies have demonstrated that displacement of the distal UP element subsite by one helical turn, while retaining the proximal subsite in its normal location, does not impair UP element-dependent transcription, whereas displacement of the distal subsite by two or three turns abolishes its contribution (15). To examine whether extending the alpha interdomain linker compensates for the negative effect of displacing the distal UP element subsite alone, we performed beta-galactosidase measurements on a series of strains harbouring single copy fusions of the lacZ gene to rrnB P1 promoter derivatives in which the distal UP element subsite had been displaced by a half, one, two and three turns of the helix (15). Our results showed that the activity of all the promoter variants was unaffected in cells containing Omega6 or Omega13 alpha subunits (results not shown). These results suggest that extending the alpha subunit linker also does not compensate for the negative effect on transcription of displacement of the distal UP element subsite.
###end p 37
###begin title 38
DISCUSSION
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 301 305 297 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 576 578 568 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c60">60</xref>
###xml 579 581 571 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c61">61</xref>
###xml 709 713 697 701 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 962 966 938 942 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 1069 1070 1045 1046 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c7">7</xref>
###xml 1071 1073 1047 1049 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c32">32</xref>
###xml 1115 1117 1091 1093 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c23">23</xref>
The experiments described here provide important information regarding the requirements for productive interactions between alphaCTD and UP elements. We have shown that upstream displacement of the full UP element by 5 bp or more greatly reduces UP element-dependent stimulation of transcription from rrnB P1. Our results are consistent with previous observations that alphaCTD can sometimes bind to the opposite face of the DNA helix to that occupied by the rest of RNAP, but that such interactions do not stimulate transcription and can in fact induce an inhibitory effect (60,61). As there appears to be a requirement for UP element:alphaCTD interactions to be located on the same face of the DNA helix at rrnB P1 as the rest of RNAP, adjacent to sigma bound at the -35 region, it is possible that optimal UP element function requires contact between alphaCTD and sigma at some promoters. Recent evidence supports the importance of such an interaction at the rrnB P1 promoter containing the proximal UP element subsite (W.E.Ross and R.L.Gourse, unpublished results; 7,32) and at Class I CRP-dependent promoters (23; N.J.Savery, G.Lloyd, S.J.W.Busby, M.S.Thomas, R.H.Ebright and R.L.Gourse, unpublished results). Alternatively, it is possible that insertions between the -35 hexamer and the UP element result in perturbations in DNA structure that interfere with alphaCTD function.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 72 76 72 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 346 348 342 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c15">15</xref>
###xml 545 547 537 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c60">60</xref>
###xml 548 550 540 542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c62">62</xref>
###xml 660 678 636 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas putida</italic>
###xml 678 680 654 656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pu</italic>
###xml 691 693 667 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c12">12</xref>
###xml 740 741 699 700 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 741 742 700 701 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L</sub>
###xml 753 755 712 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c40">40</xref>
###xml 756 758 715 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c41">41</xref>
###xml 1060 1072 1011 1023 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac(ICAP)UV5</italic>
###xml 1105 1107 1056 1058 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c39">39</xref>
 Displacement of the full UP element by two (or more) turns upstream of rrnB P1 (i.e. moving the centres of the proximal and distal subsites to -64 and -75, respectively) essentially abolishes UP element-dependent stimulation, and displacement of the distal UP element subsite was shown previously to decrease the efficiency of alphaCTD binding (15). Conversely, experiments with promoters containing displaced CRP sites have revealed that alphaCTD can bind adjacent to CRP at positions as far as 85 bp upstream of the transcription start site (60,62). alphaCTD is also able to access naturally occurring UP elements located approximately80 bp upstream of the Pseudomonas putidaPu promoter (12) or approximately90 bp upstream of the lambda PL promoter (40,41). However, in these cases, the interaction between alphaCTD and more distant DNA binding sites is facilitated by the binding of the transcription factors CRP and IHF, respectively, to upstream sequences. Although alphaCTD can be crosslinked to DNA sites as far as 63, 73, 83 and 93 bp upstream of the lac(ICAP)UV5 promoter in the absence of CRP (39), these presumably transient interactions do not facilitate transcription initiation.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 9 10 9 10 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f3">3</xref>
###xml 11 12 11 12 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f5">5</xref>
###xml 191 195 183 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 291 295 279 283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 383 385 371 373 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c38">38</xref>
###xml 489 497 477 485 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 608 612 592 596 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 625 632 609 616 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 639 640 623 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f4">4</xref>
###xml 708 714 692 698 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacUV5</italic>
###xml 991 992 971 972 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c8">8</xref>
###xml 1051 1055 1031 1035 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 1076 1080 1056 1060 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 1098 1102 1078 1082 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ</italic>
###xml 1415 1416 1375 1376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c8">8</xref>
###xml 1489 1492 1449 1452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrn</italic>
###xml 1538 1544 1498 1504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 1703 1704 1663 1664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c8">8</xref>
###xml 1705 1707 1665 1667 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c58">58</xref>
###xml 1708 1710 1668 1670 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c63">63</xref>
###xml 1799 1806 1755 1762 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1877 1878 1833 1834 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke549f4">4</xref>
###xml 1538 1544 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
 Figures 3-5 indicate that there is a good correlation between alpha linker length, the ability of alphaCTD to access the UP element and the degree of UP element-dependent stimulation at the rrnB P1 promoter. Our observations on the effect of alpha linker deletion on transcription from the rrnB P1 promoter are also broadly similar to the results obtained by Fujita and colleagues (38), although they reported a more pronounced effect of the shorter deletion derivatives on transcription in vitro. In contrast, shortening the alpha subunit interdomain linker exerted a strong stimulatory effect on the core rrnB P1 promoter in vivo (Fig. 4C). This phenomenon was not observed for the UP element-independent lacUV5 promoter (W.Meng, S.J.W.Busby and M.S.Thomas, unpublished results). The decreased efficiency of UP element utilisation by RNAPs containing deletions within the alpha subunit linker would be expected to result in a transient decrease in rRNA synthesis. As reported previously (8), when UP element function is compromised by mutations in rpoA, the activity of an rrnB P1 core promoter-lacZ fusion increases. For reasons that remain unclear, the magnitude of the 'derepression' observed in the presence of overexpressed linker modified alpha subunits was greater than the approximately3-fold increase in core promoter activity measured in the presence of alpha subunits devoid of the C-terminal domain (8). The increase in UP element-independent promoter activity in the seven rrn operons responsible for rRNA biosynthesis in E.coli compensates for the decrease in UP element function and the resulting transient reduction in ribosome synthesis, a phenomenon referred to as 'rRNA feedback' (8,58,63). However, the effects of the alpha linker mutants on UP element function were apparent in vivo when the effects of rRNA feedback were taken into consideration (Fig. 4D and E).
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 91 95 87 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 321 325 313 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrnB</italic>
###xml 607 609 595 597 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c37">37</xref>
 Increasing the length of the alpha interdomain linker also impairs transcription from the rrnB P1 promoter, although RNAP appears less sensitive to linker insertions than deletions. This result indicates that the length of the wild type alpha interdomain linker is optimal for efficient utilisation of the UP element at rrnB P1. It is probable that the linker length adopted by bacterial RNAP results, at least in part, from selective pressure for UP element utilisation at rRNA promoters. In this context, it is interesting that chloroplasts contain alpha subunits possessing considerably longer linkers (37), suggesting that chloroplast RNAP is not subject to the same constraints as the bacterial enzyme.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 696 698 668 670 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke549c38">38</xref>
 Increasing the length of the linker by 16 amino acids (essentially doubling its length) would be expected to result in extending the 'reach' of alphaCTD by approximately58 A, enough to allow utilisation of UP elements positioned one or two turns upstream of the normal location (the pitch of B-form DNA is 34 A per turn). As RNAP containing alpha subunits with linkers lengthened by up to 32 amino acids did not increase utilisation of displaced UP elements, it is possible that the alpha linkers containing insertions are not fully extended. Studies by Fujita and colleagues suggest, in fact, that segments of the alpha linker may adopt an ordered (helical) structure under certain conditions (38). In conjunction with the possible requirement for alpha-sigma interactions and/or DNA structural requirements at the junction of the -35 region and UP element, the possibility that the linker has structure might explain why lengthening the linker does not extend the operational distance of alphaCTD with respect to UP element utilisation.
###end p 43
