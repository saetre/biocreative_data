Cloning of somatolactin alpha, beta forms and the somatolactin receptor in Atlantic salmon: Seasonal expression profile in pituitary and ovary of maturing female broodstock
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 427 442 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 444 455 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Salmo salar</span>
Somatolactin (Sl) is a fish specific adenohypophyseal peptide hormone related to growth hormone (Gh). Some species, including salmonids, possess two forms: Sl alpha and Sl beta. The somatolactin receptor (slr) is closely related to the growth hormone receptor (ghr). Sl has been ascribed many physiological functions, including a role in sexual maturation. In order to clarify the role of Sl in the sexual maturation of female Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar), the full length cDNAs of slr, Sl alpha and Sl beta were cloned and their expression was studied throughout a seasonal reproductive cycle using real-time quantitative PCR (RTqPCR).
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 0 15 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
Atlantic salmon Sl alpha, Sl beta and slr cDNAs were cloned using a PCR approach. Gene expression of Sl alpha, SL beta and slr was studied using RTqPCR over a 17 month period encompassing pre-vitellogenesis, vitellogenesis, ovulation and post ovulation in salmon females. Histological examination of ovarian samples allowed for the classification according to the degree of follicle maturation into oil drop, primary, secondary or tertiary yolk stage.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 172 187 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 298 313 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
The mature peptide sequences of Sl alpha, Sl beta and slr are highly similar to previously cloned salmonid forms and contained the typical motifs. Phylogenetic analysis of Atlantic salmon Sl alpha and Sl beta shows that these peptides group into the two Sl clades present in some fish species. The Atlantic salmon slr grouped with salmonid slr amongst so-called type I ghr. An increase in pituitary Sl alpha and Sl beta transcripts before and during spawning, with a decrease post-ovulation, and a constant expression level of ovarian slr were observed. There was also a transient increase in Sl alpha and Sl beta in May prior to transfer from seawater to fresh water and ensuing fasting.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusion
###end title 8
###begin p 9
The up-regulation of Sl alpha and Sl beta during vitellogenesis and spawning, with a subsequent decrease post-ovulation, supports a role for Sl during gonadal growth and spawning. Sl could also be involved in calcium/phosphate mobilization associated with vitellogenesis or have a role in energy homeostasis associated with lipolysis during fasting. The up-regulation of both Sl alpha and Sl beta prior to fasting and freshwater transfer, suggests a role for Sl linked to reproduction that may be independent of the maturation induced fasting.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Background
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 173 174 173 174 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 175 176 175 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 261 277 261 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pars intermedia </italic>
###xml 496 497 489 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 562 580 555 573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oncorhynchus masou</italic>
###xml 583 584 576 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 822 823 815 816 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 549 560 <span type="species:ncbi:8020">masu salmon</span>
###xml 562 580 <span type="species:ncbi:8020">Oncorhynchus masou</span>
###xml 830 841 <span type="species:ncbi:8020">masu salmon</span>
Somatolactin (Sl) is a peptide hormone exclusive to fish, and belongs to the class I helical cytokine family which includes growth hormone (Gh), prolactin (Prl) and leptin [1-3]. Two distinct forms coded by separate genes and produced in different cells in the pars intermedia of the pituitary have been isolated: Sl alpha (Slalpha) which is present in all fish, and Sl beta (Slbeta) which has only been found in a limited number or species spending all or part of their lifecycle in freshwater [4]. The Sl receptor (slr), recently characterized in masu salmon (Oncorhynchus masou) [5], is closely related to the Gh receptor (ghr) with which it shares many structural features. The degree of sequence similarity has led to categorization of all known putative fish ghrs and slrs into two types of ghr; type I and type II [6]. The masu salmon slr appears more closely related to the type I ghr than the type II ghr.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 202 203 202 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 204 205 204 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 239 248 239 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O. mykiss</italic>
###xml 270 284 270 284 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O. tshawytscha</italic>
###xml 338 339 338 339 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 340 342 340 342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 365 372 365 372 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O. keta</italic>
###xml 432 434 432 434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 554 562 554 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O. nerka</italic>
###xml 590 592 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 593 595 593 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 695 697 695 697 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 224 237 <span type="species:ncbi:8022">rainbow trout</span>
###xml 239 248 <span type="species:ncbi:8022">O. mykiss</span>
###xml 254 268 <span type="species:ncbi:74940">chinook salmon</span>
###xml 270 284 <span type="species:ncbi:74940">O. tshawytscha</span>
###xml 352 363 <span type="species:ncbi:8018">chum salmon</span>
###xml 365 372 <span type="species:ncbi:8018">O. keta</span>
###xml 554 562 <span type="species:ncbi:8023">O. nerka</span>
###xml 574 588 <span type="species:ncbi:74940">chinook salmon</span>
Like Gh and Prl, Sl is thought to have multiple functions including a undefined role in reproduction. In salmonids, an increase in plasma Sl levels concomitant with final gonadal growth has been found [7,8]. Also, in mature rainbow trout (O. mykiss) and chinook salmon (O. tshawytscha), plasma Sl levels are higher than in immature fish [9,10], and in chum salmon (O. keta), Sl mRNA expression is elevated during sexual maturation [11]. Further, immunocytochemistry indicates an activation of somatolactotrophs in sexually maturing and spawning sockeye (O. nerka), chum and chinook salmon [12,13]. However, other studies have found no correlation between plasma Sl and final gonadal maturation [14], and therefore, the specific actions of Sl during salmonid reproduction are not clear.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 62 64 62 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 87 88 87 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 89 90 89 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 514 530 514 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pars intermedia </italic>
###xml 531 533 531 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 534 536 534 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 635 654 635 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">color interfere (ci</italic>
###xml 657 672 657 672 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oryzias latipes</italic>
###xml 675 677 675 677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 707 711 707 711 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr </italic>
###xml 776 777 776 777 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 778 780 778 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 467 480 <span type="species:ncbi:8022">rainbow trout</span>
###xml 628 634 <span type="species:ncbi:8090">medaka</span>
###xml 657 672 <span type="species:ncbi:8090">Oryzias latipes</span>
###xml 752 763 <span type="species:ncbi:8019">coho salmon</span>
###xml 768 774 <span type="species:ncbi:8090">medaka</span>
The hormone has been suggested to act during early oogenesis [15], gonadal maturation [7,8] and gonadal steroid biosynthesis [16]. Sl has also been proposed to act as a facilitator of oocyte maturation through its regulation of lipid metabolism which in salmonids is believed to be linked to suppression of organ accumulation of fat. This is based on the high amount of intraperitoneal fat and an enlarged liver with high glycogen and fat content found in the cobalt rainbow trout mutant which lacks the pituitary pars intermedia [17-19], the high triglyceride and cholesterol content in liver and muscle of Sl deficient mutant medaka color interfere (ci) (Oryzias latipes) [20], and the high expression of slr transcripts in liver and visceral fat in coho salmon and medaka [5,20].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 121 132 114 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmo salar</italic>
###xml 379 382 372 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr</italic>
###xml 399 414 392 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945;, Sl&#946;</italic>
###xml 104 119 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 121 132 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Salmo salar</span>
###xml 544 559 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
The current study has two major aims: 1) to obtain the full cDNA sequence of Slalpha, Slbeta and slr in Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) and to phylogenetically analyse their structure in relationship to other fish Sl, slr and ghr sequences, and 2) to begin to clarify the role of these genes in late oocyte female maturation. To do this, gene expression was measured in the ovary (slr) and pituitary (Slalpha, Slbeta) using RTqPCR in 3+ year old female salmon during a 17-month period comprising late oocyte growth, ovulation and post ovulation. Atlantic salmon was chosen as an important model salmonid species of commercial value.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Methods
###end title 15
###begin title 16
###xml 14 29 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
Sequencing of Atlantic salmon Slalpha and Slbeta
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 372 374 372 374 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 429 431 425 427 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 553 554 537 538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 649 651 630 632 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 761 762 733 734 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 1335 1336 1268 1269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 64 79 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 401 414 <span type="species:ncbi:8022">rainbow trout</span>
###xml 453 468 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 584 597 <span type="species:ncbi:8022">rainbow trout</span>
###xml 685 700 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 1425 1440 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
Total RNA was extracted from six pooled pituitaries of juvenile Atlantic salmon stored in RNAlater (Ambion, Austin, TX) using the RNeasy Mini kit (Qiagen, Germantown, MD) with treatment with RNase free DNase I (Qiagen). Three micrograms of this total RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA using Power Script Reverse Transcriptase (Clontech, Palo Alto, CA) using oligo (dT)12. Two primers based on the rainbow trout Slalpha cDNA [21] were used to obtain Atlantic salmon Slalpha open reading frame (ORF) of 721 nucleotides: SlalphaF1 and SlalphaR1 (Table 1). A primer pair based on the rainbow trout Sl-like protein (rtSLP), the putative Slbeta form [22], was used to obtain the partial Atlantic salmon Slbeta ORF of 627 nucleotides: SlbetaF1 and SlbetaR1 (Table 1). PCR was carried out for 30 cycles of 94degreesC for 15 s, 55degreesC for 30 s and 68degreesC for 60 s using Platinum Pfx DNA polymerase (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). The resulting bands, 720 bp for Slalpha and 610 bp for Slbeta as seen on 1.2% agarose gel, were purified and directly sequenced by an ABI capillary sequencer (MWG Biotech, Ebersberg, Germany). The corresponding 5' and 3' ends were obtained by RACE with the SMART RACE cDNA Amplification Kit (Clontech) using gene specific primers: Slalpha5RACErev, Slalpha3RACEfor, Slbeta5RACErev and Slbeta3RACEfor (Table 1). All RACE products were gel purified, subcloned and sequenced and yielded the complete Atlantic salmon Slalpha and Slbeta cDNA.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
Primer sequences for PCR cloning.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
###xml 14 29 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
Sequencing of Atlantic salmon slr
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 243 244 243 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 398 399 398 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 931 932 913 914 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 59 74 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 226 237 <span type="species:ncbi:8020">masu salmon</span>
###xml 265 280 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 790 805 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
Total RNA was extracted from about 30 mg of liver of adult Atlantic salmon and this and production of cDNA was carried out as described above for the two Sl forms. Three overlapping pairs of primers were designed based on the masu salmon slr [5] and differing from Atlantic salmon ghr and trout prl receptor; pair one slrF1 and slrR1, pair two slrF3 and slrR2 and pair three slrF7 and slrR9 (Table 1). cDNA was amplified by PCR with Advantage 2 Polymerase (Clontech). Cycling consisted in 35 cycles of 95degreesC for 30 s, 61degreesC for 45 s and 72degreesC for 90 s. The resulting products yielded bands of the expected sizes. The bands were purified from the gel, subcloned into pGEM-T Easy Vector (Promega, Madison, WI) and sequenced, resulting in 2064 nucleotides of sequence. The full Atlantic salmon slr was obtained using the SMART RACE cDNA Amplification Kit using gene specific primers: slr5RACErev and slr3RACEfor (Table 1).
###end p 20
###begin title 21
Phylogenetic analysis
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 76 78 76 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 234 236 234 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
Protein sequence alignments were carried out with ClustalW (1.83) software [23]. Phylogenetic analysis of putative fish slr and ghr sequences and Sl sequences were made by the maximum likelihood method using the PHYLIP 3.6a3 package [24]. Branch lengths show genetic change and bootstrap values are shown as % on the branches (100 datasets). The analysis using parsimony and distance-matrix methods such as UPGMA and neighbour joining generated trees of similar topology and lower bootstrap values.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
###xml 23 27 23 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr </italic>
###xml 41 49 41 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 53 60 49 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
Measurement of ovarian slr and pituitary Slalpha and Slbeta gene expression
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 235 242 235 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ad lib </italic>
###xml 0 15 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
Atlantic salmon were raised from fertilized eggs at the Matre Aquaculture Research Station, Matredal, Norway. After smoltification in their second spring (1+ aged fish), the mixed population was transferred to seawater netpens and fed ad lib with a commercial dry pelleted feed. Three year old females were sampled on 15 occasions during a 17-month period from August 2004 to December 2005, a month after spawning. The fish were held in seawater netpens until May 2005, when they were moved to freshwater tanks just after the May sampling. In the freshwater tanks, the fish were subject to simulated natural photoperiod and ambient water temperature. They stopped voluntary feeding after transfer to the tanks and fasted until the end of the experiment. Thus, the project covers the period of oocyte maturation from early secondary growth (oil-drop stage) through vitellogenesis, ovulation and post ovulation.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 480 497 474 491 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">medulla oblongata</italic>
###xml 829 830 814 815 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 879 881 864 866 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
At approximately monthly intervals, six to ten females were sampled under metomidate anesthesia (6 mg/L, Syndel, Victoria, B.C.). All fish were treated and killed according to Norwegian National Legislation for laboratory animals. The animals were measured and weighed. Blood was drawn from the caudal vein, plasma obtained by centrifugation and quick frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at -80degreesC until analysis. Then the fish were immediately sacrificed by sectioning the medulla oblongata, gonads excised and weighted to calculate gonadosomatic index (GSI; gonad weight as a percentage of total body weight). Ovary samples consisting of a piece of ovarian lamella and whole pituitaries were quick frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored in -80degreesC until analyzed for RNA extraction. Plasma levels of 17beta-estradiol (E2) and testosterone (T) were determined by ELISA [25].
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 787 793 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
To assess the expression profile in the ovaries, total RNA was isolated from about 60 mg frozen tissue using the FastPrepPro RNA Isolation System (Qbiogene, Illkirch, France) according to the manufacturer's instructions. For pituitaries, total RNA from individual pituitaries was obtained with the PARIStrade mark Kit (Ambion) in combination with the FastPrep RNA Isolation System (Qbiogene) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Quantity and quality of the RNA were determined by UV absorbance at 230, 260 and 280 nm. Total RNA was treated with DNase I (Promega, Southampton, UK) and the integrity of the RNA was verified with a Bioanalyzer 2100 expert system (Agilent Technologies, Palo Alto, CA, USA). Reverse transcription into cDNA took place in the presence of the Moloney murine leukaemia virus reverse transcriptase using a Reverse Transcription Core kit (RT-RTCK-05, Eurogentec, Searing, Belgium) according to the manufacturer's instructions, with 500 ng total RNA in a 30 mul reaction volume. The cDNA was diluted 10-fold with nuclease-free water.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 28 35 28 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945;</italic>
###xml 37 44 33 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 48 52 41 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr </italic>
###xml 418 455 409 442 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">elongation factor 1 alpha (ef1&#945;) </italic>
###xml 562 566 549 553 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr </italic>
###xml 622 626 609 613 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr </italic>
###xml 642 643 629 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 697 698 684 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 858 862 845 847 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174; </sup>
###xml 1124 1126 1097 1099 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 1196 1204 1169 1173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ef1&#945;</italic>
###xml 402 417 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 629 640 <span type="species:ncbi:8020">masu salmon</span>
Relative gene expression of Slalpha, Slbeta and slr was measured by TaqMan PCR assays in duplicate, using 96-well optical plates on an ABI PRISM 7700 Sequence Detector, using default settings. For each 25 mul PCR reaction, 5 mul cDNA was mixed with 200 nM fluorogenic probe, 900 nM sense primer, 900 nM antisense primer in 1xTaqMan Universal PCR Master Mix (Applied Biosystems (ABI), Foster City, CA). Atlantic salmon elongation factor 1 alpha (ef1alpha) [GenBank:] was used as a house-keeping reference gene after validating its stable temporal expression. The slr primer pair and probe were based on those used to study slr in masu salmon [5] and were located in the intracellular domain (Table 2). The specificity of all primer pairs and corresponding probes was validated by PCR amplification, sequencing of products and melting curve analysis using SYBR(R) Green (Invitrogen). The efficiency of the systems was validated by running dilution series which gave DeltaCt/log cDNA curves with slopes <0.1 Relative gene expression was calculated using the comparative method (DeltaDeltaCt) as described in detail previously [26] where the target gene is normalized to the internal reference gene (ef1alpha) and target amount given relative to the calibrator (initial August sample).
###end p 27
###begin p 28
Primers and probes for RT qPCR.
###end p 28
###begin title 29
Histological analysis of oocyte maturation
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 386 388 386 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
At the time of sampling, a piece of ovarian lamella was isolated by transversal cuts with a scalpel blade from each female and fixed in Bouin's fixative for histological analysis. Fixed samples were transferred to ethanol (70%), and subsequently dehydrated and embedded in paraffin according to conventional techniques. Histological examination was carried out as described previously [27] using 5 mum sections after hematoxylin-eosin staining. Animals considered as immature, i.e. showing primary growth phase follicles only, were not found in the present study. As salmon females are not fully synchronous in their sexual maturation, females sampled at a certain date may have reached different levels of maturity. Thus, it is important not only to analyze the data in relation to date, but also in relation to maturational status. This was determined for each sampled female according to the most advanced type of follicles as follows: (i) oocytes with cortical alveoli and perinuclear oil droplets (oil drop), or (ii) showing follicles with oocytes in true vitellogenesis. The latter were further differentiated in oocytes showing a limited number of small eosinophilic yolk globules in the periphery of the oocyte (primary yolk stage), with numerous but still small yolk globules distributed throughout the oocyte (secondary yolk stage), or with oocytes filled with large yolk globules (tertiary yolk stage). This analysis was carried out until all ovaries were found to have reached tertiary yolk state (June).
###end p 30
###begin p 31
Both in the time-based (n = 6-10) and in the maturation-based (n = 14-24) analyses, the data were tested for homogeneity of variance using Levene's test, and where appropriate, the data were log-transformed or arctan-transformed before analysis of variance (ANOVA with P < 0.0166 to compensate for comparison of three variables) using SPSS version 15.0 (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL). Tukey's multiple means comparison test was performed as post-hoc test. Correlation analysis using two-tailed Spearman's rho was carried out at each sampling date and for each maturational category. All data are presented as means +/- standard error of the mean (SEM).
###end p 31

###xml 0 7 0 3 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945;</italic>

In order to determine expression levels of Slalpha, Slbeta and slr mRNA, a series of tissue samples from 3 immature post-smolt females from the same strain that was used in the maturation expression study where collected, flash frozen and stored at -80degreesC. Total RNA was extracted as described above and cDNA obtained from pituitary, brain, gill, liver, ovary, head kidney, kidney, spleen, muscle, intestine, pylorous and heart. Gene expression was determined by RTqPCR using the same primer pairs and cycling conditions as described above.

###begin title 34
Results

###begin title 35

Atlantic salmon Slalpha and Slbeta
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 211 212 207 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 15 30 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
The cloning of Atlantic salmon Slalpha yielded the complete cDNA of 2331 base pairs (bp) that included 30 bp of 5'untranslated region (UTR), 699 bp of open reading frame (ORF), and a long 3'UTR of 1602 bp (Fig. 1). The ORF translated into 232 amino acids (aa) including a 23 aa signal peptide and 209 aa mature protein with a predicted molecular weight (MW) of 24.2 KDa. There are eight cysteine residues in the full-length protein of which seven are present in the mature protein. There are two polyadenylation signals.

###xml 39 40 39 40 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 304 319 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>

###xml 546 561 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 605 618 <span type="species:ncbi:8022">rainbow trout</span>
###xml 623 634 <span type="species:ncbi:8018">chum salmon</span>
The phylogenetic tree of the Sls (Fig. 3) shows the two well-differentiated clades of Slalpha and Slbeta. Up to date, seven species have beeen found to have putative Slbeta and all these species spend all or part of their lives in freshwater, none is exclusively marine. The degree of similarity between Atlantic salmon Slalpha and Slbeta was 54% for the protein and 65% for the coding DNA. The similarity of Slalpha and Slbeta with Atlantic salmon Gh1 was 26% and 23%, respectively, and with Atlantic salmon Prl, 19% and 17%, respectively. Both Atlantic salmon Slalpha and Slbeta were 96% similar to the rainbow trout and chum salmon Sls. The degree of similarity between the Slbeta of different fish species is less (42-53%) than for Slalpha (54-81%).

###xml 0 15 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
Atlantic salmon Sl receptor (slr)
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 114 115 114 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 4 19 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 370 385 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 876 891 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 927 942 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 979 994 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
The Atlantic salmon slr cDNA sequenced consists of 2,369 bp, including a 5'UTR of 77 bp and 3'UTR of 324 bp (Fig. 4). The ORF of 1968 nucleotides translates into 655 aa, including a 20 aa signal peptide, 228 aa extracellular domain (ECD), 23 aa transmembrane domain, and 384 aa intracellular domain (ICD). The predicted MW of the mature receptor is 70.5 KDa. The cloned Atlantic salmon slr shares features and domains present in the ghr: the typical FGEFS motif, and slightly different Box 1 (PPVPAPKIKGI) and Box 2 (DLYQDMPWVEFIELD) in the ICD. In addition to the five cysteine residues present in the ECD of most teleost ghr and prlr, it has two additional cysteine residues not present in most type II ghr, making a total of seven ECD cysteines. It has four, instead of five, potential N-linked glycosylation sites in the ECD and 11 intracellular tyrosine residues whereas Atlantic salmon ghr1 and ghr2 have only seven. The Atlantic salmon slr is 61 and 65 aa longer than the Atlantic salmon ghr1 and ghr2, respectively, and the differences are mainly found in the ICD signaling region.

The aa sequence of the Atlantic salmon slr is most similar (94%) to the masu salmon slr, while sharing 44%- 43% aa and 59% nucleotide similarity with Atlantic salmon ghr isoforms 1 and 2 (86% alike), and 17% similarity with rainbow trout prlr. Structure and characteristics of Atlantic salmon Slalpha and Slbeta
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 134 135 127 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>

###xml 1106 1108 1059 1061 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 1109 1111 1062 1064 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 1172 1173 1125 1126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 1222 1223 1175 1176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 1249 1263 1202 1216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sparus auratus</italic>
###xml 1441 1443 1394 1396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 1444 1446 1397 1399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 1507 1509 1460 1462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 1568 1584 1518 1534 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pars intermedia </italic>
###xml 1585 1586 1535 1536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 1694 1706 1641 1653 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gadus morhua</italic>
###xml 1709 1710 1656 1657 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 1767 1769 1711 1713 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 4 19 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 94 105 <span type="species:ncbi:8018">chum salmon</span>
###xml 110 123 <span type="species:ncbi:8022">rainbow trout</span>
###xml 181 196 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 743 755 <span type="species:ncbi:70862">teleost fish</span>
###xml 1242 1247 <span type="species:ncbi:323764">bream</span>
###xml 1249 1263 <span type="species:ncbi:8175">Sparus auratus</span>
###xml 1589 1604 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 1680 1692 <span type="species:ncbi:8049">Atlantic cod</span>
###xml 1694 1706 <span type="species:ncbi:8049">Gadus morhua</span>
###xml 1737 1750 <span type="species:ncbi:8022">rainbow trout</span>
The Atlantic salmon Slalpha and Slbeta bear high degree of aa similarity to previously cloned chum salmon and rainbow trout Sl forms [3,21,22]. When analyzed phylogenetically, each Atlantic salmon Sl form groups in a distinct Slalpha or Slbeta clade, as previously established for other species [4]. Slalpha and Slbeta are paralogous and are encoded by two separate genes [4]. Although three polyadenylation sites can be found in salmonid Slalpha, there appears to be only one 2.4 kb mRNA transcript [3]. Slalpha has been found in all fish species studied and its structure is more conserved among fish groups than the structure of Slbeta. This suggests a more central role of Slalpha in the endocrine regulation of physiological processes in teleost fish. The present data indicate that although Slbeta has a 29-fold lower expression level than Slalpha, the two genes have a very similar expression pattern during sexual maturation. The two Sl forms are produced by different cells in the pituitary gland. Slalpha is produced in somatolactotrophs in the posterior pars intermedia of the pituitary [1,2,18,28,29] that are periodic acid-Schiff (PAS)-negative in salmonids [2], perhaps due to lack of N-glycosylation sites [3]. In gilthead sea bream (Sparus auratus), these somatolactotrophs have been found to co-localize with parathyroid hormone related protein (PTHrP) cells and in some instances, both hormones are found in the same cell [30,31]. PTHrP is considered to be a hypercalcemic factor in fish [32]. Slbeta is produced by somatolactotrophs in the anterior pars intermedia [4]. Atlantic salmon Slbeta has the Asn-Lys-Thr site which has been found to be glycosylated in Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) [2] and is also conserved in rainbow trout Slbeta (rtSLP) [22], but not in salmonid Slalpha forms.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 31 52 31 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Protopterus annectens</italic>
###xml 65 88 65 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Acipenser transmontanus</italic>
###xml 214 216 214 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 375 388 375 388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fugu rubripes</italic>
###xml 390 413 390 413 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tetraodon nigroviridis </italic>
###xml 522 523 515 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 524 526 517 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 731 733 717 719 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 31 52 <span type="species:ncbi:7888">Protopterus annectens</span>
###xml 55 63 <span type="species:ncbi:61674">sturgeon</span>
###xml 65 88 <span type="species:ncbi:7904">Acipenser transmontanus</span>
###xml 94 106 <span type="species:ncbi:70862">teleost fish</span>
###xml 345 354 <span type="species:ncbi:61674">sturgeons</span>
###xml 375 388 <span type="species:ncbi:31033">Fugu rubripes</span>
###xml 390 412 <span type="species:ncbi:99883">Tetraodon nigroviridis</span>
###xml 417 423 <span type="species:ncbi:8090">medaka</span>
Sl has been found in lungfish (Protopterus annectens), sturgeon (Acipenser transmontanus) and teleost fish. It is therefore assumed to have existed in the ancestral tetrapod, but been lost in the tetrapod lineage [33]. The two Sl forms are thought to have originated during the teleost genome duplication event (3R), after the divergence of the sturgeons. A genome search in Fugu rubripes, Tetraodon nigroviridis and medaka reveals only the Slalpha form, so that in some groups, the Slbeta form appears to have been lost [4,33]. For the species studied, the intra-species aa similarity between the two forms is relatively low, only 41.8-49.8%. The beta form lacks the third cysteine of the seven conserved found in the alpha form [34] which could reduce its dimerization potential.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 168 170 164 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 282 284 278 280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 285 287 281 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>

###xml 341 343 337 339 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 344 346 340 342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 347 349 343 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 350 352 346 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 408 410 404 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 439 441 435 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 452 454 448 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 469 471 465 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 472 474 468 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 475 477 471 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 496 497 492 493 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 508 509 504 505 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 529 531 525 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 560 562 556 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
Like its sister hormones Gh and Prl, Slalpha is thought to have multiple functions. The hypothalamic regulation of Sl release appears different from that of Gh or Prl [35]. Some of the functions have been confirmed such as its role in body coloration through xantophore regulation [36-39] and control of lipolysis and energy mobilization [9,18,20,29,40]. Sl also appears to have a role in acid-base balance [41], homeostasis of phosphate [42], sodium [36] and calcium [14,31,43], smoltification [8], stress [9], immune function [44] and in gonadal maturation [15].
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 116 135 113 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ictalurus punctatus</italic>
###xml 154 177 151 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ctenopharyngodon idella</italic>
###xml 191 202 188 199 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Danio rerio</italic>
###xml 218 235 215 232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Carassius auratus</italic>
###xml 238 239 235 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 240 242 237 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 243 245 240 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 329 346 326 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anguilla anguilla</italic>
###xml 531 533 522 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 99 114 <span type="species:ncbi:7998">channel catfish</span>
###xml 116 135 <span type="species:ncbi:7998">Ictalurus punctatus</span>
###xml 142 152 <span type="species:ncbi:7959">grass carp</span>
###xml 154 177 <span type="species:ncbi:7959">Ctenopharyngodon idella</span>
###xml 180 189 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 191 202 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">Danio rerio</span>
###xml 208 216 <span type="species:ncbi:7957">goldfish</span>
###xml 218 235 <span type="species:ncbi:7957">Carassius auratus</span>
###xml 315 327 <span type="species:ncbi:7936">European eel</span>
###xml 329 346 <span type="species:ncbi:7936">Anguilla anguilla</span>
###xml 376 389 <span type="species:ncbi:8022">rainbow trout</span>
###xml 394 409 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 520 529 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Slbeta has only been found in a small number of species all of which are either freshwater-living (channel catfish, Ictalurus punctatus), the grass carp (Ctenopharyngodon idella), zebrafish (Danio rerio) and goldfish (Carassius auratus) [4,22,45], or spend a part of their life in fresh water within a catadromous (European eel, Anguilla anguilla) or an anadromous lifecycle (rainbow trout and Atlantic salmon). The specific roles of Slbeta are not clear though Slbeta has been found to induce melanosome aggregation in zebrafish [34].
###end p 63
###begin title 64
###xml 33 48 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
Structure and characteristics of Atlantic salmon slr
###end title 64
###begin p 65
###xml 351 352 351 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 703 704 703 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 801 803 801 803 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 261 276 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 334 345 <span type="species:ncbi:8020">Masu salmon</span>
###xml 412 427 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
The slr, as the ghr, belongs to the class I cytokine receptor super-family which also includes the prlr and the leptin receptor. These are known to act through JAKs (Janus kinases) and STATs (signal transducers and activators of transcription). The full-length Atlantic salmon slr cloned is very similar (93% aa) to the characterized Masu salmon slr [5]. It possesses basically the same structure and domains as Atlantic salmon ghr isoforms 1 and 2, but shares only 43 and 44% peptide similarity. Some important differences between slr and ghr are observed such as the number and position of extracellular cysteine residues which can form three disulfide bonds in the Atlantic slr and two in ghrs. Slr [5] possess a different site B than teleost ghr, which is believed to be a Gh-binding site in ghr [46]. The ICD is somewhat longer in the slr and has a larger number of tyrosine residues (eleven instead of seven). These are believed to be involved in intracellular signaling along with the Box I and Box II domains, which are also slightly different. The implications of the structural differences between the salmonid slr and ghr could be large in terms of differential specific binding and intracellular signaling.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 59 60 59 60 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 155 156 155 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 232 234 232 234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 235 237 235 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 502 504 502 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 682 683 682 683 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 736 738 736 738 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 876 878 876 878 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 917 919 917 919 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 1079 1081 1079 1081 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 346 361 <span type="species:ncbi:72011">black sea bream</span>
###xml 585 596 <span type="species:ncbi:8020">Masu salmon</span>
###xml 614 625 <span type="species:ncbi:8020">Masu salmon</span>
###xml 690 701 <span type="species:ncbi:8020">Masu salmon</span>
A phylogenetic analysis of putative fish slr and ghr (Fig. 5) reveals that salmonid slr form a clade amongst other non-salmonid ghr, so-called type I ghr [6]. In contrast, the two isoforms of salmonid ghr previously characterized [[47-49], GenBank: and GenBank:] form a distinct clade amongst other recently cloned non-salmonid type II ghr. Both black sea bream type I and type II ghr have been characterized in ligand binding studies as ghr, not slr, with the use of heterologous (salmon) Prl and Sl [50]. Native hormones have only been used to obtain binding characteristics for the Masu salmon ghr and slr. The Masu salmon slr was found to bind Sl with greater affinity than Gh [5]. The Masu salmon type II ghr bound Gh exclusively [47]. This has led some authors to postulate that type I ghr can phylogenetically be regarded as slr, while type II ghr would be "true" ghr [20]. However, a recent synteny analysis [33] has established that slr is a teleost-specific paralogue of ghr, i.e. it probably appeared by duplication of ghr during the teleost genome duplication event [28], in contrast to Sl, which existed before, but was lost in tetrapods.
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 114 116 114 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
The Salmonidae family underwent an additional whole-genome duplication event (4R) about 25-100 million years ago [51]. This gave rise to two closely related salmonid ghr type II isoforms. Only one type of salmonid slr has been found so far, but there could be another closely related isoform which could perhaps differentially bind Slalpha and Slbeta. The long evolutionary history of fish with episodes of genome doubling could have resulted in lineage-specific diversification of ghr and slr, binding Gh and Sl with varying degrees of affinity, and triggering different cellular responses in different groups of fish.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 148 150 148 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 332 333 332 333 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 493 502 493 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 506 514 506 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 133 138 <span type="species:ncbi:9940">sheep</span>
###xml 143 146 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Salmon slr appears to have reduced affinity for Gh, perhaps due to the lack of conserved site B involved in Gh binding, as found for sheep and rat [46]. Salmon slr was found to be less specific in differentiating between Sl and Gh than salmon type II ghr, and an eight-fold concentration of Gh displaced 50% of the Sl bound to slr [5]. This implies that in situations such as fasting, where Gh levels in plasma may become 10-fold higher than Sl, Gh could induce responses via the slr. Further in vitro and in vivo studies are needed to distinctly characterize both types of receptors in their binding, signaling and biological actions.
###end p 68
###begin title 69
###xml 38 53 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
Putative role of Sl and slr in female Atlantic salmon sexual maturation
###end title 69
###begin p 70
###xml 112 113 112 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 114 116 114 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 177 179 177 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 180 182 180 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 279 281 279 281 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 452 455 452 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ci </italic>
###xml 470 472 470 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 247 258 <span type="species:ncbi:8018">chum salmon</span>
###xml 445 451 <span type="species:ncbi:8090">medaka</span>
In salmonids, most studies link Sl to sexual maturation, as plasma Sl levels increase during sexual maturation [7-11] and somatolactotrophs appear activated during this period [12,13]. There are, however, conflicting data as shown in one study on chum salmon spawning migration [14] that found no correlation between plasma Sl and final gonadal maturation. Moreover, Sl function may not be crucial for reproductive success as the Sl-null mutant medaka ci can reproduce [20].
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 68 70 68 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 295 299 295 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr </italic>
###xml 482 486 482 486 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr </italic>
###xml 599 600 599 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 659 660 659 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F9">9</xref>
###xml 55 66 <span type="species:ncbi:8019">coho salmon</span>
###xml 526 537 <span type="species:ncbi:8019">coho salmon</span>
###xml 625 640 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
A recent study on previtellogenic oocyte maturation in coho salmon [15] found little evidence for a role of Sl during the cortical alveoli or lipid-droplet stage in this species. However, they did find indications that Sl may have a role at even earlier stages of oocyte development, as ovarian slr transcripts became downregulated as maturation advanced during the autumn and spring preceding ovulation. Furthermore, tissue screens have shown a relatively high expression level of slr transcripts in the gonads of 1 year old coho salmon parr, lesser only to that of liver, visceral fat and muscle [5] and in immature female Atlantic salmon post-smolts (Fig. 9).
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 43 47 43 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr </italic>
###xml 149 150 149 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 256 264 256 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 268 275 264 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 474 482 467 471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 486 493 475 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 757 765 743 747 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 769 776 751 755 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 937 945 916 920 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 949 956 924 928 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 400 415 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
In the present study, no change in ovarian slr transcripts was found between oocyte oil-drop stage, vitellogenesis, spawning and post ovulation (Fig 6.). This lack of receptor up- and down-regulation contrasts with the pronounced up and down regulation of Slalpha and Slbeta pituitary transcripts observed that supports the notion that Sl could play important roles during final maturation of female Atlantic salmon. Two important instances of significant increases of both Slalpha and Slbeta transcripts in the pituitary are found. The most noticeable increase takes place during late vitellogenesis and spawning (September, October and November), with an ensuing down-regulation to baseline levels during ovarian post ovulation in December. The fact that Slalpha and Slbeta transcripts reach their highest levels at the time of spawning (November) and then drop drastically to baseline levels post ovulation suggests a direct role for Slalpha and Slbeta in the final stages of ovarian growth and spawning.
###end p 72
###begin p 73
###xml 67 75 67 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 79 86 75 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 131 132 124 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 410 412 403 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 413 415 406 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 451 452 441 442 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 602 604 592 594 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
Another possible explanation for the observed changes in pituitary Slalpha and Slbeta during September, October and November (Fig. 6), could be an indirect role for Sl related to maturational changes in plasma calcium and phosphate metabolism during vitellogenesis and spawning. It has been suggested that Sl may play an important role in the increase in plasma calcium that takes place during vitellogenesis [52,53]. At this time, estradiol-17beta (E2) stimulates hepatic vitellogenin production and mobilizes calcium and phosphate from the environment and/or internal stores to the salmonid oocytes [54].
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 62 64 62 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 217 219 217 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 368 370 368 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 374 376 374 376 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 401 402 401 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 403 404 403 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 436 444 436 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 472 474 468 470 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 502 509 498 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 537 539 530 532 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 694 702 687 691 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 706 713 695 699 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 779 787 765 769 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 791 798 773 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 1317 1319 1296 1298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 1320 1322 1299 1301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 1363 1365 1342 1344 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 1366 1368 1345 1347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 1387 1388 1366 1367 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1416 1418 1395 1397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 1494 1498 1473 1477 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr </italic>
###xml 1579 1580 1558 1559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F9">9</xref>
###xml 1599 1600 1578 1579 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1820 1822 1799 1801 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 88 103 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 1557 1572 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 1586 1597 <span type="species:ncbi:8020">masu salmon</span>
Somatolactotrophs are activated by low environmental calcium [43] and a study in Baltic Atlantic salmon found that most of the calcium mobilization and incorporation into oocytes occurs in a hypocalcemic environment [54]. Studies have demonstrated that in salmonids not only does vitellogenin correlate with total plasma calcium during the months preceding ovulation [55], E2 also correlates with Sl [7,8]. The present study shows that Slalpha correlates positively with E2 during post ovulation while Slbeta correlates positively with E2 in August when the GSI increases from 2.8% to 7.9%, and also in November during spawning and post ovulation. During the final months of oocyte maturation, Slalpha and Slbeta correlate with both gonad weight and GSI. The largest increase in Slalpha and Slbeta coincides with the sharpest increase in GSI starting in August, which is also the time when calcium-containing vitellogenin is rapidly incorporated into the oocytes. It is also tempting to speculate that the transient peak in Sl observed in February could signal the start of the Ca mobilization that occurs around this time (i.e. onset of exogenous vitellogenesis). The putative role of Sl in calcium mobilization could be in part mediated by PTHrP which incidentally is produced in the vicinity of somatolactotrophs [30,31], mediates the hypercalcemic effect of E2 [56] and potentiates E2-stimulated vitellogenesis [57]. Sl could also have a direct role in calcium absorption from the water as slr expression is high in the gills (but not in the kidney) of Atlantic salmon (Fig. 9) and masu salmon [5]. Likewise, Sl could play a similar role in the mobilization of phosphorous for the incorporation into vitellogenin: Sl increases inorganic phosphate reabsorption by the proximal tubule cells of the kidney in flounder [42].
###end p 74
###begin p 75
###xml 47 48 47 48 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 138 146 138 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 150 157 146 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 319 320 312 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 321 322 314 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 390 392 383 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 493 495 486 488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 496 498 489 491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 499 501 492 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 302 317 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 371 388 <span type="species:ncbi:13489">European sea bass</span>
On the other hand, it has long been suggested [7] that Sl may have a facilitating metabolic role during maturation. The large increase in Slalpha and Slbeta (September, October and November) coincides with the shift from somatic to gonadal growth that has also previously observed in maturing coho and Atlantic salmon [7,8]. Sl has been found to have lipolytic action in European sea bass [29] and like GH, seems to act as mediator to fasting that maintains lipolytic tonus in temperate fish [40,58-60]. Taken together this could imply a role for Sl in lipid mobilization during sexual maturation in salmonids.
###end p 75
###begin p 76
###xml 58 66 58 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 70 77 66 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 107 108 100 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 244 246 237 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
###xml 590 593 583 586 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl </italic>
###xml 971 972 964 965 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 973 974 966 967 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 975 977 968 970 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 978 980 971 973 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 1042 1043 1035 1036 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 227 242 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 1029 1040 <span type="species:ncbi:8019">coho salmon</span>
In May, another important transient increase in pituitary Slalpha and Slbeta transcripts takes place (Fig. 6), prior to transfer from seawater to fresh water. After the transfer, the fish stop feeding as is normal for maturing Atlantic salmon [61]. This transient increase in May is concurrent with the end of primary and secondary yolk formation, when all the fish have reached the tertiary yolk stage. The transient increase in May coincides again both with the first doubling of the GSI and the start of the decline in somatic weight so it is not clear whether the increase in pituitary Sl expression is a direct regulatory response linked to ovarian development, or a preparatory response to a stressful life-stage transition which includes both changes in salinity (seawater to freshwater) and nutritional status (voluntary anorexia) during the anadromous spawning migration. However, there is no evidence in salmonids that Sl has a direct freshwater-adapting role [7,8,11,20] and the peak observed during smoltification in coho salmon [8] is believed to be related to its role in energy balance, especially its lipolytic function.
###end p 76
###begin p 77
###xml 75 83 75 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 87 94 83 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 106 110 99 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr </italic>
The current study is based on correlations between expression of pituitary Slalpha and Slbeta and ovarian slr transcripts and stage of gonadal development/time during a reproductive cycle. Hence it was not designed to precisely distinguish between different possible roles associated with reproductive development in salmon such as body colour transformations, feed deprivation and anorexia, gonadal growth, spawning and associated metabolic changes. More studies are therefore needed to further clarify the specific roles of Sl during sexual maturation, to elucidate specific functions related to gonadal growth and development, lipolysis, calcium and phosphate metabolisms and body coloration.
###end p 77

###xml 266 274 259 263 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 373 380 362 366 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 461 469 447 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#945; </italic>
###xml 473 480 455 459 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sl&#946; </italic>
###xml 88 103 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
###xml 198 213 <span type="species:ncbi:8030">Atlantic salmon</span>
In summary, the aim of the study has been to sequence and phylogenetically characterize Atlantic salmon Slalpha, Slbeta and slr, and to elucidate their potential role in sexual maturation of female Atlantic salmon. The study confirms an increased mRNA expression of Slalpha during gonadal growth and down-regulation during post ovulation, and shows for the first time that Slbeta expression also changes before, during and after spawning. The increases of both Slalpha and Slbeta during late vitellogenesis and spawning, and downregulation to baseline levels after ovulation strongly suggests an important role for Sl either directly or indirectly related to reproductive functions in salmon. Among these functions, Sl could be involved in plasma calcium and/or phosphate regulation, lipid metabolism or the regulation of maturationally induced fasting as well as have a direct effect on gonadal development and spawning.
