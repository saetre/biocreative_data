###begin article-title 0
###xml 15 16 11 12 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Bacterial alpha2-macroglobulins: colonization factors acquired by horizontal gene transfer from the metazoan genome?
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 26 27 22 23 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Homologs of metazoan alpha2-macroglobulins have been found in bacteria. The distribution of these genes in diverse bacterial clades suggests they have been acquired by multiple horizontal transfers.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 204 224 204 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Helicobacter pylori </italic>
###xml 228 250 228 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Helicobacter hepaticus</italic>
###xml 365 366 361 362 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 204 223 <span type="species:ncbi:210">Helicobacter pylori</span>
###xml 228 250 <span type="species:ncbi:32025">Helicobacter hepaticus</span>
Invasive bacteria are known to have captured and adapted eukaryotic host genes. They also readily acquire colonizing genes from other bacteria by horizontal gene transfer. Closely related species such as Helicobacter pylori and Helicobacter hepaticus, which exploit different host tissues, share almost none of their colonization genes. The protease inhibitor alpha2-macroglobulin provides a major metazoan defense against invasive bacteria, trapping attacking proteases required by parasites for successful invasion.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 37 38 33 34 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 136 137 128 129 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 244 245 232 233 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 485 486 469 470 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 669 670 649 650 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 718 719 694 695 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Database searches with metazoan alpha2-macroglobulin sequences revealed homologous sequences in bacterial proteomes. The bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin phylogenetic distribution is patchy and violates the vertical descent model. Bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin genes are found in diverse clades, including purple bacteria (proteobacteria), fusobacteria, spirochetes, bacteroidetes, deinococcids, cyanobacteria, planctomycetes and thermotogae. Most bacterial species with bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin genes exploit higher eukaryotes (multicellular plants and animals) as hosts. Both pathogenically invasive and saprophytically colonizing species possess bacterial alpha2-macroglobulins, indicating that bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin is a colonization rather than a virulence factor.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 14 15 10 11 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 153 154 145 146 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 354 358 346 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM</italic>
###xml 379 380 367 368 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 437 441 425 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pbpC</italic>
###xml 676 677 660 661 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Metazoan alpha2-macroglobulins inhibit proteases of pathogens. The bacterial homologs may function in reverse to block host antimicrobial defenses. alpha2-macroglobulin was probably acquired one or more times from metazoan hosts and has then spread widely through other colonizing bacterial species by more than 10 independent horizontal gene transfers. yfhM-like bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin genes are often found tightly linked with pbpC, encoding an atypical peptidoglycan transglycosylase, PBP1C, that does not function in vegetative peptidoglycan synthesis. We suggest that YfhM and PBP1C are coupled together as a periplasmic defense and repair system. Bacterial alpha2-macroglobulins might provide useful targets for enhancing vaccine efficacy in combating infections.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 43 44 39 40 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 65 66 57 58 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 518 519 510 511 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 692 693 684 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 963 964 951 952 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 968 969 956 957 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 182 188 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
The broad-spectrum protease inhibitor alpha2-macroglobulin (alpha2M) and the complement factors C3, C4 and C5 belong to a gene family present in all metazoans ranging from corals to humans. These large (approximately 1,500 residue) proteins all undergo proteolytic processing and structural rearrangement as part of their role in host defense. The family is characterized by a unique thioester motif (CxEQ; single-letter amino-acid code), and a propensity for multiple conformationally sensitive binding interactions [1], which define their functional properties. The highly reactive thioester bond is buried inside the molecule in the native protein, protected from precocious inactivation [2]. Upon proteolytic cleavage, the thioester bond becomes exposed and can then mediate covalent attachment to activating self and non-self surfaces, in the case of complement factors, or covalent or noncovalent crosslinking to the attacking proteases in the case of alpha2Ms [3]. The proteolytic activation of these proteins also mediates interactions with receptors.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 105 106 101 102 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 206 207 198 199 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 376 377 368 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 492 493 480 481 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 507 508 495 496 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 509 510 497 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 535 536 519 520 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 646 647 626 627 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 727 728 707 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 729 730 709 710 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
In contrast to complement factors, which are activated by specific 'convertase' protease complexes, alpha2Ms have an accessible 'bait' region with target sites for many proteases. The rearrangement of alpha2M that follows cleavage of the bait region entraps the attacking protease in a cage-like structure, hindering protein substrates from reaching the protease active site [4]. In this way, exported proteases that are essential for parasitic infections can be rendered ineffective by alpha2M entrapment [5-7]. Protease-reacted alpha2M is then cleared from circulation by binding to the receptor CD91, triggering endocytosis. In addition, alpha2Ms bind cytokines and growth factors and regulate their clearance and activity [8,9].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 459 460 455 456 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 574 576 570 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 615 616 607 608 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Vertebrate complement factors C3, C4 and C5 are part of an activation cascade that leads to the assembly of the membrane-attack complex and lysis of the pathogen. Binding of C3 also targets pathogens for phagocytosis. Proteolytic activation of all three complement proteins yields anaphylatoxins (cleaved amino-terminal fragments) which are recognized by specific receptors and activate the inflammatory response at the site of infection. In contrast to alpha2Ms, complement factors also possess a carboxy-terminal domain extension, the netrin or NTR module (PFAM:PF01759) [10]. Some members of the complement/alpha2M family (for example, C5 and ovostatin) have lost the thioester motif.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 8 9 4 5 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 222 238 218 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Swiftia exserta </italic>
###xml 667 668 659 660 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 710 711 698 699 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 794 802 782 790 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Limulus </italic>
###xml 820 821 804 805 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 984 986 968 970 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 989 997 973 981 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Limulus </italic>
###xml 1002 1003 982 983 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1179 1181 1159 1161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 1229 1230 1205 1206 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1288 1292 1264 1268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TEP </italic>
###xml 1385 1409 1361 1385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster </italic>
###xml 1417 1421 1393 1397 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TEP </italic>
###xml 1449 1467 1425 1443 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anopheles gambiae </italic>
###xml 1475 1477 1451 1453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1527 1531 1503 1507 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TEP </italic>
###xml 1637 1639 1613 1615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1739 1741 1715 1717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 1763 1782 1739 1758 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plasmodium berghei </italic>
###xml 1808 1810 1784 1786 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 1838 1839 1810 1811 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 222 237 <span type="species:ncbi:216506">Swiftia exserta</span>
###xml 1385 1408 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 1449 1466 <span type="species:ncbi:7165">Anopheles gambiae</span>
###xml 1763 1781 <span type="species:ncbi:5821">Plasmodium berghei</span>
No alpha2M-related proteins have been found in any eukaryotes outside metazoans. Within the Metazoa, representatives have been found in all species examined, with a so-called 'C3-like' protein sequenced from the cnidarian Swiftia exserta (SWISS-PROT acc:Q8IYP1). There is no information from sponges as yet. We may speculate that the gene family evolved in an early metazoan in response to challenge from invasive microorganisms exploiting the new niche provided by the interstitial spaces and body cavities. The more derived role of the complement factors, together with their extra netrin domain, suggests that they arose by gene duplication from an ancestral alpha2M-like gene. Apart from vertebrates, alpha2M-group proteins have been most actively studied in arthropods. The horseshoe crab Limulus has a plasma alpha2M that is a component of an ancient invertebrate defense system; it is able to inhibit a wide range of proteases as well as to modulate plasma cytolytic activity [11]. Limulus alpha2M forms tetramers, binding covalently across the multimers rather than to the attacking proteases, but still traps these in a cage-like structure after proteolytic activation [12]. In dipteran insects, there are multiple alpha2M homologs, the thioester-containing proteins (TEPs). The TEP genes have been amplified by a process of tandem duplication into linked multigene families. Drosophila melanogaster has six TEP genes, whereas the mosquito Anopheles gambiae has 15 [13]. It is thought that the impressive expansion of TEP genes in the mosquito might be linked to the parasitic challenge provided by its blood-sucking lifestyle [13]. The first characterized TEP in mosquitoes, TEP1, binds to and promotes phagocytosis of bacteria [14]. TEP1 also binds to Plasmodium berghei and mediates its killing [15]. Thus the complement/alpha2M protein family is part of an innate immune system in metazoans that long pre-dates the immunoglobulin-based immune system of vertebrates, yet remains vital for combating parasites in all animal lineages examined.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 41 42 37 38 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 77 79 73 75 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 248 249 240 241 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 278 279 266 267 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 395 396 379 380 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
While reviewing the distribution of alpha2M/TEP proteins from invertebrates [16], we conducted BLAST searches of the protein databases and were surprised to discover a number of bacterial sequences with BLAST E-values indicating homology with alpha2M. Given the absence of alpha2Ms in all non-metazoan eukaryotic lineages, it immediately seemed clear that horizontal gene transfer (HGT) of alpha2Ms must have occurred between metazoans and bacteria. But which way? Here we summarize the evidence for numerous horizontal transfers between bacterial lineages and discuss some biochemical and medical implications of the finding.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 194 195 190 191 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 288 307 284 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Thermotoga maritima</italic>
###xml 463 464 455 456 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 597 614 589 606 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 639 658 631 650 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas putida </italic>
###xml 678 697 670 689 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rhizobium meliloti </italic>
###xml 753 754 741 742 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 927 935 915 923 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1046 1047 1030 1031 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1084 1085 1064 1065 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1220 1221 1196 1197 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1235 1236 1207 1208 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 183 188 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 288 307 <span type="species:ncbi:2336">Thermotoga maritima</span>
###xml 597 613 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 639 657 <span type="species:ncbi:303">Pseudomonas putida</span>
###xml 678 696 <span type="species:ncbi:382">Rhizobium meliloti</span>
###xml 927 934 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Our BLAST2SRS server provides the species in the BLAST output page: this is useful for quick visual surveys of the taxonomic distribution of a protein family. A BLAST2SRS search with human alpha2M unexpectedly listed an entry (SWISS-PROT accession number Q9X079) with E-value 2.3e-8 from Thermotoga maritima, a thermophilic eubacterium. With a length of 1,538 residues, a signal sequence and a matching CxEQ motif, there was no doubt that this was a genuine alpha2M homolog. Numerous other bacterial sequences with lower E-values but obvious topological equivalence were also listed: for example, Escherichia coli YfhM (P76578) at 5.8e-5; Pseudomonas putida AAN66197 at 1.3e-4; Rhizobium meliloti Q92VA6 at 5.0e-3. Profile searches with a metazoan alpha2M alignment and subsequently with an alignment of the stronger bacterial hits revealed a number of additional, highly diverged homologs, some lacking the CxEQ. For example, E. coli has a second divergent homolog, YfaS (P76464). It is noteworthy that not a single instance of an archaeal alpha2M sequence could be found. Thus alpha2M-like sequences are restricted to eubacteria and metazoans. No function has been experimentally ascribed to any of the bacterial alpha2Ms (bact-alpha2Ms).
###end p 15
###begin title 16
###xml 15 16 11 12 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin sequences
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 7 9 7 9 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1a</xref>
###xml 111 112 107 108 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 142 143 134 135 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 176 184 168 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 648 649 636 637 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 874 875 858 859 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1129 1131 1113 1115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1b</xref>
###xml 1257 1265 1241 1249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1307 1309 1291 1293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 1310 1312 1294 1296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1597 1598 1577 1578 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1636 1644 1616 1624 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Limulus </italic>
###xml 1645 1647 1625 1627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 1679 1680 1655 1656 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1970 1972 1946 1948 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 176 183 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1257 1264 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1905 1912 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
Figure 1a shows an alignment of the segment spanning the CxEQ motif for a representative set of bacterial alpha2M homologs. Not all bact-alpha2Ms possess the CxEQ motif. Using E. coli as the reference, YfhM is the archetype of a large group, mostly with the thioester motif, and YfaS is the archetype of a smaller, diverged group always lacking the motif. The sequences of the YfhM group are sufficiently divergent that accurate alignment proved time-consuming, but was achieved over almost the whole sequence length, other than the highly variable amino termini. We did not attempt to align together the YfhM and YfaS groups and the metazoan alpha2Ms. This would only be useful if the trees would be informative, but the high divergence between the groups precludes accurate alignment, leading to unreliable tree calculation. (In future, given more YfaS sequences and alpha2Ms from more metazoan lineages and a solved three-dimensional structure to guide alignment, this might be worth revisiting.) One feature apparent in many of the aligned YfhM sequences is a conserved cysteine directly following the signal peptide (Figure 1b), indicating palmitoylation. The presence of an aspartic acid residue following the palmitoylated cysteine has been shown in E. coli to dictate sorting to the inner membrane [17,18], in which case YfhM will be found in the periplasmic space, attached to the inner membrane. Given the CxEQ motif, covalent trapping of proteases in the periplasmic space seems to be the most likely function (whether the covalent links are to the trapped protease or between the alpha2M multimers, as in the horseshoe crab Limulus [12]). The YfaS group of bact-alpha2Ms lack a palmitoylable cysteine, so may be secreted, while absence of the CxEQ motif indicates the molecular function must be different, at least in part, though this does not, of itself, rule out protease entrapment, as in chicken ovostatin which also lacks the reactive thioester motif [19].
###end p 17
###begin title 18
###xml 34 35 30 31 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Genomic context of bacterial alpha2-macroglobulins
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 114 115 110 111 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 185 186 181 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 261 262 253 254 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
A survey of completely sequenced bacterial genomes was undertaken to establish which lineages possessed bact-alpha2Ms and which did not. Representative results are summarized in Figure 2. It is clear that there is a highly inconsistent correlation of bact-alpha2M possession and phylogenetic relationship, except for very closely related species.

###xml 10 11 6 7 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>

Bact-alpha2Ms are absent from the full proteomes of the following anciently diverged free-living species: the hyperthermophilic chemolithoautotroph Aquifex aeolicus, the thermophilic photolithoautotroph Chlorobium tepidum, the cyanobacteria Synechocystis, Synechococcus and Prochlorococcus, all firmicutes including Bacillus subtilis, all actinobacteria including Streptomyces coelicolor, the beta-proteobacterium Nitrosomonas europaea and the delta-proteobacterium Geobacter metallireducens. Furthermore, possession of bact-alpha2M is inconsistently represented within clades such as the proteobacteria, spirochetes and cyanobacteria. This is well illustrated by the two species of Helicobacter, one exploiting the acidic stomach and the other the very different environment of the liver: only the latter has a bact-alpha2M. The H. hepaticus genome lacks essentially all the proposed H. pylori virulence factors and is believed to possess a quite different set, adapted to its hepatobiliary habitat [20]. The irregular phylogenetic correlation suggests that bact-alpha2Ms are 'lifestyle' genes, affecting which niches a bacterium is able to exploit. Although an association with colonization seems clear (Figure 2), there is a strong bias in bacterial genome sequencing in favor of pathogenic species: this currently precludes a statistical assessment and might create a misleading phylogenetic perspective.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 19 21 19 21 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 105 106 101 102 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 123 128 119 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM </italic>
###xml 131 136 127 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfaS </italic>
###xml 222 223 214 215 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 264 265 256 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 271 276 263 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM </italic>
###xml 304 308 296 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pbpC</italic>
###xml 414 419 406 411 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pbpC </italic>
###xml 423 428 415 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM </italic>
###xml 509 517 501 509 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anabaena</italic>
###xml 658 660 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 739 741 731 733 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 1206 1208 1198 1200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 1382 1384 1374 1376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 1406 1411 1398 1403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pbpC </italic>
###xml 1536 1538 1528 1530 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 1703 1705 1692 1694 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 1737 1745 1726 1734 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1834 1842 1823 1831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo </italic>
###xml 1882 1884 1871 1873 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
The STRING server [21] was used to check for neighboring genes that persistently co-occur with bact-alpha2Ms. Using either yfhM or yfaS as seed, STRING reported two conserved gene sets that are widely found with bact-alpha2Ms. The results are summarized in Figure 2. The yfhM group always co-occurs with pbpC, which encodes penicillin-binding protein 1C (PBP1C). The gene topology is almost always consistent with pbpC and yfhM being in the same operon (or co-transcribed from a bidirectional promoter, as in Anabaena). The more strongly an operon structure is conserved across species, the more likely are the encoded proteins to have associated functions [22]. Moreover, products of conserved gene pairs very often associate physically [23]. Therefore, if YfhM is involved in colonizing or pathogenic lifestyles, so should be its partner. PBP1C is a paralog of the periplasmic cell-wall biosynthesis proteins PBP1A and PBP1B, though with the addition of a carboxy-terminal non-enzymatic domain of approximately 100 residues (PFAM:PF06832). The PBP1A and PBP1B peptidoglycan synthases each have two enzymatic domains, an amino-terminal transglycosylase and a carboxy-terminal transpeptidase (reviewed in [24]). Although it possesses the two enzymatic domains, studies have shown that PBP1C does not substitute for these proteins in cell-wall biosynthesis during vegetative growth [25]: indeed deletion of pbpC has a weak phenotype not affecting cell viability in the laboratory, although the number of peptide crosslinks is increased [25]. The transpeptidase domain in PBP1C is thought not to bind to most of the beta-lactams that inhibit the paralogous enzymes, nor to be a functional transpeptidase [25]. One curious finding is that, in vitro, PBP1C accounts for 75% of transglycosylase activity, yet is responsible for only 3% of de novo peptidoglycan biosynthesis in the cell [25]. As PBP1C does not substitute for the biosynthetic enzymes, a possible role would be in emergency repairs to the peptidoglycan, where its efficient transglycosylase activity would be appropriate.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 4 9 4 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfaS </italic>
###xml 28 29 24 25 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 124 125 120 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 187 199 183 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli yfaA</italic>
###xml 201 205 197 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfaQ</italic>
###xml 207 212 203 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfaP </italic>
###xml 216 221 212 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfaT </italic>
###xml 525 526 517 518 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 569 579 561 571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Thermotoga</italic>
###xml 581 593 573 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Deinococcus </italic>
###xml 597 612 589 604 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rhodopirellula </italic>
###xml 187 194 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The yfaS group of bact-alpha2Ms is likewise usually found in a candidate operon, at least within the proteobacteria (Figure 2), in this case with four other gene families, defined by the E. coli yfaA, yfaQ, yfaP and yfaT genes. All these genes have signal sequences and their encoded proteins are expected to be secreted or periplasmic, but, otherwise, sequence analysis has yielded no clues to their function. It is possible that all the encoded proteins function to disrupt or resist host defenses. The YfaS-like bact-alpha2Ms of the free-living and highly divergent Thermotoga, Deinococcus and Rhodopirellula (none of which is known to be invasive) are not found associated with most of these other genes.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
Microarray expression data
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 77 81 77 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM</italic>
###xml 83 88 83 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfaS </italic>
###xml 142 150 142 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 194 196 194 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 249 250 245 246 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 510 518 506 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 142 149 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 510 517 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The STRING server was also used to check for any significant coexpression of yfhM, yfaS and other members of the two candidate operons, using E. coli data from the Stanford microarray database [26]. All the genes associated with those for bact-alpha2Ms are present in the experiments included in the STRING database, and are expressed at levels significantly above background. However, none of the genes exhibits coordinated variation in expression levels either with each other or with any other genes in the E. coli genome under the conditions investigated.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Calculation of sequence trees
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 54 59 54 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM </italic>
###xml 175 180 175 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM </italic>
###xml 210 214 210 214 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pbpC</italic>
An initial rough tree calculated from an alignment of yfhM family sequences gave strong indications that several horizontal transfers had occurred among the available set. As yfhM is always found together with pbpC, indicating that the paired genes should have a shared phylogenetic history, a quick check of the PBP1C tree was also done. The two trees, which provide controls for each other's topologies, were very similar, indicating that the apparent HGTs were unlikely to be artifacts. Therefore, we undertook a more careful phylogenetic analysis with a view to improving the phylogenetic signal-to-noise ratio and using a method that is less prone to rate variation artifacts than neighbor-joining.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 230 232 230 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 428 429 428 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
Alignments were reviewed and edited by hand, then processed to remove especially noisy segments, as outlined in Materials and methods. Trees were calculated with MrBayes, a Bayesian resampling protocol that is now widely adopted [27]: MrBayes approaches the quality of maximum-likelihood methods while being quicker to calculate (though still computationally demanding). Results of the tree calculations are presented in Figure 3. The two trees differ by only three branch placements, indicating that the topologies are mostly sound, except for a few branches with low support (low posterior probabilities). As the calculated trees are unrooted, the ordering of the deepest branches cannot be mapped onto time.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
Fitting the observed tree topologies to the vertical descent model
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 211 212 211 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 451 453 451 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
The number of ancestral genes required to explain an observed tree topology can be determined by embedding the sequence tree within a species tree. We prepared a species tree for the bacterial species in Figure 3 such that currently uncertain affinities were assigned in favor of the observed trees: this will provide a minimum estimate of ancestral gene number. The sequence tree topology was embedded into the bacterial species tree using GeneTree [28]. The reconciled tree required six gene-duplication events and 29 lineage-specific deletions. The last common ancestor (LCA) of the full set had a minimum of three genes, the LCA of the proteobacteria had four genes, while the LCA of the alpha/beta-proteobacteria had six genes. The tree reveals a tendency for increasing gene number over time when vertical descent has strictly occurred.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 119 129 119 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM/pbpC </italic>
The problems of the vertical descent model are manifold. First, all sequenced extant genomes have single copies of the yfhM/pbpC genes, yet vertical descent shows a progression toward increasing gene number over time. This requires late but fully independent massive gene loss to have occurred in all lineages. Second, the observed robust sequence tree topologies would require a clear affinity between cyanobacteria and spirochetes, an affinity that has hitherto gone entirely unnoticed in the field of bacterial phylogeny. Third, the number of events (gene duplications and deletions) found to be required under a model of vertical descent is based on a species tree chosen to minimize this number (see Materials and methods.) As the species tree used is unlikely to be accurate in places where bacterial phylogeny is unresolved, the number of such events required under a vertical descent model is probably greater than described (and hence, correspondingly less likely.)
###end p 30
###begin p 31
Although bizarre evolutionary scenarios can always be invoked, the given tree topologies are difficult to explain solely by vertical descent from a common ancestral eubacterium.
###end p 31
###begin title 32
###xml 28 33 28 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM </italic>
###xml 37 42 37 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pbpC </italic>
Horizontal transfers of the yfhM and pbpC gene couplet
###end title 32
###begin p 33
Difficulties in accounting for the observed YfhM and PBP1C trees disappear if it is assumed that a number of horizontal gene transfers have occurred. Vertical transmission then only occurred among some sets of quite closely related bacteria. There are four deeply diverged sets within the tree, which will be discussed in turn.
###end p 33
###begin title 34
The major proteobacterial grouping
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 195 223 195 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Desulfovibrio desulfuricans </italic>
###xml 284 286 284 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 387 413 383 409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium tumefaciens </italic>
###xml 458 465 450 457 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 481 482 469 470 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 937 958 914 935 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bordetella pertussis </italic>
###xml 1037 1054 1006 1023 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D. desulfuricans </italic>
###xml 1169 1188 1126 1145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rickettsia conorii </italic>
###xml 1192 1207 1149 1164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia pestis</italic>
###xml 195 222 <span type="species:ncbi:876">Desulfovibrio desulfuricans</span>
###xml 387 412 <span type="species:ncbi:358">Agrobacterium tumefaciens</span>
###xml 458 465 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 916 936 <span type="species:ncbi:86027">beta-proteobacterium</span>
###xml 937 957 <span type="species:ncbi:520">Bordetella pertussis</span>
###xml 988 1009 <span type="species:ncbi:86473">gamma-proteobacterium</span>
###xml 1015 1036 <span type="species:ncbi:34034">delta-proteobacterium</span>
###xml 1037 1053 <span type="species:ncbi:876">D. desulfuricans</span>
###xml 1169 1187 <span type="species:ncbi:781">Rickettsia conorii</span>
###xml 1192 1207 <span type="species:ncbi:632">Yersinia pestis</span>
Of the 22 proteobacterial species sampled, 18 are exclusively grouped together in the two trees. The species are all plant or animal pathogens and symbionts - even the anaerobic sulfate-reducing Desulfovibrio desulfuricans is a symbiont of deep-sea hydrothermal vent polychete worms [29]. Sub-branches compatible with vertical descent are present for five alpha-proteobacteria including Agrobacterium tumefaciens and for seven gamma-proteobacteria including E. coli. For bact-alpha2M and PBP1C to have existed in proteobacteria before the alpha/gamma split, these gene sequences would have to be evolving more slowly than in other parts of the tree. It is more likely that the genes spread via HGT through these groups some time ago and then have been vertically inherited (at least in part). The remainder of the grouping consists of unambiguous HGT, although the direction of transfer is not always clear-cut. The beta-proteobacterium Bordetella pertussis has acquired the genes from a gamma-proteobacterium. The delta-proteobacterium D. desulfuricans has acquired the genes from an alpha-proteobacterium. An outlier set of alpha- and gamma-proteobacteria, including Rickettsia conorii and Yersinia pestis, indicate two further transfers, but in this case the order of the transfers is not determined. Therefore to create the topology of this grouping, a minimum of four unique horizontal transfers has occurred.
###end p 35
###begin title 36
The bacteroidete/fusobacteria/epsilon-proteobacteria grouping
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 133 155 127 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Helicobacter hepatica </italic>
###xml 184 198 178 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fusobacterium </italic>
###xml 226 254 220 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacteroides thetaiotamicron </italic>
###xml 310 311 300 301 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 378 400 368 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cytophaga hutchinsonii</italic>
###xml 493 506 477 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. hepaticus </italic>
###xml 596 609 580 593 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Helicobacter </italic>
###xml 165 170 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 378 400 <span type="species:ncbi:985">Cytophaga hutchinsonii</span>
###xml 493 505 <span type="species:ncbi:32025">H. hepaticus</span>
This group consists of three unrelated taxa which exploit niches related to the animal digestive system. The epsilon-proteobacterium Helicobacter hepatica colonizes mouse liver ducts, Fusobacterium species colonize the teeth, Bacteroides thetaiotamicron (not shown on the tree owing to an incomplete bact-alpha2M sequence) is a major gut bacterium, while a second bacteroidete, Cytophaga hutchinsonii, exploits cellulose-rich animal waste. Horizontal transfer into the epsilon-proteobacterium H. hepaticus is clear-cut, as it is isolated on the trees from all other proteobacteria, whereas other Helicobacter lack these genes. Another transfer has occurred between fusobacterial and bacteroidete lineages, but the direction is not clear. A third HGT is likely to have originally introduced the genes into these lineages but cannot be formally assigned without a root.
###end p 37
###begin title 38
###xml 13 30 13 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Magnetospirillum </italic>
The isolated Magnetospirillum alpha-proteobacteria branch
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 0 33 0 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Magnetospirillum magnetotacticum </italic>
###xml 43 44 39 40 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 216 233 208 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Magnetospirillum </italic>
###xml 0 32 <span type="species:ncbi:188">Magnetospirillum magnetotacticum</span>
Magnetospirillum magnetotacticum bact-alpha2M and PBP1C are deeply diverged from all other species, including other alpha-proteobacteria. This positioning away from its relatives indicates that HGT occurred into the Magnetospirillum lineage. The strong divergence from other sequences may indicate that the sequence has undergone rapid evolution. This latter point may be addressed in future if the branch becomes populated by some closer relatives.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
The cyanobacteria/spirochete/beta-proteobacteria grouping
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 303 311 300 308 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anabaena</italic>
###xml 371 387 368 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anabaena-Azolla </italic>
###xml 458 460 455 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 507 520 504 517 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Synechococcus</italic>
###xml 599 609 596 606 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ralstonia </italic>
###xml 613 629 610 626 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chromobacterium </italic>
###xml 708 719 705 716 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Leptospira </italic>
###xml 803 824 800 821 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Borrelia burgdorferi </italic>
###xml 863 882 860 879 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Treponema pallidum </italic>
###xml 803 823 <span type="species:ncbi:139">Borrelia burgdorferi</span>
###xml 863 881 <span type="species:ncbi:160">Treponema pallidum</span>
This branch consists of three very unrelated taxa: cyanobacteria facultatively symbiotic with plants, spirochetes pathogenic to metazoans and a pair of closely related genera of beta-proteobacteria that each include free-living, symbiotic and pathogenic forms. The deepest diverged in the group are the Anabaena-like symbiotic cyanobacteria. The economically significant Anabaena-Azolla symbiosis provides the nitrogen fixation that fertilizes paddy fields [30]. As other free-living cyanobacteria, such as Synechococcus, lack these genes, HGT into this lineage is very likely. The isolation of the Ralstonia and Chromobacterium clade from other proteobacteria also indicates HGT into their lineage. HGT for Leptospira (the causal agent of leptospirosis) is also indicated, as other spirochetes such as Borrelia burgdorferi (the causal agent of Lyme disease) and Treponema pallidum (the causal agent of syphilis) lack these genes. Thus, this set of genes that are clearly grouped together by molecular phylogeny, yet are found within very diverse taxa, appear to have been transmitted three times.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
Discussion
###end title 42
###begin title 43
Sifting the evidence for bacterial HGT
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 493 495 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 523 525 523 525 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 526 528 526 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 632 634 632 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 635 637 635 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
There is increasing evidence that HGT has had - and continues to have - a major role in the adaptation of organisms, especially prokaryotes, to exploiting new environments. Nevertheless, it is often hard to demonstrate HGT, and there is considerable confusion about how to do so. The default hypothesis should remain vertical transmission unless there is good evidence for HGT. The over-hasty assignment of recent bacterial-to-vertebrate gene transfers, solely on the basis of BLAST E-values [31], has been firmly refuted [32,33]. Such premature HGT assignments have been surveyed and used to provide guidelines for evaluating HGT [34,35]. Sometimes the evidence is clear-cut, as when adaptive genes are carried on phage, plasmid or transposon. Inconsistent phylogenetic distribution may be evidence for HGT but must be carefully balanced against gene-loss models, recognizing that the two processes are not mutually exclusive. Phylogenetic trees only provide good evidence for HGT when branching is robust and clearly delimited by appropriate outgroups: the HGT must carry a diagnostic molecular evolutionary signal.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 121 136 117 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio cholerae</italic>
###xml 205 212 201 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio </italic>
###xml 465 467 461 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 513 518 509 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ctxAB</italic>
###xml 603 605 597 599 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 844 846 838 840 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 1090 1092 1084 1086 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 1112 1124 1106 1118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. cholerae </italic>
###xml 1441 1448 1435 1442 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio </italic>
###xml 1667 1679 1657 1669 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. cholerae </italic>
###xml 1713 1714 1699 1700 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 99 120 <span type="species:ncbi:86473">gamma-proteobacterium</span>
###xml 121 136 <span type="species:ncbi:666">Vibrio cholerae</span>
###xml 577 601 <span type="species:ncbi:141904">filamentous phage CTXphi</span>
###xml 1112 1123 <span type="species:ncbi:666">V. cholerae</span>
###xml 1608 1629 <span type="species:ncbi:86473">gamma-proteobacterium</span>
###xml 1667 1678 <span type="species:ncbi:666">V. cholerae</span>
One of the best paradigms for investigating recent and ongoing HGT in parasitic prokaryotes is the gamma-proteobacterium Vibrio cholerae, which acquired pathogenicity late in recorded history. Free-living Vibrio species are common, harmless aquatic microorganisms. The first recorded cholera pandemic occurred in 1817, the sixth and seventh occurred recently enough to be investigated with modern molecular techniques, and the eighth is probably underway now (see [36] for details). The basic pathogenicity genes ctxAB, which encode cholera toxin, lie within the genome of the filamentous phage CTXphi [37]. Other pathogenicity gene 'islands' include the toxin-co-regulated pilus, needed for colonization, and the VSP-1 and VSP-2 islands, which appeared in strains of the seventh pandemic and are suggested to have been integral to that event [38]. The recent O139 serotype arose by wholesale replacement of the pre-existing gene cluster encoding lipopolysaccharide O side-chain synthesis, yielding an outer surface with a different architecture, less susceptible to pre-existing immunity [39]. Thus, pathogenic V. cholerae continues to adapt to the invasive lifestyle, to a large extent through HGT-mediated acquisition of new capabilities, including, but not limited to, better avoidance of host defenses. Although many of the functions encoded by the genes within pathogenic islands are not understood, their absence from the free-living Vibrio species is good evidence that they have been incorporated, and then conserved, because of a direct or indirect role in enhancing virulence. Even though it is a gamma-proteobacterium, the genomic sequence data show that V. cholerae has not (re-)acquired a bact-alpha2M gene. At least, not yet.
###end p 45
###begin title 46
###xml 12 13 8 9 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
HGT of alpha2-macroglobulin among colonizing bacteria
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 33 34 29 30 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 372 377 368 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM </italic>
###xml 465 469 461 465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pbpC</italic>
Our unexpected finding that alpha2-macroglobulins, hitherto only known from metazoans, are widely present in eubacterial genomes has provided one of the most clear-cut examples of widespread HGT between extremely divergent bacterial taxa that can be monitored by molecular phylogenetic approaches. We have been able to infer a minimum of 11 independent HGTs for the major yfhM group among 27 sequences tested. Because this group always coexists with a second gene, pbpC, shared evolutionary history means the trees are controlled for topological consistency, so that the assignment of HGT is not in doubt. This work does not address an earlier evolutionary history preceding the link-up of this gene pair.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 355 356 351 352 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 362 366 358 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pbpC</italic>
###xml 541 542 533 534 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 700 701 692 693 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
It is striking that all four deeply diverged groups in the trees include proteobacterial species. This alone clearly indicates that HGT has occurred. Because this is the most heavily researched bacterial taxon and provides most of the sequenced genomes, it is not yet clear whether other taxa will also show multiple independent acquisitions of bact-alpha2M and pbpC. Currently, the trees show a minimum of 11 independent HGT events, even if the originating (but unknown) taxon were represented here. A twelfth HGT is indicated if bact-alpha2M was originally captured from a metazoan (or vice versa). Extensive gene loss is also likely to have contributed to the phylogenetic distributions in Figure 2, particularly amongst the alpha-,beta-, and gamma-proteobacteria, where possession seems the default yet both vertical and horizontal transmission occur. Quite possibly, a cycle of gain-loss-gain has repeatedly occurred as strains adapt between colonization and free-living environments. The role of gene loss cannot be quantified with current data, but this may become possible in the future with more comprehensive genome coverage.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 335 337 335 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 338 340 338 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 368 369 364 365 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 663 664 655 656 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 723 724 711 712 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Where pathogenic bacteria and their eukaryotic hosts share related genes that appear to be transferred from one to the other, it is believed that the direction is overwhelmingly from the eukaryote to the bacterium. The failure to find phylogenetic evidence for bacterium-to-vertebrate gene transfers is consistent with this direction [32,33]. We expect that bact-alpha2M was transferred from a metazoan host to a pathogenic bacterium, but this is not yet demonstrable and remains supposition. Given a simple early metazoan, where the germ cells would not be physically isolated from any bacterial infection, one can see how selection could act to fix a bact-alpha2M gene transferred in the opposite direction, if bact-alpha2M was originally bacterial. This issue may become resolvable in future given much more extensive phylogenetic coverage.
###end p 49
###begin title 50
###xml 15 16 11 12 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin in apparently free-living bacteria
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 133 143 133 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ralstonia </italic>
###xml 147 156 147 156 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anabaena </italic>
###xml 182 194 182 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia </italic>
###xml 198 208 198 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Treponema </italic>
###xml 870 871 866 867 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 941 957 937 953 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Magnetospirillum</italic>
###xml 959 971 955 967 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caulobacter </italic>
###xml 975 985 971 981 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Thermotoga</italic>
###xml 1002 1003 994 995 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1019 1026 1011 1018 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aquifex</italic>
###xml 1028 1038 1020 1030 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chlorobium</italic>
###xml 1040 1053 1032 1045 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Synechocystis</italic>
###xml 1055 1068 1047 1060 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Synechococcus</italic>
###xml 1070 1085 1062 1077 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Prochlorococcus</italic>
###xml 1087 1100 1079 1092 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nitrosomonas </italic>
###xml 1104 1113 1096 1105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Geobacter</italic>
###xml 1193 1194 1181 1182 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1427 1428 1411 1412 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Many bacterial taxa contain a plethora of strains adapted for free-living, symbiotic and pathogenic lifestyles. Examples include the Ralstonia and Anabaena genera adapted to plants, Escherichia and Treponema adapted to animals and pseudomonads adapted to both. Many free-living bacterial strains are also facultative colonizers. This creates some difficulty in cataloguing genes that are adapted to colonizing niches versus free-living: it is rarely certain whether an apparently free-living species never colonizes a higher organism, or is not part of a continuum of strains frequently exchanging lifestyle genes. Given this caveat, we reviewed all the currently completed genomes of bacteria that are not in any way known to have close associations with higher eukaryotes. The available set of Gram-positive bacterial genomes stand out as never possessing a bact-alpha2M gene (see below). Only three apparently free-living Gram-negatives (Magnetospirillum, Caulobacter and Thermotoga) have bact-alpha2Ms while seven (Aquifex, Chlorobium, Synechocystis, Synechococcus, Prochlorococcus, Nitrosomonas and Geobacter) do not. Thus this crude estimate would suggest that possession of a bact-alpha2M gene is associated with colonization, not as a core colonization factor, but as an accessory that enhances fitness for the colonization environment. Further, it may imply that the three 'free-living' species possessing a bact-alpha2M gene have undocumented facultative symbiotic capabilities with higher eukaryotes.
###end p 51
###begin title 52
###xml 19 20 15 16 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Usage of host alpha2-macroglobulin by invasive Gram-positive bacteria
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 86 87 82 83 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 103 104 99 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 194 195 186 187 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 218 241 210 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Streptococcus pyogenes </italic>
###xml 267 268 255 256 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 359 361 347 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 362 364 350 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 670 671 654 655 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 700 702 684 686 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 816 817 796 797 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 986 987 962 963 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 218 240 <span type="species:ncbi:1314">Streptococcus pyogenes</span>
The Gram-positive firmicutes and actinobacteria stand out as always lacking bact-alpha2M genes (Figure 2). However, certain Gram-positives have found a more direct way to take advantage of alpha2M proteins. Pathogenic Streptococcus pyogenes directly co-opt host alpha2M for defense against host proteases through the cell-surface proteins GRAB and protein G [40,41]. As Gram-positive bacteria do not possess an outer membrane, defensive strategies are likely to differ from those of Gram-negatives. Invasive Gram-positives are found to coat themselves in a selected set of host proteins to obstruct host defenses. Streptococcal GRAB mutants that are unable to bind alpha2M have attenuated virulence [40]. It seems remarkable that prokaryotes have evolved two totally independent strategies to take advantage of alpha2M. On the one hand, Gram-positives are able to use the host's own protein, on the other, Gram-negatives have acquired their own gene. The clear implication is that alpha2M functionality has a wide and general significance spanning many bacterial taxa.
###end p 53
###begin title 54
###xml 15 16 11 12 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin YfhM/PBP1C: a second line of defense?
###end title 54
###begin p 55
###xml 236 238 236 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 301 303 301 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 424 426 424 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 671 673 671 673 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
The lipopolysaccharide (LPS) layer of the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria provides a first line of defense. The outer membrane barrier is sufficient to prevent the enzyme lysozyme from lysing Gram-negative bacteria in culture [42]. Under attack from host immunity and antimicrobial peptides [43], LPS can be disrupted or stripped away - for example, when released into the circulation, it can lead to septic shock [44] - leaving the peptidoglycan cell wall and inner membrane exposed. There is current interest in antibacterial strategies that endeavor to enhance lysozyme activity by co-administration with agents that disrupt the outer membrane, such as EDTA [42].
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 69 70 65 66 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 128 129 120 121 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 342 343 330 331 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
The following assumptions lead us to a hypothesis for YfhM bact-alpha2M/PBP1C as a periplasmic defense system. First, bact-alpha2M and PBP1C form a complex, probably through the carboxy-terminal non-enzymatic domain of PBP1C. Second, the complex resides in the periplasmic space, attached by acylation to the inner membrane. Third, bact-alpha2M functions to entrap attacking proteases. Fourth, PBP1C is a transglycosylase that polymerizes glycan chains. Fifth, a periplasmic defense is only needed when the outer membrane has been breached and peptidoglycan is under attack.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 26 27 22 23 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 144 145 140 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 200 208 196 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 252 260 248 256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 261 263 257 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 480 481 472 473 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 622 630 614 622 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 631 633 623 625 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 622 629 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The role of the bact-alpha2M/PBP1C system is then perceived to be defense at, and repair of, peptidoglycan breaches induced by the host (Figure 4). PBP1C provides 75% of the transglycosylase activity in vitro, but only 3% of peptidoglycan biosynthesis in vivo [25]: it is a fast linear transglycosylase, ideal for traversing and repairing a breach. During repair it will, however, be exposed to attacking proteases and may be rapidly rendered dysfunctional. The role of bact-alpha2M will be to entrap attacking proteases, protecting PBP1C and other periplasmic proteins such as the high-affinity lysozyme inhibitor Ivy in E. coli [45]. In this way, the fate of the invading bacterial cell will depend on the relative balance of the host's attacking forces versus the bacterial defense systems. Under an optimized host attack, such defenses would be rapidly overwhelmed but when (or where) the host is not well prepared, these defenses may serve to prolong colonization.
###end p 57
###begin title 58
Potential experimental and medical applications
###end title 58
###begin p 59
###xml 4 14 4 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM/pbpC </italic>
###xml 215 216 211 212 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 394 395 386 387 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 546 547 534 535 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 707 712 695 700 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PbpC </italic>
###xml 783 792 771 780 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pbpC/ivy </italic>
The yfhM/pbpC gene pair in bacteria not only suggests experimental research strategies, but may have medical potential to help combat pathogenic organisms. Predicted periplasmic location and complexing of bact-alpha2M and PBP1C with each other (and any other periplasmic proteins) should be straightforward to investigate biochemically. Elucidation of the host proteases entrapped by bact-alpha2Ms should reveal which host defense proteases are targeted at which parasites, leading to enhanced understanding of host defense mechanisms. Bact-alpha2M-inhibited proteases should be directly active against pathogen proteins - or else act indirectly as, for example, do the proteases of the complement cascade. PbpC deletions should show increased sensitivity to lysozyme treatments and pbpC/ivy double mutants, yet more so.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 14 15 10 11 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 155 156 147 148 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 231 232 219 220 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 317 318 301 302 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 503 504 483 484 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 598 599 574 575 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
The bact-alpha2M/PBP1C proteins also provide targets for medical intervention, for example by training host immunity, the administration of anti-bact-alpha2M monoclonal antibody or in combination therapies. Antibodies to bact-alpha2Ms should act not just by promoting immune clearance but also to block the bact-alpha2M activity, so that the host antibacterial proteases are unhindered. This dual effect may provide an enhanced prophylactic efficacy for vaccines that are augmented with extra bact-alpha2M protein (probably as an inactive variant) or be directly invoked by targeted anti-bact-alpha2M antibody administration for combating acute infection. PBP1C should also be rendered dysfunctional by specific antibodies, perhaps in combination with transglycosylase inhibitors such as the antibiotic moenomycin.
###end p 60
###begin title 61
Conclusions
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 10 11 6 7 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 196 197 188 189 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 215 223 207 215 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 361 365 353 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM</italic>
###xml 366 371 358 363 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pbpC </italic>
###xml 215 222 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Bact-alpha2Ms are spread widely amongst symbiotic and pathogenic bacteria. The implication is that protease inhibition is often an aid to colonizing higher eukaryotes. The major form of bact-alpha2Ms is typified by E. coli YfhM and is a periplasmic protein that co-occurs with periplasmic PBP1C, a candidate peptidoglycan repair enzyme. The distribution of the yfhM/pbpC gene pair is inconsistent with the established bacterial phylogeny. Molecular trees calculated for each of the proteins are in good agreement with each other. Each tree provides a control for the other tree's topology, allowing confidence in the general topology. This allows us to state with high confidence that at least 11 separate gene transfers have occurred between highly diverged bacterial taxa. An additional gene transfer has occurred between bacteria and metazoans. We are not yet able to determine in which direction this transfer occurred, and therefore the title question is not yet answerable.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 29 30 25 26 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 135 136 127 128 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
The known properties of alpha2Ms and PBP1C point to a periplasmic line of defense at cell-wall breaches, mounted by the YfhM bact-alpha2M and PBP1C. This defensive line should be sensitive to antibody-based therapeutic approaches, whether enhanced vaccine efficacy or direct administration of antibody.
###end p 63
###begin title 64
Materials and methods
###end title 64
###begin title 65
Sequence database searches
###end title 65
###begin p 66
###xml 15 16 11 12 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 66 68 62 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 87 89 83 85 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 152 154 148 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 188 190 184 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 223 225 219 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 354 355 346 347 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 473 475 465 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 476 478 468 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 541 543 533 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 604 605 592 593 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Bacterial alpha2Ms were clearly revealed in a search of SWISSALL [46] using BLAST2SRS [47] in which the species names are included in the BLAST output [48]. Profile searches as described [49] using the EMBL Bioccelerators [50] supported and extended the findings and were used to retrieve a set of bacterial sequences. Reciprocal searches with bact-alpha2M profiles reconfirmed the findings with good E-values (<1.e-25). The sets of proteomes provided by the BLAST server [51,52] at the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) [53] were surveyed to determine the presence or absence of alpha2Ms in bacteria and in non-metazoan eukaryotes.
###end p 66
###begin title 67
Survey of genomic context
###end title 67
###begin p 68
###xml 19 21 19 21 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 160 162 160 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 189 190 185 186 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 198 206 194 202 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 282 283 274 275 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 597 598 585 586 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 761 763 749 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 820 822 808 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 198 205 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The STRING server [54] is a resource for exploring genome context (for example, identifying groups of genes found in close proximity in many different genomes [21]). Queries with bact-alpha2Ms from E. coli or other bacteria yielded a recurring result: in most species the bact-alpha2Ms cluster consistently with certain other gene families. This behavior is typical of gene sets belonging to the same operon. These families were retrieved and used for further database explorations, alignments and trees. To identify the location of these gene families in other genomes where linkage to bact-alpha2Ms is less direct than those presented by STRING, we downloaded genomic database entries from the NCBI, converted the format of these files to EMBL using BioPerl [55], and assessed the location of the genes using Artemis [56]. In addition, linkage of these gene families was investigated in organisms not included in STRING using the same method.
###end p 68
###begin title 69
Sequence alignment and editing
###end title 69
###begin p 70
###xml 45 47 45 47 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 304 306 304 306 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 591 615 591 615 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Deinococcus radiodurans </italic>
###xml 619 646 619 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacteroides thetaiotamicron</italic>
###xml 591 614 <span type="species:ncbi:1299">Deinococcus radiodurans</span>
Sequences were aligned using Clustal X 1.83 [57]. Because many sequences are very dissimilar to each other, misaligned regions were to be expected. These were identified using the 'low scoring segments' check and either realigned using the 'realign selected range' option or were hand-edited in SeaView [58]. Corrections were assessed by both improvements to conserved hydrophobic columns (indicating structurally important residues) and with the 'low scoring segments' check. Sequences excluded because they were either too divergent to be aligned or may contain sequencing errors included Deinococcus radiodurans and Bacteroides thetaiotamicron.
###end p 70
###begin title 71
Calculation of sequence trees
###end title 71
###begin p 72
###xml 49 51 49 51 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 306 308 306 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 339 340 335 336 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 552 554 548 550 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
###xml 685 687 681 683 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 757 759 753 755 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
###xml 1154 1156 1150 1152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B63">63</xref>
###xml 1414 1416 1410 1412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>
Preliminary trees were made by neighbor-joining [59] as implemented in Clustal X, excluding gaps and correcting for multiple substitutions with the Kimura PAM model. These initial trees indicated that HGT had occurred, warranting more careful assessment. Alignments were processed with the Gblocks server [60] (for the divergent bact-alpha2Ms, the low stringency settings were used). Gblocks heuristically removes poorly conserved excessively divergent segments of alignments with low signal-to-noise ratio in order to enhance the phylogenetic signal [61]. Processed alignments were used to derive tree topologies using Bayesian inference of phylogeny as implemented by MrBayes v2.01 [27] with maximum-likelihood branch-length estimates provided by PUZZLE [62]. MrBayes was used with four heated chains over 250,000 generations, sampling every 20 trees. The likelihoods of these trees were examined to estimate the length of the burn-in phase, and all trees sampled 20,000 generations later than this point were used to create a consensus tree using the 50% majority rule. Both MrBayes and PUZZLE were used with the JTT model of amino-acid substitution [63], assuming the presence of invariant sites and using a gamma distribution approximated by four different rate categories to model rate variation between sites, estimating amino-acid frequencies from the alignment. Trees were displayed and rooted in Njplot [64].
###end p 72
###begin title 73
###xml 22 27 22 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfhM </italic>
Estimation of minimum yfhM gene number in the bacterial last common ancestor
###end title 73
###begin p 74
###xml 22 24 22 24 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 524 541 524 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Magnetospirillum </italic>
The program GeneTree [28] was used to evaluate the cost of embedding the YfhM sequence tree in a bacterial species tree. Magnetospirillum was excluded from the analysis as its position is not stable in the YfhM and PBP1C trees. Phylogenetic distribution of bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin homologs (alpha2M). Pink, species that possess bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin genes; yellow, species without bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin genes. Shared genomic context is indicated for genes found to co-occur with bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin genes. Because bacterial phylogeny has many uncertainties, the tree is simplified into multiple nodes representing three levels of divergence. There is little phylogenetic consistency for bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin possession. Colonizing proteobacteria are overwhelmingly expected to have a bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin gene, although exceptions occur, notably Helicobacter pylori, Vibrio cholerae and Neisseria meningitidis. No examples of bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin genes have been found in colonizing Gram-positives in the Firmicutes or Actinobacteria, which include such major infectious clades as streptococci and mycobacteria. Anabaena is a facultative plant symbiont, while other free-living cyanobacteria (here represented by Synechocystis) lack bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin. Thermotoga maritima, Magnetospirillum magnetotacticum and Caulobacter crescentus are the only species possessing bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin for which no apparent connection exists with niches linked to exploitation of higher eukaryotes. Genome context of bacterial alpha2Ms is based on automated STRING annotation [21], supplemented by re-analysis of individual genomes. Double slanted bars between genes indicate that they are not tightly linked. Bacterial alpha2-macroglobulins make up two distinct groups typified by the E. coli genes yfhM and yfaS. The members of the yfhM group (on the left side of the figure) almost always co-occur with pbpC and are often, but not always, found adjacent to and on the same strand as one another in an operon configuration. Members of the yfaS group (grouped on the right side of the figure), when present in beta- or gamma-proteobacteria, are linked to four other gene families. All their predicted gene products also possess signal peptides, but are otherwise of unknown function. In other taxa, members of the yfaS group of bacterial alpha2-macroglobulins are either unassociated with any of these gene families (planctomycetes and deinococci), or linked to a member of just one of the families (thermotogae).

Schematic outline of the proposed defense of breaches of the bacterial outer membrane. Host systems (whether antimicrobial peptides, antibody and/or complement) have opened the outer membrane, allowing lysozyme and host proteases to attack periplasmic components, leading to a further breach of the peptidoglycan. Host attack is hampered by protease trapping (bacterial alpha2-macroglobulin) and lysozyme inhibition (Ivy), giving PBP1C a chance to repair the glycan chains. The fate of the colonizing bacterial cell will now depend on whether the bacterial defenses are exhausted or the host attacking components are too limited to achieve cell lysis.
