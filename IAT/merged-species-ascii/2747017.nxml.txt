###begin article-title 0
betaalpha-Hairpin Clamps Brace betaalphabeta Modules and Can Make Substantive Contributions to the Stability of TIM Barrel Proteins

###begin p 3
###xml 207 214 189 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 291 293 273 275 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 477 492 452 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. solfataricus</italic>
###xml 497 504 472 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 794 795 741 742 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">8</sub>
###xml 207 214 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 477 492 <span type="species:ncbi:2287">S. solfataricus</span>
###xml 497 504 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Non-local hydrogen bonding interactions between main chain amide hydrogen atoms and polar side chain acceptors that bracket consecutive betaalpha or alphabeta elements of secondary structure in alphaTS from E. coli, a TIM barrel protein, have previously been found to contribute 4-6 kcal mol-1 to the stability of the native conformation. Experimental analysis of similar betaalpha-hairpin clamps in a homologous pair of TIM barrel proteins of low sequence identity, IGPS from S. solfataricus and E. coli, reveals that this dramatic enhancement of stability is not unique to alphaTS. A survey of 71 TIM barrel proteins demonstrates a 4-fold symmetry for the placement of betaalpha-hairpin clamps, bracing the fundamental betaalphabeta building block and defining its register in the (betaalpha)8 motif. The preferred sequences and locations of betaalpha-hairpin clamps will enhance structure prediction algorithms and provide a strategy for engineering stability in TIM barrel proteins.

###begin title 4
Introduction
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 15 16 8 9 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">8</sub>
###xml 134 137 127 130 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Nagano1">[1]</xref>
###xml 152 155 145 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Zitzewitz1">[2]</xref>
###xml 175 178 168 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Nagano1">[1]</xref>
###xml 180 183 173 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Gerstein1">[3]</xref>
###xml 185 188 178 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Frenkel1">[4]</xref>
###xml 651 654 610 613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Gerstein1">[3]</xref>
###xml 1117 1120 1059 1062 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-FarzadFard1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1167 1170 1109 1112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Yang1">[6]</xref>
###xml 1171 1174 1113 1116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Stickle1">[8]</xref>
The (betaalpha)8, TIM barrel is one of the most common folds in biology, supporting a myriad of catalytic functions essential to life [1]. Experimental [2] and bioinformatics [1], [3], [4] analyses of TIM barrel proteins have led to the conclusion that a pair of adjacent parallel beta-strands and the intervening anti-parallel alpha-helix, i.e., the betaalphabeta module, serve as the minimal unit of stability. Gene duplication of this elemental betaalphabeta building block into higher-order structures has been suggested to result in several common betaalpha-repeat structures, including the TIM barrel, Rossman, flavodoxin and leucine-rich folds [3]. The interactions stabilizing this super-secondary structure include: (1) main chain-main chain (MC-MC) hydrogen bonds (H-bonds) between the beta-strands, (2) intra-helical MC-MC H-bonds, (3) hydrophobic interactions between the side chains (SC) protruding from the beta-strands and the alpha-helix, (4) side chain-side chain (SC-SC) H-bonds and salt bridges, (5) dipole-dipole interactions between the alpha-helix and the pair of beta-strands on which it docks [5] and (6) main chain-side chain (MC-SC) H-bonds [6]-[8]. The surprising role of a subset of non-local MC-SC H-bond interactions in structure and stability is the subject of this communication.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 96 99 96 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Baker1">[7]</xref>
###xml 101 104 101 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Stickle1">[8]</xref>
###xml 188 191 184 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Presta1">[9]</xref>
###xml 193 197 189 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Aurora1">[10]</xref>
###xml 333 335 329 331 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 381 385 377 381 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Horovitz1">[11]</xref>
###xml 386 390 382 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-IbarraMolero1">[14]</xref>
###xml 580 582 572 574 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 643 646 635 638 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Yang1">[6]</xref>
###xml 1067 1070 1027 1030 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Yang1">[6]</xref>
###xml 1160 1163 1116 1119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Yang1">[6]</xref>
A majority of MC-SC interactions in proteins are local in sequence, usually within six residues [7], [8], and are often involved in capping either the N- or the C-termini of alpha-helices [9], [10]. Mutational analysis has shown that these non-covalent interactions usually contribute modestly, typically in the range of 1-2 kcal mol-1, to the stability of their resident proteins [11]-[14]. In contrast, the removal of three non-local MC-SC H-bond interactions each reduce the stability of the alpha subunit of tryptophan synthase (alphaTS), a TIM barrel protein, by 4-6 kcal mol-1, and disrupt the complete formation of the TIM barrel motif [6]. These three interactions in alphaTS, between MC amide H-bond donors and SC H-bond acceptors, connect the N-terminus of one element of secondary structure, either beta-strand or alpha-helix, to the C-terminus of the subsequent element of structure, either alpha-helix or beta-strand, respectively. These non-local MC-SC interactions were designated as betaalpha-hairpin clamps and alphabeta-hairpin clamps, respectively [6]. The significant contribution to structure and stability by three such clamps in alphaTS [6] raises the possibility that potent betaalpha- and alphabeta-hairpin clamps may be an important general feature of TIM barrel proteins.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 230 245 223 238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. solfataricus</italic>
###xml 258 265 251 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 425 429 411 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Gromiha1">[15]</xref>
###xml 230 245 <span type="species:ncbi:2287">S. solfataricus</span>
###xml 258 265 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
A two-pronged approach was taken to probe the significance of betaalpha-hairpin clamps in TIM barrel proteins. First, mutational analysis of two representative TIM barrel proteins, indole-3-glycerol phosphate synthase (IGPS) from S. solfataricus (sIGPS) and E. coli (eIGPS), shows that a subset of their betaalpha-hairpin clamps make significant contributions to protein stability. Second, a survey of 71 TIM barrel proteins [15] explored the frequency, location and sequence preferences of all betaalpha-hairpin clamps. The observed preferences for location and sequence for the betaalpha-hairpin clamps and their contribution to the structure and stability of TIM barrel proteins suggest that the recognition of these interactions can enhance protein structure prediction algorithms and provide a strategy for engineering stability in TIM barrel proteins.

###begin title 13
Perturbation of the secondary and tertiary structure by clamp deletion in sIGPS and eIGPS
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 615 631 542 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007179-g002">Figure 2A and 2B</xref>
###xml 673 689 600 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007179-g002">Figure 2C and 2D</xref>
The contribution of each betaalpha-clamp interaction to the structure of the TIM barrel proteins, sIGPS and eIGPS, was assessed by replacing the H-bond acceptor SC with alanine and monitoring the effects on the secondary and tertiary structure by far-UV and near-UV circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy. The far-UV CD spectra for the wild-type (WT) and clamp-deletion variants of sIGPS (sIGPS-WT, sIGPS-Deltabeta2alpha2-E74A, sIGPS-Deltabeta3alpha3-D128A and sIGPS-Deltabeta7alpha7-N228A) and eIGPS (eIGPS-WT, eIGPS-Deltabeta1alpha1-S82A, eIGPS-Deltabeta3alpha3-D132A and eIGPS-Deltabeta7alpha7-N231A) are shown in Figure 2A and 2B, and the near-UV CD spectra are shown in Figure 2C and 2D. Relatively small changes in the far-UV and near-UV CD spectra are observed for sIGPS-Deltabeta3alpha3-D128A, eIGPS-Deltabeta1alpha1-S82A and eIGPS-Deltabeta3alpha3-D132A compared to their respective WT sequences. However, the significant changes in the near-UV CD spectra for the sIGPS-Deltabeta2alpha2-E74A, sIGPS-Deltabeta7alpha7-N228A and eIGPS-Deltabeta7alpha7-N231A variants imply that the deletion of the beta7alpha7 clamps in both proteins and the beta2alpha2 clamp in sIGPS result in altered aromatic side chain packing.

###xml 866 869 803 806 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Yang1">[6]</xref>
The effect of betaalpha-hairpin clamp deletion on the stability of sIGPS and eIGPS was determined by urea denaturation. As for alphaTS [16], both sIGPS [17] and eIGPS [18] unfold via a highly populated intermediate, and their unfolding titration curves are well described by a three-state model, N50% shaded block I50% shaded block U. With the exception of eIGPS-Deltabeta7alpha7-N231A, the urea-induced unfolding transition for each of the remaining five clamp-deletion variants is also well-described by this three-state model (Figure 3A and 3B). Because a distinct transition between the native state (N) and the intermediate state (I) is not observed during the urea induced denaturation of eIGPS-Deltabeta7alpha7-N231A (Figure 3B), kinetic unfolding experiments were performed to verify the existence of I and measure the free energy difference between N and I [6].

###begin title 30
Survey of betaalpha-hairpin clamps in the TIM barrel proteins
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 384 388 377 381 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Gromiha1">[15]</xref>
###xml 458 460 451 453 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NH</sub>
###xml 458 460 451 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>NH</sub></italic>
###xml 551 572 535 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="sec" rid="s4">Materials and Methods</xref>
The observation that betaalpha-hairpin clamps can have a significant effect on structure and stability in three TIM barrel proteins motivated a survey of the prevalence of such non-local MC-SC H-bonds in the TIM barrel fold. This analysis was carried out for a structural database of 71 TIM barrel domains, previously reported as a non-redundant representation of the TIM barrel fold [15]. H-bonds between main chain amide hydrogens and polar side chains (MCNH --> SC) that serve as betaalpha-hairpin clamps in the TIM barrel domains were identified (Materials and Methods) for a direct comparison with experimental results.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 56 58 56 58 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NH</sub>
###xml 56 58 56 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>NH</sub></italic>
###xml 110 118 101 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0007179.s004">Table S1</xref>
###xml 183 184 172 173 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
###xml 184 189 173 178 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yates</sub>
###xml 233 237 222 226 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;128</sup>
In the 71 TIM barrel proteins examined, there are 131 MCNH --> SC betaalpha-hairpin clamps. As can be seen in Table S1, there is a very significant preference, >42% of the clamps (chi2Yates = 592.49, n = 131, d = 3, p-value = 4.26x10-128), for aspartic acid SCs forming H-bonds with the MC amide hydrogen of isoleucine, leucine and valine residues. Inspection of the location of the donor and acceptor residues in the beta-strands reveals that every betaalpha-hairpin clamp secures the N-terminus of one beta-strand to the loop preceding the subsequent beta-strand in the barrel.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 43 45 43 45 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NH</sub>
###xml 43 45 43 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>NH</sub></italic>
###xml 95 104 86 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007179-g004">Figure 4A</xref>
###xml 1223 1225 1100 1102 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nd</sup>
###xml 1365 1374 1236 1245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007179-g004">Figure 4B</xref>
###xml 1579 1583 1444 1448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Branden1">[20]</xref>
The locations of the entire group of 131 MCNH --> SC betaalpha-hairpin clamps are displayed in Figure 4A, with each clamp interaction represented as a bridge across two adjacent beta-strands. A very strong preference (77%) is seen for beta1alpha1, beta3alpha3, beta5alpha5 and beta7alpha7 clamps, where the SC acceptor is C-terminal to the MC H-bond donor. With the exception of 13 beta8alpha8 clamps, the paucity of beta2alpha2, beta4alpha4 and beta6alpha6 clamps is distinct from their odd beta-strand counterparts. The relatively large number of clamps for the beta8beta1 interface may reflect the necessity for securing the N- and C-terminal beta-strands. Far fewer betaalpha-hairpin clamps, in which the SC acceptor is N-terminal to the MC H-bond donor, are observed. Highlighting the significance of this distribution pattern, the 55 Ile, Leu and Val (I/L/V) MC --> SC Asp (D) sub-group of betaalpha-hairpin clamps always have their MC H-bond donor I/L/V located in the odd-numbered stands, beta1, beta3, beta5 or beta7, and their SC acceptor, D, is always located before the succeeding even-numbered beta-strands, beta2, beta4, beta6 and beta8. There is also a strong preference for the I/L/V residue to occupy the 2nd position in the odd-numbered beta-strand and for the D residue to occupy the position immediately preceding the even-numbered beta-strand (Figure 4B). This positional preference braces two consecutive and adjacent beta-strands, along with the intervening helix, and reinforces the beta-strand register required for the canonical TIM barrel architecture [20].

###begin title 36
Discussion
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 137 140 126 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Yang1">[6]</xref>
###xml 1083 1090 1051 1058 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0007179-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 1184 1188 1152 1156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Schneider1">[21]</xref>
###xml 1189 1193 1157 1161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Hyde1">[23]</xref>
###xml 1499 1501 1460 1462 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
Experimental analysis of betaalpha-hairpin clamps between MC H-bond donors and SC H-bond acceptors in three TIM barrel proteins, alphaTS [6], sIGPS and eIGPS, has shown that a subset of these non-covalent interactions make substantive contributions to stability. Comparisons of the potency of the betaalpha-hairpin clamps in these three proteins shows no correlation between the contributions of these clamps to stability and either the location of the clamps in the structure, their contributing residues or their relative exposure (0-25%) to the solvent. The observation of potent clamps formed by the neutral N228 in sIGPS and N231 in eIGPS, the beta7alpha7 clamps, also shows that the formal negative charge on the aspartic acid H-bond acceptors in the remaining two potent betaalpha-hairpin clamps is not determinative of the strength of the clamp interaction. An examination of the crystal structures of the three proteins, however, suggests that the length of the H-bond in each structure differentiates between the clamps that make major or minor contributions to stability (Table 1). Although the nominal resolutions of the crystal structures of these proteins, 2.0 to 2.8 A [21]-[23], dictate that the correlation between H-bond length and the clamp contribution to protein stability be viewed as tentative, it appears betaalpha-hairpin clamps whose H-bonds are less than 2.8 A in length are those, which when replaced with alanine, reduce the stability of the native state by 4-6 kcal mol-1. The apparent correlation provides a logical and testable hypothesis for future experiments on betaalpha-hairpin clamps in other TIM barrel proteins.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 549 565 539 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007179-g002">Figure 2C and 2D</xref>
The assay for the contribution of the betaalpha-hairpin clamps to the stability of three TIM barrel proteins involves the replacement of the polar side chain H-bond acceptors, asparagine, aspartic acid, glutamic acid and serine, with alanine. The absence of the H-bond acceptor moiety is accompanied by the introduction of a potential void for these buried side chains, reflecting the absence of chemical mass as the side chain is truncated to the beta-carbon. The perturbations in the secondary and/or tertiary structures induced by the mutations (Figure 2C and 2D) show that the loss of the clamp is propagated to numerous other non-covalent interactions via the global cooperativity of the native conformation.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 181 184 174 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Yang1">[6]</xref>
###xml 357 360 346 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Yang1">[6]</xref>
The absence of the betaalpha-hairpin clamps in the I states of all three TIM barrel proteins demonstrates that the potent effects of these clamps only appear as the N state appears [6]. Kinetic folding studies on alphaTS revealed further that each clamp is crucial for accessing the transition state ensemble required to reach the properly-folded structure [6]. Although the local connectivity of the betaalphabeta modules might have been expected to enable the clamp to have a role in the early stages of the folding reaction, the primary role of the potent set of clamps is to drive the final stage of the reaction to completion and fully develop global cooperativity.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 487 495 455 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007179-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 603 607 568 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Branden1">[20]</xref>
###xml 766 770 719 723 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Wierenga1">[24]</xref>
###xml 798 802 751 755 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Branden1">[20]</xref>
###xml 804 808 757 761 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Wierenga1">[24]</xref>
###xml 1060 1061 996 997 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">8</sub>
###xml 1080 1088 1016 1024 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007179-g005">Figure 5</xref>
The 4-fold symmetry of the preferred betaalpha-hairpin clamps is mirrored, not only in the symmetry of the betaalphabeta modules, but also in the packing of the side chains in the interior of the beta-barrel. A residue oriented towards the inside of the beta-barrel from an odd-numbered beta-strand is at the same level as corresponding residues from the three remaining odd-numbered beta-strands. The next layer is comprised of the four side chains from the even-numbered beta-strands (Figure 5); the third, and usually final layer, is comprised again of side chains from the odd-numbered beta-strands [20]. The layering of side chains inside the barrel has its origin in the tilt of the beta-strands (35degrees) with respect to the central axis of the beta-barrel [24]. The resulting S = 8 shear [20], [24] provides a favorable orientation for the H-bonding network between adjacent parallel beta-strands and provides opportunities for MC-SC betaalpha-hairpin clamp interactions. Together, these non-covalent interactions and others stabilize the (betaalpha)8, TIM barrel fold (Figure 5). The observation of similarly placed non-local MC-SC interactions in a limited survey of flavodoxin fold proteins (data not shown) suggests that betaalpha-hairpin clamps are a common structural feature of betaalpha-repeat proteins.
###end p 40

###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 205 213 199 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007179-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 350 353 338 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-FarzadFard1">[5]</xref>
###xml 477 485 458 466 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007179-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 522 525 503 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Nagano1">[1]</xref>
###xml 711 715 686 690 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Aurora1">[10]</xref>
###xml 1155 1163 1104 1112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0007179.s004">Table S1</xref>
The chemical origin for the asymmetry between odd- and even-numbered beta-strands is apparent from an inspection of the residue preference (>15%) at positions preceding the N-terminus of each beta-strand (Figure 5). The conserved proline just before odd-numbered beta-strands provides a kink in the backbone that marks the beginning of a beta-strand [5]. The preferred sequence pattern of the tight turn connecting the alpha-helix and the subsequent even-numbered beta-strand (Figure 5), GAD, has been reported previously [1]. The positive phi angle allowed by glycine and the hydrophobic nature of alanine immediately following the alpha-helix enables a Schellman motif for the C-terminal capping of the helix [10] and a tight turn to the next beta-strand. The aspartic acid just prior to the beginning of even-numbered beta-strands forms the betaalpha-hairpin clamp and braces the betaalphabeta module. This N-terminal cap for the odd-numbered beta-strand is very often complemented by a MC-MC H-bond, with the amide group of the aspartic acid acting as the donor to the MC carbonyl oxygen of the partner residue. While other SC acceptors are observed (Table S1), the length of the aspartic acid side chain appears to be optimal for the reinforcement of the MC-SC H-bond with the MC-MC H-bond, providing a plausible explanation for its higher frequency in betaalpha-hairpin clamps.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 43 45 43 45 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NH</sub>
###xml 43 45 43 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>NH</sub></italic>
###xml 176 180 173 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Branden1">[20]</xref>
###xml 316 320 313 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Radzicka1">[25]</xref>
###xml 635 651 621 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007179-g001">Figure 1C and 1D</xref>
###xml 784 788 766 770 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Vadrevu1">[26]</xref>
###xml 789 793 771 775 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Gu1">[28]</xref>
###xml 851 857 833 839 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 857 861 839 843 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Gao1">[29]</xref>
###xml 1160 1162 1139 1141 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
The preference for I/L/V residues at the MCNH H-bond donor position may reflect, in part, the >40% occurrence of these residues in parallel beta-strands of TIM barrel proteins [20]. Further, along with alanine and glycine, I/L/V are the only amino acids that do not partition favorably from the vapor phase to water [25]. As such, these large aliphatic side chains are especially effective at excluding water from MC-SC H-bonds in the betaalpha-hairpin clamps. The exclusion of water, that is apparent from the limited access to solvent for the H-bond donor and acceptor atoms of potent clamp interactions in alphaTS, sIGPS and eIGPS (Figure 1C and 1D), is expected to strengthen these H-bonds and make them more resistant to exchange with solvent, as observed previously for alphaTS [26]-[28]. This presumption is supported by the conclusions of Gao et al.[29], who recently reported that the strength of a MC-MC H-bond is inversely related to the polarity of its local environment. Valine more effectively screened an underlying beta-sheet MC-MC H-bond from solvent than alanine in a Pin WW domain, increasing the strength of the H-bond by up to 1.2 kcal mol-1.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 80 83 70 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Gerstein1">[3]</xref>
###xml 85 89 75 79 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Branden1">[20]</xref>
###xml 221 224 197 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Presta1">[9]</xref>
###xml 226 230 202 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Aurora1">[10]</xref>
###xml 506 510 461 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Kunin1">[30]</xref>
###xml 590 594 545 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Rohl1">[31]</xref>
###xml 606 610 561 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Brylinski1">[32]</xref>
###xml 633 637 588 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Sali1">[33]</xref>
The occurrence of the betaalphabeta motif in a large number of protein families [3], [20] suggests that the N-terminal capping of beta-strands by betaalpha-hairpin clamps, akin to the analogous N-capping of alpha-helices [9], [10], may be a useful property for the refinement of protein fold prediction and for engineering stability in betaalpha-repeat proteins. betaalpha-repeat proteins are readily recognized from their sequences and the predicted alternating patterns of alpha-helices and beta-strands [30]. The refinement of the 3D structures predicted from knowledge-based potentials [31], threading [32] and homology modeling [33] of these protein sequences, could be enhanced by screening for betaalpha-hairpin clamps between the MC amide hydrogens at favored positions near the N-terminus of a beta-strand and H-bond acceptor SC in the loop before the subsequent beta-strand (approximately25 residues apart in sequence). These clamps would establish the register of the pair of beta-strands, and, with the very short loop linking the intervening alpha-helix to the second beta-strand, it might be possible to establish the register of the alpha-helix on the beta-strand pair in the betaalphabeta module. Although TIM barrel proteins typically contain only a few betaalpha-hairpin clamps, defining the spatial relationships of the components of a subset of betaalphabeta modules might increase the probability of predicting the packing of adjacent betaalpha-repeats in the structures. The effect of accurately predicting the structure of one betaalphabeta module might, therefore, propagate throughout the TIM barrel protein.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 109 112 109 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Nagano1">[1]</xref>
###xml 230 234 230 234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Gerlt1">[34]</xref>
###xml 235 239 235 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Wieczorek1">[37]</xref>
###xml 286 290 286 290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Rothlisberger1">[38]</xref>
The TIM barrel architecture provides a scaffold that is capable of a very diverse set of enzymatic functions [1], and this property has enabled TIM barrel enzymes to be re-engineered in order to accommodate alternative substrates [34]-[37] and even to catalyze non-biological reactions [38]. Because the active sites of TIM barrel enzymes are invariably comprised of the loops protruding from the C-termini of the beta-strands, engineering betaalpha-hairpin clamps at the N-termini of the beta-strands offer a unique opportunity to enhance the stability of TIM barrel proteins without compromising function.
###end p 46

###begin title 52
Circular dichroism
###end title 52
###begin p 53
Far- and near-UV CD spectroscopy was employed to monitor the secondary and the tertiary structure near aromatic side chains, respectively. Spectra were obtained on a Jasco Model J-810 spectropolarimeter equipped with a thermoelectric cell holder. Far-UV CD data were collected from 280 nm to 185 nm at a scan rate of 50 nm/min and at 1 nm intervals using a 0.1 cm pathlength cell, a bandwidth of 2.5 nm, with an averaging time of 8 s. Three replicate spectra were collected and averaged. The protein concentration was 5 microM. Near-UV CD data were collected from 350 nm to 250 nm at 5 nm/min using a 0.5 cm path length cell, and the protein concentration was 50-150 microM. The temperature was maintained at 25degreesC with a computer-controlled Peltier system.

The stability of the IGPS clamp-deletion variants was measured by urea denaturation as described previously [17] in a buffer containing 10 mM potassium phosphate, pH 7.8 for sIGPS and pH 7.0 for eIGPS, 0.2 mM K2EDTA, and 1 mM betaME. ###begin title 60
Definitions of betaalpha-hairpin clamp interactions
###end title 60
###begin p 61
###xml 207 211 200 204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-McDonald1">[45]</xref>
###xml 467 470 458 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Presta1">[9]</xref>
###xml 472 476 463 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Aurora1">[10]</xref>
###xml 659 661 636 638 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NH</sub>
###xml 659 661 636 638 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>NH</sub></italic>
###xml 823 827 792 796 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Delano1">[46]</xref>
For each protein, the 8 canonical beta-strands and alpha-helices in the context of the TIM barrel architecture were identified and labeled accordingly. H-bonding partners identified using the HBPLUS program [45], were subjected to the following filters: 1) the H-bonds must be between a MC amide donor and a SC acceptor, 2) the amino acid chain length between the donor and acceptor must be >/=15 residues thereby eliminating shorter-range helix-capping interactions [9], [10] and 3) the chain must include exactly one beta-strand and one alpha-helix identified in the context of the TIM barrel architecture. For the case of the beta8alpha8-hairpin clamps, MCNH --> SC H-bonds between the residues prior to beta1 and beta8 were included. The H-bonds that passed each stage of the filtering process were exported to a PyM0L [46] script in color-coded fashion for manual confirmation.
###end p 61
###begin title 62
Statistical significance of residue preference for betaalpha-hairpin clamps
###end title 62
###begin p 63
###xml 19 21 19 21 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NH</sub>
###xml 19 21 19 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>NH</sub></italic>
###xml 507 508 481 482 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
###xml 559 563 533 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007179-Yates1">[47]</xref>
The frequency of MCNH --> SC H-bonds in the 71 TIM barrel proteins, where the donor and acceptor residues were at least 15 amino acids apart and were not involved in betaalpha-hairpin clamp interactions, was determined. This frequency was used to calculate the expected frequency of H-bonding between any two types of residues and compared to that observed in betaalpha-hairpin clamps. Four categories, Ile MC --> SC D, Leu MC --> SC D, Val MC --> SC D, Other MC --> SC Other, were used to determine the chi2 distribution probabilities, with Yates correction [47], of observed betaalpha-hairpin clamps.
