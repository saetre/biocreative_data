###begin article-title 0
###xml 0 22 0 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3 / Pgc7 / stella </italic>
###xml 94 98 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Dppa3 / Pgc7 / stella is a maternal factor and is not required for germ cell specification in mice
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Background
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 126 132 126 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3 </italic>
###xml 147 152 147 152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pgc7 </italic>
###xml 155 161 155 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">stella</italic>
###xml 3 7 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In mice, germ cells are specified through signalling between layers of cells comprising the primitive embryo. The function of Dppa3 (also known as Pgc7 or stella), a gene expressed in primordial germ cells at the time of their emergence in gastrulating embryos, is unknown, but a recent study has claimed that it plays a central role in germ cell specification.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Results
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 8 14 8 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3'</italic>
###xml 171 176 171 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 270 275 270 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 357 362 357 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 72 77 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
To test Dppa3's role in germ cell development, we disrupted the gene in mouse embryonic stem cells and generated mutant animals. We were able to obtain viable and fertile Dppa3-deficient animals of both sexes. Examination of embryonic and adult germ cells and gonads in Dppa3-deficient animals did not reveal any defects. However, most embryos derived from Dppa3-deficient oocytes failed to develop normally beyond the four-cell stage.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Conclusion
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 14 20 14 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3 </italic>
###xml 78 83 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We found that Dppa3 is an important maternal factor in the cleavage stages of mouse embryogenesis. However, it is not required for germ cell specification.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Background
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 451 452 451 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 453 454 453 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 608 609 608 609 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 610 611 610 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
Among the many specialized cell types present in adult mammals, the first to be programmed or specified during embryogenesis are germ cells, which give rise to eggs and sperm. Which molecules direct this programming of germ cells? In many other animals, including flies and worms, material known as "germ plasm" is laid down in the egg before fertilization, and its subsequent passage to a subset of embryonic cells dictates their fate as germ cells [1,2]. In mammalian embryos, germ cells are specified in a very different manner, through signalling between layers of cells comprising the primitive embryo [3,4].
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 146 147 146 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 148 149 148 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 215 222 215 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">stella </italic>
###xml 224 229 224 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PGC7 </italic>
###xml 231 236 231 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 322 323 322 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 324 325 324 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 326 327 326 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 356 361 356 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 478 483 478 483 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 688 694 688 694 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3 </italic>
###xml 829 834 829 834 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 140 144 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 382 387 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
###xml 675 679 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 845 849 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Recently, Saitou, Barton and Surani proposed a molecular pathway by which these intercellular signals are translated into germ cell fate in mice [5,6]. Central to this proposed program of germ cell specification is stella / PGC7 / Dppa3, a gene expressed in primordial germ cells and their descendants, including oocytes [5,7,8]. Here we will use the name Dppa3, as approved by the Mouse Genome Informatics Database, when referring to this gene. Saitou and colleagues' model of Dppa3's role in germ cell specification was based on the timing and site of the gene's expression, not on functional analysis. Nonetheless, the model makes clear predictions as to the phenotype of mice lacking Dppa3 function: such embryos should not form germ cells. We tested this prediction and sought to clarify the gene's importance by generating Dppa3-deficient mice and examining their germline development.
###end p 9

Results and Discussion
###end title 10
###begin p 11

###xml 734 740 734 740 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3 </italic>
###xml 779 781 779 781 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 857 862 857 862 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 104 108 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 178 183 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 486 490 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We disrupted the Dppa3 gene in cultured embryonic stem (ES) cells and thereby generated Dppa3-deficient mice. Specifically, we replaced the entire open reading frame of Dppa3 in mouse V6.5 ES cells [9] with a hygromycin-thymidine kinase selection cassette flanked by loxP sites (Figure 1A). The selection cassette was subsequently removed via transient expression of Cre recombinase in targeted ES cells. The resulting heterozygous Dppa3tm1WHT/+ ES cells were used to generate chimeric mice, which transmitted the mutation to offspring. Intercrosses between Dppa3tm1WHT/+ heterozygous animals yielded Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT homozygotes as well as Dppa3tm1WHT/+ heterozygotes and +/+ offspring, demonstrating that zygotic function of Dppa3 is not essential for viability (Figure 1B). This allowed us to characterize germ cell development in animals lacking Dppa3.

Our findings do not support the proposed centrality of Dppa3 in germ cell programming. First, the gonads of Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT embryos contained germ cells, identified by expression of alkaline phosphatase, in numbers comparable to those of Dppa3tm1WHT/ + and +/+ embryos (Figure 2A). Second, the ovaries of Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT adult females expressed Oct4, a marker of oocytes [10,11], despite the absence of Dppa3 expression (Figure 2B). Third, histological examination of the gonads of Dppa3tm1WHT/ Dppa3tm1WHT adults revealed no morphological defects; spermatogenesis in males and ovarian follicle development in females appeared to be normal (Figure 2C,2D). Finally, we obtained fertile Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT mice of both sexes (though litters from Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT females were small, as described below). Each of these findings demonstrates that the Dppa3 gene is not required for germ cell specification.

###xml 424 430 418 424 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tm1WHT</italic>
###xml 424 430 418 424 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>tm1WHT</italic></sup>
###xml 512 517 506 511 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 517 523 511 517 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tm1WHT</italic>
###xml 517 523 511 517 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>tm1WHT</italic></sup>
###xml 524 529 518 523 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 529 536 523 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tm1WHT </italic>
###xml 529 536 523 530 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>tm1WHT </italic></sup>
###xml 539 544 533 538 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 544 550 538 544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tm1WHT</italic>
###xml 544 550 538 544 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>tm1WHT</italic></sup>
We found that Dppa3 plays an important role in early embryonic development as a maternal factor. While Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT males were fully fertile, Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT females had small litters. This was true regardless of whether such females were crossed with Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT, Dppa3tm1WHT/+ or wild type males (3.5 +/- 1.5, 3.1 +/- 2.1, or 3.0 +/- 0.9 viable pups/litter, respectively). By contrast, Dppa3tm1WHT/+ females of the same (mixed) genetic background had large litters when mated to Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT or Dppa3tm1WHT/+ males (9.4 +/- 3.5 or 10.1 +/- 3.2 viable pups/litter, respectively).
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 36 41 36 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 41 47 41 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tm1WHT</italic>
###xml 41 47 41 47 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>tm1WHT</italic></sup>
###xml 48 53 48 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 53 60 53 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tm1WHT </italic>
###xml 53 60 53 60 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>tm1WHT </italic></sup>
###xml 221 226 221 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 293 304 293 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3A,3B,3C,3D</xref>
###xml 384 389 384 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3E,3F</xref>
###xml 418 423 418 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 565 570 565 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 656 661 656 661 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3E,3F</xref>
###xml 716 722 716 722 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3 </italic>
We attribute the small litters from Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT mothers to abnormalities that manifest early in embryogenesis, during the cleavage stages of pre-implantation development. While nearly all embryos derived from Dppa3-deficient oocytes developed to the 2-cell or 4-cell stage (Figure 3A,3B,3C,3D), subsequent development was severely compromised in most such embryos (Figure 3E,3F). Some embryos derived from Dppa3-deficient oocytes failed to reach the 8-cell stage and instead showed evidence of compaction at the 4-cell stage. Other embryos derived from Dppa3-deficient oocytes cleaved to form 8 to 16 blastomeres, but failed to compact (Figure 3E,3F). These observations suggest that maternally supplied Dppa3 function is important in the cleavage stages of pre-implantation development.

###xml 270 272 266 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tg</italic>
###xml 273 291 269 283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pou5f1 &#916;PE-GFP</italic>
###xml 292 298 284 290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">10WHT </italic>
###xml 272 298 268 290 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">(<italic>Pou5f1 &#916;PE-GFP</italic>)<italic>10WHT </italic></sup>
###xml 361 366 353 358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oct4 </italic>
###xml 723 728 715 720 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3G,3H</xref>
###xml 787 792 779 784 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 872 876 864 868 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oct4</italic>
###xml 931 937 923 929 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3 </italic>
Might maternal Dppa3 induce zygotic expression of Oct4/Pou5f1, which encodes a transcription factor that is crucial to pre-implantation development [10,12]? To test this possibility, we crossed Dppa3tm1WHT/Dppa3tm1WHT females with Dppa3 +/+, Tg(Pou5f1 DeltaPE-GFP)10WHT/Tg(Pou5f1 DeltaPE-GFP)10WHT males, the latter transmitting an Oct4-GFP transgene, with the Oct4 promoter driving expression of GFP. We retrieved the resulting embryos at the 2-cell stage and cultured them in vitro for 72 hours to monitor expression of the Oct4-GFP transgene. All such embryos were observed to express the fluorescent marker, regardless of the degree to which the embryos developed or failed to develop during the culture period (Figure 3G,3H). Thus, the poor development of many embryos derived from Dppa3-deficient oocytes cannot be attributed to the absence of zygotic expression of Oct4. Further analysis of the maternal-effect phenotype of Dppa3 should illuminate the molecular and biological context and consequences of the gene's activity.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Conclusions
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 17 23 17 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3 </italic>
###xml 170 176 170 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3 </italic>
###xml 70 74 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We conclude that Dppa3 is not required for germ cell specification in mice. The identity of the mammalian gene or genes that program germ cells remains an open question. Dppa3 appears to function as a maternal factor, with an important role early in embryogenesis, during cleavage.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Methods
###end title 24
###begin title 25
###xml 14 19 14 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
Generation of Dppa3-deficient animals
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 4 10 4 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3 </italic>
###xml 112 117 112 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dppa3</italic>
###xml 207 208 207 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 383 384 383 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 68 73 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The Dppa3 targeting construct contained 1.3-kb and 3-kb segments of mouse genomic DNA, the former located 5' of Dppa3's translation initiation site and the latter located 3' of the termination codon (Figure 1). At the center of the construct was a 3-kb hygromycin-thymidine kinase selection cassette (Hygro-TK) flanked by two loxP direct repeats. V6.5 (C57BL/6 x 129/Sv)F1 ES cells [9] were transfected by electroporation, and recombined clones were selected in the presence of hygromycin (Invitrogen). Correctly targeted clones were identified by long-distance genomic PCR. The Hygro-TK cassette was removed via transient transfection of ES cells with a Cre-expressing plasmid in the presence of ganciclovir (Sigma). The final genomic structure of the resulting clones was verified by Southern analysis. Two independently targeted ES cell clones were microinjected into Balb/c blastocysts to generate chimeras. Animals used in this study were of a mixed C57BL/6 x 129/Sv genetic background.
