Structure and function of the fourth subunit (Dpb4p) of DNA polymerase epsilon in Saccharomyces cerevisiae

###xml 1360 1372 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
DNA polymerase epsilon (Polepsilon) of Saccharomyces cerevisiae is purified as a complex of four polypeptides with molecular masses of >250, 80, 34 (and 31) and 29 kDa as determined by SDS-PAGE. The genes POL2, DPB2 and DPB3, encoding the catalytic Pol2p, the second (Dpb2p) and the third largest subunits (Dpb3p) of the complex, respectively, were previously cloned and characterised. This paper reports the partial amino acid sequence of the fourth subunit (Dpb4p) of Polepsilon. This protein sequence matches parts of the predicted amino acid sequence from the YDR121w open reading frame on S.cerevisiae chromosome IV. Thus, YDR121w was renamed DPB4. A deletion mutant of DPB4 (Deltadpb4) is not lethal, but chromosomal DNA replication is slightly disturbed in this mutant. A double mutant haploid strain carrying the Deltadpb4 deletion and either pol2-11 or dpb11-1 is lethal at all temperatures tested. Furthermore, the restrictive temperature of double mutants carrying Deltadpb4 and dpb2-1, rad53-1 or rad53-21 is lower than in the corresponding single mutants. These results strongly suggest that Dpb4p plays an important role in maintaining the complex structure of Polepsilon in S.cerevisiae, even if it is not essential for cell growth. Structural homologues of DPB4 are present in other eukaryotic genomes, suggesting that the complex structure of S.cerevisiae Polepsilon is conserved in eukaryotes.

###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 3 27 3 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 126 127 112 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c1">1</xref>
###xml 561 562 519 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c2">2</xref>
###xml 563 564 521 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c3">3</xref>
###xml 798 799 736 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c1">1</xref>
###xml 800 801 738 739 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c4">4</xref>
###xml 802 803 740 741 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c5">5</xref>
###xml 3 27 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
In Saccharomyces cerevisiae, three DNA polymerases, alpha, delta and epsilon, participate in chromosomal DNA replication (see 1 for a review). DNA polymerase alpha (Polalpha) consists of four subunits [Pol1p (Cdc17p), Pol12p, Pri1p and Pri2p] and its primary role is in the initiation of DNA replication and priming of Okazaki fragments. DNA polymerases delta and epsilon (Poldelta and Polepsilon) are required to complete synthesis of both the leading and lagging strands. Consistent with this notion, it has been shown by chromatin immunoprecipitation assay (2,3) that both Poldelta and Polepsilon bind at/or near replication origins and move with the replication fork. However, the precise functions performed by Poldelta and Polepsilon on leading and lagging strands have not yet been defined (1,4,5).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 0 24 0 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 107 108 103 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c5">5</xref>
###xml 125 126 121 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c6">6</xref>
###xml 139 140 135 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c6">6</xref>
###xml 141 142 137 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c7">7</xref>
###xml 169 194 165 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</italic>
###xml 231 232 227 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c8">8</xref>
###xml 409 410 397 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c5">5</xref>
###xml 530 531 512 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c9">9</xref>
###xml 532 534 514 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c10">10</xref>
###xml 0 24 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 169 194 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
Saccharomyces cerevisiae Poldelta is a multi-subunit complex that contains the three subunits Pol3 (Cdc2) (5), Hys2 (Pol31) (6) and Pol32 (6,7), which are homologues of Schizosaccharomyces pombe Pol3, Cdc1 and Cdc27, respectively (8). Poldelta requires additional replication factors, PCNA and RF-C complex, to catalyse processive DNA synthesis, suggesting that Poldelta is the leading strand polymerase (see 5 for a review). Poldelta also has a 3'-->5' exonuclease activity that acts as an editing function during DNA synthesis (9,10).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 88 89 82 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c1">1</xref>
###xml 90 92 84 86 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c11">11</xref>
###xml 93 95 87 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c12">12</xref>
###xml 258 260 246 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c11">11</xref>
###xml 261 263 249 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c13">13</xref>
###xml 264 266 252 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c14">14</xref>
###xml 334 338 322 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POL2</italic>
###xml 345 347 333 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c15">15</xref>
###xml 576 578 562 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c16">16</xref>
###xml 604 610 590 596 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2-9</italic>
###xml 614 621 600 607 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2-18</italic>
###xml 664 668 650 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POL2</italic>
###xml 728 730 714 716 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c17">17</xref>
###xml 581 586 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
Polepsilon is also a multi-subunit complex consisting of Pol2p, Dpb2p, Dpb3p and Dpb4p (1,11,12). Although Polepsilon itself is a highly processive enzyme, it also requires PCNA and RF-C complex to catalyse processive DNA synthesis under certain conditions (11,13,14). The Pol2p protein is the catalytic subunit and is encoded by the POL2 gene (15). It is a class B polymerase, characterised by a series of conserved domains, called domains I-VI, containing the 3'-->5' exonuclease subdomains and the DNA polymerase active site residues in the N-terminal half of the protein (16). Yeast strains with the pol2-9 or pol2-18 mutations within the polymerase domain in POL2 are temperature-sensitive for chromosomal DNA replication (17).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POL2</italic>
###xml 153 157 147 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POL2</italic>
###xml 239 241 233 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c18">18</xref>
###xml 292 299 286 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2-11</italic>
###xml 304 311 298 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2-12</italic>
###xml 353 355 347 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c18">18</xref>
###xml 356 358 350 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c20">20</xref>
###xml 482 484 470 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c21">21</xref>
###xml 485 487 473 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c23">23</xref>
###xml 528 532 516 520 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POL2</italic>
###xml 592 595 574 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pol</italic>
###xml 600 602 582 584 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c11">11</xref>
###xml 475 480 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
POL2 also includes a large region that is conserved in Polepsilon from all organisms but is not found in any other class B polymerase. The C-terminus of POL2 has dual roles in DNA replication and the response to DNA damage during S phase (18), as demonstrated by the fact that the C-terminal pol2-11 and pol2-12 mutants are deficient in both processes (18-20). In addition, suppressor studies, synthetic lethal tests and purification of various Polepsilon subassemblies from yeast (21-23) have shown that the C-terminal half of POL2 is important in the assembly of the Polepsilon holoenzyme [PolII* (11)].
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 56 60 50 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB2</italic>
###xml 148 150 142 144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c12">12</xref>
###xml 157 161 151 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB3</italic>
###xml 353 355 341 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c21">21</xref>
###xml 84 89 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 242 247 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The second subunit of Polepsilon (Dpb2), encoded by the DPB2 gene, is essential for yeast cell growth and required for chromosomal DNA replication (12). The DPB3 gene encodes the third subunit of Polepsilon; this subunit is not essential for yeast cell growth, although it plays an important role in stabilising the subunit structure of the polymerase (21).
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 235 236 229 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c3">3</xref>
###xml 237 239 231 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c23">23</xref>
###xml 487 489 469 471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c24">24</xref>
###xml 490 492 472 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c25">25</xref>
###xml 728 730 686 688 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c16">16</xref>
###xml 932 939 884 891 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 941 943 893 895 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c26">26</xref>
###xml 1068 1070 1014 1016 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c27">27</xref>
###xml 684 689 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 918 931 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 932 939 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 1002 1006 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
It was recently shown that Polepsilon along with its interacting proteins, Dpb11p and Sld2p, plays an important role bringing other polymerase complexes and replication proteins to replication origins before elongation of DNA strands (3,23). More recent studies have shown that although Polepsilon may play an enzymatic role during DNA replication, this function does not appear essential, whereas the essential function of Polepsilon is provided by its non-catalytic C-terminal domain (24,25). Nonetheless, Polepsilon, like Poldelta, has the 3'-->5' exonuclease activity that provides editing function during DNA synthesis. Both Poldelta and Polepsilon 3'-->5' exonuclease-deficient yeast strains are spontaneous mutators (see 16 for a review), strongly suggesting that both polymerases are involved in normal chromosomal DNA replication. The subunit structure of Polepsilon in other eukaryotic systems including the fission yeast S.pombe (26) is not well characterised yet, although it is known that calf thymus Polepsilon also includes at least the second subunit (27).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 177 184 165 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">YDR121w</italic>
###xml 211 223 199 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 211 223 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
In this paper, the gene encoding the fourth subunit of Polepsilon is identified and characterised for the first time. The fourth subunit (Dpb4p) of Polepsilon is encoded by the YDR121w open reading frame on the S.cerevisiae chromosome IV. Although the gene is not essential for cell growth, its gene product plays an important function not only in maintaining the complex structure of Polepsilon, but also in interactions between Polepsilon and other replication proteins in the cell.
###end p 10

Expression of Dpb4 protein in Escherichia coli
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 4 7 4 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nco</italic>
###xml 9 12 9 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bam</italic>
###xml 43 50 43 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">YDR121w</italic>
###xml 138 154 138 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 285 286 279 280 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
###xml 879 880 861 862 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c6">6</xref>
###xml 138 159 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">Escherichia coli BL21</span>
###xml 847 854 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbits</span>
The NcoI-BamHI DNA fragment containing the YDR121w gene was inserted into pET15b plasmid (Novagen), resulting in the pET15b-DPB4 plasmid. Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) cells transformed with pET15b-DPB4 plasmid DNA were grown at 37degreesC in LB + 0.1 mg/ml ampicillin (900 ml) to 8 x 108 cells/ml and 0.5 mM IPTG was added into the cell culture to induce protein overexpression. After 3 h incubation, cells were collected by centrifugation, suspended in 10 ml buffer A (lacking PMSF) and sonicated at 0degreesC. The cell lysate was centrifuged at 8000 r.p.m. for 20 min in a Beckman JA20 rotor. The supernatant in the sample buffer was heated at 95degreesC for 3 min and subjected to SDS-PAGE. The 29-kDa polypeptide was eluted from the gel and identified as Dpb4 protein by protein sequencing. The protein eluted from the gel was used to immunise rabbits as reported previously (6).

###begin title 18
Protein sequencing
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 668 669 636 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c6">6</xref>
Polepsilon holoenzyme (200 microg) was fractionated by electrophoresis in a 5% SDS-polyacrylamide gel. After staining the gel with Coomassie brilliant blue, the 29-kDa band (Dpb4p) was excised and electroeluted. The eluted polypeptide (approximately20 microg) was digested in 0.2 M Tris-HCl (pH 9.5) containing 5 pmol of lysylendopeptidase (Wako) at 37degreesC overnight. To terminate the proteolysis, 1/10 vol of 10% trifluoroacetic acid was added and the supernatant was collected. Oligopeptides were separated by reverse-phase high-pressure liquid chromatography and were subjected to amino acid sequencing using a PSQ-10 protein sequencer (Shimadzu) as described (6).

In order to prove that YDR121w encodes the 29-kDa polypeptide (Dpb4), a yeast genomic DNA fragment containing YDR121w was cloned and expressed in E.coli as described in Materials and Methods. The expressed polypeptide was used as an antigen to raise rabbit antiserum. As shown in Figure 1, this antiserum specifically recognised the 29-kDa polypeptide that co-purified with Pol2p (>200 kDa polypeptide, the catalytic subunit of Polepsilon) in both partially and highly purified Polepsilon fractions. As described below, a deletion mutant (Deltadpb4) of DPB4 did not express this 29-kDa polypeptide (Fig. 1). These results indicate that the open reading frame YDR121w encodes Dpb4p. We therefore rename it DPB4.

###begin title 34
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 26 31 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
DPB4 is not essential for yeast cell growth
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 23 27 23 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 130 134 130 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">URA3</italic>
###xml 138 142 138 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TRP1</italic>
###xml 215 219 215 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 298 302 298 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 369 373 365 369 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 634 638 626 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 712 716 694 698 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 932 936 904 908 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 999 1001 967 969 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c23">23</xref>
###xml 1229 1230 1185 1186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd582f2">2</xref>
###xml 1282 1286 1234 1238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 1411 1415 1347 1351 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 1444 1445 1380 1381 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 1541 1542 1467 1468 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1557 1558 1483 1484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd582f2">2</xref>
###xml 1601 1605 1527 1531 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 344 349 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1632 1637 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
To investigate whether DPB4 is essential for cell growth, the open reading frame of the chromosomal gene was replaced with either URA3 or TRP1 by the one step replacement technique. Haploid strains with the deleted DPB4 allele were viable and readily obtained (data not shown), indicating that the DPB4 gene is not essential for cell growth in yeast. The obtained Deltadpb4 haploid strains were tested for their sensitivity to UV light, X-rays, methylmethane sulfonate and hydroxyurea. The results showed that they were not sensitive to any of those treatments (data not shown). However, a slight growth defect was observed in a Deltadpb4 haploid strain at both 25 and 37degreesC. The generation times of a Deltadpb4 haploid strain at 25 and 37degreesC in YPD medium were 210 and 250 min, respectively, while those of a parental wild-type strain at both temperatures were 180 and 140 min, respectively. Logarithmically growing Deltadpb4 mutant and wild-type cells were synchronised by alpha factor (23) and released into fresh YPD medium at either 25 or 37degreesC. After incubation at either 25 or 37degreesC, cells were harvested, fixed with ethanol, stained with propidium iodide and analysed by a FACScan. As shown in Figure 2, a slightly prolonged S phase was observed in Deltadpb4 mutant cells at 25degreesC. This prolonged cell cycle progression in S phase was enhanced at 37degreesC. A majority of Deltadpb4 mutant cells were still in G1/S phase 90 min after alpha factor release at 37degreesC, when wild-type cells were already in G2/M phase (Fig. 2). These results suggest that although the DPB4 gene is not essential for yeast cell growth, its gene product plays an important role in chromosomal DNA replication.

###begin title 36
###xml 42 46 32 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
Polepsilon complex is less stable in Deltadpb4 mutant cells than in wild-type cells
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 39 43 35 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 111 115 97 101 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 174 176 160 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c11">11</xref>
###xml 198 199 184 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd582f1">1</xref>
###xml 348 352 312 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 557 558 509 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd582f1">1</xref>
###xml 915 917 843 845 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c11">11</xref>
###xml 918 920 846 848 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c14">14</xref>
###xml 1055 1059 979 983 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 1192 1193 1116 1117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd582f1">1</xref>
###xml 1282 1286 1196 1200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
To prove that Dpb4p is missing in Deltadpb4 mutant cells, Polepsilon activity was partially purified from Deltadpb4 mutant cells by MonoQ column chromatography as described (11). As shown in Figure 1A, Polepsilon activity was detected in fractions different from those of wild-type Polepsilon. Furthermore, the total amount of Polepsilon from Deltadpb4 mutant cells was ~50% of Polepsilon from wild-type cells. The fractions containing the activity were subjected to western blot, and probed with Polepsilon complex and Dpb4p antibodies. As shown in Figure 1B and C, no Dpb4p was detected in the fractions where a degraded form of Polepsilon and Polepsilon activities eluted. Furthermore, an intact form of Polepsilon (which contains >250 kDa Pol2 polypeptide) could not be separated from a degraded form of Polepsilon (~145 kDa polypeptide) by MonoQ column chromatography, which does not occur in wild-type cells (11,14). In addition, both Dpb2p and Dpb3p dissociate more easily from the Pol2 polypeptide after MonoQ chromatography in extracts from Deltadpb4 mutant cells, resulting in a much smaller amount of Dpb3p rather than wild-type Dpb3p being associated with Pol2p polypeptide (Fig. 1B). Therefore, these results suggest that Polepsilon complex is much less stable in Deltadpb4 mutant cells than in wild-type cells. This observation is consistent with the notion that Dpb4 plays an important role in maintaining the Polepsilon complex in the cell.

###begin p 39

###xml 155 159 139 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 286 288 270 272 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">10</sub>

To investigate whether Dpb4p plays a role in an in vitro DNA synthesis catalysed by Polepsilon, we compared biochemical properties of Polepsilon from Deltadpb4 mutant cells with those from wild-type cells using various templates/primers including an activated calf thymus DNA, oligo(dT)10/poly(dA)>500 (1:50) and singly-primed phiX174 viral single-stranded DNA as previously published (11,14). However, the mixture exhibited the same optimal pH, optimal MgCl2 concentration, salt sensitivity, Km for dNTPs and processivity as those of wild-type Polepsilon complex (data not shown). Therefore, we concluded that Dpb4p does not have any positive role for in vitro DNA synthesis catalysed by Polepsilon.

As shown in Figure 2, the Deltadpb4 mutant exhibits a slight delay in S phase progression, suggesting that it has a slight defect in chromosomal DNA replication. This prompted us to investigate genetic interactions between Deltadpb4 and other DNA replication mutants. A Deltadpb4 mutant was genetically crossed with other DNA replication mutants. The resultant diploids were sporulated and the tetrads were micro-dissected, germinated at 25degreesC for 4 days and each of the spores was analysed genetically. When Deltadpb4 was crossed with a temperature-sensitive pol2-11 mutant, no spores containing both Deltadpb4 and pol2-11 were recovered from more than 40 tetrads (data not shown). On the other hand, a haploid spore containing both mutations could be easily recovered from the tetrads, if a single copy plasmid containing either POL2 or DPB4 was introduced into the strain before crossing (data not shown). Thus, these results indicate that a haploid strain with Deltadpb4 and pol2-11 is not viable (synthetic lethal) at 25degreesC. Similar synthetic lethality was also observed between Deltadpb4 and pol2-12 and Deltadpb4 and dpb11-1 (Table 2 and data not shown). In contrast, double mutant haploid strains were viable with Deltadpb4 and either rad53-1, rad53-21, dpb2-1, pol3-12, hys2-2 or Deltapol32. The growth of these double mutant strains (and the control single mutant strains) was tested on a YPD plate at various temperatures (31). As shown in Table 2, the double mutants Deltadpb4 rad53-1, Deltadpb4 rad53-21, Deltadpb4 dpb2-1, Deltadpb4 hys2-2 and Deltadpb4 Deltapol32 had significantly lower restrictive temperatures than the corresponding single mutant strains. In particular, Deltadpb4 rad53-1 and Deltadpb4 rad53-21 double mutants exhibited a very poor growth phenotype at 25degreesC. Significantly, a Deltadpb3 Deltadpb4 deletion mutant had the same restrictive temperature as the single deletion mutant Deltadpb3 or Deltadpb4. Nevertheless, these results indicate a wide variety of genetic interactions between the DPB4 and other DNA replication genes, which suggests that Dpb4p plays an important function as the subunit of Polepsilon during chromosomal DNA replication in S.cerevisiae.

###begin p 43
###xml 37 41 37 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 107 111 107 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>

###xml 185 192 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>

The predicted amino acid sequence of DPB4 is similar to several other predicted proteins in the databases: DPB4 has 30% identity and 55% similarity to a putative DNA binding protein of S.pombe (DDBJ/EMBL/GenBank accession no. CAB09118); 23% identity and 51% similarity to Caenorhabditis elegans putative DNA binding protein (DDBJ/EMBL/GenBank accession no. AAC77511); 26% identity and 50% similarity to Xenopus and human nuclear transcription factor Y (DDBJ/EMBL/GenBank accession nos AAC82336 and NP-006157, respectively); and 26% identity and 50% similarity to mouse CCAAT-binding transcription factor subunit A (CBF-A) (DDBJ/EMBL/GenBank accession no. P25209). These six proteins were aligned using the program ClustalW 1.7 (32) and the result is shown in Figure 3. The block Maker program identified one conserved region at the N-terminal end of the protein that contains a histone-fold motif (shown by a double-line in the figure). The histone-fold motif resembles an extended helix-strand-helix motif first identified in the core histone proteins as primarily responsible for dimerisation of the H2A/H2B and H3/H4 histone pairs (33). Recently, this motif has been found in a number of proteins involved in protein-protein and protein-DNA interactions (34).

###xml 38 45 38 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">YDR121w</italic>
###xml 68 80 68 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 156 163 150 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">YDR121w</italic>
###xml 181 185 175 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 229 230 223 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd582f3">3</xref>
###xml 675 677 669 671 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c35">35</xref>
###xml 746 753 740 747 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1195 1197 1183 1185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c23">23</xref>
###xml 1272 1276 1256 1260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 1342 1343 1326 1327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd582tb2">2</xref>
###xml 1409 1413 1389 1393 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 1418 1425 1398 1405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb11-1</italic>
###xml 1634 1636 1608 1610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c23">23</xref>
###xml 1668 1672 1638 1642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>

###xml 1708 1715 1678 1685 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2-11</italic>
###xml 1720 1727 1690 1697 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2-12</italic>
###xml 1751 1757 1721 1727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2-9</italic>
###xml 1759 1766 1729 1736 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2-18</italic>

###xml 1816 1820 1782 1786 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 1837 1838 1803 1804 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd582tb2">2</xref>
###xml 2039 2043 1995 1999 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 2218 2219 2174 2175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd582tb2">2</xref>
###xml 68 80 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 446 451 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
This paper presents evidence that the YDR121w open reading frame on S.cerevisiae chromosome IV encodes the fourth subunit of Polepsilon complex. Therefore, YDR121w has been renamed DPB4. In addition, it is shown that Dpb4p (Fig. 3) and Dpb3p (our unpublished results) contain the histone-fold motif that is also present in many other proteins. The histone-fold motifs of Dpb4p and Dpb3p are related to those of subunits A and C, respectively, of human CCAAT binding factor CBF. An essential function of the histone-fold motifs of the CBF subunits is to create a protein-protein interaction surface for binding to the histone acetyltransferase enzymes such as GCN5 and p/CAF (35). These histone acetyltransferases can activate CBF transactivation in vivo, possibly by acetylation of the N-terminal lysine residues of histones which disrupts local chromatin structure, thereby facilitating CBF access to CCAAT promoter sites. At this time there is no evidence that Dpb3p and Dpb4p are part of the CCAAT binding factor; however, Dpb3p and Dpb4p may also create a protein-protein interaction surface allowing the Polepsilon complex to interact with other proteins, such as the Dpb11p-Sld2p complex (23), which could also modify chromatin structure. Consistent with this, Deltadpb4 genetically interacts with many other replication mutants (Table 2). Among those genetic interactions, the interaction between Deltadpb4 and dpb11-1 is very strong. This strong interaction suggests that two proteins physically interact inside the cell. In fact, previous works showed that Dpb11p physically interacts with Polepsilon complex during S phase (23). The interaction between Deltadpb4 and pol2 is allelic specific. Only pol2-11 and pol2-12 mutations, but neither pol2-9, pol2-18 or pol2-16, showed synthetic lethality with Deltadpb4 mutation (Table 2 and our unpublished results). Thus, these results strongly suggest that Dpb4p physically interacts with the C-terminal portion of Polepsilon polypeptide. Other weak interactions observed between Deltadpb4 and other DNA replication mutants may reflect their indirect interactions. Interestingly, no physical or genetic interaction has been detected between Dpb3p and Dpb4p (Table 2 and our unpublished results). Therefore, it is possible that Dpb3p and Dpb4p function independently.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
Compared to the extensive knowledge of the influence of histone acetylation on transcription, little is known of its influence on chromatin structure in replication. It is possible that local disruption of chromatin structure by histone acetylation might facilitate access of replication factors to nucleosomal DNA. Thus, more information on the possible interaction between Polepsilon subunits and proteins including histone acetyltransferases will provide insight into chromosomal DNA replication and the role of Polepsilon in that process.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 12 18 12 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al.</italic>
###xml 148 160 142 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 192 194 186 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd582c36">36</xref>
###xml 296 303 284 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus</italic>
###xml 148 160 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 276 281 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 296 303 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus</span>
###xml 511 516 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Recently Li et al. identified and cloned the two small subunits (p17 and p12 polypeptides) of HeLa Polepsilon and found that they are homologues of S.cerevisiae Dpb4p and Dpb3p, respectively (36). Furthermore, we detected two smaller subunits similar to those two subunits of human Polepsilon in Xenopus egg Polepsilon (T.Ohya, S.Waga and A.Sugino, unpublished results). Thus, it is highly possible that the subunit structure of Polepsilon is well conserved from lower eukaryotes to higher eukaryotes including human cells.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 56 68 56 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 56 68 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
In summary, this study shows that the fourth subunit of S.cerevisiae Polepsilon complex, and the overall subunit structure of Polepsilon, is conserved in eukaryotes. In addition, the results indicate that while Dpb4p is not essential for cell viability, it plays a crucial role, along with Dpb3p, in maintaining the subunit structure of Polepsilon and may facilitate interaction of the Polepsilon complex with other replication proteins.
###end p 48
