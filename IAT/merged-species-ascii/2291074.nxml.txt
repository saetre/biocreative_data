Anti-chaperone betaA3/A1102-117 peptide interacting sites in human alphaB-crystallin
###end article-title 0

###begin title 2
Purpose
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 234 241 231 238 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 560 567 543 550 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 162 167 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our previous work identified 23 low molecular weight (<3.5 kDa) crystallin peptides in the urea-soluble fractions of normal young, normal aged, and aged cataract human lenses. We found that one of these crystallin fragments, betaA3/A1102-117 peptide (SDAYHIERLMSFRPIC), that are present in aged and cataract lens, increased the scattering of light by beta- and gamma-crystallins and alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and also reduced the chaperone-like activity of alphaB-crystallin. The present study was performed to identify the interacting sites of the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide in alphaB-crystallin.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 9 16 6 13 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 703 710 675 682 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
betaA3/A1102-117 peptide was first derivatized with sulfo-succinimidyl-2-[6-(biotinamido)-2-{p-azidobenzamido}-hexanoamido] ethyl-1-3 dithio propionate (sulfo-SBED), a photoactivable, heterotrifunctional biotin-containing cross-linker. The biotin-derivatized peptide was then incubated with alphaB-crystallin at 37 degreesC for 2 h to allow complex formation followed by photolysis to facilitate the transfer of the biotin label from the peptide to alphaB-crystallin. Label transfer was confirmed by western blot, and the labeled alphaB-crystallin was digested with trypsin. Tryptic peptides from alphaB-crystallin carrying the biotin label were purified by avidin affinity chromatography, and betaA3/A1102-117 peptide interacting sites in alphaB-crystallin were identified by matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization-time-of-flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF MS) and nanospray quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry (QqTOF MS/MS).
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 27 34 24 31 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 85 87 78 80 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">70</sup>
###xml 92 94 85 87 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">74</sup>
###xml 96 98 89 91 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">83</sup>
###xml 108 110 101 103 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">92</sup>
###xml 112 114 105 107 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">91</sup>

###xml 149 152 142 145 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">107</sup>
###xml 158 161 151 154 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">121</sup>
###xml 164 167 157 160 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">123</sup>
###xml 321 328 303 310 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>

###xml 430 433 408 411 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">157</sup>
###xml 435 438 413 416 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">164</sup>
###xml 449 452 427 430 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">174</sup>
###xml 458 461 436 439 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">164</sup>
###xml 473 476 451 454 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">175</sup>
###xml 527 534 502 509 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
We found that the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide interacted with alphaB-crystallin regions 70LEKDR74, 83HFSPEELKVK92, 91VKVLGDVIEVHGK103, 93VLGDVIEVHGKHEER107, and 121KYR123, which are part of the alpha-crystallin domain, and were previously shown to be part of the functional chaperone site in alphaB-crystallin. The betaA3/A1102-117 peptide also interacted with regions at the COOH-terminal extension of alphaB-crystallin, 150KQVSGPER157, 164EEKPAVTAAPK174, and 164EEKPAVTAAPKK175. When two of the hydrophobic residues of betaA3/A1102-117 peptide were replaced with hydrophilic residues, the resulting substituted peptide, SDADHGERLMSFRPIC, did not show the anti-chaperone property.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusions
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 192 199 182 189 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
This study confirmed the interactions between a low molecular weight peptide derived from betaA3/A1-crystallin found in aged and cataract lenses and alphaB-crystallin. The binding of betaA3/A1102-117 peptide to the chaperone site and the COOH-terminal extension of alphaB-crystallin may explain its anti-chaperone property.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Introduction
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 327 328 316 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 329 330 318 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 464 465 453 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 466 467 455 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 789 790 778 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 791 793 780 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Human lens crystallins are organized into three classes, alpha-, beta-, and gamma-crystallins, based on sequence homology and the size of aggregates isolated under physiologic conditions. Since lens crystallins undergo little or no turnover, there is opportunity for numerous post-translational modifications as the lens ages [1-4]. Some modifications of lens crystallins identified in young clear lenses may reflect normal development and maturation of the lens [5,6] whereas other modifications associated with aged lenses- such as deamidation, phosphorylation, truncation, glycation, oxidation, and cross-linking could negatively impact crystallin conformation, aggregation state, or solubility, resulting in increased light scattering and eventual loss of transparency in aged lenses [7-10].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 110 111 110 111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 112 114 112 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 115 117 115 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 228 229 228 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 230 232 230 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 233 235 233 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 444 446 444 446 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 607 609 601 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 213 218 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 419 424 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 429 435 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
In comparison to normal lenses, cataract lenses show greater fragmentation of crystallins due to proteolysis [1,11-13]. Increased crystallin fragmentation has also been reported in aged and cataract lenses of non-human species [6,14-17]. It has been reported that proteolysis may be a contributing factor in the insolubilization of crystallins occurring during normal maturation of lens or during cataract formation in human and bovine lenses [18]. It has been shown that the fragments of betaA3/A1- and betaB1-crystallins are selectively insolubilized during cataract development compared to normal aging [13]. These authors also reported increased crystallin truncation, the deamidation of Asn to Asp residues, and the oxidation of a Trp residue in cataract lenses.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 104 106 104 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 107 109 107 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 563 565 563 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 709 711 709 711 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r26">26</xref>
###xml 854 856 854 856 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r27">27</xref>
###xml 1142 1144 1142 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
###xml 1314 1316 1314 1316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
###xml 671 676 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Several proteolytic enzymes have been shown to play a role in aging of the lens and cataract formation [19-25]. Activities of several peptidases were reported to be highest in the outer cortical fibers and decreased to one half or below in the inner cortical fibers and nucleus. An inverse correlation between peptidase activities and the amount of crystallin fragments was observed in different regions of the lens. The amount of crystallin fragments and the amount of water-insoluble proteins were greater in the lens nucleus than in the outer cortical fibers [21]. An age-dependent decline in proteasome activity and a concomitant accumulation of modified proteins in human lens has been reported earlier [26]. Increased opacification of the lens nucleus in cataract was significantly correlated with decreased peptidase activities of the proteasome [27]. Although a protein oxidation reaction may render the protein susceptible to proteolysis, heavily oxidized proteins appear to first aggregate (via new hydrophobic and ionic bonds) and then to form covalent cross-links that make them highly resistant to proteolysis by 20S proteasome [28]. In fact, these aggregated, cross-linked oxidized proteins actually bind to the 20S proteasome and inhibit its ability to degrade the oxidized forms of other proteins [29].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 210 212 210 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 213 215 213 215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r30">30</xref>
###xml 216 218 216 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r32">32</xref>
###xml 465 467 454 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r31">31</xref>
###xml 757 759 746 748 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
###xml 897 899 886 888 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 1051 1052 1037 1038 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">L</sub>
###xml 1134 1136 1116 1118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
###xml 1137 1139 1119 1121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r35">35</xref>
###xml 451 456 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Low molecular weight crystallin fragments have been isolated and characterized from both the water-soluble and water-insoluble fractions of the lens with greater prevalence in the water-insoluble fractions [11,21,30-32]. As many as 13 crystallin fragments with molecular masses between 3 and 17 kDa and originating from alpha-, beta-, and gamma-crystallins have been isolated from the water-soluble, high molecular weight aggregates of 60-80-year-old human lenses [31]. Accumulation of such crystallin fragments may be a cause for age-related lens opacity. It has been hypothesized that the interaction of short peptides derived from crystallins with lens proteins may increase the formation of high molecular weight aggregates and the scattering of light [33]. Though it is hypothesized that cataract develops as a result of improper interaction of crystallin fragments generated by proteolysis [25], the underlying mechanism is not clear. Earlier work by our group has shown that in vitro-oxidized crystallin peptides enhance the aggregation of betaL-crystallin and gamma-crystallin and also exhibit anti-chaperone-like properties [33-35]. Interaction of peptide fragments with lens proteins and their aggregation-enhancing nature may have some implications for age-related cataract formation.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 270 272 270 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r36">36</xref>
###xml 384 385 376 377 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 597 601 581 585 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;18</sub>
###xml 646 653 627 634 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 792 794 765 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r36">36</xref>
###xml 844 848 813 817 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;18</sub>
###xml 862 869 828 835 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 185 190 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Recent studies in our laboratory have revealed the presence of 23 low molecular weight (<3.5 kDa) peptides in the urea-soluble fractions of normal young, normal aged, and aged cataract human lenses, and the amount of crystallin fragments was found to increase with age [36]. Out of these 23 peptides, 15 were derived from alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin. Most of the 15 came from the NH2-terminal region, a few from the alpha-crystallin domain, and none from the COOH-terminal extension. Two crystallin peptides predominant in the nucleus of aged lens and cataract lens but not in young lens, alphaB1-18 (MDIAIHHPWIRRPFFPFH, GenBank ) and betaA3/A1102-117 (SD(N)AYHIERLMSFRPIC, GenBank ), increased the molecular mass, polydispersity, and hydrodynamic radius of alphaA- and alphaB-crystallins [36]. Our study also showed that the peptides, alphaB1-18 and betaA3/A1102-117, increased the scattering of light by beta- and gamma-crystallins and alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and also reduced the chaperone-like activity of alpha-crystallin.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 80 87 77 84 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 269 271 262 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r37">37</xref>
###xml 343 350 329 336 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 373 380 356 363 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
The present study was undertaken to identify the binding sites for the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide in alphaB-crystallin. We used the photoactive cross-linker sulfo-SBED (sulfo-succinimidyl-2-[6-(biotinamido)-2-{p-azidobenzamido}-hexanoamido] ethyl-1-3 dithio propionate) [37] to confirm the interaction between alphaB-crystallin and the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide. The betaA3/A1102-117 peptide interacting sites in alphaB-crystallin are reported here.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Methods
###end title 17
###begin title 18
Reagents
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 377 384 370 377 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 974 975 961 962 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">L</sub>
###xml 1057 1059 1044 1046 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r35">35</xref>
###xml 875 886 <span type="species:ncbi:3704">horseradish</span>
###xml 963 969 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">Bovine</span>
Trypsin gold (mass spectrometry grade) was purchased from Promega Corporation (Madison, WI). Sulfo-SBED, immobilized monomeric avidin kit, and micro-bicinchoninic acid (BCA) protein assay kit were procured from Pierce (Rockford, IL). Trypsin inhibitor, TLCK (N alpha-p-tosyl-L-lysine chloromethyl ketone), was obtained from Sigma Chemical Company (St. Louis, MO). The betaA3/A1102-117 peptide, SDAYHIERLMSFRPIC, and the substituted peptide, SDADHGERLMSFRPIC (in which the tyrosine and isoleucine corresponding to 105 and 107 in betaA3/A1-crystallin are replaced by aspartate and by glycine, respectively), were synthesized at the University of Missouri core facility and purified by HPLC. Their masses (1936.9 Da and 1833 Da, respectively) were ascertained by matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization-time-of-flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF MS). Blotting grade avidin-horseradish peroxidase conjugate was obtained from Bio-Rad Laboratories (Hercules, CA). Bovine betaL-crystallin was isolated from lens extract using the procedure described earlier [35]. All other chemicals were of analytical grade.

To identify the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide binding sites in alphaB-crystallin, the purified peptide was derivatized with sulfo-SBED, a biotin-containing and photoactivable as well as heterotrifunctional and amine-group specific reagent [37]. The procedure described by the supplier (Pierce) was used. In brief, 1.78 mg of betaA3/A1 peptide was dissolved in 10 mul DMSO and the volume was made up to 50 mul with PBS. To study the interaction between the sulfo-SBED-derivatized betaA3/A1102-117 peptide (or the substituted peptide) and alphaB-crystallin, 0.25 mg of the derivatized peptide was incubated with 0.25 mg of human recombinant alphaB-crystallin at 37 degreesC for 2 h. Transfer of the biotin label from the sulfo-SBED-derivatized peptide to alphaB-crystallin was confirmed by SDS-PAGE followed by western blot. The betaA3/A1102-117 peptide found in aged lenses is present in both deamidated (at Asn103) and non-deamidated forms in vivo. Both the native peptide and the deamidated form of the same peptide displayed similar anti-chaperone activity (data not shown here). In this study, we used the deamidated form of the peptide. Thermal denaturation of betaL-crystallin at 55 degreesC resulted in aggregation and light scattering. The addition of the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide to the incubation mixture resulted in increased light scattering by betaL-crystallin by 58% whereas the substituted peptide increased the light scattering only by 4% at 60 min. The betaA3/A1102-117 peptide by itself did not scatter light (Figure 1A). When the betaL-crystallin was heat-denatured in the presence of alphaB-crystallin, the light scattering was decreased by 41% at 60 min due to the chaperone-like action of alphaB-crystallin. However, when betaL-crystallin was denatured in the presence of alphaB-crystallin and betaA3/A1102-117 peptide, the light scattering increased by 20% showing the decreased chaperone action of alphaB-crystallin. The substituted peptide, on the other hand, caused a slight improvement in the chaperone action of alphaB-crystallin. The light scattering by denatured betaL-crystallin was decreased by 53% in the presence of alphaB-crystallin and substituted peptide (Figure 1B). The alphaB-crystallin-betaA3/A1102-117 peptide complex showed a lowered ability to prevent thermal aggregation of betaL-crystallin. This complex (5 mug) decreased the light scattering by heat-denatured betaL-crystallin at 60 min by only 3% (Figure 2, curve D) as opposed to a 51% decrease caused by an equivalent amount of the control, alphaB-crystallin (Figure 2, curve B). When a higher concentration of alphaB-crystallin-betaA3/A1102-117 peptide complex (10 mug) was used in the assay, it caused only a 24% decrease in light scattering (Figure 2, curve E) compared to the 81% decrease caused by an equivalent amount of the control, alphaB-crystallin (Figure 2, curve C).

###begin title 40
Discussion
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 132 134 132 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 135 137 135 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r40">40</xref>
###xml 265 267 265 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
###xml 540 542 540 542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r36">36</xref>
###xml 581 588 578 585 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 738 745 725 732 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 1008 1010 984 986 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r36">36</xref>
###xml 455 460 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The presence of crystallin fragments in water-soluble and water-insoluble fractions of lens proteins has been known for some years [11,40]. We first suggested that anti-chaperone peptides accumulating in vivo may be contributing to lens opacification during aging [33]. Recent studies in our laboratory have revealed the presence of 23 low molecular weight (<3.5 kDa) peptides in the urea-soluble fractions of normal young, normal aged, and aged cataract human lenses, and the amount of crystallin fragments was found to increase with age [36]. Our study also showed that betaA3/A1102-117 peptide, found in aged and cataract lens, increased the scattering of light by beta- and gamma-crystallins and alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH). betaA3/A1102-117 peptide also exhibited an anti-chaperone property by decreasing the ability of alpha-crystallin to prevent aggregation of beta- and gamma-crystallins and non-crystallin protein substrates, a process believed to be essential for maintenance of lens transparency [36].
###end p 41

The present study has confirmed the aggregation-enhancing property of the betaA3/A1-crystallin-derived peptide, 102SDAYHIERLMSFRPIC117, since it promoted the aggregation of betaL-crystallin during thermal denaturation (Figure 1A). The presence of this peptide in the assay mixture reduced the ability of alphaB-crystallin to protect betaL-crystallin against thermal aggregation (Figure 1B). Since the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide interacts with both betaL-crystallin and alphaB-crystallin, the enhanced light scattering observed in its presence may be due to a combination of its interaction with the substrate as well as the chaperone protein. To confirm the anti-chaperone nature of the peptide, we pretreated alphaB-crystallin with betaA3/A1102-117 peptide and separated the complex from the free peptide. The alphaB-crystallin-betaA3/A1102-117 peptide complex showed a considerable loss of chaperone-like activity against heat-denatured betaL-crystallin (Figure 2). Since free peptide was removed before the assay, we believe that the decreased chaperoning ability of alphaB-crystallin was specifically due to the binding of the peptide to alphaB-crystallin. When the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide was derivatized with sulfo-SBED, incubated with alphaB-crystallin, and photolyzed, the biotin label transferred to alphaB-crystallin (Figure 3, lane 1). The MALDI-TOF MS of the peptides derived from the alphaB-crystallin by trypsin digestion showed the presence of biotin from sulfo-SBED in several peptides (Figure 4). The identity of those peptides was obtained by sequencing in a nanospray QqTOF mass spectrometer. The sites on alphaB-crystallin where the label was observed (Figure 5) included the sequences, 70LEKDR74 and 83HFSPEELKVK92. Earlier, we showed that 73DRFSVNLDVKHFSPEELKVK92 is one of the well characterized functional chaperone sites in alphaB-crystallin [41]. Therefore, we can conclude that the anti-chaperone peptide betaA3/A1102-117 binds to alphaB-crystallin at the chaperone site. The binding of the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide to this substrate-binding site in alphaB-crystallin may have diminished the ability of the protein to effectively chaperone the denaturing betaL-crystallin.

###xml 1326 1328 1281 1283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r45">45</xref>
Other sites of interaction between the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide and alphaB-crystallin included the sequences, 91VKVLGDVIEVHGKHEER107 and 121KYR123. These sequences are part of the alpha-crystallin domain. Earlier, Arg120 was shown to be important for the chaperone-like activity of alphaB-crystallin [42]. A recent study demonstrated that 73DRFSVNLDVKHFS85 and 101HGKHEERQDE110 sequences in alphaB-crystallin inhibited the fibrillation of disease-related amyloidogenic proteins including alpha-synuclein and amyloid-beta [43]. Other binding sites of the betaA3/A1peptide to alphaB-crystallin included the COOH-terminal region of 150KQVSGPER157 and 164EEKPAVTAAPKK175. An earlier study has shown that mutations of the two terminal lysines (174 and 175) of alphaB-crystallin to leucine or glycine greatly reduced its chaperone-like activity. It was postulated that mutations in these two amino acids may have resulted in the COOH-terminus folding back on itself, losing its flexibility, and sterically hindering protein binding [44]. The COOH-terminal extensions of alpha-crystallins are considered polar, highly flexible, and solvent-exposed. These extensions also act as solubilizing agents for the relatively hydrophobic alpha-crystallin molecule and the high molecular weight complex that forms during the chaperone action [45].

alphaB-Crystallin with five amino acids deleted from its COOH-terminus was found in lenses of hereditary cataractous iCR/f rat, and this truncated crystallin showed decreased chaperone activity [46]. Protein pin array studies have identified 157RTIPITRE164 as one of the interactive sequences for chaperone activity in human alphaB-crystallin [47]. Deletion of the polar COOH-terminal sequence, 155PERTIPITREE165, from human alphaB-crystallin resulted in poor solubility and limited or no chaperone activity against unfolding betaL-crystallin, alcohol dehydrogenase, and citrate synthase. These results demonstrated the importance of the COOH-terminal residues in the recognition, selection, and maintenance of solubility of unfolding substrate proteins [48]. As identified by protein pin-array studies, the 150KQVSGPER157, 164EEKPAVTAAPK174, and 164EEKPAVTAAPKK175 sequences, which are identified as peptide interacting sites in alphaB-crystallin in the present study, overlap with sequences involved in dimerization and assembly of human alphaB-crystallin [49]. The binding of the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide to the COOH-terminal extension of alphaB-crystallin in the present study may have interfered with substrate binding and decreased the chaperoning ability of the protein. Our recent studies showed that the betaA3/A1102-117 peptide caused partial precipitation of alphaB-crystallin [36]. We believe that the peptide interaction at the COOH-terminal extension of alphaB-crystallin may have led to decreased flexibility and decreased solubilization potential of alphaB-crystallin. Additionally, the interactions between crystallins and low molecular weight peptides may limit the access of these peptides to peptidases, resulting in the accumulation of peptides with aging.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 246 247 243 244 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">L</sub>
###xml 272 281 269 278 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1A</xref>
###xml 313 322 310 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1B</xref>
###xml 925 932 903 910 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
###xml 974 982 952 960 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f3">Figure 3</xref>
The substituted peptide we used in this study, SDADHGERLMSFRPIC, had two hydrophobic residues (tyrosine and isoleucine) replaced by hydrophilic (aspartate and glycine) residues. The substitution abolished the ability of the peptide to induce betaL-crystallin aggregation (Figure 1A) and to act as anti-chaperone (Figure 1B). This suggests that the hydrophobic amino acids play a critical role in the interaction of the anti-chaperone peptide with alphaB-crystallin. The substituted peptide can also be considered a control peptide for the label transfer study. If there were any non-specific interactions between the derivatized substituted peptide and alphaB-crystallin, we would have seen a transfer of biotin to alphaB-crystallin following photolysis. The fact that the alphaB-crystallin incubated with derivatized substituted peptide showed only about 1.2% of the label transfer when compared to the derivatized betaA3/A1102-117 peptide during the blotting experiments (Figure 3 lane 2) suggests that the non-specific interaction and label transfer was insignificant in this study.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 67 74 64 71 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">102&#8211;117</sub>
In summary, this study confirmed the interactions between betaA3/A1102-117 peptide and alphaB-crystallin. The binding of this peptide to regions critical for chaperone action of alphaB-crystallin may explain its anti-chaperone property. The modulation of structural and functional properties of crystallins by low molecular weight peptides may play an important role in protein aggregation and cataract formation.
