RNA editing in hornwort chloroplasts makes more than half the genes functional
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 155 181 155 181 <email xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sckyosi@ipc.shizuoka.ac.jp</email>
To whom correspondence should be addressed at Faculty of Science, Shizuoka University, Oya 836, 422-8529 Shizuoka, Japan. Tel/Fax: +81 54 238 4929; Email: sckyosi@ipc.shizuoka.ac.jp
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 200 219 200 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros formosae</italic>
###xml 854 857 854 857 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cis</italic>
###xml 191 199 <span type="species:ncbi:4428">hornwort</span>
###xml 200 219 <span type="species:ncbi:48387">Anthoceros formosae</span>
RNA editing in chloroplasts alters the RNA sequence by converting C-to-U or U-to-C at a specific site. During the study of the complete nucleotide sequence of the chloroplast genome from the hornwort Anthoceros formosae, RNA editing events have been systematically investigated. A total of 509 C-to-U and 433 U-to-C conversions are identified in the transcripts of 68 genes and eight ORFs. No RNA editing is seen in any of the rRNA but one tRNA suffered a C-to-U conversion at an anticodon. All nonsense codons in 52 protein-coding genes and seven ORFs are removed in the transcripts by U-to-C conversions, and five initiation and three termination codons are created by C-to-U conversions. RNA editing in intron sequence suggests that editing can precede intercistronic processing. The sequence complementary to the edited site is proposed as a distant cis-recognition element.

###begin title 4
INTRODUCTION
###end title 4
###begin p 5

###xml 142 143 142 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c4">4</xref>

###xml 902 906 902 906 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 938 942 938 942 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atpB</italic>
###xml 976 978 976 978 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c12">12</xref>
###xml 979 981 979 981 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c13">13</xref>
###xml 527 538 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis</span>
###xml 622 627 <span type="species:ncbi:4577|species:ncbi:381124">maize</span>
###xml 645 652 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
###xml 673 683 <span type="species:ncbi:58042">black pine</span>
###xml 844 852 <span type="species:ncbi:4428">hornwort</span>
###xml 853 872 <span type="species:ncbi:48387">Anthoceros formosae</span>
###xml 1044 1052 <span type="species:ncbi:4428">hornwort</span>
RNA editing to modify genetic information at the transcript level has been identified in both mitochondria and chloroplasts of land plants (1-4). All the editing involves conversions from a cytidine (C) to uridine (U) residue and in rare cases from U to C by base modification (5). In higher plants, essentially all protein-encoding mRNA is subject to RNA editing in mitochondria but the phenomenon is significantly less frequent in chloroplasts, where it affects only some transcripts (6). Indeed, systematic investigation of Arabidopsis mitochondria has revealed 456 RNA editing sites (7). In contrast, only 25 sites in maize (8), 31 sites in tobacco (9) and 26 sites in black pine (10) have been identified in chloroplasts. In the early land plants, however, numerous editing sites have been observed in chloroplast transcripts (11). In the hornwort Anthoceros formosae, 20 RNA editing sites in the rbcL transcript and 29 sites in the atpB transcript have been identified (12,13). We report here 509 C-to-U and 433 U-to-C conversions in the hornwort chloroplast.
###end p 5

###xml 432 434 432 434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c22">22</xref>
###xml 449 454 449 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trans</italic>
###xml 534 536 534 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c23">23</xref>
###xml 566 568 566 568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c15">15</xref>
###xml 584 586 584 586 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c16">16</xref>
###xml 587 589 587 589 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c22">22</xref>
###xml 697 699 697 699 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c24">24</xref>
###xml 794 798 794 798 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atpB</italic>
###xml 803 807 803 807 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 809 811 809 811 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c13">13</xref>
###xml 911 921 911 921 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
The reaction for RNA editing requires at least three factors; trans-acting factors involved in editing site recognition (14-16), cis-acting elements interacting with the corresponding trans-acting factors (14,17-21) and an editing enzyme catalyzing base modifications (5). In higher plant chloroplast, the cis-acting elements have been identified in the region upstream of the editing site by in vivo (14,17-19,21) and in vitro (16,22) studies. The trans-acting factors and the editing enzyme, forming a hypothetical editing complex (23), are extraplastidic origin (15) and proteins (16,22). Moreover, a RNA segment complementary to edited sequence was shown to have no effect on the RNA editing (24). However, we have found RNA segment complementary to every edited site of the transcripts of atpB and rbcL (13). The complementary sequences, which are longer than 5 nt, are located in the same transcript from Anthoceros chloroplasts. They are not restricted in a specific transcript but are always found in the transcripts examined, except in a group II intron. The role of the complementary sequence is discussed.

###begin title 7
MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 7
###begin title 8
Preparation of RNA and cDNA
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 93 95 93 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c12">12</xref>
###xml 186 188 186 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c25">25</xref>
###xml 242 252 242 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A.formosae</italic>
###xml 242 252 <span type="species:ncbi:48387">A.formosae</span>
Total RNA was isolated and complementary DNA (cDNA) was synthesized as described previously (12). Total cellular nucleic acids were prepared using cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) (25) with a slight modification. Frozen thalli (15 g) of A.formosae were disrupted with quartz sand and nucleic acids were extracted in 15 ml of extraction buffer containing 100 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.0), 20 mM EDTA, 1.4 M NaCl, 1% CTAB and 1% 2-mercaptoethanol at 60degreesC for 30 min. Total RNA was precipitated by adding LiCl to a final concentration of 2 M after extraction of chloroform-isoamyl alcohol (24/1, v/v). Contaminating DNA was removed from the RNA sample by digestion with RNase-free DNase (Boehringer Mannheim). A cDNA bank was constructed using the total RNA as the template with a commercial kit (cDNA synthesis kit, Takara, Kyoto). Total RNA (0.5 microg) and 20 pmol random hexamer primer were annealed at 70degreesC for 2 min and cooled to 0degreesC. They were then mixed with 10 pmol of each nucleotide triphosphate (dNTP), 20 U of recombinant RNase inhibitor and 200 U of MLV reverse transcriptase (Wako, Osaka) in a total volume of 20 microl. The reaction proceeded at 42degreesC for 60 min and at 94degreesC for 5 min, then 80 microl of water was added.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Sequencing of cDNA
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 31 34 31 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Taq</italic>
###xml 155 157 155 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c12">12</xref>
###xml 501 504 497 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Taq</italic>
###xml 652 653 636 637 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T</italic>
###xml 653 654 637 638 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</sub>
###xml 1397 1413 1375 1391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 1477 1479 1451 1453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c26">26</xref>
###xml 1397 1413 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
The cDNA was amplified using a Taq polymerase (Ex Taq, Takara) and appropriate primers with a Mini Cycler (PTC-150, MJ Research), as described previously (12). The primers (accession no. AB086179) for the 5' and 3' of each transcript were designed from the genomic sequence 20-50 nt upstream and downstream of the coding region, respectively, so as to be able to sequence the entire coding region. A typical reaction (50 microl) contained 25 pmol of each primer pair, 10 mM of each dNTP and 1.25 U of Taq polymerase. One thermo cycle was 96degreesC for 1 min, annealing for 2 min and 72degreesC for 2 min. The annealing temperature was adjusted to the Tm value of the primer pair. The reaction proceeded through 30 cycles, followed by 72degreesC for 5 min. Amplified cDNA was directly analyzed using a BigDye Terminator Cycle Sequencing Ready Reaction (ver. 3.0, Applied Biosystems) with a 310 Genetic Analyzer (Perkin Elmer). Additional primers within the cDNA were also designed from the genomic sequence and used to analyze the sequence of long cDNA. Amplified cDNA, which was not homogeneous, was separated by agarose gel electrophoresis and the homogeneous cDNA was extracted from the gel using a QIAquick Gel Extraction Kit (Qiagen, Tokyo). It was used for direct sequencing, or ligated into Bluescript vector (Stratagene) using a Blunting Kination Ligation Kit (Takara) and introduced into Escherichia coli DH5alpha. The plasmid was prepared by standard alkaline lysis (26) and used for sequencing.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Analysis of RNA editing
###end title 12
###begin p 13
RNA editing was analyzed by comparing each sequence of cDNA with that of genomic DNA. When the sequences did not fit, the genomic DNA and cDNA were re-examined after the re-amplification of genomic DNA and the cDNA library, respectively, using the same primer pair.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
RESULTS
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 75 85 75 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A.formosae</italic>
###xml 87 89 87 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c27">27</xref>
###xml 558 559 558 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg327f1">1</xref>
###xml 817 828 817 828 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 885 886 885 886 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c7">7</xref>
###xml 75 85 <span type="species:ncbi:48387">A.formosae</span>
###xml 817 828 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis</span>
cDNA for every transcript expected from the complete chloroplast genome of A.formosae (27) has been synthesized and its nucleotides sequenced (accession nos AB087416- AB087494 and AB097086), except for some tRNAs. A comparison of the sequences of genomic DNA and cDNA has revealed RNA editing at 509 sites in the form of C-to-U conversions and at 433 sites in the form of U-to-C conversions. The efficiency of RNA editing at individual sites is virtually complete. The overall conversion of nucleotides examined is 1.2%. The results are summarized in Figure 1. RNA editing has been found in the transcripts of 68 protein-coding genes and eight ORFs, and one tRNA. No editing has been found in any rRNA or the rest of the tRNA examined. The editing frequency of 942 sites in a genome of 161 kb is even higher than the Arabidopsis mitochondrial editing frequency of 456 sites in 367 kb (7).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 28 38 28 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
###xml 259 261 259 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c28">28</xref>
###xml 262 264 262 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c29">29</xref>
###xml 310 314 310 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 319 323 319 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atpB</italic>
###xml 325 327 325 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c12">12</xref>
###xml 328 330 328 330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c13">13</xref>
The most notable feature of Anthoceros RNA editing is the numerous U-to-C conversions. The number of U-to-C conversions is comparable with that of C-to-U conversions, though this type of RNA editing had been described as rare and called reverse type editing (28,29). It is not restricted to the transcripts of rbcL and atpB (12,13), but widely observed in the transcripts of many genes. Indeed, nonsense codons in 52 protein-coding genes and seven ORFs become sense codons through U-to-C conversions.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 208 209 208 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c7">7</xref>
###xml 237 247 237 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
###xml 280 281 280 281 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg327tb1">1</xref>
###xml 780 784 780 784 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
The RNA editing replaced 868 out of 24275 amino acids corresponding to a 3.6% change in sequence identity. The general tendency is for RNA editing to increase the proportion of hydrophobic amino acid codons (7). This is also the case in Anthoceros chloroplasts. As shown in Table 1, the three most frequent amino acid transitions are Ser to Leu at 136 sites, Ser to Phe at 104 sites and Pro to Leu at 87 sites, which are modifications of hydrophilic to hydrophobic residues. The reverse modifications reducing hydrophobicity are rather rare and include transitions of Leu to Pro at 60 sites, Cys to Arg at 40 sites and Leu to Ser at 26 sites. The RNA editing is required to form a functional protein structure, because the amino acid sequence deduced from the genomic sequence of rbcL will not allow the known three-dimensional structure of the Rubisco large subunit (K.Yura, K.Kobayashi and M.Go, manuscript in preparation).
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 359 360 359 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg327tb2">2</xref>
###xml 739 750 739 750 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 765 766 765 766 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c7">7</xref>
###xml 911 921 911 921 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
###xml 1113 1123 1113 1123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
###xml 739 750 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis</span>
One of the most notable modifications is the conversion from nonsense to sense codons; 110 nonsense codons are converted into Gln and 54 into Arg. They are found in 52 protein-coding genes and seven ORFs, showing that RNA editing is essential to the translation of functional proteins. Numbers of editing sites at different codon positions are shown in Table 2. The most frequent editing of C-to-U is at the second position and then at the first, and that of U-to-C conversion is at the first position and then at the second. In total, 58.6% of RNA editing is found at the second position, 38.4% at the first position and only 3.0% at the third position. Editing sites are restricted at the first and second codon positions in the case of Arabidopsis mitochondria (7). The biased distribution of editing sites within the codons is not explained by the general distribution of pyrimidines within the mRNA of the Anthoceros chloroplast genome, because 41.4, 53.0 and 52.5% of pyrimidines are estimated to occur at the first, second and third position of the codon, respectively, from the overall codon usage of the Anthoceros chloroplast genome.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
Silent conversion
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 444 445 444 445 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 463 464 463 464 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 499 500 499 500 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 507 508 507 508 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 556 557 556 557 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 592 593 592 593 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 693 695 693 695 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CC</underline>
###xml 702 704 702 704 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CC</underline>
###xml 715 717 715 717 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CC</underline>
###xml 750 752 750 752 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UU</underline>
###xml 755 757 755 757 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UU</underline>
###xml 764 766 764 766 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UU</underline>
###xml 975 985 975 985 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
###xml 1047 1049 1047 1049 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c30">30</xref>
###xml 1118 1122 1118 1122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ndhA</italic>
###xml 1437 1439 1437 1439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c31">31</xref>
More than 95% of RNA editing alters amino acid identity or converts a nonsense codon into a sense codon. However, several silent conversions, which do not alter the amino acid, are also observed in the transcripts of seventeen genes and one ORF. These occur mainly at the third position of the codon as observed at 28 sites. However, two peculiar silent conversions have been observed. One is a conversion at the first codon position where one CUG codon and four CUA codons for Leu are changed into UUG and UUA, respectively, by C-to-U conversion, and two UUA codons for Leu are changed into CUA by U-to-C conversion. The other involves two sites of RNA editing within one codon whereby three CCG, one CCA and two CCC codons for Pro are changed into UUG, UUA and CUU for Leu, respectively, by two C-to-U conversions, in which only the change at the second position affects the amino acid identity. RNA editing outside of coding regions has been detected at nine sites in the Anthoceros, though only one case is known in higher plant chloroplasts (30). One of them locates in a putative Shine-Dalgarno (SD) sequence of ndhA, where the base conversion by RNA editing can increase a base pairing with the 3' end of the 16S rRNA and form the conserved SD sequence of the gene, suggesting the requirement of RNA editing for effective translation. RNA editing in the SD sequence is not known in chloroplasts but is seen in plant mitochondria (31).
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 77 81 77 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbD</italic>
###xml 104 108 104 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbC</italic>
###xml 137 138 137 138 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 168 169 168 169 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 197 198 197 198 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 228 229 228 229 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 250 254 250 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbD</italic>
###xml 277 278 277 278 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 297 298 297 298 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 332 336 332 336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbC</italic>
Another unique RNA editing is found in an overlapped gene. The 3' portion of psbD and the 5' portion of psbC overlap each other, where CCUGU sequence is changed into CCCGU by a U-to-C editing. The UGU coding Cys is altered into CGU coding Arg in the psbD transcript, but the CCU coding Pro into CCC does not alter amino acid in the psbC transcript.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
RNA editing in immature transcripts
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 131 135 131 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">petG</italic>
###xml 136 140 136 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">petL</italic>
###xml 226 230 226 230 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">petG</italic>
###xml 321 325 321 325 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbJ</italic>
###xml 326 330 326 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbL</italic>
###xml 331 335 331 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbF</italic>
###xml 336 340 336 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbE</italic>
###xml 347 351 347 351 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbH</italic>
###xml 352 356 352 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbN</italic>
###xml 357 361 357 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbT</italic>
###xml 369 373 369 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ndhA</italic>
###xml 374 378 374 378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ndhI</italic>
###xml 385 390 385 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps18</italic>
###xml 391 396 391 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl33</italic>
###xml 404 408 404 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbD</italic>
###xml 409 413 409 413 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbC</italic>
###xml 421 426 421 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl36</italic>
###xml 427 431 427 431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">infA</italic>
###xml 432 436 432 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps8</italic>
###xml 443 448 443 448 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl22</italic>
###xml 449 454 449 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps19</italic>
###xml 465 470 465 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl21</italic>
###xml 471 476 471 476 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl32</italic>
###xml 483 484 483 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg327f1">1</xref>
###xml 557 566 557 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbH-psbT</italic>
###xml 571 576 571 576 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl36</italic>
###xml 577 581 577 581 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps8</italic>
###xml 693 695 693 695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c32">32</xref>
Nucleotide sequences of the cDNAs to several co-transcripts have been analyzed to determine editing sites. In the co-transcript of petG-petL, seven editing sites have been observed including three of U-to-C conversions in the petG region. Numerous editing sites have also been observed in the other co-transcripts; 11 in psbJ-psbL-psbF-psbE, 5 in psbH-psbN-psbT, 25 in ndhA-ndhI, 3 in rps18-rpl33, 19 in psbD-psbC, 10 in rpl36-infA-rps8, 2 in rpl22-rps19 and 13 in rpl21-rpl32 (Fig. 1). Some of them will be the mature poly-cistronic mRNA, however at least psbH-psbT and rpl36-rps8 will be cleaved into the mono-cistronic mRNAs because they contain putative terminator or stabilizer sequence (32) in front of the following gene. These suggest that the RNA editing can precede intercistronic processing.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 82 86 82 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ndhA</italic>
###xml 256 261 256 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trans</italic>
###xml 293 295 293 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c33">33</xref>
###xml 320 321 320 321 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 328 332 328 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ndhA</italic>
###xml 338 348 338 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
###xml 367 368 367 368 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 760 762 760 762 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c34">34</xref>
###xml 763 765 763 765 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c35">35</xref>
RNA editing has also been detected in an intron-containing immature transcript of ndhA, namely at the 3' end of the 5' exon corresponding to the intron-binding site (IBS). The IBS serves in tertiary interaction with the exon-binding site (EBS) and then in trans-esterification to the 5' exon (33). The IBS sequence 5'-CUUUAC of ndhA from Anthoceros is edited to 5'-CUCUAC by U-to-C conversion, which is complementary to the EBS sequence. This edited IBS sequence is well conserved within several land plants, suggesting that the RNA editing is required for the tertiary interaction between IBS and EBS, and allows precise splicing. These findings of RNA editing in immature transcripts support the idea that the editing reaction takes place prior to splicing (34,35).
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 59 63 59 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trnK</italic>
###xml 331 333 331 333 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c36">36</xref>
###xml 334 336 334 336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c39">39</xref>
###xml 397 399 397 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c40">40</xref>
No editing has been detected in the immature transcript of trnK-UUU, however cDNA synthesized within the tRNA has revealed a C-to-U conversion at the 3' position of the anticodon. This is the first finding of RNA editing in the anticodon of a plant tRNA, though RNA editing in tRNA has been detected in several plant mitochondria (36-39) and in an anticodon of the mitochondrial tRNA of protists (40).
###end p 25
###begin title 26
RNA editing in introns
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 76 80 76 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atpF</italic>
###xml 95 105 95 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
###xml 125 126 125 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg327f2">2</xref>
###xml 322 324 316 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c33">33</xref>
###xml 325 327 319 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c41">41</xref>
###xml 340 341 334 335 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 445 447 427 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c42">42</xref>
###xml 474 475 456 457 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 654 655 636 637 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 672 673 654 655 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 770 772 752 754 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c43">43</xref>
###xml 917 919 896 898 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c42">42</xref>
Three editing sites have been found in the intron sequence of the unspliced atpF transcript in Anthoceros chloroplasts (Fig. 2). The first at position 149 relative to the translation start site is located 4 nt downstream of the 5' end of the intron, which corresponds to the epsilon site of the conserved group II intron (33,41). The 2 nt GC, which is known to involve epsilon-epsilon' interaction and to be required for self-splicing activity (42), have been created from GU by a U-to-C conversion. The second at position 419 is found in the predicted helix of domain I of the group II intron, which could strengthen the helix by the conversion of an A-C mispair to an A-U pair. The same type of RNA editing in group II introns has been reported in plant mitochondria (43). The third is a C-to-U conversion at 612 corresponding to the zeta locus of the group II intron, which is called U381 by Boudvillain and Pyle (42), and has an important role in the catalysis of self-splicing through its tertiary contact with domain 5. These findings of RNA editing in the intron also support that the process is a prerequisite for splicing.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
Guide-type RNA
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 32 34 32 34 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c12">12</xref>
###xml 35 37 35 37 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c13">13</xref>
###xml 40 44 40 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atpB</italic>
###xml 49 53 49 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 274 278 274 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atpB</italic>
###xml 283 287 283 287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 501 502 501 502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg327tb3">3</xref>
###xml 741 745 741 745 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ndhB</italic>
###xml 817 819 817 819 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;7</sup>
###xml 906 916 906 916 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
###xml 1083 1087 1083 1087 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trnK</italic>
###xml 1132 1133 1132 1133 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 1232 1233 1232 1233 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 1288 1289 1288 1289 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 1314 1315 1314 1315 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 1693 1696 1693 1696 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cis</italic>
As shown in our previous paper (12,13), atpB and rbcL transcripts contain a sequence complementary to every edited site. The sequence is longer than 5 nt and located in the same transcript. The existence of the complementary sequence is not restricted in the transcripts of atpB and rbcL, but they are always detected in the editing site examined, except in a group II intron. Most complementary sequences locate in the coding region of the transcript but some locate in the introns as shown in Table 3. The short complementary sequences such as pentamer or hexamer can easily be found, however the longer one is the more difficult. For example, the probability to find the dodecamer 5'-CAUUGGAUCAUA, complementary to edited site 473 in the ndhB transcript, from an AU biased sequence containing 67% A + U is 1.2 x 10-7, where the AU content of 67% is conventionally assumed from the overall AT content of Anthoceros chloroplast genome. Indeed the identical sequence is not found in the rest of the chloroplast genome. Another unique complementary sequence is found in the intron of trnK-UUU, where a complementary sequence 3'-CGAAAAUU-5' in the intron could form double-stranded structure with the 3' end of the first exon 5'-GCUUUCAA containing anticodon of the pre-tRNA, and mispair A-C can be corrected into A-U by C-to-U editing. This might be an IBS-EBS-like interaction for splicing, though the splicing mechanism of chloroplast pre-tRNA is not known. Thus, RNA sequence containing the nucleotide to be edited might form a double stranded structure with the complementary strand during a temporal secondary or tertiary interaction, and the complementary sequence might act as a distant cis-recognition element determining the editing site, though we have had no experimental evidence of this in chloroplasts until now.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
DISCUSSION
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 51 52 51 52 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U</underline>
###xml 87 88 87 88 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 369 371 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c21">21</xref>
Although we have predicted that non-Watson-Crick G-U base pairs will be converted to G-C by U-to-C editing, we do not know why the G-U pairs that naturally occur in the tRNA and rRNA secondary structure are not edited. However, it could be that the recognition of editing site requires some additional factors such as the upstream element reported by Hermann and Bock (21).
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 151 153 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c13">13</xref>
###xml 361 371 361 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
###xml 379 383 379 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atpB</italic>
###xml 388 392 388 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 396 406 396 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Marchantia</italic>
###xml 411 418 411 418 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nitella</italic>
###xml 629 655 629 655 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Selaginella moellendorffii</italic>
###xml 664 669 664 669 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 692 696 692 696 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ndhB</italic>
###xml 747 755 747 755 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 629 655 <span type="species:ncbi:88036">Selaginella moellendorffii</span>
###xml 713 720 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
It is theoretically difficult to find such a long guide-type RNA sequence from a limited length of random sequence as described in our previous paper (13). However, it is not certain whether the complementary sequence does concern as a site-determining factor in RNA editing or not. Therefore, we tried to detect the complementary sequences similar to those of Anthoceros in the atpB and rbcL of Marchantia and Nitella sp. in which RNA editing has not been found, but failed to detect any homologous double-stranded structures. However, possible guide-type RNA sequences have been found in the chloroplasts of lycopsids, such as Selaginella moellendorffii (K.Ueda et al. unpublished), and in ndhB transcript from tobacco (data not shown). Studies in vitro might elucidate the meaning of the complementary sequence and the mechanism of RNA editing in chloroplasts.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 135 155 135 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Selaginella uncinata</italic>
###xml 235 239 235 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atpB</italic>
###xml 333 337 333 337 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 370 389 370 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lycopodium clavatum</italic>
###xml 394 410 394 410 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Isoetes asiatica</italic>
###xml 446 455 446 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pteridium</italic>
###xml 727 731 727 731 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cox1</italic>
###xml 749 759 749 759 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A.formosae</italic>
###xml 1000 1010 1000 1010 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anthoceros</italic>
###xml 1020 1022 1020 1022 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg327c44">44</xref>
###xml 135 155 <span type="species:ncbi:307165">Selaginella uncinata</span>
###xml 370 389 <span type="species:ncbi:3252">Lycopodium clavatum</span>
###xml 394 410 <span type="species:ncbi:140027">Isoetes asiatica</span>
###xml 749 759 <span type="species:ncbi:48387">A.formosae</span>
A characteristic of RNA editing in early land plants is the presence of U-to-C conversions and numerous editing sites. In the lycopsid Selaginella uncinata, 112 editing sites corresponding to 7.7% of nucleotides have been found in the atpB transcript, which includes four U-to-C conversions. U-to-C conversions are also found in the rbcL transcript from chloroplasts of Lycopodium clavatum and Isoetes asiatica (K.Ueda, unpublished). In the fern Pteridium, 94 editing sites have been detected in the transcripts of 18 genes including two sites of U-to-C conversion (K.Yamada and T.Wakasugi, unpublished). These characters of RNA editing are not restricted to chloroplasts but found in mitochondria of early land plants. In the cox1 transcripts from A.formosae, 13 editing sites including four U-to-C conversions have been found (K.Kadowaki, unpublished). Numerous editing sites of U-to-C conversion together with the removal of non-sense codons are reported in the mitochondrial transcripts of other Anthoceros species (44), possibly suggesting that the editing processes in the two plant cell organelles have common roots.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 559 562 559 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cis</italic>
###xml 34 42 <span type="species:ncbi:4428">hornwort</span>
In conclusion, RNA editing in the hornwort chloroplast shows that almost all editing serves to reconstruct functional RNA. (i) Nonsense codons in almost half the genes are repaired as sense codons by RNA editing, (ii) >95% of codons containing editing sites are converted to conserved forms to translate functional proteins, (iii) the anticodon of tRNA-Lys is corrected and made functional, (iv) a SD sequence is created for efficient translation, (v) an intron sequence is corrected for precise splicing, and (vi) possible guide-type RNA sequence, a distant cis-recognition element, is detected for every editing site except in a group II intron.
###end p 34
