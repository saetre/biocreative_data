###begin article-title 0
DJ-1 Is a Redox-Dependent Molecular Chaperone That Inhibits alpha-Synuclein Aggregate Formation
###end article-title 0
###begin article-title 1
Sensitivity to Oxidative Stress in DJ-1-Deficient Dopamine Neurons: An ES-Derived Cell Model of Primary Parkinsonism
###end article-title 1
###begin article-title 2
A New Cell Model for Parkinson's Disease
###end article-title 2
###begin article-title 3
Molecules That Cause or Prevent Parkinson's Disease
###end article-title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 370 374 370 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">DJ-1</italic>
###xml 454 474 454 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Bonifati1">Bonifati et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 501 521 501 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Martinat1">Martinat et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 164 172 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Parkinson's disease (PD) pathology is characterized by the degeneration of midbrain dopamine neurons (DNs) ultimately leading to a progressive movement disorder in patients. The etiology of DN loss in sporadic PD is unknown, although it is hypothesized that aberrant protein aggregation and cellular oxidative stress may promote DN degeneration. Homozygous mutations in DJ-1 were recently described in two families with autosomal recessive inherited PD (Bonifati et al. 2003). In a companion article (Martinat et al. 2004), we show that mutations in DJ-1 alter the cellular response to oxidative stress and proteasomal inhibition. Here we show that DJ-1 functions as a redox-sensitive molecular chaperone that is activated in an oxidative cytoplasmic environment. We further demonstrate that DJ-1 chaperone activity in vivo extends to alpha-synuclein, a protein implicated in PD pathogenesis.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
The interaction of the proteins DJ-1 and alpha- synuclein described here may be important for understanding the molecular basis of Parkinson's disease
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Introduction
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 242 268 242 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Dauer1">Dauer and Przedborski 2003</xref>
###xml 562 588 554 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Polymeropoulos1">Polymeropoulos et al. 1997</xref>
###xml 679 705 671 697 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Goldberg1">Goldberg and Lansbury 2000</xref>
###xml 736 755 728 747 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Rideout1">Rideout et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 828 851 816 839 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Spillantini2">Spillantini et al. 1998</xref>
###xml 889 913 877 901 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Trojanowski1">Trojanowski and Lee 1998</xref>
###xml 944 951 932 939 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Parkin,</italic>
###xml 1002 1021 990 1009 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Hattori1">Hattori et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 1143 1164 1131 1152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Staropoli1">Staropoli et al. 2003</xref>
Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive movement disorder that is characterized pathologically by the relatively selective degeneration of midbrain DNs and the presence of prominent intracytoplasmic neuronal inclusions, termed Lewy bodies (Dauer and Przedborski 2003). The identification of several genes that underlie familial forms of primary parkinsonism has allowed for the molecular dissection of mechanisms of dopamine neuron (DN) survival. Autosomal dominant mutations in alpha-synuclein (alphaSyn) lead to a rare familial form of primary Parkinsonism (Polymeropoulos et al. 1997), and there is evidence that these mutations generate toxic, abnormal protein aggregates (Goldberg and Lansbury 2000) and proteasomal dysfunction (Rideout et al. 2001). Of note, Lewy body inclusions are particularly enriched for alphaSyn (Spillantini et al. 1998) and neurofilament protein subunits (Trojanowski and Lee 1998). Mutations in a second gene, Parkin, lead to autosomal recessive primary Parkinsonism (Hattori et al. 2000). Parkin is a ubiquitin ligase that appears to participate in the proteasome-mediated degradation of several substrates (Staropoli et al. 2003).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 38 43 38 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">DJ-1,</italic>
###xml 116 136 116 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Bonifati1">Bonifati et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 139 143 139 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">DJ-1</italic>
###xml 241 261 241 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Nagakubo1">Nagakubo et al. 1997</xref>
###xml 457 484 457 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Mitsumoto1">Mitsumoto and Nakagawa 2001</xref>
###xml 486 507 486 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Mitsumoto2">Mitsumoto et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 619 640 619 640 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-McCormack1">McCormack et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 739 759 739 759 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-de1">de Nobel et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 329 334 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 664 669 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Homozygous mutations in a third gene, DJ-1, were recently associated with autosomal recessive primary Parkinsonism (Bonifati et al. 2003). DJ-1 encodes a ThiJ domain protein of 189 amino acids that is broadly expressed in mammalian tissues (Nagakubo et al. 1997). Interestingly, DJ-1 was independently identified in a screen for human endothelial cell proteins that are modified with respect to isoelectric point in response to sublethal doses of paraquat (Mitsumoto and Nakagawa 2001; Mitsumoto et al. 2001), a toxin which generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) within cells and has been associated with DN toxicity (McCormack et al. 2002). Gene expression of a yeast homolog of DJ-1, YDR533C, is upregulated in response to sorbic acid (de Nobel et al. 2001), an inducer of cellular oxidative stress. These data suggest a causal role for DJ-1 in the cellular oxidative stress response.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 135 152 135 152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Halio1">Halio et al. 1996</xref>
###xml 154 168 154 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Du1">Du et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 487 505 487 505 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Wilson1">Wilson et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 712 728 712 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Huai1">Huai et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 730 745 730 745 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee3">Lee et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 747 765 747 765 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Wilson1">Wilson et al. 2003</xref>
ThiJ domain proteins are highly conserved and have been associated with several functions including protease and chaperone activities (Halio et al. 1996; Du et al. 2000). The crystal structure of DJ-1 demonstrates the presence of a highly conserved nucleophile elbow-like domain at cysteine 106, but the relative position of this residue differs from that of a structurally related ThiJ protease, PH1704, and does not appear to be permissible for proton transfer and protease catalysis (Wilson et al. 2003). Furthermore, DJ-1 forms an asymmetric homodimer with a prominent carboxy-terminal helical region present at the dimerization interface, which appears to limit access to the nucleophile elbow-like domain (Huai et al. 2003; Lee et al. 2003; Wilson et al. 2003).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 73 89 73 89 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">Escherichia coli</named-content>
###xml 432 453 432 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Chelikani1">Chelikani et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 456 475 456 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Taira1">Taira et al. (2004)</xref>
###xml 698 718 698 718 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Martinat1">Martinat et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1002 1022 1002 1022 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Martinat1">Martinat et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 73 89 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
DJ-1 displays significant homology to the carboxy-terminal domain of the Escherichia coli HPII catalase, as both proteins are divergent members of the type I glutamine amidotransferase family. Interestingly, the carboxy-terminal DJ-1 homology domain of HPII catalase lacks catalase activity, but rather appears to function as a chaperone in the correct folding of the catalytic core of the protein, and in thermal enzyme stability (Chelikani et al. 2003). Taira et al. (2004) recently reported that purified DJ-1 harbors catalase activity, and that overexpression of DJ-1 by transfection of neuroblastoma tumor cells inhibits the accumulation of ROS. In contrast, analysis of DJ-1-deficient cells (Martinat et al. 2004) revealed that such cells display an apparently normal initial accumulation of ROS, indicating that DJ-1 likely functions in a protective role downstream of ROS insult. Consistent with this, DJ-1-deficient cells are predisposed to apoptotic death in the context of oxidative stress (Martinat et al. 2004).
###end p 10
###begin p 11
Here we demonstrate that DJ-1 functions as a redox-regulated molecular chaperone that is activated in an oxidizing environment. DJ-1 chaperone activity extends in vivo to alphaSyn, a protein that has been implicated in PD pathogenesis. DJ-1 activity is abrogated by the L166P mutation, associated with primary Parkinsonism, as a consequence of defective dimerization and reduced stability.
###end p 11

Results
###end title 12
###begin title 13
DJ-1 Lacks Apparent Protease and Antioxidant Activities In Vitro
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 53 70 53 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Halio1">Halio et al. 1996</xref>
###xml 72 86 72 86 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Du1">Du et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 88 103 88 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee3">Lee et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 138 162 138 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Horvath1">Horvath and Grishin 2001</xref>
###xml 277 296 277 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Honbou1">Honbou et al. 2003a</xref>
###xml 298 314 298 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Huai1">Huai et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 316 331 316 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee3">Lee et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 333 350 333 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Tao1">Tao and Tong 2003</xref>
###xml 352 370 352 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Wilson1">Wilson et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 561 569 561 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="st001">Table S1</xref>
###xml 595 604 595 604 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="sg001">Figure S1</xref>
###xml 747 767 747 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Martinat1">Martinat et al. 2004</xref>
DJ-1 homologs have been reported to harbor protease (Halio et al. 1996; Du et al. 2000; Lee et al. 2003) and amidotransferase activities (Horvath and Grishin 2001). However, crystal structure analyses of DJ-1 suggest that this protein may not retain such catalytic activities (Honbou et al. 2003a; Huai et al. 2003; Lee et al. 2003; Tao and Tong 2003; Wilson et al. 2003). Consistent with this, purified DJ-1 preparations failed to display in vitro protease activity toward a variety of synthetic or natural substrates, and, similarly, DJ-1 lacked antioxidant (Table S1) or catalase activities (Figure S1) in vitro. Furthermore, cells deficient in DJ-1 appear unaltered in the initial accumulation of ROS in the context of acute oxidative stress (Martinat et al. 2004).
###end p 14
###begin title 15
DJ-1 Is a Redox-Dependent Molecular Chaperone
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 236 243 236 243 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">E. coli</named-content>
###xml 263 264 263 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">,</italic>
###xml 327 345 327 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Sastry1">Sastry et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 347 364 347 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Malki1">Malki et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 931 939 917 925 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 1453 1461 1439 1447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 236 243 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1212 1217 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Every organism responds to ROS and other toxic environmental stresses by overexpressing a highly conserved set of heat shock proteins (Hsps), many of which function as molecular chaperones to assist other proteins in folding. Hsp31, an E. coli ThiJ domain protein, has been shown to function as a molecular chaperone in vitro (Sastry et al. 2002; Malki et al. 2003). We hypothesized that DJ-1 may similarly function as a protein chaperone to protect cells from ROS. DJ-1 chaperone activity was quantified in the suppression of heat-induced aggregation of citrate synthase (CS) and glutathione S-transferase (GST), two well-characterized protein chaperone assays. These proteins lose their native conformation and undergo aggregation during incubation at 43 degreesC and 60 degreesC, respectively. Addition of 0.5-4.0 muM polyhistidine (His)-tagged DJ-1 was found to effectively suppress the heat-induced aggregation of 0.8 muM CS (Figure 1A). The chaperone activity was independent of the His tag used for purification, as cleavage and removal of the His tag did not alter DJ-1 chaperone function (unpublished data). DJ-1 chaperone activity is comparable to that of a well-described small cytoplasmic chaperone, human Hsp27. In contrast, RNase A failed to demonstrate chaperone activity and served as a negative control. Interestingly, the Parkinsonism-associated L166P DJ-1 mutation abrogated chaperone activity relative to the wild-type (WT) protein (Figure 1B).
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 110 119 110 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="sg001">Figure S1</xref>
###xml 239 247 239 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 807 824 807 824 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Jakob1">Jakob et al. 1999</xref>
DJ-1 similarly functioned as a molecular chaperone in the context of the heat-induced aggregation of GST (see Figure S1). In contrast, DJ-1 failed to display activity in a third chaperone assay, aggregation suppression of reduced insulin (Figure 1C). Reduction of the disulfide bonds between the A and B chains of insulin with dithiothreitol (DTT) leads to aggregation of the B chains. Hsp27 effectively inhibited the aggregation of insulin in the presence of 20 mM DTT, whereas neither DJ-1 nor the negative control protein RNase A displayed chaperone activity in this assay. As the insulin aggregation assay is performed in a reduced environment, we hypothesized that DJ-1 chaperone activity may be redox regulated. Interestingly, such a redox switch in a molecular chaperone has been described in Hsp33 (Jakob et al. 1999), a dimeric bacterial Hsp unrelated to DJ-1.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 316 324 310 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 648 649 642 643 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 650 651 644 645 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 851 852 839 840 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 853 854 841 842 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 923 931 911 919 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 980 981 968 969 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 982 983 970 971 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1007 1008 995 996 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1009 1010 997 998 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
To test the redox regulation of DJ-1, we assayed chaperone activity in the CS aggregation assay in the presence or absence of the reducing agent DTT. DJ-1 chaperone activity in the CS aggregation assay was completely abrogated by preincubation of DJ-1 with 0.5 mM DTT in aggregation buffer for 10 min at 4 degreesC (Figure 1D). DTT did not significantly alter CS aggregation in the absence of DJ-1 and did not modify suppression of CS aggregation by Hsp27 (unpublished data). To further test whether redox regulation might govern DJ-1 chaperone activity, reactivation studies using reduced DJ-1 were performed. DTT-reduced DJ-1 was incubated with H2O2 (10 mM in aggregation buffer for 10 min at 4 degreesC followed by dialysis against aggregation buffer for 2 h), and subsequently chaperone activity was measured in the CS thermal aggregation assay. H2O2 effectively reactivated the chaperone activity of DTT-treated DJ-1 (Figure 1D). This was not an indirect effect of residual H2O2 on CS aggregation, as H2O2 treatment of CS increased aggregation (unpublished data). These results suggest that redox regulation of DJ-1 is reversible and is regulated by the redox environment.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 75 93 75 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Sastry1">Sastry et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 342 351 336 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="sg001">Figure S1</xref>
Molecular chaperones typically display marked stability to thermal stress (Sastry et al. 2002). Consistent with this, the ultraviolet-circular dichroism (CD) spectrum of WT DJ-1 is consistent with a well-folded protein, and thermal denaturation of WT DJ-1 revealed a cooperative thermal unfolding transition at approximately 75 degreesC (see Figure S1). In contrast, the CD spectrum of the DJ-1 L166P mutant protein is typical of a partially unfolded polypeptide, suggesting that the L166P mutation causes a significant loss of helical structure. The mutant protein does not exhibit a thermal unfolding transition in the range studied (0-90 degreesC).
###end p 19
###begin title 20
DJ-1 Inhibits the Generation of alphaSyn Aggregates
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 93 101 89 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 315 334 303 322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Conway1">Conway et al. 2000a</xref>
###xml 456 479 440 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Spillantini1">Spillantini et al. 1997</xref>
###xml 568 587 548 567 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Weinreb1">Weinreb et al. 1996</xref>
###xml 751 759 721 729 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 765 766 735 736 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g002">2</xref>
###xml 770 788 740 758 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Volles1">Volles et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 790 808 760 778 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Gosavi1">Gosavi et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 923 932 893 902 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="sg001">Figure S1</xref>
###xml 1101 1109 1063 1071 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 1115 1116 1077 1078 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g002">2</xref>
###xml 616 621 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We extended the analysis of DJ-1 chaperone function to a candidate DJ-1 substrate, alphaSyn (Figure 2). The aggregation of alphaSyn has been implicated in familial and sporadic forms of PD, as mutations associated with autosomal dominant familial primary Parkinsonism alter the propensity of alphaSyn to aggregate (Conway et al. 2000a), and as alphaSyn fibrils are a major constituent of the Lewy body intracytoplasmic inclusions that typify PD pathology (Spillantini et al. 1997). In vitro, monomeric alphaSyn is disordered or "natively unfolded" in dilute solution (Weinreb et al. 1996). Incubation of purified WT human alphaSyn for 2 h at 55 degreesC results in the generation of high molecular weight multimers that likely represent protofibrils (Figure 2A and 2B) (Volles et al. 2001; Gosavi et al. 2002). This treatment does not result in formation of mature amyloid fibrils, as determined by Congo red staining (see Figure S1). WT DJ-1 effectively inhibits the formation of soluble alphaSyn protofibrils at a molar ratio of 1:2 (DJ-1: alphaSyn). In contrast, L166P mutant DJ-1, GST, and Hsp27 (Figure 2A and 2B) failed to inhibit the generation of alphaSyn protofibrils.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 428 436 416 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 708 726 684 702 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Volles1">Volles et al. 2001</xref>
alphaSyn protofibrils have been shown to be an intermediate in the formation of mature amyloid fibrils. Because DJ-1 chaperone activity is effective at inhibiting the accumulation of alphaSyn protofibrils, we sought to investigate the role of this activity in the generation of Congo red-positive mature fibrils. Congruently, WT DJ-1 inhibited formation of Congo red-positive alphaSyn fibrils, while L166P DJ-1 and GST did not (Figure 2C). Thus, DJ-1 seems to inhibit formation of alphaSyn fibrils by preventing formation of alphaSyn high molecular weight oligomers, or protofibrils. Interestingly, PD-associated clinical mutations in alphaSyn appear to accelerate oligomerization and protofibril formation (Volles et al. 2001).
###end p 22
###begin title 23
DJ-1 Chaperone Activity In Vivo
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 233 234 225 226 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 246 262 238 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee1">Lee and Lee 2002</xref>
###xml 264 279 256 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee2">Lee et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 457 478 445 466 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Staropoli1">Staropoli et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 604 605 584 585 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 642 643 622 623 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 779 795 751 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee1">Lee and Lee 2002</xref>
###xml 938 946 906 914 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 952 953 920 921 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g003">3</xref>
###xml 1009 1017 977 985 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 1101 1110 1065 1074 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="sg002">Figure S2</xref>
###xml 429 435 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
We sought to investigate the chaperone activity of DJ-1 toward alphaSyn in vivo. alphaSyn has been shown to form aggregates that consist of both protofibrils and mature amyloid fibrils in the context of oxidative stress (such as FeCl2 treatment [Lee and Lee 2002; Lee et al. 2002]) in neuroblastoma cells. We evaluated the activity of DJ-1 overexpression on alphaSyn aggregation in this tissue culture model system. Briefly, CAD murine neuroblastoma cells (Staropoli et al. 2003) were transfected with Flag epitope-tagged alphaSyn (Flag-alphaSyn), differentiated via serum withdrawal, and exposed to FeCl2 (2 mM) for 18 h. Treatment with FeCl2 induced accumulation of alphaSyn in the Triton X-100-insoluble fraction, which has been shown to correlate with alphaSyn protofibrils (Lee and Lee 2002). Overexpression of WT DJ-1, but not L166P clinical mutant DJ-1, significantly inhibited the accumulation of Triton X-100-insoluble alphaSyn (Figure 3A and 3B). DJ-1 overexpression did not alter the accumulation (Figure 3A) or half-life of soluble alphaSyn, as determined by pulse-chase kinetic analysis (Figure S2). Thus, DJ-1 overexpression is sufficient to inhibit the formation of alphaSyn aggregates in vivo, consistent with the in vitro analysis.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 94 98 90 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">DJ-1</italic>
###xml 194 198 190 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">DJ-1</italic>
###xml 413 433 409 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Martinat1">Martinat et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 953 969 921 937 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee1">Lee and Lee 2002</xref>
###xml 990 991 958 959 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1121 1129 1085 1093 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 1263 1264 1223 1224 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1340 1349 1296 1305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="sg002">Figure S2</xref>
###xml 1481 1496 1433 1448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee2">Lee et al. 2002</xref>
To investigate whether DJ-1 is necessary to inhibit alphaSyn aggregation in vivo, we utilized DJ-1 "knockout" embryonic stem (ES) cells, which display increased sensitivity to oxidative stress. DJ-1 homozygous knockout or control heterozygous ES cells (heterozygous cells were used as controls because they were the source of the knockout subclones) were differentiated in vitro using the embryoid body protocol (Martinat et al. 2004) and transfected with Flag-alphaSyn or control vector. Upon differentiation, both endogenous alphaSyn and transfected Flag-alphaSyn are accumulated to a similar extent in the soluble fraction of knockout and control cell lysates, as determined by Western blotting with an antibody for alphaSyn. In contrast, DJ-1-deficient cells (but not control cells) additionally accumulate Triton X-100-insoluble alphaSyn (both endogenous alphaSyn and transfected Flag-alphaSyn), which likely corresponds to protofibril aggregates (Lee and Lee 2002). As predicted, FeCl2 treatment further promoted the accumulation of insoluble alphaSyn in DJ-1-deficient cells but not in control heterozygous cells (Figure 3C). Interestingly, transfection of Flag-alphaSyn into undifferentiated knockout or control ES cells in the presence or absence of FeCl2 treatment did not lead to the accumulation of insoluble Flag-alphaSyn (see Figure S2), consistent with a prior study suggesting a role for neuronal differentiation in the generation of insoluble alphaSyn aggregates (Lee et al. 2002).
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 131 132 127 128 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 491 492 479 480 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>

###xml 556 564 544 552 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 718 719 702 703 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 816 824 796 804 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 962 963 942 943 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 975 983 955 963 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 286 291 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 371 377 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
To investigate the mechanism of DJ-1 activity toward alphaSyn, we performed coimmunoprecipitation experiments on untreated and FeCl2-treated CAD cells transfected with DJ-1 and Flag-alphaSyn (or control vector) as above. Triton X-100-soluble cell lysates were immunoprecipitated with a mouse monoclonal antibody for the Flag epitope, and Western blots were probed with a rabbit polyclonal antibody for DJ-1. DJ-1 failed to interact with Flag-alphaSyn in the absence of pretreatment with FeCl2, but an association was evident in FeCl2-treated cell lysates (Figure 3D). Furthermore, overexpression of alphaSyn (but not vector control) leads to a reduction in the soluble pool of DJ-1, particularly in the context of FeCl2 treatment, indicating that DJ-1 additionally associates with an insoluble fraction of alphaSyn (Figure 3D, bottom panel). Consistent with this, we found that a significant fraction of DJ-1 protein localizes to the insoluble fraction upon FeCl2 treatment (Figure 3E) in cells that have been cotransfected with Flag-alphaSyn.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 158 166 150 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g004">Figure 4</xref>

To further evaluate alphaSyn aggregation, we performed immunohistochemical analyses of CAD cells transfected with alphaSyn along with DJ-1 or control vector (Figure 4). Overexpression of alphaSyn in neuroblastoma cells induces the formation of visible cytoplasmic aggregates (Lee and Lee 2002) (Figure 4J-4L). Additional overexpression of WT DJ-1 significantly decreased the number of cells containing alphaSyn aggregates (Figure 4D-4F and 4M), whereas the L166P DJ-1 mutant fails to do so (Figure 4G-4I and 4M). ###xml 318 340 318 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Perez-Olle1">Perez-Olle et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 454 462 454 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 703 711 703 711 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 713 714 713 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g005">5</xref>
###xml 770 779 770 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="sg003">Figure S3</xref>
###xml 181 186 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 232 238 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
In a separate set of experiments, we assayed the ability of DJ-1 to inhibit aggregation of a second substrate, neurofilament light subunit (NFL). Overexpression of a mutant form of human NFL, Q333P, by transient transfection of CAD murine neuroblastoma cells, leads to the accumulation of intracytoplasmic inclusions (Perez-Olle et al. 2002). Co-overexpression of WT DJ-1 along with mutant NFL significantly inhibited the accumulation of NFL inclusions (Figure 5), whereas overexpression of the L166P Parkinsonism-associated mutant form of DJ-1 with NFL failed to inhibit the accumulation of aggregates. Coimmunostaining for DJ-1 and NFL indicated that DJ-1 does not colocalize with the NFL inclusions (Figure 5M-5R). DJ-1 did not appear to alter the expression of NFL (Figure S3). These data are consistent with our analysis of DJ-1 chaperone activity toward alphaSyn and indicate that DJ-1 harbors chaperone activity toward a range of substrates in vivo.

###begin title 29
DJ-1 Function Requires Cysteine 53
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 97 112 97 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee3">Lee et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 114 132 114 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Wilson1">Wilson et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 151 170 151 170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Honbou2">Honbou et al. 2003b</xref>
###xml 326 334 326 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 456 464 456 464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 495 504 495 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="sg001">Figure S1</xref>
###xml 856 864 856 864 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g006">Figure 6</xref>
The DJ-1 crystal structure suggests the presence of two highly reactive cysteines, cysteine 106 (Lee et al. 2003; Wilson et al. 2003) and cysteine 53 (Honbou et al. 2003b). To test whether reactive cysteines play a critical role in the function or regulation of DJ-1 activity, we mutagenized each cysteine in DJ-1 to alanine (Figure 6). Surprisingly, mutation of cysteine 106, at the predicted nucleophile elbow of DJ-1, does not alter the basal activity (Figure 6A) or the DTT sensitivity (See Figure S1) of DJ-1 chaperone function. In contrast, mutation of cysteine 53, which is present at the dimeric interface of DJ-1, completely abrogates chaperone activity. Similarly, mutation of all three cysteines in DJ-1 (cysteine 106, cysteine 53, and cysteine 47) leads to the loss of chaperone function. The cysteine mutations do not alter DJ-1 dimerization (Figure 6D) or the apparent stability of DJ-1 in vivo (unpublished data), unlike the L166P Parkinsonism-associated mutation.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 181 201 181 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Martinat1">Martinat et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 411 412 411 412 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 413 414 413 414 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 480 488 480 488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 586 587 586 587 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 588 589 588 589 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1001 1020 1001 1020 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Honbou1">Honbou et al. 2003a</xref>
###xml 273 278 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
DJ-1-deficient ES cells display increased sensitivity to oxidative stress, and this phenotype can be "rescued" by overexpression of WT DJ-1 but not PD-associated L166P mutant DJ-1 (Martinat et al. 2004). We further investigated the activity of the cysteine-mutant forms of human DJ-1 in vivo in the complementation of DJ-1-deficient ES cells. Cysteine 106-mutant DJ-1 robustly rescued DJ-1 knockout cells from H2O2 toxicity, consistent with the in vitro chaperone activity assay (Figure 6C). In contrast, cysteine 53 and the triple-cysteine mutant forms of DJ-1 failed to protect from H2O2 toxicity. These data support a role for cysteine 53-dependent chaperone activity in DJ-1-mediated ROS protection, and demonstrate a direct correlation between DJ-1 in vitro chaperone activity and cellular protection from oxidative stress. Our data are consistent with the prior observation that mutation of cysteine 53 to alanine abrogates the low-isoelectric point variant that is induced by oxidative stress (Honbou et al. 2003a).

###begin title 32
Discussion
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 245 265 241 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Martinat1">Martinat et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 757 768 753 764 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Jenner1">Jenner 2003</xref>
We provide evidence that DJ-1 functions as a cytoplasmic redox-sensitive molecular chaperone in vitro and in vivo. This activity extends to alphaSyn and the neurofilament subunit NFL, proteins implicated in PD pathology. In a companion article (Martinat et al. 2004), we show that DJ-1 deficiency sensitizes cells to oxidative stress, leading to increased apoptosis in the context of an ROS burst. Taken together, our data strongly support the notion that DJ-1 functions as a redox-dependent protein chaperone to mitigate molecular insults downstream of an ROS burst. Oxidation-modified proteins have been shown to accumulate in the context of normal aging and PD, and may participate in the generation of protein aggregates in neurodegenerative disorders (Jenner 2003).
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 217 234 217 234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Gusev1">Gusev et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 672 685 668 681 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Bonifati1">Bonifati 2003</xref>
###xml 687 704 683 700 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-CanetAviles1">Canet-Aviles 2004</xref>
###xml 828 837 824 833 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="sg002">Figure S2</xref>
It is of interest to identify relevant in vivo substrates for DJ-1 activity in the context of DNs in PD. Our data suggest that DJ-1 activity extends to multiple targets, reminiscent of other small protein chaperones (Gusev et al. 2002), and consistent with this, DJ-1 activity is not ATP-dependent (unpublished data). Candidate substrates for DJ-1 chaperone activity in the context of PD include alphaSyn and neurofilament proteins, based on their presence in PD protein inclusions. Our data suggest that DJ-1 functions to suppress protein aggregates in the cytoplasm. It is possible that DJ-1 plays additional roles in the mitochondria or nucleus, as has been suggested (Bonifati 2003; Canet-Aviles 2004), although DJ-1 appears to remain localized diffusely in the cytoplasm with or without toxin treatment in our studies (see Figure S2).
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 307 325 299 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Volles1">Volles et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 559 560 539 540 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 674 675 650 651 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
Our data indicate that DJ-1 can suppress an early step in the formation of alphaSyn aggregates, the generation of high molecular weight oligomers (protofibrils). Interestingly, it has been suggested that such protofibrils, rather than the large fibrillar aggregates, may underlie alphaSyn toxicity in vivo (Volles et al. 2001). DJ-1 inhibits the aggregation of alphaSyn in differentiated cells in vivo, and loss of DJ-1 leads to increased accumulation of insoluble alphaSyn. DJ-1 appears to associate with alphaSyn in the Triton X-100-soluble fraction of FeCl2-treated lysates, and DJ-1 colocalizes with alphaSyn in the Triton X-100-insoluble fraction in the context of FeCl2 treatment. However, DJ-1 does not colocalize with the punctate protein aggregates visible by immunostaining in the case of either alphaSyn or NFL. This supports the notion that DJ-1 functions at an early step in the aggregation process, when the substrate protein may be misfolded, but has not yet formed a mature aggregate. We hypothesize that DJ-1 may promote the degradation of such misfolded proteins, either through the proteasome or through other cellular pathways such as chaperone-mediated autophagy.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 147 162 147 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee3">Lee et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 248 249 248 249 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 250 251 250 251 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 316 331 316 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Lee3">Lee et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 401 420 401 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Olzmann1">Olzmann et al. 2003</xref>
A recent study investigated the chaperone activity of WT DJ-1 in vitro toward CS and concluded that redox regulation was not a significant factor (Lee et al. 2003). This is most likely a consequence of the use of only oxidizing conditions (0.5 mM H2O2) but not reducing conditions in the described chaperone assays (Lee et al. 2003). A second report failed to detect DJ-1 chaperone activity in vitro (Olzmann et al. 2003), but importantly, this study employed only reducing conditions in which DJ-1 chaperone activity is abrogated. In the present study we demonstrate that DJ-1 chaperone activity is inhibited by reducing conditions, and can be stimulated by oxidation. Thus, in the normal reducing environment of the cell, DJ-1 may be inactive. Production of ROS and alteration of the redox state of the cytoplasm may activate DJ-1 chaperone activity as a mechanism of coping with protein aggregation and misfolding.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 164 184 164 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Martinat1">Martinat et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 232 236 232 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">DJ-1</italic>
###xml 314 334 314 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Martinat1">Martinat et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 454 472 454 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Miller1">Miller et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 889 897 889 897 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g004">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 899 900 899 900 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0020362-g004">4</xref>
We find that that the PD-associated L166P mutant DJ-1 fails to function as a molecular chaperone in vivo or in vitro. Consistent with this, in a companion article (Martinat et al. 2004), we show that this mutant fails to complement DJ-1 knockout cells in vivo, even when overexpressed at artificially high levels (Martinat et al. 2004). Furthermore, the L166P mutant form fails to dimerize even when expressed at WT levels. Thus, although prior studies (Miller et al. 2003) and our analyses (unpublished data) have found that the L166P PD-associated DJ-1 mutation leads to decreased protein stability, it is apparent that even overexpression of the L166P mutant protein does not restore function. The L166P clinical phenotype is not due simply to reduced levels of DJ-1 protein, and, furthermore, we do not observe evidence of altered subcellular localization of the L166P mutant protein (Figure 4M-4R). Rather, our studies favor a model by which the pathological mechanism of this mutation is a consequence of altered structure and resultant loss of function.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 288 307 288 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Honbou1">Honbou et al. 2003a</xref>
###xml 471 489 471 489 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0020362-Wilson1">Wilson et al. 2003</xref>
Mutation of cysteine 53 in DJ-1 abrogates both chaperone and protective functions of this protein. Interestingly, cysteine 53 has previously been implicated as a reactive cysteine required for the in vivo modification of DJ-1 to a lower isoelectric point in response to oxidative stress (Honbou et al. 2003a), consistent with a role for such redox regulation in vivo. In contrast, cysteine 106, which has been reported to be sensitive to oxidative modification in vitro (Wilson et al. 2003), does not appear to be required for the in vitro and in vivo DJ-1 activities.
###end p 38
