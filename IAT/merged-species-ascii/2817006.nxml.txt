###begin article-title 0
Unexpected Tolerance of alpha-Cleavage of the Prion Protein to Sequence Variations
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: JBOM SIY AMC FB PD AA. Performed the experiments: JBOM SIY PD. Analyzed the data: JBOM SIY AMC CB FB AA. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: JBOM SIY CB AA. Wrote the paper: JBOM AA.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 43 44 43 44 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 153 154 136 137 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 264 265 243 244 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 363 364 338 339 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 1123 1124 1074 1075 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 166 181 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
The cellular form of the prion protein, PrPC, undergoes extensive proteolysis at the alpha site (109Kdownward arrowH110). Expression of non-cleavable PrPC mutants in transgenic mice correlates with neurotoxicity, suggesting that alpha-cleavage is important for PrPC physiology. To gain insights into the mechanisms of alpha-cleavage, we generated a library of PrPC mutants with mutations in the region neighbouring the alpha-cleavage site. The prevalence of C1, the carboxy adduct of alpha-cleavage, was determined for each mutant. In cell lines of disparate origin, C1 prevalence was unaffected by variations in charge and hydrophobicity of the region neighbouring the alpha-cleavage site, and by substitutions of the residues in the palindrome that flanks this site. Instead, alpha-cleavage was size-dependently impaired by deletions within the domain 106-119. Almost no cleavage was observed upon full deletion of this domain. These results suggest that alpha-cleavage is executed by an alpha-PrPase whose activity, despite surprisingly limited sequence specificity, is dependent on the size of the central region of PrPC.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 131 134 131 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Beler1">[1]</xref>
###xml 136 139 136 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Brandner1">[2]</xref>
###xml 254 255 254 255 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 305 307 302 304 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sc</sup>
###xml 361 364 358 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Prusiner1">[3]</xref>
###xml 366 369 363 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Aguzzi1">[4]</xref>
###xml 536 538 533 535 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sc</sup>
###xml 598 601 595 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Aguzzi1">[4]</xref>
###xml 603 606 600 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Aguzzi2">[5]</xref>
The prion protein is necessary for the development of prion diseases, also termed transmissible spongiform encephalopathies (TSEs) [1], [2]. The most established molecular model for these diseases proposes that the cellular form of the prion protein (PrPC) is refolded into beta-sheet-rich aggregates (PrPSc), which constitute the infectious agent termed prion [3], [4]. While the concept of a nucleic-acid-free infectious agent spreading through protein aggregation is supported by a large body of evidence, the mechanisms by which PrPSc aggregates and induces neurotoxicity are poorly understood [4], [5].
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 40 41 40 41 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 49 51 49 51 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sc</sup>
###xml 71 72 71 72 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>

###xml 170 171 166 167 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>

###xml 273 276 256 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Kornblatt1">[9]</xref>
###xml 313 314 296 297 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 373 374 356 357 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 374 378 357 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Riek1">[10]</xref>
###xml 493 496 476 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Chen1">[7]</xref>
###xml 537 539 520 522 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sc</sup>
###xml 590 593 570 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Mange1">[6]</xref>
###xml 653 656 633 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Chen1">[7]</xref>
###xml 658 662 638 642 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Caughey1">[11]</xref>
###xml 794 796 770 772 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sc</sup>
###xml 796 799 772 775 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Chen1">[7]</xref>
###xml 801 805 777 781 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Caughey1">[11]</xref>
###xml 142 147 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 149 155 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 303 309 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
Studies on proteolytic processing of PrPC and PrPSc have shown that PrPC is mainly cleaved in a region termed the alpha-cleavage site [6]. In human, bovine, and ovine PrPC, the cleavage has been reported to occur at the conserved residues 109Kdownward arrowH1110 [7], [8], [9] (the residue numbering of murine PrPC is used in this report) in the unstructured portion of PrPC[10]. The resultant products are the globular C-proximal and the flexible N-proximal domains (C1 and N1, respectively) [7]. In contrast, the disease-associated PrPSc conformer undergoes a process named beta-cleavage [6], which generates a larger C-proximal product designated C2 [7], [11]. One likely explanation for this differential processing is that the alpha-cleavage site may be buried within the aggregates of PrPSc[7], [11] and may therefore be protected from proteolysis.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 191 195 187 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Shyng1">[12]</xref>
###xml 304 305 300 301 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 386 390 382 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Walmsley1">[13]</xref>
###xml 250 255 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The subcellular site of alpha-cleavage is controversially discussed. On the one hand, cleavage is inhibited by ammonium chloride, suggesting that it occurs in an acidic endocytic compartment [12]. On the other hand, C1 accumulates intracellularly in human SH-SY5Y cells expressing secreted anchorless PrPC, suggesting that cleavage occurs in a late compartment of the secretory pathway [13].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 11 12 11 12 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 60 71 56 63 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;23&#8211;134</sup>
###xml 88 99 76 83 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;23&#8211;121</sup>
###xml 101 105 85 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Baumann1">[14]</xref>
###xml 140 143 124 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Chen1">[7]</xref>
###xml 145 149 129 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Baumann1">[14]</xref>
###xml 187 190 171 174 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Aguzzi2">[5]</xref>
###xml 192 196 176 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Shmerling1">[15]</xref>
###xml 239 246 219 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 295 296 275 276 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 320 322 300 302 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sc</sup>
###xml 437 438 417 418 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
Certain PrPC deletion mutants, such those termed DeltaF (PrPDelta23-134) and DeltaE (PrPDelta23-121) [14], resemble the C1 cleavage product [7], [14] and do not support prion replication [5], [15]. Therefore, enhancement of alpha-cleavage in vivo may diminish the availability of full-length PrPC for conversion into PrPSc. If this conjecture proves correct, it will be important to identify the proteases responsible for cleavage of PrPC, here termed alpha-PrPases, and any hypothetical factors that may control their activity.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 21 22 21 22 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 218 219 214 215 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 219 223 215 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Baumann1">[14]</xref>
###xml 225 229 221 225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Shmerling1">[15]</xref>
###xml 231 235 227 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Li1">[16]</xref>
###xml 237 241 233 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Bremer1">[17]</xref>
###xml 301 302 297 298 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 379 380 375 376 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 132 136 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Moreover, certain PrPC variants bearing deletions close to the alpha-cleavage site induce toxicity when transgenically expressed in mice. Toxicity can be rescued in a dose-dependent manner by coexpression of normal PrPC[14], [15], [16], [17]. This raises the question whether the toxicity of these PrPC mutants may be related to abnormalities in the proteolytic processing of PrPC.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 40 41 40 41 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 136 137 132 133 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>

###xml 282 286 270 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Prnp</italic>
###xml 286 289 274 277 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 295 299 283 287 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Baumann1">[14]</xref>
###xml 301 305 289 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Li1">[16]</xref>
###xml 342 354 330 338 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;114&#8211;121</sup>
###xml 409 413 393 397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Baumann1">[14]</xref>
###xml 420 427 404 411 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 465 476 449 456 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;94&#8211;134</sup>
###xml 511 522 491 498 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;95&#8211;132</sup>
###xml 566 570 542 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Sakudo1">[18]</xref>
###xml 116 131 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 290 294 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
But which structural determinants of PrPC control the interaction with the putative alpha-PrPase? When expressed in transgenic mice, PrPC mutants bearing large deletions of the alpha-cleavage site, such as PrPDelta94-134, had a complete cleavage inhibition and were highly toxic in Prnp-/- mice [14], [16], whereas the downstream deletion PrPDelta114-121 exhibited some cleavage and showed only mild toxicity [14]. This in vivo data on proteolytic processing of PrPDelta94-134 replicate the observation that PrPDelta95-132 is not cleaved when expressed in Hpl cells [18].
###end p 9

###xml 20 21 20 21 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 107 108 107 108 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>

###xml 428 432 416 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Wegner1">[19]</xref>
###xml 614 618 602 606 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Wegner1">[19]</xref>
###xml 726 730 710 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Shmerling1">[15]</xref>

###xml 800 804 784 788 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Watt1">[20]</xref>

###xml 938 942 918 922 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Tveit1">[21]</xref>
###xml 985 990 965 970 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">K113R</sup>
###xml 997 1002 977 982 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">K113D</sup>
###xml 1009 1014 989 994 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">K113A</sup>
###xml 1024 1037 1004 1017 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">KHV113&#8211;115AAA</sup>
###xml 1169 1170 1149 1150 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
Other studies on PrPC processing in cultured cells suggest that the alanine and glycine residues in the PrPC palindromic region localized in the downstream flank of the alpha-cleavage site may be important for alpha-cleavage [19]. It was reported that in transfected N2a cells, PrPC bearing two point mutations that transformed the palindromic domain 112-119 into an alanine chain had no remarkable impairment in alpha-cleavage [19]. Conversely, the cleavage product C1 was not, or very weakly, detectable in constructs where this palindrome was converted into a glycine chain, or where region 105-125 was deleted [19]. Other studies also suggested that alpha-cleavage is not affected by the removal of the octapeptide region [15], [18], [20] or by the insertion of supernumerary octapeptide repeats [20]. alpha-cleavage of PrPC mutants was also assessed in cells expressing ovine PrP (ovPrP) with GFP inserted into the N-proximal region [21]. In this system, the point mutations ovPrPK113R, ovPrPK113D, ovPrPK113A and ovPrPKHV113-115AAA did not appear to affect cleavage, with the respective mutant proteins processed similarly to the fusion protein of wild-type ovPrPC with GFP.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 88 89 88 89 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 142 143 138 139 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 186 187 182 183 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 228 229 224 225 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 583 584 571 572 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 731 734 719 722 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 727 734 715 722 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Prnp<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 786 787 774 775 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 795 796 783 784 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 132 138 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 720 726 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
Here we attempted to test certain predictions regarding the sequence requirements to PrPC for alpha-cleavage. We confirmed that the murine PrPC domain 106-119 is indeed important for PrPC cleavage. In addition, we found that PrPC cleavage was independent of the precise sequence at the cleavage site, and was largely independent of the charges and hydrophobicity in the vicinity of alpha-cleavage site. Instead, the efficacy of alpha-cleavage was linearly dependent on the size of deletions between residues 106-119. Likewise, substitutions of alanines to glycine residues in the PrPC palindromic region 112-119 had no influence on the degree of C1 generation. Finally, we did not detect any C1 generation in lysates of murine Prnp-/- cells and brains expressing anchorless secreted PrPC (secPrPC) indicating that cleavage is membrane anchor-dependent.

We found that the extent of glycosylation of both PrPC and DeltaF was quantitatively and qualitatively similar in each of the above cell lines (Fig. 1A), and essentially corresponded to that of brain tissue (data not shown). Upon deglycosylation with PNGase, a sharp C1 band with electrophoretic mobility similar to that of DeltaF (ca. 15 kDa) was detectable in all cell lines (Fig. 1B). ###xml 137 141 137 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Vincent1">[27]</xref>

###xml 431 438 431 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g001">Fig. 1D</xref>
###xml 91 97 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 118 123 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 152 159 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 167 173 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
The size of the N-proximal fragment observed here is in agreement with previous reports in murine TSM1 neurons and in human HEK293 cells [27], and with chicken PrP in murine N2a cells [28]. Instead, we did not confirm a report that the dominant N-terminal band in N2a and GT1 cells has an apparent molecular weight of 7.6 KDa [6]. The C1 fragment was also detected after PNGase treatment of total cell lysate of the same cultures (Fig. 1D). The observation of these two well-defined cleavage fragments, C1 and N1, supports the conjecture that alpha-cleavage is a controlled proteolytic process rather than mere exoproteolytic digestion of the unstructured region by nonspecific proteases.

###xml 156 162 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>

###xml 403 418 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 451 457 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 744 750 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 763 768 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>

To assess whether PrPC is universally cleaved in other cell systems, PrPC and the mutant C39 were transfected into human HeLa cells [29] (Fig. 1E) and into murine primary embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs) derived from Prnp-/- mice (Fig. 1F). C39 lacks the palindromic region that flanks the alpha-cleavage site of PrPC (Fig. 2A), and a deletion within the same region was reported to impair C1 generation in transgenic mice [14]. In both HeLa and MEFs the murine PrPC molecule was efficiently cleaved (Fig. 1E-F), whereas alpha-cleavage of the mutant C39 was impaired in both cell lines. We conclude that the structural features that control alpha-cleavage of PrPC are common to many cell types of diverse histogenetic origin, including established murine cell lines, human cell lines, and murine primary cells.

###xml 64 65 60 61 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
Impact of Residues Neighbouring the alpha-Cleavage Site onto PrPC Processing
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 30 31 26 27 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 204 211 200 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>
###xml 381 382 373 374 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 634 641 622 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>
###xml 795 799 775 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Baumann1">[14]</xref>
###xml 801 805 781 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Shmerling1">[15]</xref>
The alpha-cleavage site of PrPC is located between a charge cluster (CC) and a hydrophobic core (HC), and is flanked N-proximally by positively charged residues and C-proximally by a palindrome sequence (Fig. 2A). We first investigated whether the positive charges at the alpha-cleavage site contribute to defining the proteolytic site. For this purpose we designed a series of PrPC mutants with substitutions of the basic amino acids. In the BOM6 construct we mutated to neutral alanine residues the two charged amino acids of the alpha-cleavage site, whereas in BOM3 we mutated the positive charges into anionic aspartate residues (Fig. 2A). Because DeltaF and C1 were previously found to display similar electrophoretic mobility, DeltaF was used as a molecular size marker for identifying C1 [14], [15].
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 42 43 42 43 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 83 84 83 84 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 255 256 251 252 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 364 365 360 361 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 411 412 407 408 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 428 435 424 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2B</xref>
###xml 648 652 644 648 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Baumann1">[14]</xref>
###xml 654 658 650 654 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Christensen1">[30]</xref>
Cell lysates of lines transfected with PrPC and its mutants were collected, and PrPC glycosylation and processing were assessed before and after treatment with PNGase. BOM6, BOM3, and the deletion mutant DeltaF all underwent glycosylation similarly to PrPC in both cell lines. Therefore, charge inversion or large deletions in the flexible N-proximal domain of PrPC did not exert any appreciable impact onto PrPC glycosylation (Fig. 2B). This observation is in agreement with reports suggesting that large alterations in the region of the CC and HC have no measurable impact in the distribution of the mutants between various cellular microdomains [14], [30].

###xml 616 623 612 619 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2B</xref>
###xml 860 867 848 855 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2B</xref>
###xml 930 931 918 919 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
The N1 fragment is released into the medium, whereas C1 retains its GPI anchor and its membrane localization. Furthermore, N1 encompasses the unstructured domain of PrP [10] and is unstable. Because of these circumstances, we reasoned that the degree of cleavage of the various PrPC mutants would be more reliably assessed by measuring C1 rather than N1. Therefore, we quantitated the C1 band and compared it to the integrals of the band corresponding to uncleaved PrPC (Fig. 2B). In both Hpl and Npl cells the degree of alpha-cleavage of BOM6 and BOM3 was comparable to, or <25% lower than, that of wild-type PrPC (Fig. 2B). Hence removal, or even inversion, of the positive charges at the alpha-cleavage site did not result in a remarkable inhibition of alpha-cleavage. The values for cleavage inhibition of the mutants varied <7% between Hpl and Npl cells (Fig. 2B), indicating that the pattern of proteolytic processing of PrPC in these two cell lines was qualitatively and quantitatively similar. For this reason we focused on the Hpl cell line for most of the studies described in the following.
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 278 285 270 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>
###xml 404 405 392 393 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 517 524 501 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>
###xml 644 645 628 629 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 775 776 759 760 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 778 785 762 769 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2C</xref>
To further test the possibility that alpha-cleavage is independent of the charges and sequence of the cleavage site, we designed the BOM8 mutant in which all five basic histidine and lysine residues neighbouring the alpha-cleavage site were mutagenized into uncharged alanines (Fig. 2A). We also asked whether swapping the position of the alpha-cleavage site would alter the proteolytic processing of PrPC. For this the mutant BOM7 was elaborated, whose alpha-cleavage site was shifted downstream by two amino acids (Fig. 2A). We found that the cleavage efficiency of BOM6, BOM8 and BOM7 was between 95 and 100% of the cleavage of wild-type PrPC. Hence, as with the previously assessed constructs, BOM8 and BOM7 generated C1 with a similar efficiency to that of wild-type PrPC (Fig. 2C).
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 381 388 377 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>
###xml 558 565 554 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>
###xml 861 862 857 858 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 1025 1026 1017 1018 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 1028 1037 1020 1029 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2D,F</xref>
###xml 1219 1228 1207 1216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2D,F</xref>
###xml 1393 1394 1377 1378 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
Having established that the above modifications had little impact onto the alpha-cleavage, we assessed the impact of stronger alterations of the CC region. For this purpose we analyzed BOM8 together with two constructs bearing charge inversions. FBOM3 had all six cationic residues of the CC mutated into anionic glutamate residues, resulting in complete inversion of all charges (Fig. 2A). In FBOM1 the positive charges of four lysines were inverted into anionic glutamate residues, but these acidic residues were interspersed with two cationic histidines (Fig. 2A). Constructs C39 and C40 (which carries a large deletion of the region 100-129) were analyzed in parallel and used as "molecular milestones" for assessing moderate and extensive cleavage inhibition, respectively. Quantitation of the C1 fragment generated in these constructs and in wild-type PrPC confirmed that removal of the five positive charges around the alpha-cleavage site and in the neighbouring region did not affect the proteolytic processing of PrPC (Fig. 2D,F). Even extremely drastic modifications, such as the polarity inversion of four or even six positive charges (FBOM1 and FBOM3), resulted in only about 38% alpha-cleavage impairment (Fig. 2D,F). On the basis of these results it is unavoidable to conclude that the charges surrounding the alpha-cleavage site are largely irrelevant for the proteolysis of PrPC, with neutralization and even complete charge reversal of the CC region resulting in a surprisingly slight impairment of alpha-cleavage.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
###xml 15 16 15 16 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>

###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 181 188 181 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>

###xml 465 472 465 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Prnp<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 483 487 483 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Kuwahara1">[25]</xref>
###xml 489 496 489 496 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>
###xml 626 627 622 623 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 629 638 625 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2D,F</xref>
###xml 954 955 950 951 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>

###xml 1133 1140 1129 1136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g002">Fig. 2E</xref>
###xml 1383 1387 1375 1379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Baumann1">[14]</xref>
###xml 1439 1443 1431 1435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Wegner1">[19]</xref>
###xml 1580 1581 1568 1569 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 925 931 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 944 949 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1367 1382 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
Next, we studied the residues located carboxy proximally to the cleavage site. This region contains a palindrome which is immediately adjacent to the N-terminal boundary of the HC (Fig. 2A). A previous study reported that substitution of all palindromic glycines with alanines, as in construct BOM9, abrogated C1 generation in N2a cells [19]. We therefore assessed the cleavability of BOM9 and of BOM11, which bears only three palindromic alanine substitutions, in Prnp-/- Hpl cells [25] (Fig. 2A). To our surprise, quantification of C1 revealed that both constructs were processed by alpha-cleavage similarly to wild-type PrPC (Fig. 2D,F). In order to evaluate the robustness of these results, the palindromic mutants BOM9 and BOM11, the palindromic-deletion construct C39, and the charge-neutralized BOM8 were transfected into HeLa cells. Fragments were analyzed with antibody POM19 which recognizes the globular domain of murine, but not of human, PrPC[26]. The proteolytic processing of these mutants was indistinguishable in both HeLa (Fig. 2E) and Hpl cells (Fig. 2D,F) and was essentially identical to that of wild-type PrPC (Fig. 2E). In contrast, C39 displayed partial alpha-cleavage inhibition in both Hpl and HeLa cells, which is in agreement with the finding that a mutant bearing a deletion of the region 114-121 is inefficiently cleaved in the brain of transgenic mice [14]. We conclude that, in contrast to published claims [19], the substitution of the palindrome with a total or partial polyglycine chain does not have a measurable impact on alpha-cleavage of PrPC.

###begin title 30
The Impact of Hydrophobicity onto alpha-Cleavage
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 66 67 62 63 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 197 201 193 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Hegde1">[31]</xref>
Another striking feature that might modulate alpha-cleavage of PrPC is the strong hydrophobicity of the residues neighbouring the cleavage site. Since the HC may be associated to the cell membrane [31], we reasoned that alpha-cleavage may be affected by the distance between the cleavage site and the cell membrane. In this scenario, reduction of the hydrophobicity of the HC may modify its interaction with the cell membrane and thereby decrease the rate of alpha-cleavage.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 52 53 52 53 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 301 308 301 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g003">Fig. 3A</xref>
###xml 551 552 551 552 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 612 621 612 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g003">Fig. 3A&#8211;B</xref>
###xml 784 793 780 789 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g003">Fig. 3A&#8211;B</xref>
To test the latter hypothesis, we designed three PrPC constructs in which we substituted hydrophobic residues with non-ionic hydrophilic analogues of roughly similar molecular size. Accordingly, we substituted alanine with glycine residues, whereas leucines and valines were replaced with glutamines (Fig. 3A). The maximal deviation in primary sequence between each pair of constructs was four residues. Construct BOM26 displayed only a slight reduction of hydrophobicity and therefore was expected to be cleaved almost as efficiently as wild-type PrPC, whereas BOM27 had a stronger reduction of hydrophobicity (Fig. 3A-B). Finally, BOM25 bears almost no positive hydrophobic integral and was designed to stringently test whether hydrophobicity would play any role in alpha-cleavage (Fig. 3A-B). Again, constructs C39 and C40 were used in control experiments and served as molecular milestones of moderate and extensive inhibition of cleavage, respectively.

No differences in alpha-cleavage were detected between BOM12, BOM14, and PrPC (Fig. 4B-C). In contrast, BOM13, with deletion in the residues 105-110, and C39, which lacks the domain 111-120, showed about 50% reduction in C1 abundance (Fig. 4B-C). This suggests that the space occupied by residues 105-120, but not necessarily the chemical identity of those residues (Fig. 2), is important for alpha-cleavage. Surprisingly, although the removal of the region 100-110 did not affect alpha-cleavage in BOM12, the shorter deletion of 105-110 resulted in about 50% inhibition of alpha-cleavage in BOM13. This suggests a negative modulation of cleavage by residues 100-104 in this context. The sequence present in BOM13, but absent from BOM12, is KPSKP (Fig. 4A) and is highly conserved domain in mammals [32], [33]. Even lower vertebrates, including chicken and fish, generally conserve the KPxKP motif [33], [34]. Perhaps the alpha-PrPase is partially blocked in BOM13, but not in BOM12, due to an entity interacting with the conserved region 101-104. Alternatively, this constellation of prolines and charged residues may contribute to a decrease in the flexibility of this segment and, in turn, enhance the antagonistic effects of the deletion on the efficiency of alpha-cleavage.

###xml 1094 1101 1078 1085 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009107-g004">Fig. 4D</xref>
Because the domain 105-120 appeared to be important for alpha-cleavage, we set out to identify the minimal region within this domain that would affect the proteolytic processing of PrPC. For this purpose, we assessed the degree of C1 generation in the mutants BOM17, BOM15, and BOM16 bearing deletions of 14, 9, and 4 residues within the domain 105-120, respectively (Fig. 4A). Quantification of PrPC proteolysis in these constructs revealed a C1 reduction by about 28% for BOM16 (4-residue deletion; Fig. 4B-C). The extent of cleavage impairment was proportional to the size of the deletions (Fig. 4B-D), provided that said deletions encompassed the alpha-cleavage site and were situated within the domain 106-119. When the domain 106-119 was deleted, alpha-cleavage was only marginally higher than in C40 (Fig. 4B-D). These findings suggest that alpha-cleavage is not controlled by a specific primary sequence, but rather by the length of the unstructured stretch encompassing residues 105-120. Accordingly, 50% cleavage inhibition is expected to be achieved with a deletion of six residues (Fig. 4D).

Increased hydrophobicity in the HC may result in increased generation of a transmembrane species of PrP termed CtmPrP, which may be toxic [35]. In order to further test the hypothesis that a slight impairment on PrPC cleavage could be mildly toxic and therefore be translated into human diseases, the degree of proteolysis of constructs carrying mutations linked to human prion diseases was quantified. PrPP101L is associated with Gerstmann-Straussler-Scheinker (GSS) syndrome [36], PrPD177L replicates fatal familial insomnia (FFI) [37], and PrPE199K is found in familiar CJD cases [38], [39]. For control, the proteolysis of PrPQ218K was assessed, which is a construct reported to be a dominant negative of PrPSc replication [40]. Another control was secPrPC, which is secreted and has been shown to be convertible into PrPSc, but not fatal in mice [41]. Quantification of C1 generation in Hpl cells expressing these mutants showed reduced alpha-cleavage in PrPE199K, and even more so in PrPD177N (Fig. 5B,D). Also, PrPP101L showed a high variability in terms of alpha-cleavage rate, with low values of C1 generation observed sporadically (Fig. 5B,D). In contrast, the PrPSc dominant negative PrPQ218K was cleaved in a similar fashion as normal PrPC (Fig. 5A,D), suggesting a link between toxicity of inherited human prion diseases, and inhibition of alpha-cleavage of PrP. However, secPrPC did not show any C1 generation in Hpl cell lysates (Fig. 5B,D), in contrast to a previous report using human SH-SY5Y cells [13]. In order to clarify this discrepancy, we assessed the cleavage of this mutant in brain of transgenic mice expressing secPrPC in Prnp-/- mice [41]. Also here we were not able to detect any C1 fragment (Fig. 5C). We conclude that secPrPC does not generate cell-associated C1 in the systems analyzed here. This finding, in combination with results reported elsewhere [42], suggests that the GPI anchor is necessary for the toxicity of uncleavable PrPC mutants.

###begin title 48
Discussion
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 21 22 17 18 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 135 139 131 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Riek1">[10]</xref>
###xml 201 204 197 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Chen1">[7]</xref>
###xml 206 210 202 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Baumann1">[14]</xref>
###xml 212 216 208 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-JimenezHuete1">[43]</xref>
###xml 258 262 254 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Vincent1">[27]</xref>
###xml 264 268 260 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Harris1">[28]</xref>
###xml 442 443 438 439 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 623 624 615 616 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
alpha-cleavage of PrPC occurs in many cell types and tissues at a well-defined site located in the unstructured portion of the protein [10], generating a stable carboxy terminal fragment of ca. 15 kDa [7], [14], [43] and an N-terminal fragment of ca. 12 kDa [27], [28]. The robust and stereotypic nature of these processing events suggests the existence of specific proteases which we term PrPases. However, we were surprised to find that PrPC proteolysis is remarkably tolerant of variations in the sequence surrounding the site of cleavage. A large number of manipulations had little or no effect in alpha-cleavage of PrPC; some of these manipulations were very far-reaching and included removal of the canonical cleavage site, inversion of all charges, radical manipulations of the hydrophobicity, and alteration of the palindrome domain.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 141 142 141 142 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 437 438 433 434 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
As the resolution of SDS-PAGE does not allow for detecting single amino-acid shifts in the cleavage site, it is possible that some of the PrPC mutants may have experienced subtle shifts in the alpha-cleavage site. Even if that were the case, all manipulations consistently preserved the generation of a C1 cleavage product whose abundance and electrophoretic motility was similar to bona fide C1 generated by proteolysis of wild-type PrPC. Conversely, deletion of residues 106-119 strongly reduced alpha-cleavage, whereas shorter deletions within this region had only a moderate effect. The efficiency of cleavage was inversely proportional to the size of deletions centered onto the alpha-cleavage site.
###end p 50

###xml 137 138 133 134 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>

###xml 788 794 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 889 895 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 900 905 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>

The results described in this study allow drawing several surprising conclusions on the molecular preconditions for alpha-cleavage of PrPC. Firstly, substitution of positively charged residues at the alpha-cleavage site has no major effect on the degree of PrPC proteolysis. This is in agreement with previous studies of N2a cells transfected with GFP-fused ovine PrPC mutants [21]. Secondly, replacement of all six positive charges of the CC with negative charges, including those of the alpha-cleavage site, resulted only in a partial inhibition of C1 generation. Thirdly, even total or partial substitution of the palindromic alanines flanking the alpha-cleavage site with glycines had little impact in the normal proteolysis of PrPC. This observation contradicts a previous report in murine N2a cells [19], yet was particularly robust in our experience and appears to be valid in both murine and human cells. Fourthly, amino acid substitutions reducing the hydrophobicity of the region surrounding the alpha-cleavage site failed to reduce the proteolysis of PrPC. However, impairment of alpha-cleavage was observed upon substitution of the charged residues within the cleavage site for two highly hydrophobic isoleucines. Fifthly, deletions in the vicinity of the alpha-cleavage site, as the domains 100-110 and 121-129, had no impact onto cleavage whereas deletions of the domains 105-110 and 111-120 resulted in about 50% impairment on alpha-cleavage. The latter result is in agreement with our previous report that a PrPC mutant lacking residues 114-121 undergoes inefficient alpha-cleavage in transgenic mice [14].

###begin p 52
###xml 276 280 272 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Kim1">[33]</xref>

###xml 463 467 459 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Lysek1">[34]</xref>
###xml 469 473 465 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Kay1">[44]</xref>
###xml 475 479 471 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Mayer1">[45]</xref>
###xml 786 789 770 773 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cis</italic>
It is somewhat counterintuitive that deletion of residues 100-110 had no apparent effect whereas the shorter deletion 105-110 partially blocked alpha-cleavage. This finding points to the importance of the 100-104 KPSKP residues. This region is highly conserved in vertebrates [33], [34] and has been suggested to be important for protein binding [44]. One of the domains indentified in the consensus sequence in vertebrates is PxxP, which is an SH3-binding motif [34], [44], [45]. All of the above suggests the existence of a macromolecule that interacts with the domain 100-104 and may occlude the alpha-cleavage site. This putative macromolecule may not necessarily be a protein. Alternatively, a proline kink at the alpha-cleavage site may impair the function of the alpha-PrPase in cis.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 288 289 284 285 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
In our experiments, the C1 fragment was suppressed below detectability only when the entire segment 106-119, or major portions thereof, was removed. However, shorter deletions within the domain 106-119 centered in the alpha-cleavage site region only partially inhibited proteolysis of PrPC. Assuming that the efficiency of cleavage is linearly dependent on the length of this segment, a 50% blockage of alpha-cleavage would be obtained with a six-residue deletion.

###xml 355 359 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 366 371 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 384 389 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 417 432 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
In summary, alpha-cleavage of PrPC has a strong tolerance towards sequence degeneration. Such tolerance may underlie evolutionary pressure, since PrPC mutants that do not undergo alpha-cleavage are neurotoxic [14], [15], [16]. We found the determinants of PrPC cleavage to be invariant in many biological systems, including Npl cells derived from Prnp-/- mice [24], human HeLa cells, mouse embryonic fibroblasts, and transgenic mice [14]. This suggests that the relevant alpha-PrPases are functionally conserved and present in many tissues. What might underlie the surprising resilience of alpha-cleavage to sequence variation? Maybe several distinct proteases display redundant alpha-PrPase activity. If that were the case, alterations of the cleavage site may affect the recognition by some-but not all-proteases, resulting in unhindered global alpha-PrPase activity. Alternatively, PrPC may be processed by a single alpha-PrPase with high plasticity of its substrate specificity. Examples of such proteases are the desintegrin and metalloproteases (ADAM) 10 and 17 (also known as TACE, or alpha-secretase of APP). These proteins have been reported to cleave a broad range of basic, acidic, or zwitterionic substrates [46] with no common consensus sequences [46], [47], [48], and have therefore been also termed "sheddases". The activity of sheddases is typically tolerant of point mutations [49], [50] and their cleavage sites appears to rely on the distance of unfolded substrate regions from the cell membrane [49]. Analogously, the alpha-cleavage site of PrPC is localized in an unfolded region and is flanked by a hydrophobic domain which may associate with the cell membrane [31]. Analogously to various substrates of ADAM 10 and 17, the cleavage of PrPC appears to tolerate charge removal or inversion. Indeed, ADAM-10 and ADAM-17 have been suggested to act as alpha-PrPases [51], [52], yet this assertion is not entirely uncontroversial. The function of ADAMs necessitates divalent cations, and it was reported that 10 microM of EDTA does not inhibit alpha-cleavage [12], yet others have reported that 5 mM EDTA inhibits cleavage in a Cu2+ and Fe2+ dependent manner [43].

###xml 606 621 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
Some of the mutations analogous to those found in familial prion diseases appear to impair the proteolytic processing of PrPC. In addition, alpha-cleavage appeared to be also impaired in PrPKH109-110II, which is toxic in transgenic mice [35]. In construct PrPQ218K, which dominantly inhibits PrPSc replication [40], the degree of alpha-cleavage was similar to the one detectable in PrPC. It remains to be established whether PrPC cleavage inhibition is a cause or a consequence of the toxicity of these mutants. In this context it may be interesting to assess the degree of cleavage of these constructs in transgenic mice before the onset of the disease.

###xml 530 534 530 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Chesebro1">[41]</xref>
###xml 690 691 690 691 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 38 53 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 405 420 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
Through the study of a large panel of transgenic mice expressing many different PrPC mutants, we found that the toxicity of PrPC deletion mutants was abolished by removal of the GPI anchor [42]. This finding argues that toxicity is exerted through some process that occurs at the membrane, such as interaction with signal-transducing molecules. In this context it is interesting to note that infection of transgenic mice expressing secPrP, which did not produce any C1 fragment in their brains, results in only weak neurotoxicity [41]. These results may be taken to suggest that similar neuropathogenic cascades are activated both in scrapie infection and in expression of non-cleavable PrPC variants.

###begin p 57
###xml 57 59 53 55 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sc</sup>

###xml 422 426 414 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Polymenidou1">[26]</xref>
###xml 477 478 469 470 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 516 520 508 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Rutishauser1">[53]</xref>
###xml 600 601 592 593 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
Absence of alpha-cleavage is characteristic shared by PrPSc[7], [43] and toxic PrPC mutants [14], [15], [16], suggesting that alpha-cleavage is important for the function of PrPC and possibly even for the pathogenesis of prion diseases. Yet the identity and the functional characteristics of the enzymes relevant for this process are still unknown. A promising way forward may take advantage of newly developed antibodies [26] that allow for isolation and native elution of PrPC-containing supramolecular assemblies [53]. The differential analysis of multiprotein complexes containing variants of PrPC that are permissive or refractory to cleavage may lead the identification of the relevant sheddases and possibly also of downstream players involved in prion toxicity.

The PCR products and the open plasmid were ligated using the Roche Rapid DNA Ligation Kit. Other constructs were performed in two PCR steps using the primers and templates listed in Table S1. In the first step, PCR was performed with: 1) SY6 (Table S1) and the reverse plasmid referred in Table S1; 2) SY7 (Table S1) and the forward plasmid referred in Table S1. The second step was performed with a PCR using 1 uL of PCR product 1) and 2), and using SY6 and SY7 to make the full construct. The construct secPrP was cloned by one additional step with SY6 and A33 primers (Table S1). PCR product purification, digestion and ligation were done in a similar way as described for PrPC. Constructs PrPDelta118-125 and PrPDelta111-125 were made using the QuikChange kit (Stratagene), using the primers indicated in Table S1. Dv1 construct originates from previous experiments [54].

###begin p 63
###xml 817 818 811 812 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 824 828 818 822 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Polymenidou1">[26]</xref>
###xml 836 840 830 834 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Polymenidou1">[26]</xref>
###xml 851 855 845 849 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009107-Polymenidou1">[26]</xref>
###xml 867 872 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 957 962 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Transfection was performed using Lipofectamine (Invitrogen) and Plus Reagent (Invitrogen), into 80% confluent cells. Cells were harvested using 10 mM EDTA, 2 days after transfection. Cell lysis was performed on ice for 1 h, using 1% Triton-X-100 and 1% NP-40, in Tris buffer at pH 7.5, with NaCl and protease inhibitors (Complete; Roche). For brain lysis, a 10% homogenate was prepared in Ripa buffer with protease inhibitors (Complete; Roche), and lysed for 1 h on ice. For all samples, lysate supernatant was collected after centrifugation for 30 min at 16000 g at 4degreesC. Supernatant was then treated with PNGase (NEB), and loaded onto 12% SDS-polyacrylamide gels. After electrophoresis, proteins were transferred to nitrocellulose membranes (Schleicher & Schuell) by wet blotting. Primary antibody was anti PrPC POM1 [26], POM11 [26], or POM19 [26] in case of human derived Hela cells, diluted 1:10'000 from a 1 ug/ul stock. Peroxidase-labelled anti-mouse IgG1 diluted 1:10'000 (Zymed) was used as secondary antibody. Antibodies were probed for 1 h at room temperature or over-night at 4degreesC in a 1% Top-Block solution (FLUKA) in PBS-Tween 20. For membrane blocking, a 5% Top-Block solution was used. Development was done using ECL detection system (Pierce). Densitometric assessment of PrP bands was performed using TINA v2.09g (Raytest Isotopenmessgerate), in the linear range of the band intensity. Hydrophobicity plots were calculated for using DNAMAN software (Lynnon BioSoft, Canada), for window intervals of nine residues.
