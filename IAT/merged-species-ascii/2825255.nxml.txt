###begin article-title 0
PI3Kgamma Protects from Myocardial Ischemia and Reperfusion Injury through a Kinase-Independent Pathway
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: BJH GGN KK YI OP EH JMP BM. Performed the experiments: BJH GGN JGJV FD KK YI VK AM. Analyzed the data: BJH GGN JGJV AM JMP BM. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: OP EH JMP BM. Wrote the paper: BJH GGN JMP BM.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
PI3Kgamma functions in the immune compartment to promote inflammation in response to G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) agonists and PI3Kgamma also acts within the heart itself both as a negative regulator of cardiac contractility and as a pro-survival factor. Thus, PI3Kgamma has the potential to both promote and limit M I/R injury.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 18 21 14 17 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 67 72 59 64 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">KD/KD</sup>
###xml 143 150 135 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 347 350 335 338 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 798 801 774 777 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 854 859 822 827 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">KD/KD</sup>
###xml 894 899 858 863 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">KD/KD</sup>
###xml 29 33 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 87 91 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 120 124 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 238 242 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 351 355 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 802 806 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 870 874 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 900 904 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Complete PI3Kgamma-/- mutant mice, catalytically inactive PI3KgammaKD/KD (KD) knock-in mice, and control wild type (WT) mice were subjected to in vivo myocardial ischemia and reperfusion (M I/R) injury. Additionally, bone-marrow chimeric mice were constructed to elucidate the contribution of the inflammatory response to cardiac damage. PI3Kgamma-/- mice exhibited a significantly increased infarction size following reperfusion. Mechanistically, PI3Kgamma is required for activation of the Reperfusion Injury Salvage Kinase (RISK) pathway (AKT/ERK1/2) and regulates phospholamban phosphorylation in the acute injury response. Using bone marrow chimeras, the cardioprotective role of PI3Kgamma was mapped to non-haematopoietic cells. Importantly, this massive increase in M I/R injury in PI3Kgamma-/- mice was rescued in PI3Kgamma kinase-dead (PI3KgammaKD/KD) knock-in mice. However, PI3KgammaKD/KD mice exhibited a cardiac injury similar to wild type animals, suggesting that specific blockade of PI3Kgamma catalytic activity has no beneficial effects.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Our data show that PI3Kgamma is cardioprotective during M I/R injury independent of its catalytic kinase activity and that loss of PI3Kgamma function in the hematopoietic compartment does not affect disease outcome. Thus, clinical development of specific PI3Kgamma blockers should proceed with caution.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 322 325 322 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Baines1">[1]</xref>
###xml 327 330 327 330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Hausenloy1">[2]</xref>
###xml 374 377 374 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Nian1">[3]</xref>
###xml 379 382 379 382 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Yaoita1">[4]</xref>
###xml 384 387 384 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Fisher1">[5]</xref>
###xml 507 510 507 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Brodde1">[6]</xref>
Cardiac damage in acute myocardial infarction results from a disruption of blood flow to the heart (ischemia) that may be followed by either spontaneous or therapeutically induced restoration of blood supply (reperfusion). Myocardial ischemia/reperfusion (M I/R) results in infarction due to intrinsic cardiomyocyte death [1], [2] and damage via excessive immune activation [3], [4], [5]. Many inflammatory factors that build up during injury exert their effects through G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs) [6] suggesting that mediators of GPCRs might be critically involved in the M I/R damage response. One key signaling pathway downstream of GPCRs is mediated via phosphoinositide 3-kinase gamma (PI3Kgamma).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 434 437 419 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Hawkins1">[7]</xref>
###xml 448 451 429 432 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 571 574 552 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Hirsch1">[8]</xref>
###xml 576 579 557 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Sasaki1">[9]</xref>
###xml 646 650 623 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Hawkins2">[10]</xref>
###xml 770 774 743 747 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Crackower1">[11]</xref>
###xml 776 780 749 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Patrucco1">[12]</xref>
###xml 1008 1011 973 976 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1104 1108 1066 1070 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Oudit1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1131 1134 1089 1092 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1230 1234 1188 1192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Curcio1">[14]</xref>
###xml 1265 1269 1223 1227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Patrucco1">[12]</xref>
###xml 1371 1375 1325 1329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Nienaber1">[15]</xref>
###xml 1531 1535 1481 1485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Doukas1">[16]</xref>
###xml 452 456 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1012 1016 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1135 1139 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1687 1693 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Phosphatidylinositol-3-OH kinases (PI3K) are lipid kinases that convert phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP2) into phosphatidylinositol (3,4,5)-trisphosphate (PIP3). In mammals, there are four class I PI3K, three of which (alpha, beta, and delta) are primarily activated by tyrosine kinase signaling pathways, whereas PI3Kgamma is the principal PI3K that relays signals via GPCRs to downstream pathways such as AKT and ERK/1/2 [7]. PI3Kgamma-/- mice (lacking the catalytic subunit p110) are viable and in certain experimental settings exhibit reduced inflammation [8], [9] as the PI3Kgamma pathway controls migration of inflammatory cells [10]. In addition, we have previously shown that PI3Kgamma acts as an intrinsic negative regulator of cardiac contractility [11], [12]. Given its dual roles in cardiomyocyte function and inflammation, it is not clear if the net effect of PI3Kgamma loss is cardio-protective or cardio-destructive during M I/R. For example, we have previously shown that PI3Kgamma-/- mice are resistant to the detrimental effects of chronic beta-adrenergic receptor signaling [13]. Conversely, PI3Kgamma-/- mice showed a worse outcome both in a model of chronic myocardial ischemia without reperfusion [14] and chronic pressure overload [12], while overexpression of catalytically inactive PI3Kgamma protected from heart failure in this model [15]. Moreover, it has been reported that a PI3Kgamma blocker might protect from M I/R injury due to inhibition of the inflammatory response during reperfusion [16]. However, since such blockers are not specific for PI3Kgamma and PI3Kgamma inhibitors are currently developed as a novel treatment option for M I/R in humans, it is paramount to genetically define the role of PI3Kgamma in cardiac ischemia/reperfusion injury.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 75 78 67 70 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 656 661 632 637 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">KD/KD</sup>
###xml 673 678 645 650 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">KD/KD</sup>
###xml 828 835 796 803 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 79 83 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 117 121 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 585 589 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 679 683 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 730 734 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We therefore assessed the role of PI3Kgamma in M I/R injury using PI3Kgamma-/- mice. We report that PI3Kgamma mutant mice develop massive early and late stage cardiac M I/R injury. PI3Kgamma seems to be the key kinase that activates the Reperfusion Injury Salvage Kinase (RISK) pathway (AKT/ERK1/2) in the acute setting of M I/R. Using bone marrow chimeras these effects were mechanistically mapped to non-haematopoetic cells suggesting that the effects are intrinsic to cardiac tissue. To assess whether these effects are dependent on catalytic activity, we performed M I/R injury in mice with a knock-in mutation in the PI3Kgamma kinase domain (PI3KgammaKD/KD). PI3KgammaKD/KD mice developed cardiac injury similar to wild type mice. Thus, PI3Kgamma protects the heart from M I/R injury through a kinase-independent mechanism in vivo.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Results
###end title 12
###begin title 13
Genetic Inactivation of PI3Kgamma Results in Severe Cardiac Damage to M I/R Injury
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 62 69 58 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 126 129 118 121 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 338 341 322 325 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 459 468 443 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009350-g001">Figure 1A</xref>
###xml 577 586 561 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009350-g001">Figure 1B</xref>
###xml 692 695 672 675 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 760 771 740 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009350-g001">Figure 1C&#8211;E</xref>
###xml 135 139 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 175 179 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 181 183 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">WT</span>
###xml 342 346 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To assess the role of PI3Kgamma in M I/R injury, we performed in vivo reversible LAD ligation in sex and age matched PI3Kgamma-/- (KO) mice and PI3Kgamma expressing wild type mice (WT). The extent of injury and heart functions were assayed 3 hours, 24 hours, 1 week, and 3 weeks after initiation of reperfusion. Intriguingly, in PI3Kgamma-/- mice serum Troponin T levels, a marker of cardiac damage, were significantly increased after 3 hours of reperfusion (Figure 1A), without a significant change in the number of inflammatory cells infiltrating the damaged cardiac tissue (Figure 1B). In parallel, we observed a massive increase of the infarction size within the area at risk in PI3Kgamma-/- hearts at the early stage of M I/R injury (assayed at 24 hours, Figure 1C-E). Thus, immediately after M I/R the loss of PI3Kgamma expression results in massive early heart damage.
###end p 14

It was possible that in the long term the early cardioprotective effects of PI3Kgamma would not outweigh secondary pro-inflammatory effects due to impaired migration of inflammatory cells into the damaged heart; therefore, the net result of disrupting PI3Kgamma expression could be cardioprotective. To address this possibility we performed M I/R injury on wild type (WT) and PI3Kgamma-/- (KO) mice and followed these mice for up to three weeks. One week after M I/R injury, PI3Kgamma-/- mice still exhibited a massive increase in infarction size as compared to the control cohort (Figure 2A,B). Again we observed no significant differences in the numbers of inflammatory cells infiltrating the cardiac tissue between control and PI3Kgamma-/- mice (Figure 2C), suggesting that PI3Kgamma is not required for recruitment of inflammatory cells to the infarction site during the early (3 hours) as well as later stages (1 week) stages of M I/R injury.

Following the inflammatory phase of M I/R injury, scar formation occurs at the infarction site. In both wild type and PI3Kgamma-/- mice the primary damage site was replaced by scar tissue 3 weeks after the initial M I/R injury (Figure 2D). In line with the increased injury, collagen deposition and scar formation was markedly increased in PI3Kgamma-/- mice (Figure 2D, E). We next assessed cardiac function 3 weeks after M I/R injury. Functionally, the excessive early damage and increased scar formation in M I/R injured PI3Kgamma-/- mice culminated in a severe reduction in cardiac contractility as defined by changed fractional shortening from the baseline (Figure 2F). As previously reported [11], [13], wild type and PI3Kgamma-/- mice exhibit comparable baseline phosphorylation of both AKT and ERK1/2. Following 30 minutes of ischemia due to in vivo LAD ligation, AKT and ERK1/2 activation were comparably reduced in both genotypes. Ischemia-induced decrease in AKT and ERK1/2 phosphorylation was rapidly restored in wild type animals within 40 minutes of reperfusion. By contrast, in PI3Kgamma-/- mice AKT and ERK1/2 phosphorylation were still markedly reduced 40 minutes after reperfusion (Figure 4A, B). These marked differences in AKT and ERK1/2 activation were not apparent after 24 hours of reperfusion, indicating a role of PI3Kgamma in the early initiation of pro-survival signaling through AKT and ERK1/2. Immunohistochemical staining to detect phosphorylated AKT (S473) showed that active AKT is localized to the area at risk of wild type hearts (Figure 4C). Of note, in vivo M I/R did not alter the PI3Kgamma protein content in the early phase of reperfusion (Figure 4D). Thus, PI3Kgamma-/- mice fail to activate AKT and ERK1/2 immediately following M I/R injury, and this lack of activation can be localized to the area at risk.

In addition to AKT and ERK1/2 activation, PI3Kgamma is a regulator of myocardial contractility [11] via regulation of phosphodiesterases and subsequent phospholamban (PLB) phosphorylation in the sarcoplasmatic reticulum [12], [18]. Phosphorylation of PLB results in increased contractility [11]. Confirming previous results, PI3Kgamma-/- hearts exhibit a much higher basal level of phosphorylated PLB (Figure 4E). Whereas in wild type hearts phosphorylated PLB was decreased following ischemia and 40 minutes of reperfusion, such decrease in phosphorylated PLB was less pronounced in ischemic PI3Kgamma-/- hearts. Moreover, as compared to wild type control mice, in PI3Kgamma-/- mice phosphorylation of PLB was restored to basal levels during the vulnerable early stage (40 minutes) of reperfusion. Thus, loss of PI3Kgamma results in marked dysregulation of the RISK pathway and phospholamban.

PI3Kgamma exerts kinase-dependent and kinase-independent functions [12]. To address the issue of kinase dependency we subjected wild type, PI3Kgamma-/- mice, and PI3Kgamma kinase-dead knock-in mice (PI3KgammaKD/KD) to M I/R injury. However, PI3KgammaKD/KD mice exhibited a cardiac injury similar to wild type animals.

###begin title 36
Discussion
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 263 266 255 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Hirsch1">[8]</xref>
###xml 268 271 260 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Sasaki1">[9]</xref>
###xml 273 280 265 272 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vivo</italic>
###xml 291 294 279 282 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 389 392 377 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Hirsch1">[8]</xref>
###xml 394 397 382 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Sasaki1">[9]</xref>
###xml 432 436 420 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-DelPrete1">[19]</xref>
###xml 460 463 444 447 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 504 508 488 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Chang1">[20]</xref>
###xml 570 574 550 554 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Heit1">[21]</xref>
###xml 700 707 680 687 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 861 865 829 833 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Liu1">[22]</xref>
###xml 295 299 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 464 468 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In the current study we surprisingly observed little requirement for PI3Kgamma during inflammation associated with M I/R injury. Genetic evidence has implicated PI3Kgamma as a pro-inflammatory intracellular signaling component important for neutrophil chemotaxis [8], [9]. In vivo, PI3Kgamma-/- mice have been shown defective in diverse models of inflammation, including acute peritonitis [8], [9] and delayed type hypersensitivity [19]. In addition, PI3Kgamma-/- mice develop attenuated atherosclerosis [20]. While loss of PI3Kgamma alone may slow neutrophil migration [21], this loss does not appear to affect inflammation even in the early stages of our M I/R injury model. It has been shown that in vivo PI3Kgamma cooperates with PI3Kdelta to promote efficient neutrophil migration in response to chemokines and pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNF-alpha [22]. Most importantly, our bone marrow transplantation experiments exclude a requirement for PI3Kgamma in cardiac inflammation following M I/R injury.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 56 60 56 60 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Maroko1">[23]</xref>
###xml 62 66 62 66 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Cross1">[24]</xref>
###xml 116 119 116 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Hausenloy1">[2]</xref>
###xml 192 195 188 191 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 278 283 266 271 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">KD/KD</sup>
###xml 413 416 397 400 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 651 655 631 635 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Doukas1">[16]</xref>
###xml 657 661 637 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Rommel1">[25]</xref>
###xml 904 907 872 875 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1253 1257 1217 1221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Musiolik1">[26]</xref>
###xml 1259 1263 1223 1227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Skyschally1">[27]</xref>
###xml 1480 1488 1440 1448 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1488 1492 1448 1452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Ban1">[28]</xref>
###xml 196 200 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 225 229 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 644 650 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 908 912 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1332 1336 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1362 1366 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1591 1599 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Enhanced contractility by increased PLB phosphorylation [23], [24] and/or reduced RISK signaling via AKT and ERK1/2 [2] could contribute to the severe injury phenotype we observed in PI3Kgamma-/- mice. We found that knock-in mice lacking the PI3Kgamma kinase activity (PI3KgammaKD/KD) display an M I/R injury phenotype that is similar to wild type animals. Therefore, the detrimental outcome observed in PI3Kgamma-/- animals appears to be due to kinase-independent events, possibly via phospholamban hyperphospholryation and hypercontractility. PI3Kgamma blockers have entered clinical development for chronic inflammation and heart attacks in humans [16], [25]. Our data on PI3Kgamma in M I/R injury suggest that specific inhibition of PI3Kgamma will not result in any overall improvement concerning cardiac function and the extent of injury. Moreover, we observed a loss of RISK activation in PI3Kgamma-/- mice, suggesting that treatment with PI3Kgamma blockers may interfere with these early pro-survival signals in some contexts. Of note, the observed loss of immediate RISK activation itself does not appear to compromise M I/R outcome in our model. These data are consistent with other recent studies questioning the cardioprotective role of RISK [26], [27]. Similar to our first in vivo evidence on M I/R injury using mutant mice and kinase-dead knock-in mice, previous work has shown that PI3Kgamma is an important signaling pathway that controls ischemic preconditioning in vitro[28], arguing that if anything, strategies to activate, not inhibit, PI3Kgamma may improve outcome for patients with ischemic heart disease.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 71 78 67 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 193 196 185 188 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 84 89 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 197 201 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 423 427 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The present study provides evidence that PI3Kgamma is cardioprotective in vivo in a mouse model of myocardial ischemia/reperfusion. Surprisingly, the severe cardiac damage observed in PI3Kgamma-/- mice occurs very rapidly and is not influenced by PI3Kgamma expression within the hematopoietic compartment, suggesting that PI3Kgamma exerts a cell autonomous protective function in cardiomyocytes. Using kinase dead knock-in mice, these detrimental effects were mapped to be independent of PI3Kgamma lipid kinase activity. However, loss of PI3Kgamma catalytic activity does not translate into a beneficial outcome but could negatively influence RISK signaling during the acute phase of reperfusion. In conclusion, our genetic data indicate that specific inhibition of PI3Kgamma is not cardioprotective. Therefore, treatment of acute infarctions with PI3Kgamma inhibitors should proceed with caution.

###xml 194 198 186 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Metzler1">[29]</xref>

PI3Kgamma-/-, PI3KgammaKD/KD, and control mice were randomised into a three-hour, 24-hour, one-week, and three-week reperfusion groups and underwent in vivo reversible LAD ligation as described [29], [30]. Briefly, following sedation, tracheotomy, ventilation, and thoracotomy, the LAD was reversibly ligated aided by a PE tube. After 30 minutes of ischemia, the PE tube was removed and reperfusion was visually confirmed. The overall peri- and post-procedural mortality was below 10% (control mice: 5,7%; PI3Kgamma-/-: 6,1%; PI3KgammaKD/KD: 9,7%).
###end p 44
###begin title 45
Infarct Evaluation
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 1320 1324 1316 1320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009350-Griswold1">[31]</xref>
Heparinized blood was collected from the retrobulbar vein plexus. Plasma levels of troponinT were evaluated as a biomarker for cardiac damage using a quantitative assay (Roche Diagnostics, Austria). Moreover, hearts were fixed in 4% formaldehyde overnight and embedded in paraffin. Three 5-microm sections were cut from defined levels of the left ventricle, stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E). Infarct sizes were analyzed using Axio Imager M1 (Zeiss, Axiovert, Jena, Germany) and TissueFAXS software package (TissueGnostics, Vienna, Austria). In addition, formalin-fixed heart sections were stained with Trichrome and Aniline Blue to detect differences in collagen deposition and scar size. Using a light microscope (Axioskop2 equipped with AxioCam MRc5, Zeiss), Axio Vision 4.0 (Zeiss), and Adobe Photoshop CS3, the mean values of inflammatory infiltrate were determined within 3x40 visual fields of each area of infarction. To evaluate the size of the infarction in relation to the area at risk (AAR) and left ventricle size, we performed 2,3,5-triphenyltetrazolium chloride (TTC) staining: The LAD was firmly re-occluded after 3 hours of reperfusion and 0.5 ml of Evan's blue was injected into the left ventricle. The presence of Evan's blue indicates perfusion, whereas its absence indicates lack of perfusion [31]. Sections from the left ventricles were put into a 2% TTC solution (Sigma) and stained for 15 minutes under constant shaking. Whereas brick red areas indicate viable myocardium, white regions demarcate tissue injury. Left ventricle size (LV), area at risk (AAR) and infarct area (Inf) were assessed by a blinded investigator. Measurements from multiple heart slices were averaged and Inf/AAR and AAR/LV calculated.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
Echocardiography
###end title 47
###begin p 48
Myocardial contractility was determined by transthoracic echocardiography using a sector scanner (AcuNav 10MHz, Acuson Sequoia C256, Acuson Corporation Mountain View, CA) and Visualsonics Vevo 770. Short-axis 2D-targeted M-mode images of the left ventricle were obtained. Fractional shortening (FS) was calculated from digital images by using a standard formula.
