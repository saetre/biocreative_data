Bidirectional transcription of a novel chimeric gene mapping to mouse chromosome Yq
###end article-title 0

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 136 143 136 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1/2</italic>
###xml 145 149 145 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 153 157 153 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty</italic>
###xml 32 37 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The male-specific region of the mouse Y chromosome long arm (MSYq) contains three known highly multi-copy X-Y homologous gene families, Ssty1/2, Sly and Asty. Deletions on MSYq lead to teratozoospermia and subfertility or infertility, with a sex ratio skew in the offspring of subfertile MSYqdel males
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 70 74 70 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly</italic>
###xml 163 168 163 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 201 206 201 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1</italic>
###xml 208 213 208 213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty </italic>
###xml 217 221 217 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 247 254 247 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1- </italic>
###xml 258 262 258 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly-</italic>
###xml 375 379 375 379 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly</italic>
###xml 503 508 503 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 470 475 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We report the highly unusual genomic structure of a novel MSYq locus, Orly, and a diverse set of spermatid-specific transcripts arising from copies of this locus. Orly is composed of partial copies of Ssty1, Asty and Sly arranged in sequence. The Ssty1- and Sly-derived segments are in antisense orientation relative to each other, leading to bi-directional transcription of Orly. Genome search and phylogenetic tree analysis is used to determine the order of events in mouse Yq evolution. We find that Orly is the most recent gene to arise on Yq, and that subsequently there was massive expansion in copy number of all Yq-linked genes.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 76 80 76 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly</italic>
###xml 97 103 97 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos</italic>
###xml 201 206 201 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 210 217 210 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos </italic>
###xml 246 250 246 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 276 281 276 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 333 338 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Orly has an unprecedented chimeric structure, and generates both "forward" (Orly) and "reverse" (Orlyos) transcripts arising from the promoters at each end of the locus. The region of overlap of known Orly and Orlyos transcripts is homologous to Sly intron 2. We propose that Orly may be involved in an intragenomic conflict between mouse X and Y chromosomes, and that this process underlies the massive expansion in copy number of the genes on MSYq and their X homologues.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 367 368 367 368 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 369 370 369 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
The mammalian Y chromosome is constitutively haploid, restricted to males, and subject to ongoing genetic deterioration due to lack of recombinational exchange with a homologous partner. Set against this, however, there is strong evolutionary drive to preserve the function of male-benefit genes on the Y chromosome, and to acquire novel male-benefit genes on the Y [1-7]. These opposing effects lead to a heterogeneous structure of Y chromosomal DNA, with functional genes (often male specific, sometimes highly amplified) set among a sea of degenerate pseudogenes, repetitive sequence, and parasitic transposable elements.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 212 213 212 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 214 216 214 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 441 442 441 442 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 443 445 443 445 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 556 557 556 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 558 560 558 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 561 563 561 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 690 691 690 691 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 752 754 752 754 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 20 25 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 232 237 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The long arm of the mouse Y chromosome is a spectacular example of this process, being highly repetitive, transcriptionally silent in the majority of cell types, and yet indispensable for normal spermatogenesis [8-14]. Deletions on mouse Yq lead to teratozoospermia and reduced fertility. The severity of the phenotypes varies according to the extent of the deletion, with large deletions (> = 9/10 of Yq) resulting in complete infertility [8,13], while smaller deletions (~2/3 of Yq) result in reduced fertility and a less severe sperm shape abnormality [9,11,14]. Intriguingly, the offspring of males with 2/3 Yq deletions show an approximately 60:40 sex ratio skew in favour of females [9], and this is due to reduced efficiency of Y-bearing sperm [15].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 129 138 129 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly, Asty</italic>
###xml 140 142 140 142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 192 197 192 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1</italic>
###xml 311 317 311 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1 </italic>
###xml 321 325 321 325 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty</italic>
###xml 358 368 358 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty(rec) </italic>
###xml 369 371 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 77 82 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Recently, we have made considerable progress in defining the gene content of mouse Yq, identifying two new repeat gene families (Sly, Asty)[16] in addition to the one family previously known (Ssty1/2). During this work, we observed novel "recombinant" transcripts arising from loci that contain exons from both Ssty1 and Asty, and termed this new transcript Asty(rec) [16]. Here, we describe the detailed genomic arrangement of this rearranged locus and show expression of a large variety of transcriptional variants arising from these rearranged loci. These variant transcripts are differentially regulated during testis development.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 133 138 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We were also interested to know how these rearranged loci arose, and whether there were further examples of such "exon shuffling" on mouse Yq. We therefore compared the genomic organisation of the loci encoding all known Yq genes to each other and to their X-linked homologues, in order to more clearly delineate the composition of the novel rearranged loci, the differences between each of the Yq genes and their X-linked relatives, and the sequence of events involved in the genesis and amplification of these genes.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 77 87 77 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in silico </italic>
Finally, we investigated the wider genomic context of the rearranged loci by in silico mapping of the location of all known MSYq genes within the currently-released draft Y chromosome sequence contigs. The MSYq gene copies located by the mapping project were used to construct phylogenetic trees elucidating the sequence of events in MSYq evolution
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
A rearranged locus formed by chimerism between three Yq-specific genes
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 50 56 50 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1 </italic>
###xml 60 65 60 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty </italic>
###xml 97 107 97 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty(rec) </italic>
###xml 108 110 108 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 389 391 389 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 462 467 462 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1</italic>
###xml 494 498 494 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty</italic>
###xml 548 552 548 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 677 687 677 687 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty(rec) </italic>
###xml 827 832 827 832 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 833 834 833 834 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O</underline>
###xml 857 858 857 858 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">r</underline>
###xml 868 869 868 869 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">l</underline>
###xml 881 882 881 882 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y</underline>
While we previously reported the presence of both Ssty1 and Asty exons within the locus encoding Asty(rec) [16], further analysis of public genome sequence data shows this locus to be entirely composed of regions with high sequence similarity (greater than 90% across all regions) to members of all three known Yq-linked gene families. The structure of this novel locus is shown in Figure 1A. It comprises sequence homologous to exons 1, 2 and partial exon 3 of Ssty1, followed by exons 2-4 of Asty, followed by exons 1-2 (and partial intron 2) of Sly in the opposite orientation. Since the locus contains segments of all three Yq-linked gene families, we feel the designation Asty(rec) is no longer appropriate. Given its highly unusual composition, and the fact that it is transcribed in both directions, we propose the name Orly (Oppositely-transcribed, reassorted locus on the Y).

As previously detailed [16], a search of the nr database revealed two full-length cDNAs arising from Orly, both originating from the relict Ssty1 promoter. In this article we will refer to these transcripts as Orly_v1 (accession number [GenBank:]) and Orly_v2 ([Genbank:AK016790], referred to in our previous work as Asty(rec)). The splicing patterns of Orly_v1 and Orly_v2 are shown in Figure 1B.

###begin title 19
Orly generates a wide diversity of alternative splice variants
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 21 29 21 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly_v1 </italic>
###xml 33 41 33 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly_v2 </italic>
###xml 109 113 109 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly</italic>

###xml 224 226 224 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1C</xref>
###xml 247 248 247 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>

###xml 415 420 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The existence of the Orly_v1 and Orly_v2 transcripts indicates differential splicing of the central exons in Orly. We performed a screen using primers from the various exons present in Orly_v2 (locations indicated in Figure 1C, sequences in Table 1) in order to see what further Orly splice variants were expressed. All primer pairs that gave an RT-PCR product from adult testis were tested using a range of normal mouse testis RNAs from different ages post partum in order to resolve the developmental onset of expression for each splice variant form (Figure 2). Primer pairs S1.f2/N2.r2 and N1.f/N2.r2 were also tested against a range of tissues to check tissue specificity of expression, and proved to be testis specific (Figure 3).

###begin p 24
###xml 106 114 106 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly_v1 </italic>
###xml 169 174 169 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 278 279 278 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 421 427 421 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1 </italic>
###xml 440 445 440 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty </italic>
###xml 662 667 662 667 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
When using primer pairs directed at the outermost exons (S1 and N2), a single major band corresponding to Orly_v1 is observed, suggesting that this is the most abundant Orly transcript. Two faint larger bands were also detected by the S1.f2/N2.r2 primer pair (arrowed in Figure 3), however, we were unable to obtain sequence for these products. These upper bands are likely to represent transcripts including portions of Ssty1 exons 2/3 or Asty exons 2-4, as detected in the other reactions (see below). No larger bands were seen in the S1.f1/N2.r1 reaction. It is possible that the larger bands detected by the S1.f2/N2.r2 primer pair arises from copies of the Orly locus where the exon S1.f1 and/or N2.r1 primer binding sites are mutated.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 41 49 41 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly_v2 </italic>
###xml 204 209 204 209 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 530 535 530 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 554 556 554 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1D</xref>
###xml 708 716 708 716 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly_v1 </italic>
###xml 719 726 719 726 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly_v2</italic>
###xml 777 782 777 782 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
Using primer pairs directed at the other Orly_v2 exons gave a wide variety of bands. Many of the products generated from RT-PCR on adult testis were gel purified and sequenced to confirm which regions of Orly are included in each detected transcript. Unfortunately we were unable to generate clean sequence for the products produced by the S3a.f/A3.r primer pair. This is likely due to the presence of a large number of similarly-sized transcripts which cannot be separated on a gel. The resulting partial transcriptional map for Orly is shown in Figure 1D. In most cases, the sequenced bands correspond to spliced transcripts, however, the majority of the products do not conform to the splicing pattern of Orly_v1 or Orly_v2. It appears that there is a plethora of different Orly isoforms expressed at low levels, which are only detected when specific primers are used.
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 44 49 44 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 100 106 100 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1 </italic>
###xml 149 154 149 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1</italic>
###xml 191 196 191 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 283 288 283 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 342 346 342 346 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 389 394 389 394 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 449 454 449 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty </italic>
###xml 504 508 504 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty</italic>
###xml 528 533 528 533 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 607 612 607 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 724 728 724 728 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol </italic>
We investigated whether any of the detected Orly isoforms had any significant coding potential. The Ssty1 open reading frame is encoded by exon 3 of Ssty1, which is not fully included in any Orly transcriptional variant (though two shorter forms of this exon are variably included). Orly transcripts thus do not encode the SSTY1 protein. The Sly portion of the locus is in antisense, thus Orly transcripts cannot encode any portion of SLY. Finally, Asty does not contain any open reading frame, thus the Asty-related portion of Orly also has no coding potential. Further electronic searching of the various Orly transcriptional variants revealed no significant open reading frames other than a partial degenerate retroviral pol sequence (see below).
###end p 26
###begin title 27
###xml 51 57 51 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1 </italic>
###xml 61 64 61 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly</italic>
Orly retains potential promoter sequence from both Ssty1 and Sly
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 46 51 46 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 133 137 133 137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty</italic>
###xml 176 179 176 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly</italic>
###xml 350 350 350 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1"/>
###xml 353 361 353 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly_v1 </italic>
###xml 365 373 365 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly_v2 </italic>
###xml 417 423 417 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1 </italic>
###xml 433 435 433 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
The regions of high sequence identity between Orly and its various progenitor loci extend a further 5 kb into the upstream region of Ssty, and 3 kb into the upstream region of Sly, indicating that the rearranged locus has retained the proximal upstream promoter regions of both genes, in antisense orientation relative to each other (Additional File ). Orly_v1 and Orly_v2 are known to be transcribed from the relict Ssty1 promoter [16], which is thus shown to be functional.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 22 26 22 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 120 122 120 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 237 241 237 241 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 296 300 296 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly</italic>
###xml 384 385 384 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 422 426 422 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 430 435 430 435 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 557 561 557 561 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 702 708 702 708 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pgk-2 </italic>
###xml 718 720 718 720 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
Turning to the relict Sly promoter region, we conducted a search for transcription factor binding sites using TFSEARCH [17]. This showed that of the 42 predicted transcription factor binding located between -600 and +10 of the reference Sly sequence, 35 were present at the corresponding site in Orly, indicating retention of potentially functional promoter elements (Additional File 2). Overall sequence identity between Sly and Orly across this region is 95.7%. Significantly, the conserved elements include a GCCAAT box at position -161 of the reference Sly locus. This motif is a strong transcriptional signal, and is known to be present in other spermatid specific TATA-less promoters such as the Pgk-2 promoter [18].
###end p 29
###begin title 30
###xml 42 47 42 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
Both of the promoters at opposite ends of Orly are functional
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 66 70 66 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 100 105 100 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 246 252 246 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos</italic>
###xml 257 264 257 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos </italic>
###xml 404 405 404 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 422 429 422 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos </italic>
This electronic promoter analysis suggested to us that the relict Sly promoter at the 3' end of the Orly locus may have retained functionality, and be able to generate opposite-strand transcripts. We designate such opposite-strand transcripts as Orlyos. No Orlyos transcripts were present in the nr or dbEST databases. We used strand-specific RT-PCR to determine whether any of the bands shown in Figure 2 corresponded to Orlyos transcripts.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 128 132 128 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly</italic>
###xml 223 224 223 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 309 314 309 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 369 373 369 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 440 445 440 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 468 471 468 471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly</italic>
###xml 496 503 496 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos </italic>
Importantly, of the three bands obtained using the N1.f/N2.r2 primer pair, the lower band corresponds to a forward orientation (Orly) transcript, while the upper band corresponds to a reverse orientation transcript (Figure 4). Sequencing confirmed that this RT-PCR product shows 99% identity to the reference Orly genomic sequence over 622 nt, and only 91% identity to Sly over 464 nt. This demonstrates that this transcript arises from an Orly locus rather than from Sly, and constitutes a true Orlyos transcript. The middle band seen in this reaction is not always detected by RT-PCR, and we were unable to determine the direction of transcription of this band.

Of the other splice variants shown in Figure 2, all were confirmed by strand-specific RT-PCR to be "forward" (Orly) transcripts (data not shown). This is unsurprising as the primers were designed against the forward transcript Orly_v2. Orlyos transcripts must necessarily have different exon boundaries, which presumably do not include the majority of the primer locations included in our screen.
###end p 34
###begin title 35
###xml 22 27 22 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 76 80 76 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
The terminal exons of Orly derive from a retrovirus and are in antisense to Sly and Orlyos
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 18 23 18 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 105 109 105 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 121 123 121 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 179 184 179 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 244 251 244 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos </italic>
###xml 296 301 296 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 350 357 350 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos </italic>
###xml 385 389 385 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
Both of the known Orly transcripts terminate with two novel exons with antisense homology to intron 2 of Sly (see Figure 1A, exons N1 and N2). As discussed above, this section of Orly is also transcribed in the opposite orientation, generating Orlyos transcripts. Thus there is the potential for Orly transcripts to form dsRNA either by pairing with Orlyos transcripts or with nascent Sly transcripts.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 131 133 131 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 172 176 172 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 189 196 189 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly-F </italic>
###xml 521 526 521 526 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 565 570 565 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 630 638 630 638 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly_v1 </italic>
###xml 755 759 755 759 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol </italic>
###xml 851 855 851 855 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly</italic>
###xml 94 99 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Exons N1 and N2 derive from a partial degenerate retrovirus belonging to the MuRVY lineage of mouse Y chromosome specific repeats [19], which is embedded in this intron of Sly (see below). Orly-F transcripts terminate at the transcription stop site of this MuRVY-related element. We therefore deduce that although the MuRVY element is degenerate and does not encode a functional retrovirus, its transcriptional termination site has remained functional and become co-opted to form the transcriptional termination site for Orly forward transcripts. None of the known Orly transcripts contain any large open reading frames, however, Orly_v1 contains a short ORF running from bases 117-284. This ORF has 63% identity and 75% similarity over 49aa to a partial pol gene (data not shown), further demonstrating the retroviral origin of the terminal exons of Orly.
###end p 37
###begin title 38
###xml 55 60 55 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
Tissue- and developmental stage-specific expression of Orly isoforms
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 231 237 231 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1 </italic>
###xml 241 245 241 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 279 280 279 280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 281 283 281 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 284 286 284 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 395 400 395 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 967 972 967 972 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 1055 1063 1055 1063 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in situ </italic>
Orly transcripts are under tight transcriptional control. All variant forms (both forward and reverse) are only observed after day 19 of postnatal life, and thus are deduced to be spermatid-specific. This is to be expected as both Ssty1 and Sly promoters are spermatid specific [9,13,16]. The age of first appearance for each band varied from day 19 to 23, indicating differential regulation of Orly isoforms in successive spermatid stages. This variation was observed both between different primer pairs (e.g. the majority of S1.f1/A4.r bands appear at 23 dpp, while the majority of S1.f1/A3.r bands appear at 21 dpp), and between different bands detected by the same primer pair (e.g. the upper, lower and middle bands in the N1.f/N2.r2 reaction appear at 19, 21 and 23 days respectively). This differential regulation may be due to spermatid stage dependent splicing of transcripts, or may represent varying subsets of transcripts arising from different copies of Orly with subtly different promoter activities. It is unfortunately not possible to use in situ or Northern blot data to confirm the detailed cellular expression patterns of these transcripts, since there is no portion of any of them which is not also part of a different Y-linked gene or retrovirus with a confounding expression pattern.
###end p 39

The structure of the reference genomic loci encoding Ssty1/2 is shown in Figure 5 (see Additional File 3 for ClustalW alignment). Sequence identity is 82.7% across the locus as a whole. The structure of the reference genomic loci for Xmr and Xlr is shown in Figure 6. Xmr has two transcriptional variants arising from alternative transcription start sites, as indicated in this figure. ###begin p 53
###xml 40 44 40 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>

The LINE element present in intron 2 of Sly is interrupted by a stretch of DNA with distant sequence similarity to the mouse MSYq-specific retrovirus, MuRVY. This LINE element is uninterrupted in the progenitor Xmr, thus we conclude that the MuRVY insertion occurred subsequent to the creation of Sly. The MuRVY-related sequence is inserted in antisense orientation relative to Sly itself. The extent of the MuRVY-related stretch of DNA varies between Sly copies (see phylogenetic tree analysis below), but in all cases the terminal portion (including MuRVY transcription termination site) is retained. RepeatMasker analysis [21] of the insert shows 13.1% divergence, 13.1% deletion and 3.2% insertion relative to the consensus MuRVY LTR sequence, and 32.5% divergence for non-LTR portions of the insert. As discussed above, the MuRVY-related sequence in Sly intron 2 forms the source for the terminal Orly exons.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 32 36 32 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xmr </italic>
###xml 40 44 40 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 85 88 85 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xlr</italic>
###xml 107 109 107 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 152 155 152 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xlr</italic>
###xml 302 305 302 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly</italic>
###xml 313 317 313 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xmr </italic>
###xml 363 367 363 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xlr </italic>
###xml 435 439 435 439 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xmr </italic>
###xml 443 447 443 447 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
Recent work has shown that both Xmr and Sly are cytoplasmic proteins, in contrast to Xlr, which is nuclear[22]. The KRKR nuclear localisation signal in Xlr, which is conserved from the autosomal progenitor gene SCP3, is located in exon 5. This signal is mutated to KRKW in the corresponding portion of Sly, while Xmr does not include this exon at all. Thus while Xlr retains the ancestral nuclear pattern of protein localisation, both Xmr and Sly have become cytoplasmic via different mechanisms.
###end p 54
###begin title 55
###xml 22 31 22 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty/Astx</italic>

###end title 55
###begin p 56

###xml 373 378 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Using the fingerprints as a guide, we were able to assemble a "super-contig" containing 3 copies of Ssty1, 3 copies of Ssty2, two copies of Asty, two copies of Sly and one copy of Orly. This "super-contig" indicates the presence on mouse Yq of a highly amplified repeat unit of greater than 500 kb in length, which presumably corresponds to the Huge Repeat Array reported at conferences by Alfoldi et al [23]. Sequence identity between the various contigs contributing to this "super-contig" is very high (> 98% excluding indels), indicating substantial homogeneity between copies of the Huge Repeat.

###begin title 69
Dynamics of Yq gene family expansion
###end title 69
###begin p 70
###xml 52 71 52 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1/2, Asty, Sly </italic>
###xml 75 79 75 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly</italic>
###xml 120 125 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
A key question is whether these four gene families (Ssty1/2, Asty, Sly and Orly) were amplified separately on Yq during mouse evolution, or whether there was a single period of amplification increasing the copy number of all genes simultaneously.

###begin p 81
###xml 599 600 599 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S5">5</xref>

From these trees, we also observed that all genes within each family showed very similar degrees of divergence from the root of the tree in all cases. This is to be expected as all three trees were based on noncoding sequence. The sequence used to build the trees is thus likely to be evolving at nearly neutral rates. Given nearly neutral rates of evolution, the degree of sequence divergence forms a "molecular clock" indicating the timing of the various events on mouse Yq. We therefore also generated trees using the UPGMA algorithm, which explicitly assumes a molecular clock (Additional Files 5, 6, 7).

The percentage divergence between the Orly branches of the tree (representing the date of amplification of the Huge Repeat Array) is 0.47% for the Ssty1-derived region, 0.41% for the Asty-derived region and 0.43% for the Sly-derived region. While the absolute rate of the clock cannot be determined from these data, the numbers obtained from the three trees are in good agreement with each other, strengthening our inferences of the timing of events on Yq.

4) Moderate amplification of Ssty1, Ssty2 and Asty
###end p 88
###begin p 89
###xml 17 22 17 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 43 48 43 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1</italic>

###end p 89
###begin p 90
###xml 144 149 144 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 227 233 227 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1 </italic>

6) Massive amplification of two familes of large-scale repeat on Yq. The first repeat family contains representatives of all Yq genes including Orly and constitutes the Huge Repeat Array, while the second specifically contains Ssty1 and Asty.
###end p 90
###begin p 91
###xml 154 158 154 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>

At present unresolved is the question of when the MuRVY retrovirus arrived on Yq. The presence of MuRVY-related sequence within intron 2 of every copy of Sly indicates that Sly acquired its MuRVY-derived insert in intron 2 some time between stages (3) and (6), however, the origin of MuRVY itself cannot be placed in the above sequence from available evidence.

###begin title 92
Discussion
###end title 92
###begin p 93
###xml 36 41 36 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 116 121 116 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 260 264 260 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly </italic>
###xml 354 358 354 358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xmr </italic>
###xml 380 383 380 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xlr</italic>
###xml 443 446 443 446 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xmr</italic>
###xml 644 649 644 649 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 758 765 758 765 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos </italic>
###xml 792 797 792 797 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 1038 1041 1038 1041 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly</italic>
We report here on the genomic locus Orly and the wide variety of alternatively spliced transcripts arising from it. Orly has a complex and unusual genomic structure, being derived from partial copies of three other Yq-linked genes. Intriguingly, we also found Sly to be derived by combination of existing genes, in this case a fusion of the 5' region of Xmr with the 3' region of Xlr, together with an internal duplication of exons 3-4 of the Xmr-derived segment. This may indicate that chimerism and "exon shuffling" are a general feature of novel Y chromosome gene creation. Significantly, the two outermost partial gene loci contributing to Orly are in antisense orientation relative to each other, and retain their upstream promoter regions. We detected Orlyos transcripts in addition to Orly transcripts, and thus deduce that both promoters have retained their activity. In particular, exons N1, N2 and the intervening intron are transcribed in both directions. This region derives from a MuRVY retroviral insertion into intron 2 of Sly.
###end p 93
###begin p 94
###xml 53 62 53 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Stellate </italic>
###xml 72 95 72 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 164 173 164 173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Stellate </italic>
###xml 187 195 187 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Su(Ste) </italic>
###xml 209 211 209 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 229 237 229 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Stellate</italic>
###xml 348 350 348 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 351 353 351 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 366 374 366 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Su(Ste) </italic>
###xml 449 457 449 457 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Su(Ste) </italic>
###xml 461 470 461 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Stellate </italic>
###xml 511 513 511 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 514 516 514 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 530 535 530 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 539 546 539 546 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos </italic>
###xml 605 608 605 608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly</italic>
###xml 682 688 682 688 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos</italic>
###xml 728 735 728 735 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1- </italic>
###xml 738 743 738 743 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty-</italic>
###xml 72 95 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
There is an intriguing parallel to be drawn with the Stellate system in Drosophila melanogaster, where there is a sense/antisense regulatory loop between X-encoded Stellate and Y-encoded Su(Ste) repeat genes [25]. In the case of Stellate, the Y gene arose from the X gene by insertion of a transposon (with active promoter) in reverse orientation [26,27]. Antisense Su(Ste) transcripts primed from the transposon promoter act to regulate both sense Su(Ste) and Stellate transcript levels via an RNAi mechanism [25,28]. Similarly, Orly and Orlyos transcripts could potentially regulate each other and also Sly. A key avenue of future work is to determine the full length sequence of Orlyos, in particular whether it contains any Ssty1- or Asty-derived regions which may in turn regulate these genes.
###end p 94
###begin p 95
###xml 18 27 18 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Stellate </italic>
###xml 159 167 159 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Su(Ste) </italic>
###xml 170 181 170 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 268 277 268 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Stellate </italic>
###xml 296 298 296 298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 301 310 301 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Stellate </italic>
###xml 355 357 355 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 358 360 358 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 394 396 394 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 490 491 490 491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 492 494 492 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 495 497 495 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 547 549 547 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 618 619 618 619 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 620 622 620 622 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 89 93 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 407 411 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 629 633 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The comparison to Stellate is especially interesting given the sex ratio skewing in male mice bearing partial Yq deletions. Partial deletions of the repressor Su(Ste) on Drosophila Y chromosome lead to sex ratio skewing or infertility dependent upon the X chromosomal Stellate haplotype present [29]. Stellate was hypothesised to be a meiotic drive gene [30,31], although this is now disputed [32]. In male mice, partial deletions of Yq lead to mild teratozoospermia and sex ratio skewing [9,11,14], with reduced effectiveness of Y-bearing sperm [15]. Larger deletions lead to severe teratozoospermia and infertility [8,13]. The mice with partial deletions show normal fertility and fecundity (in terms of number of successful matings and number of offspring per litter), thus the only effect of the decrease in Yq gene copy number appears to be the sex ratio skew.
###end p 95
###begin p 96
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 495 497 495 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 1014 1016 1014 1016 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 1062 1083 1062 1083 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus musculus musculus</italic>
###xml 1084 1108 1084 1108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus musculus molossinus </italic>
###xml 1141 1143 1141 1143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 51 56 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 661 666 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1062 1083 <span type="species:ncbi:39442">Mus musculus musculus</span>
###xml 1084 1107 <span type="species:ncbi:57486">Mus musculus molossinus</span>
It should be understood that the sex ratio skew in mouse with Yq deletions does not constitute meiotic drive in the classical sense, since equal numbers of X- and Y-bearing gametes are generated at meiosis [15]. Nevertheless, the presence of Yq-encoded genes affecting sex ratio indicates the potential for a conflict between these Yq-encoded genes and other interacting X- or autosomally-encoded factors. Given that Yq deletion also leads to a spermatid-specific derepression of X transcripts [33], with increasing X gene expression correlated with the extent of the deletion, we have suggested that there may indeed be an ongoing genomic conflict between the mouse X and Y chromosomes, with X-linked sex ratio distorter genes acting to favour generation of female offspring, and Yq-linked repressor genes acting to restore a normal 50:50 sex ratio. Such an intragenomic conflict is expected to lead to massive amplification of gene number on both chromosomes due to an "arms race" between the conflicting genes [34]. Intriguingly, the hybrid sterility seen in Mus musculus musculus/Mus musculus molossinus consomic strains is X-dependent [35].
###end p 96
###begin p 97
###xml 346 350 346 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly</italic>
###xml 336 341 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Whether genomic conflict is involved or not, the fact that Yq-encoded genes are necessary for normal levels of Y chromosome transmission necessarily leads to a strong and direct evolutionary pressure to maintain the function of these genes. This may be one of the factors behind the recent and highly unusual gene amplification seen on mouse Yq. Orly, being composed of portions of all the other known MSYq-linked genes, must also necessarily be the most recent known addition to MSYq gene content.
###end p 97
###begin title 98
Conclusion
###end title 98
###begin p 99
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 107 112 107 112 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 116 123 116 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orlyos </italic>
###xml 241 246 241 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ssty1</italic>
###xml 247 252 247 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asty </italic>
###xml 256 259 256 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sly</italic>
###xml 317 322 317 322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 451 456 451 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 611 618 611 618 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">en bloc</italic>
###xml 689 694 689 694 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Orly </italic>
###xml 34 39 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 904 909 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Orly is a novel chimeric locus on mouse chromosome Yq which is bidirectionally transcribed, giving rise to Orly and Orlyos transcripts. These transcripts may potentially form dsRNA in partnership with each other, or with the progenitor loci Ssty1,Asty and Sly. A phylogenetic tree analysis of Yq genes indicates that Orly arose shortly prior to a massive expansion in copy number of all the Yq-linked genes. Also, potentially significantly, copies of Orly are only found in the context of the Huge Repeat Array that distinguishes MSYq - a particular segment of around 500 kb that appears to have been amplified en bloc. Taking the above evidence together, we propose that the emergence of Orly may have been one of the triggers that led to massive amplification of Yq sequence. Further analysis of the genomic complement of MSYq, and the copy number of the corresponding X genes, in a range of different mouse subspecies should help date these events more precisely, and establish whether X-Y genomic competition is a contributing factor to the gene amplifications.
###end p 99
