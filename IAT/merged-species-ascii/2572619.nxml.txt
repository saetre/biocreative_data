###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 16 38 16 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plasmodium falciparum </italic>
###xml 76 78 76 78 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM</sub>
###xml 116 129 116 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum</italic>
###xml 270 273 270 273 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 296 300 296 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 396 400 396 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 421 424 421 424 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UM </sub>
###xml 476 480 476 480 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 613 627 613 627 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum </italic>
###xml 16 37 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
###xml 116 129 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
###xml 225 233 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 613 626 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
A subset of the Plasmodium falciparum erythrocyte membrane protein 1 (PfEMP1SM) is involved in the cytoadherence of P. falciparum-infected red blood cells (iRBC) contributing to the pathogenesis of severe disease among young children in malaria endemic areas. The PfEMP1SM are encoded by group A var genes that are composed of a more constrained range of amino acid sequences than groups B and C var genes encoding PfEMP1UM associated with uncomplicated malaria. Also, unlike var genes from groups B and C, those from group A do not have sequences consistent with CD36 binding - a major cytoadhesion phenotype of P. falciparum isolates.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 12 15 12 15 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 28 30 28 30 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM</sub>
###xml 259 262 259 262 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 377 381 377 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 497 500 497 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var</italic>
A 3D7 PfEMP1SM sub-line (3D7SM) expressing VAR4 (PFD1235w/MAL8P1.207) was selected for binding to CD36. The protein expression of this parasite line was monitored by surface staining of iRBC using VAR4-specific antibodies. The serological phenotype of the 3D7SM parasites was determined by flow cytometry using malaria semi-immune and immune plasma and transcription of the 59 var genes in 3D7 were analysed by real-time quantitative reverse transcriptase-polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) using var-specific primers.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 45 48 45 48 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 83 88 83 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var4 </italic>
A selection-induced increased adhesion of 3D7SM iRBC to CD36 resulted in a reduced var4 transcription and VAR4 surface expression.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusion
###end title 8
###begin p 9
VAR4 is not involved in CD36 adhesion. The current findings are consistent with the notion that CD36 adhesion is not associated with particular virulent parasite phenotypes, such as those believed to be exhibited by VAR4 expressing parasites.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Background
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 38 59 38 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plasmodium falciparum</italic>
###xml 162 163 162 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 164 165 164 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 268 269 268 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 270 272 270 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 349 351 349 351 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 352 354 352 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 38 59 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
Variant surface antigens (VSA) on the Plasmodium falciparum-infected red blood cells (iRBC) are involved in both cytoadherence contributing to disease pathology [1-8] and immune evasion which probably contributes to the severity and persistence of malaria infections [9-11]. Anti-VSA antibodies have been shown to contribute to protective immunity [12-16] and data from several studies indicate that some important targets for immunity seem to have restricted heterogeneity.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 4 26 4 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plasmodium falciparum </italic>
###xml 127 131 127 131 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 201 203 201 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 204 206 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 356 358 356 358 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 359 361 359 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 362 364 362 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 513 515 513 515 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UM</sub>
###xml 623 626 623 626 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 712 714 712 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 725 728 725 728 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 820 824 820 824 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 830 835 830 835 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var4 </italic>
###xml 836 855 836 855 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PFD1235w/MAL8P1.207</italic>
###xml 857 859 857 859 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 885 889 885 889 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 1109 1111 1109 1111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 4 25 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
###xml 592 597 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 702 710 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 979 987 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 1066 1074 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The Plasmodium falciparum Erythrocyte Membrane Protein-1 (PfEMP1) is the most studied VSA. PfEMP1 molecules are encoded by the var gene family comprising 50-60 highly diverse genes per haploid genome [17-22]. Any single parasite nuclei transcribes only one variant at a time, a phenomenon referred to as allelic exclusion or mutually exclusive expression [20,23-26]. Cultures of unselected 3D7 parasites predominantly express PfEMP1 mRNA species resembling those of parasites causing uncomplicated malaria (PfEMP1UM). The dominant serological phenotype (recognition by semi-immune and immune human plasma) changes to PfEMP1SM following selection of 3D7 using DynaBeads coated with IgG from semi-immune children [27]. This 3D7SM shows transcriptional upregulation and protein surface expression of one particular group A var gene, var4 (PFD1235w/MAL8P1.207)[28]. More recently, group A var genes, together with certain other B/A type were found to be transcribed in isolates from children with cerebral malaria, but not from isolates from equally highly parasitaemic patients without severe malaria syndromes [29].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 8 12 8 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 68 72 68 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 398 400 398 400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 401 403 401 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 424 428 424 428 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 466 470 466 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 600 602 600 602 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 693 697 693 697 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 731 732 731 732 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 733 735 733 735 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
Group A var genes are one of three major groups (A, B and C) of 3D7 var gene sequences categorized according to chromosomal location, gene orientation, domain structure of the encoded proteins and similarities in coding and non-coding regions. Group A contains seven genes encoding large PfEMP1s of about 400 kDa, with complex domain arrangements and three genes encoding PfEMP1s of about 150 kDa [30,31]. Unlike most other var genes from group B and C, the Group A var genes do not encode sequences thought to be consistent with binding to CD36, a major endothelial receptor for iRBC sequestration [31]. It should be noted however that the correlation between severity, binding phenotype and var gene expression is not clear-cut [4,32].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 51 55 51 55 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 173 176 173 176 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
In this study, changes in the transcription of all var genes and surface expression levels of VAR4 as a result of drifting and changes following selection for binding of 3D7SM to CD36 was investigated.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Methods
###end title 15
###begin title 16
Parasites
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 55 69 55 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum </italic>
###xml 139 141 139 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 186 193 186 193 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-CD36</sub>
###xml 197 206 197 206 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-drift </sub>
###xml 213 215 213 215 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UM</sub>
###xml 55 68 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
All parasites used in this study were derived from the P. falciparum isolate, 3D7 and were cultured in O Rh+ RBCs as described previously [33]. Three 3D7 sub-lines were used, namely: 3D7SM-CD36,3D7SM-drift and 3D7UM.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 3 11 3 11 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-CD36 </sub>
###xml 42 45 42 45 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 102 105 102 105 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 202 204 202 204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 205 207 205 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 242 245 242 245 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 282 284 282 284 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 332 335 332 335 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 570 573 570 573 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 48 63 <span type="species:ncbi:10029">Chinese hamster</span>
###xml 192 200 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 471 476 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
3D7SM-CD36 was obtained by bio-panning 3D7SM on Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells expressing CD36. 3D7SM has previously been selected from 3D7 using a pool of plasma from semi-immune Ghanaian children [27,28]. To select for a CD36-binding 3D7SM population, the method described in [27] was followed. Gelatine enriched late-stage 3D7SM was panned on a monolayer of wild-type CHO cells. Unbound iRBC and uninfected RBC (uRBC) were then panned on CHO cells transfected with human CD36. The monolayer was washed repeatedly to remove unbound iRBC and uRBC. To allow bound 3D7SM parasites to reinvade and grow, culture medium and uRBC were added to the monolayer of CD36-transfected CHO cells and incubated overnight.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 3 6 3 6 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 109 117 109 117 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-drift</sub>
###xml 139 148 139 148 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-drift </sub>
###xml 215 217 215 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 233 241 233 241 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-CD36 </sub>
###xml 323 326 323 326 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UM </sub>
###xml 400 402 400 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
3D7SM was allowed to drift without routine selection using the Ghanaian plasma pool to obtain the subline 3D7SM-drift. For consistency, 3D7SM-drift and unselected 3D7 were late-stage enriched by gelatine flotation [34] every time 3D7SM-CD36 was selected for CD36 binding. The gelatine-enriched unselected 3D7 was termed 3D7UM and was serologically similar to parasites causing uncomplicated malaria [27].
###end p 19
###begin p 20
###xml 109 113 109 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">msp1</italic>
###xml 115 119 115 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">msp2</italic>
###xml 125 131 125 131 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glurp </italic>
###xml 137 139 137 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
The genotypic identity of the 3D7 isolate and its derived sub-lines were confirmed by PCR at the polymorphic msp1, msp2, and glurp loci [35].
###end p 20
###begin title 21
Immuno-staining of live cells and flow cytometry
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 66 68 66 68 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 69 71 69 71 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 271 273 271 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 274 276 274 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 336 337 336 337 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 464 466 454 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 1184 1186 1173 1175 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sup>
###xml 372 377 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 393 399 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 486 491 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 611 615 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 621 626 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 635 641 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 671 675 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 825 830 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1037 1045 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 1143 1148 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1239 1244 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1329 1335 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 1341 1346 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Immuno-staining and flow cytometry were carried out as described [28,33] with minor modifications. To monitor VAR4 expression on the surface of live cells, late stage-iRBC were enriched by magnet-activated cell sorting (MACS, Miltenyl BioTec, Bergisch Gladbach, Germany)[36,37]. MACS purified and ethidium bromide-labeled iRBC (2.5 x 105) was incubated for 1 hr in 20 muL mouse sera or 40 muL rabbit sera raised against the CIDR1alpha or DBL5delta domains of VAR4[28] depleted for anti-human RBC antibodies prior to use. To detect surface bound antibodies, the iRBC were sequentially exposed to 100 muL of 1:25 goat anti-mouse or anti-rabbit Ig (DAKO), biotinylated anti-goat Ig (DAKO) and 1:1,000 fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)-conjugated streptavidin (BD Pharmingen) for 30 minutes each. For serological phenotyping, 20 human plasma samples from Magoda village in Tanzania with high malaria transmission were used to determine the recognition profile of the various parasite lines. Out of the 20 Magoda plasma samples, 10 came from children aged 3-5 years and 10 came from adolescents and adults aged 12-63 years. Staining of the iRBC by human IgG was done by incubating 2.5 x 105 MACS purified ethidium bromide-labeled iRBC in 5 muL human plasma for 30 minutes and then sequentially exposed to 100 muL of 1:50 biotinylated rabbit anti-human IgG (DAKO) and 1:2000 FITC-conjugated streptavidin (DAKO) for 30 minutes each. All immuno-stained samples were analysed in a Coulter EPICS XL-MCL flow cytometer (Coulter Electronics, Luton, UK) or in a FACScan (Becton Dickinson). Data were analysed in WinMDI software  and WinList version 5.0 (Verity Software House, Inc., Maine, USA). Student's t-test was used to determine whether the samples compared were significantly different (P = </=0.05).
###end p 22
###begin title 23
RNA extraction, cDNA synthesis and Quantitative Real-Time PCR
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 97 99 97 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 100 102 100 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 187 196 187 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 445 449 445 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 469 471 469 471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 1017 1021 993 997 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 1034 1048 1010 1024 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum </italic>
###xml 1060 1062 1036 1038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 1063 1065 1039 1041 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 1303 1324 1277 1298 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">seryl-tRNA synthetase</italic>
###xml 1521 1523 1495 1497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 1595 1599 1569 1573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 1034 1051 <span type="species:ncbi:36329">P. falciparum 3D7</span>
RNA extraction, cDNA synthesis and quantitative real-time PCR were done as previously described [28,38]. Trophozoite/schizont-iRBC (36-48 h after invasion) were isolated from mixed stage in vitro cultures by exposure to a strong magnetic field as described in the previous section. To obtain ring stage parasites (30 h), the late stage enriched iRBC were cultured overnight. The ring stage has previously been shown to be optimal for studies of var gene transcription [38] and is therefore used for RNA extraction. Total RNA was purified using Trizol (Invitrogen) and treated with DNAseI (Invitrogen) for 15 min at 37degreesC. Superscript II was used to reverse transcribe DNA-free RNA primed with random hexamer primers (Invitrogen) at 25degreesC for 10 min, 42degreesC for 50 min followed by 70degreesC for 15 min. Quantitative real-time PCR was done using a Rotorgene thermal cycler system (Corbett Research, Motlake, Australia) and real-time PCR-optimized and gene-specific primers for each of the 59 full-length var genes in the P. falciparum 3D7 genome [28,38]. Reactions were performed in 20 mul volumes using QuantiTect SYBR Green PCR master mix (Qiagen, Merck Eurolab, Albertslund, Denmark) and 1 muM primers. Quantification was done using Rotorgene software version 4.6. The housekeeping gene seryl-tRNA synthetase, which shows a uniform transcription profile in different parasite isolates and an unchanged pattern throughout the parasite life cycle, was used as an endogenous control as previously described [38] and used for calculations of percent measured transcript out of total var transcipts by the DeltaDeltaCT method (User Bulletin no. 2, Applied Biosystems).
###end p 24
###begin title 25
CD36 binding assay
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 66 68 66 68 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 194 195 194 195 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 443 445 442 444 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">7 </sup>
###xml 289 294 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The CD36 binding assay was done as described in detail elsewhere [27]. To radiolabel parasites, cultures were incubated overnight in 10% non-immune Danish plasma in RPMI 1640 in the presence of 3H-hypoxanthine. Monolayers of wild type, CD36- and CD54-transfected CHO cells and transformed human bone marrow endothelial cells (TrHBMEC) were grown in 96-well microtiter plates (Nunc, Roskilde, Denmark). Late stage enriched iRBC (100 mul, 1 x 107 iRBC/ml) were added to the CHO cell monolayer and incubated for 1 hour at 37degreesC. After removal of unbound iRBC, the number of iRBCs adhering to the CHO cells was determined by liquid scintillation spectrometry.
###end p 26
###begin title 27
Results
###end title 27
###begin title 28
Reduced expression of VAR4 following selection for CD36 binding
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 179 181 175 177 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UM</sub>
###xml 186 194 182 190 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-CD36 </sub>
###xml 201 210 197 206 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-drift </sub>
###xml 302 304 298 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 357 359 353 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 365 368 361 364 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 403 406 399 402 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UM </sub>
###xml 492 496 488 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1i-A</xref>
###xml 512 515 508 511 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 527 532 523 528 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var4 </italic>
###xml 558 561 554 557 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UM </sub>
###xml 578 583 574 579 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var4 </italic>
###xml 611 615 607 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1i-B</xref>

###end title 41
###begin p 42

###xml 774 776 774 776 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 777 779 777 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 780 782 780 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 783 785 783 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 786 788 786 788 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 789 791 789 791 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 803 809 803 809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al </italic>
###xml 810 812 810 812 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 1235 1241 1235 1241 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al </italic>
###xml 1242 1244 1242 1244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 1308 1313 1308 1313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var4 </italic>
###xml 1435 1438 1435 1438 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 383 391 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 467 480 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
###xml 617 625 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
A previous study has shown that var4, a group A var gene, is highly transcribed and surface expressed in 3D7 parasites expressing PfEMP1SM (3D7SM) compared to 3D7 expressing PfEMP1UM (3D7UM), suggesting an association between VAR4 and severe malaria [28]. CD36 binding has in some studies been associated with disease severity. In a study in Kenya, a mutation in the CD36 gene among children has been associated with protection from severe malaria [39]. In addition, P. falciparum isolates from Thai adults suffering from severe but not cerebral malaria had higher CD36 adhesion than those from uncomplicated malaria patients [40]. In contrast, several studies are consistent with the notion that CD36 binding is not associated with particular virulent parasite phenotypes [27,28,30-32,41-43]. Robinson et al [31] has demonstrated in a heterologous expression assay that the CIDR1 region of VAR4 did not bind to CD36. There might be a conformational difference between the heterologous recombinant protein and the native protein presented on the iRBC or binding could be through a different domain. However, it remains that the current data on VAR4 expression on iRBC and CD36 binding phenotype support the prediction made by Robinson et al [31]. The current study has shown that VAR4 protein expression and var4 mRNA transcription are reduced following selection for CD36 binding, lending further support to the hypothesis that PfEMP1SM antigens such as VAR4 are not involved in CD36 binding.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 120 124 120 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 158 159 158 159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 160 162 160 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 250 252 250 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 253 255 253 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 287 293 287 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al </italic>
###xml 294 295 294 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 426 430 426 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 437 439 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
However, it is important to note that the correlation between severe malaria, the inability to bind to CD36 and group A var gene expression is not clear-cut [4,31]. ICAM-1 binding may be associated with cerebral malaria as shown by previous studies [43-45]. But as demonstrated by Smith et al [4], at least two group B PfEMP1 that bind to ICAM-1 also bind to CD36. In addition, not all severe malaria isolates express group A var genes [42].
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 16 19 16 19 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 57 62 57 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var4 </italic>
###xml 132 136 132 136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 151 159 151 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PFF0010w</italic>
###xml 173 177 173 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 200 203 200 203 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 217 222 217 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var4 </italic>
###xml 300 302 300 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 368 377 368 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PFF0010w </italic>
###xml 497 500 497 500 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 585 588 585 588 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
###xml 595 604 595 604 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-drift </sub>
###xml 612 619 612 619 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1i&#8211;iiiA</xref>
###xml 710 712 710 712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 713 715 713 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
Selection of 3D7SM for binding to CD36 lead to a reduced var4 gene transcription, but did not show any significant changes in other var genes as such. PFF0010w, a group B/A var gene upregulated in 3D7SM together with var4 was previously predicted to be a non-CD36 binder based on its CIDR1 sequence [31]. In the current study however, selection on CD36 did not reduce PFF0010w transcription as would be expected. The reason for this is that PFF0010w is probably not expressed on the surface of 3D7SM parasites as indicated by one distinct population of VAR4 expressing parasites in 3D7SM and 3D7SM-drift (Figure 1i-iiiA). Previous studies have shown that only one PfEMP1 is expressed on the surface at a time [24,46] so these VAR4 expressing parasites are less likely to be expressing PFF0010w. However, a PFF0010w-specific antibody is needed to confirm this.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 23 27 23 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 67 69 67 69 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 70 72 70 72 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 117 125 117 125 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-CD36 </sub>
###xml 229 231 229 231 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM</sub>
###xml 278 287 278 287 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-drift </sub>
###xml 294 301 294 301 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-CD36</sub>
###xml 419 427 419 427 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-CD36 </sub>
###xml 616 620 616 620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 662 670 662 670 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-CD36 </sub>
###xml 741 745 741 745 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 815 821 815 821 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1iii-B</xref>
Although Group B and C var genes have been linked to CD36 binding [30,31], new protein bands were not detected in 3D7SM-CD36 using Western blots and anti-ATS antibodies compared to the protein band pattern seen in extracts of 3D7SM. Furthermore, the serological phenotype of 3D7SM-drift and 3D7SM-CD36remained similar when analysed by flow cytometry. These results might be explained by the fact that only 8% of the 3D7SM-CD36 parasites bound to the CD36 receptor while transcription and serological analysis was done on the entire parasite population. With the present set up it is difficult to determine the exact var genes involved in the CD36 adhesion of 3D7SM-CD36 although a slight increase in the total transcription level of group B var genes was noted in this sub-line at the end of the experiment (Figure 1iii-B). In order to determine the PfEMP1 involved in CD36 binding, both transcription and protein expression data are required.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Var </italic>
###xml 153 158 153 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var4 </italic>
###xml 209 214 209 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1ii-A</xref>
###xml 219 221 219 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 297 302 297 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var4 </italic>
###xml 347 353 347 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1iii-A</xref>
###xml 358 360 358 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 498 503 498 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1iiiA</xref>
Var gene transcription does not always reflect phenotypic expression as observed in this study, where VAR4 protein expression was poorly correlated with var4 transcription after the third CD36 panning (Figure 1ii-A and 1B). However, the fifth CD36 panning resulted to a higher correlation between var4 transcription and protein expression (Figure 1iii-A and 1B) most likely due to the more homogenous population as indicated by the dominant peak of VAR4 negative parasites in the histogram (Figure 1iiiA).
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 24 28 24 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 103 105 103 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 106 108 106 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 131 135 131 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 215 217 215 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 297 301 297 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 473 477 473 477 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 685 687 685 687 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
Studies have shown that var gene transcription peaks during the ring stage at 3-18-hour post invasion [47,48] and that full length var transcript is not seen at all by Northern blotting at 24-34-hour post invasion [49]. In the current study, a 30-hour post invasion ring stage was used to measure var gene transcription. At 30 hours, the parasites appeared as non-pigmented thick ring forms under the microscope. An improved quantification of transcript abundances of each var gene obtained by quantitative real time PCR and the use of cross intron primers show that although the dominant full length transcript peaks at the ring stage, it remains dominant through out the life cycle [50].
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 34 38 34 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 97 105 97 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 169 171 169 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 193 195 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 340 349 340 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 587 593 587 593 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al </italic>
###xml 594 596 594 596 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 672 676 672 676 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 746 750 746 750 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 797 803 797 803 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al </italic>
###xml 804 806 804 806 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 842 847 842 847 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">upsA </italic>
###xml 927 931 927 931 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 963 968 963 968 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">upsB </italic>
###xml 983 988 983 988 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var4 </italic>
###xml 1007 1011 1007 1011 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 1024 1029 1024 1029 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">upsA </italic>
###xml 1096 1100 1096 1100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 1161 1165 1161 1165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">vars</italic>
In the absence of immune pressure var genes have been reported to have variable transition rates in vitro. There have been reports of transition rates as low as 0.025% [51] to as high as 2.4% [52] per generation. In the absence of CD36 selection and immune pressure, VAR4 surface expression is stable for more than six months in continuous in vitro culture with a transcription off rate of less than 0.005%. For a PfEMP1 implicated in severe disease and expected to dominate in a host with limited immunity, it may not be surprising that VAR4 appears to have a low switching rate. Frank et al [53] have recently observed that, in the 3D7 isogenic line NF54, sub-telomeric var genes have higher off rates of approximately 1% compared with central var genes with 0 to 0.3% off rates. However, Frank et al [53] did not observe any activation of upsA genes in their experiments hence the high switch off rates of the sub-telomeric var genes reported were that of the upsB promoter type. var4 is a sub-telomeric var gene with an upsA promoter suggesting the possibility that at least for some Group A var genes the switch rate might be comparable to that of central vars.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 8 17 8 17 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-drift </sub>
###xml 24 32 24 32 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-CD36 </sub>
###xml 90 99 90 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 165 174 165 174 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-drift </sub>
###xml 362 364 362 364 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM</sub>
###xml 408 411 408 411 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">UM </sub>
###xml 539 543 539 543 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">VAR4</sub>
###xml 689 691 689 691 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM</sub>
###xml 516 524 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
Both 3D7SM-drift and 3D7SM-CD36 drifted away from a serological SM phenotype at prolonged in vitro culture to a more UM like phenotype. However, surprisingly the 3D7SM-drift remained positive for VAR4 surface expression raising the question whether VAR4 is the only PfEMP1 molecule contributing to the SM phenotype of the original antibody selected parasite, 3D7SM. To clarify this, in the current study, 3D7UM was selected using VAR4 specific antibodies and found that only some and not all semi-immune plasma from children recognized 3D7VAR4. This is an important finding because it points to the possibility that VSAs other than VAR4 might contribute to the serological phenotype of 3D7SM.
###end p 49
###begin title 50
Conclusion
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 56 63 56 63 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM-CD36</sub>
###xml 191 194 191 194 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SM </sub>
This study shows that increased CD36 adhesiveness of 3D7SM-CD36resulted in a reduction in VAR4 surface expression. In the absence of CD36 selection and immune pressure, VAR4 expression by 3D7SM parasites is stable for at least six months while the population's serological phenotype drifts towards the UM phenotype. Results of this study shed more light on the characteristics of 3D7 parasites expressing VAR4 - a PfEMP1 previously associated with severe malaria phenotypes.
