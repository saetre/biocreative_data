###xml 55 60 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Site-Directed Mutations in the C-Terminal Extension of Human alphaB-Crystallin Affect Chaperone Function and Block Amyloid Fibril Formation
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: MW TT HE SM JC. Performed the experiments: TT HE DW. Analyzed the data: TT HE. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: MW JC. Wrote the paper: MW TT HE SM JC.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
Alzheimer's, Parkinson's and Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease are associated with inappropriate protein deposition and ordered amyloid fibril assembly. Molecular chaperones, including alphaB-crystallin, play a role in the prevention of protein deposition.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 41 46 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
A series of site-directed mutants of the human molecular chaperone, alphaB-crystallin, were constructed which focused on the flexible C-terminal extension of the protein. We investigated the structural role of this region as well as its role in the chaperone function of alphaB-crystallin under different types of protein aggregation, i.e. disordered amorphous aggregation and ordered amyloid fibril assembly. It was found that mutation of lysine and glutamic acid residues in the C-terminal extension of alphaB-crystallin resulted in proteins that had improved chaperone activity against amyloid fibril forming target proteins compared to the wild-type protein.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Together, our results highlight the important role of the C-terminal region of alphaB-crystallin in regulating its secondary, tertiary and quaternary structure and conferring thermostability to the protein. The capacity to genetically modify alphaB-crystallin for improved ability to block amyloid fibril formation provides a platform for the future use of such engineered molecules in treatment of diseases caused by amyloid fibril formation.
###end p 7

Introduction

###xml 1714 1717 1706 1709 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Ehrnsperger1">[8]</xref>
###xml 1801 1804 1793 1796 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Treweek1">[9]</xref>
###xml 2043 2047 2019 2023 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-deJong1">[10]</xref>
###xml 2268 2272 2244 2248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Kato1">[11]</xref>
###xml 2495 2498 2467 2470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Clark1">[4]</xref>
###xml 2500 2504 2472 2476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Klemenz1">[12]</xref>
###xml 2506 2510 2478 2482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Ochi1">[13]</xref>
###xml 2512 2516 2484 2488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-vanRijk1">[14]</xref>
The classic experiments of Anfinsen [1] on the folding of ribonuclease in vitro revealed that all the information required for folding of a polypeptide chain into its final native conformation is contained within the polypeptide chain itself. This is indeed evident with small proteins used for in vitro folding studies (e.g. barnase, 110 residues) which are able to refold to their active conformation in the absence of other proteins. In the case of large, multi-domain proteins encoded by long sequences, however, only a limited proportion achieve their native state unassisted [2]. Most are prevented from reaching this state by incorrect intermolecular interactions that occur when the protein is in a partially folded, intermediate state, whereby hydrophobic regions on their surface interact resulting in protein aggregation and precipitation. Thus, protein folding and unfolding are exquisitely regulated in the cell and involve molecular chaperone proteins that assist other proteins in adopting their correct, native state. The small heat shock proteins (sHsps) act in a chaperone manner by recognizing and stabilizing the intermediate states of target proteins, thereby preventing improper or incorrect folding that would otherwise result in protein misfolding, aggregation, precipitation and possibly disease [3], [4], [5], [6]. alphaB-Crystallin is a sHsp that is capable of interacting with a multitude of target proteins to prevent their aggregation and precipitation [7]. However, unlike the classical bacterial chaperonin GroEL, sHsps (including alphaB-crystallin) do not directly participate in refolding of the denatured proteins, except in the presence of another chaperone protein, e.g. Hsp70 [8]. sHsps act specifically with target proteins that are on their off-folding pathway [9]. alphaB-Crystallin is primarily found in the eye lens, where it associates with the closely related alphaA-crystallin, which has 57% sequence homology with alphaB-crystallin and shares the conserved 'alpha-crystallin domain' (reviewed in [10]), to form large hetero-oligomeric species. However, it is also constitutively expressed in many non-lenticular tissues, including the brain, lung and cardiac and skeletal muscle where it forms complexes with other sHsps [11]. As with other members of the sHsp family, the expression of alphaB-crystallin is dramatically up-regulated in response to stress and pathological conditions such as Alzheimer's, Parkinson's and Creutzfeldt-Jakob diseases [4], [12], [13], [14].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 109 113 109 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-deJong1">[10]</xref>
###xml 383 387 379 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Sun1">[15]</xref>
###xml 742 746 738 742 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Carver1">[16]</xref>
###xml 791 792 787 788 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sup>
###xml 988 992 980 984 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Carver1">[16]</xref>
###xml 994 998 986 990 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Carver2">[17]</xref>
###xml 1000 1004 992 996 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Carver3">[18]</xref>
The sHsps form a structurally divergent protein family with members present in archaea, bacteria and eukarya [10]. Monomeric molecular masses of the sHsps range between 12 and 40 kDa, however, most form large oligomeric assemblies of 150-800 kDa. All members are characterized by the presence of a homologous sequence of 80-100 residues, referred to as the 'alpha-crystallin' domain [15]. This domain is preceded by an N-terminal domain, which is highly variable in size and sequence, and is followed by a C-terminal extension. Whilst the C-terminal extensions of family members share little sequence similarity, they have the common characteristics of being polar and having conformational flexibility similar to peptides of the same length [16]. In previous studies, we have identified by 1H Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy that Hsp25 and alpha-crystallin have short, flexible and solvent exposed C-terminal extensions, which protrude from the domain core of the molecule [16], [17], [18].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 221 225 217 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Lindner1">[19]</xref>
###xml 227 231 223 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Lindner2">[20]</xref>
###xml 327 331 323 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-vanMontfort1">[21]</xref>
###xml 345 349 341 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Kim1">[22]</xref>
###xml 540 544 536 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-White1">[23]</xref>
###xml 741 745 733 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Kamei1">[24]</xref>
###xml 786 794 778 786 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 822 826 814 818 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Takemoto1">[25]</xref>
###xml 828 832 820 824 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Kelley1">[26]</xref>
###xml 890 894 882 886 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Smulders1">[27]</xref>
###xml 1206 1210 1194 1198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Smulders1">[27]</xref>
###xml 1212 1216 1200 1204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Giese1">[28]</xref>
###xml 1218 1222 1206 1210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Andley1">[29]</xref>
###xml 1224 1228 1212 1216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Fernando1">[30]</xref>
###xml 496 501 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The primary role of the flexible C-terminal extension of alpha-crystallin is thought to be to act as a solubilizing agent for the relatively hydrophobic protein and the sHsp-protein complex formed by its chaperone action [19], [20]. It may also play a role in subunit interaction since the resolved crystal structures of plant [21] and archaeal [22] sHsps indicate that their C-terminal extensions wrap around the outer surface of the sHsp complex. A similar role for the C-terminal extension of yeast Hsp26 has also recently been reported [23]. During ageing, alphaB-crystallin undergoes truncation of four C-terminal residues which correlates with a reduction in the protective ability of the protein and an increase in cataract formation [24]. Enzymatic truncation of the C-terminus in vitro with calpain II or trypsin [25], [26] or immobilization of its flexibility through mutagenesis [27], results in a reduction in its chaperone ability against amorphously aggregating target proteins. Other site-directed modifications within the C-terminal domain have also been shown to decrease the chaperone activity of alpha-crystallin and other vertebrate sHsps against amorphously aggregating target species [27], [28], [29], [30]. Together these studies show that the C-terminal extension plays an important role in the chaperone function of sHsps. However, to date the role of individual amino acids in the C-terminal domain of the protein has not been explored.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 82 86 82 86 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Kudva1">[31]</xref>
###xml 88 92 88 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Stege1">[32]</xref>
###xml 187 191 183 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Hatters1">[33]</xref>
###xml 284 288 273 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Liang1">[34]</xref>
###xml 309 313 294 298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Rekas1">[35]</xref>
###xml 502 506 479 483 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Ecroyd1">[36]</xref>
###xml 709 713 674 678 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Raman1">[37]</xref>
The interaction of sHsps with fibril-forming proteins has been investigated (e.g. [31], [32]) and it has been shown that alpha-crystallin inhibits fibril formation by apolipoprotein C-II [33]. Moreover, wild-type alphaB-crystallin is able to suppress fibril formation by beta-amyloid [34] and alpha-synuclein [35]. There is emerging evidence that conformational changes in alphaB-crystallin give rise to a more effective form of the chaperone. Phosphomimics of alphaB-crystallin with altered structure [36] and a chimeric form of alpha-crystallin (comprising alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin) have been shown to have enhanced chaperone activity against amyloid fibril formation compared to the wild-type protein [37].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
In this study we have generated several mutants of alphaB-crystallin in which the mutation sites are located in the C-terminal region of the protein, in particular the flexible C-terminal extension. In order to examine the role of specific C-terminal residues in regulating the oligomeric structure and chaperone function of alphaB-crystallin we have used a variety of biophysical techniques. As well, the chaperone function of these mutants was compared with that of the wild-type protein under different types of protein aggregation, i.e. disordered amorphous aggregation and ordered amyloid fibril assembly. The latter is of particular importance due to its association with protein deposits found in diseases such as Alzheimer's, Parkinson's and Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease.
###end p 13

###begin title 41
Results
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 103 110 99 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0001046-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 519 526 491 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0001046-t002">Table 2</xref>
###xml 625 628 592 595 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">max</sub>
###xml 828 832 795 799 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Ecroyd1">[36]</xref>
###xml 834 838 801 805 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Kronman1">[44]</xref>
###xml 840 844 807 811 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Burstein1">[45]</xref>
###xml 846 850 813 817 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Bera1">[46]</xref>
###xml 852 856 819 823 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Copeland1">[47]</xref>
###xml 987 990 945 948 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">max</sub>
###xml 1249 1252 1198 1201 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">max</sub>
###xml 1322 1329 1267 1274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0001046-t002">Table 2</xref>
###xml 33 38 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Molecular masses and purities of human recombinant alphaB-crystallin proteins were assessed by ESI-MS (Table 1). The calculated masses of the wild-type (alphaB-WT) and mutant alphaB-crystallins were in excellent agreement with their predicted masses (max. 1.2 Da difference). Peaks in the mass spectra arising from contaminating proteins were minor (approximately10% or less). Intrinsic tryptophan fluorescence of wild-type alphaB-crystallin produced a peak of fluorescence at 338.2 nm that arises from Trp9 and Trp60 (Table 2). The K174A/K175A, K175L and I159A/I161A mutants had red shifts in their wavelength maxima (lambdamax) compared to the wild-type protein (338.8, 340.0 and 341.0 nm, respectively). These data indicate that the tryptophan residues of these mutant proteins are more exposed than in the wild-type protein [36], [44], [45], [46], [47]. The E164A/E165A alphaB-crystallin mutant displayed the most significant change in tryptophan polarity with a blue shift in lambdamax to 336.0 nm implying a conformational change in this mutant in which the tryptophan residues are buried in the hydrophobic core to a greater extent than in wild-type alphaB-crystallin. Similarly, the C-terminal truncation mutant (R163STOP) displayed a lambdamax at a shorter wavelength (337.0 nm) than wild-type alphaB-crystallin (Table 2).

###begin title 43
Maximum tryptophan emission wavelengths for wild-type and mutant alphaB-crystallins.
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 177 178 149 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">A</italic>
###xml 171 178 143 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g001">Fig. 1<italic>A</italic></xref>
###xml 236 240 208 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Horwitz2">[40]</xref>
###xml 242 246 214 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Bova1">[48]</xref>
###xml 248 252 220 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Kumar1">[49]</xref>
###xml 467 468 435 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">A</italic>
###xml 461 468 429 436 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g001">Fig. 1<italic>A</italic></xref>
###xml 663 667 627 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Kelly1">[50]</xref>
The near-UV CD spectra of wild-type alphaB-crystallin exhibited a distinct minima at approximately267 nm and 292 nm and maxima at around 258, 264 and approximately273 nm (Fig. 1A) and was consistent with CD spectra presented previously [40], [48], [49]. The K175L, E164A/E165A and I159A/I161A mutants had very similar near-UV CD spectra to wild-type alphaB-crystallin indicating that their tertiary structures were not significantly altered by the mutation(s) (Fig. 1A). However, the spectra of K174A/K175A and R163STOP alphaB-crystallin showed a reduction in tertiary structure with a marked decrease in the maximum ellipticity at 273 nm. As summarized by Kelly [50] changes in this region of the spectrum are representative of the environment of the protein's tryptophan residues. Likewise, there is a reduction in the ellipticity of the bands corresponding to the phenylalanine residues (approximately270 nm-250 nm). Alteration in the tryptophan environment of R163STOP and K174A/K175A is also reflected in the region of the spectrum from 280-292 nm. Taken together, these data are indicative of major tertiary structural changes induced by these mutations.
###end p 44

###begin p 47
###xml 61 62 57 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">B</italic>
###xml 55 62 51 58 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g001">Fig. 1<italic>B</italic></xref>
###xml 101 105 97 98 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 291 298 284 291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g001">Fig. 1B</xref>
###xml 1156 1160 1106 1110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Das1">[51]</xref>
The far-UV CD spectrum of wild-type alphaB-crystallin (Fig. 1B) is characteristic of a predominantly beta-sheet protein, with a minimum ellipticity at 213 nm. The majority of mutants had far-UV CD spectra almost identical to the wild-type protein with slight variations in ellipticity only (Fig. 1B). The R163STOP mutant showed significantly altered secondary structure compared to the wild-type and other mutant alphaB-crystallins. The change in overall shape of the spectrum, increased negative ellipticity (particularly in the region of approximately225 nm) and appearance of the strong positive band at approximately195 nm are all indicative of increased alpha-helical content. This would be consistent with the loss of the entire C-terminal extension, decreasing the relative amount of beta-sheet content compared to alpha-helix. In the case of the K174A/K175A mutant, the loss of tertiary structure observed in the near-UV region with concurrent retention of secondary structure as seen in the far-UV region of the CD spectrum is indicative of a molten-globule like state similar to that observed for alpha-crystallin in the presence of a denaturant [51].
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 205 206 201 202 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</italic>
###xml 199 206 195 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g001">Fig. 1<italic>C</italic></xref>

###xml 385 389 377 381 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Aquilina1">[52]</xref>
###xml 492 493 480 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</italic>
###xml 486 493 474 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g001">Fig. 1<italic>C</italic></xref>
###xml 1218 1219 1202 1203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</italic>
###xml 1212 1219 1196 1203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g001">Fig. 1<italic>C</italic></xref>
Most of the alphaB-crystallins eluted from the size exclusion column as relatively broad symmetrical peaks at early elution times indicating they exist as polydisperse high molecular mass oligomers (Fig. 1C). Wild-type alphaB-crystallin eluted in a single peak corresponding to an average molecular mass of 740 kDa and mass range of 420-980 kDa, consistent with previous studies [40], [52]. All of the mutants form oligomers that are more polydisperse than wild-type alphaB-crystallin (Fig. 1C). Both the E164A/E165A and K175L mutants form oligomers that have a slightly reduced average molecular mass (720 kDa and 670 kDa respectively) compared to the wild-type protein. In contrast, the average molecular mass of the K174A/K175A and I159A/I161A oligomers is higher than wild-type alphaB-crystallin (780 kDa and 880 kDa respectively). The average molecular masses of oligomers formed by the I159A/I161A mutant (up to 1.9 MDa) were found to be significantly larger than the wild-type when tested using an ANOVA with post-hoc analysis by Tukey's test (P<0.019). Upon visual inspection in can be seen that the ability of the R163STOP mutant to form large and consistently sized aggregates was profoundly impaired (Fig. 1C). Specifically, this mutant eluted in the form of a high molecular mass species in the void of the column (>2 MDa), at least two large molecular mass oligomers of 630 kDa and 310 kDa and a predominate peak corresponding to a species of less than 100 kDa.

When its thermostability was assessed, wild-type alphaB-crystallin began to precipitate from solution at 68degreesC and underwent a second phase of precipitation at 78degreesC (Fig. 1D). The E164A/E165A alphaB-crystallin mutant was much less thermostable than wild-type alphaB-crystallin and the other mutants used in this study as evidenced by its rapid, large-scale precipitation from solution at 60degreesC (Fig. 1D). The change in light scattering due to precipitation for the E164A/E165A mutant was more than 7-fold greater than for the other alphaB-crystallin proteins. The K175L mutant showed very similar thermostability to wild-type alphaB-crystallin with precipitation occurring in two phases at 68degreesC and 78degreesC. The R163STOP and I159A/I161A mutants were slightly less thermostable than wild-type alphaB-crystallin with onset of precipitation observed at 63degreesC and 65degreesC respectively. The K174A/K175A alphaB-crystallin showed enhanced thermostability compared to wild-type alphaB-crystallin, with only minimal precipitation evident at 85degreesC (Fig. 1D).

The chaperone ability of the mutant alphaB-crystallin proteins was compared to wild-type alphaB-crystallin under a variety of conditions of induced target protein aggregation. The heat-induced amorphous aggregation of bovine betaL-crystallin (a natural target of alphaB-crystallin in the lens), incubated in the absence of the chaperone, commenced after 20 min and the increase in light scattering due to protein precipitation reached a maximum after 80 min (Fig. 2A). Wild-type alphaB-crystallin was able to suppress the increase in light scattering in a concentration dependent manner, such that, at a 1.0:0.5 molar ratio of betaL-crystallin: alphaB-crystallin, there was complete inhibition of protein precipitation (data not shown). At a 1.0:0.2 molar ratio of betaL-crystallin:alphaB-crystallin the change in light scattering after 90 min was reduced by 90+/-2% (mean+/-SEM) (Fig. 2A). When the mutant alphaB-crystallin proteins were used at the same molar ratio (Fig. 2B), the E164A/E165A mutant was a significantly worse chaperone than the wild-type protein (p<0.01) and increased the amount of light scattering due to its own precipitation from solution at this temperature (see Fig. 2A). Whilst the I159A/I161A mutant delayed the onset of betaL-crystallin's aggregation to 40 min, by the end of the assay the amount of light scattering in the presence of this mutant was similar to when betaL-crystallin was incubated alone. The K174A/K175A and K175L proteins were effective chaperones at this sub-stoichiometric molar ratio to the target protein having activity similar to the wild-type protein.

We employed these two model systems since both form fibrils at physiological pH and temperature [36]. Fibril formation by RCMkappa-CN, as monitored by an increase in ThT fluorescence, showed a gradual increase over the time course of the assay (Fig. 3A). The addition of alphaB-crystallin to the sample slowed the rate of fibril formation such that, at a 1.0:0.3 molar ratio of RCMkappa-CN: alphaB-crystallin, the increase in ThT fluorescence was reduced by 26+/-3% (Fig. 3B). The I159A/I161A mutant was found to have a similar level of chaperone ability against amyloid forming RCMkappa-CN as the wild-type protein (Fig. 3B).

###begin title 59
Discussion
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 425 429 421 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Lindner1">[19]</xref>
###xml 431 435 427 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Lindner2">[20]</xref>
###xml 437 441 433 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Andley1">[29]</xref>
###xml 443 447 439 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Leroux1">[54]</xref>
We have successfully produced and expressed a number of mutants in the C-terminal region of alphaB-crystallin in order to investigate the role of this region, and individual residues within it, on the structure and chaperone function of this sHsp. In particular, we have targeted key residues within the C-terminal extension of the protein since it is thought to play an important role in the chaperone action of other sHsps [19], [20], [29], [54]. Overall, our studies are consistent with previous work showing that a highly flexible C-terminal extension in sHsps is important in the overall structure of the chaperone protein. Significantly, we found that certain residues in alphaB-crystallin play a key role in regulating its chaperone activity towards particular types of protein aggregation (i.e. ordered or disordered) and, therefore, these results may provide a foundation for future studies aimed at preventing diseases associated with protein aggregation.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 276 280 268 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Aquilina1">[52]</xref>
###xml 282 286 274 278 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Thampi1">[55]</xref>
###xml 806 810 794 798 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-vanMontfort1">[21]</xref>
###xml 831 835 819 823 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Kim1">[22]</xref>
###xml 1070 1074 1058 1062 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Studer1">[56]</xref>
###xml 1175 1181 1159 1165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g001">Fig. 1</xref>
###xml 2551 2555 2511 2515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Thampi1">[55]</xref>
###xml 2557 2561 2517 2521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Aquilina2">[57]</xref>
###xml 2990 2994 2930 2934 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Ghosh1">[58]</xref>
###xml 3241 3245 3181 3185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Smulders1">[27]</xref>
###xml 3434 3435 3374 3375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</italic>
###xml 3428 3435 3368 3375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g001">Fig. 1<italic>C</italic></xref>
###xml 791 796 <span type="species:ncbi:4565">wheat</span>
###xml 2966 2971 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our results have also shown that the C-terminal extension plays a significant role in the oligomerization of alphaB-crystallin. Altered oligomerization properties and increased polydispersity have been shown to correlate with altered chaperone action of the alpha-crystallins [52], [55]. Each of the alphaB-crystallin mutants in this study exhibited perturbed oligomeric association such that they had broader molecular mass ranges than the wild-type protein. The most dramatic changes were for the I159A/I161A and R163STOP mutants. The I159A/I161A mutant eluted earlier and formed aggregates that were much more polydisperse than the wild-type protein, i.e. approximately 1.8 MDa to 490 kDa in mass (with an average mass of 880 kDa). The I-X-I/V motif is well conserved among sHsps, and in wheat Hsp 16.9 [21] and archael Hsp16.5 [22], plays a critical role in oligomeric assembly. Indeed, elimination of this motif from bacterial HspH leads to the complete abrogation of the ability of the protein to oligomerise and chaperone amorphously aggregating citrate synthase [56]. Our data support a role for this motif in regulating the oligomerization of alphaB-crystallin (see Fig. 1). The altered fluorescence spectra of the various alphaB-crystallin mutants compared to the wild-type protein are particularly interesting since the tryptophan residues in alphaB-crystallin are located in the N-terminal domain (Trp9 and Trp60). Our results indicate that in the case of K175L, K174A/K175A and I159A/I161A alphaB-crystallin, the mutations introduced resulted in conformational changes which led to the N-terminal tryptophan residues becoming more exposed to solvent. In contrast, the tryptophan fluorescence data of the E164A/E165A mutant show that the conformational changes induced by the loss of the two charged glutamic acid residues resulted in the N-terminal region becoming buried to a greater extent. A similar change was observed with the truncation of the C-terminal extension in R163STOP alphaB-crystallin. As shown by size-exclusion chromatography, the ability of this mutant to form regular multimeric complexes was also profoundly impaired. The removal of the highly flexible C-terminal extension led to the formation of heterodisperse alphaB-crystallin multimers ranging from more than 100 subunits to less than 5 in size. These results are consistent with studies of alphaA-crystallin showing that removal of residues from the C-terminal extension of the protein alters its oligomeric distribution and decreases its subunit exchange rate [55], [57]. Together, the results show that the C-terminal extensions of both alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin are not only important in maintaining the solubility of alpha-crystallin and its complex with target proteins but also in regulating the oligomerization of alpha-crystallin subunits into multimeric complexes. The C-terminal extension was one region identified as being a subunit-subunit interaction site in human alphaB-crystallin [58]. It has been proposed that the flexibility and polarity of the C-terminal extension of Hsps plays an important role in maintaining the spacing between adjacent complexes, which would otherwise be prone to aggregation via hydrophobic interactions [27]. Our results are consistent with this since disruption of this spacing mechanism would be expected to give rise to greater polydispersity, as is observed by size exclusion HPLC (see Fig. 1C).
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 153 166 149 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g001">Fig. 1A and B</xref>
###xml 610 614 602 606 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Lindner2">[20]</xref>
###xml 799 803 787 791 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Lindner2">[20]</xref>
###xml 818 828 806 816 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 828 832 816 820 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Leroux1">[54]</xref>
###xml 855 859 839 843 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Andley1">[29]</xref>
###xml 818 828 <span type="species:ncbi:238774|species:ncbi:386067|species:ncbi:147878|species:ncbi:193182|species:ncbi:313250|species:ncbi:323266|species:ncbi:315362|species:ncbi:148887|species:ncbi:124036|species:ncbi:48798|species:ncbi:529401|species:ncbi:314391|species:ncbi:646318|species:ncbi:254962|species:ncbi:323047|species:ncbi:287681|species:ncbi:269614|species:ncbi:103475|species:ncbi:114942|species:ncbi:212608|species:ncbi:457136|species:ncbi:227469|species:ncbi:449387|species:ncbi:551876|species:ncbi:248142|species:ncbi:252961|species:ncbi:466267|species:ncbi:119832|species:ncbi:228196|species:ncbi:316080|species:ncbi:217897|species:ncbi:109099|species:ncbi:328850|species:ncbi:266263|species:ncbi:348465|species:ncbi:181251|species:ncbi:6239|species:ncbi:320029|species:ncbi:293163|species:ncbi:4853|species:ncbi:160529|species:ncbi:292745|species:ncbi:368875|species:ncbi:323318|species:ncbi:209968|species:ncbi:405608|species:ncbi:480533">C. elegans</span>
Truncation of the entire C-terminal extension (R163STOP) of alphaB-crystallin also significantly altered the protein's secondary and tertiary structure (Fig. 1A and B). Together with the tryptophan fluorescence data, these results indicate that the C-terminal extension, although unstructured, plays a significant role in stabilizing the structure of the entire protein. A similar effect was observed in a C-terminally truncated mutant of Hsp25 (Hsp25DeltaC18) where a loss of secondary and tertiary structure, compared to the wild-type protein, was evidenced by far-UV CD spectroscopy and 2D NMR spectroscopy [20]. The poor chaperone activity of R163STOP alphaB-crystallin against amorphously aggregating target proteins is in keeping with previous findings on deletion mutants of Hsps, i.e. Hsp25 [20], Hsp16.2 from C. elegans[54] and alphaA-crystallin [29].

###begin p 63
###xml 161 162 154 155 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">L</sub>
###xml 280 286 273 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001046-g002">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 450 454 439 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Pasta1">[59]</xref>
###xml 1397 1401 1374 1378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-vanMontfort1">[21]</xref>
Compared to the wild-type protein, the I159A/I161A alphaB-crystallin mutant was a significantly worse chaperone against heat-induced amorphously aggregating betaL-crystallin, but was a significantly better chaperone against the reduction-induced amorphous aggregation of insulin (Fig. 2). These results are consistent with previous studies on an I159/161G mutant of alphaB-crystallin which exhibited enhanced chaperone ability under reduction stress [59]. In the present study, this mutant had similar chaperone ability against amyloid fibril forming targets to the wild-type protein highlighting the role of particular residues within the protein in regulating chaperone action of alphaB-crystallin during different stress conditions. For example, the increase in chaperone activity of I159A/I161A compared to wild-type alphaB-crystallin in the reduction-induced amorphous aggregation assay conducted at physiological temperatures may be due to the substitution of the two bulky, hydrophobic isoleucine residues in the I-X-I motif with more compact alanine residues. This in turn is likely to provide the C-terminal extension with greater flexibility and hence, increase the capacity of alphaB-crystallin to bind to some target proteins. Another possibility is that the substitution of these residues gives rise to a conformational change which further exposes putative chaperone binding site(s) [21]. Interestingly, however, these residues were not found to play a significant role in the chaperone action of alphaB-crystallin against amyloid fibril forming target proteins as there was no significant difference in the chaperone action of this mutant and the wild-type protein. Together, these data imply that the highly conserved I-X-I motif in sHsps regulates exposure of the hydrophobic groove of the alpha-crystallin domain of the protein, thereby controlling chaperone action with amorphously aggregating target proteins.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 88 94 88 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 254 258 250 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Liao1">[60]</xref>
###xml 438 442 434 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Liao1">[60]</xref>
The increased thermostability of the K174A/K175A is consistent with the results of Liao et al. who showed that removal of the C-terminal lysine residue in porcine alphaB-crystallin led to a mutant with a higher thermostability than the wild-type protein [60]. In contrast, replacement of the ultimate lysine residue of the C-terminal extension with a leucine residue did not affect the protein's thermostability to any significant extent [60]. Significantly, our results show that the removal of these lysine residues does not compromise the chaperone activity of the protein against amorphously aggregating target proteins and increased its chaperone ability against aggregating RCMkappa-CN. Thus, these data suggest that alphaB-crystallin does not require the two electropositive C-terminal lysine residues in order to interact with aggregating target proteins.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 1227 1231 1203 1207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Karshikoff1">[61]</xref>
###xml 1233 1237 1209 1213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Karshikoff2">[62]</xref>
###xml 1407 1411 1383 1387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Karshikoff2">[62]</xref>
###xml 1413 1417 1389 1393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Cambillau1">[63]</xref>
###xml 1419 1423 1395 1399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Kreil1">[64]</xref>
The double glutamic acid alphaB-crystallin mutant (E164A/E165A) showed greatly decreased thermostability and as a result was ineffective as a chaperone in the heat stress assay. Whilst the E164A/E165A mutant was able to delay the onset of aggregation of reduced insulin, by the end of the assay it was found to be a significantly worse chaperone than wild-type alphaB-crystallin. This probably reflects the relative instability of the complex formed between the insulin B-chain and E164A/E165A mutant protein. Interestingly, E164A/E165A alphaB-crystallin was a significantly better chaperone against the two amyloid fibril forming target proteins used in this study. Thus, E164 and E165, which are conserved between alphaA-and alphaB-crystallin, may play a role in regulating chaperone interaction during different types of target protein aggregation. The thermal instability of E164A/E165A alphaB-crystallin may be attributable to the removal of hydrophilicity that normally facilitates hydrophobic interactions within the protein, since hydrophobic interactions increase with increasing temperature. By analogy with hyperthermophilic proteins, non-covalent intermolecular interactions e.g. electrostatic and hydrogen bonding [61], [62] are facilitated by a high proportion of charged amino acids that act to stabilise the protein at high temperatures, via the formation of hydrogen bonds and salt bridges [62], [63], [64]. This solubilising role is likely to be important both for the alphaB-crystallin aggregate and the complex it forms with target proteins. Furthermore, due to electrostatic repulsion, these two glutamic acid residues may also function to separate protein subunits by blocking hydrophobic interactions and thereby regulating aggregate formation under heat stress.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 603 607 595 599 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Ecroyd1">[36]</xref>
###xml 900 904 884 888 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Rekas1">[35]</xref>
###xml 1019 1023 999 1003 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Hatters1">[33]</xref>
###xml 1206 1210 1179 1183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Stege1">[32]</xref>
###xml 1369 1373 1342 1346 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Raman1">[37]</xref>
Our results therefore highlight the important role of the C-terminal region, and in particular the flexible C-terminal extension, in the structure and chaperone function of alphaB-crystallin. Of particular interest, specific amino acid residues within the C-terminal region regulate its chaperone action against different types of protein aggregation (i.e. ordered and disordered). The mechanism by which alphaB-crystallin inhibits fibril growth is not known but it may do so by interacting with and binding pre-fibrillar aggregates in a soluble complex as it does with amorphously aggregating proteins [36]. In the case of alpha-synuclein, a component of Lewy bodies in Parkinson's disease, alphaB-crystallin is thought to interact with the fibril-forming species at an early stage of its aggregation pathway to reduce the nucleation rate thereby inhibiting fibril formation at the nucleation phase [35]. alpha-Crystallin also interacts with early amyloidogenic precursors of apolipoprotein C-II to inhibit nucleation [33]. Studies on the Alzheimer's disease protein, Abeta peptide, showed that alphaB-crystallin affected the elongation phase of fibril formation resulting in shorter, non-regular fibrils [32]. More recent studies on the same protein have shown that the chaperone preferentially binds to the fibril nucleus preventing propagation of fibrillar species [37].
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 242 246 238 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Sathish1">[65]</xref>
###xml 248 252 244 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Sathish2">[66]</xref>
###xml 634 638 626 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Young1">[67]</xref>
###xml 647 654 639 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 750 754 742 746 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Randall1">[68]</xref>
###xml 781 787 773 779 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 908 912 900 904 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Haslbeck1">[69]</xref>
###xml 1110 1114 1098 1102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-Haslbeck1">[69]</xref>
###xml 647 654 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
It has been suggested that alphaB-crystallin operates more effectively as a chaperone against some target proteins than others as a result of the differing affinities of the chaperone for the various intermediately folded forms of its target [65], [66]. These observations are also consistent with alphaB-crystallin possessing more than one chaperone binding site, e.g. for amorphously aggregating and/or amyloid forming proteins. The occurrence of numerous binding sites on proteins which perform diverse functions has been well documented. Hsp90 has been shown to possess two independent binding sites with differential specificity [67] and the E. coli chaperone SecB has multiple binding sites which are both hydrophobic and hydrophilic in nature [68]. As discussed by Haslbeck et al., the lack of conservation of residues amongst sHsps makes it difficult to locate potential target protein binding sites [69]. It is possible, therefore, that variable regions of sequence are involved in binding which would help to explain why sHsps such as alphaB-crystallin have such different target proteins affinities [69]. It may also support the hypothesis that multiple target-protein binding sites exist in the alphaB-crystallin sequence, which are affected to a greater or lesser degree by conformational change.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 221 225 217 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001046-vanMontfort1">[21]</xref>
Studies have shown that contacts between the I-X-I motif in the C-terminal region and a hydrophobic grove in the alphaB-crystallin domain on an adjacent monomer are critical for proper oligomeric assembly in a plant sHsp [21]. It is possible; therefore, that irregular oligomeric assembly of mutants such as E164A/E165A and I159A/I161A alphaB-crystallin which form oligomers that are smaller and larger, respectively, than wild-type alphaB-crystallin also alters target protein binding at various sites. In the case of the E164A/E165A mutant, for example, removal of the pair of charged residues induced a conformational change which decreased the protein's stability under heat stress, altered oligomerization and unfavorably affected the binding of amorphously aggregating proteins. It is unclear at this stage whether the reduced binding of such disordered aggregating species occurs via obstruction or destabilization of such sites. In doing so, however, it is feasible that the same conformational changes may have exposed or stabilized another binding site on the molecule for fibril forming proteins.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 323 330 319 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
The observation that genetically modified alphaB-crystallin, in comparison to the wild-type protein, demonstrates superior chaperone ability to suppress the formation of amyloid fibrils is a step in the potential development of treatments for diseases associated with amyloid formation and deposition. Future studies using in vivo models of amyloid fibril formation will elucidate the potential of modified alphaB-crystallin (i.e. E164A/E165A) to serve as therapeutic agents.
###end p 69
