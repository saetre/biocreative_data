###begin title 3
Summary
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 36 38 36 38 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 95 96 92 93 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 712 713 699 700 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1020 1022 1007 1009 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
Cyclic ADP-ribose is an important Ca2+-mobilizing cytosolic messenger synthesized from beta-NAD+ by ADP-ribosyl cyclases (ARCs). However, the focus upon ectocellular mammalian ARCs (CD38 and CD157) has led to confusion as to how extracellular enzymes generate intracellular messengers in response to stimuli. We have cloned and characterized three ARCs in the sea urchin egg and found that endogenous ARCbeta and ARCgamma are intracellular and located within the lumen of acidic, exocytotic vesicles, where they are optimally active. Intraorganelle ARCs are shielded from cytosolic substrate and targets by the organelle membrane, but this barrier is circumvented by nucleotide transport. We show that a beta-NAD+ transporter provides ARC substrate that is converted luminally to cADPR, which, in turn, is shuttled out to the cytosol via a separate cADPR transporter. Moreover, nucleotide transport is integral to ARC activity physiologically because three transport inhibitors all inhibited the fertilization-induced Ca2+ wave that is dependent upon cADPR. This represents a novel signaling mechanism whereby an extracellular stimulus increases the concentration of a second messenger by promoting messenger transport from intraorganelle synthesis sites to the cytosol.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Results and Discussion
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 68 70 68 70 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 199 200 196 197 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 200 206 197 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib1 bib2" ref-type="bibr">[1, 2]</xref>
###xml 231 233 228 230 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 309 315 306 312 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib3 bib4" ref-type="bibr">[3, 4]</xref>
###xml 408 414 405 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib1 bib2" ref-type="bibr">[1, 2]</xref>
###xml 513 516 510 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib5" ref-type="bibr">[5]</xref>
The mechanisms coupling extracellular stimuli to synthesis of the Ca2+-mobilizing messenger cyclic ADP-ribose (cADPR) are poorly understood. ADP-ribosyl cyclases (ARCs) synthesize cADPR from beta-NAD+[1, 2] (and possibly another Ca2+-mobilizing messenger, NAADP, though this remains uncertain physiologically [3, 4]). The fact that mammalian ARC isoforms (CD38 and CD157) appear to be primarily ectocellular [1, 2] has raised questions as to how they could generate intracellular messengers in a regulated manner [5].
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Cloning of Sea Urchin ARCs
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 133 136 133 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib6" ref-type="bibr">[6]</xref>
###xml 161 168 161 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Aplysia</italic>
###xml 205 234 205 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Strongylocentrotus purpuratus</italic>
###xml 242 245 242 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib7" ref-type="bibr">[7]</xref>
###xml 294 297 294 297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib8" ref-type="bibr">[8]</xref>
###xml 482 495 471 484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. purpuratus</italic>
###xml 798 807 776 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S1</xref>
###xml 827 830 805 808 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib1" ref-type="bibr">[1]</xref>
###xml 899 908 877 886 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S1</xref>
###xml 975 978 953 956 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib9" ref-type="bibr">[9]</xref>
###xml 1060 1063 1038 1041 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib7" ref-type="bibr">[7]</xref>
###xml 1114 1123 1092 1101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S1</xref>
###xml 1223 1232 1201 1210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S1</xref>
###xml 205 234 <span type="species:ncbi:7668">Strongylocentrotus purpuratus</span>
###xml 482 495 <span type="species:ncbi:7668">S. purpuratus</span>
To address these issues, we cloned and characterized three ARC enzymes from sea urchin egg where cADPR and ARC were first discovered [6]. Using CD38, CD157, and Aplysia ARC as query sequences to probe the Strongylocentrotus purpuratus genome [7] and expressed-sequence tag (EST) cDNA databases [8], we identified three ARC isoforms, ARCalpha, ARCbeta, and ARCgamma. To obtain the complete cDNA sequences, we designed gene-specific primers and carried out RT-PCR with total RNA from S. purpuratus eggs, ovaries, or testes. The cDNA sequences obtained for ARCalpha, ARCbeta, and ARCgamma have open reading frames corresponding to proteins with predicted molecular masses of 36.3, 35.4, and 40.1 kDa, all containing the ARC domain (Rib-hydrolase), catalytic residues, and critical disulphide bridges (Figure S1A available online) [1]. The mRNAs for all three ARCs were found in ovary, testis, and egg (Figure S1D). Recently, sea urchin ARCs were cloned via a different approach [9], and our slightly different sequences are probably attributable to polymorphisms [7] identified in multiple independent RT-PCR clones (Figure S1E). Sea urchin ARCs are predicted to be membrane bound with different modes of membrane attachment (Figure S1F).
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Immunolocalization of ARCs in Sea Urchin Eggs
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 262 271 262 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figures 1</xref>
###xml 402 410 398 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;1</xref>
###xml 463 471 459 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;1</xref>
###xml 523 531 519 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;1</xref>
###xml 636 645 625 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figures 1</xref>
###xml 710 719 698 707 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figures 1</xref>
###xml 773 782 761 770 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figures 1</xref>
###xml 891 900 872 881 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S2</xref>
The subcellular distribution of endogenous ARCs is currently unknown, so we first examined immunofluorescence in eggs costained with Lysotracker Red to label acidic organelles. In fixed, permeabilized eggs, specific ARC staining was peripheral for all isoforms (Figures 1B, 1F, and 1J) but with crucial differences: ARCalpha staining was ectocellular at the outer edge of the Lysotracker Red staining (Figure 1B), was smooth in three-dimensional reconstructions (Figure 1E), and was even observed in nonpermeabilized eggs (Figure 1D). In contrast, ARCbeta and ARCgamma were intracellular proteins that colocalized with Lysotracker Red (Figures 1F and 1J), distributed in punctate vesicles 1-2 mum in diameter (Figures 1I and 1M), and not observed in nonpermeabilized eggs (Figures 1H and 1L). The distribution of ARCbeta and ARCgamma was not mimicked by endoplasmic reticulum (ER) staining (Figure S2).
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 165 173 158 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;1</xref>
###xml 215 219 208 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib10" ref-type="bibr">[10]</xref>
###xml 415 424 408 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figures 1</xref>
###xml 434 438 427 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib11" ref-type="bibr">[11]</xref>
###xml 524 533 510 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figures 1</xref>
###xml 602 611 588 597 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figures 1</xref>
###xml 679 687 661 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;1</xref>
###xml 702 710 684 692 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;1</xref>
###xml 787 795 769 777 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;1</xref>
Intriguingly, the localization of ARCbeta and ARCgamma was reminiscent of cortical granules, the acidic, exocytotic vesicles that are docked at the plasma membrane (Figure 1N) to generate the fertilization envelope [10]. We confirmed this locus in a number of ways. First, unlike other organelles, cortical granules do not migrate upon stratification of live eggs by centrifugation unless pretreated with urethane (Figures 1O and 1S) [11]. Accordingly, ARCbeta and ARCgamma remained peripheral in untreated stratified eggs (Figures 1Q and 1R), but were dislodged and migrated in urethane-treated eggs (Figures 1U and 1V). In contrast, ARCalpha staining remained peripheral with (Figure 1T) or without (Figure 1P) urethane, as expected for a plasma membrane and/or vitelline layer (PMVL, Figure 1N) protein.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 42 50 42 50 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;2</xref>
###xml 146 150 146 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib12" ref-type="bibr">[12]</xref>
###xml 210 219 210 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Figures 2</xref>
###xml 280 289 273 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Figures 2</xref>
###xml 317 326 306 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Figures 2</xref>
###xml 456 465 438 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Figures 2</xref>
Second, by the shearing of adherent eggs (Figure 2A), cortical lawns that comprised cortical ER, docked cortical granules, and PMVL were prepared [12]. Spheroid cortical granules stained positively for hyalin (Figures 2B, 2D, and 2F), which colocalized with ARCbeta and ARCgamma (Figures 2D and 2F) but not ARCalpha (Figures 2B and 2C). Importantly, ARCbeta and ARCgamma appeared to be luminal because immunostaining was not seen in unpermeabilized lawns (Figures 2E and 2G).
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 186 195 179 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Figures 2</xref>
Third, electron microscopy and immunogold labeling confirmed that ARCbeta and ARCgamma associate with the cortical granule lumen, but not with underlying yolk platelets, cytosol, or ER (Figures 2H-2K). The data support an ectocellular ARCalpha, whereas ARCbeta and ARCgamma are within the cortical granule lumen.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
pH Dependence of ARC Activity and Distribution in Subcellular Fractions
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 97 105 97 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;1</xref>
###xml 288 296 284 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;3</xref>
###xml 375 383 364 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;3</xref>
We extended these studies by using biochemical cell fractionation. Cell-surface complexes (CSCs, Figure 1N) were prepared and further fractionated into their constituent PMVL and cortical granules where indicated. In agreement with the above, ARCalpha was only detected in CSCs and PMVL (Figure 3A), whereas ARCbeta and ARCgamma were confirmed in CSCs and cortical granules (Figure 3A).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 90 92 87 89 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 200 208 194 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;3</xref>
###xml 332 341 326 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S1</xref>
###xml 366 370 360 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib10" ref-type="bibr">[10]</xref>
###xml 438 446 424 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;3</xref>
###xml 493 502 479 488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S1</xref>
###xml 646 654 632 640 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;3</xref>
A luminal localization of ARCbeta was further reinforced. First, eggs were treated with Ca2+ ionophore to induce cortical granule exocytosis, and ARCbeta was then detected in the surrounding exudate (Figure 3A), consistent with its release from the cortical granule lumen, possibly by clipping of the putative transmembrane domain (Figure S1F) by resident proteases [10]. By contrast, ARCalpha and ARCgamma did not appear in the exudate (Figure 3A), presumably because of membrane attachment (Figure S1F). Second, ARC activity in CSCs or cortical granules was enhanced upon vesicle lysis, consistent with restricted substrate access when intact (Figure 3B). Taken together, these data overwhelmingly suggest that ARCalpha is ectocellular whereas ARCbeta and ARCgamma reside within the lumen of the cortical granule, the first molecularly identified ARCs to be found exclusively within an organelle.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 127 130 124 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib9" ref-type="bibr">[9]</xref>
Our conclusion differs with the heterologous expression of one recombinant ARC (equivalent to our ARCbeta) localized in the ER [9]. One possibility is that this is a function of the expression system because another cortical granule protein (SFE9) is also retained within the ER when expressed heterologously (data not shown).
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 107 111 95 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib10" ref-type="bibr">[10]</xref>
###xml 143 151 131 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;3</xref>
###xml 170 178 158 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;3</xref>
###xml 402 410 390 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;3</xref>
###xml 657 661 638 642 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib13" ref-type="bibr">[13]</xref>
We next asked whether luminal ARCs are active. Cortical granules are acidic vesicles (pH approximately5.5, [10]), and, fittingly, recombinant (Figure 3D) and endogenous (Figure 3C) sea urchin ARCs preferentially synthesize cADPR (and not ADPR) at acidic pH. Lysed cortical granules were also separated into membranes, and soluble proteins and ARC activity was greater for the soluble fraction at pH 5 (Figure 3C). The low activity in the particulate fraction at pH 5 (close to the predicted pIs of 4.7 and 5.7 for ARCbeta and ARCgamma, respectively) is probably due to protein aggregation and precipitation at this pH (data not shown; cf. CD38 inactivation [13]). Our findings confirm that ARC activity is compartmentalized within the lumen of an organelle with an optimal pH.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 73 81 73 81 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib14 bib15" ref-type="bibr">[14, 15]</xref>
###xml 145 147 145 147 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 199 203 199 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib12" ref-type="bibr">[12]</xref>
###xml 397 406 397 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S3</xref>
###xml 445 447 445 447 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 449 458 449 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S3</xref>
###xml 323 328 <span type="species:ncbi:42554">tetra</span>
Exoplasmic sea urchin ARCs contrast with reports of soluble ARC activity [14, 15], but this may be an artifact of homogenate preparation where Ca2+ contamination promotes cortical granule exocytosis [12] and the disgorging of ARC to the "cytosol." Homogenate prepared in the presence of EGTA [glycol-bis(2-aminoethylether)-tetra acetic acid] preserves vesicular integrity and ARC remains luminal (Figure S3) until lysed or treated with excess Ca2+ (Figure S3).

###begin title 20
###xml 41 43 41 43 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
Nucleotide Transport and Fertilization Ca2+ Signals
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 110 119 110 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S4</xref>
###xml 215 223 215 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;3</xref>
###xml 310 312 310 312 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
If ARCs are luminally active, then substrates and products must be transported in and out during stimulation (Figure S4) and the small ARC activity in intact CSCs and cortical granules is consistent with transport (Figure 3B). Therefore, we investigated the effect of transport inhibitors upon physiological Ca2+ responses.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 55 57 55 57 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 80 88 80 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib16 bib17" ref-type="bibr">[16, 17]</xref>
###xml 97 105 97 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib18 bib19" ref-type="bibr">[18, 19]</xref>
###xml 179 181 179 181 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 230 239 218 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figures 4</xref>
###xml 304 311 292 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib5 bib20" ref-type="bibr">[5, 20]</xref>
###xml 356 358 344 346 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 404 414 392 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figures S6</xref>
###xml 477 486 465 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figures 4</xref>
###xml 516 518 504 506 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 538 546 526 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;4</xref>
###xml 549 558 537 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S6</xref>
###xml 603 605 591 593 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 645 654 633 642 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figures 4</xref>
###xml 753 762 729 738 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S5</xref>
###xml 853 861 829 837 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib16 bib17" ref-type="bibr">[16, 17]</xref>
At fertilization, cADPR contributes to the prominent Ca2+ wave (spike upstroke) [16, 17] via ARC [18, 19]. In control eggs, fertilization induced a cortical flash followed by a Ca2+ wave that crossed the egg in approximately14 s (Figures 4A and 4D). With the cADPR transport inhibitor dipyridamole (DPM) [5, 20], the early events (the cortical flash and Ca2+ wave initiation) were relatively unaffected (Figures S6A and S6B; p > 0.05), whereas the wave transit time (p < 0.01, Figures 4B and 4D) and the antipodal Ca2+ release (p < 0.01, Figure 4B, Figure S6B) were dramatically slowed. Globally, the Ca2+ spike amplitude was reduced (p < 0.01, Figures 4C and 4F). Note that the intracellular DPM concentration is approximately10% of the extracellular (Figure S5). This partial inhibition by DPM mimics cADPR antagonism or ryanodine receptor inhibition [16, 17].
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 52 53 52 53 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 120 121 120 121 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 150 158 150 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib16 bib17" ref-type="bibr">[16, 17]</xref>
###xml 195 197 195 197 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 246 255 246 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figures 4</xref>
###xml 332 334 332 334 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 345 354 345 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figures 4</xref>
At fertilization, cADPR acts synergistically with IP3, and, consequently, cADPR antagonism appears more striking when IP3 is antagonized with heparin [16, 17]. Heparin alone did not affect the Ca2+ spike amplitude but extended the lag (p > 0.05, Figures 4E and 4F). However, heparin and DPM together almost completely blocked the Ca2+ response (Figures 4E and 4F), consistent with DPM acting upon the cADPR pathway.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 84 91 84 91 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib5 bib20" ref-type="bibr">[5, 20]</xref>
###xml 107 115 107 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib21 bib22" ref-type="bibr">[21, 22]</xref>
###xml 160 162 160 162 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 180 188 180 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;4</xref>
###xml 318 326 318 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;4</xref>
###xml 412 414 412 414 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 435 436 435 436 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 455 465 455 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figures S7</xref>
###xml 508 517 508 517 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S7</xref>
We also tested other nucleotide transport inhibitors, nitrobenzylthioinosine (NBTI) [5, 20] and indoprofen [21, 22]. Alone, these agents had no effect on the Ca2+ spike amplitude (Figure 4F, p > 0.05), but, like DPM, they synergistically inhibited the response in heparinized eggs (albeit less efficaciously than DPM, Figure 4F). Note that at relevant concentrations, these inhibitors did not directly inhibit Ca2+ release evoked by IP3, cADPR, or NAADP (Figures S7A-S7C), nor did they directly inhibit ARC (Figure S7D). All inhibitors act in a manner diagnostic for antagonism of the cADPR pathway.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 115 116 112 113 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 135 137 132 134 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 167 175 164 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib18 bib19" ref-type="bibr">[18, 19]</xref>
###xml 195 197 192 194 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 218 219 212 213 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 272 281 266 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figures 4</xref>
###xml 344 353 338 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S7</xref>
###xml 418 420 412 414 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 430 438 424 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;4</xref>
###xml 525 534 519 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S8</xref>
DPM was used to narrow down the locus of action. We circumvented fertilization by injecting ARC substrate, beta-NAD+, which releases Ca2+ upon its conversion to cADPR [18, 19]. DPM blocked the Ca2+ response to beta-NAD+ (p < 0.01) but not to the product, cADPR (p > 0.05, Figures 4G-4I), pointing to an inhibition of ARC that must be indirect (Figure S7D). We also discount side effects of transport inhibitors upon Ca2+ removal (Figure 4H), and upon the activity of cyclic nucleotide phosphodiesterases and cyclo-oxygenase (Figure S8).
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 41 42 38 39 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 63 78 60 75 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib5 bib20 bib23 bib24" ref-type="bibr">[5, 20, 23, 24]</xref>
###xml 112 119 109 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib25 bib26 bib27" ref-type="bibr">[25&#8211;27]</xref>
###xml 190 192 187 189 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">32</sup>
###xml 202 203 196 197 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 205 214 199 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figures 4</xref>
###xml 222 224 216 218 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">32</sup>
###xml 234 235 225 226 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 334 335 322 323 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 364 366 352 354 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">32</sup>
###xml 393 401 381 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;4</xref>
###xml 432 441 420 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S9</xref>
###xml 512 520 496 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;4</xref>
###xml 556 558 540 542 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">32</sup>
###xml 574 582 558 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;4</xref>
More is known about plasmalemmal beta-NAD+ and cADPR transport [5, 20, 23, 24] than organelle nucleotide fluxes [25-27]. Therefore, we characterized transport in cortical granules by using [32P]beta-NAD+ (Figures 4J-4L). [32P]beta-NAD+ uptake into the lumen via specific sites was confirmed with digitonin lysis and unlabeled beta-NAD+, respectively. DPM enhanced 32P accumulation (p < 0.001, Figure 4J) in a dose-dependent manner (Figure S9) and was replicated by NBTI at 1 muM (175 +/- 15%, n = 7) and 10 muM (Figure 4K). By contrast, indoprofen reduced 32P accumulation (Figure 4K).
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 55 56 52 53 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 98 100 95 97 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">32</sup>
###xml 115 118 112 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib5" ref-type="bibr">[5]</xref>
###xml 219 221 216 218 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">32</sup>
###xml 294 296 291 293 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">32</sup>
###xml 318 326 315 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;4</xref>
Our explanation is that indoprofen affects the beta-NAD+ transporter, whereas DPM and NBTI block [32P]cADPR efflux [5], thereby trapping it in the lumen. Indeed, thin-layer chromatography (TLC) analysis confirmed that [32P]cADPR is generated within the lumen and that DPM selectively enhanced [32P]cADPR accumulation (Figure 4L). We conclude that organellar nucleotide transport is an integral element of the ARC signaling cascade.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
Conclusions
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 101 104 101 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib5" ref-type="bibr">[5]</xref>
###xml 251 261 251 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib2 bib5 bib28" ref-type="bibr">[2, 5, 28]</xref>
###xml 309 316 309 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib29 bib30 bib31" ref-type="bibr">[29&#8211;31]</xref>
###xml 443 445 436 438 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 474 482 467 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib16 bib32" ref-type="bibr">[16, 32]</xref>
###xml 505 507 498 500 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 540 544 533 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib32" ref-type="bibr">[32]</xref>
###xml 615 624 608 617 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S4</xref>
###xml 684 692 677 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib15 bib18" ref-type="bibr">[15, 18]</xref>
###xml 726 734 719 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib10 bib33" ref-type="bibr">[10, 33]</xref>
###xml 805 812 798 805 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib5 bib34" ref-type="bibr">[5, 34]</xref>
Our understanding of ARC regulation has been hitherto confounded by the focus upon ectocellular ARCs [5]. Instead, we propose that ARCs natively sequestered within acidic organelles are the most important forms. Reports of ARCs that are intracellular [2, 5, 28] and capable of synthesizing cADPR at acidic pH [29-31] hint at a significance beyond the sea urchin egg. At fertilization, ARCbeta and ARCgamma locally provide cADPR for cortical Ca2+ signals to drive exocytosis [16, 32] and then for global Ca2+ waves following cADPR diffusion [32]. We speculate that multiple mechanisms modulate ARC at fertilization (Figure S4), including transporter phosphorylation (possibly via cGMP [15, 18]), changes in cortical granule pH [10, 33], and exocytosis. Subsequent endocytosis (and endosomal acidification) [5, 34] could even replenish and reactivate membrane-bound ARCs.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 83 87 79 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib35" ref-type="bibr">[35]</xref>
###xml 232 236 228 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib36" ref-type="bibr">[36]</xref>
###xml 292 302 284 294 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S10</xref>
###xml 389 393 381 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib37" ref-type="bibr">[37]</xref>
In spite of the presence of ARCalpha, ectocellular synthesis of cADPR cannot occur [35], probably because of the inhibitory pH of sea water. However, ectocellular proteins reach the cell surface via the lumen of a secretory pathway [36], and ARCalpha is indeed vesicular in immature oocytes (Figure S10), where it may play a role during egg maturation when cADPR responses begin to appear [37]. Thus ARC effectiveness may depend upon when it is in its active, vesicular form.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 90 91 87 88 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 196 197 190 191 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 223 230 217 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib5 bib24" ref-type="bibr">[5, 24]</xref>
###xml 288 291 282 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib7" ref-type="bibr">[7]</xref>
The identity of the transporters is unclear, but the differential pharmacology of beta-NAD+ uptake and cADPR release suggests different proteins. Nonetheless, we can exclude the candidate beta-NAD+ transporter, connexin 43 [5, 24], because connexins are absent from the sea urchin genome [7].
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 223 225 223 225 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 267 269 267 269 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 277 281 277 281 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib33" ref-type="bibr">[33]</xref>
In summary, we propose a model whereby cell-surface stimuli couple to vesicular ARCs by regulating vesicle substrate and/or product transporters and vesicle pH. Our results further raise the profile of acidic vesicles as Ca2+-signaling organelles-not only are they Ca2+ stores [33], but they are also reservoirs of second messenger mobilized by physiological stimuli.
###end p 32

###begin title 38
Preparations of Eggs and Subfractions
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 332 349 332 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app3" ref-type="sec">Supplemental Data</xref>
