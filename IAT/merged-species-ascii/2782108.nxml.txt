###xml 38 43 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The d subunit plays a central role in human vacuolar H+-ATPases
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 33 34 33 34 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 57 58 57 58 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 114 115 114 115 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 162 163 161 162 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 194 195 193 194 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 674 683 673 682 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In silico</italic>
###xml 954 962 953 961 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 614 619 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 648 653 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 723 728 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 927 932 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1116 1119 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
The multi-subunit vacuolar-type H+-ATPase consists of a V1 domain (A-H subunits) catalyzing ATP hydrolysis and a V0 domain (a, c, c', c'', d, e) responsible for H+ translocation. The mammalian V0 d subunit is one of the least-well characterized, and its function and position within the pump are still unclear. It has two different forms encoded by separate genes, d1 being ubiquitous while d2 is predominantly expressed at the cell surface in kidney and osteoclast. To determine whether it forms part of the pump's central stalk as suggested by bacterial A-ATPase studies, or is peripheral as hypothesized from a yeast model, we investigated both human d subunit isoforms. In silico structural modelling demonstrated that human d1 and d2 are structural orthologues of bacterial subunit C, despite poor sequence identity. Expression studies of d1 and d2 showed that each can pull down the central stalk's D and F subunits from human kidney membrane, and in vitro studies using D and F further showed that the interactions between these proteins and the d subunit is direct. These data indicate that the d subunit in man is centrally located within the pump and is thus important in its rotary mechanism.

###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 46 47 46 47 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 289 300 289 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">Forgac 2007</xref>
###xml 324 325 324 325 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 449 450 449 450 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 561 579 561 579 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">Wagner et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 614 631 614 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">Blair et al. 1989</xref>
###xml 666 690 666 690 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">Pastor-Soler et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 767 802 767 802 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">Sennoune and Martinez-Zaguilan 2007</xref>
###xml 806 807 806 807 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
The multi-subunit vacuolar-type proton pump (H+- or V-ATPase) is essential for acidification of diverse intracellular compartments in eukaryotic cells, including endosomes, lysosomes, clathrin-coated and synaptic vesicles, chromaffin granules and the central vacuoles of plants and fungi (Forgac 2007). In higher organisms H+-ATPases also function to pump protons across the plasma membrane of certain specialized cell types. These plasma membrane H+-ATPases are involved in processes such as urinary acidification and the maintenance of acid-base homeostasis (Wagner et al. 2004), bone resorption by osteoclasts (Blair et al. 1989), sperm maturation and viability (Pastor-Soler et al. 2005), and the invasiveness of some tumour cells and vascular endothelial cells (Sennoune and Martinez-Zaguilan 2007). H+-ATPases thus represent potential drug targets in the treatment of diseases such as osteoporosis and cancer.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 1 2 1 2 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 47 48 47 48 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 54 55 54 55 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 73 74 73 74 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 199 200 199 200 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 325 326 324 325 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 387 405 386 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">Hirata et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 407 426 406 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">Imamura et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 476 477 475 476 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 576 577 575 576 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 635 636 634 635 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 808 809 806 807 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 296 301 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
H+-ATPases consist of two functional sectors, V1 and V0. The peripheral V1 domain (subunits A-H) binds and hydrolyzes ATP, providing the energy for proton translocation across the integral membrane V0 domain (subunits a, c, c' c'', d and e). Whether the c' subunit is found in species other than yeast is currently unclear. H+-ATPases have been shown to function via a rotary mechanism (Hirata et al. 2003; Imamura et al. 2003). ATP hydrolysis is carried out by a hexamer of V1 subunits A and B. Energy from this reaction drives the rotation of a central stalk consisting of V1 subunits D and F and this is coupled to rotation of the V0 proteolipid ring made up of c, c' and c''. Proton translocation across the membrane takes place at the interface between the rotating proteolipid ring and the stationary V0 subunit a, which is held fixed in relation to the catalytic head by peripheral stalks which consist of subunits C, E, G and H.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 192 203 192 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">Forgac 2007</xref>
###xml 220 221 220 221 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 497 498 497 498 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
In higher eukaryotes, the B, C, E, G, a, d and e subunits have recently been shown to have multiple forms encoded by different genes and with differing tissue expression patterns (reviewed in Forgac 2007). Importantly, H+-ATPases that function in different physiological processes at different locations appear to contain unique combinations of these subunit isoforms, and thus it may be possible to design isoform-specific inhibitors that could serve as potential therapeutic agents in treating H+-ATPase-related diseases. However for this to be feasible, it is important to understand better the overall structure, subunit composition and function of all the components of the proton pump complex.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 5 6 5 6 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 63 64 63 64 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 357 374 357 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR15">Smith et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 615 634 615 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">Bauerle et al. 1993</xref>
###xml 121 124 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
The V0 d subunit is one of the least characterized of all the H+-ATPase components, and we have previously shown that in man it exists in two different forms, d1 and d2, encoded by separate genes. These are located on chromosomes 16q22 and 8q21 respectively. The d1 gene is ubiquitously expressed and d2 is predominantly expressed in kidney and osteoclast (Smith et al. 2002). The function of the d subunit and its position within the pump complex are still under debate. It associates with the membrane bound V0 domain, probably by protein-protein interactions rather than being directly anchored in the membrane (Bauerle et al. 1993).
###end p 7

###xml 134 139 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 439 445 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
The second hypothesis is based on yeast studies and has resulted from the recent determination of the low-resolution structure of the yeast d subunit in solution using small-angle X-ray scattering data (Thaker et al. 2007), which showed the protein to be a boxing glove shaped molecule. The authors of this study have suggested that their structure corresponds to the peripheral density seen partly covering the cytoplasmic opening of the bovine H+-ATPase proton channel in the 3D structural model derived from single particle image analysis of electron micrographs by Wilkens and Forgac (2001; Fig. 1b). Thus the d subunit would be located above and slightly lateral to the proteolipid ring, possibly forming part of a peripheral stalk, rather than part of the central rotor, as shown schematically in Fig. 1c.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 69 70 69 70 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 111 112 111 112 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 82 85 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
###xml 104 109 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 356 359 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
Here we report an investigation to determine which model applies to H+-ATPases in man. We show that the human H+-ATPase d subunits are the structural orthologues of bacterial A-ATPase subunit C, despite the low level of primary sequence identity that exists between them. We also provide the first experimental evidence demonstrating that the d subunit in man forms part of the pump's central stalk and is thus likely to be important in its rotary mechanism.

Human kidney cDNA, prepared as described previously (Smith et al. 2005), was used as template to amplify the full-length coding regions of ATP6V0D1, ATP6V0D2, ATP6V1D and ATP6V1F with the primers listed in Table 1, using standard polymerase chain reaction (PCR) conditions. After digestion with appropriate restriction endonucleases, PCR products were ligated into either the glutathione S-transferase (GST) fusion vector pGEX-4T-1 (ATP6V0D1 and ATP6V0D2) or the maltose binding protein (MBP) fusion vector pMAL-c4X (ATP6V1D and ATP6V1F). ###begin title 31
Results
###end title 31
###begin title 32
###xml 0 9 0 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In silico</italic>
###xml 44 45 44 45 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 37 42 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In silico structural analysis of the human H+-ATPase d1 and d2 subunits
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 11 12 11 12 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 260 275 260 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T. thermophilus</italic>
###xml 499 516 499 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">Iwata et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 635 650 635 650 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T. thermophilus</italic>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 260 275 <span type="species:ncbi:274">T. thermophilus</span>
###xml 570 575 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 635 650 <span type="species:ncbi:274">T. thermophilus</span>
The human H+-ATPase d1 and d2 subunits are 68% identical and share six conserved cysteine residues. They have rather low amino acid identity of 16.2% and 14.5%, respectively, with subunit C of the related A-ATPase that is present in the thermophilic bacterium T. thermophilus. They also do not share any common cysteine residues with the bacterial subunit. Despite these differences however, the eukaryotic d subunit has been proposed to be similar in structure and function to bacterial subunit C (Iwata et al. 2004). Therefore we first sought to determine whether the human d subunits are likely to be true structural orthologues of T. thermophilus subunit C.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 103 129 103 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">Bennett-Lovsey et al. 2008</xref>
###xml 395 421 395 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">Bennett-Lovsey et al. 2008</xref>
###xml 743 744 743 744 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">e</italic>
###xml 789 790 789 790 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">e</italic>
###xml 1103 1104 1103 1104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">e</italic>
We analysed d1 and d2 using the ensemble-based fold recognition and template modelling software Phyre (Bennett-Lovsey et al. 2008), which compares the query sequence with solved crystal structures from the PDB. This approach was used because it is generally recognised that an ensemble of multiple predictive algorithms usually has greater accuracy than any of the component individual methods (Bennett-Lovsey et al. 2008). Following submission of a query sequence, Phyre identifies any known structures in the PDB on which the query sequence can be modelled, and generates pairwise primary sequence alignments, secondary structure assignments and the coordinates of predicted structural models. The output generated by Phyre also includes an e-value and an estimated precision value. The e-value represents the number of hits that would be expected to have a match equal to or better than the reported one by chance alone. This means that the closer the e-value is to zero, the higher the significance of the match. The estimated precision value represents the percentage of times a match with a given e-value was found to be a true homologue during testing of a benchmark set of proteins and is a measure of the likelihood that the match is correct.
###end p 34

###begin title 50
Discussion
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 14 15 14 15 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 83 84 83 84 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 93 112 93 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">Bauerle et al. 1993</xref>
###xml 422 439 422 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">Iwata et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 546 564 546 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">Thaker et al. 2007</xref>
###xml 467 472 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 609 614 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The vacuolar H+-ATPase d subunit is known to associate with the integral membrane V0 domain (Bauerle et al. 1993), but its function and precise position within the pump complex are still under debate. Two hypotheses regarding its localization have been put forward that predicate potentially different roles, the first suggests that it is similar to bacterial A-ATPase subunit C and constitutes part of the central rotor (Iwata et al. 2004), and the second, based on yeast studies, proposes a more lateral position as part of a peripheral stalk (Thaker et al. 2007). The data from our study indicate that the human d1 and d2 subunits are centrally located within the pump, thereby supporting the first hypothesis.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 6 15 6 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in silico</italic>
###xml 79 80 79 80 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 144 159 144 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T. thermophilus</italic>
###xml 72 77 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 144 159 <span type="species:ncbi:274">T. thermophilus</span>
###xml 546 549 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
Using in silico structural analysis, we have here demonstrated that the human H+-ATPase d subunits show remarkable structural conservation with T. thermophilus A-ATPase subunit C, despite their low level of primary sequence identity, indicating that these proteins are likely to be true structural, and presumably functional, orthologues. Therefore, despite the extensive sequence divergence that has occurred, these subunits must have been subjected to strong structural and functional evolutionary pressure during the evolution of bacteria and man.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 341 342 340 341 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 394 411 393 410 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">Iwata et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 527 545 526 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">Makyio et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 1016 1017 1015 1016 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1285 1293 1284 1292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 693 698 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1009 1014 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1089 1094 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The solved structure of bacterial subunit C indicates that the protein has an alpha-helical three-domain structure and an overall shape resembling a bottle-stopper. Cross-linking experiments have shown that it is in close proximity to the L subunits of the proteolipid ring, which correspond to the c, c' and c'' subunits of the eukaryotic H+-ATPase, as well as subunit F of the central stalk (Iwata et al. 2004). Further cross-linking experiments have shown that subunits D and F are in turn in close proximity to each other (Makyio et al. 2005). It has thus been proposed that subunit C has a socket-like function attaching the central stalk subunits D and F to the proteolipid ring. If the human d subunits have a similar role to bacterial subunit C, one would predict that they should interact with D and F, and also with components of the proteolipid ring. In this study we have provided the first experimental evidence showing that d1 and d2 do indeed interact directly with the D and F subunits of the human H+-ATPase. Unfortunately we are presently unable to determine whether the human d subunits are also able to bind to the proteolipid subunits, since there are no antibodies available that recognize these proteins, nor have these proteins ever been successfully expressed in vitro.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 226 246 226 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">Thaker et al. (2007)</xref>
###xml 721 736 721 736 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T. thermophilus</italic>
###xml 812 829 812 829 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">Owegi et al. 2006</xref>
###xml 969 970 969 970 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1116 1136 1116 1136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">Sun-Wada et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 1164 1178 1164 1178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">Su et al. 2008</xref>
###xml 334 339 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 611 616 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 721 736 <span type="species:ncbi:274">T. thermophilus</span>
###xml 779 784 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1043 1048 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1090 1095 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1146 1151 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our data do not support the alternative hypothesis-that the eukaryotic d subunit is located above and lateral to the proteolipid ring, without any connection to it, possibly forming part of a peripheral stalk, as suggested by Thaker et al. (2007). These authors observed structural differences between the bacterial subunit C and the yeast d subunit, with the latter being not only larger, but also more anisometric than subunit C. However, the apparent differences might be due to methodological variation, as they were comparing the subunit C crystal structure with a low-resolution solution structure of the yeast d subunit etermined from small-angle X-ray scattering data. They also suggest that the inability of the T. thermophilus orthologue to rescue the phenotype of the yeast d subunit deletion strain (Owegi et al. 2006) indicates that the proteins are not functional equivalents. This is not necessarily the case as there are several examples of eukaryotic H+-ATPase subunits being unable functionally to complement the orthologous yeast subunit deletion strain, for example the mouse G1 and G3 subunits (Sun-Wada et al. 2003) and the human a4 subunit (Su et al. 2008).
###end p 54

The idea of peripheral stalk residency for the d subunit came in part from consideration of the 3D structural model of the bovine H+-ATPase proton channel generated by Wilkens and Forgac (2001), in which there are two protein densities covering the cytoplasmic opening of the channel. Although as described earlier, the more peripheral one is that proposed by Thaker and collaborators to correspond to subunit d (Fig. 1), Wilkens and collaborators suggested that the central density has a clear three-domain structure like bacterial subunit C (Fig. 7) and is thus likely to be the d subunit (Wilkens et al. 2005). Our data support this model for localization of the d subunit within the pump complex. If it forms part of the central stalk, it is likely to play a role in the rotary mechanism of the H+-ATPase, and therefore be important in the coupling of ATP hydrolysis and proton transport as has been proposed recently (Nishi et al. 2003; Owegi et al. 2006). ###xml 83 85 83 85 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">36</sub>

###xml 910 927 910 927 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">Iwata et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 929 947 929 947 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">Numoto et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1073 1074 1073 1074 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1195 1212 1195 1212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">Iwata et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 30 35 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 214 217 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
###xml 230 245 <span type="species:ncbi:274">T. thermophilus</span>
###xml 543 548 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
It has been suggested that in yeast, the formation of a disulphide bond between Cys36 and Cys329 is important for folding (Thaker et al. 2007). These cysteines are conserved across all eukaryotic species including man, but not in T. thermophilus. However, how important disulphide bond formation is to the function of subunit d is unclear, since the protein is normally present in the reducing environment of the cytoplasm where such bonds would not usually persist. Indeed, this bond had little effect on in vitro tertiary structure. Also, a yeast strain expressing a C329A d subunit mutation retained 72% proton transport and 41% ATPase activity levels relative to wild type at its vacuolar membrane (Owegi et al. 2006), suggesting that the substitution is not highly disruptive. Furthermore, the bond between Cys267 and Cys322 seen in both hexagonal and monoclinic forms of the bacterial crystal structure (Iwata et al. 2004; Numoto et al. 2004), was proposed by Iwata to be an artefact formed during sample preparation under aerobic conditions. In addition, bacterial H+-ATPases containing mutant subunit C with both cysteines replaced by serine are properly assembled and fully functional (Iwata et al. 2004). All these data suggest that disulphide bond formation is not critical to the function of these orthologous proteins in their native cellular environments.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 96 97 96 97 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 243 244 243 244 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 407 408 407 408 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 410 411 410 411 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 704 721 704 721 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">Smith et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 52 55 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
In summary, our data indicate that the d subunit in man forms part of the central rotor of the H+-ATPase. The determination of the location of proton pump components is important, since in order to facilitate the therapeutic targeting of the H+-ATPase in diseases such as osteoporosis and cancer, it is essential to understand the proper isoform composition and precise localization of subunits within the V1/V0 complex. Such information may aid the development of small molecules that can target exposed regions of the complex in an isoform-specific manner. For example, the possible exclusive co-expression of the a3 and d2 subunit isoforms in the ruffled border of osteoclasts, described previously, (Smith et al. 2005), may in the future allow the design of antiresorptive agents that can specifically target this specialized proton pump in the treatment of osteoporosis.
