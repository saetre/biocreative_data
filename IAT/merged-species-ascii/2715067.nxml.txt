###begin article-title 0
iNKT cell development is orchestrated by different branches of TGF-beta signaling
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
K.-P. Yan's present address is The Wistar Institute, Philadelphia, PA 19104.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
J.-M. Doisne and L. Bartholin contributed equally to this paper.
###end p 2
###begin p 3
Invariant natural killer T (iNKT) cells constitute a distinct subset of T lymphocytes exhibiting important immune-regulatory functions. Although various steps of their differentiation have been well characterized, the factors controlling their development remain poorly documented. Here, we show that TGF-beta controls the differentiation program of iNKT cells. We demonstrate that TGF-beta signaling carefully and specifically orchestrates several steps of iNKT cell development. In vivo, this multifaceted role of TGF-beta involves the concerted action of different pathways of TGF-beta signaling. Whereas the Tif-1gamma branch controls lineage expansion, the Smad4 branch maintains the maturation stage that is initially repressed by a Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent branch. Thus, these three different branches of TGF-beta signaling function in concert as complementary effectors, allowing TGF-beta to fine tune the iNKT cell differentiation program.
###end p 3

###xml 797 798 797 798 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">4</xref>
Natural killer T cells (NKT) are a unique T cell lineage with the capacity to regulate immune responses. NKT cells are multifunctional. They have been described as being important in a variety of immune responses, including the response to tumors, infectious agents, the maintenance of self-tolerance, and the prevention of autoimmunity (1, 2). In contrast to conventional T lymphocytes and other regulatory T cells, NKT cells do not recognize peptide antigen presented by classical MHC-encoded class I or II molecules; instead, their TCR interacts with glycolipid antigen presented by the MHC class I-like molecule CD1d (3). These T lymphocytes are mainly CD4+CD8-, but a subset can be CD4-CD8-. NKT cells branch away from T cell lineage at the double-positive (DP) CD4+CD8+ stage in the thymus (4).

###xml 1840 1841 1796 1797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">5</xref>
Invariant NKT (iNKT) cells represent a subclass of NKT cells. They derive from DP thymocytes that express a TCR with a CDR3alpha region formed by a semiinvariant rearrangement of Valpha14-Jalpha18. iNKT cells are positively selected upon recognition of CD1d expressed on DP thymocytes loaded with glycolipids (1). iNKT react to a glycosphingolipid alpha-galactosylceramide (alphaGalCer) presented by CD1d (5). CD1d-alphaGalCer tetramers, which uniformly stain all iNKT cells, have been used to characterize some NKT cell developmental steps and precursors. As CD1d-alphaGalCer tetramer+ precursors progress to mature HSAlow cells, three differentiation stages have been reported. iNKT cells go from the CD44low NK1.1- stage, through the CD44high NK1.1- stage, and finally reach the CD44high NK1.1+ stage. This iNKT differentiation sequence is accompanied by a massive cellular expansion occurring between the CD44low NK1.1- stage and the CD44high NK1.1- stage. A majority of CD44high NK1.1- cells emigrate to the peripheral tissues, where they cease to proliferate and rapidly express a mature phenotype, becoming CD44high NK1.1+ and producing both IL-4 and IFN-gamma (6). iNKT cells represent approximately2.5% of splenic T lymphocytes and 30% of liver T cells (5). Interestingly, a fraction of the CD44high NK1.1- cells do not emigrate to the periphery, but instead proceed to their terminal maturation inside the thymus, where they acquire the NK1.1 marker and other NK-related markers that characterize the lineage (e.g., Ly49 and NKG2 family members). The CD44high NK.1.1+ population constitutes long-lived resident cells for several months (7). Although the importance of peripheral iNKT cells in the regulation of immune system has been widely reported, the significance of the iNKT intrathymic long-lived residents remains unknown (5).

###xml 883 885 877 879 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">14</xref>
###xml 887 889 881 883 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">15</xref>
Although it is known that CD1d expression on DP thymocytes is required for iNKT cell selection and lineage differentiation (6), and that both IL-15 and CD122 (IL-15Rbeta) expression are involved in the transition between the CD44high NK1.1- and CD44high NK1.1+ stages, factors that control iNKT cell differentiation are poorly documented (8, 9). Some studies have proposed a role for two cytokines, GM-CSF and lymphotoxin, in iNKT cell development (10, 11). However, a recent investigation has reported that GM-CSF does not affect iNKT cell numbers in the thymus or in the periphery, but rather regulates their ability to secrete cytokines (12). Moreover, a role for lymphotoxin has been ascribed in iNKT cell emigration from the thymus rather than in iNKT cell development (13). Recently, it has been suggested, by us and others, that TGF-beta could influence iNKT differentiation (14, 15).

###begin p 7
###xml 884 886 854 856 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">16</xref>
###xml 1363 1365 1319 1321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">17</xref>
###xml 1367 1369 1323 1325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">18</xref>
TGF-beta is a widely expressed cytokine involved in the control of proliferation and differentiation of numerous cell types both inside and outside the immune system. The three isoforms of TGF-beta (TGF-beta1, TGF-beta2, and TGF-beta3) signal through a common serine-threonine kinase receptor complex composed of the TGF-betaRI and TGF-betaRII subunits. Binding of TGF-beta to its receptor leads to the activation of the intracellular kinase domain of TGF-betaRII, which in turn phosphorylates the kinase domain of the TGF-betaRI subunit. The latter then phosphorylates the receptor-associated Smads (R-Smads), Smad2 and/or Smad3, which bind the common Smad (C-Smad), Smad4. Complexes of Smad2-Smad4 and Smad3-Smad4 then migrate to the nucleus and regulate the transcription of various genes. Interestingly, Smad-independent pathways, such as MEK/MAP kinase, have also been reported (16). For many years Smad4 had been considered to be an obligate partner for the R-Smads Smad2 and Smad3 and the mediation of the TGF-beta response through the Smad branch. However, the differences in phenotype observed between R-Smad-null and Smad4-null organisms suggested that Smad2 and Smad3 could have Smad4-independent biological functions. A role has recently been proposed for the transcriptional intermediary factor 1gamma (Tif-1gamma) in the TGF-beta signaling pathway (17, 18). This ubiquitous protein (also known as TRIM33, RFG7, PTC7, and ectodermin) selectively binds receptor-phosphorylated Smad2/Smad3 in competition with Smad4. Although the Smad2-Tif-1gamma interaction has biological effects independent of the Smad3-Tif-1gamma complex in vitro, the importance of the Tif-1gamma branch in TGF-beta signaling in vivo remains unknown.
###end p 7

###xml 66 70 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>

TGF-beta signaling plays a crucial role on T cell biology because mice selectively deprived of TGF-betaRI or -betaRII on their T cells develop massive autoimmunity and die by 3 wk of age (15, 19). Thymic development of conventional CD4 and CD8 T cells does not appear to be under TGF-beta control, but TGF-beta seems clearly involved in the peripheral differentiation of selective T cell subsets such as Foxp3+ regulatory T cells and Th17 cells (15, 20, 21, 22). Interestingly, the complete deprivation of TGF-beta signaling in alpha/beta T lymphocytes, obtained by the crossing of a strain of floxed TGF-betaRII mice with CD4-Cre transgenic mice (CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl), leads to a sharp defect in CD1d-alphaGalCer tetramer+ NK.1.1+ cells in both the thymus and the spleen (14, 15). These observations suggest that TGF-beta could control the thymic development of the iNKT cells. However, given that the ablation of TGF-beta signaling in T cells is associated with strong autoimmune disorders, T cell activation and acute cytotoxicity (15), it has been difficult to decipher whether the defect of iNKT cells in CD4-Cre x TGF-RIIfl/fl mice is a direct effect of TGF-beta on iNKT cell precursors or the consequence of autoimmune disorders that affect the capacity of the thymus environment to differentiate iNKT cells. Whether TGF-beta constitutes a specific factor directly controlling the thymic differentiation of iNKT precursor thus remains totally unknown.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
In this study, we demonstrate that TGF-beta signaling directly orchestrates several steps of iNKT precursor differentiation, ascribing a crucial and specific role for this cytokine in iNKT thymic development. We provide evidence that three branches of TGF-beta signaling, Smad4, Tif-1gamma, and the Tif-1gamma-Smad4-independent signaling pathway, function in concert. Each of these TGF-beta signaling branches is selectively dedicated to the control of a specific differentiation step of iNKT cells, allowing TGF-beta to fine tune the iNKT cell differentiation program.

We have previously reported that thymic development of conventional CD4, CD8 T lymphocytes is not affected by TGF-beta signaling (14, 15, 21). In the thymus, iNKT cells branch away from the T cell lineage at the CD4+CD8+ (DP) stage and are positively selected upon recognition of CD1d expressed on DP cells that are themselves loaded with glycolipids. Thus, a putative effect of TGF-beta on the thymic development of iNKT cells could affect iNKT precursors alone, the capacity of DP to select iNKT precursors, or both. To test these three hypotheses, we have performed mixed BM transfers in reconstituted, sublethally irradiated B cell- and T cell-deficient recipients (rag2ko). BM cells from CD1dko mice, which DP cells can lead to iNKT precursors but cannot positively select, were transferred into rag2ko recipients with congenic Ly9.1-expressing BM cells from either CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl mice or CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIwt littermate controls (Fig. 1 A). In CD4-Cre mice, the Cre transgene is expressed at the DP stage of thymocyte development. Consistent with this, DP thymocytes and their progeny from CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl BM cells escape TGF-beta control. 6-7 wk after BM transfers, we analyzed the capacity of CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl DP thymocytes to complement the deficiency of DP from CD1dko BM to select iNKT cells. Strikingly, iNKT cells from CD1dKO donors were found in the spleens of all chimeric mice. However, iNKT cells from CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl BM were barely detectable compared with iNKT cells from CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIwt BM (Fig. 1 A). In addition, from the CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl/CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIwt mixed BM transfer, only iNKT cells from wild-type mouse BM were detectable (unpublished data). Thus, in the absence of TGF-beta control, DP thymocytes remain a suitable environment for iNKT differentiation. In agreement with this, the expression of CD1d at the surface of DP thymocytes from CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl mice was similar to that of DP cells from littermate controls (unpublished data) and co-culture of Valpha14 hybridoma cell lines with DP thymocytes from either CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl mice or littermate controls showed no significant difference in the capacity of DP to present alphaGalCer antigen (Fig. 1 B).

Thymic iNKT precursor differentiation goes through several established chronological steps defined by CD44 and NK1.1 expression. To determine the effects of TGF-beta signaling on iNKT precursor differentiation, CD44 and NK1.1 stainings were performed on CD1d-alphaGalCer tetramer+ thymocytes, purified either from CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl mice or CD4-Cre x Stopfl/fl TGF-betaRICA mice, the latter exhibiting a constitutively active (CA) TGF-beta signaling in their T cells, and a normal thymic development of CD4 and CD8 conventional T lymphocyte (21). Whereas in wild-type thymus, iNKT cell precursors were mainly NK1.1- at 2 wk of age, CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl iNKT cell precursors from littermate animals exhibited a repartition among the different steps of differentiation that was more similar to what is usually observed in adult mice. The early stages of development were rarely represented because the majority of the precursors were NK1.1+ and also express NK markers such as NKG2D, NKG2A/C/E, and Ly49C/I (). In clear contrast with the aforementioned observations, our analysis on CD1d-alphaGalCer-tetramer+ thymocytes purified from CD4-Cre x Stopfl/fl TGF-betaRICA mice revealed an overrepresentation of the early differentiation stage CD44low NK1.1- and a large deficiency in both CD44high NK1.1- precursors and the mature CD44high NK1.1+ cell subset (Fig. 2 A). Moreover, as in the thymus, the few iNKT cells observed at the periphery of CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl mice were mostly NK1.1+ (Fig. 2, B and C), but produced both IFN-gamma and IL-4, which is similar to iNKT cells from wild-type animals (Fig. 2 C), suggesting that these cells are fully functional. In clear contrast, the overexpression of TGF-beta signaling in T cells lead to an increase of peripheral CD1d-alphaGalCer tetramers+ T cells, but these cells presented a defect of maturation with both a defective NK.1.1 expression and a weak IFN-gamma production compared with iNKT cells from wild-type animals (Fig. 2, B and C).

Because the iNKT differentiation step between the CD44low NK1.1- and the CD44high NK.1.1- stage is accompanied by a massive cellular expansion (6), it is possible that the defect of both CD44low NK1.1- and CD44high NK.1.1- cells observed in CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl mice, results from an impairment of their proliferation capacity. Given that the role of the Smad4 branch appears to be restricted to the late maturation steps of iNKT precursors, we proceeded by investigating the contribution of another TGF-beta signaling pathway, the Tif-1gamma branch, in iNKT cell development. We generated mice selectively deficient for Tif-1gamma in their T cells, from the DP stage, by breeding CD4-Cre transgenic animals with floxed tif-1gamma mice (). The CD4-Cre x Tif-1gammafl/fl mice did not develop any sign of autoimmunity and inflammation (unpublished data). Moreover, in these animals, the conventional CD4/CD8 T cell development in the thymus was similar that observed in wild-type animals (Fig. S3 B). However, as observed in CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl animals, the deprivation of the Tif-1gamma-dependent signaling pathway led to a large decrease in the proportion of CD1d-alphaGalCer-tetramer+ cells in the thymus. As in the absence of total TGF-beta signaling, the deprivation of the Tif-1gamma branch led to a large defect in CD44low NK1.1- precursors associated with their apoptosis and to a loss of CD127 surface expression (Fig. 6, A and B). However, contrary to what we observed in CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl mice, no effect, either at the CD44high NK1.1+ precursor stage or on CD122 and T-bet expression, was observed in CD4-Cre x Tif-1gammafl/fl mice (unpublished data), excluding a potential role for the Tif-1gamma branch in the transition of CD44high NK1.1- precursors to CD44high NK1.1+ precursors.

###xml 1408 1410 1382 1384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">25</xref>
###xml 1412 1414 1386 1388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib29">29</xref>
###xml 1893 1895 1847 1849 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">15</xref>
###xml 1897 1899 1851 1853 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">21</xref>
###xml 136 140 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 166 171 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
iNKT cells constitute a unique thymus-dependent T cell subset, characterized by the expression of a semiinvariant (Valpha14-Jalpha18 in mice and Valpha24-Jalpha18 in human) TCR-alpha chain and markers traditionally associated with the NK cell lineage. iNKT cells are developmentally and functionally distinct from the mainstream CD4 and CD8 T cells. iNKT cells have been associated with a broad array of disorders, ranging from transplant rejection to tumors, various forms of autoimmunity, atherosclerosis, allergy, and infection (5). Because of the importance of iNKT cells in immune regulation during various pathological processes (28), it is critical to define the factors that govern their development. Our findings reveal TGF-beta to be a key factor for thymic iNKT cell development. Although thymic precursors of both iNKT cells and conventional T cells require interaction with their respective self-ligands in the thymus environment for positive selection, the HSAlow iNKT cell precursors subsequently undergo a complex series of activation and differentiation events. We have clearly demonstrated that TGF-beta directly orchestrates the sequence of thymic events that iNKT precursors undergo. This sequence includes a lineage expansion, acquisition of the CD44high phenotype before either their migration to the periphery or their long stay in the thymus, and their maturation to CD44high NK1.1+ (25, 29). Unlike IL-15, whose action is restricted to the maturation step, we revealed that TGF-beta controls several key steps of iNKT cell differentiation, ascribing a crucial role for this cytokine iNKT cell development. Moreover, although iNKT cells and conventional alpha/beta T cells are derived from a common lineage at the DP stage, neither the ablation nor the overexpression of TGF-beta signaling in DP cells affects the thymic development of conventional alpha/beta T cells (15, 21). Thus, TGF-beta appears as a key factor whose action in the thymus is confined to the control of several steps of iNKT cell differentiation.

###xml 1738 1740 1700 1702 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib31">31</xref>
###xml 2000 2003 1951 1954 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 2011 2015 1962 1966 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>

###xml 1489 1498 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
iNKT cell lineage expansion has been regarded as the consequence of multiple rounds of cell division occurring between the CD44low NK1.1- and CD44high NK1.1- stages (5). Our results suggest that when they escape from TGF-beta control, the CD44low NK1.1- and CD44high NK1.1- precursors keep dividing, but a large proportion of them undergo apoptosis, which dramatically reduces the pool of cells for the next iNKT differentiation steps, and thus reduces the total number of iNKT cells. Our observations strongly indicate that the iNKT lineage expansion is not only restricted to precursor cell proliferation, but also results from the survival maintenance of these dividing precursors by TGF-beta. This TGF-beta antiapoptotic effect is correlated with the maintenance of IL-7Ralpha (CD127) expression at the cell surface of iNKT precursors, suggesting a role for the IL-7/IL-7R signaling pathway in early iNKT precursors' survival. In agreement with this, a large decrease in iNKT precursors has been reported in IL-7KO mice (24). Furthermore, our findings demonstrate that both the survival of iNKT early precursors and the maintenance of CD127 expression are under the control of the Tif-1gamma branch of TGF-beta signaling. However, whether Tif-1gamma directly controls CD127 expression remains to be investigated. Tif-1gamma is a ubiquitous nuclear protein that has been recently shown to be involved in hematopoietic cell fate via the TGF-beta pathway in vitro (17). Interestingly, in zebrafish, a mutation in mon, which is a gene identified as being an orthologous gene of tif-1gamma in mammals, leads to massive apoptosis of hematopoietic cells (30). Moreover, that TGF-beta has largely been reported to play an antiapoptotic role (31) supports our results, suggesting that the Tif1-gamma branch could be a key signaling pathway for the TGF-beta antiapoptotic effect. In addition to the control of apoptosis, Tif-1gamma could regulate CD44 expression, and thus control the progression from CD44low to CD44high precursors.
###end p 41
###begin p 42

###xml 1898 1901 1862 1865 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38</italic>
###xml 1906 1909 1870 1873 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Erk</italic>
###xml 2125 2128 2089 2092 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 2134 2135 2098 2099 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 2144 2148 2108 2112 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 2154 2155 2118 2119 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 2566 2567 2519 2520 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 2608 2613 2561 2566 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">fl/fl</sup>
###xml 2624 2626 2574 2576 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CA</sup>
###xml 1029 1033 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1198 1202 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 2627 2631 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 2954 2958 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
After the expansion phase, precursors that become CD44high NK1.1- can either stay in the thymus or colonize peripheral organs and undergo a maturation phase accompanied by the cell-surface expression of both CD122 (IL-15betaR chain) and NK1.1. This maturation step has been reported to be dependent on the expression of T-bet, a transcription factor originally implicated in the Th1 cell lineage commitment (26). T-bet has been shown to induce CD122 in iNKT cell precursors (32). We have demonstrated a role for TGF-beta in the repression of both T-bet and CD122 expression in iNKT cell precursors. Interestingly, the ectopic expression of T-bet in immature iNKT cell precursors has been shown to be sufficient to induce their full maturation and function (32). Given the central role of T-bet in iNKT cell maturation, by controlling the expression of T-bet in iNKT cells, TGF-beta is a key regulator for iNKT cell maturation. The absence of overrepresentation of CD44high NK1.1+ mature stage in both single- and double-knockout mice for Tif-1gamma and Smad4 revealed that a Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent branch of TGF-beta signaling represses iNKT precursor maturation. Interestingly, in wild-type mice, TGF-beta signaling in CD44high NK1.1- precursors is impaired, leading to iNKT precursor maturation. Several arguments suggest the MEK/MAPK branch as a putative candidate of the Tif1-gamma/Smad4-independent pathway to explain how TGF-beta represses both T-bet and iNKT precursor maturation. TGF-beta has been proposed to repress T-bet during Th1/Th2 lineage commitment in vitro. Recent studies have demonstrated that this repression is under the MEK signaling pathway (33). Moreover, in wild-type animals, we found that the down-regulation of TGF-beta signaling at the CD44high NK1.1- stage was associated with the repression of mek1, whereas the expression of other actors of the MAPK, such as p38 and Erk, remained constant during iNKT cell differentiation (). Additional experiments should be carried out to confirm the role of MEK in iNKT cell differentiation. By repressing the maturation of both highly dividing CD44low NK1.1- and CD44high NK1.1- cells, the Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent pathway allows precursors to stay longer in highly divided stages, and thus could also contribute to promote iNKT lineage expansion along with the Tif-1gamma branch. Interestingly, the TGF-beta signaling effects on the control of iNKT maturation are not restricted to the thymus because iNKT peripheral maturation was similarly affected. The large defect of mature NK1.1+ cells at the periphery in CD4-Cre x Stopfl/fl TGF-betaRICA mice is also associated with their incapacity to produce IFN-gamma, underlining the role of TGF-beta in the repression of iNKT cell maturation. Moreover, the rare but fully mature, peripheral iNKT cells generated in the absence of TGF-beta control produce both IL-4 and IFN-gamma, which is similar to iNKT cells from wild-type mice, suggesting that TGF-beta is not mandatory for iNKT cell function.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 13 17 13 17 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 23 24 23 24 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 197 201 188 192 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 207 208 198 199 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 479 483 467 471 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 489 490 477 478 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1018 1023 989 994 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">fl/fl</sup>
###xml 1024 1028 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Maturing CD44high NK1.1+ precursors express high levels of TGF-betaR at their cell surface, and their responsiveness to TGF-beta is highly increased. This reactivation of TGF-beta signaling in CD44high NK1.1+ precursors is not associated with a repression of T-bet expression. This apparent discrepancy can be explained by the relay role of the Smad4 branch of TGF-beta signaling. The Smad4 branch is responsible for the maintenance of both T-bet and CD122 expression in the CD44high NK1.1+ precursors, and allows them to mature and acquire a high level of NK1.1. Our data suggest that once precursors have turned on their maturation program, the Smad4 branch thwarts the TGF-beta inhibitory effect on maturation driven by the Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent branch. Thus, TGF-beta has a double-edged sword effect on iNKT cell differentiation, through the Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent branch, which represses their maturation, and through the Smad4 branch, which continues the maturation process. In CD4-Cre x TGF-betaRIIfl/fl mice, all TGF-beta signaling branches are turned off, thus the maturation is not repressed by Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent branch, and the iNKT cell precursor matures fully.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 986 988 946 948 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib34">34</xref>
###xml 1108 1115 1064 1071 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Xenopus</italic>
###xml 1296 1298 1244 1246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">18</xref>
###xml 1671 1673 1608 1610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">17</xref>
###xml 1108 1115 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus</span>
###xml 1423 1428 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1644 1649 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Therefore, the multifaceted role of TGF-beta during iNKT cell differentiation can be explained not only by the concerted action of different signaling branches, such as the Tif-1gamma branch and the Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent branch, but also by the interaction, or competition, between branches, such as the Smad4 branch and the Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent branch. Interestingly, the analysis of TGF-beta branches during the differentiation of several cell types has also suggested an interaction between the TGF-beta signaling pathways. In numerous cancers, at the early stage of tumor development, TGF-beta represses tumor-growth, whereas at later stages TGF-beta facilitates tumor progression. The activation of the Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent branch (MAPK) by TGF-beta has been proposed to facilitate tumor progression by TGF-beta. The loss of tumor growth inhibition by TGF-beta and the acquisition of oncogenic capacity correlates with the loss of the Smad4 branch function (34). Competition between the Smad4 branch and the Tif-1gamma branch has also been suggested. During the blastula stage of Xenopus, a role for Tif-1gamma in the targeting of Smad4 for ubiquitination and degradation has been reported, suggesting a potential regulation of the Smad4 branch by Tif-1gamma protein (18). However, the interactions between TGF-beta signaling branches appear to be specific for a given cell type, because during human hematopoiesis, although two distinct roles have been ascribed to the Tif-1gamma branch and the Smad4 branch, no variation in Smad4 protein levels have been reported after overexpression or deletion of Tif-1gamma in human hematopoietic cells (17). In iNKT cell differentiation, the expression levels of both Tif-1gamma and Smad4 stay constant over the different stages of iNKT cell development (unpublished data) suggesting that the Tif-1gamma branch and the Smad4 branch do not interfere with one another, but work in concert during a cellular differentiation program, each branch dedicated to a specific task.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 1153 1159 1116 1122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig8">Fig. 8</xref>
In sum, our results propose that TGF-beta is a key regulator of iNKT cell differentiation. We have revealed that through different branches of its signaling pathway, TGF-beta can fine tune the complex iNKT cell differentiation cell program. These different signaling pathways function as complementary effector arms that, in association with a modulation of TGF-beta responsiveness between the differentiation stages, can explain the multiple roles of TGF-beta during iNKT cell development. TGF-beta assures lineage expansion via the Tif-1gamma branch and maturation acquisition via the Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent branch, whereas the maintenance of the maturation process involves the Smad4-dependent pathway. At an early stage, the Tif-1gamma/Smad4-independent branch represses precursor maturation and allows iNKT lineage expansion in concert with Tif-1gamma branch. Next, the TGF-beta responsiveness is impaired, allowing precursor maturation. Finally, maturing precursors restore their capacity to fully respond to TGF-beta and the Smad4 branch assures the continuation of the maturation program by maintaining high level of both T-bet and CD122 (Fig. 8). The action of each branch is conditioned by the action of the others. These sequential roles of the TGF-beta signaling branches during iNKT cell development ascribe a crucial role for TGF-beta in the iNKT cell differentiation.
###end p 45
