###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 170 177 170 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 325 344 325 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium avium</italic>
###xml 352 368 352 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">paratuberculosis</italic>
###xml 325 368 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">Mycobacterium avium subsp. paratuberculosis</span>
###xml 370 373 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
The etiology of type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM) is still unknown; numerous studies are performed to unravel the environmental factors involved in triggering the disease. SLC11A1 is a membrane transporter that is expressed in late endosomes of antigen presenting cells involved in the immunopathogenic events leading to T1DM. Mycobacterium avium subsp. paratuberculosis (MAP) has been reported to be a possible trigger in the development of T1DM.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 87 94 87 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 263 270 263 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 16 24 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 134 137 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 209 217 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 315 323 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 361 364 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 413 421 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Fifty nine T1DM patients and 79 healthy controls were genotyped for 9 polymorphisms of SLC11A1 gene, and screened for the presence of MAP by PCR. Differences in genotype frequency were evaluated for both T1DM patients and controls. We found a polymorphism in the SLC11A1 gene (274C/T) associated to type 1 diabetic patients and not to controls. The presence of MAP DNA was also significantly associated with T1DM patients and not with controls.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 11 18 11 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SCL11A1</italic>
###xml 258 265 258 265 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 96 99 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 120 123 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 306 309 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 369 377 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The 274C/T SCL11A1 polymorphism was found to be associated with T1DM as well as the presence of MAP DNA in blood. Since MAP persists within macrophages and it is also processed by dendritic cells, further studies are necessary to evaluate if mutant forms of SLC11A1 alter the processing or presentation of MAP antigens triggering thereby an autoimmune response in T1DM patients.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 188 191 188 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Akerblom1">[1]</xref>
###xml 514 517 514 517 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Dow1">[2]</xref>
###xml 519 522 519 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Knip1">[3]</xref>
Type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM) is a multifactorial autoimmune disease in which T-lymphocytes infiltrate the islets of the pancreas and destroy the insulin-producing beta cell populations [1]. The exact cause of T1DM is not clearly known. However T1DM constitutes interactions of polygenic traits with environmental factors that are not clearly defined in the available literature and it is not known what triggers autoimmunity to self-antigens such as those expressed in the pancreatic islets of Langerhans cells [2], [3].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 49 68 49 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium avium</italic>
###xml 76 92 76 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">paratuberculosis</italic>
###xml 154 157 154 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Dow1">[2]</xref>
###xml 159 162 159 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Rosu1">[4]</xref>
###xml 164 167 164 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Sechi1">[5]</xref>
###xml 270 273 270 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Polymeros1">[6]</xref>
###xml 390 393 390 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Sechi2">[7]</xref>
###xml 512 515 512 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Rosu1">[4]</xref>
###xml 774 777 774 777 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Knip1">[3]</xref>
###xml 779 782 779 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Daneman1">[8]</xref>
###xml 784 787 784 787 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Guarner1">[9]</xref>
###xml 789 793 789 793 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Rook1">[10]</xref>
###xml 49 92 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">Mycobacterium avium subsp. paratuberculosis</span>
###xml 94 97 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 169 172 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 323 326 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 335 343 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 421 424 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 447 455 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 503 511 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 694 697 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
Accumulating line of evidence points to role for Mycobacterium avium subsp. paratuberculosis (MAP) in the development of T1DM as an environmental trigger [2], [4], [5]. MAP bacteria have been generally known to harness molecular mimicry as a strategy to avoid clearance [6]. Recently our group observed immune responses to MAP in T1DM patients, thus supporting an infectious cause for T1DM [7]. Moreover, the presence of MAP was confirmed in T1DM patients by culture and was isolated from blood of T1DM patients [4]. It has long been held that genetic susceptibilities, epitope homologies, and endemic bacterial load in the environment might support the case for an infectious trigger, such as MAP, to be the probable agent of T1DM in genetically susceptibility individuals [3], [8], [9], [10].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 38 45 38 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 72 78 72 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NRAMP1</italic>
###xml 208 212 208 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Blackwell1">[11]</xref>
###xml 214 218 214 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Bellamy1">[12]</xref>
###xml 220 224 220 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Nishino1">[13]</xref>
###xml 226 230 226 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Sechi3">[14]</xref>
###xml 254 259 254 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 261 265 261 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Kissler1">[15]</xref>
###xml 284 291 284 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 464 471 464 471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 578 585 578 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 705 709 705 709 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Dai1">[16]</xref>
###xml 330 334 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Regarding genetic susceptibility, the SLC11A1 gene (previously known as NRAMP1) is a functional and a positional candidate gene that associates with T1DM as well as susceptibility to mycobacterial infections [11], [12], [13], [14]. Additionally, Kissler et al. [15] demonstrated that SLC11A1 gene silencing using RNAi approach in mice reduced the frequency of T1DM and protected against experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis, advocating thereby for a role for SLC11A1 in autoimmunity. Moreover, it was recently demonstrated that association of variants of the gene encoding SLC11A1 with T1DM may reflect its function in processing and presentation of islet cell self-antigens to dendritic cells (DCs) [16].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
Thus, non-MHC genes could affect the MHC-restricted T-cell response through altered antigen processing and presentation.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 42 49 42 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 146 150 146 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Blackwell2">[17]</xref>
###xml 226 233 226 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 339 343 339 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Blackwell2">[17]</xref>
###xml 345 353 345 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vitro</italic>
###xml 518 522 518 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Rook1">[10]</xref>
###xml 524 528 524 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Blackwell2">[17]</xref>
###xml 560 567 560 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 707 711 707 711 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Nishino1">[13]</xref>
###xml 713 717 713 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Takahashi1">[18]</xref>
To date, a number of polymorphisms at the SLC11A1 locus have been associated with susceptibility to infectious agents and to autoimmune disorders [17]. Specifically, a 5'(GT)n repeat polymorphism in the promoter region of the SLC11A1 gene seems to be of particular interest, since it has been shown to affect the levels of gene expression [17]. In vitro studies of this polymorphism suggested direct contribution of particular alleles either to autoimmune (allele 3) or to infectious (allele 2) disease susceptibility [10], [17]. Nevertheless, variants of the SLC11A1 located within the coding region, the introns, and the 3'-UTR have been shown to influence susceptibility to autoimmune disorders and T1DM [13], [18].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 52 59 52 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 105 108 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 133 141 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Our study aimed at examining the association of the SLC11A1 polymorphisms in relation to the presence of MAP infection, with T1DM in patients from Sardinia.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Methods
###end title 15
###begin title 16
###xml 0 8 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Patients</span>
Patients and controls
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 147 154 147 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0007109-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 189 196 189 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>

###xml 50 58 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 231 234 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 445 453 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
A total of 131 participants comprising of 59 T1DM patients (28 females and 31 males with age ranging between 18-94 years) and 76 healthy controls (Table 1) were tested for the detection of SLC11A1 polymorphisms and the presence of MAP specific IS900 signature using total DNA extracted from peripheral blood mononuclear cells collected at the Institute of Diabetology, medical clinic of Sassari University, Italy. Informed written consents from patients including other necessary clearances were obtained before blood samples were drawn. Institutional review board of the University of Sassari approved the study.

DNA extraction
###end title 23
###begin p 24

To extract MAP DNA, samples were processed by using the lysor instrument - 500 microl of glass beads (Sigma) were added, followed by standard phenol-chloroform extraction and DNA precipitation with absolute ethanol and 10M ammonium acetate; the pellet was washed with 70% ethanol and resuspended in 70 microl of TE buffer.

MAP DNA detection

###xml 0 3 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
MAP DNA detection was performed by PCR as previously reported [14], [19]. Sequence analysis of the amplicons confirmed IS900 identity. For SCL11A1 polymorphisms, 100 ng of template (genomic) DNA, obtained from blood cells, was amplified. The primer sequences and PCR cycling parameters that we used were previously reported [5].

SLC11A1 genotyping

###begin p 29

Candidates were genotyped across these polymorphisms by PCR restriction fragment length polymorphism (PCR-RFLP), in which primers and restriction enzymes were used as previously described [14]. Restriction enzyme digestion products were resolved by electrophoresis on 3% agarose gels stained with ethidium bromide.

###begin title 31
Results
###end title 31
###begin title 32
###xml 0 3 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
MAP detection
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 204 211 204 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0007109-t001">Table 1</xref>

###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 74 78 74 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-CanonneHergaux1">[20]</xref>
###xml 212 216 205 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Blackwell2">[17]</xref>
###xml 218 222 211 215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-AlterKoltunoff1">[21]</xref>
###xml 402 406 395 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-CanonneHergaux1">[20]</xref>
###xml 408 412 401 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Blackwell3">[22]</xref>
###xml 448 455 441 448 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 518 522 511 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Hofmeister1">[23]</xref>
###xml 579 583 572 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Yang1">[24]</xref>
###xml 585 589 578 582 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Yen1">[25]</xref>
###xml 609 613 602 606 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Comabella1">[26]</xref>
###xml 631 635 624 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Takahashi1">[18]</xref>
###xml 683 687 676 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Bellamy2">[27]</xref>
###xml 689 693 682 686 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Bellamy3">[28]</xref>
###xml 695 699 688 692 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Jin1">[29]</xref>
###xml 712 716 705 709 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Abel1">[30]</xref>
###xml 718 722 711 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Ferreira1">[31]</xref>
###xml 747 754 740 747 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 903 907 896 900 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Nishino1">[13]</xref>
SLC11A1 gene has a vital function in the pathway of macrophage activation [20]. It is involved in the expression of chemokines, interleukin-1beta, inducible nitric oxide synthase, MCH II molecules, and TNF-alpha [17], [21]. The localization of SLC11A1 is within the late endosomal/lysosomal compartments in phagocytes and it has been hypothesised to perform a transporter function as a divalent cation [20], [22]. Recent reports show a role of the SLC11A1 gene in different autoimmune diseases such as Crohn's disease [23], rheumatoid arthritis and juvenile rheumatoid arthritis [24], [25] multiple sclerosis [26], diabetes type 1 [18] and infectious diseases including tuberculosis [27], [28], [29] and leprosy [30], [31]. There is evidence that SLC11A1 gene is involved in modifying susceptibility to T1DM as shown by RNA silencing that inversely correlated with increased susceptibility to infection [13].
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 89 93 89 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-OKeeffe1">[32]</xref>
###xml 95 99 95 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Piganelli1">[33]</xref>
###xml 318 321 318 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Daneman1">[8]</xref>
###xml 323 327 323 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Litherland1">[34]</xref>
###xml 497 501 497 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Blackwell2">[17]</xref>
###xml 503 507 503 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Blackwell3">[22]</xref>
###xml 771 775 771 775 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-CanonneHergaux1">[20]</xref>
###xml 781 787 781 787 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 891 895 891 895 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Dai1">[16]</xref>
###xml 922 928 922 928 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 928 932 928 932 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Dai1">[16]</xref>
Antigen presenting cells have long been considered associated to diabetes susceptibility [32], [33] due to their exclusive expression of MHC class II molecules, the major genetic factors underlying T1DM. Different workers have identified diabetes-associated genes that are also involved in antigen-processing pathways [8], [34]. SLC11A1 functions as a membrane transporter of divalent cations but its mechanism of mediating natural resistance to bacterial and parasitic infections remains unclear [17], [22]. Previous studies on the innate response of macrophages during intracellular bacterial infection suggested that SLC11A1-mediated deprivation of divalent cations might change the phagosomal microenvironment that impairs the pathogenesis of intracellular pathogens [20]. Dai et al. found that SLC11A1 contributes to enhanced phagosomal acidification in macrophages and dendritic cells [16]. Based on these data, Dai et al.[16] suggest that SLC11A1-mediated changes in antigen processing within the vesicular system may have general effects in autoimmune as well as in infectious diseases.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 59 63 59 63 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Chacon1">[35]</xref>
###xml 112 116 112 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Chacon1">[35]</xref>
###xml 118 122 118 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Sechi4">[36]</xref>
###xml 205 208 205 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Sechi1">[5]</xref>
###xml 355 359 355 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Rosu2">[19]</xref>
###xml 450 453 450 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Rosu1">[4]</xref>
###xml 0 3 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 168 171 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 196 204 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 252 255 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 297 305 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 333 341 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 379 382 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 428 436 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
MAP is the causative agent of Johne's disease in ruminants [35] and it has been associated with Crohn's disease [35], [36]. We have previously reported the presence of MAP DNA in the PBMC of T1DM patients [5]. These finding were confirmed by detecting MAP specific antibodies in the blood of T1DM patients but not in type 2 diabetic patients and controls [19]. More recently two MAP strains were isolated from the blood of T1DM patients by our group [4].
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 95 102 95 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SLC11A1</italic>
###xml 453 457 453 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007109-Jin1">[29]</xref>
###xml 551 557 551 557 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SCLA11</italic>
###xml 730 737 730 737 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SCL11A1</italic>
###xml 903 910 903 910 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SCL11A1</italic>
###xml 128 131 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 154 162 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 227 230 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 240 246 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">people</span>
###xml 444 452 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 950 953 <span type="species:ncbi:1770">MAP</span>
###xml 967 973 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
In this study we report the novel association of T1DM with the 274 C/T polymorphism within the SLC11A1 gene and the presence of MAP DNA in Sardinian T1DM patients. This polymorphism may be the link of a permissive infection by MAP in these people. Recent studies have reported that the 4-bp TGTG deletion located 55 nucleotides downstream of the last codon in exon 15 (1729 +del55del4) is associated with tuberculosis susceptibility in Chinese children [29]. We did not find any statistical difference at this locus between T1DM and healthy controls. SCLA11 polymorphisms were analyzed also by stratification by sex and no differences were observed (data not shown). It may be possible that polymorphisms in different loci of the SCL11A1 gene may confer susceptibility to different intracellular pathogens although genetically very close. Further studies are necessary in order to elucidate the role of SCL11A1 polymorphisms and how it may influence MAP infection in humans.
