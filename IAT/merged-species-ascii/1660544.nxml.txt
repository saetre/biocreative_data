###begin article-title 0
Rtt107/Esc4 binds silent chromatin and DNA repair proteins using different BRCT motifs
###end article-title 0

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 92 96 92 96 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>
###xml 105 129 105 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 212 213 212 213 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">e</underline>
###xml 222 223 222 223 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</underline>
###xml 229 230 229 230 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</underline>
###xml 105 129 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
By screening a plasmid library for proteins that could cause silencing when targeted to the HMR locus in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, we previously reported the identification of Rtt107/Esc4 based on its ability to establish silent chromatin. In this study we aimed to determine the mechanism of Rtt107/Esc4 targeted silencing and also learn more about its biological functions.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 55 59 55 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR </italic>
###xml 306 311 306 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BRCA1</italic>
###xml 518 530 518 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RTT107/ESC4 </italic>
###xml 826 830 826 830 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SLX </italic>
###xml 837 848 837 848 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RTT107ESC4 </italic>
###xml 876 880 876 880 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SGS1</italic>
###xml 882 886 882 886 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">esc4</italic>
###xml 892 896 888 892 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sgs1</italic>
###xml 129 134 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 271 276 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Targeted silencing by Rtt107/Esc4 was dependent on the SIR genes, which encode obligatory structural and enzymatic components of yeast silent chromatin. Based on its sequence, Rtt107/Esc4 was predicted to contain six BRCT motifs. This motif, originally identified in the human breast tumor suppressor gene BRCA1, is a protein interaction domain. The targeted silencing activity of Rtt107/Esc4 resided within the C-terminal two BRCT motifs, and this region of the protein bound to Sir3 in two-hybrid tests. Deletion of RTT107/ESC4 caused sensitivity to the DNA damaging agent MMS as well as to hydroxyurea. A two-hybrid screen showed that the N-terminal BRCT motifs of Rtt107/Esc4 bound to Slx4, a protein previously shown to be involved in DNA repair and required for viability in a strain lacking the DNA helicase Sgs1. Like SLX genes, RTT107ESC4 interacted genetically with SGS1; esc4Delta sgs1Delta mutants were viable, but exhibited a slow-growth phenotype and also a synergistic DNA repair defect.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Rtt107/Esc4 binds to the silencing protein Sir3 and the DNA repair protein Slx4 via different BRCT motifs, thus providing a bridge linking silent chromatin to DNA repair enzymes.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 47 72 47 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 110 114 110 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>
###xml 118 121 118 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HML</italic>
###xml 292 293 292 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 297 301 297 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>
###xml 305 309 305 309 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HML </italic>
###xml 324 326 324 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E </italic>
###xml 330 332 330 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I </italic>
###xml 415 421 415 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E </italic>
###xml 559 563 559 563 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;3 </sub>
###xml 41 46 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 47 71 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
Transcriptional silencing in the budding yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae occurs at the silent mating-type loci HMR and HML, telomeres, and at the rDNA locus. At all of these silenced regions, DNA binding proteins recognize specific motifs and recruit a silencing protein complex (reviewed in [1]). HMR and HML are flanked by E and I "silencers." Each silencer has binding sites for ORC, and Rap1 or Abf1. The potent HMR-E silencer has a binding site for all three proteins. At telomeres, Rap1 also contributes a critical DNA-binding function, binding to the TG1-3 repeats.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 165 166 165 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 173 178 173 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E</italic>
###xml 266 267 266 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 314 315 314 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 316 317 316 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 384 385 384 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 386 387 386 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 588 589 588 589 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O</italic>
###xml 609 610 609 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 611 612 611 612 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 807 810 807 810 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HM </italic>
###xml 1069 1072 1069 1072 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HM </italic>
###xml 1078 1080 1078 1080 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
At both the silent mating loci and at telomeres, the DNA-binding proteins recruit a Sir protein complex that can spread to silence genes at a distance (reviewed in [2]). At HMR-E, for example, this is achieved by ORC recruitment of Sir1 via a Sir1-Orc1 interaction [3], and Rap1 and Abf1 binding to Sir4 and Sir3 [4,5]. Sir4 and Sir3 multimerize, both with themselves and each other [4,6]. Sir4 also binds Sir2, and Sir2 plays a crucial role in the spreading of a Sir2, Sir3, Sir4 complex on chromatin by deacetylating histone H4 lysine 16. The deacetylation produces a novel product, 2' O-acetyl-ADP-ribose [7-9]. This compound has recently been shown to produce a conformational change in Sir3 that is likely to promote spreading of the Sir complex. Sir2, Sir3 and Sir4 are essential for silencing at the HM loci and at telomeres, while Sir1 plays a prominent role in silencing at the mating type loci but not at telomeres. The Yku70/Yku80 heterodimer that binds to DNA ends plays an important role in silencing at telomeres, while being dispensable for silencing at HM loci [10].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 16 22 16 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E </italic>
###xml 74 78 74 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>
###xml 85 87 85 87 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 88 90 88 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 184 190 184 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E </italic>
###xml 259 261 259 261 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BD</sub>
###xml 392 398 392 398 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E </italic>
###xml 400 402 400 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 421 422 421 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 559 560 559 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 561 563 561 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 564 566 564 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 648 652 648 652 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>
###xml 653 655 653 655 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 811 812 811 812 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">e</underline>
###xml 821 822 821 822 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</underline>
###xml 828 829 828 829 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</underline>
###xml 912 924 912 924 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RTT107/ESC4 </italic>
###xml 1028 1030 1028 1030 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1031 1033 1031 1033 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 1077 1082 1077 1082 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESC4 </italic>
###xml 1228 1230 1228 1230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 1375 1377 1375 1377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
Deletion of the HMR-E silencer leads to loss of silencing of genes at the HMR locus [11,12]. However, if heterologous DNA binding sites (such as Gal4 sites) are integrated in place of HMR-E and the strain is transformed with certain Gal4 DNA binding domain (GBD) - silencing protein hybrids, silencing can be restored due to targeting of Sir proteins or proteins that bind to Sir proteins to HMR-E ([13]; see also Figure 1). This so-called "targeted silencing" has been a useful tool for investigating the process of Sir protein recruitment to silenced loci [3,13-17]. We previously described a screen for proteins capable of targeted silencing at HMR [16]. In this screen many known proteins were identified, as well as several proteins not characterized at the time, which we called Esc proteins because they establish silent chromatin. One of these was Esc4 whose characterization we describe in this report. RTT107/ESC4 was also identified in screens for mutants with increased Ty transposon mobility or DNA repair defects [18-20]. For simplicity we will only use the name ESC4 in the remainder of this paper. Esc4 has been shown to be phosphorylated by Mec1 kinase on SQ/TQ motifs in response to DNA damage during S phase [21]. The phosphorylation by Mec1 has quite recently been proposed to be regulated by Slx4, which was also shown to form a complex containing Esc4 [22].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 186 188 186 188 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BR</underline>
###xml 192 193 192 193 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 345 347 345 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 486 488 486 488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 489 491 489 491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 624 626 624 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 627 629 627 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 744 746 744 746 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 747 749 747 749 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 819 821 819 821 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 822 824 822 824 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 1042 1044 1042 1044 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 294 299 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 383 388 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In this report we show that Esc4 has 6 BRCT motifs and that they are important for its function. Many proteins that function in repair, and even a silencing protein, Rap1, contain BRCT (BRCA1 C-terminus) motifs. This motif was first identified by database searching using the C-terminus of the human breast cancer susceptibility protein, BRCA1 [23]. Since then, BRCT motifs from the human DNA repair protein XRCC1 and the two tandem BRCT motifs of BRCA1 protein have been crystallized [24,25] and crystal and/or solution structures have also been solved of BRCTs from 53BP1, DNA ligase III, and an NAD-dependent DNA ligase [26-29]. More recently, it was discovered that BRCT motifs could specifically bind to phosphoserine-containing proteins [30,31] and structures of such complexes were subsequently also determined [32-34]. Overall, BRCT motifs are thought to mediate a diverse array of protein-protein interactions, binding to proteins with different structures, as well as to other BRCTs, both inter- and intra-molecularly (reviewed in [35]).
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 118 148 118 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae SGS1 </italic>
###xml 227 248 227 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli recQ</italic>
###xml 291 294 291 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BLM</italic>

###xml 452 457 452 457 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SGS1 </italic>
###xml 619 621 619 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>

###xml 625 627 625 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 634 638 634 638 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SLX </italic>
###xml 701 705 701 705 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sgs1</italic>
###xml 719 721 715 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 744 749 740 745 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SLX2 </italic>
###xml 753 757 749 753 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SLX3</italic>
###xml 828 833 824 829 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SLX1 </italic>
###xml 892 894 888 890 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>

###xml 1029 1031 1025 1027 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 1197 1201 1193 1197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sgs1</italic>
###xml 1217 1219 1209 1211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 1256 1260 1248 1252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SLX </italic>
###xml 1295 1299 1287 1291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sgs1</italic>
###xml 1346 1348 1334 1336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 1418 1420 1406 1408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 1421 1423 1409 1411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 1424 1426 1412 1414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 1533 1537 1521 1525 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sgs1</italic>
###xml 1567 1571 1551 1555 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESC4</italic>
###xml 1661 1666 1645 1650 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SGS1 </italic>
###xml 1670 1675 1654 1659 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESC4 </italic>
###xml 1676 1678 1660 1662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 1679 1681 1663 1665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 118 142 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 227 243 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 285 290 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 312 337 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
###xml 446 451 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
DNA repair requires many DNA-modifying enzymes such as nucleases, ligases, topoisomerases, polymerases and helicases. Saccharomyces cerevisiae SGS1 is a member of the RecQ family of genes encoding DNA helicases. In addition to Escherichia coli recQ, this helicase gene family includes human BLM, WRN, RECQL, and Schizosaccharomyces pombe rqh1+. Members of the RecQ family of helicases have been implicated in genomic stability, aging and cancer. Yeast SGS1 has been shown by several labs to suppress DNA recombination, gross chromosomal rearrangements, and Ty1 transposition, and to exhibit 3' to 5' helicase activity [20,36-38]. Six SLX genes have been isolated in a synthetic lethal screen using an sgs1Delta mutant [39]. Two of these genes, SLX2 and SLX3, encode the Mus81/Mms4 nuclease that acts on branched DNA structures. SLX1 is also predicted to be a nuclease, based on its sequence [40]. Slx1 and Slx4 coimmunoprecipitate and slx1 and slx4 mutants display similar phenotypes and have been proposed to function together [39]. In addition, a genome-wide genetic interaction (synthetic genetic array, SGA) screen isolated a total of 24 genes that show a synthetic growth interaction with an sgs1Delta mutation [41]. This screen identified 4 of the 6 SLX genes previously identified in the sgs1Delta synthetic lethal screen described above [39], as well as three other genes known to show synthetic interactions [39,42-45]. The screen also identified 16 other genes that caused synthetic lethality or sickness when mutated with sgs1Delta. One of these genes was ESC4. Subsequently, there have been other reports confirming this genetic interaction between SGS1 and ESC4 [46,47].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 100 103 100 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR</italic>
###xml 136 139 136 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR</italic>
Here we demonstrate that the C-terminal two BRCT motifs of Esc4 bind to Sir3 and are sufficient for SIR-dependent targeted silencing at HMR. Furthermore, the N-terminal four BRCT motifs in Esc4 bind to Slx4, thus linking this DNA repair protein to silent chromatin.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Results
###end title 15
###begin title 16
###xml 53 56 53 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR</italic>
Esc4 establishes targeted silencing when targeted to HMR
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 115 119 115 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>
###xml 153 159 153 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E </italic>
###xml 169 171 169 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 199 203 199 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>
###xml 262 264 262 264 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BD</sub>
###xml 309 310 309 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</italic>
###xml 330 336 330 336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E </italic>
###xml 353 354 353 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 388 393 388 393 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">URA3 </italic>
###xml 425 429 425 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>
In a screen described previously, we identified Esc4 as a protein that could restore silencing when targeted to an HMR locus harboring a deletion of the HMR-E silencer [16]. Targeting of proteins to HMR was mediated by the binding of a Gal4 DNA binding domain (GBD)-hybrid protein to a Gal4 DNA binding site (G) that replaced the HMR-E silencer (Figure 1). Silencing was assessed using a URA3 reporter gene integrated at the HMR locus.

###begin title 18
###xml 30 33 30 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR</italic>
Targeted silencing by Esc4 is SIR-dependent
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 72 76 72 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>

###xml 657 659 657 659 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BD</sub>
###xml 736 748 736 748 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E Aeb::G</italic>
###xml 772 784 772 784 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E aeB::G</italic>
###xml 793 799 793 799 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E </italic>
###xml 939 940 939 940 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
In order to understand how Esc4 promoted silencing when targeted to the HMR locus, we tested silencing by GBD-Esc4 in various strains. First, targeted silencing by GBD-Esc4 was compared to GBD alone or to the potent targeted silencing factor GBD-Esc1 in the strain in which the screen was performed, which has the entire HMR-E silencer deleted and replaced with a Gal4 DNA binding site (HMR-E aeb::G) [17]. The fraction of cells silenced by GBD-Esc4 was not as great as by GBD-Esc1 but, nevertheless, significant targeted silencing at HMR was observed despite the complete absence of the HMR-E silencer (Figure 2, HMR-E aeb::G). When targeted silencing by GBD-Esc4 was assessed in strains harboring deletions of just the E and B sites (HMR-E Aeb::G) or the A and E sites (HMR-E aeB::G) of the HMR-E silencer, silencing was increased roughly 50 to 100-fold, as expected for strains with at least one element of the natural silencer (Figure 2).

###begin p 20
###xml 39 41 39 41 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BD</sub>
###xml 51 54 51 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR</italic>
###xml 107 111 107 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR2</italic>
###xml 113 118 113 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR3 </italic>
###xml 121 126 121 126 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR4 </italic>
###xml 151 152 151 152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 195 199 195 199 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR </italic>
###xml 288 291 288 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR</italic>
###xml 337 339 337 339 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 340 342 340 342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
To test whether targeted silencing by GBD-Esc4 was SIR-dependent, it was tested in strains deleted for the SIR2, SIR3 or SIR4 genes. As seen in Figure 2, silencing by Esc4 required each of these SIR genes. This was expected since targeted silencing by other proteins has been shown to be SIR-dependent in every case examined previously [14,16]. It thus seemed likely that Esc4 caused silencing by recruiting the Sir protein complex.

Saccharomyces Esc4 proteins contain six BRCT motifs and are homologous to S. pombe Brc1
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 125 127 125 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 189 196 189 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">YHR154w</italic>
###xml 249 251 249 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 252 254 252 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 378 380 378 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 381 383 381 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 407 409 407 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 410 412 410 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 413 415 413 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 452 457 452 457 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pfam </italic>
###xml 458 460 458 460 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 461 463 461 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 711 713 711 713 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 723 724 723 724 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 775 783 775 783 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe</italic>
###xml 797 802 797 802 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pfam </italic>
###xml 918 921 918 921 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-5 </sup>
###xml 933 936 933 936 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-2 </sup>
###xml 1015 1018 1015 1018 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-6 </sup>
###xml 1029 1033 1029 1033 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-14 </sup>
###xml 1137 1138 1137 1138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 77 82 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 179 184 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 775 783 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
Esc4 contains several copies of the BRCT motif, originally identified in the human breast cancer susceptibility gene, BRCA1 [23]. The presence of these motifs in Esc4 (encoded by yeast ORF YHR154w) has been observed previously by database searches [48,49], although there has been disagreement as to how many BRCTs exist in this protein, with some reports suggesting six BRCTs [49,50] and others only four [21,22,48]. Our own analyses including use of Pfam [51,52] confirms the first five putative BRCTs in Esc4, and it seems likely that residues 935-1049, which are BRCT-like with a conserved W or Y residue near the C-terminus, also form a domain that folds into a BRCT type structure as previously reported [49] (Figure 3). Furthermore, analysis of a homolog of Esc4 from S. pombe, Brc1, using Pfam shows six BRCTs in this homologous protein, with the last two again being the most alternative (E-values of 4.1 x 10-5 and 5.8 x 10-2 for the fifth and sixth, respectively, compared to values ranging from 2.8 x 10-6 to 1.7 x 10-14 for the first four BRCTs), further supporting a conserved total of six motifs in these homologs (Figure 3).
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 145 146 145 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 337 351 337 351 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 362 377 362 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ashbya gossipii</italic>
###xml 379 399 379 399 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kluyveromyces lactis</italic>
###xml 405 421 405 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Candida glabrata</italic>
###xml 442 443 442 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 563 589 563 589 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe </italic>
###xml 597 598 597 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 614 623 614 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe </italic>
###xml 900 902 900 902 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 903 905 903 905 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 1078 1079 1078 1079 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 1134 1144 1134 1144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bona fide </italic>
###xml 291 297 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeasts</span>
###xml 337 350 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 362 377 <span type="species:ncbi:33169">Ashbya gossipii</span>
###xml 379 399 <span type="species:ncbi:28985">Kluyveromyces lactis</span>
###xml 405 421 <span type="species:ncbi:5478">Candida glabrata</span>
###xml 485 490 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 549 562 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 563 588 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
###xml 614 622 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
###xml 818 823 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 828 833 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The six BRCT motifs of Esc4 protein exist as a set of four tandem motifs at the N-terminus of the protein and two more at the C-terminus (Figure 3, yellow lines). These two sets of BRCT motifs are separated by a 375 amino acid linker region. Although there are proteins from various budding yeasts with compelling sequence similarity to S. cerevisiae Esc4 (e.g. Ashbya gossipii, Kluyveromyces lactis, and Candida glabrata, as shown in Figure 3), currently the only obvious non-budding yeast homolog candidate is Brc1 from the evolutionarily distant fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe (Figure 3, bottom line). S. pombe Brc1 is 878 amino acids in length and also contains four BRCTs at the N-terminus and two at the C-terminus, separated by a linker region. Esc4 and Brc1 are 52% similar and 21% identical overall. Human and mouse PTIP proteins have been reported to be quite similar in sequence [21,53], but do not contain the classically-conserved C-terminal aromatic residue present in all of the six BRCTs highlighted with a red asterisk in the alignment shown in Figure 3. Therefore, it is not yet clear if these proteins are bona fide Esc4/Brc1 homologs.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
The C-terminal two BRCT motifs of Esc4 are sufficient for targeted silencing
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 83 85 83 85 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 221 224 221 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR</italic>
###xml 277 280 277 280 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BD </sub>
###xml 330 332 330 332 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BD</sub>
###xml 416 418 416 418 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BD</sub>
###xml 483 485 483 485 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BD</sub>
###xml 613 625 613 625 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E Aeb::G</italic>
###xml 636 640 636 640 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sir2</italic>
###xml 680 681 676 677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 693 694 689 690 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 768 770 764 766 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BD</sub>
###xml 859 861 855 857 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BD</sub>
###xml 872 875 868 871 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR</italic>
###xml 935 936 931 932 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 1025 1026 1021 1022 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
The Esc4 hybrid protein isolated in our targeted silencing screen was full-length [16] and therefore contained all six predicted BRCT motifs. To determine which part of Esc4 and which BRCT motifs were responsible for the SIR-dependent targeted silencing, we constructed three GBD hybrids: one to the N-terminal four BRCT motifs (GBD-Esc4N, aa 1-480), one to the linker between the N- and C-terminal sets of motifs (GBD-Esc4L, aa 480-836), and one to the C-terminal two BRCT motifs (GBD-Esc4C, aa 818-1070). These constructs were tested for targeted silencing in a strain harboring deletions of the E and B sites (HMR-E Aeb::G) and in a sir2Delta derivative of that strain (Figure 4; see Table 1 for strain information). Significant targeted silencing was observed by GBD-Esc4C, although it was not as much as with full-length Esc4. The observed silencing by GBD-Esc4C was SIR-dependent, as observed for the full-length protein (Figure 2). No significant silencing was seen with Esc4N or with the linker region, Esc4L (Figure 4).

###begin title 26
The C-terminal BRCT motifs of Esc4 interact with Sir3
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 246 248 246 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 404 405 404 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 406 408 406 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 409 411 409 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 446 451 446 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 630 631 630 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 763 764 763 764 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 916 920 916 920 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sir3</italic>
###xml 1166 1167 1162 1163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 1177 1181 1173 1177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sir4</italic>
Because the C-terminal two BRCT motifs of Esc4 gave targeted silencing, we suspected that this region of the protein was binding to a silencing protein to recruit the Sir complex. Using a LexA-Esc4C hybrid and the two-hybrid reporter strain L40 [54], we tested for two-hybrid interactions with several Gal4 activation domain (GAD)-silencing protein constructs, including Sir1, Sir2, Sir3, Sir4 and Rap1 [3,17,55]. A strong interaction (i.e., the lacZ reporter gene generated blue color from X-gal visible in 15 minutes) with GAD-Sir3 (aa 252-978) was identified, as well as a weaker interaction with GAD-Sir4 (aa 839-1358) (Table 2). None was detected with Sir1, Sir2 or Rap1. Because the GAD-Sir4 (aa 839-1358) hybrid contained the region known to bind to Sir3 [7], we hypothesized that LexA-Esc4 was binding to GAD-Sir4 via a bridge of endogenous Sir3. To test this, we used a derivative of strain L40 harboring a sir3Delta mutation and examined the LexA-Esc4 interaction with GAD-Sir4. In this case, the interaction with GAD-Sir4 was no longer observed, whereas the interaction with GAD-Sir3 and an unrelated two-hybrid control interaction were unaffected (Table 2). When a sir4Delta derivative of L40 was used, no change in the LexA-Esc4 interactions with GAD-Sir3 or GAD-Sir4 was observed, further supporting the idea that Esc4 requires Sir3 to interact with Sir4, and not vice versa. Taken together, the targeted silencing data strongly suggest an interaction (probably a direct one) between the C-terminal BRCT motifs and the silencing protein Sir3.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
The N-terminal four BRCT motifs of Esc4 bind to Slx4
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 45 48 45 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR</italic>
###xml 320 321 320 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 333 335 333 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 526 528 526 528 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">7 </sup>
###xml 659 660 659 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T3">3</xref>
###xml 748 749 748 749 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T3">3</xref>
###xml 1099 1101 1099 1101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 1151 1158 1151 1158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
The above results indicated that Esc4 caused SIR-dependent targeted silencing primarily by binding to Sir3 through its C-terminal two BRCT motifs. In addition, Esc4 also contains four BRCT motifs at its N-terminus and these BRCT motifs are more similar to those found in various proteins from diverse eukaryotes (Figure 3, and ref. [49]). In order to identify proteins that bind to these N-terminal BRCT motifs, a two-hybrid screen was performed using a LexA-Esc4N (aa 1-480) fusion protein. Strikingly, from screening ~2 x 107 library plasmids expressing GAD protein hybrids, thirteen clones containing in-frame fusions of GAD to Slx4 were identified (Table 3). In-frame GAD fusions to the six different positions in Slx4 were isolated (see Table 3) and all contained at least the C-terminal half of the protein (residues 383-748). The Esc4N-Slx4C two-hybrid interaction was a very strong one and was specific. Also, Slx4 did not show an interaction with Esc4C. Thus, the N-terminal BRCT motifs of Esc4 are not only necessary for binding Slx4, as reported while this manuscript was in preparation [22], but are indeed sufficient for this interaction in vivo. Furthermore, our two-hybrid data show that the region of Slx4 sufficient for Esc4 binding resides in its C-terminus.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
###xml 35 39 35 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESC4</italic>
Genetic and phenotypic analysis of ESC4
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 58 63 58 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESC4 </italic>
###xml 133 137 133 137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">esc4</italic>
###xml 233 236 229 232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HM </italic>
###xml 269 273 265 269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">esc4</italic>
A heterozygous diploid strain with a complete deletion of ESC4 was constructed and dissected to generate a null mutant haploid. This esc4Delta mutant grew normally and also mated with normal efficiency (suggesting no gross defect in HM silencing). Furthermore, when an esc4Delta mutation was introduced into a strain with a telomere reporter gene, no telomeric silencing defect was seen (data not shown). Thus, although Esc4 binds to Sir3, Esc4 does not appear to be a protein required for Sir protein-mediated silencing.

###xml 390 392 390 392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>

The BRCT motif was originally identified in the human BRCA1 tumor suppressor protein [23]. BRCA1 functions in DNA repair and DNA damage-sensing in cell cycle checkpoints (reviewed in [56]). As shown in Figure 5, strains deleted for ESC4 grew significantly less well than wild type on medium containing either MMS or HU. This result confirms reports that have since been published [18,21,22,46], as does our observation that esc4Delta mutants are not sensitive to ultraviolet radiation [18,21] (data not shown).

###begin p 33
###xml 19 20 19 20 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 27 31 27 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">esc4</italic>
###xml 46 50 42 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slx4</italic>
###xml 111 115 103 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">esc4</italic>
###xml 121 125 109 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slx4</italic>
###xml 221 223 205 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5A</xref>
###xml 339 343 323 327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">esc4</italic>
###xml 349 353 329 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slx4</italic>
###xml 454 456 430 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 475 479 451 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">esc4</italic>

###xml 599 603 567 571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">esc4</italic>
As shown in Figure 5, like esc4Delta mutants, slx4Delta mutants were sensitive to 0.032% MMS. Furthermore, the esc4Delta slx4Delta double mutant did not exhibit a greater MMS sensitivity than either single mutant (Figure 5A and data not shown), suggesting that they cooperate in providing resistance to MMS. Another group reported that an esc4Delta slx4Delta double mutant was more sensitive than either single mutant but the difference was very slight [22]. In contrast, an esc4Delta, but not a slx4Delta mutant, was significantly HU-sensitive, and the double mutant was no more sensitive than the esc4Delta strain. Thus, Esc4 appears to act independently of Slx4 in providing resistance to HU.

###xml 141 146 141 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SLX4 </italic>

###xml 592 593 576 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 707 709 691 693 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 710 712 694 696 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 215 220 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Because Esc4 bound to Slx4 and because the mutant was sensitive to MMS, this suggested that Esc4 might function in the same pathway as Slx4. SLX4 was first identified in a screen for genes required for viability of yeast cells deleted for SGS1 [39]. Therefore, we tested if esc4 was also synthetically lethal with sgs1. To do this, an esc4Delta mutant was crossed with an sgs1Delta mutant, the diploid was sporulated and dissected and meiotic progeny were analyzed. Haploid esc4Delta sgs1Delta cells were viable, but were noticeably slower-growing that either single mutant (e.g., see Figure 5, YPD plate). While this work was in progress, this genetic interaction was also observed in genome-wide studies [41,46].
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 3 7 3 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sgs1</italic>
###xml 66 67 62 63 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 124 128 120 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asf1</italic>
###xml 234 236 226 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 237 239 229 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 260 264 252 256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">esc4</italic>
###xml 270 274 258 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sgs1</italic>
###xml 392 394 376 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5A</xref>
###xml 399 401 383 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5B</xref>
###xml 666 670 650 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">esc4</italic>
###xml 676 680 656 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sgs1</italic>
An sgs1Delta mutant showed sensitivity to both MMS and HU (Figure 5), as expected based on previously published results. An asf1Delta mutant was used as a control and displayed sensitivity to both DNA damaging chemicals, as expected [57,58]. Interestingly, an esc4Delta sgs1Delta mutant displayed MMS and HU sensitivity that was much more pronounced than that of either single mutant (Figure 5A and 5B). The enhanced sensitivity of this double mutant (10,000-fold on 0.014% MMS plates) seemed to be due to a synergistic repair defect and not entirely due to the growth defect (10-fold difference in colony number on YPD control plates) that was also observed in the esc4Delta sgs1Delta strain.
###end p 35
###begin title 36
Discussion
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 70 76 70 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR-E </italic>
###xml 145 146 145 146 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">e</underline>
###xml 155 156 155 156 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</underline>
###xml 162 163 162 163 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</underline>
###xml 173 175 173 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 475 479 475 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>
###xml 495 498 495 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR</italic>
###xml 707 710 707 710 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR</italic>
###xml 914 917 914 917 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR</italic>
###xml 1276 1279 1276 1279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIR</italic>
By screening a library of factors that could function in place of the HMR-E silencer when targeted to DNA, we identified Esc4 for its ability to establish silent chromatin [16]. Protein sequence analysis showed that Esc4 protein contains six BRCT motifs; four are found in tandem at the amino-terminus and two more are at the carboxy-terminus. The entire Esc4 protein was present in the hybrid identified in the targeted silencing screen. Since targeted silencing by Esc4 at HMR was found to be SIR-dependent, it seemed likely that some region within Esc4 was attracting a silencing protein complex to DNA. We tested subsets of the BRCT motifs, as well as the linker between them, for targeted silencing at HMR. These experiments demonstrated that the C-terminal two BRCTs caused targeted silencing that was nearly as strong as with full-length Esc4. Because silencing by this pair of BRCT motifs of Esc4 was also SIR-dependent, it seemed very likely that this region was recruiting a Sir protein when tethered to DNA. Therefore, we tested the C-terminal BRCT motifs for interactions with known silencing proteins by two-hybrid analysis. We identified a specific interaction with Sir3 (aa 252-978). We conclude that binding of Sir3 by Esc4 is likely to be responsible for the SIR-dependent targeted silencing activity.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 184 186 184 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 381 383 381 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 384 386 384 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 574 576 574 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
In some cases BRCT motifs have been shown to bind to phosphorylated serine residues. Specifically, they have been shown to bind to phosphopeptides with the following consensus: pSxxF [32]. Interestingly, Sir3 has an SxxF sequence (aa 583-586) within the Esc4-interacting region that we describe here (aa 252-978) and, furthermore, Sir3 protein has been shown to be phosphorylated [59,60], suggesting that an Esc4 BRCT motif or perhaps the combination of the two in the C-terminus may bind to phospho-Sir3. However, not all proteins bound by BRCT motifs have the SxxF motif [61], so the precise BRCT-interacting region of Sir3 could be elsewhere.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 365 367 365 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 730 732 730 732 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
###xml 988 990 988 990 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
In addition to binding Sir3 via C-terminal tandem BRCT motifs, Esc4 also binds to Slx4 via four tandem N-terminal BRCTs, as we have shown here by two-hybrid screening. This two-hybrid result demonstrates that these four BRCTs are sufficient for binding Slx4 and agrees with a recent report showing that the N-terminal BRCT motifs are required for this interaction [22]. It seems quite possible that Esc4 could bind Sir3 and Slx4 concurrently, given that these nuclear proteins' binding sites within Esc4 map to BRCT clusters separated by a long linker. Slx4 has been shown to heterodimerize with the endonuclease Slx1 to cleave DNA containing 5' -flap structures, such as in stalled replication forks, to facilitate their repair [62]. Thus, Esc4 binds the silencing protein Sir3 and also to Slx4, an important DNA repair complex component. Esc4 may play a role in facilitating repair of aberrant DNA structures, perhaps specifically within silent chromatin. Esc4 is a Mec1 kinase target [21] and this phosphorylation is required for its repair function. It is possible that phosphorylation of Esc4 by Mec1, which occurs just N-terminal to the Sir3-binding BRCTs, regulates association with Sir3 or other factors required for its ability to repair particular chromosomal loci in S phase.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 66 67 66 67 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 280 282 280 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 503 504 503 504 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 518 520 518 520 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 603 609 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeasts</span>
We analyzed Esc4 protein alignments (such as that shown in Figure 3) for evidence of conserved regions in the protein other than BRCT motifs. One region of interest was the SQ/TQ motifs between amino acids 743 and 807, which were shown to be important for function in DNA repair [21]. We did not find that these motifs were well-conserved, suggesting that the specific site of phosphorylation is not particularly critical in proteins with otherwise similar overall BRCT domain architecture (i.e., [BRCT]4-linker-[BRCT]2 arrangement). This may be because of some differences in Esc4 functions in diverse yeasts or may suggest that flexibility is tolerated in positioning of the phosphorylation sites, and therefore the exact relative location of kinase target sites has not been constrained during evolution.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
Future structural and genome sequencing studies are likely to unveil similarities and differences between multi-BRCT domain containing proteins. Whether these proteins play largely protein-scaffolding roles or also contain intrinsic enzymatic properties will be interesting to discover.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
Conclusion
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 78 82 78 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMR </italic>
We have shown that Esc4 caused targeted silencing when tethered at a weakened HMR locus. The targeted silencing activity was primarily due to the C-terminal two tandem BRCT motifs in Esc4, which bound to Sir3, probably through a direct interaction. This interaction led to the recruitment of the Sir complex and hence caused targeted silencing. The N-terminal BRCT domains were sufficient for binding to Slx4, which functions with Esc4 in DNA repair. Thus, the nuclear Esc4 protein uses its six BRCT motifs to connect diverse proteins involved in DNA repair and silent chromatin.
###end p 43
