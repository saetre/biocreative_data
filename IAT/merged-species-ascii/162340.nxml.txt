Human activation-induced cytidine deaminase causes transcription-dependent, strand-biased C to U deaminations
###end article-title 0

###begin p 3
###xml 423 439 423 439 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 610 616 610 616 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 711 719 711 719 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 384 389 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 423 439 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 586 591 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 610 616 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Activation-induced cytidine deaminase (AID) is required for the maturation of antibodies in higher vertebrates, where it promotes somatic hypermutation (SHM), class switch recombination and gene conversion. While it is known that SHM requires high levels of transcription of the target genes, it is unclear whether this is because AID targets transcribed genes. We show here that the human AID promotes C to T mutations in Escherichia coli which are stimulated by transcription. The mutations are strand-biased and occur preferentially in the non-transcribed strand of the target gene. Human AID purified from E.coli is active without prior treatment with a ribonuclease and deaminates cytosines in plasmid DNA in vitro. Further, the action of this enzyme is greatly stimulated by the transcription of the target gene in a strand-dependent fashion. These results confirm the prediction that AID may act directly on DNA and show that it can act on transcribing DNA in the absence of specialized DNA structures such as R-loops. It suggests that AID may be recruited to variable genes through transcription without the assistance of other proteins and that the strand bias in SHM may be caused by the preference of AID for the non-transcribed strand.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
INTRODUCTION
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 182 183 182 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c1">1</xref>
###xml 316 317 316 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c2">2</xref>
###xml 318 319 318 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c3">3</xref>
###xml 384 385 384 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c1">1</xref>
###xml 438 454 438 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 456 457 456 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c4">4</xref>
###xml 595 596 595 596 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c5">5</xref>
###xml 396 401 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 438 454 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Activation-induced cytidine deaminase (AID) was first described as a protein that is required for somatic hypermutations (SHM) and class switch recombination (CSR) in B lymphocytes (1). Subsequently, it was also found to be required for the third mutational process involved in antibody maturation, gene conversion (2,3). AID is a homolog of a known RNA-cytosine deaminase, Apobec-1 (1), and the human AID gene causes C to T mutations in Escherichia coli (4). AID protein purified from insect cells and pre-treated with a ribonuclease was recently shown to deaminate cytosines to uracil in DNA (5), providing an explanation for its mutator phenotype.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 183 184 183 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c6">6</xref>
###xml 297 298 297 298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c7">7</xref>
###xml 299 301 299 301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c10">10</xref>
###xml 371 373 371 373 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c11">11</xref>
###xml 548 554 548 554 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 589 595 589 595 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 525 530 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 548 554 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 589 595 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
It is not clear how AID causes base substitutions other than C to T that are observed during SHM, or how it creates the double-strand breaks that are thought to be necessary for CSR (6). Additionally, it is known that high transcription is a requirement for SHM in B cells as well in non-B cells (7-10) and that there is a strand bias in mutations introduced during SHM (11). It is unclear whether these properties of antibody maturation can be explained by biochemical properties of AID. We show here that the properties of human AID expressed in E.coli, and of the protein purified from E.coli are consistent with the dependence of antibody maturation on transcription and that its mutagenic action is strand-biased.
###end p 6

Plasmid pUP21 was described previously (13), but was referred to as pUP21-op75. Purification of human AID
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Human AID cDNA (I.M.A.G.E. Consortium ID 4054915) was obtained from the American Type Culture Collection (Rockville, MD) and the gene was amplified using the primers 5'-CTCTGGACGAATTCCATGGACAGCCTCTTC-3' and 5'-CCTGGAAGCTCGAGTCAAAGTCCCAAAGTA-3'. It was cloned into pGEX4T3 (Amersham Biosciences) as an EcoRI-XhoI fragment.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
The plasmid clone containing GST-AID fusion, pGhAID1, was introduced into BL21 DE3 and the transcription of the fusion gene was induced by adding IPTG to the growth media to 0.2 mM. Cells were grown at 30degreesC for 4 h and harvested. The cell pellet was resuspended in 20 ml of 1x PBS and sonicated for 10 pulses of 20 s duration separated by 1 min on ice. The fusion protein was purified through a glutathione- Sepharose column according to the recommendations of the manufacturer (Amersham Biosciences) and the protein from the fractions was separated on 12% SDS-polyacrylamide gel to identifiy those containing GST-AID. The appropriate fractions were pooled, the protein was concentrated using a YM30 centricon filter (Amicon) and equilibrated with the storage buffer (25 mM Tris-HCl pH 7.5, 0.1 mM EDTA, 1.0 mM DTT and 10% glycerol).
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Kanamycin-resistance reversion assays
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 61 62 61 62 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 104 106 104 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c13">13</xref>
The genetic assays for reversion to kanamycin-resistance (KanR) were performed as described previously (13), with the modification that two plasmids were maintained in the cells; one plasmid being pSU24 or pSU-AID and the other being pDeltaUP21, pUP21 or pUP27. Briefly, one culture was grown to early log phase and then diluted 1000-fold to create several independent cultures. IPTG was added to 0.5 mM in half of the cultures and all the cultures were grown to mid-log phase before plating. Plates containing carbenicillin were used to determine the total number of cells in each culture and plates with 50 microg/ml kanamycin were used to score the revertants.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 21 29 21 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 128 129 128 129 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 453 459 445 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 562 563 554 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f3">3</xref>
###xml 875 876 861 862 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 453 459 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
To treat plasmid DNA in vitro, pAB7 or pAB8 DNA was included at 2 nM in transcription buffer [40 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.9, 10 mM MgCl2, 10 mM NaCl, 2 mM spermidine, 100 mM potassium glutamate and 10 mM dithiothreitol (DTT)] containing 8 U of Topoisomerase II (Amersham Biosciences) and 87.5 nM T7 RNA polymerase in a 100 microl reaction. When nucleotide triphosphates were included in the reaction, they were at 3.75 mM each. GST-AID (1.2 microg) and 2 U of E.coli uracil-DNA glycosylase (UDG) (New England Biolabs) were included as indicated in the legend to Figure 3. The reactions were performed at 37degreesC for 4 h and terminated by the addition of EDTA to 10 mM and RNase A (Boehringer Mannheim). Following another 15 min incubation, the DNA was deproteinized and introduced into BH156 through electroporation, and colonies were scored for resistance to carbenicillin (CarbR) and kanamycin.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Treatment of a bubble DNA duplex with AID
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 12 14 12 14 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>
###xml 311 317 297 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 311 317 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
200 fmol of 32P end-labeled DNA was incubated with 2 microg GST-AID in a 10 microl reaction containing 50 or 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5 at 37degreesC for 45 min. Where indicated, EDTA or 1,10-phenanthroline were respectively added to 2 mM or 5 mM final concentration. The DNA was subsequently incubated with 1 U of E.coli UDG (New England Biolabs) for an additional 45 min and the reactions were terminated by the addition of NaOH to 0.1 M, followed by heating to 95degreesC for 7 min. The products of the reactions were separated on a 20% sequencing gel. The gel was scanned using a phosphorimager and the intensities of the bands were quantified.

###begin title 19
RESULTS
###end title 19
###begin title 20
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Human AID is a DNA cytosine deaminase
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 27 33 27 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 143 144 143 144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f1">1</xref>
###xml 497 498 497 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f1">1</xref>
###xml 650 651 650 651 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f1">1</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 27 33 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Human AID was expressed in E.coli as a fusion with GST and purified over an affinity column. A 56 bp DNA duplex containing a 5 nt bubble (Fig. 1A) was sequentially treated with AID, UDG and NaOH. If AID deaminates cytosines in this DNA to uracil, UDG would excise these uracils, and cleavage at the resulting abasic sites by NaOH would create shorter DNA fragments. Treatment of this substrate with AID resulted in the conversion of 17% of labeled DNA into a single, shorter oligonucleotide (Fig. 1B, lane 3). This oligomer is 27 nt in length and its length is consistent with the conversion of one of the two cytosines in the bubble to uracil (Fig. 1B). Remarkably, under the reaction conditions used, the other cytosine in the bubble and the 12 cytosines in the rest of the DNA strand appeared untouched by AID.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 156 157 156 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f1">1</xref>
###xml 384 385 384 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f1">1</xref>
However, the other cytosine in the bubble and some of the other cytosines in the duplex become susceptible to AID under different reaction conditions (Fig. 1C) or if the bottom DNA strand is used as a substrate without its sequence complement (not shown). We also found that AID action is inhibited by 1,10-phenanthroline, a strong chelator, but not by the weaker chelator EDTA (Fig. 1B, lanes 4 and 5). These results show that AID has a strong preference for unpaired cytosines and requires a tightly bound metal ion for its action.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
###xml 31 37 31 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 31 37 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Cytosine deamination by AID in E.coli is transcription-dependent
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 11 17 11 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 131 134 131 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</italic>
###xml 141 149 141 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kanS-94D</italic>
###xml 218 221 218 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ung</italic>
###xml 238 239 238 239 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 265 267 265 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c14">14</xref>
###xml 349 350 349 350 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 432 435 428 431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</italic>
###xml 487 490 483 486 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</sub>
###xml 521 524 517 520 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</italic>
###xml 568 571 564 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tac</italic>
###xml 604 607 600 603 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tac</italic>
###xml 613 616 609 612 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</sub>
###xml 618 620 614 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c15">15</xref>
###xml 628 629 624 625 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f2">2</xref>
###xml 699 702 695 698 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tac</italic>
###xml 747 750 743 746 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</sub>
###xml 752 754 748 750 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c15">15</xref>
###xml 755 757 751 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c16">16</xref>
###xml 11 17 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
We used an E.coli plasmid-based genetic reversion assay to study the transcription-dependence of AID mutagenesis. An allele of the kan gene, kanS-94D, reverts only through C to T changes in cells lacking UDG (genotype ung), conferring KanR phenotype upon its host (14). If AID deaminates cytosines in DNA to uracil, there would be an increase in KanR revertants and would be scored as such. In one of the plasmids used, pDeltaUP21, kan is transcribed from a weak constitutive promoter (Pkan), while in the other plasmids kan is either transcribed from an inducible UP-tac promoter or a combination of UP-tac and Pkan (15) (Fig. 2A). Previous studies have shown that transcription from an induced UP-tac promoter dominates over transcription from Pkan (15,16); thus different DNA strands of the gene are predominantly transcribed in pUP21 and pUP27 when IPTG is present in the growth media.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 42 44 42 44 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c17">17</xref>
###xml 122 128 122 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 201 202 197 198 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 269 271 265 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c18">18</xref>
###xml 333 334 329 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</italic>
###xml 365 366 361 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</italic>
###xml 453 454 437 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg464tb1">1</xref>
###xml 519 520 503 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</italic>
###xml 570 571 554 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg464tb1">1</xref>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 122 128 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The human AID gene was cloned into pSU24 (17) (pSU-AID) under the control of an inducible T7 promoter and introduced into E.coli. In cells containing pDeltaUP21, AID expression results in increased KanR revertants in the cultures. The Lea-Coulson method of the median (18) was used to determine the number of revertants per culture, m, and AID expression increased m by approximately12-fold compared to the uninduced cultures (310 compared to 25; Table 1). In control cultures lacking AID, there was little increase in m upon addition of IPTG to the growth media (Table 1).
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 138 141 138 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</italic>
###xml 164 165 164 165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</italic>
###xml 203 204 191 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg464tb1">1</xref>
###xml 338 341 326 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tac</italic>
###xml 378 380 366 368 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c16">16</xref>
###xml 381 383 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c19">19</xref>
###xml 506 507 494 495 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</italic>
###xml 539 540 515 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg464tb1">1</xref>
###xml 624 627 600 603 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</italic>
###xml 745 751 721 727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 779 782 755 758 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</italic>
###xml 745 751 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Similar experiments were also performed using cells containing pUP21. When transcription was induced from the strong promoter in front of kan in cells lacking AID, m increased approximately4-fold (Table 1). This increase in mutations is due to spontaneous cytosine deaminations in the non- transcribed strand during transcription from UP-tac, and has been described previously (16,19). When parallel experiments were performed using cells containing pUP21 and pSU-AID, induction of transcription increased m by approximately50-fold (Table 1). The number of reversions per culture obtained when transcription of both AID and kan genes was induced (4545) is in vast excess of the sum of revertants obtained as a result of overexpression of AID in E.coli (310) and transcription of kan (158). Thus, the extent of transcription of the target gene directly correlates with increased AID mutagenesis.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 192 193 192 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f2">2</xref>
###xml 249 250 249 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</italic>
###xml 306 309 306 309 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</italic>
###xml 336 337 332 333 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg464tb1">1</xref>
###xml 402 405 398 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tac</italic>
###xml 430 431 426 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</italic>
###xml 498 499 482 483 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg464tb1">1</xref>
###xml 596 599 580 583 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</italic>
Further, this increase in mutations was dependent on which of the two DNA strands was being copied by the RNA polymerase. When the experiments were repeated using pUP27 instead of pUP21 (Fig. 2A), the increase in mutations caused by AID was modest (m = 289) and was comparable to the increase observed for kan in pDeltaUP21 (310; Table 1). The target cytosine is in opposite strands with respect to UP-tac in the two plasmids and m is approximately16-fold lower with pUP27 compared to pUP21 (Table 1). These data suggest that AID acts preferentially on cytosines in the non-transcribed strand of kan.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 25 26 25 26 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 129 132 129 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</italic>
###xml 249 255 249 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 257 258 257 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c4">4</xref>
###xml 325 329 325 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoB</italic>
###xml 418 419 418 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c4">4</xref>
###xml 249 255 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Sixty-two independent KanR revertants from different experiments were sequenced and all contained a C to T change in codon 94 of kan (not shown). Our observations are consistent with the spectra of rifampicin-resistance mutations promoted by AID in E.coli (4). AID increased C to T mutations in the non-transcribed strand of rpoB from 30 to 63%, while G to A transitions in this strand increased from 1.4 to only 16% (4).
###end p 28
###begin title 29
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In vitro</italic>
In vitro cytosine deaminations by AID are also transcription-dependent
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 24 32 24 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kanS-94D</italic>
###xml 39 40 39 40 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f2">2</xref>
###xml 132 142 132 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ung E.coli</italic>
###xml 155 156 155 156 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 242 243 242 243 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 504 505 480 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f3">3</xref>
###xml 528 529 504 505 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 578 584 554 560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 711 712 687 688 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 136 142 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 578 584 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Plasmid pAB7 containing kanS-94D (Fig. 2B) was incubated with GST-AID under a variety of conditions and the DNA was introduced into ung E.coli to score KanR revertants. Incubation of the plasmid DNA with GST-AID increased the frequency of KanR revertants and the magnitude of this increase was dependent on the reaction conditions. While the increase was approximately5-fold in the absence of transcription, it was approximately1600-fold when the gene was actively transcribed by T7 RNA polymerase (Fig. 3A). The increase in KanR frequency was almost completely eliminated when E.coli UDG was included in the reaction suggesting that AID had converted the target cytosine to uracil. The residual increase in KanR frequency by AID in the presence of UDG is most likely due to some uracils escaping repair. If non-transcribing plasmid DNA is treated with GST-AID in the presence of UDG, no increase in revertants is oberved compared to the control reaction (not shown).
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 49 50 49 50 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 128 129 128 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f3">3</xref>
###xml 204 205 204 205 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 339 340 327 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f3">3</xref>
###xml 670 673 658 661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kan</italic>
Treatment of pAB8 with GST-AID also increased KanR frequency, but this effect was not strongly dependent on transcription (Fig. 3B). In contrast to pAB7, transcription caused only a slight increase in KanR revertants; the maximum revertant frequency obtained with pAB8 was approximately50-fold lower than what was observed with pAB7 (Fig. 3). Total RNA was quantified from representative reactions and all the reactions were found to contain comparable amounts of RNA (not shown). Consequently, the differences in the mutagenicity of AID for pAB7 and pAB8 are not due to poor transcription of the latter plasmid and must be related to the transcriptional orientation of kan within these plasmids.
###end p 31
###begin title 32
DISCUSSION
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 82 89 82 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 94 103 94 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro,</italic>
###xml 311 312 311 312 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c5">5</xref>
###xml 313 315 313 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c20">20</xref>
###xml 316 318 316 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c21">21</xref>
###xml 409 417 409 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 419 421 419 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c22">22</xref>
###xml 23 28 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We have shown that the human AID protein is an active DNA cytosine deaminase both in vivo and in vitro, and AID mutageneis is stimulated by transcription. Further, the protein targets cytosines in the non-transcribed strand. After this work was completed, similar results were reported from other laboratories (5,20,21). Additionally, purified Apobec-1 was shown to deaminate cytosines in single-stranded DNA in vitro (22). While the results presented here are generally consistent with the results of those studies, they clarify the biochemical activity and substrate specificity of AID in important ways.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 120 121 120 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c5">5</xref>
###xml 249 250 249 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c5">5</xref>
###xml 457 463 457 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 640 641 640 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f1">1</xref>
###xml 774 780 774 780 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 848 849 848 849 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c5">5</xref>
###xml 936 941 936 941 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 944 946 944 946 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c20">20</xref>
###xml 457 463 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 774 780 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
It was reported that GST-AID purified from an insect expression system was inactive unless treated with a ribonuclease (5). The sequence of the RNA bound to GST-AID is unknown, but it was speculated that it may have a regulatory role in AID action (5). In contrast to those results, we did not find it necessary to include a ribonuclease in AID reactions to detect its biochemical activity. To assess whether a significant fraction of GST-AID purified from E.coli was bound to RNA, the strand cleavage assay was repeated with the inclusion of ribonuclease A in the reaction. Ribonuclease treatment did not improve the activity of AID (Fig. 1C) and may have decreased it slightly. These data show that unlike the GST-AID purified from insect cells, the protein purified from E.coli does not contain RNA and is active. Further, if AID does bind RNA (5), it is likely to bind specific RNA sequences. It should also be noted that Chaudhari et al. (20) did not treat the partially purified AID from B-cells with ribonucleases to demonstrate its cytosine deaminase activity. Consequently, whether AID binds RNA and whether such binding has biological significance remains unclear.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 168 170 168 170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c23">23</xref>
###xml 171 173 171 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c25">25</xref>
###xml 195 200 195 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 203 205 203 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c20">20</xref>
###xml 332 340 332 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 421 422 421 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f3">3</xref>
It has been argued that the segment of DNA where CSR occurs (S region) may contain RNA-DNA hybrids (R-loops), and that such structures are essential for recombination (23-25). Further, Chaudhuri et al. (20) showed that partially purified AID targets the unpaired DNA strand when such R-loops are formed as a result of transcription in vitro. We have shown here that R-loop formation is not essential for AID action (Fig. 3). Normal transcription of DNA, which presumably creates transient transcription bubbles, may be all that is necessary for AID action.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 22 27 22 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 30 31 30 31 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c5">5</xref>
###xml 328 329 328 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f1">1</xref>
###xml 336 337 336 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f1">1</xref>
###xml 534 535 534 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg464f1">1</xref>
Finally, Bransteitter et al. (5) have argued that the propensity by AID to cause mutations within WRCY sequences (W = A or T; R = purine and Y = pyrimidine) is the result of preferential targeting of AID at these sites. The data presented here show that such preferences may depend on specific reaction conditions (compare Fig. 1B with 1C). Only one of the cytosines in the DNA bubble is within a WRCY sequence (product 27 nt), but both cytosines are converted to uracil at about the same frequency under one reaction condition (Fig. 1C). Hence, conclusions regarding the specificity of AID must await a more thorough biochemical analysis than has been presented so far.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 92 93 92 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c8">8</xref>
###xml 94 96 94 96 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c26">26</xref>
###xml 206 207 206 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c9">9</xref>
###xml 412 414 412 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c26">26</xref>
###xml 415 417 415 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c27">27</xref>
###xml 479 487 479 487 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 495 501 495 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 848 850 848 850 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c28">28</xref>
###xml 851 853 851 853 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c29">29</xref>
###xml 934 935 934 935 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg464c9">9</xref>
###xml 495 501 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
It has been argued that the dependence of SHM on the transcription of immunoglobulin genes (8,26) is the result of the action of a mutator protein that associates with the transcription elongation complex (9). The results presented here suggest that AID is likely to be this mutator. It is also unknown whether the targeting of the transcribing immunoglobulin genes by this mutator requires additional proteins (26,27). Our observation that AID targets a highly transcribed gene in vitro and in E.coli suggests that the targeting of AID may not require additional mammalian proteins. Once AID is brought to the region of the chromatin containing immunoglobulin genes, it may find the actively transcribed variable gene and help mutate it. The strand bias in SHM has variously been interpreted as evidence for transcription-dependent damage to DNA (28,29), or the involvement of transcription-coupled repair in eliminating DNA damage (9). While it is clear that downstream events may process uracils generated by AID and influence the mutation spectrum of SHM, it is tempting to suggest that the strand selectivity of AID with respect to transcription is the source of the mutational strand bias.
###end p 37
