###begin article-title 0
The calcium channel beta2 (CACNB2) subunit repertoire in teleosts
###end article-title 0

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 283 284 276 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 520 521 504 505 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 388 393 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 551 556 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 927 936 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Cardiomyocyte contraction is initiated by influx of extracellular calcium through voltage-gated calcium channels. These oligomeric channels utilize auxiliary beta subunits to chaperone the pore-forming alpha subunit to the plasma membrane, and to modulate channel electrophysiology [1]. Several beta subunit family members are detected by RT-PCR in the embryonic heart. Null mutations in mouse beta2, but not in the other three beta family members, are embryonic lethal at E10.5 due to defects in cardiac contractility [2]. However, a drawback of the mouse model is that embryonic heart rhythm is difficult to study in live embryos due to their intra-uterine development. Moreover, phenotypes may be obscured by secondary effects of hypoxia. As a first step towards developing a model for contributions of beta subunits to the onset of embryonic heart rhythm, we characterized the structure and expression of beta2 subunits in zebrafish and other teleosts.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 671 692 650 671 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tetraodon nigrovidis </italic>
###xml 696 710 675 689 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fugu rubripes </italic>
###xml 15 24 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 145 154 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">Zebrafish</span>
###xml 696 709 <span type="species:ncbi:31033">Fugu rubripes</span>
###xml 805 810 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Cloning of two zebrafish beta2 subunit genes (beta2.1 and beta2.2) indicated they are membrane-associated guanylate kinase (MAGUK)-family genes. Zebrafish beta2 genes show high conservation with mammals within the SH3 and guanylate kinase domains that comprise the "core" of MAGUK proteins, but beta2.2 is much more divergent in sequence than beta2.1. Alternative splicing occurs at the N-terminus and within the internal HOOK domain. In both beta2 genes, alternative short ATG-containing first exons are separated by some of the largest introns in the genome, suggesting that individual transcript variants could be subject to independent cis-regulatory control. In the Tetraodon nigrovidis and Fugu rubripes genomes, we identified single beta2 subunit gene loci. Comparative analysis of the teleost and human beta2 loci indicates that the short 5' exon sequences are highly conserved. A subset of 5' exons appear to be unique to teleost genomes, while others are shared with mammals. Alternative splicing is temporally and spatially regulated in embryo and adult. Moreover, a different subset of spliced beta2 transcript variants is detected in the embryonic heart compared to the adult.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
These studies refine our understanding of beta2 subunit diversity arising from alternative splicing, and provide the groundwork for functional analysis of beta2 subunit diversity in the embryonic heart.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 27 30 27 30 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
###xml 165 168 165 168 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
###xml 317 318 317 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 347 350 347 350 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
###xml 478 481 478 481 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
###xml 536 537 536 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 538 539 538 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 544 547 544 547 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
###xml 610 611 610 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 616 619 616 619 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
###xml 725 726 725 726 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 727 728 727 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 729 731 729 731 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 775 778 775 778 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
Voltage-dependent L-type Ca2+ channels (V-LTCC) are essential for the initiation and regulation of excitation-contraction coupling in cardiac muscle. In addition, Ca2+ entry through V-LTCC channels can also serve as a second messenger to modulate regulatory protein kinases, calmodulin and beta-adrenergic responses [3]. Genetic mutation of key Ca2+ homeostasis proteins is frequently associated with defects in both cardiac cell differentiation and contractility, suggesting Ca2+ signaling is essential for normal cardiac development [4-7]. Ca2+ signals can also contribute to the control of gene expression [8]. Ca2+ influx through V-LTCC can dramatically affect the phosphorylation, activity, or expression of many genes [5,8-10]. These data support the hypothesis that Ca2+ signaling through V-LTCC impacts not only cardiac contraction but also embryonic cardiac growth and morphology, physiology, and gene expression.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 187 189 168 170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 190 192 171 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 431 433 392 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 434 436 395 397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 482 483 439 440 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 571 573 528 530 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 574 576 531 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
V-LTCCs are oligomeric proteins composed of a pore-forming alpha1 subunit and up to four auxiliary subunits, termed alpha2, beta, gamma, and delta, associated in a 1:1:1:1 stoichiometry [11,12]. Mammals encode 10 distinct alpha1 subunit genes (four of which form V-LTCC) and four distinct beta subunit genes (beta1 - beta4). Particular alpha1 and beta subtypes are able to associate non-exclusively to form heterogeneous channels [13-17]. V-LTCC that contain the alpha1c subunit (Cav1.2) constitute the vast majority of L-type channels expressed in adult cardiac muscle [17-22].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 138 139 135 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 140 142 137 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 143 145 140 142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 173 176 167 170 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
###xml 392 394 378 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 395 397 381 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 612 614 595 597 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 790 792 773 775 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 923 925 903 905 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 926 928 906 908 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 1093 1094 1073 1074 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 1095 1097 1075 1077 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 1098 1100 1078 1080 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
The V-LTCC beta subunit genes have important functions in cardiac, skeletal and smooth muscle, the central nervous system and the retina [2,23-28]. beta subunits modulate Ca2+ channel function in two distinct ways. First, they facilitate transport of the alpha1 to the plasmalemma. In addition, they interact with alpha1 to modulate both the kinetics and voltage-sensitivity of the channels [29-31]. As members of the membrane-associated guanylate kinase (MAGUK) family, the V-LTCC beta subunit genes include conserved SH3 and guanylate kinase (GK) domains connected by a bridging region termed the HOOK domain [32]. The larger MAGUK protein family appears to be specific to metazoans, and its core structure, including the SH3 and GK domains, can be traced back to early metazoan history [33]. All known beta genes undergo alternative splicing of 5' exons, and some exhibit alternative splicing of internal exons as well [34-36]. Alternative splicing in internal exons can create truncated proteins, some of which have been shown to mediate calcium channel-independent functions in the cell [1,36,37].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 148 149 142 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 274 275 262 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 276 278 264 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 279 281 267 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 486 488 468 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 646 648 625 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 765 767 741 743 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 917 919 890 892 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 920 922 893 895 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 1118 1121 1085 1088 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/-</sup>
###xml 1209 1210 1172 1173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 1211 1213 1174 1176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 1277 1281 1234 1238 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 1298 1302 1252 1256 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 1615 1616 1569 1570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 1729 1731 1671 1673 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 1732 1734 1674 1676 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 1735 1737 1677 1679 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 1768 1772 1707 1711 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 1779 1787 1718 1726 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">relaxed </italic>
###xml 1928 1930 1867 1869 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 7 12 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 494 497 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1056 1061 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1202 1207 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1309 1313 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1410 1414 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1641 1645 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1753 1762 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
In the mouse, the earliest documented expression of a cardiac beta protein is a Western blot showing beta2 protein in the linear heart tube (E8.5) [2]. By E9.5 (chamber morphogenesis stages), beta4 and beta2 mRNA and protein are clearly present in the embryonic myocardium [2,10,38]. beta2 and beta4 proteins show detectable differences in spatial distribution as early as E9.5, which become more pronounced in mid- to late embryogenesis (E10.5 - E15.5) and in later fetal development [38]. In rat postnatal development (4.5 weeks), RT-PCR studies indicate all four beta subunits are expressed in cardiac tissue, though they differ in abundance [39]. Significantly, by 4.5 weeks RNA from atria and ventricles show different beta subunit RT-PCR expression profiles [39]. In brain and heart, newborn and juvenile animals express different subsets of beta genes (including different transcript variants) than the adult [39,40]. The physiological significance of these observations is not known. However, a recent report describing the targeted mutagenesis of mouse beta2 indicated that homozygous loss of this gene (beta2-/-) is embryonic lethal at E10.5, significantly earlier than the alpha1C-deficient mouse [2,19]. No up-regulation of other beta subunits was observed in beta2-/- mutants. In beta2-/- mutant mice, cardiac contractility is severely compromised and V-LTCC currents diminished. Hearts in mutant mice exhibit severe bradycardia and vascular patterning defects, but these phenotypes may be secondary to severe morphological defects that develop in the heart or to the overall reduced embryonic growth [2]. In contrast to beta2, mice with null mutations in beta1, beta3 and beta4 have no reported cardiac phenotypes [23,25-28]. However, the zebrafish beta1-/- mutant relaxed develops pericardial edema and reduced circulation at 4 dpf (days post-fertilization), suggesting a possible compromise in cardiac function [41]. Thus, while multiple beta subunits are clearly present in the embryonic heart, much remains to be learned about their functional roles in development.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 465 467 462 464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 78 87 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 353 362 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 504 513 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 568 577 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 770 779 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
As a model for the study of beta subunit developmental function in the heart, zebrafish has several advantages, including the external fertilization of embryos and rapid, transparent development of the larvae, which allows experimental access to the developing heart and circulatory system. Moreover, for about the first 6 days of development the small zebrafish larvae subsist on oxygen acquired via diffusion, enabling them to survive without a functional heart [42]. As a first step in establishing a zebrafish model, we cloned two beta2 subunit gene homologs from zebrafish. We find one beta2 subunit is much more similar than the other to known vertebrate beta2 subunits. Significant alternative splicing occurs involving several ATG-containing first exons in both zebrafish genes, as well as two other teleost species. The separation of these short exons by extremely large introns suggests potential independent cis-regulatory control of alternatively spliced transcripts. Unexpectedly, both beta2 subunits were maternally expressed, consistent with possible early roles for this gene in morphogenetic movements or patterning. Additionally, both genes showed evidence of tissue-, stage- and transcript-specific regulatory control in embryos and adults.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
###xml 18 27 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Identification of zebrafish and pufferfish calcium channel beta2 subunit (CACNB2) genes
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 139 141 133 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 467 468 452 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 470 471 455 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 492 493 477 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 705 707 684 686 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 708 710 687 689 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 826 827 802 803 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 832 834 808 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2D</xref>
###xml 1509 1510 1458 1459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 1624 1625 1570 1571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 55 60 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 100 109 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 406 415 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 587 596 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
TBLASTN searches of the GenBank database at NCBI using human beta2 subunit sequences suggested that zebrafish encode two beta2 homologues [43]. We therefore designed primers to highly conserved sequences within the SH3 and GK protein domains, and performed 5' and 3' RACE-PCR on RNA extracted from embryos aged 1-3 dpf. Using RACE and reverse-transcriptase PCR (RT-PCR), we isolated cDNAs representing two zebrafish beta2 genes, termed beta2.1 and beta2.2 (see Figs. 1, 2 and Additional File 1). The beta2.1 gene showed a near perfect match with genomic sequences located within Genbank zebrafish chromosome 22 (NC_007133.1), whereas beta2.2 matched genomic sequences found on chromosome 2 (NC_007113.1) [43-45]. Alternative splicing occurred at the N-terminus for both genes and within the HOOK domain for beta2.1 (see Figs. 1 and 2D for descriptions of transcript variants). In the initial RACE analysis for beta2.1, we recovered four beta2.1_tv1 clones, and five beta2.1_tv6 clones, suggesting these may be the most abundant transcript variants. One clone each was found for the beta2.1_tv2, 3, 4, 5, 7 and 8 transcripts, suggesting they may be less abundant. For beta2.2, we recovered two beta2.2_tv1 and three beta2.2_tv2 5' RACE clones. In additional RT-PCR analysis using primers closely flanking the HOOK domain, we confirmed that beta2.1 transcript variants containing more than one exon between exons 6 and 10 (i.e. beta2.1_tv3, 4, 7 and 8) could be detected in the RNA samples (see Additional file 2). Conversely, we confirmed that no alternative splicing occurred in the beta2.2 HOOK domain (see Additional File 2). Several 3' RACE clones were of a single variant for beta2.1 and a single variant for beta2.2, suggesting that no alternative splicing occurred in the GK or C-terminal regions of the genes.

###xml 1304 1305 1279 1280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S3">3</xref>
###xml 1308 1310 1283 1285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 1311 1313 1286 1288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>

To better assess the conservation of the new genes, we performed a comparative search for orthologous beta2 sequences in the pufferfish Tetraodon nigrovidis and Fugu rubripes genomes [45-47]. We identified a single beta2 subunit gene in each pufferfish genome (Fig. 2A). All four teleost beta2 genes contained highly conserved SH3 and GK domains characteristic of MAGUK family proteins (Fig. 2B). The size and number of exons comprising these domains are nearly identical in teleosts and mammals (Fig. 2A, yellow and green sections). Pairwise comparisons of amino acid sequences within these core (SH3 through GK) domains, using the most similar transcript variants available, indicated the zebrafish beta2.1, Fugu and Tetraodon beta2 genes shared ~87% identity with other vertebrate beta2 genes (Figs. 2C; see Fig S3 for alignment of core regions). We extended this analysis to five teleost species by including ESTs or proteins derived from genomic sequences. Three crystallographic studies on mammalian CACNB genes have identified a total of 21 amino acid residues as critical for interaction of the beta subunit with the alpha subunit AID (alpha interaction domain). In our five different teleost species, we observed that all 21 residues were nearly 100% conserved with mammals (see Additional File 3) [48-50].

The zebrafish beta2.1 and beta2.2 genes undergo alternative splicing within the N-terminus and within the internal HOOK domain (Figs. 1, 2A, and 2D). The beta2.1 and beta2.2 genes each encode two mutually exclusive N termini (Fig. 3). Zebrafish beta2.1 and beta2.2, as well as the Tetraodon and Fugu beta2 genes, share some 5' exons in common with mammals (Fig. 2A, lines 4 and 8; Fig. 3). In contrast, the beta2.1 exon 1 sequence could not be found in any mammalian, Xenopus or chick databases, but was present in the Tetraodon and Fugu genomes, suggesting this exon may be specific to teleosts (Fig. 2A, line 6). The beta2.2 exon 2 was found only in one other sequence, the stickleback EST DW608729, although the small size of this exon (6 amino acids) could make it difficult to recognize if it had diverged in other species (Fig. 2A, line 5). Additional 5' exons occur in mammals that were not observed in any teleost cDNAs or in genomic sequences available to date, although the limitation of this analysis is that complete genomic sequences are not available for all teleost species examined, and that our data relies in part on genome mining rather than extensive cDNA analysis. However, our data support the hypothesis that each of the teleost beta2 loci have evolved to contain a unique combination of 5' exons, each of which is predicted to encode an alternative protein N-terminus.

###begin p 29

###xml 841 846 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 924 929 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>

###xml 1147 1152 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1193 1199 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 1357 1377 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
###xml 1379 1389 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 1391 1396 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>

###xml 1589 1594 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1657 1662 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Remarkably, the introns that separate the first few 5' exons of the beta2 loci are among the largest introns known in the zebrafish, pufferfish or human genomes. Each Tetraodon and Fugu beta2 locus contains one intron over 10 kb, a size that ranks within the top 5% of the largest introns in pufferfish [51]. In addition, the two pufferfish genes, the stickleback beta2.2-like gene, and both zebrafish beta2.2 and beta2.1 each contain introns of > 5000 bp that separate 5'-most exons (Fig. 5). In pufferfish, the modal value for intron size is 79 bp, with 75% of introns < 425 bp in length [51]. Given the compact nature of the pufferfish genomes [52], it was not surprising that introns of the human beta2 locus exceeded the size of those in fish. Nevertheless, the trend of megasized introns in the N-terminus of beta2 loci extends to the human genome. Three giant introns ranging in size from 60-100 kb each separate the human beta2 exons in the N-terminus. Thus, when human exon 3 is spliced to exon 7 (beginning GSAD...), the splicing machinery must exclude over 250 kb of intronic sequence. In contrast, introns in the remaining part of the human beta2 gene averaged 3261 bp in size. In humans, the mean size of introns adjoining coding sequences is 3749 bp, but 75% of introns are smaller than 2609 bp [51,53,54]. A recent analysis of the genomes of Arabidopsis thaliana, Drosophila, mouse and human indicated that the median sizes of introns separating 5' UTR (non-coding) sequence are significantly larger than introns separating coding sequence [54]. Even so, the median size of human 5' UTR introns (8223 bp, [54]) is still much smaller than the human 5' beta2 introns, which separate coding sequences.

We used CLUSTALW software to align the beta2 subunit core domain amino acid sequences and TreePuzzle and PAUP* to construct phylogenetic trees using maximum likelihood and maximum parsimony, respectively [55]. By using only core domains (~SH3-GK), we minimized differences due to alternative splicing (Fig. 6). The Fugu and Tetraodon beta2 subunit genes, zebrafish beta2.1, zebrafish beta2.2, and other vertebrate beta2 genes form a monophyletic group (MLQP = 87%, MPBP < 50%). The sequence of beta2.2 is substantially more diverged from other vertebrate beta2 genes than is beta2.1, both in amino acid replacements (see branch lengths in Fig. 6) and insertions/deletions. Portions of the gene were sufficiently divergent to be essentially random in sequence with respect to all other taxa, and the region 5' of the SH3 domain also contained an 18-amino acid deletion. No convincing region of synteny could be established between any of the teleost beta2 genes and human beta2, despite the fact that human chromosome 10 and mouse chromosome 2 share a region of synteny inclusive of the beta2 genes. Thus, a syntenic approach was not useful to confirm or refute the orthology of zebrafish beta2.1 or beta2.2 with mammalian beta2 genes.

###begin title 34
Heterogeneity of calcium channel beta2 expression in the embryo and adult
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 269 271 266 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2D</xref>
###xml 406 407 403 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>
###xml 543 545 534 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7A</xref>
###xml 616 619 607 610 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">th </sup>
###xml 657 659 648 650 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 554 563 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
To determine whether beta2 genes are expressed in a stage- or tissue-specific manner in early embryogenesis, we performed RT-PCR on RNA isolated from embryos of several stages in early development. To track the expression patterns of specific transcript variants (Fig. 2D), we used forward primers specific to the 5' exons 1 or 2, and reverse primers in exon 10 (the GK domain) in RT-PCR experiments (Fig. 7). Surprisingly, amplification of beta2.1 and beta2.2 transcripts occurred in embryos as young as the 4-cell and 1000-cell stages (Fig. 7A). Since zebrafish zygotic transcription does not initiate until the 10th cell division (~the 1000-cell stage) [56], the presence of mRNA in 4-cell embryos indicates that the transcripts are of maternal origin. The beta2.2 transcript variant 2 was expressed steadily from the 4-cell stage through 72 hours post-fertilization (hpf). In contrast, beta2.2 transcript variant 1 showed a pulse of expression in early epiboly stages. beta2.1 transcript variant 6 was robustly detected from 26 hpf through at least 3 dpf. Other beta2.1 transcript variants (1 and 2) were detected more sporadically or were undetectable in this assay, consistent with their rare recovery in RACE reactions. Thus, both beta2.1 and beta2.2 are expressed from the earliest stages of embryogenesis, but show significant heterogeneity in patterns of transcript variant expression throughout the first three days of embryogenesis.

###xml 335 341 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 455 464 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 624 629 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
To determine which transcript variants of the zebrafish beta2 genes are expressed in the embryonic heart, we performed RT-PCR on RNA extracted from cardiac tissue at 72 hpf (Fig. 7B). These data indicate that only a subset of beta2.1 and beta2.2 transcript variants are expressed in the embryonic heart relative to the adult heart. In humans, at least seven beta2 transcript variants are expressed in the heart, including beta2a and beta2e [34,35,39]. In zebrafish, the beta2.1 transcript variant 6 (resembling beta2e at the N-terminus) was expressed only in the embryo, whereas the beta2.2 transcript variant 1 (resembling human beta2a at the N-terminus) was expressed in the adult but not embryonic heart.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 207 209 198 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7C</xref>
The expression of beta2 subunits in several adult organs or tissues was assayed. In most adult tissues, we observed transcript variant-specific patterns of expression for the beta2.1 and beta2.2 genes (Fig. 7C). Mutually exclusive expression of beta2.1 transcript variants 1 and 6 occurred in the heart and brain, respectively. Some other tissues express more than one beta2.1 transcript variant. Many adult tissues express both beta2.2 transcript variants 1 and 2, but a few tissues (muscle, gill and skin) expressed only beta2.2 transcript variant 2. Thus, adult tissues also show significant heterogeneity in expression of beta2 subunit transcript variants.
###end p 38
###begin title 39
Discussion
###end title 39
###begin title 40
Teleost calcium channel beta subunits and embryonic development
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 124 129 121 126 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fugu </italic>
###xml 133 143 130 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tetraodon </italic>
###xml 89 98 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
We have identified four new calcium channel beta2 subunits in teleosts, including two in zebrafish and one gene each in the Fugu and Tetraodon genomes. Like the mammalian calcium channel beta subunits, the four teleost beta2 subunit genes encode characteristic MAGUK family proteins, with SH3 and GK domains that are highly conserved with other vertebrates. We show that several alternatively spliced transcripts arise from both beta2.1 and beta2.2 subunits, which are expressed in the embryo and adult but subject to both temporal and spatial regulation. Only selected transcript variants of each beta2 subunit gene are expressed in the embryonic heart, and these differ from adult transcript variants. Thus, the heterogeneity of beta subunits and their transcript variants in teleost species is extensive.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
Gene structure of teleost calcium channel beta2 subunits
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 992 994 959 961 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 1140 1142 1104 1106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 1143 1145 1107 1109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 1161 1169 1125 1133 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 1316 1318 1277 1279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 1319 1321 1280 1282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 1571 1573 1520 1522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
###xml 1731 1733 1668 1670 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
###xml 2005 2007 1942 1944 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B63">63</xref>
###xml 2008 2010 1945 1947 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>
###xml 2192 2194 2126 2128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 128 137 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 735 740 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 800 805 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 953 958 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1016 1021 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1196 1201 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 2234 2243 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 2309 2318 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Alternative splicing occurs within the N-terminus as well as internally in the beta2.1 and beta2.2 subunit genes. Notably, each zebrafish beta2 subunit gene encodes at least two mutually exclusive 5' exons, each with a separate translation initiation site. Each gene has one 5' exon conserved with mammals, and one 5' exon unique to fish. Similarly, some of the internal alternatively spliced exons are similar to those in mammals, while others are unique to teleosts. This variety of transcript variants potentially encodes an array of different beta2 subunit proteins. Previous studies have demonstrated that 5' variation due to alternative splicing can be functionally significant in beta subunits. When expressed in HEK 293 cells, human 5' beta2 subunit variants beta2a and beta2e (starting with human exons 4 or 6, respectively) showed differential sub-cellular localization compared to transcript variants beta2b, beta2c and beta2d (starting with human exons 4, 1 and 2, respectively) [57]. Moreover, the five human 5' beta2 subunit variants differentially affected open probability, peak current and availability of L-type channels [57,58]. Likewise, in Xenopus oocyte expression studies, human beta4 subunits with alternatively spliced N-termini showed functionally distinct electrophysiological properties [59,60]. Domain swapping experiments further indicate the functional significance of the N-terminal sequences in beta subunit proteins. Replacing the beta1b N-terminus with beta2a N-terminus created a chimeric beta protein with slow inactivation kinetics [61]. Conversely, replacing beta2a N-terminus with the non-palmitoylated beta3 N-terminus created a chimeric beta protein with beta3-like inactivation kinetics [62]. Post-translational modifications such as phosphorylation or palmitoylation, present on exons near the N-terminus, may account for some of the functional diversity, although this functional relationship has been experimentally demonstrated for only a few genes to date [63,64]. Intriguingly, a recent report indicates that the length of the beta2 N-terminus is, independent of sequence, an important factor in mediating the magnitude of channel modulation [58]. Further functional studies using the zebrafish model system will be necessary to determine whether the array of zebrafish beta2 subunit proteins indeed have different biochemical functions within the cell.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 436 438 427 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B65">65</xref>
###xml 1286 1288 1274 1276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B65">65</xref>
###xml 1289 1291 1277 1279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B66">66</xref>
###xml 90 95 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
A curious feature of at least five species of teleost beta2 subunit genes, as well as the human beta2 subunit gene, is that some of the largest introns of the genome separate the several short, alternatively spliced 5' coding exons. Do these large introns have any adaptive significance for beta2 subunit biology? In several instances, introns have been shown to incorporate enhancer or repressor elements that influence transcription [65]. Expanding this idea, we propose that the unusual beta2 gene structure may provide a mechanism for independent cis-regulation of each transcript variant; that is, sequences within individual introns might independently direct the tissue and temporally-specific expression of transcript variants utilizing the associated ATG-containing 5' exon. This hypothesis is supported by the observation that transcript variant-specific patterns of expression do in fact occur in both embryo and adult. However, it is increasingly becoming appreciated that, beyond regulatory elements, introns can impact mRNA metabolism in a number of other ways. Potential effects include modulating transcription rates by regulating DNA accessibility, modulating editing and polyadenylation of the pre-mRNA, and affecting nuclear export, translation and mRNA decay rates [65,66].
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 567 569 564 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B67">67</xref>
###xml 777 779 774 776 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B67">67</xref>
###xml 780 782 777 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B68">68</xref>
###xml 978 980 975 977 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B69">69</xref>
###xml 981 983 978 980 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B71">71</xref>
###xml 110 119 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 514 519 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 524 529 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The inclusion of different subsets of 5' exons (and their associated introns) among each of the pufferfish or zebrafish beta2 subunit genes is interesting from an evolutionary standpoint. A canonical view is that variations in protein structure and function form the basis of evolutionary innovation and phenotypic divergence. However, as more genomes are sequenced and annotated, it is becoming evident that alternative splicing substantially increases the proteome in many species. (For example, about 40-50% of human and mouse genes contain alternative promoters [67]). A corollary to this hypothesis is that the regulatory circuitry regulating alternative splicing or the expression of particular transcript variants is also an important source of evolutionary innovation [67,68]. The mechanisms by which new introns arise during evolution, and the impact of large intron size per se on cis-regulation or other adaptive phenomena, remain actively debated areas of research [69-71].
###end p 45
###begin title 46
The evolutionary origin of the duplicated calcium channel beta2 subunit genes
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 154 156 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B72">72</xref>
###xml 157 159 154 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B74">74</xref>
###xml 322 327 316 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fugu </italic>
###xml 330 340 324 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tetraodon </italic>
###xml 599 601 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B75">75</xref>
###xml 696 698 687 689 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B75">75</xref>
###xml 850 852 826 828 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 853 855 829 831 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B76">76</xref>
###xml 1008 1010 984 986 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B73">73</xref>
###xml 1209 1211 1185 1187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B73">73</xref>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">Zebrafish</span>
###xml 507 516 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 685 694 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 782 791 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1228 1237 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Zebrafish beta2 duplication may be the consequence of a genome-wide duplication thought to have occurred in teleosts approximately 300 million years ago [72-74]. Alternatively, duplication could be the result of an ancient region-specific duplication. The beta2 subunit gene does not appear to be duplicated in either the Fugu or Tetraodon genomes. The existence of single beta2 subunit homologs was of itself not particularly surprising since phylogeny and synteny data suggest that the common ancestor of zebrafish and pufferfish underwent a large-scale gene or complete genome duplication event [75]. Subsequently, pufferfish may have lost many duplicates that were retained in the zebrafish [75]. Data from the other beta gene paralog groups (beta1 and beta4) are equivocal, as zebrafish have one described copy of beta1 and two copies of beta4 [41,76]. The fate of most duplicated genes is the accumulation of degenerative mutations in coding or regulatory regions that leads to gene loss or silencing [73]. Alternatively, genes may acquire separable patterns of expression or separable functions that then require the maintenance of functional copies of both genes in the genome (subfunctionalization) [73]. Since the two zebrafish beta2 genes are both robustly but differentially expressed, our data are more consistent with subfunctionalization.
###end p 47
###begin title 48
Developmental expression patterns of calcium channel beta2 subunit transcript variants
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 73 75 73 75 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B77">77</xref>
###xml 934 936 922 924 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B76">76</xref>
###xml 481 490 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 820 829 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Calcium is an important signal in the embryo even prior to gastrulation [77]. Calcium gradients, waves and pulses have been described in the blastula and early gastrula that may represent key pattern forming events. The mechanism underlying these calcium signals, and whether they encompass voltage-gated calcium channels, is not clear. Surprisingly, both beta2 subunits show maternal as well as the early zygotic expression of several transcript variants. Our recent study of two zebrafish beta4 subunits, which are also expressed maternally and zygotically in the gastrulating embryo, indicates that these genes are essential for normal epiboly. Initially, we predicted that beta subunits might be involved in the gastrulating embryo voltage-gated calcium channel-related functions. However, our study showed that the zebrafish beta4 subunits, at least, operate independently of voltage-gated calcium channels in mediating epiboly [76].
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 701 703 683 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 704 706 686 688 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 852 854 831 833 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B78">78</xref>
###xml 855 857 834 836 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B80">80</xref>
###xml 230 235 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 844 850 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
In addition to possible early roles, we find that expression of beta2.1 or beta2.2 is under strong temporal control in the post-gastrula embryo and in adult tissues. Of particular interest, given the cardiac lethality observed in mouse beta2-deficient embryos, is the observation of differential subsets of beta2.1 and beta2.2 transcript variants expressed in the embryonic and adult heart. This observation supports the hypothesis, previously proposed by others, that the heterogeneity of beta subunit transcript variant expression within the heart may provide a mechanism for fine-tuning the cardiac voltage-activated current as the organism progresses through embryonic, juvenile and adult stages [38,39]. In addition, the expression levels of particular beta2 transcript variants has also been linked to pathophysiology of heart failure in humans [78-80]. Clearly, the first step in interpreting the function of beta subunits in any model system is simply to understand when and where the various transcript variants are expressed.
###end p 50
###begin title 51
Conclusion
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 106 115 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 580 585 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 802 811 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
The primary novel findings of this study are as follows: 1) We have cloned two new beta2 subunit genes in zebrafish, one of which is phylogenetically quite divergent in amino acid sequence. They are classified as MAGUK proteins on the basis of high conservation with mammalian MAGUK core regions. 2) Alternative splicing occurs at the beta2 N-termini and internally. A comparative analysis showed that a subset of 5' exons present in several teleosts species is shared with mammals, while a different subset appears to be unique to teleosts. 3) Some of the largest introns in the human or teleost genomes separate the small, alternatively spliced 5' exons of beta2 genes, leading us to hypothesize that beta subunit transcript variant expression may be under independent cis-regulatory control. 4) The zebrafish beta2 genes are expressed maternally and zygotically in the gastrulating embryo and show strong evidence of temporal and spatial regulation in embryogenesis and in adults. 5) Only a subset of beta2 subunit transcript variants is expressed in the embryonic heart, and they differ from those expressed in the adult heart. In sum, this work provides the groundwork for a study of functional aspects of beta2 subunit biology. The high degree of temporal and spatial control of beta subunits, combined with recently identified non-canonical functions for particular beta subunits or transcript variants, suggests that a functional analysis of these genes would provide intriguing insight.
###end p 52

###end title 58
###begin p 59
###xml 67 72 64 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fugu </italic>

###xml 1192 1201 1174 1183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tetraodon</italic>
###xml 1408 1410 1390 1392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 1411 1413 1393 1395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 1797 1799 1779 1781 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B82">82</xref>
###xml 8 13 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 18 27 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 199 204 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 208 217 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1097 1106 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1110 1115 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1702 1708 <span type="species:ncbi:8090">medaka</span>
We used human and zebrafish beta2 sequences to identify homologous Fugu and Tetraodon beta2 sequences by a multi-step process. First, we performed protein vs. translated DNA (TBLASTN) searches using human or zebrafish beta2 sequences against the NCBI, Sanger Centre, UCSC Genome Bioinformatics and pufferfish genome databases [43-47]. This process identified the best matches among the Fugu or Tetraodon predicted proteins derived from contig assemblies and other sequences available for pufferfish. The homologous predicted Fugu or Tetraodon proteins were re-tested by TBLASTN against mammalian Refseq proteins to confirm they were more similar to beta2 subunit genes than other beta subunit family members. Next, we used the homologous Fugu or Tetraodon seqeunces in BLAT searches of UCSC Genome Bioinformatics database sequences to identify the genomic location of Fugu or Tetraodon beta2 exons 3-14 and to determine conserved splice donor and splice acceptor sites [45]. To identify the short N terminal exons (exons 1 and 2) or search for alternative HOOK domain exons in pufferfish, we used zebrafish or human exons in 1) BLAT searches of UCSC Genome Bioinformatics pufferfish (Fugu or Tetraodon) sequences, 2) MEGABLAST, peptide motif and BLASTP searches of NCBI, and 3) BLAST or BLAT searches of the Fugu Genome Browser, the Tetraodon Genome Browser, or the Sanger Center Ensemble Genomic databases [43-47]. Exons were numbered according to their sequential location in the genome, and probable splice donor and splice acceptor sites were identified for each exon. The pufferfish sequences were submitted to the Third Party Annotation database of NCBI. Additional teleost sequences from trout, medaka, and stickleback were obtained by searching the NCBI EST or WGS databases, or from JGI [82].

###begin title 60
Phylogenetic analysis
###end title 60
###begin p 61
###xml 339 341 336 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B83">83</xref>
###xml 466 477 460 471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 901 903 890 892 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B84">84</xref>
###xml 1144 1146 1133 1135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B85">85</xref>
###xml 1274 1276 1263 1265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 1484 1495 1473 1484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 360 369 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 466 476 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 672 681 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1484 1494 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 1553 1562 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Amino acid sequences representing the beta subunit "core" (i.e., from the amino acids "GSAD" just prior to the SH3 domain through the end of the GK domain) were aligned using ClustalW, varying gap opening and extension parameters; regions for which reliable homology could not be established because of indels were excluded from analysis [83]. Portions of the zebrafish beta2.2 gene were sufficiently divergent in sequence from all other genes (including those from Drosophila and mosquito) to be essentially random. To minimize the phylogenetic analytical problems associated with such extreme rate variation among lineages, we excluded regions of the alignment in which zebrafish beta2.2 contained autapomorphic amino acid substitutions for >/= 3 adjacent amino acid positions. Such exclusions resulted in an alignment of 252 amino acids. Maximum likelihood analyses were implemented in TreePuzzle [84] using the Muller & Vingron (VT) model of amino acid substitution with a mixed model of rate heterogeneity; amino acid frequencies, the gamma distribution parameter alpha, and the proportion of invariant sites were estimated from the data [85]. Nodal support was assessed using quartet puzzling. Equally-weighted maximum parsimony analyses were carried out using PAUP* [55]. A heuristic search was performed with 25 random additional replicates and TBR branch-swapping. Bootstrap proportions for clades were assessed with 1,000 pseudo-replicates. Resulting trees were rooted with Drosophila and mosquito. Teleost sequences other than pufferfish and zebrafish were not included because they only span a portion of the core domain.
###end p 61
###begin title 62
Reverse transcription assays
###end title 62
###begin p 63
###xml 207 217 207 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cmlc2:GFP </italic>
###xml 247 253 247 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cmlc2 </italic>
###xml 306 308 306 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B86">86</xref>
As an RNA source, we used whole embryos, or individual organs or tissues dissected from adults aged approximately 1 year. Cardiac tissue from embryos was isolated on the basis of its GFP expression from the cmlc2:GFP transgenic line, in which the cmlc2 promoter drives expression of GFP only in the heart [86]. RNA was extracted using the Trizol method and stored at -80degreesC. RT-PCR was performed for beta2.1 and beta2.2 genes using the Access RT-PCR System (Promega) using high-fidelity polymerases. The thermal cycling program was as follows: 48degreesC for 45 minutes (reverse transcription), followed by 94degreesC for 2 minutes (activation of PCR enzyme) and 40 cycles of 94degreesC for 30 seconds, 60degreesC for 1 minute, and 68degreesC for 2 minutes. A final step of 68degreesC for 7 minutes allowed for a final extension. Electrophoresis of the final product was performed on a 2% agarose gel containing ethidium bromide. Gels were imaged using a digital camera and imaging software (Scion Corporation, Frederick, MD).
