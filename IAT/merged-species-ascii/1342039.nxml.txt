Nucleolin links to arsenic-induced stabilization of GADD45alpha mRNA

###begin p 3
###xml 252 254 244 246 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 335 337 323 325 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 545 547 521 523 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 551 553 527 529 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 680 682 652 654 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 763 765 735 737 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 900 902 872 874 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1149 1151 1105 1107 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 175 180 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The present study shows that arsenic induces GADD45alpha (growth arrest and DNA damage inducible gene 45alpha) mainly through post-transcriptional mechanism. Treatment of the human bronchial epithelial cell line, BEAS-2B, with arsenic(III) chloride (As3+) resulted in a significant increase in GADD45alpha protein and mRNA. However, As3+ only exhibited a marginal effect on the transcription of the GADD45alpha gene. The accumulation of GADD45alpha mRNA is largely achieved by the stabilization of GADD45alpha mRNA in the cellular response to As3+. As3+ is able to induce binding of mRNA stabilizing proteins, nucleolin and less potently, HuR, to the GADD45alpha mRNA. Although As3+ was unable to affect the expression of nucleolin, treatment of the cells with As3+ resulted in re-distribution of nucleolin from nucleoli to nucleoplasm. Silencing of the nucleolin mRNA by RNA interference reversed As3+-induced stabilization of the GADD45alpha mRNA and accumulation of the GADD45alpha protein. Stabilization of GADD45alpha mRNA, thus, represents a novel mechanism contributing to the production of GADD45alpha and cell cycle arrest in response to As3+.

###begin title 4
INTRODUCTION
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 242 243 234 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1">1</xref>
###xml 339 340 327 328 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 373 374 361 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2">2</xref>
###xml 501 502 485 486 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">3</xref>
###xml 604 605 584 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b4">4</xref>
###xml 606 607 586 587 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b5">5</xref>
###xml 827 828 796 797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2">2</xref>
###xml 915 916 880 881 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6">6</xref>
###xml 924 925 889 890 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b7">7</xref>
###xml 946 947 911 912 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8">8</xref>
###xml 959 960 924 925 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9">9</xref>
###xml 968 970 933 935 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10">10</xref>
###xml 988 990 953 955 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b11">11</xref>
###xml 614 619 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Growth arrest and DNA damage inducible gene 45alpha (GADD45alpha) is a widely expressed, inducible nuclear protein that plays critical role in the checkpoint function of cells in response to a wide spectrum of DNA-damaging or stress signals (1). GADD45alpha has been shown to inhibit cyclin B/CDC2, a key protein kinase complex governing G2/M transition of the cell cycle (2). In addition, GADD45alpha is an important protein involved in genomic stability by its contributions to DNA excision repair (3). Furthermore, GADD45alpha has been implicated in cell apoptosis, cell survival and innate immunity (4,5). The human GADD45alpha is an acidic protein composed of 165 amino acids, with some similarities to GADD45beta, GADD45gamma and ribosomal protein S12. In addition to binding to cyclin B/CDC2 as originally demonstrated (2), GADD45alpha is also capable of interacting with proliferating cell nuclear antigen (6), p21 (7), histone proteins (8), TAFII70 (9), p38 (10) and MTK1/MEKK4 (11), a MAPK kinase kinase that can activate JNK and p38 subgroups of MAP kinase.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 221 222 213 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6">6</xref>
###xml 727 729 707 709 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b12">12</xref>
###xml 959 961 927 929 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b13">13</xref>
###xml 1080 1082 1044 1046 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14">14</xref>
###xml 1091 1093 1055 1057 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b15">15</xref>
###xml 1108 1110 1068 1070 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b16">16</xref>
###xml 1120 1122 1080 1082 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b17">17</xref>
###xml 1145 1147 1105 1107 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b18">18</xref>
###xml 1208 1210 1164 1166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19">19</xref>
###xml 1222 1224 1178 1180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b20">20</xref>
The transcriptional regulation of GADD45alpha has been extensively studied during the past several years. The best-studied transcriptional regulator for the expression of GADD45alpha is the tumor suppressor protein, p53 (6). In response to ionizing radiation or methyl methansulfonate, GADD45alpha was rapidly up-regulated through a p53-dependent mechanism. A consensus p53 binding site has been identified in the third intron region of the GADD45alpha gene. Ionizing radiation or certain other DNA-damaging signals induce binding of p53 to this site, followed by the recruitment of acetyltransferase p300/CBP and protein arginine methyltransferases PRMT1 or CARM1 to this region to stimulate the transcription of GADD45alpha (12). The promoter region of GADD45alpha lacks a consensus p53 binding site. However, p53 can also stimulate the transcription of GADD45alpha by forming a complex with WT1 that binds directly to the proximal promoter of GADD45alpha (13). Other transcription factors that possibly contribute to a p53-independent regulation of GADD45alpha include FoxO3a (14), Oct1 (15), C/EBPalpha (16), Egr-1 (17), POU family members (18), and two transcriptional repressors of GADD45alpha, c-myc (19) and ZBRK (20).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 97 99 97 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b21">21</xref>
###xml 100 102 100 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b22">22</xref>
###xml 184 186 184 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b23">23</xref>
###xml 264 266 264 266 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 295 297 295 297 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5+</sup>
###xml 629 631 629 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b24">24</xref>
###xml 879 881 875 877 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b25">25</xref>
###xml 882 884 878 880 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b26">26</xref>
###xml 1031 1033 1023 1025 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b27">27</xref>
###xml 1149 1151 1137 1139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b28">28</xref>
###xml 1191 1193 1175 1177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b29">29</xref>
###xml 300 306 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Humans</span>
###xml 866 871 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1398 1403 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Arsenic is a naturally occurring metalloid that exhibits potent carcinogenic effects in mammals (21,22). It exists in both inorganic and organic forms with different oxidation states (23). The primary forms of arsenic in environment are the inorganic trivalent (As3+) and pentavalent arsenic (As5+). Humans are exposed to arsenic mainly through oral consumption of contaminated water, food or drugs, and inhalation of arsenic-containing dust or smoke in several occupational settings. Paradoxically, arsenic has also been used as an effective single therapeutic agent for several tumors, especially acute promyelocytic leukemia (24). However, the molecular mechanisms of arsenic-induced carcinogenesis or arsenic-induced remissions of tumors are not fully understood. We and others have previously shown that arsenic is a potent inducer of GADD45alpha expression in human cells (25,26). We have also shown that activation of c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK) might be partially responsible for the induction of GADD45alpha by arsenic (27). The involvement of JNK in GADD45alpha expression was further confirmed in the cellular response to UV radiation (28) or a PPARgamma agonist, troglitazone (29). In an attempt to gain insight into the detailed mechanism of arsenic-induced expression of GADD45alpha, we examined the transcriptional and post-transcriptional regulations of GADD45alpha expression in human bronchial epithelial cells subjected to arsenic exposure. The data presented here reveal that the arsenic-induced expression of GADD45alpha is mainly regulated by post-transcriptional mechanism in which the mRNA of GADD45alpha was bound and stabilized by the RNA binding proteins, mainly nucleolin.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 8
###begin title 9
Cell culture, transfections and luciferase assays
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 211 212 205 206 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 170 174 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 359 364 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The human bronchial epithelial cell line, BEAS-2B, was purchased from American Tissue Culture Collection (Manassas, VA) and maintained in DMEM supplemented with 5% fetal calf serum and grown at 37degreesC, 5% CO2 in a humidified incubator. Transfections were performed using lipofectamine 2000 as suggested by the manufacturer (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). The human GADD45alpha promoter and intron 3 luciferase reporter constructs were provided by Dr Albert J. Fornace at National Institutes of Health (NIH, Bethesda, MD). In these vectors, the GADD45alpha promoter region from -994 to +26 and the entire intron 3 region were inserted into the upstream of the luciferase reporter gene, respectively. Cells were harvested at 36 h and analyzed for luciferase activity using the Promega Dual-Luciferase Assay System (Promega, Madison, WI). The data shown are the mean of at least three independent experiments with error bars displaying standard deviations.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
Cell treatment and western blotting
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 76 77 76 77 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5</sup>
###xml 197 199 197 199 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 235 236 235 236 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 237 238 237 238 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 382 384 382 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b30">30</xref>
###xml 488 490 488 490 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
The BEAS-2B cells were seeded in 6-well tissue plates at a density of 2 x 105 cells/well and cultured for 60 h. The cells were treated with the indicated concentrations of arsenic(III) chloride (As3+) (Sigma-Aldrich, St Louis, MO) or H2O2 (Sigma, MO) in the absence or presence of 10 mM N-acetyl-L-cysteine (NAC) (Sigma, MO). Total cell lysate was prepared as described previously (30). Twenty-five micrograms of the protein lysate from the cells cultured in the absence or presence of As3+ were analyzed by SDS-PAGE and immunoblotted with the indicated antibodies. The antibodies against GADD45alpha, actin, nucleolin, HuR and IKKgamma were purchased from Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Inc. (Santa Cruz, CA). The antibodies against phospho-FoxO3a, total FoxO3a, phospho-Akt and total Akt were purchased from Cell Signaling (Beverly, MA).

###begin title 13
RT-PCR
###end title 13
###begin p 14
The levels of GADD45alpha and GAPDH mRNA in cell lysate or immune complex were determined by RT-PCR using the AccessQuick RT-PCR system (Promega, Madison, WI). The cells cultured in 6-well tissue culture plates were washed with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and lysed using cell lysis buffer from Cells-to-cDNA II kit (Ambion, Austin, TX) as suggested by the manufacturer. RT-PCR was performed using 3 microl of cell lysate and primer sets as follows: GADD45alpha sense: 5'-GGAGAGCAGAAGACCGAAA-3' and GADD45alpha antisense: 5'-TCACTGGAACCCATTGATC-3'; GAPDH sense: 5'-CTGAACGGGAAGCTCACTGGCATGGCCTTC-3' and antisense: 5'-CATGAGGTCCACCACCCTGTTGCTGTAGCC-3'.

###begin title 15
Real-time RT-PCR
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 134 137 130 131 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#174;</sup>
###xml 775 777 744 746 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>

###xml 1078 1079 1041 1042 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</italic>
###xml 1079 1080 1042 1043 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T</sub>
###xml 1184 1187 1147 1148 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#174;</sup>
###xml 1296 1297 1257 1258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</italic>
###xml 1297 1298 1258 1259 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T</sub>
To verify the results of RT-PCR, a quantitative real-time RT-PCR was performed. The GADD45alpha mRNA levels were measured using TaqMan(R) primers designed using Universal Probe Library Assay Design Center () with the ABI 7500 Sequence Detector (PE Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA). The primers for GADD45 (Accession no. L24498) were forward, 5'-TCAGCCCAGCTACTCCCTAC; reverse, 5'-AATCTGCCCTGCTAAAGGAAT, used with Universal Probe #16. The primers for the house-keeping gene GAPDH (NM_002046) were forward, 5'-AGCCACATCGCTCAGACAC; reverse, GCCCAATACGACCAAATCC, used with Universal Probe #60. Total RNA was isolated using RNAqueoustrade mark -4PCR kits (Ambion, Austin, TX) from BEAS-2B cells (approximately2 million cells) cultured in the absence or presence of 20 microM As3+ for 1-8 h. One to two micrograms of the DNAse I-treated RNA was reverse transcribed, using Superscript II (Life Technologies, Gaithersburg, MD). The cDNA generated was diluted 1:100 and 15 microl was used to conduct the PCR according to the TaqMan(R) Master mix PCR kit instructions. The comparative CT (threshold cycle) method was used to calculate the relative concentrations (User Bulletin #2, ABI PRISM(R) 7700 Sequence Detector, PE Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA). Briefly, the method involves obtaining the CT values for the GADD45alpha mRNA, normalizing to a house-keeping gene, GAPDH, and deriving the fold increase compared with control, unstimulated cells.

###begin title 17
RNA immunoprecipitation assay
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 70 72 66 68 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 153 155 149 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b31">31</xref>
###xml 156 158 152 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b32">32</xref>
###xml 303 304 295 296 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 396 397 385 386 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 399 400 388 389 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 522 523 501 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
BEAS-2B cells were cultured in the absence or presence of 20 microM As3+ for 4 h and subjected to RNA immunoprecipitation assay as described previously (31,32) with minor modifications. Briefly, cells were lysed in 500 microl of cell lysis buffer containing 20 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.5), 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM Na2EDTA, 1 mM EGTA, 1% Triton, 2.5 mM sodium pyrophosphate, 1 mM beta-glycerophosphate, 1 mM Na3VO4 and 1 microg/ml of leupeptin for 30 min at 4degreesC. Cell debris in the lysates was removed by centrifugation at 14 000 g for 15 min at 4degreesC. The supernatants were incubated overnight with the indicated antibodies at 4degreesC under rotation. The protein-mRNA binding complex was immunoprecipitated by incubation of the lysates with Protein A-Agarose for 4 h at 4degreesC. The immune complex was washed three times in lysis buffer. The mRNA of GADD45alpha and GAPDH in both the immune complex and supernatant were determined by RT-PCR.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
Immunofluorescence staining
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 207 209 207 209 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 588 594 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
BEAS-2B cells were seeded into 24-well tissue culture plate without glass slides at a concentration of 5000 to 10 000 cells/well and cultured for 24 h. The cells were then either untreated or treated with As3+ for an additional 4 h. The cells were fixed directly in the culture plate by 10% formalin and permeabilized with 0.1% Triton X-100 for 10 min at room temperature, respectively. Cells were incubated for 6 h at 4degreesC with primary antibody diluted (1:100) in PBS containing 5% BSA. After extensive washing with PBS, cells were incubated with Fluorescein (FITC)-conjugated anti-rabbit IgG (Santa Cruz, CA) in 1:100 dilution in PBS containing 5% BSA and 1 microg/ml of propidium iodide (PI) for 1 h at room temperature. Fluorescein images were captured by using a Zeiss Axiovert100 microscope connected with a Pixera Pro150ES digital camera.

###xml 580 582 560 562 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>

###xml 722 724 694 696 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 942 944 902 904 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 946 955 906 915 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1A</xref>
###xml 988 990 948 950 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1320 1322 1272 1274 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
We have previously shown that As3+ induced cell cycle arrest at the G2/M phase, which correlated with the induction of GADD45alpha protein (25). To obtain insight into the possible mechanism of As3+-induced GADD45alpha, the cells were pre-treated with 10 mM N-acetyl-cysteine (NAC), a widely used antioxidant that provides cells with exogenous glutathione (GSH) precursor, for 12 h and then treated with 0-20 microM As3+ for an additional 12 h. The expression of GADD45alpha was barely detectable in the cells without As3+ treatment (Figure 1A). The induction of GADD45alpha by As3+ was dose-dependent. A plateau of GADD45alpha induction was reached when the cells were treated with 20 microM As3+. Further elevation of As3+ concentrations (more than 50 microM) did not increase the expression of GADD45alpha due to cytotoxicity (data not shown). Pre-treatment of the cells with 10 mM NAC completely blocked the induction of GADD45alpha by As3+ (Figure 1A, lanes 7-12), suggesting that As3+-induced GADD45alpha expression is possibly through either an oxidative stress response or a direct depletion of GSH. In an additional experimental setting, we pre-treated cells with increasing concentrations of aspirin, another antioxidant which acts as a free radical scavenger, and found that the induction of GADD45alpha by As3+ was partially inhibited by 10-20 mM aspirin (data not shown).

The inhibition of As3+-induced GADD45alpha by NAC and aspirin implies a possible involvement of reactive oxygen species in this process. Indeed, our previous report had demonstrated a substantial accumulation of H2O2 in the cells treated with As3+ (30). To determine whether H2O2 itself is able to induce GADD45alpha, the cells were treated with 50-800 microM H2O2 for 12 h. Figure 1B indicates that the induction of GADD45alpha by H2O2 is very marginal in comparing with the cells treated with As3+. An appreciable induction of GADD45alpha could be observed only in the cells treated with 400-800 microM H2O2 (Figure 1B, lanes 5 and 6, upper panel). At this concentration, however, the cells showed cytotoxic responses as indicated by the notable cell death determined microscopically (data not shown). Densitometry analysis of the GADD45alpha protein bands in four separate experiments indicated a more than 20-fold induction of the GADD45alpha by 20 microM As3+ and a 3- to 4-fold induction of the GADD45alpha by 800 microM H2O2, respectively (Figure 1C).

###begin title 27
###xml 2 4 2 4 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
As3+ has a weak effect on the transcription of GADD45alpha gene
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 196 198 188 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b12">12</xref>
###xml 225 227 217 219 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 454 456 438 440 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 611 620 583 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1D</xref>
###xml 670 679 642 651 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1B</xref>
###xml 683 684 655 656 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 685 686 657 658 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 797 806 765 774 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1D</xref>
###xml 938 939 902 903 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 940 941 904 905 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 943 952 907 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1D</xref>
###xml 101 106 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Earlier studies have indicated that the consensus p53 binding site in the third intron region of the human GADD45alpha gene is critical for the genotoxic stress-induced expression of GADD45alpha (12). It is unclear whether As3+ induces GADD45alpha expression through transcriptional regulation in a manner of either p53-dependent or p53-independent. By the use of GADD45alpha promoter- and intron3-based luciferase reporter gene vectors, we noted that As3+, at 20 microM, only induced 3- and 2-fold increase of GADD45alpha promoter-luciferase activity and GADD45alpha intron3-luciferase activity, respectively (Figure 1D, left panel). Similar to that of immunoblotting (Figure 1B), H2O2 exhibited no significant induction on the GADD45alpha promoter-luciferase activity at each dose point tested (Figure 1D, right panel). Only about 1.5-fold induction of intron3-luciferase activity was observed in the cells treated with 400-800 microM H2O2 (Figure 1D, right panel).
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 192 194 192 194 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 403 405 395 397 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 469 471 457 459 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 537 539 525 527 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 721 723 709 711 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
There is considerable limitation in reporter gene-based transcriptional analysis due to the absence of distant transcription enhancer elements in the reporter constructs. To address whether As3+ truly regulates the transcription of the GADD45alpha gene, we next performed a RT-PCR-based nuclear run-on assay. Since we had demonstrated that the accumulation of the GADD45alpha mRNA was peaked by a 4 h As3+ treatment (following), we incubated the cells with 20 microM As3+ for 4 h in this nuclear run-on assay. Exposure of the cells to As3+ did not induce an appreciable transcription in this assay (data not shown). Thus, these data indicate that it is unlikely that transcriptional regulation is the main mechanism of As3+-induced expression of the GADD45alpha.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
###xml 84 86 80 82 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
Inhibition of Akt has marginal effect on the expression of GADD45alpha induced by As3+
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 129 131 129 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b35">35</xref>
###xml 326 328 326 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b36">36</xref>
###xml 468 470 464 466 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14">14</xref>
###xml 731 733 719 721 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 915 917 899 901 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 995 997 975 977 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1009 1018 989 998 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Figure 2A</xref>
###xml 1174 1176 1154 1156 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1280 1282 1256 1258 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1396 1398 1372 1374 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 801 806 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1241 1246 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Akt signaling pathway is best known for its ability to counteract stress responses that lead to growth arrest or cell apoptosis (35). As a serine-threonine kinase, Akt is able to phosphorylate and inactivate proteins involved in cell cycle arrest or apoptosis. These proteins include FoxO3a, GSK3, Bad, eNOS and procaspase-9 (36). In response to DNA damage signals, FoxO3a appears to be the key transcription factor that up-regulates the transcription of GADD45alpha (14). Phosphorylation of FoxO3a by Akt suppresses the transcriptional activity of FoxO3a on the expression of GADD45alpha gene. Thus, inhibition of Akt, a negative regulator of FoxO3a, might indirectly contribute to the induction of GADD45alpha. To test whether As3+-induced GADD45alpha is through its effect on Akt-FoxO3a pathway in human epithelial cells, the phosphorylation status of Akt and FoxO3a was investigated in the cells treated with As3+ for different time periods. Induction of GADD45alpha occurred at 4-20 h of As3+ treatment (Figure 2A). A significant increase, rather than decrease of phosphorylation of FoxO3a and Akt, was observed at these time points. Thus, these results suggest that As3+-induced GADD45alpha is not through the inhibition of Akt in the human epithelial cells. In contrast, As3+ induces activation of Akt that subsequently phosphorylates and inactivates FoxO3a, which offsets the effect of As3+ on the induction of GADD45alpha.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 95 97 95 97 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 167 169 167 169 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 393 402 389 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Figure 2B</xref>
###xml 477 479 469 471 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 543 552 531 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Figure 2B</xref>
###xml 663 665 647 649 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 748 757 732 741 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Figure 2C</xref>
###xml 809 811 793 795 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1025 1027 1001 1003 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
We observed an increase in the phosphorylation of Akt and FoxO3a in the cellular response to As3+. Thus, it is worth testing whether inhibition of Akt amplifies the As3+-induced expression of GADD45alpha. Ly294002, a relatively specific inhibitor for phosphatidylinositol 3 kinase (PI3K), could completely block the activation of Akt and substantially, decrease the phosphorylation of FoxO3a (Figure 2B). However, only about 1- to 2-fold increase of GADD45alpha induction by As3+ was observed in the cells pre-treated with 10 microM Ly294002 (Figure 2B). Similarly, in a GADD45alpha promoter-based luciferase activity analysis, only a marginal amplification of As3+-induced luciferase activity could be seen in the cells pre-treated with Ly294002 (Figure 2C). Thus, these observations suggest that although As3+ is capable of stimulating the activation of Akt, a negative regulator for FoxO3a and the subsequent transcription of GADD45alpha gene, inhibition of Akt has a very weak effect on the induction of GADD45alpha by As3+.
###end p 32

As3+ induces accumulation GADD45alpha mRNA

###begin p 34

###xml 1118 1120 1062 1064 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1332 1334 1272 1274 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1346 1355 1286 1295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig3">Figure 3B</xref>
To demonstrate the correlation between the levels of protein and gene expression, the effect of As3+ on the induction of GADD45alpha mRNA was determined by RT-PCR. A substantial induction of GADD45alpha mRNA was observed in the cells treated with 20 microM As3+ for 1-6 h (Figure 3A). After 8 h, the GADD45alpha mRNA was declined to the basal level, indicating turnover of mRNA. The PCR primers we used correspond to the exon1 and exon4 region of the GADD45alpha gene, respectively, which amplify a fragment of GADD45alpha mRNA with a size of 453 bp (Figure 3A, fragment a). Interestingly, two additional fragments with size of approximately430 bp and approximately350 bp were observed in this RT-PCR analysis (Figure 3A, fragments 'b' and 'c'), possibly resulted from mRNA alternative splicing. DNA sequencing indicated that the fragment 'a' is indeed the full-length GADD45alpha mRNA as expected. The fragment 'c' was resulted from the splicing out of the entire exon2 region (GenBank ID ), whereas the sequencing of fragment 'b' was inclusive (data not shown). The accumulation of the GADD45alpha mRNA induced by As3+ was further verified by a quantitative real-time RT-PCR. In fully agreement with the results of the traditional RT-PCR, a 6- to 10-fold induction of the GADD45alpha mRNA was observed in the cells treated with As3+ for 1-4 h (Figure 3B).
###end p 34
###begin title 35
###xml 2 4 2 4 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
As3+ does not affect the degradation of GADD45alpha protein
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 129 131 121 123 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>

###xml 807 809 791 793 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>

###xml 1133 1142 1105 1114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figures 1</xref>
###xml 1147 1148 1119 1120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">2</xref>
We have observed a more than 10- to 20-fold induction of GADD45alpha protein and 6- to 10-fold increase of GADD45alpha mRNA by As3+ in our western blotting and RT-PCR experiments, respectively (Figures 1-3). However, we have failed to observe a significant transcriptional induction of the GADD45alpha gene by As3+ in both promoter/intron3 luciferase activity assay and nuclear run-on assay (Figure 1D and data not shown). In several other experimental settings, we have also tested the effect of As3+ on some different GADD45alpha promoter constructs that contain 1-2 kb promoter regions. In these experiments, we have failed to observe a more than a 3-fold induction of these promoter-luciferase activities by As3+ (D. Bhatia, V. Castranova and F. Chen, manuscript in preparation). Thus, we assume that As3+-induced GADD45alpha might be mainly through post-transcriptional mechanisms including alterations in mRNA or protein stability. We have failed to determine the protein stability of GADD45alpha by using cycloheximide (data not shown), since the GADD45alpha protein was barely detectable in the cells without As3+ treatment (Figures 1 and 2).

###begin p 37
###xml 23 25 23 25 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 225 227 217 219 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 279 281 271 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b37">37</xref>
###xml 317 319 309 311 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 421 423 409 411 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 588 590 572 574 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1318 1320 1290 1292 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1352 1354 1324 1326 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1465 1467 1433 1435 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
One possibility that As3+ induces accumulation of GADD45alpha protein is through interfering with either the ubiquitination of or the subsequent proteasome-mediated degradation of GADD45alpha. We have previously shown that As3+ induced proteasomal degradation of Cdc25C protein (37). Therefore, it is unlikely that As3+ induces GADD45alpha through inhibiting the proteolytic activity of the proteasome. To test whether As3+ is able to interfere with the process of GADD45alpha ubiquitination, the cells were pre-treated with a proteasome inhibitor, MG132, for 2 h and then treated with As3+ for 12 h. The ubiquitination of proteins can be visualized as smear high molecular weight bands in immunoblotting using lysates from the cells treated with MG132 or other proteasome inhibitors. The cell lysates were immunoprecipitated using antibody against GADD45alpha (Supplement Figure 1, lanes 1-4) or ubiquitin (Supplement Figure 1, lanes 5-8) and then the proteins in the immune complexes were immunoblotted with either anti-GADD45alpha antibody (Supplement Figure 1, upper panel) or re-probed with anti-ubiquitin antibody after stripping (Supplement Figure 1, lower panel). As can be seen in this figure, we did observe induction and ubiquitination of GADD45alpha in the cells pre-treated with MG132 in the absence of As3+. Treatment of the cells with As3+ did not decrease, but rather increased the ubiquitination of GADD45alpha protein. Thus, it is unlikely that As3+-induced accumulation of GADD45alpha is through preventing the ubiquitination of GADD45alpha protein.
###end p 37
###begin title 38
###xml 2 4 2 4 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
As3+ stabilizes GADD45alpha mRNA through nucleolin
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 39 41 39 41 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 165 167 157 159 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 398 407 382 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Figure 4A</xref>
###xml 651 653 627 629 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 673 675 649 651 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 773 775 745 747 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
Next, we tested the possibility that As3+ might be able to regulate the stability of GADD45alpha mRNA. To this end, cells were incubated with or without 20 microM As3+ for 4 h before that the transcription was blocked by adding 5 microg/ml of actinomycin D. The level of GADD45alpha mRNA was monitored by quantitative RT-PCR after 0, 1, 2, 4 or 8 h of post-actinomycin D treatment. As indicated in Figure 4A, the GADD45alpha mRNA from untreated cells displayed a strong reduction by almost 50% in the mRNA level after 8 h of transcription inhibition. In contrast, more than 80% GADD45alpha mRNA remained at this time point in the cells treated with As3+, indicating that As3+ stabilizes GADD45alpha mRNA substantially. The stability of the GAPDH mRNA was not affected by As3+. In fact, the GAPDH mRNA appears to be relatively stable (Supplement Figure 2).
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 460 469 456 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Figure 4B</xref>
###xml 563 565 559 561 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 626 635 622 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Figure 4B</xref>
###xml 704 706 700 702 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 743 752 739 748 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Figure 4B</xref>
The stability for many inducible mRNAs is regulated by a number of RNA-binding proteins that either stabilize or destabilize mRNAs. In mammalian cells, the functional characteristic of several mRNA stabilizing proteins, including nucleolin, HuR and YB-1, has been extensively investigated. To determine the involvement of these RNA-binding proteins in the regulation of GADD45alpha mRNA, the expression of nucleolin, HuR and YB-1 was investigated. As shown in Figure 4B, the expression of nucleolin and HuR was detectable under the basal condition. Addition of As3+ for 4, 8, 12 or 20 h did not change the level of nucleolin (Figure 4B, top panel), whereas the level of HuR was marginally decreased by As3+ in a roughly time-dependent manner (Figure 4B, the second panel). The expression of YB-1 was undetectable under the conditions tested.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 162 164 158 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b31">31</xref>

###xml 2668 2670 2592 2594 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 2783 2792 2699 2708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Figure 4C</xref>
###xml 2952 2954 2864 2866 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
To determine the binding of nucleolin and HuR to GADD45alpha mRNA, we next performed RNA immunoprecipitation, an established method described in the literatures (31,32), by using antibody against either nucleolin or HuR. The mRNAs of GADD45alpha and GAPDH in the immune complexes and the supernatants post-immunoprecipitation were determined by RT-PCR. In agreement with the western blotting data (Figure 4B), the amount of nucleolin in the immune complexes was unchanged upon treatment of cells with As3+, whereas the level of HuR was marginally reduced after the treatment of As3+ (Figure 4C, bottom panel). Trace amount of GADD45alpha mRNA in the control cells could be co-precipitated by either anti-nucleolin or anti-HuR antibody, indicating basal association of nucleolin and HuR with GADD45alpha mRNA. Treatment of the cells with 20 microM As3+ for 4 h increased the association of GADD45alpha with nucleolin (Figure 4C, the panel of GADD45alpha in IP, lane 2). As3+ was also capable of inducing the association of GADD45alpha mRNA with HuR, although in a less potent fashion compared with nucleolin (Figure 4C, the panel of GADD45alpha in IP, lane 5). Since there are reports indicating interconnection between PI3K-Akt and mRNA stability or nucleolin (38-40), we then tested the possible involvement of Akt signaling in the association of RNA-binding proteins with the GADD45alpha mRNA. Pre-treatment of the cells with Ly294002 inhibits phosphorylation of Akt (Figure 2). However, Ly294002 showed no effect on the As3+-induced association of GADD45alpha mRNA with nucleolin or HuR (Figure 4C, lanes 3 and 6). The association of GADD45alpha mRNA with nucleolin and HuR appeared to be specific, since there was no detectable GAPDH mRNA in the immune complexes (Figure 4C, the panel of GAPDH in IP, lanes 1, 2, 4 and 5). A non-specific association of GAPDH mRNA with either nucleolin or HuR was observed in the cells pre-treated with Ly294002 alone (data not shown) or in the presence of As3+ (Figure 4C, lanes 3 and 6). We also monitored the levels of GADD45alpha mRNA in the supernatants after immunoprecipitation with anti-nucleolin and anti-HuR antibody, respectively. The GADD45alpha mRNA was barely detected in these supernatants (Figure 4C, the panel of GADD45alpha in lysate), indicating that the majority of GADD45alpha mRNA had been co-precipitated by immunoprecipitation for either nucleolin or HuR. In a control experiment, we used an antibody against IKKgamma in immunoprecipitation and found no association of GADD45alpha mRNA with IKKgamma protein in the cells without or with As3+ treatment (Figure 4C, top panel, lanes 7 and 8). The basal and As3+-induced GADD45alpha mRNAs remained in the cell lysates that had been subjected to IKKgamma immunoprecipitation (Figure 4C, the 'GADD45 in lysate' panel, comparing lanes 7 and 8 with lanes 1-6). Therefore, these data strongly suggest that the stabilization of GADD45alpha mRNA by As3+ is through the inducible binding of nucleolin and less potently, HuR to GADD45alpha mRNA.

As3+ appeared to be very potent in inducing binding of nucleolin to the GADD45alpha mRNA (Figure 4C). However, As3+ was unable to influence the expression of nucleolin (Figure 4B). Thus, it is worth testing whether the functional aspect of nucleolin was modulated by As3+. To address the importance of nucleolin in As3+-induced stabilization of the GADD45alpha mRNA, we next used small interference RNA (siRNA) technique to knockdown nucleolin and determined the mRNA stability of the GADD45alpha in the cells treated with As3+. ###begin title 46
DISCUSSION
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 49 51 49 51 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 284 286 272 274 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 503 505 487 489 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
In this report, we have provided evidence that As3+-induced expression of GADD45alpha is through both transcriptional and more importantly, post-transcriptional mechanisms: stabilization of GADD45alpha mRNA. We have demonstrated that the accumulation of GADD45alpha mRNA induced by As3+ is very likely due to the inducible binding of nucleolin, and less potently, HuR, two RNA stabilizing proteins, to the GADD45alpha mRNA. Silencing of nucleolin by an siRNA specifically targeting nucleolin reversed As3+-induced stabilization of the GADD45alpha mRNA and elevation of the GADD45alpha protein.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 244 246 240 242 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 299 301 295 297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b30">30</xref>
###xml 302 304 298 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b41">41</xref>
###xml 384 386 376 378 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 485 487 477 479 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 533 541 521 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 626 627 614 615 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 628 629 616 617 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 685 694 669 678 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1B</xref>
###xml 788 790 768 770 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 853 854 829 830 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 855 856 831 832 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 895 904 871 880 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1D</xref>
###xml 959 961 935 937 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
A number of stress signals can induce accumulation of GADD45alpha mRNA or protein. Oxidative stress due to the generation of reactive oxygen species appears to be a common feature in cellular responses to a variety of stress signals, such as As3+- or inflammatory cytokine-induced stress responses (30,41). It is plausible, therefore, to assume that the induction of GADD45alpha by As3+ is mediated by oxidative stress. Indeed, pre-treatment of the cells with antioxidants prevented As3+-induced accumulation of GADD45alpha protein (Figure 1). However, administration of the cells with the exogenous reactive oxygen species, H2O2, only resulted in a marginal induction of GADD45alpha (Figure 1B). The reporter gene assay using GADD45alpha promoter and intron3 constructs indicated that As3+ regulated GADD45alpha promoter and intron3 activity, whereas H2O2 only exhibited its effect on intron3 (Figure 1D). Thus, these data provide evidence indicating that As3+-induced GADD45alpha is independent of oxidative stress.
###end p 48

###xml 323 325 315 317 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 479 488 467 476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1D</xref>
###xml 521 523 509 511 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 686 691 670 675 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 694 696 678 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b42">42</xref>
###xml 1012 1014 988 990 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1215 1217 1187 1189 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1283 1285 1255 1257 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 1468 1470 1440 1442 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
Transcriptional up-regulation appears to be the most important and common mechanism in genes encoding stress response proteins. The majority studies on the expression of GADD45alpha induced by a variety of stress signals focused on the transcription of the GADD45alpha gene. The data presented in this study suggest that As3+ has a very limited effect on the transcription of the GADD45alpha gene, as can be seen in both reporter gene activity analysis and nuclear run-on assay (Figure 1D and data not shown). However, As3+ appeared to be very capable of inducing a nucleolin-dependent stabilization of the GADD45alpha mRNA. These observations are compensatory to a recent study by Fan et al. (42) who showed an increased ratio of UVC-induced GADD45alpha mRNA transcript versus UVC-induced transcription in a nuclear run-on assay (Supplementary Table 2, row 938). The involvement of nucleolin in GADD45alpha mRNA stabilization was further verified by siRNA-mediated gene knockdown of nucleolin, which reversed As3+-induced stabilization of the GADD45alpha mRNA. Immunoblotting and immunofluorescent staining suggested that nucleolin was constitutively expressed in the cells used in the present studies. Although As3+ exhibited no influence on the expression of nucleolin protein, As3+ was able to induce intracellular re-distribution of nucleolin from nucleoli to nucleoplasm. This could be an indication in the functional up-regulation of nucleolin in response to As3+, which contributes to the stabilization of the GADD45alpha mRNA.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 235 237 227 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b43">43</xref>
###xml 238 240 230 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b45">45</xref>
###xml 465 467 449 451 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 469 478 453 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Figure 4C</xref>
###xml 733 735 713 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b46">46</xref>
###xml 870 872 850 852 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b47">47</xref>
###xml 1098 1100 1078 1080 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b31">31</xref>
###xml 1141 1143 1118 1120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b48">48</xref>
###xml 1152 1154 1129 1131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b49">49</xref>
###xml 1164 1166 1141 1143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b50">50</xref>
###xml 1179 1181 1156 1158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b51">51</xref>
###xml 1342 1344 1319 1321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b31">31</xref>
###xml 1481 1483 1454 1456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b52">52</xref>
###xml 1492 1500 1465 1473 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1563 1568 1536 1541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1571 1573 1544 1546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b53">53</xref>
###xml 1878 1880 1851 1853 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b54">54</xref>
###xml 2465 2467 2430 2432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b54">54</xref>
###xml 169 184 <span type="species:ncbi:10029">Chinese hamster</span>
###xml 200 205 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 793 798 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1431 1436 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1457 1462 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 2287 2292 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Several earlier reports suggested that UV, DNA-damaging agents, retinoid CD437 or glutamine deprivation induced GADD45alpha through stabilization of GADD45alpha mRNA in Chinese hamster ovary cells or human breast carcinoma cell lines (43-45). It was unclear, however, how the stability of GADD45alpha mRNA was regulated in these cells under such conditions. The findings that nucleolin and less potently, HuR, bind to GADD45alpha mRNA in the cellular response to As3+ (Figure 4C) provide a mechanistic explanation for the stress-induced accumulation of GADD45alpha. Nucleolin is a ubiquitous nucleolar phosphoprotein that consists of four RNA-binding domains that are responsible for the binding of this protein to pre-rRNA or mRNA (46). In addition, nucleolin has also been implicated as the human helicase IV that destabilizes helices of DNA-DNA, DNA-RNA and RNA-RNA (47). Accumulating evidence indicates that nucleolin is a key protein involved in the post-transcriptional regulation of mRNAs. Previous studies by other laboratories suggested that nucleolin was able to stabilize mRNAs of IL-2 (31), beta-amyloid precursor protein (APP) (48), bcl2 (49), renin (50) and CD154 (51). In response to T-cell activation, nucleolin stabilizes IL-2 mRNA by interacting with the 5'-untranslated region (UTR) of IL-2 mRNA in a JNK-dependent manner (31). Recently, we have demonstrated an oxidative stress-mediated binding of nucleolin to mouse GADD45alpha mRNA in mouse fibroblast cells (52). In an in vitro analysis for the selection of mRNA ligands by nucleolin, Yang et al. (53) demonstrated a binding of nucleolin to a number of other mRNAs, such as heat shock protein 90, glutathione peroxidase, peroxiredoxin 1, etc. Several lines of evidence indicate that nucleolin binds to pre-rRNA that contains a consensus sequence, (U/G)CCCG(A/G), in a loop of stem structure with 7-14 bp (54). Although the recognition elements of nucleolin in the 5'- or 3'-UTR of IL-2, APP, bcl2 and CD154 have been identified, no consensus sequence or homology sequence has been found in these mRNAs. Sequence comparison suggested that there is no sequence similarity among the 5'-UTRs of GADD45alpha mRNA and the mRNAs of IL-2, APP, bcl2 or CD154. However, it is interesting to note that both 5'- and 3'-UTR of human GADD45alpha mRNA contain a potential stem-loop with sequence, GCCCGG. This sequence matches completely with the nucleolin recognition element, (T/G)CCCG(A/G), in pre-rRNA (54).
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 142 144 142 144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b55">55</xref>
###xml 295 297 291 293 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 123 140 <span type="species:ncbi:11103">hepatitis C virus</span>
###xml 180 185 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Nucleolin has also been implicated in the cap-independent but internal ribosome entry site (IRES)-dependent translation of hepatitis C virus (55). Analysis of the 5'-UTR region of human GADD45alpha mRNA revealed a potential IRES domain proximal to the AUG code. We have recently observed that As3+ was also very potent in the induction of GADD45alpha protein in the growth-arrested cells where the general protein synthesis machinery was inhibited by rapamycin (data not shown). This phenomenon is very likely due to the IRES-dependent translational regulation. Whether nucleolin or other factors participated in this process remains to be investigated.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 103 105 99 101 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 203 205 195 197 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 551 553 531 533 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
In summary, our data suggest that elevation in the expression of GADD45alpha in cellular response to As3+ is mainly through the regulation of mRNA stability of GADD45alpha. Treatment of the cells with As3+ increased binding of nucleolin and to lesser extent, HuR to the mRNA of GADD45alpha, which extends the half-life of GADD45alpha mRNA. It is unknown at present how the association of nucleolin with the GADD45alpha mRNA is regulated, despite we noted a re-distribution of nucleolin protein from nucleoli to nucleoplasm in the cells treated with As3+. Changes in mRNA stability have been considered important mechanisms in which cells sense stress or damage in concert with transcriptional and/or other mechanisms. The contributions of GADD45alpha mRNA stabilization to the cell cycle regulation and apoptosis are currently under investigation.
