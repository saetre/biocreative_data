On the origin and evolution of biosynthetic pathways: integrating microarray data with structure and organization of the Common Pathway genes

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 154 171 154 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 237 241 237 241 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA</italic>
###xml 243 248 243 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL </italic>
###xml 252 256 252 256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lysC</italic>
###xml 415 419 415 419 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 539 542 539 542 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asd</italic>
###xml 564 572 564 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 154 170 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 564 571 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The lysine, threonine, and methionine biosynthetic pathways share the three initial enzymatic steps, which are referred to as the Common Pathway (CP). In Escherichia coli three different aspartokinases (AKI, AKII, AKIII, the products of thrA, metL and lysC, respectively) can perform the first step of the CP. Moreover, two of them (AKI and AKII) are bifunctional, carrying also homoserine dehydrogenasic activity (hom product). The second step of the CP is catalyzed by a single aspartate semialdehyde dehydrogenase (ASDH, the product of asd). Thus, in the CP of E. coli while a single copy of ASDH performs the same reaction for three different metabolic routes, three different AKs perfom a unique step. Why and how such a situation did emerge and maintain? How is it correlated to the different regulatory mechanisms acting on these genes? The aim of this work was to trace the evolutionary pathway leading to the extant scenario in proteobacteria.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 76 80 76 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 84 88 84 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 300 304 296 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 308 312 304 308 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 474 482 466 474 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Moreover</italic>
###xml 738 746 730 738 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 750 773 742 765 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 738 745 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 750 772 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
The analysis of the structure, organization, phylogeny, and distribution of ask and hom genes revealed that the presence of multiple copies of these genes and their fusion events are restricted to the gamma-subdivision of proteobacteria. This allowed us to depict a model to explain the evolution of ask and hom according to which the fused genes are the outcome of a cascade of gene duplication and fusion events that can be traced in the ancestor of gamma-proteobacteria. Moreover, the appearance of fused genes paralleled the assembly of operons of different sizes, suggesting a strong correlation between the structure and organization of these genes. A statistic analysis of microarray data retrieved from experiments carried out on E. coli and Pseudomonas aeruginosa was also performed.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 167 171 167 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 175 179 175 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
The integration of data concerning gene structure, organization, phylogeny, distribution, and microarray experiments allowed us to depict a model for the evolution of ask and hom genes in proteobacteria and to suggest a biological significance for the extant scenario.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 232 233 232 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 243 244 243 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 358 362 358 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 504 507 501 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asd</italic>
###xml 697 701 694 698 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dapA</italic>
###xml 761 764 758 761 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom</italic>
###xml 1272 1289 1269 1286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 1308 1309 1305 1306 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 1319 1320 1316 1317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 1272 1288 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
The metabolic routes leading to the synthesis of lysine\diaminopimelic acid, methionine and threonine\isoleucine are closely interconnected forming a complex system, three steps of which represent the so-called Common Pathway (CP) [1] (Figure 1). The first of them is the phosphorylation of aspartate, carried out by an aspartokinase (AK, the product of the ask gene) leading to beta-aspartyl-phosphate, which, in turn, is oxidised by an aspartate semialdehyde dehydrogenase (ASDH, the enzyme encoded by asd) to aspartate semialdehyde that, finally, may be transformed either into dihydrodipicolinate, the precursor of diaminopimelic acid and lysine, by dihydrodipicolinate synthase (coded for by dapA) or homoserine by homoserine dehydrogenase (HD, encoded by hom). Homoserine can be then channeled towards threonine and/or methionine biosyntheses. From an evolutionary point of view, the genes coding for these three enzymes are particularly interesting, since at least two different molecular mechanisms, i.e. paralogous gene duplication and gene fusion, appeared to have played a key role in their origin and evolution. In addition to this, in some bacteria each CP step is catalyzed by enzymes coded for by single monofunctional genes, whereas in the enterobacterium Escherichia coli it has been shown [2] (Figure 1) that:
###end p 9
###begin p 10
i) the first step of the CP can be performed by three different aspartokinases (AKI, AKII and AKIII);
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 69 73 69 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lysC</italic>
ii) the second step is catalyzed by a monofunctional ASDH encoded by lysC; and, lastly,
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 237 242 237 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA </italic>
###xml 246 250 246 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL</italic>
iii) the third step is carried out by two different homoserine dehydrogenases, referred to as HDI and HDII, which are fused to two of the three AKs: AKI and AKII, respectively. These two bifunctional proteins are coded for by two genes, thrA and metL, respectively.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 26 34 26 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 135 139 135 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA</italic>
###xml 235 240 235 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL </italic>
###xml 405 409 405 409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lysC</italic>
###xml 495 496 495 496 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 26 33 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The expression of the two E. coli bifunctional proteins are differently regulated: threonine and isoleucine regulate the expression of thrA, and threonine controls both enzymatic activities by a negative feedback. The transcription of metL is repressed by methionine but no feedback inhibition, by methionine itself, has been observed on this enzyme. Finally, the expression of the gene coding for AKIII (lysC) and the activity of its product, are regulated in response to lysine concentration [2].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 166 167 166 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 1049 1050 1049 1050 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 1404 1405 1404 1405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 1556 1557 1556 1557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1558 1559 1558 1559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1698 1699 1698 1699 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1861 1863 1861 1863 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 1998 1999 1998 1999 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 2100 2102 2100 2102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 2181 2183 2181 2183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 2323 2328 2323 2328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL </italic>
###xml 2332 2336 2332 2336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA</italic>
This particular structure pattern has raised the question of how and why it emerged in the course of evolution. On the basis of limited sequence data, Cassan et al. [3] proposed that the present-day bifunctional enzymes may have arisen from a fusion event involving the AK and the HD ancestral coding genes. The duplication of this bifunctional gene may have originated two redundant copies carrying both AK and HD activity. Another gene duplication event may have led to the formation of the three AK copies we observe nowadays. According to this model, the monofunctional AK could have emerged in two different ways: either by a partial gene duplication event involving only the AK activity coding region of the bifunctional genes, or by inactivation, as a result of accumulation of mutations, of the HD coding sequence. Thus, both paralogous gene duplication and gene fusion might have been responsible for shaping the CP. The importance of gene duplication in the course of evolution of genomes and metabolic pathways is well established, (see [4] and references therein): the production of two copies of a DNA sequences leads to an increase of genome size, and it also allows the rapid diversification of enzymatically catalyzed reactions, providing new material for the invention of new enzymatic properties and complex regulatory and developmental patterns. In addition to gene duplication, (see [4] and references therein), one of the major routes of gene evolution is the fusion of independent cistrons leading to bi- or multifunctional proteins [5-9]. Gene fusions provide a mechanism for the physical association of different catalytic domains or of catalytic and regulatory structures [5]. Fusions frequently involve genes coding for proteins functioning in a concerted manner, such as enzyme catalyzing sequential steps within a metabolic pathway [10]. Fusion of such catalytic centres likely promotes the channelling of intermediates that may be unstable and/or in low concentration [5]; this, in turn, requires that enzymes catalysing sequential reactions are colocalized within cell [11] and may (transiently) interact to form complexes that are termed metabolons [12]. The high fitness of gene fusions can also rely on the tight regulation of the expression of the fused domains. This might be the case of metL and thrA.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 598 602 598 602 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 606 610 606 610 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 976 979 976 979 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask</italic>
###xml 981 984 981 984 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom</italic>
###xml 990 994 990 994 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asd </italic>
###xml 1190 1191 1190 1191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 1192 1193 1192 1193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 1194 1195 1194 1195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
Thus, the CP might represent a very interesting model study to shed some light on the mechanisms driving the assembly of metabolic pathways and the refinement of regulatory networks. Nonetheless, in spite of the availability of several completely sequenced genomes and microarray data, neither a detailed analysis of the structure and organization of CP genes has been carried out nor any correlation of these data with expression (microarray) ones has been established until now. The aim of this work was to try to reconstruct the possible evolutionary and timing pathway(s) leading to the extant ask and hom genes, to analyse their phylogenetic distribution, to shed some light on the molecular mechanisms responsible for the assembly of the CP genes in bacteria and on the role that gene duplication(s), fusion(s) and clustering might have had in this context. To this purpose, the structure, organization and phylogenetic distribution of all the available proteobacterial ask, hom, and asd genes were analysed. Data obtained were integrated with expression data deriving from microarray analyses. We focused our attention on Proteobacteria for the following reasons: i) previous works [6,7,9] have shown that gene rearrangement events, such as gene duplication, fusion, and/or clustering have strongly influenced their evolution, ii) this phylogenetic branch includes the gamma-subdivision, that is thought to be one of the most recent branching point among Bacteria and iii) they represent a good case-study since comprise organisms living in very different habitats (going from the deep-sea hydrothermal environments of the epsilon-subdivision to the roots of plants in the case of some alpha-proteobacteria), and with very different lifestyles, including endosymbionts and parasites.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Results and discussion
###end title 16
###begin title 17
Structure and phylogenetic distribution of the genes coding for AK, ASDH and HD in Proteobacteria
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 31 39 31 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 206 208 206 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 445 446 445 446 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 638 642 638 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asd </italic>
###xml 676 677 676 677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 794 795 794 795 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 31 38 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The aminoacid sequences of the E. coli AK, ASDH, and HD sequences were used as a query to probe the protein database of completely sequenced proteobacterial genomes with the BLASTP option of BLAST program [13], in order to retrieve the most similar sequences. To this purpose 58 proteobacterial genomes were selected and, in most cases, only one strain for each species was taken into account. Data obtained are schematically reported in Figure 2, where a phylogenetic tree constructed using the RpoD sequences of the 58 proteobacteria is shown together with the number and the structure of all the retrieved AK, and HD coding genes. The asd genes were not included in Figure 2, since just one copy of this gene was retrieved from the 58 proteobacteria. The analysis of data reported in Figure 2 revealed that:
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 14 19 14 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;</italic>
###xml 22 26 18 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 32 45 25 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#948;\&#949;</italic>
a) in all the alpha-, beta- and delta\epsilon-proteobacterial genomes a single, monofunctional, stand-alone, copy of the gene coding for AK or HD was detected; moreover, neither duplicated copies nor fusion events involving these genes were detected.
###end p 19
###begin p 20
###xml 754 759 738 739 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;</italic>
###xml 762 766 742 743 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 772 785 749 752 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#948;\&#949;</italic>
b) multiple as well as fused copies of AK and HD were found only in gamma-proteobacteria, where the scenario is (apparently) more complex and intriguing. Indeed, a variable structure and copy-number of genes coding for AK (1 to 5) and HD (1 to 2) can be observed. Moreover, there is an apparent increasing complexity concerning these genes that is parallel to the evolutionay branching of gamma-proteobacteria, with enterobacteria and vibrionaceae showing the highest number of redundant and fused copies of AK and HD. This phylogenetic distribution strongly suggests that the duplication of AK coding genes and the fusion to HD apparently can be traced within gamma-proteobacteria or soon after the divergence of the gamma-proteobacterial ancestor from alpha-, beta- and delta\epsilon-proteobacteria.
###end p 20
###begin title 21
A model for the evolution of the AK and HD coding genes
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 242 243 242 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 315 318 315 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom</italic>
###xml 320 324 320 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 328 332 328 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asd </italic>
###xml 425 430 425 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;</italic>
###xml 433 437 429 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 443 456 436 439 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#948;\&#949;</italic>
###xml 705 709 677 681 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 864 871 836 843 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio </italic>
###xml 926 930 898 902 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 1009 1013 981 985 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 1175 1180 1147 1152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL </italic>
###xml 1184 1188 1156 1160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA</italic>
###xml 1330 1335 1298 1303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hisN </italic>
###xml 1339 1344 1307 1312 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hisB </italic>
###xml 1374 1375 1342 1343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 1376 1377 1344 1345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 1378 1379 1346 1347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1607 1612 1575 1580 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL </italic>
###xml 1616 1621 1584 1589 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA </italic>
###xml 1708 1709 1676 1677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
On the basis of the phylogenetic distribution of stand-alone and bifunctional genes of the CP we propose a possible, plausible evolutionary and timing model explaining the extant scenario. The model, which is schematically reported in Figure 3, predicts that the proteobacterial ancestor possessed a single copy of hom, ask and asd genes. During evolution, this organization was maintained in proteobacteria belonging to the alpha-, beta- and delta\epsilon-subdivisions. One of the cross-roads for the evolution of these genes is represented by the branching point between beta- and gamma-proteobacteria. It appears quite possible that, in the ancestor of gamma-proteobacteria, a first duplication of the ask gene may have taken place, generating two redundant copies that underwent an evolutionary divergence. The finding that no bacterium (with the exception of Vibrio strains, see below) shows two copies of monofunctional ask genes, strongly suggests that this duplication event and its further fusion to hom might have occurred in a relatively short evolutionary time, giving raise to an ancestral bifunctional gene, which might have retained the function of the extant metL and thrA. This sort of "gene duplication-gene fusion coupling" is quite similar to that described recently for the evolution of gamma-proteobacterial hisN and hisB histidine biosynthetic genes [6,7,9]. Finally, a paralogous duplication event of this bifunctional ancestor gene followed by evolutionary divergence (which very likely concerned with the regulatory mechanism, rather than the catalytic activity) led to the extant metL and thrA genes. On the basis of the phylogenetic distribution of the bifunctional genes (Figure 3), this "final" step might have occurred just before the separation between the "clusters" 1 and 2 of the gamma-proteobacterial subdivision.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 169 171 169 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 506 507 506 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 868 917 868 917 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">evolution and refinement of regulatory mechanisms</italic>
The biological significance of this cascade of duplication and fusion events might rely on the "patchwork" hypothesis on the origin and evolution of metabolic pathways [14]. According to this idea, metabolic pathways may have been assembled through the recruitment of primitive enzymes that could react with a wide range of chemically related substrates. Such relatively slow, unspecific enzymes may have been enabled primitive cells containing small genomes to overcome their limited coding capabilities [4]. Paralogous gene duplication event(s) followed by evolutionary divergence might have permitted the appearance of enzymes with an increase and narrow specificity and/or the diversification of function. In this way, an ancestral enzyme belonging to a given metabolic route, is "recruited" to serve a single or other (novel) pathways. Besides, it may permit the evolution and refinement of regulatory mechanisms coincident with the development of new pathways and/or the refinement of pre-existing ones.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 100 105 100 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL </italic>
###xml 109 114 109 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA </italic>
###xml 227 231 227 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 235 239 235 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
In our opinion, the evolutionary model proposed here to explain the origin and evolution the extant metL and thrA genes is in full agreement with the Jensen hypothesis and the cascade of gene duplications and fusions involving ask and hom genes might actually represent a mechanism for the refinement of the feedback regulation mechanisms controlling the activity of the enzymes they code for.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Phylogenetic analysis
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 388 390 388 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 477 478 477 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 483 484 483 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 522 523 522 523 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 885 886 860 861 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
If the evolutionary model proposed here is correct, one should expect that the fused copies of AK (AKI and AKII) and HD (HDI and HDII) share a degree of sequence similarity higher than that exhibited with AKIII and HD, respectively, and cluster together in a phylogenetic tree. In order to check this hypothesis, the AK and HD aminoacid sequences were aligned using the program ClustalW [15] and the multialignments obtained used to draw the phylogenetic trees shown in Figure 4 and 5. The analysis of the AK tree (Figure 4) showed that all the alpha-, beta- and delta\epsilon-proteobacterial sequences form a unique cluster separated from gamma-proteobacterial ones. Besides, the gamma-proteobacterial AKI, AKII, and AKIII sequences form three different and separated clusters with AKIII representing the root of the others. A similar situation can be observed in the HD tree (Figure 5): alpha-, beta- and delta\epsilon-proteobacterial HD sequences form a distinct unique cluster, while HDI and HDII form two close clusters.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 300 301 300 301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
The topology of the phylogenetic trees obtained fits well with the evolutionary model proposed and indicates that horizotal gene transfer of these genes rarely occurred and did not strongly influenced the evolution of AK and HD domanis. However, even though the evolutionary model reported in Figure 3 is in agreement with gene structure and phylogenetic analyses, the following exceptions have to be explained:
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 18 23 18 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lysC </italic>
###xml 27 32 27 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL </italic>
###xml 62 82 62 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Buchnera aphidicola </italic>
###xml 91 124 91 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Candidatus Blochmannia floridanus</italic>
###xml 126 152 126 152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Wigglesworthia glossinidia</italic>
###xml 161 183 161 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Haemophilus influenzae</italic>
###xml 222 231 222 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. ducrey</italic>
###xml 249 253 249 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 256 273 256 273 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Coxiella burnetii</italic>
###xml 275 295 275 295 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ricketsia prowazekii</italic>
###xml 297 347 297 347 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Wolbachia endosymbiont of Drosophila melanogaster </italic>
###xml 351 377 351 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus</italic>
###xml 62 81 <span type="species:ncbi:9">Buchnera aphidicola</span>
###xml 91 124 <span type="species:ncbi:203907">Candidatus Blochmannia floridanus</span>
###xml 126 152 <span type="species:ncbi:51229">Wigglesworthia glossinidia</span>
###xml 161 183 <span type="species:ncbi:727">Haemophilus influenzae</span>
###xml 256 273 <span type="species:ncbi:777">Coxiella burnetii</span>
###xml 323 333 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 297 346 <span type="species:ncbi:163164">Wolbachia endosymbiont of Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 351 377 <span type="species:ncbi:959">Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus</span>
1) The absence of lysC and metL in a group of enterobacteria (Buchnera aphidicola strains, Candidatus Blochmannia floridanus, Wigglesworthia glossinidia) and in Haemophilus influenzae, the absence of bifunctional genes in H. ducrey, and the lack of hom in Coxiella burnetii, Ricketsia prowazekii, Wolbachia endosymbiont of Drosophila melanogaster and Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus. This is very likely due to the absence of the corrensponding metabolic route(s), which, in turn, is correlated to the parasitic lifestyle of these proteobacteria. Such a lifestyle may allow the bacteria to acquire essential compounds directly from the metabolic activities of their host and the adaptation to this environmental condition might have caused the loss of entire metabolic routes or part thereof.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 36 43 36 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vibrio </italic>
2) The increase of the AK copies in Vibrio strains in respect to other gamma-proteobacteria is probably related to the high genomic rearrangement rate typical of these species.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 31 39 31 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask-hom </italic>
###xml 48 60 48 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 64 89 64 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Methylococcus capsulatus </italic>
###xml 245 250 241 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;</italic>
###xml 253 257 245 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 263 276 252 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#948;\&#949;</italic>
###xml 435 436 414 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 437 438 416 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 64 88 <span type="species:ncbi:414">Methylococcus capsulatus</span>
3) The absence of bifunctional ask-hom genes in Pseudomonas and Methylococcus capsulatus that, in spite of their taxonomical position within gamma-proteobacteria, exhibit the same structural and organization pattern of bacteria belonging to the alpha-, beta- and delta\epsilon-subdivisions. This is not an isolated example; in fact, the same situation has been recorded for other biosynthetic pathways, such as histidine biosynthesis [6,7]. The reason(s) of such structure and organization is still unclear.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 17 21 17 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 24 29 24 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lysA </italic>
###xml 32 48 32 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xanthomonadaceae</italic>
###xml 151 155 151 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 205 208 205 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom</italic>
###xml 268 272 268 272 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lysA</italic>
###xml 610 614 610 614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lysA</italic>
4) The fusion of ask to lysA in Xanthomonadaceae, which represents an exception to this general model. In these bacteria the paralogous duplication of ask gene originated two copies, one of which fused to hom, whereas the other one underwent another fusion event with lysA, a gene coding coding for DAPDC activity). The biological significance of the last fusion might rely in the spatial colocalization of the products of the two modules and a faster feedback inhibition of the first enzyme (AK) by the end product of the pathway (lysine), whose last biosynthetic step is catalyzed by the enzyme coded for by lysA.
###end p 31
###begin title 32
Analysis of gene organization
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 630 635 630 635 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA </italic>
###xml 639 643 639 643 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL</italic>
###xml 1541 1542 1537 1538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 1543 1544 1539 1540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 1545 1546 1541 1542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1647 1651 1643 1647 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 1655 1659 1651 1655 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 1810 1813 1806 1809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lys</italic>
###xml 1815 1819 1811 1815 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">met </italic>
###xml 1823 1827 1819 1823 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thr </italic>
If the model proposed and its biological significance is correct, i.e. that the duplication and fusion events, and the successive evolutionary divergence allowed the three copies of AKs and the two of HDs to narrow their specificity and to become increasingly more sensitive to specific regulatory signals, then it is plausible to assume that the ancestral copy of AK (AKIII) might serve different metabolic pathways and hence might have been under the control of multiple different regulatory signals (i.e. the availability of DAP, lysine, threonine, methionine etc). On the other hand, the expression of the bifunctional genes, thrA and metL, once they were channelled towards the biosynthesis of threonine and methionine, should have become increasingly more dependent on more specific signals (for example the concentration of the final product of that route). In general, it is plausible that once a "new" gene introgresses and becomes part of a pre-existing metabolic pathway, it will become co-regulated with the other genes belonging to the same metabolic pathway. In some cases, co-regulation of genes of the same biosynthetic route is achieved by organizing genes in operon structures, even though co-regulation may also be obtained by regulon construction. This is particularly true for fused genes; as reported in previous works, based on the analysis of the histidine biosynthetic pathway in gamma-proteobacteria, the appearance of fused genes (specific for a single pathway) is often parallel to their presence within operons [6,7,9]. This raises the question whether the structure and distribution of duplicated and fused copies of ask and hom genes might somehow be correlated to their organization in the proteobacterial genome. Therefore, we analysed the organization of all the genes of the lys, met and thr biosynthesis in all the 58 proteobacteria. Data obtained revealed that:
###end p 33
###begin p 34
1. Genes involved in the DAP\lysine biosynthesis are scattered throughout the chromosome(s) of all the 58 proteobacteria taken into account (data not shown).
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 18 21 18 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask</italic>
###xml 23 27 23 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asd </italic>
###xml 31 35 31 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 98 103 98 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrB </italic>
###xml 107 111 107 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrC</italic>
###xml 223 234 206 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bordetella </italic>
###xml 253 262 236 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom-thrC </italic>
###xml 278 279 261 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 433 438 412 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA </italic>
###xml 524 530 503 509 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrABC</italic>
2. In addition to ask, asd and hom genes, the other two genes involved in threonine biosynthesis (thrB and thrC) are scattered on the chromosome of bacteria belonging to alpha-, beta- and delta\epsilon subdivisions (except Bordetella strains that own a hom-thrC operon) (Figure 6). The gamma-proteobacterial scenario is completely different; according to the hypothesis mentioned above, in all of organisms possessing a bifunctional thrA gene, it is endowed within a three-cystronic operon, in the same relative gene order (thrABC), also suggesting that its construction should have been occurred once during evolution.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 105 109 105 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lys </italic>
###xml 112 116 112 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thr </italic>
###xml 180 183 163 166 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">all</underline>
###xml 188 192 171 175 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">met </italic>
###xml 254 255 237 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>
###xml 370 374 349 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL</italic>
###xml 400 405 379 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metB </italic>
###xml 427 433 406 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metLB </italic>
3. The organization of methionine biosynthetic genes in proteobacteria partly reflects that exhibited by lys or thr genes. In fact, in the alpha-, beta- and delta\epsilon branches all the met biosynthetic genes are scattered on the chromosome(s) (Figure 7). This organization is also shared by gamma-proteobacteria; the only exception is represented by the bifunctional metL, which is clustered with metB to form a bicistronic metLB operon.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 414 415 414 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 417 418 417 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 424 425 424 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
Thus, no bifunctional gene of the CP is located outside operons. Data obtained strongly suggest that the production of genes coding for enzymes specific of a single metabolic pathway coincides with their presence within a polycistronic transcriptional unit that includes all (or at least some of) the other genes of that route. Concerning the timing of the operons construction, the comparative analysis of Figure 2, 5, and 6 revealed that the "gene duplication-gene fusion coupling" occurring in gamma-proteobacteria appears to be coincident with gene clustering and the formation of operons of different length.
###end p 37
###begin title 38
Analysis of microarray experiments data
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 89 92 89 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lys</italic>
###xml 94 97 94 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">met</italic>
###xml 103 107 103 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thr </italic>
###xml 188 196 188 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 200 213 200 213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. aeruginosa</italic>
###xml 372 389 372 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">Additional File 1</xref>
###xml 188 195 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 200 213 <span type="species:ncbi:287">P. aeruginosa</span>
In order to elucidate the correlation existing between the structure and organization of lys, met, and thr genes and their expression within the cell, we analyzed the microarray data from E. coli and P. aeruginosa, which show two different arrays of structure and organization of CP genes. Microarray data were downloaded as supplemental material to published papers (see Additional File 1: Additional References for the Expression compendium); only normalized and filtered data were used. Values were transformed into base 2 logarithm of the ratio of the wild type (untreated) / mutant (treated) expression levels, if not yet in that form.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
For each of the three metabolic pathways we carried out a pairwise comparison of the expression pattern of each gene, by calculating the Pearson's correlation coefficient.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 37 38 37 38 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
Data obtained are reported in Figure 8, whose analysis revealed:
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 68 70 68 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8a</xref>
###xml 204 209 204 209 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL </italic>
###xml 213 218 213 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metB </italic>
###xml 287 291 287 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">met </italic>
1. A low co-regulation of the methionine biosynthetic genes (Figure 8a). Most of these genes are scarcely co-expressed, and they appeared to be expressed independently from each other. The fact that both metL and metB show very high correlation coefficient value in respect to the other met genes is in agreement with their operonic organization.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 13 28 13 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli thrABC </italic>
###xml 42 44 42 44 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8b</xref>
###xml 13 20 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
2. The three E. coli thrABC genes (Figure 8b) are highly co-expressed, with correlation coefficient > 0.84. This is in agreement with their organization in a compact operon.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 70 78 66 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 86 88 82 84 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8c</xref>
###xml 124 128 120 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lys </italic>
###xml 163 171 159 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 398 400 394 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 468 476 464 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">element </italic>
###xml 48 69 <span type="species:ncbi:86473">gamma-proteobacterium</span>
###xml 70 77 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 163 170 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
3. The trend of the lysine pathway genes in the gamma-proteobacterium E. coli (Figure 8c) is quite surprising; although the lys genes are scattered throughout the E. coli chromosome, they show a high degree of co-expression with correlation coefficient values often > 0.8. It is not clear how these genes can be highly co-expressed in the absence of an operonic organization. However, it is known [16] that lysine biosynthetic genes are regulated by the so-called LYS element (lysine-specific RNA element) located in their regulatory regions and able to repress or to allow their trascription in response to lysine concentration. The high coexpression pattern of lysine bosynthetic genes might be due to this mechanism.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 92 114 92 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</italic>
###xml 274 275 257 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
###xml 512 514 495 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8e</xref>
###xml 547 551 530 534 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thr </italic>
###xml 640 654 623 637 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 92 114 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
###xml 640 653 <span type="species:ncbi:287">P. aeruginosa</span>
The same analysis was carried out on lysine, methionine and threonine biosynthetic genes of Pseudomonas aeruginosa, whose structure and organization pattern is the same of the alpha-, beta-, and delta\epsilon subdivision of proteobacteria. Data obtained (reported in Figure 8) showed that, overall, there is a low degree of co-expression between genes belonging to the same pathway; this is particularly pronounced for methionine, where in some cases, the correlation coefficient assumes negative values (Figure 8e), and lysine genes, whereas the thr biosynthetic genes were more correlated between them. The low degree of co-expression of P. aeruginosa genes is in agreement with their scattering on the bacterial genome.

###begin title 46
Conclusion
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 254 258 254 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 262 266 262 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 531 533 531 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 534 535 534 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 554 557 554 557 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thr</italic>
###xml 559 563 559 563 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">met </italic>
###xml 567 571 567 571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lys </italic>
###xml 1114 1117 1114 1117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom</italic>
###xml 1119 1122 1119 1122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask</italic>
###xml 1128 1132 1128 1132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asd </italic>
###xml 1211 1215 1211 1215 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 1219 1223 1219 1223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
In this work a likely model for the evolution of the genes involved in the common pathway (CP) is depicted, which is based on the comparative analysis of data concerning the structure, phylogenetic distribution, organization, phylogeny and expression of ask and hom genes in proteobacteria. The analysis of the structure of the CP genes gave a strong support to the hypothesis that at least two different molecular mechanisms played an important role in shaping the pathway, that is paralogous gene duplication(s) and gene fusion [17,4]. The analysis of thr, met and lys gene organization in different proteobacteria revealed that several gene arrays exist within this phylogenetic lineage, with genes completely scattered throughout the genome, partially scattered/clustered, or strictly compacted. Even though different scenarios can be depicted for this different organization, i.e. the presence of scattered or clustered genes in the ancestor of proteobacteria, data reported in this work supported the first hypothesis. According to the model proposed, the ancestor of proteobacteria possessed monofunctional hom, ask, and asd genes scattered throughout the genome. The extant multiple and fused copies of ask and hom genes are the outcome of a cascade of paralogous gene duplication and fusion events, which led to the appearance of bifunctional enzymes catalyzing the same metabolic steps, but "sensing" different regulatory signals.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 151 153 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 438 439 438 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 440 441 440 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 518 522 518 522 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 526 530 526 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 604 608 604 608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
The evolutionary history of the CP genes gives another important support to the Jensen's hypothesis on the origin and evolution of metabolic pathways [14], strengthening the idea that gene duplication, gene fusion and recruitment of genes encoding enzyme with a broad range of substrate specificity played a crucial role in the assembly of biosynthetic pathways and in the appearance of new and/or more sophisticated regulatory networks [4,9]. Indeed, the biological significance of the presence of multiple copies of ask and hom genes might rely on the refinement of regulatory mechanisms allowing each ask copy to be regulated by specific signals, such as the availability of the end-product of the pathway.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 45 49 45 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask </italic>
###xml 58 62 58 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 261 270 261 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">a priori </italic>
###xml 646 649 646 649 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask</italic>
###xml 669 672 669 672 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom</italic>
The question of why the duplicated copies of ask fused to hom is rather intriguing. It is evident from their phylogenetic distribution that, once occurred, the fusion has been fixed; thus, it should have been evolutionary advantageous. Even though it cannot be a priori excluded, we do not favour the possibility that this fusion might permit the substrate tunnelling. It is possible that this gene fusion (and gene organization) resulted from both regulatory and metabolic constraints, for instance it might permit the spatial colocalization of their products and so a faster feedback inhibition of the first enzyme of the pathway, coded for by ask, by the product of hom.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 21 35 21 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA and metL </italic>
###xml 377 379 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 613 614 605 606 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 664 671 656 663 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrABC </italic>
###xml 675 681 667 673 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metLB </italic>
The existence of the thrA and metL gene fusions in the genome of gamma-proteobacteria is not an isolated example; additional gene fusions occurred in these genomes, such as those involving some histidine biosynthetic genes. It is worth of note that most of bifunctional proteins recognized to date are involved in metabolic pathways of the gamma-subdivision of proteobacteria [18]. Even though there is no apparent reason to think that these organisms are more prone to gene fusions than any others, it is interesting that these gene fusions appeared to be parallel to the increasing compactness of some operons [9] or to their construction, as in the case of the thrABC and metLB ones.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 80 85 80 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metL </italic>
###xml 89 94 89 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrA </italic>
###xml 212 215 212 215 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asd</italic>
Actually, the analysis of the organization of these genes revealed that all the metL and thrA genes are embedded within (compact) operons, whereas their monofunctional counterparts as well as the second CP gene, asd, are located outside gene clusters. This is not so surprising if we agree on the existence of unspecific enzymes that might serve different metabolic pathways. Indeed, it is plausible that the expression of a gene, whose product catalyses a chemical reaction leading to a product involved in different metabolic pathways should be constitutively expressed or controlled by multiple mechanisms rather than being controlled by mechanisms specific for a single route.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
This is also in agreement with expression data retrieved from the available microarray data; in fact, the greater the scattering of genes belonging to the same pathway, the lower the degree of correlation between them.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 44 51 44 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thrABC </italic>
###xml 55 61 55 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">metLB </italic>
###xml 224 227 220 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ask</italic>
###xml 228 232 224 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hom </italic>
###xml 404 405 400 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 555 561 551 557 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fused </italic>
If our model is correct, the building up of thrABC and metLB operons represents a recent invention of evolution (dated in the gamma proteobacterial ancestor) and is apparently co-incident with the appearance of bifunctional ask-hom genes. The origin and evolution of operons is still under debate, and at least six different classes of models have been proposed to explain the existence of operons (see [9] and references therein); although different forces might have driven the assembly of operons, in our opinion the major ones were those enabling the fused genes to be coregulated finely and the protein coded for synthesized in the correct stoichiometric ratio.
###end p 53
###begin title 54
Material and methods
###end title 54
###begin title 55
Sequence retrieval
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 66 68 66 68 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
Amino acid sequences were retrieved from GenBank database. BLAST [13] probing of database was performed with the BLASTP option of this program using default parameters. Only those sequences retrieved at an E-value below the 0.05 threshold were taken into account.
###end p 56
