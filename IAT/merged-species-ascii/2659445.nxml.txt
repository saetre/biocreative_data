Thymosin beta4 and beta10 Levels in Pre-Term Newborn Oral Cavity and Foetal Salivary Glands Evidence a Switch of Secretion during Foetal Development
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: SN IM TC RI GV CR AR GF MC. Performed the experiments: SN TC BM RI CF CT DF EDF. Analyzed the data: SN IM TC BM RI CF GV CT AR DF EDF GF MC. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: IM GV CR GF MC. Wrote the paper: SN IM TC GV CR AR GF MC.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 13 14 10 11 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 48 50 42 44 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 84 89 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Thymosin beta4, its sulfoxide, and thymosin beta10 were detected in whole saliva of human pre-term newborns by reversed-phase high performance chromatography coupled to electrospray ion-trap mass spectrometry.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 319 320 313 314 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 361 367 351 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
###xml 435 436 418 419 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 454 456 434 436 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 590 591 567 568 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 1019 1026 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">infants</span>
Despite high inter-individual variability, concentration of beta-thymosins increases with an inversely proportional trend to postmenstrual age (PMA: gestational age plus chronological age after birth) reaching a value more than twenty times higher than in adult whole saliva at 190 days (27 weeks) of PMA (thymosin beta4 concentration: more than 2.0 micromol/L versus 0.1 micromol/L). On the other hand, the ratio between thymosin beta4 and thymosin beta10 exhibits a constant value of about 4 along all the range of PMA (190-550 days of PMA) examined. In order to investigate thymosin beta4 origin and to better establish the trend of its production as a function of gestational age (GA), immunohistochemical analysis of major and minor salivary glands of different pre-term fetuses were carried out, starting from 84 days (12 weeks) of gestational age. Reactive granules were seen in all glands with a maximum of expression around 140-150 days of GA, even though with high inter- and intra-individual variability. In infants and adults reactive granules in acinar cells were not observed, but just a diffuse cytoplasmatic staining in ductal cells.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
This study outlines for the first time that salivary glands during foetal life express and secrete peptides such as beta-thymosins probably involved in the development of the oral cavity and its annexes. The secretion increases from about 12 weeks till to about 21 weeks of GA, subsequently it decreases, almost disappearing in the period of expected date of delivery, when the gland switches towards the secretion of adult specific salivary peptides. The switch observed may be an example of further secretion switches involving other exocrine and endocrine glands during foetal development.
###end p 7

###xml 1883 1884 1837 1838 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>

###xml 2357 2358 2305 2306 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 489 492 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
###xml 494 497 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 499 502 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 504 509 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 514 520 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 540 545 <span type="species:ncbi:9940">sheep</span>
###xml 547 551 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 556 559 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>

###xml 1327 1332 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 2159 2164 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Beta thymosins are ubiquitous peptides having interesting intra- and extra-cellular functions, whose name derives from their first characterization from thymus extracts [1], [2]. Thymosin beta4 (Tbeta4) is usually the most abundant beta-thymosin in the cytoplasm of most cell types and it plays pivotal roles in the cell cytoskeletal system as G-actin sequestring peptide [3]. Tbeta4 frequently co-exists with a second member of the beta-thymosin family, i.e. thymosin beta10 (Tbeta10) in man, cat, rat, mouse and rabbit, thymosin beta9 in sheep, calf and pig [4]. Although the secretion pathway is not fully understood, recent studies highlighted various extra-cellular roles for these peptides [1]. For instance, Tbeta4 modifies the rate of attachment and spreading of endothelial cells on matrix components inducing matrix metalloproteinases [5], it stimulates the migration of human umbilical vein endothelial cells [6], it is involved in the repair of damaged cornea [7], it induces hair growth by activating hair follicle stem cells [8], and it might be involved in the development and repair of heart [9] and brain damages [10]. Whereas Tbeta4 is a potent enhancer of angiogenesis, Tbeta10 inhibits it and changes of the two peptides ratio can exert either positive or negative control [11]. Tbeta4 has been detected in human whole saliva and tears by immunological techniques [12] and recent studies of our group evidenced that in the oral cavity a main contribution derives from crevicular fluid [13], where, as demonstrated by Reti and co-workers [14], Tbeta4 plays an important role in suppressing the production of interleukin-8 following stimulation by tumour-necrosis factor alpha and it acts on the whole as antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory and anti-apoptotic peptide on gingival fibroblasts. This study describes the detection of high concentrations of thymosins beta4 and beta10 in the whole saliva of pre-term newborns, during a survey carried out by proteomic approaches. Because a crevicular origin for these peptides in newborns has to be obviously excluded, further immunohistochemical analyses of the major and minor salivary glands of human fetuses and newborns of different postmenstrual age (PMA: gestational age (GA) plus chronological age after birth) were performed, in order to establish the source of the high amounts of Tbeta4 detected in whole saliva and to add further information on potential roles of this peptide during foetal development.

Materials and Methods

###begin title 11

The study protocol and written consent forms were approved by the Pediatric Department Ethics Committee of the Faculty of Medicine of the Catholic University of Rome (according to the instructions of the Declaration of Helsinki). Full written consent forms were obtained from the parents of the newborns and all rules were respected.

###begin title 13
Reagents
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 214 215 211 212 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 233 235 227 229 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
All common chemicals and reagents were of analytical grade and were purchased from Farmitalia-Carlo Erba, (Milan, Italy), Merck (Damstadt, Germany) and Sigma Aldrich (St. Louis, MI, USA). Standards of thymosin beta4 and thymosin beta10 were purchased from Bachem (Bubendorf, Switzerland).

###begin title 15
Apparatus
###end title 15
###begin p 16
The HPLC-ESI-IT-MS apparatus was a Surveyor HPLC system (ThermoFinnigan, San Jose, CA, USA) connected by a T splitter to a PDA diode-array detector and to an LCQ Deca XP Plus mass spectrometer. The mass spectrometer was equipped with an electrospray ion (ESI) source. The chromatographic column was a Vydac (Hesperia, CA, USA) C8 column, with 5 microm particle diameter (column dimensions 150x2.1 mm).
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Subjects enrolled
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 415 419 415 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005109-Inzitari2">[15]</xref>
###xml 207 214 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Infants</span>
Twelve newborns with a birth weight ranging between 500 g and 1250 g and gestational age between 27-31 weeks (193-217 days), admitted to the Neonatal Intensive Care Unit (NICU) were enrolled for this study. Infants with major congenital malformations or prenatal infections were excluded. Relevant clinical information and major clinical outcomes with regard to the study group are provided in our previous studies [15].
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 20 27 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">infants</span>
Moreover, 4 at-term infants (2 females, 2 males) born after uncomplicated pregnancies and vaginal delivery and admitted to Policlinico "A. Gemelli" nursery, were studied. Their mean+/-SD GA and birth weight were 272+/-7 days (38+/-1 weeks) and 3280+/-150 grams, respectively. All these 4 newborns had no clinical problems and therefore they were discharged after three days and breast-fed.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Sample collection and treatment
###end title 20
###begin p 21
Whole saliva samples were collected from pre-term and at-term newborns with a very soft plastic aspirator during the usual newborn management. Pre-term newborn sample collection was performed during several weeks after birth at established time intervals (one or two weeks). When possible, it was also performed after discharge from the neonatal unit, during the periodical check visits with about one year follow-up. Saliva was not collected from newborns when sample collection seemed to cause even a minimal stress to the newborn and/or to his/her parents. For these reasons, sample collection was more frequent during the hospital stay and sporadic after this period. After collection samples were immediately mixed with an equal volume of 0.2% 2,2,2 trifluoacetic acid (TFA) (v/v) in ice bath. After stirring, the acidic solution was centrifuged at 9000 g for three min to remove the precipitate (mainly mucins) and the acidic solution was immediately analyzed by HPLC-ESI-MS (100 microL, corresponding to 50 microL of whole saliva) or stored at -80degreesC until the analysis.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
RP-HPLC-ESI-MS analysis
###end title 22
###begin p 23
The following solutions were utilized for the chromatographic separation: (eluent A) 0.056% aqueous TFA and (eluent B) 0.050% TFA in acetonitrile-water 80/20 (v/v). The gradient applied was linear from 0 to 55% in 40 min, at a flow rate of 0.30 mL/min. The T splitter addressed a flow-rate of about 0.20 mL/min towards the diode array detector and 0.10 mL/min towards the ESI source. During the first 5 min of separation the eluate was not addressed to the mass spectrometer to avoid instrument damage due to the high salt concentration. The diode array detector was set at a wavelength of 214 and 276 nm. Mass spectra were collected every 3 millisecond in the positive ion mode. MS spray voltage was 4.50 kV and the capillary temperature was 220degreesC.
###end p 23

Deconvolution of averaged ESI mass spectra was automatically performed either by the software provided with the Deca-XP instrument (Bioworks Browser) or by MagTran 1.0 [16] software. Experimental Mass values were compared with average theoretical values available at the Swiss-Prot data bank (), where Tbeta4 and Tbeta10 have the P62328 and P63313 codes, respectively. Identification of thymosin beta4 and thymosin beta10 was based on the coincidence of elution times, ESI-MS spectra and ESI-MS/MS spectra with peptide standards, as described in previous studies [13]. Thymosin beta4 sulfoxide was identified by the coincidence of elution times, ESI-MS and ESI-MS/MS spectra with a standard obtained by treating thymosin beta4 with H2O2. Under the chromatographic conditions used the elution times measured at the peak maximum were: Tbeta4, 20.0 (+/-0.4) min; Tbeta4 sulfoxide, 18.5 (+/-0.4) min; and Tbeta10, 21.3 (+/-0.4) min. The average masses obtained after deconvolution of the ESI spectra were: Tbeta4 4963.5+/-0.4, Tbeta4 sulfoxide 4979.5+/-0.4, Tbeta10 4936.5+/-0.4 Da.

The concentration of beta-thymosins was determined by considering the extracted ion current (XIC) peak area, which is linearly related to the peptide absolute quantity, as established performing LC-MS analyses on beta-thymosin standards. The linear correlation between absolute amount of the standards and XIC peak area (r = 0.999) provided the following relationship: 1 picomol = 2.1x106 (XIC peak area) for Tbeta4, Tbeta10 and Tbeta4 sulfoxide. The concentration was therefore computed by considering the absolute quantity and injected sample volume (usually 100 microL of acidic solution corresponding to 50 microL of WS). The XIC peaks were revealed by selecting the following charged ions: Tbeta4, [M+5H]5+ = 993.8 m/z; [M+4H]4+ = 1241.9 m/z; [M+3H]3+ = 1655.5 m/z; Tbeta4 sulfoxide [M+5H]5+ = 996.9 m/z; [M+4H]4+ = 1245.9 m/z; [M+3H]3+ = 1660.8 m/z; Tbeta10, [M+5H]5+ = 988.3 m/z; [M+4H]4+ = 1235.1 m/z; [M+3H]3+ = 1646.5 m/z. The window for all these values was +/-0.5 m/z. The percentage error of the measurements was less than 10%.

###xml 1320 1326 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 1370 1381 <span type="species:ncbi:3704">horseradish</span>
Samples of the parotid, submandibular and minor salivary glands (the posterior superficial lingual glands or Weber) at the base of the tongue were obtained at autopsy from 5 subjects: two foetuses of 12 and 13 weeks of gestational age, respectively, and 3 pre-term infants, with a gestational age of 20, 21 and 31 weeks. In addition, two specimens of the major sublingual gland, the only two that we were able to obtain with certainty from this gland, belonging to the pre-term infants with 21 and 31 weeks of gestational age, were studied. Moreover, specimens of only submandibular and sublingual glands were obtained at the autopsy from a sixth subject, an 18 months old boy who underwent a sudden death. As a control group we utilized surrounding tissue of surgical specimens from the peritumoral parotid gland of 5 adult patients who underwent surgery for resection of pleomorphic adenoma. Tissue samples were formalin-fixed, paraffin embedded and routinely processed. Deparaffinized 5 micro-thick sections were immunostained with the antibody directed against Tbeta4 (rabbit) at a dilution of 1:500. The polyclonal antibody from Bachem-Peninsula Lab (San Carlos, CA, USA) has not cross reactivity against Tbeta10 and Talpha1. The staining procedure was ensured by using a biotin-labeled affinity isolated goat anti-rabbit IgG followed by streptavidin conjugated to horseradish peroxidase (Dako, Cytomation LS-AB2 System - HRP, Denmark). Sections of reactive lymph nodes rich in macrophages (positive controls) and lymphocytes (negative controls) were also used.

###xml 96 102 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">infant</span>
Figure 1 shows the concentration of Tbeta4 and Tbeta10 detected in whole saliva of one pre-term infant plotted as a function of postmenstrual age, for example. Both thymosins beta4 and beta10 show high concentration values immediately after birth at 26-27 weeks (193 days of GA, in this subject Tbeta4 concentration in the range 1.0-1.5 micromol/L) which constantly decrease as a function of PMA, reaching values comparable to that ones of adult (Tbeta4 concentration about 0.1 micromol/L [13]) after 38 weeks (270 days) of PMA. Similar age-dependent trends were observed for all the investigated pre-term newborns despite the high inter-individual variability, as evident from Fig 2 (12 pre-term newborns, 78 measurements at different days of PMA). In the majority of subjects the concentration of Tbeta4 measured in the range 27-31 weeks (190-210 days) of PMA was 1-2 micromol/L, but one subject showed a spike to about 6 micromol/L. The concentration of Tbeta10 in the same range of PMA was between 0.1-0.4 micromol/L, with one subject with a spike of 0.6 micromol/L (but not the same showing theTbeta4 spike). In Fig. 2 are also reported (squares) the mean values observed in four at-term newborns (270 days of gestational age, 38 weeks) which values for Tbeta4 were 0.21+/-0.12 micromol/L and 0.049+/-0.022 micromol/L for Tbeta10. Fig. 2 also shows the ratio between the two beta-thymosins concentrations in the different samples under analysis, which has a constant value of about 4 in all the range of PMA studied (at-term newborns comprised), except sporadic increases in some pre-term newborns not connected to any evident clinical outcome. Obviously, the constant ratio implies that the concentration of the two peptides correlates with high statistical significance (N = 78, r = 0.679, p<0.001).

Similarly to the spikes of Tbeta4/Tbeta10 concentration ratio, no clinical outcome can be associated until now to the presence of Tbeta4 sulfoxide sporadically detected in whole saliva of some pre-term newborns as shown in Fig. 3 (27 out of 78 measurements). The oxidation of Tbeta4 methionine provides a sensible decrease of the interaction with G-actin [17]. Some subjects did not show the presence of Tbeta4 sulfoxide in any sample of whole saliva collected at different days of PMA, others showed its presence immediately after birth in the period 190-230 days of PMA, others showed its presence two or three weeks after birth corresponding to 230-260 days of PMA, while Tbeta4 sulfoxide was almost completely absent in whole saliva of newborns after 270 days of PMA and it was detected only in two out of four at-term newborns, with a mean value of 0.042 micromol/L (square of Fig. 3). ###begin title 40
Histology and immunohistochemistry
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 26 27 23 24 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 159 167 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Immunoreactivity for Tbeta4 was detected in all cases tested in this study, both in major and minor salivary glands obtained from foetuses, newborns and adult patients. We observed marked inter-individual differences both in the degree of reactivity and in the immunohistochemical pattern. Moreover, a striking variability in the expression and in the localization of the peptide was observed among the different salivary glands tested, even in the same subject. As for morphological observations, the salivary gland structure before birth showed different degrees of immaturity, typical of differentiation from tubular structures towards acinar and ductal cells.

###begin title 63
Discussion
###end title 63
###begin p 64
###xml 37 38 34 35 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 48 50 42 44 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 82 89 76 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005109-g001">Figs. 1</xref>
###xml 94 95 88 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005109-g002">2</xref>
###xml 484 485 475 476 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 682 683 670 671 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 773 774 758 759 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
Trends in the concentrations of Tbeta4 and Tbeta10 observed as a function of PMA (Figs. 1 and 2) lead us to suppose higher values in oral cavity before 190 days (27 weeks) of GA. On the obvious impossibility to extend the HPLC-MS analysis of whole saliva at lower GA ranges and with the aim at finding the sources responsible for the high concentration observed in the oral cavity of pre-term newborns, we addressed our experimental plan towards immunohistochemical detection of Tbeta4. This plan was carried out utilizing salivary glands obtained by autopsy of fetuses and newborns of different gestational ages. This approach allowed to extend the detection of the potential Tbeta4 sources till 90 days (12 weeks) of GA and to obtain information on the evolution of Tbeta4 production and secretion during fetal growth.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 322 323 319 320 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 538 542 535 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005109-Inzitari1">[13]</xref>
The variability observed can partly derive from inter- and intra-individual variability of salivary flow rate. The measurement of this parameter was not possible in pre-term and at-term newborns for ethical reasons. However, on the whole, HPLC-MS and immunohistochemical data clearly indicate glandular expression of Tbeta4 in fetuses inversely related to PMA with an apex of expression at about 140/150 days (20/21 weeks) of GA, differently from what observed in adults, where oral thymosins mainly derive from gingival crevicular fluid [13].
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 70 72 67 69 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 192 193 186 187 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 315 317 306 308 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 355 356 343 344 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 400 401 385 386 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 411 413 393 395 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 483 487 465 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005109-Smart2">[11]</xref>
###xml 553 559 535 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005109-g002">Fig. 2</xref>
The actual un-availability of specific antibodies raised against Tbeta10 (to the best of our knowledge) did not allow us to compare the expression of this peptide with respect to that of Tbeta4. However, the increase in concentration of both determined by HPLC/MS analysis of oral fluid, suggested a trend for Tbeta10 similar to that one observed of Tbeta4 by immunochemistry. Some roles of the Tbeta4 and Tbeta10 couple are linked to a subtle modification of their relative amounts [11]. However, the change of concentration at constant peptide ratio (Fig. 2) observed in the present study suggests that the absolute thymosin quantities should play relevant functions during fetal development.
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 88 89 85 86 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 220 221 214 215 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 408 415 402 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005109-g009">Figs. 9</xref>
###xml 420 422 414 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005109-g010">10</xref>
###xml 560 561 551 552 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 748 763 739 754 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005109-g006">Figs. 6a and 6b</xref>
###xml 796 797 784 785 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 1157 1161 1145 1149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005109-Loureno2">[19]</xref>
###xml 388 395 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">infants</span>
The major outcomes of morphological results suggest that immunochemical pattern of Tbeta4 is dynamic and that it changes during the different stages of pre-natal and post-natal development. Particularly, a granular Tbeta4 pattern predominates in the early and middle phases of fetal development, progressively substituted by a structural diffuse cytoplasmic staining as seen in full term infants and adults (Figs. 9 and 10). The granular pattern comprises both exocrine and paracrine features, the former were represented, at immunoistochemical level, by Tbeta4-reactive granules inside the tubular lumen and in the apical cytoplasm of the acinar cells; the latter was represented by large peptide amounts in the mesenchymal space, as evident from Figs. 6a and 6b that may suggest a role of Tbeta4 in the control of salivary gland branching morphogenesis. On the other hand, the pattern of thymosin we observed in histochemical preparation reflects the glandular differentiation and namely the start of acinar cell differentiation. Such a change is reflected even in development of tight junctions as demonstrated by immunohistochemical studies of claudins [19].
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 46 47 43 44 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 56 58 50 52 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 170 177 164 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005109-g006">Figs. 6</xref>
###xml 178 182 172 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005109-g007">7a&#8211;d</xref>
###xml 240 250 234 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005109-g004">Figs. 4a&#8211;c</xref>
###xml 706 707 697 698 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 716 718 704 706 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 1046 1050 1034 1038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005109-Inzitari2">[15]</xref>
###xml 1338 1339 1323 1324 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 1632 1633 1614 1615 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 1643 1645 1622 1624 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 2082 2083 2058 2059 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 2093 2095 2066 2068 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 90 97 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">infants</span>
The presence of high amounts of thymosins beta4 and beta10 in the oral cavity of pre-term infants with a probable maximum of expression around 140-150 days (20-21 weeks, Figs. 6,7a-d), starting at least from about 85 days (12 weeks) of GA (Figs. 4a-c), put forward a role for these peptides in the development of oral cavity and its annexes. Until now the salivary glands were believed organs of slow development, reaching a complete secretory maturation after birth. Surprisingly, our results suggest that salivary glands do possess specific secretory capability even during prenatal maturation stages. In fact, at least since 85 days (12 weeks) of GA they start secreting peptides, such as thymosins beta4 and beta10, which should have specific roles during development. After an apex reached at around 150 days (21 weeks) of GA, production of these peptides decreases rapidly and the secretion of salivary glands slowly switches towards the expression and secretion of salivary peptides typical of adult age (i.e. acidic proline-rich proteins [15], statherin, histatins, basic proline-rich proteins and salivary cystatins). The switch of secretion disclosed by HPLC-MS analysis of whole saliva, was confirmed by immunoistochemical analysis of developing salivary glands, which evidenced a progressive decrease in the secretion of Tbeta4 starting from the middle phases of foetal development. The switch could involve other exocrine and endocrine glands during foetal development. This study offers only a partial description of this switching phenomenon and obviously it does not pretend to establish any role for this high Tbeta4 and Tbeta10 salivary gland production during a particular period of foetal life, even though the immunohistochemical results are suggestive for a contemporaneous paracrine and exocrine functions. The exocrine pathway arises puzzling questions about the environment where the peptides are potentially released. In fact, salivary glands discharge during foetal life directly in amniotic fluid, where thymosins may be dispersed. The secretion of Tbeta4 and Tbeta10 from salivary gland might be connected to a demand of the whole amniotic sac. On the other hand, fluid secreted by salivary glands may be ingested and addressed towards the foetal gastrointestinal and respiratory tracts putatively influencing their pre-natal development. Alternatively, potential interactions with specific mucins could maintain these peptides adherent to the epithelium of the oral cavity.
