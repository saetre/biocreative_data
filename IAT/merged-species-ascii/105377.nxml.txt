###begin article-title 0
Domain specific interaction in the XRCC1-DNA polymerase beta complex

###xml 636 637 627 628 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;</bold>

###xml 1940 1945 1895 1900 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;183</sub>

###xml 2033 2034 1985 1986 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sub>
###xml 2146 2151 2094 2099 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;159</sub>
###xml 1344 1349 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
XRCC1 (X-ray cross-complementing group 1) is a DNA repair protein that forms complexes with DNA polymerase beta (beta-Pol), DNA ligase III and poly-ADP-ribose polymerase in the repair of DNA single strand breaks. The domains in XRCC1 have been determined, and characterization of the domain-domain interaction in the XRCC1-beta-Pol complex has provided information on the specificity and mechanism of binding. The domain structure of XRCC1, determined using limited proteolysis, was found to include an N-terminal domain (NTD), a central BRCT-I (breast cancer susceptibility protein-1) domain and a C-terminal BRCT-II domain. The BRCT-I-linker-BRCT-II C-terminal fragment and the linker-BRCT-II C-terminal fragment were relatively stable to proteolysis suggestive of a non-random conformation of the linker. A predicted inner domain was found not to be stable to proteolysis. Using cross-linking experiments, XRCC1 was found to bind intact beta-Pol and the beta-Pol 31 kDa domain. The XRCC1-NTD1-183 (residues 1-183) was found to bind beta-Pol, the beta-Pol 31 kDa domain and the beta-Pol C-terminal palm-thumb (residues 140-335), and the interaction was further localized to XRCC1-NTD1-157 (residues 1-157). The XRCC1-NTD1-183-beta-Pol 31 kDa domain complex was stable at high salt (1 M NaCl) indicative of a hydrophobic contribution. Using a yeast two-hybrid screen, polypeptides expressed from two XRCC1 constructs, which included residues 36-355 and residues 1-159, were found to interact with beta-Pol, the beta-Pol 31 kDa domain, and the beta-Pol C-terminal thumb-only domain polypeptides expressed from the respective beta-Pol constructs. Neither the XRCC1-NTD1-159, nor the XRCC136-355 polypeptide was found to interact with a beta-Pol thumbless polypeptide. A third XRCC1 polypeptide (residues 75-212) showed no interaction with beta-Pol. In quantitative gel filtration and analytical ultracentrifugation experiments, the XRCC1-NTD1-183 was found to bind beta-Pol and its 31 kDa domain in a 1:1 complex with high affinity (Kd of 0.4-2.4 microM). The combined results indicate a thumb-domain specific 1:1 interaction between the XRCC1-NTD1-159 and beta-Pol that is of an affinity comparable to other binding interactions involving beta-Pol.

###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 185 186 185 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c1">1</xref>
###xml 302 303 302 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c1">1</xref>
###xml 498 499 498 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c1">1</xref>
###xml 675 676 669 670 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c2">2</xref>
###xml 677 678 671 672 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c3">3</xref>
###xml 353 358 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 505 520 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
XRCC1 (X-ray cross-complementing group 1) is a DNA repair protein that in mammals is required for efficient repair of DNA damage caused by ionizing radiation or DNA methylating agents (1). In cells lacking XRCC1 activity, aberrant levels of sister chromatid exchange have been observed during mitosis (1). Transfection of a vector carrying the gene for human XRCC1 into hamster cell lines deficient in XRCC1 activity results in complementation and restoration of normal DNA damage repair function (1). In transgenic mice, XRCC1(-/-) knockouts or DNA polymerase beta (beta-Pol)(-/-) knockouts are embryonic lethal, indicating an essential role for these interacting proteins (2,3).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 90 91 90 91 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c4">4</xref>
###xml 282 283 279 280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c5">5</xref>
###xml 284 285 281 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c7">7</xref>
###xml 328 335 325 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 421 422 418 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c8">8</xref>
###xml 472 477 469 474 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;183</sub>
###xml 504 505 498 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c5">5</xref>
###xml 506 507 500 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c6">6</xref>
###xml 508 509 502 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c9">9</xref>
###xml 539 544 533 538 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;183</sub>
###xml 598 599 592 593 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c9">9</xref>
XRCC1 has been considered a component of base excision repair (BER) (recently reviewed in 4). XRCC1 mediates DNA repair by forming DNA repair complexes consisting of XRCC1, beta-Pol, poly-ADP-ribose polymerase (PARP, preferentially in the ADP ribosylated form), and DNA ligase III (5-7). XRCC1 and DNA ligase III are associated in vivo, and expression of active DNA ligase III requires the co-expression of native XRCC1 (8). The N-terminal domain (NTD) of XRCC1 (XRCC1-NTD1-183) interacts with beta-Pol (5,6,9). Additionally, the XRCC1-NTD1-183 interacts with DNA containing a single-strand break (9).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 175 177 172 174 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c10">10</xref>
###xml 178 180 175 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c11">11</xref>
###xml 245 247 242 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c12">12</xref>
###xml 248 250 245 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c13">13</xref>
###xml 578 579 575 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c6">6</xref>
###xml 682 683 679 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c5">5</xref>
###xml 684 685 681 682 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c6">6</xref>
###xml 686 688 683 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c14">14</xref>
###xml 761 766 758 763 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">xrcc1</italic>
###xml 854 855 851 852 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c5">5</xref>
###xml 922 924 919 921 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c15">15</xref>
###xml 1011 1013 1005 1007 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c16">16</xref>
###xml 1014 1016 1008 1010 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c18">18</xref>
###xml 1068 1070 1062 1064 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c19">19</xref>
###xml 1207 1209 1198 1200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c19">19</xref>
###xml 1256 1258 1247 1249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c19">19</xref>
###xml 1259 1261 1250 1252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c20">20</xref>
###xml 1316 1321 1307 1312 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;183</sub>
###xml 1396 1397 1387 1388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c9">9</xref>
###xml 1488 1493 1476 1481 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;183</sub>
###xml 1668 1669 1644 1645 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c9">9</xref>
###xml 774 788 <span type="species:ncbi:10029">CHO cell lines</span>
The BER pathway consists of steps that include excision of the abasic site nucleotide by the deoxyribose-5-phosphate (dRP) lyase activity that is carried by the beta-Pol NTD (10,11), single nucleotide gap-filling DNA synthesis and DNA ligation (12,13). Many thousands of abasic sites are generated in cells each day by either purine hydrolysis or by a group of damage specific DNA glycosylases. Incision by AP endonuclease (APE) results in a single-strand break containing the 5'-dRP group. The XRCC1/PARP complex has been described as providing a nick sensing function in BER (6). XRCC1 may function in 'short patch' BER by recruiting ligation machinery to the site of DNA repair (5,6,14). A comparison of BER products in cell-free extracts from wild type and xrcc1 mutant CHO cell lines has shown that XRCC1 may suppress strand displacement synthesis (5), or alternatively, enhance single-nucleotide insertion ligation (15). beta-Pol has a well-defined domain architecture first shown by limited proteolysis (16-18) and later apparent from X-ray crystal structures (19). The three-dimensional structure of beta-Pol is formed from the C-terminal polymerase domain with fingers, palm and thumb sub-domains (19) and the N-terminal 8 kDa DNA binding domain (19,20). The three-dimensional NMR structure of the XRCC1-NTD1-183 has been reported together with the single-strand break binding activity (9). Mapping of the beta-Pol and DNA binding sites showed that the structure of the XRCC1-NTD1-183 is well-suited for interaction with the inside curvature of 90degrees bent DNA and with polypeptide segments in the beta-Pol palm and thumb in a beta-Pol-gapped DNA complex (9).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c21">21</xref>
###xml 333 335 333 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c22">22</xref>
###xml 402 404 399 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c23">23</xref>
###xml 405 407 402 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c25">25</xref>
###xml 485 487 482 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c26">26</xref>
###xml 553 555 550 552 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c14">14</xref>
###xml 611 613 608 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c27">27</xref>
###xml 649 651 646 648 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c28">28</xref>
###xml 1048 1050 1042 1044 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c29">29</xref>
###xml 1051 1053 1045 1047 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c30">30</xref>
While long-patch BER also generates single-strand breaks, it is not clear what role, if any, XRCC1 might serve in long-patch BER. In long-patch BER, 2-13 nt are incorporated by a DNA polymerase (reviewed in 21), and a flap endonuclease (FEN1) is required for removal of the flap structure generated by strand displacement synthesis (22). In cell-free extracts, long-patch BER is conducted by beta-Pol (23-25). A BER regulatory role for PARP, another XRCC1 partner, has been suggested (26), and PARP may provide single-strand break recognition function (14). PARP binds specifically to single-strand DNA breaks (27) and induces a V-like bend in DNA (28). Upon binding, PARP poly-ADP ribosylates itself and nearby proteins causing dissociation of the poly-ADP-ribosylated proteins from the damaged DNA. Whether there is a competition or cooperation of beta-Pol and/or XRCC1 with PARP for damaged sites is not yet known. It has been suggested that PARP could relax the nucleosome structures and recruit the BER complex through interaction with XRCC1 (29,30).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 125 126 125 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c9">9</xref>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c31">31</xref>
###xml 347 349 347 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c32">32</xref>
###xml 350 352 350 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c33">33</xref>
###xml 434 436 434 436 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c34">34</xref>
###xml 477 478 477 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c7">7</xref>
###xml 714 719 714 719 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;151</sub>
To date, structural characterization of domains shows that XRCC1 is formed by the independently folded NTD (residues 1-151) (9) and the C-terminal BRCT-II (breast cancer susceptibility protein-1 homology C-terminal) domain (residues 538-633) (31). A central BRCT domain, BRCT-I (residues 315-403), was predicted on the basis of sequence homology (32,33). BRCT-II was found to interact with a C-terminal BRCT domain of DNA ligase III (34), and BRCT-I was found to bind to PARP (7). On PARP, binding was mapped to a BRCT domain of PARP, as well as to a region N-terminal to the BRCT domain. Nearly half of the XRCC1 sequence is formed by two additional segments, one unclassified segment that separates the XRCC1-NTD1-151 from the BRCT-I domain and a second segment that separates the BRCT domains.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 583 591 577 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 630 635 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 901 906 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In the present study, we have addressed the biochemical properties of the XRCC1-beta-Pol interaction in order to understand the specificity and mechanism of binding. We have characterized the XRCC1-NTD, BRCT-I and BRCT-II domains in XRCC1 by use of limited proteolysis methods. The polypeptide segment separating the NTD from BRCT-I was found not to be stable to proteolysis, while the segment separating the BRCT domains was found to be relatively stable to proteolysis. We have studied the domain specific interaction between N-terminal regions of XRCC1 and domains in beta-Pol by in vitro cross-linking of purified components, yeast two-hybrid methods, quantitative gel filtration and sedimentation equilibrium. We show evidence for a specific interaction between the beta-Pol catalytic domain and the XRCC1-NTD with cross-linking localizing the interaction to the beta-Pol palm-thumb domain and a yeast two-hybrid analysis localizing the interaction to the beta-Pol thumb. The binding affinities and stoichiometry of the interactions have been characterized.
###end p 9

MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 10
###begin title 11
###xml 0 16 0 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>

Escherichia coli expression plasmids and purification of proteins
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 39 40 39 40 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sub>
###xml 86 88 86 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c14">14</xref>
###xml 274 277 274 277 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600</sub>
Full-length XRCC1 with a C-terminal His6 tag was expressed from the plasmid pET16BXH (14) kindly provided by Dr Keith W. Caldecott (School of Biological Sciences, University of Manchester, Manchester, UK). BL21(DE3) cells containing the plasmid were grown in LB medium to OD600 of 0.9 and induced with 1 mM IPTG for 3 h. After centrifugation, pellets were resuspended in buffer containing 10 mM Na phosphate, pH 7.9, 400 mM NaCl, 2 mM beta-mercaptoethanol (beta-ME), 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF) and sonicated. XRCC1 was purified from the crude extract by selective ammonium sulfate precipitation. XRCC1 was soluble at 30% saturation and precipitated at 50% saturation. The protein was further purified on a TALON His tag affinity column (Clontech Laboratories, Palo Alto, CA), according to the manufacturer's instructions. Finally, gel filtration was performed using a Sephadex G-75 column in the same buffer as used for cell lysis. The final purity was >90%, as estimated from acrylamide gels stained with Coomassie blue.
###end p 12

The plasmid pET23a-X183, overexpressing the first 183 residues of human XRCC1, was constructed by sub-cloning the corresponding fragment from XRCC1-delta184-633 (5) into NdeI-BamHI sites in the pET23a vector (Novagen, Madison, WI) using the polymerase chain reaction (PCR). The presence of the gene for XRCC1-NTD1-183 was confirmed by restriction digestion and sequencing. The isolated plasmid DNA was transformed into the BL21(DE3) pLysS E.coli strain for overexpression. Cultures in LB medium were grown to OD600 of 0.9 and induced with 1 mM IPTG for 3 h. After centrifugation, pellets were resuspended in lysis buffer (25 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5, 400 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, 2 mM beta-ME, 1 mM PMSF) and sonicated. XRCC1-NTD1-183 was purified from the crude extract by selective ammonium sulfate precipitation. XRCC1-NTD1-183 was largely soluble at 50% and precipitated at 77% saturation. Gel filtration was performed using a Sephadex G-50 column in the above-described buffer. The final purity was >95%, as estimated from acrylamide gels stained with Coomassie blue.

###begin p 14
###xml 234 236 228 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c18">18</xref>

###xml 59 62 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>

Full-length recombinant human beta-Pol and the recombinant rat beta-Pol 31 and 8 kDa domains, and 16 kDa fragment (consisting of the 8 kDa domain and the fingers sub-domain of the 31 kDa domain) were purified as described previously (18). Human and rat beta-Pol are 96% identical. The 27 kDa C-terminal fragment of beta-Pol (consisting of the palm and thumb sub-domains of the 31 kDa domain) was generated by cleavage of the 31 kDa domain (0.6 mg/ml) with trypsin (10 microg/ml) for 20 min at room temperature in 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.0, 100 mM NaCl and contained some residual intact 31 kDa domain. Reactions were stopped by addition of the inhibitor, 4-(2-aminoethyl)-benzenesulfonyl fluoride (AEBSF) to 3 mM final concentration.

###begin title 15
Domain characterization of XRCC1
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 102 107 102 107 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;183</sub>
###xml 537 542 527 532 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;183</sub>
A solution containing XRCC1 (0.2 mg/ml) in 20 mM Na phosphate buffer, pH 7.0, 100 mM NaCl or XRCC1-NTD1-183 (1 mg/ml) in 5 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5, 250 mM NaCl, was incubated with trypsin (0.2 or 1 microg/ml, respectively) at 25degreesC and aliquots were taken at 5, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 and 60 min. The proteolysis reaction was stopped by addition of AEBSF to a concentration of 0.1 mM and boiling in SDS sample buffer. The samples were characterized by SDS-PAGE using 10-20% gradient Tris-HCl (for XRCC1) or 16.5% Tris-Tricine (for XRCC1-NTD1-183) acrylamide gels. The separated protein fragments were electro-transferred onto Immobilon PSQ membrane (Millipore, Bedford, MA) using CAPS buffer, pH 11, at room temperature for 100 min at 40 V. The membranes were stained with Coomassie blue and bands of interest were cut out. N-terminal microsequencing was performed on an ABI/Perkin Elmer Procise 494 protein sequencer in the Laboratory for Protein Microsequencing, University of Massachusetts Medical Center, Shrewsbury, MA.

###begin title 48
Domain characterization of XRCC1
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 51 52 51 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M</italic>
###xml 52 55 52 55 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">app</sub>

###xml 609 610 609 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd345f1">1</xref>
Limited proteolysis of XRCC1 yielded products with Mapp of ~16, 23, 33 and 45 kDa that were stable for digestion periods of up to 60 min (Fig. 1A). The proteolytically obtained protein fragments were identified by sequence analysis of the first five N-terminal residues. Sequence analysis of the 16 kDa fragment indicated that the N-terminal residue was Ala311 (Fig. 1B). The size of the 16 kDa fragment together with the finding that another cleavage site in XRCC1 was observed between Lys436 and Thr437 to yield a 33 kDa fragment (discussed below) suggests that the C-terminus of the 16 kDa is Lys436 (Fig. 1B). Thus, these data indicate that the low molecular weight 16 kDa product is the proteolytically generated BRCT-I domain.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 439 440 439 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd345f1">1</xref>
###xml 570 575 570 575 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;183</sub>
The band that migrated at ~23 kDa was found to contain two proteolytic products, that were identified as the XRCC1-NTD and the C-terminal BRCT-II domain. In the 40 min proteolysis time point the XRCC1-NTD and the C-terminal BRCT-II were in an ~2:1 molar ratio. Sequence analysis indicated that the N-terminal residue in the proteolytically generated XRCC1-NTD was Pro 2 (NTD) and the presumed C-terminal residue was Lys164 or Lys169 (Fig. 1B). The N-terminal residue in the proteolytically generated BRCT-II domain was Val495. Limited proteolysis of the cloned XRCC1-NTD1-183, consisting of residues 1-183, similarly yielded a 23 kDa fragment resulting from the loss of ~2 kDa (data not shown). These results for the intact XRCC1 and for the 1-183 fragment are consistent with cleavage at Lys164 or Lys169.

###begin title 52

###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 99 100 99 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c1">1</xref>

###xml 177 179 177 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c43">43</xref>
###xml 187 188 187 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd345f1">1</xref>
###xml 600 601 600 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd345f1">1</xref>
###xml 701 703 701 703 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c36">36</xref>
###xml 704 706 704 706 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c37">37</xref>
###xml 82 87 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 103 108 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 152 162 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
There are four XRCC1 sequences in the databases, which include the sequences from human (huXRCC1) (1), mouse (muXRCC1) (40), hamster (haXRCC1) (41) and Drosophila (drXRCC1) (42,43) (Fig. 1B). The three mammalian proteins have an identity of >80%. The NTD (1-151), BRCT-I (315-403), and BRCT-II (538-633) show >90% sequence identity. The segments between the NTD and BRCT-1 and the segment between the BRCT domains consist of polar Pro, Ser and Arg/Lys-rich regions and are only ~60% identical for the mammalian proteins. There are, however, two regions within these segments with >80% identity (Fig. 1B). Residues 158-250 (conserved region 1) have significant hydrophobicity and are predicted by PHD (36,37) to have secondary structure suggestive of folding to form an ID. This predicted domain, however, is unstable to proteolysis. Conserved region 2 (residues 469-526) is shorter and more polar, with only one helix predicted by the PHD program and is not likely to form a folded domain.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 368 378 368 378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 368 378 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
The drXRCC1 shows limited homology to the mammalian XRCC1 sequences. It contains the NTD and BRCT-I domains with >40% identity. It lacks BRCT-II and has an ~100 residue insertion between the predicted ID and BRCT-I. In addition, the predicted ID shows 27% identity, but a BLAST search with residues 152-250 of huXRCC1 does not independently find the weakly homologous Drosophila sequence. Homology searches for the linker regions between the BRCT domains showed conserved region 2, residues 469-526, with 37% identity from drXRCC1 to huXRCC1. The region 2 conservation in drXRCC1 suggests that its function is not related to BRCT-II.

The sensitivity of the cross-linking reaction to increasing salt concentration was studied in order to determine the nature of the interaction. To further characterize the binding equilibria for XRCC1-NTD1-183 interaction with beta-Pol and with beta-Pol domains, we analyzed these interactions using analytical ultracentrifugation. As a first step, any possible self-association of XRCC1-NTD1-183 alone was characterized. The addition of DTT (1.5 mM) significantly reduced the formation of irreversible dimers through disulfide bonds. Data analysis, however, indicated the presence of small amount of dimer in the solution. When the data were fitted for a monomer, it was found that the mean molecular mass of the protein species in the solution slightly exceeded the monomeric mass. The data were then fit by assuming a monomer-dimer reversible association or by assuming the presence of two non-interacting species with molecular masses equal to and twice that of the monomer. The statistical fit was excellent in both cases and indicated the presence of ~5% dimer. The hypothetical reversible interaction would be rather weak with Kd equal to ~0.4 and 0.3 mM at temperatures 4 and 20degrees C, respectively.

###xml 1598 1599 1539 1540 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sup>
###xml 1601 1603 1542 1544 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 1664 1665 1601 1602 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 1665 1666 1602 1603 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sub>
###xml 1952 1957 1885 1890 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1&#8211;183</sub>
It has recently been reported that intact beta-Pol has the tendency to oligomerize forming oligomers of various sizes (39). The association has been fit by a weak indefinite isodesmic association, meaning that the addition of each monomer to the oligomeric chain is energetically identical. Therefore, the protein species in the equilibrated solution could include intact beta-Pol oligomers of all sizes, XRCC1-NTD1-183 monomer and dimer along with possible heterogeneous complexes. In each experiment, a heterogeneous complex of XRCC1-NTD1-183 and beta-Pol in a 1:1 molar ratio, was strongly present. No X2B2 complex (Materials and Methods) could be detected at either temperature and inclusion of the isodesmic association of beta-Pol or of the XRCC1-NTD1-183 dimer in the analysis model was interchangeable with little change in the final results. The association constant for the 1:1 complex of XRCC1-NTD1-183 lies between 2 x 106 and 3.4 x 106 M-1 (i.e., Kd = 0.3-0.5 microM) at the temperatures examined, with good fits at 4degreesC (Fig. 5A) and 20degreesC. This affinity is much stronger than the beta-Pol self association or the XRCC1-NTD1-183 virtual association. Similar results were obtained for XRCC1-NTD with the 31 kDa domain of beta-Pol using the same models as with intact beta-Pol (Fig. 5B). As with intact beta-Pol, there was heterogeneous association for XRCC1-NTD1-183 and the 31 kDa domain but the association was slightly weaker than with intact beta-Pol. At 4degreesC the association constant was 0.15 x 106 M-1 or Kd = 6.7 microM, and at 20degreesC the values were 0.4 x 106 M-1 and 2.4 microM, respectively. The slight differences in the Kd (0.4 versus 2.4 microM) of the XRCC1-31 kDa domain complex determined by gel filtration and analytical ultracentrifugation experiments likely result from differences in temperatures, buffer conditions and pH in the two experiments. The results indicate binding specificity by XRCC1-NTD1-183 for the 31 kDa domain of beta-Pol. The 8 kDa domain of the intact enzyme provides only a small degree of additional binding free energy.
###end p 68
###begin title 69
DISCUSSION
###end title 69
###begin p 70
###xml 401 402 401 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c9">9</xref>
###xml 760 762 760 762 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c44">44</xref>
###xml 843 853 843 853 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 956 966 956 966 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 630 635 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 843 853 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 956 966 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
Biochemical and sequence analysis of the domain structure of XRCC1 indicated that XRCC1 is formed by an NTD (XRCC1-NTD), a central BRCT domain (BRCT-I) and a C-terminal BRCT domain (BRCT-II). A predicted fourth domain (XRCC1 ID) formed by the conserved residues 158-250 adjacent to the NTD was found not to be stable to proteolysis. The XRCC1-NTD, with a low pI of 6.1, binds single-strand break DNA (9). A possible role of the adjoining sequence (residues 158-310), which includes the predicted ID, might be facilitation of DNA binding, since this segment has a high pI (10.7) and overall positive charge. In the predicted ID, a human polymorphic mutation (R194W) is seen at a conserved Arg in mammals making it of interest in terms of cancer susceptibility (44). Interestingly, Arg194 (positively charged) aligns with a Trp (hydrophobic) in Drosophila, while Ile195 (hydrophobic), which is conserved in mammals, aligns with a Lys (positively charged) in Drosophila.
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 190 191 190 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd345f1">1</xref>
###xml 490 500 490 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 517 518 517 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd345f1">1</xref>
###xml 562 572 562 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 727 737 727 737 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 896 898 896 898 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c36">36</xref>
###xml 899 901 899 901 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c37">37</xref>
###xml 490 500 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 504 510 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 562 572 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 727 737 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
Unexpectedly, the region between BRCT-I and BRCT-II was found to be relatively resistant to proteolysis, suggesting that this segment of the protein is not totally flexible or exposed (Fig. 1A). Instead, the resistance of this segment to proteolysis, even though it contains Arg and Lys residues for tryptic cleavage, suggests that it associates with one or both of the BRCT domains of XRCC1. Approximately half of the linker segment between the BRCT domains was found to be conserved from Drosophila to humans (Fig. 1B). Since the BRCT-II domain is absent from Drosophila, this 60 residue segment (conserved region II) may have an important role that is functionally distinct from BRCT-II. Moreover, the absence of BRCT-II in Drosophila XRCC1 may make BRCT-I a more likely candidate for association with the conserved region II. Residues 487-500 of conserved region II were predicted using PHD (36,37) to form a helix.
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 97 99 97 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c34">34</xref>
###xml 244 245 244 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c7">7</xref>
###xml 637 638 631 632 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 638 639 632 633 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sub>
###xml 929 930 913 914 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c6">6</xref>
Previously, the XRCC1 BRCT-II was shown to bind to the C-terminal BRCT domain of DNA ligase III (34). Additionally, XRCC1 BRCT-I has been shown to bind to the BRCT domain of PARP in conjunction with a region N-terminal to the PARP BRCT domain (7). No such mapping studies have been previously reported for the interaction between the XRCC1-NTD and domains of beta-Pol. We have found that XRCC1-NTD interacts with the C-terminal 31 kDa domain of beta-Pol and not with the 8 kDa domain. The interaction has a substantial hydrophobic contribution as judged by the negligible effect of increasing salt on binding. The dissociation constant (Kd of 0.4-2.4 microM) of the complex formed by XRCC1-NTD and either beta-Pol or its 31 kDa domain indicates that the interaction is physiologically relevant and is consistent with the finding that XRCC1 is co-immunoprecipitated with beta-Pol, DNA ligase III and PARP from HeLa cell extracts (6).
###end p 72
###begin p 73
###xml 523 524 511 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c9">9</xref>
###xml 639 645 627 633 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">75&#8211;212</sub>
###xml 675 681 660 666 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">75&#8211;212</sub>
###xml 957 958 927 928 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c9">9</xref>
###xml 176 181 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 574 579 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
We have found that residues in XRCC1 that are C-terminal to Glu159 (two-hybrid screen) or Glu157 (cross-linking) were not required for beta-Pol binding. Importantly, using the yeast two-hybrid screen, we localized the interaction of the XRCC1-NTD to the thumb of beta-Pol. Less significant contributions by the beta-Pol palm domain to XRCC1-NTD interaction were not ruled out. Primary thumb contacts as well as possible palm contacts are consistent with a previously proposed model for XRCC1-NTD interaction with beta-Pol (9). As predicted from the XRCC1-NTD structure, the yeast two-hybrid screen did not show an interaction between XRCC175-212 and beta-Pol, since the XRCC175-212 expression fragment would be missing strands betaABD on the five-stranded betaABGDE sheet and strand betaC on the three-stranded betaFCH sheet, in addition to other secondary structural elements that together are required for the central beta-sandwich fold of the XRCC1-NTD (9). Interestingly, the XRCC1-NTD could be stripped of the N-terminal 35 residues and retain interaction with beta-Pol when expressed as a fusion construct. The N-terminal 35 residues form an extended segment, two strands and a helix that pack on one side of the domain opposite to the side of beta-Pol interaction. Such a deletion apparently allows the folding of the beta-sandwich formed by the betaBGDE (missing betaA) and the betaFCH sheets as well as the packing of the secondary structures that include alpha2, alpha3 and the betaB'G' two-stranded sheet.
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 36 37 33 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 37 38 34 35 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sub>
###xml 237 238 229 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c9">9</xref>
###xml 373 375 359 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c13">13</xref>
###xml 473 475 456 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c45">45</xref>
###xml 1048 1050 1022 1024 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c39">39</xref>
###xml 1179 1180 1144 1145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 1180 1181 1145 1146 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sub>
The XRCC1-NTD-beta-Pol interaction (Kd = 400 +/- 100 nM) is slightly weaker than the XRCC1-NTD interaction with duplex DNA containing a single-strand gap (half maximal binding at ~60 nM) or with a beta-Pol single-nucleotide gap complex (9). The XRCC1-NTD binding affinity for beta-Pol is approximately 100 times weaker than the binding affinity of gapped DNA for beta-Pol (13) but is comparable to the binding affinity of dNTP substrates for a beta-Pol-gapped DNA complex (45). XRCC1 is the only protein known to interact with the catalytic domain of beta-Pol. The interaction likely involves contacts with both gapped DNA and loops in the thumb and possibly palm domains of beta-Pol. This mechanism of interaction is supported by the results from this study. An alternate pathway for DNA repair via beta-Pol utilizes DNA ligase I for ligation (46). Unlike the XRCC1-NTD interaction, DNA ligase I was found to interact with the 8 kDa domain and the contacts involved several charged residues on the 8 kDa domain as assessed by mutational analysis (39). In this interaction a ligase I trimer associates with three beta-Pol monomers to form a hexameric complex. At 32degreesC, the Kd for beta-Pol 8 kDa domain interaction with DNA ligase I was calculated to be ~10 microM. These differing interactions by XRCC1/DNA ligase III and DNA ligase I with beta-Pol will likely relate to unique BER pathways but do not suggest competitive interactions with beta-Pol.
###end p 74
###begin p 75
###xml 282 289 276 283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 402 409 393 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 564 571 555 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 922 924 907 909 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c39">39</xref>
###xml 1027 1029 1012 1014 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c48">48</xref>
###xml 1276 1278 1258 1260 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c49">49</xref>
The binding constant for the beta-Pol-XRCC1 complex should be considered in view of the ordered BER binding events during DNA repair and the mechanism by which XRCC1 is likely to participate. The binding constant and previous studies suggest that XRCC1 will associate with beta-Pol in vivo and preferentially interact with beta-Pol bound at sites of DNA damage. XRCC1 is associated with DNA ligase III in vivo through interaction between C-terminal BRCT domains on each of these proteins, and mutations in XRCC1 have been shown to decrease DNA ligase III activity in vivo. PARP interacts with the central BRCT-I domain of XRCC1. Whether PARP remains associated with XRCC1 during all steps in single-strand break repair remains to be determined. APE facilitates beta-Pol interaction at an abasic site after incision of abasic site DNA to form a strand break, but APE does not associate with beta-Pol in the absence of DNA (39,47). A recent structure shows the interaction of APE1 with bent DNA containing an abasic site analog (48). Interestingly, like beta-Pol, the APE is found to make contacts on the outside of the bend (i.e., the side opposite to XRCC1-NTD interaction). The DNA bend in these BER complexes is likely of importance in the ordered steps of the BER pathway (49).
###end p 75
###begin p 76
###xml 505 507 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd345c50">50</xref>
The XRCC1-NTD binds single-strand break DNA in conjunction with beta-Pol and contributes to the overall stability of a beta-Pol single-nucleotide gap DNA complex. The interaction of the XRCC1-NTD with the thumb is of significance, since the beta-Pol thumb is a required domain in polymerase catalysis (functioning analogously to the fingers in the Pol I family of enzymes). Furthermore, motion of the beta-Pol thumb with respect to the palm is likely to be important in polymerase catalysis and fidelity (50). Such motion during catalysis could be affected by XRCC1-NTD interaction.
###end p 76
