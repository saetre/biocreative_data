Fission yeast Arp6 is required for telomere silencing, but functions independently of Swi6
###end article-title 0

###xml 1054 1061 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
The actin-related proteins (Arps), which are subdivided into at least eight subfamilies, are conserved from yeast to humans. A member of the Arp6 subfamily in Drosophila, Arp4/Arp6, co-localizes with heterochromatin protein 1 (HP1) in pericentric heterochromatin. Fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe possesses both an HP1 homolog and an Arp6 homolog. However, the function of S.pombe Arp6 has not been characterized yet. We found that deletion of arp6+ impaired telomere silencing, but did not affect centromere silencing. Chromatin immunoprecipitation assays revealed that Arp6 bound to the telomere region. However, unlike Drosophila Arp4/Arp6, S.pombe Arp6 was distributed throughout nuclei. The binding of Arp6 to telomere DNA was not affected by deletion of swi6+. Moreover, the binding of Swi6 to telomere ends was not affected by deletion of arp6+. These results suggest that Arp6 and Swi6 function independently at telomere ends. We propose that the Arp6-mediated repression mechanism works side by side with Swi6-based telomere silencing in S.pombe.

Heterochromatin is a transcriptionally inactive region in the eukaryotic chromatin found near centromeres and telomeres (1). Heterochromatin is constituted by non-histone chromosomal proteins such as heterochromatin protein 1 (HP1) that are found in a variety of eukaryotic organisms ranging from Schizosaccharomyces pombe to humans (2). Drosophila HP1 binds to centric heterochromatin and telomeric regions and participates in chromatin packaging and gene silencing (3). The swi6+ gene of the fission yeast S.pombe encodes an HP1 homolog and is required for silencing at centromeres, telomeres and the silent mating-type loci (4-6). Recently, the chromo-domain in the HP1/Swi6 protein family was shown to interact with methylated lysine 9 of histone H3 (7-11).

The mechanisms of the transcriptional silencing at the silent mating-type loci and centromere have been well characterized in S.pombe (12-14). Mutations in several genes, including clr1+, clr2+, clr3+, clr4+, clr6+, swi6+, rik1+ and hdf1+, cause partial derepression at the centromere and silent mating-type loci (14). In S.pombe, histone deacetylases and methylases are thought to act in a sequential process. Histone deacetylases, Clr6 and Clr3, deacetylate histone H3 at lysine 9 and lysine 14, respectively, and then histone H3 is methylated at lysine 9 by Clr4 (11). Clr4 is a member of the SU(VAR)3-9 protein family, which includes human SUV39H1 and Drosophila SU(VAR)3-9. Members of this protein family contain the SET domain, which is required for methyltransferase activity (15,16). A mutation in rik1+ completely abolishes methylation of H3 at lysine 9 and localization of Swi6 at the centromere and mating-type loci (11,17).

Genes required for silencing at the centromere and silent mating-type loci, such as clr1+, clr2+, clr3+, clr4+, clr6+, swi6+, rik1+ and hdf1+, are also required for telomere silencing in S.pombe (14). However, additional trans-acting factors including taz1+, rap1+ and rif1+ are required for silencing at telomeres. Mutations in any of these genes specifically affect telomeric silencing (18-20). Taz1 is a telomere-binding protein that is a member of the TRF family, which negatively regulates telomere length. Rap1 and Rif1 localize to telomeres through Taz1 (19,21). Schizosaccharomyces pombe spHst4, which is a Sir2 homolog, is also required for silencing at telomeres (22). In addition to spHst4, there are two Sir2 homologs, spSir2 and spHst2, in S.pombe (23). Recently the S.pombe Sir2 was reported to be required for silencing at the donor mating-type loci, telomeres and the inner centromeric repeats, suggesting that Sir2 plays a conserved role in heterochromatin assembly in eukaryotes (24).

Actin-related proteins (Arps) compose the actin family together with conventional actins, and Arps are classified into subfamilies according to their sequence similarities (25). In addition to conventional actins, at least eight Arp subfamilies are conserved from yeast to humans, and some of the Arp subfamilies have been implicated in nuclear organization and function (26). Saccharomyces cerevisiae Act3/Arp4, a member of the Arp4 subfamily, was the first Arp shown to be localized in the nucleus (27,28). ACT3/ARP4 is an essential gene that is involved in transcriptional regulation (27,29). Act3/Arp4 binds to core histones in vitro and is thought to recruit chromatin remodeling and histone acetyltransferase complexes onto chromatin (30,31). Act3/Arp4 is part of the Esa1-containing NuA4 HAT complex and is recruited specifically to DNA double-strand breaks that are generated in vivo (32,33).
###end p 7

Members of the Arp6 subfamily have been reported in budding yeast, fission yeast, Drosophila, chicken and humans (34); however, the function of the Arp6 subfamily has not been well characterized. Drosophila Arp4/Arp6 co-localizes with HP1 at the centric heterochromatin, suggesting a physical interaction between these two proteins (35,36). However, the role of Arp6 in heterochromatin remains unclear. Here we characterized the function of S.pombe Arp6 in heterochromatin. We tested whether Arp6 was involved in silencing at centromeres and telomeres. We also investigated the localization of Arp6 and examined its functional link to Swi6 and Taz1.

A 2.9-kb fragment containing part of the arp6+ ORF, which was amplified by PCR using genomic DNA and primers 1 (5'-GTAGAGGAGGCAACTCC-3') and 2 (5'-TCTGGTATCATGAATCTCTC-3'), was subcloned into pT7Blue T-Vector giving the plasmid pT7arp6. ###begin title 23
Arp6 is required for transcriptional silencing at telomeres, but not centromeres
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 3 10 3 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 68 70 68 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c34">34</xref>

###xml 211 213 211 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c36">36</xref>

###xml 1090 1094 1090 1094 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">his3</italic>
###xml 1094 1095 1094 1095 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1100 1104 1100 1104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ura4</italic>

###xml 1212 1214 1200 1202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c20">20</xref>
###xml 1296 1300 1284 1288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 1360 1361 1344 1345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f1">1</xref>
###xml 1525 1529 1509 1513 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 1575 1576 1555 1556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f1">1</xref>
###xml 1737 1741 1717 1721 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">swi6</italic>

###xml 1891 1892 1863 1864 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f1">1</xref>
###xml 1911 1915 1883 1887 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">swi6</italic>
###xml 1928 1932 1896 1900 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 2036 2040 2000 2004 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 2058 2059 2018 2019 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f1">1</xref>
###xml 3 10 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 132 142 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 220 227 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 370 377 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 488 495 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
In S.pombe, one Arp6 homolog has been found in the genome database (34), but the function of this protein has not been studied. The Drosophila Arp6 subfamily colocalizes with HP1 at the centric heterochromatin (36). The S.pombe HP1 homolog, Swi6, is required for transcriptional silencing at both telomeres and centromeres (4-6). These facts prompted us to test whether S.pombe Arp6 was involved in transcriptional silencing at telomeres and centromeres. To study the in vivo function of S.pombe arp6+, we first made a heterozygous strain (arp6+/-) using a one-step gene replacement procedure, in which one of the chromosomal arp6+ genes was replaced with a ura4+ cassette. Spores derived from the heterozygote were viable regardless of auxotrophy for uracil, indicating that arp6 is not essential (data not shown). Deletion of arp6+ did not affect growth rate, suggesting that arp6+ is not required for mitotic growth (data not shown). Next we disrupted the arp6+ gene in the FY1862 strain and examined the transcriptional silencing at telomeres and centromeres. The FY1862 strain carries his3+ and ura4+ approximately300 bp from the left telomeres of chromosomes I and II, respectively, and ade6+ within cen1 (20). Wild-type FY1862 cells did not grow on SD -histidine plates, while in contrast arp6Delta cells were able to grow on SD -histidine plates (Fig. 1A), indicating that Arp6 is required for telomere silencing. We also performed RT-PCR experiments and found that a significant amount of His3 mRNA was expressed in arp6Delta cells, but not in wild-type cells (Fig. 1B). This result further suggests that Arp6 is required for telomere silencing. We next examined silencing at centromeres. As centromere silencing is impaired in swi6Delta cells, the ade6+ gene inserted at the centromere was expressed. As a result, swi6Delta cells do not become red on low adenine (YE) plates (Fig. 1C). In contrast to swi6Delta cells, arp6Delta cells became red on low adenine plates, indicating that silencing at the centromere was normal in arp6Delta cells (Fig. 1C).
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Arp6 binds to telomeres independently of Swi6 and Taz1
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 189 191 189 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c38">38</xref>
###xml 418 422 418 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ade6</italic>
###xml 422 423 422 423 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 541 545 541 545 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 561 562 561 562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f2">2</xref>
###xml 686 690 686 690 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">swi6</italic>
###xml 690 691 690 691 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 695 699 695 699 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">taz1</italic>
###xml 699 700 699 700 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 707 708 707 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f2">2</xref>
As Arp6 was required for telomere silencing, but not centromere silencing, we next tested the binding of Arp6 to telomeres by the ChIP assay. We tagged the C-terminus of Arp6 with Myc-tag (38). Telomere silencing was not affected by tagging of Arp6 with Myc-tag (data not shown). Anti-Myc antibody was used for immunoprecipitation and the precipitated DNA was amplified by PCR with primers for the telomeric region or ade6+as a control. Telomere DNA was significantly amplified in cells that expressed Myc-tagged Arp6 protein from their own arp6 promoter (Fig. 2). These results indicate that Arp6 binds to telomere DNA. The binding of Arp6 to telomeres was not affected by deletion of swi6+ or taz1+ (Fig. 2). These results indicate that Arp6 binds to telomeres independently of Swi6 and Taz1.
###end p 26
###begin title 27
Localization of Swi6 to telomeres is not affected in arp6Delta cells
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 91 95 91 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 95 96 95 96 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 191 195 191 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 274 275 270 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f3">3</xref>
###xml 307 311 303 307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 311 312 307 308 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
We next examined whether the localization of Swi6 to telomeres was affected by deletion of arp6+ by using the ChIP assay. Telomere DNA was immunoprecipitated in both the wild-type strain and arp6Delta cells when anti-Swi6 antibody was used for the immunoprecipitation (Fig. 3A). These results indicate that arp6+ is dispensable for the binding of Swi6 to telomere DNA.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 131 135 131 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 179 180 175 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f3">3</xref>
###xml 309 313 293 297 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
Swi6 interacts with methylated lysine 9 of histone H3. Thus, we next tested whether histone H3 at lysine 9 was still methylated in arp6Delta cells by using the ChIP assay. Figure 3B shows that subtelomere DNA (approximately2 kb away from telomere ends) was immunoprecipitated in both the wild-type strain and arp6Delta cells when anti-dimethyl-histone H3-K9 antibody was used for the immunoprecipitation. These results indicate that Arp6 is not involved in the modification of histone H3 at lysine 9.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 130 134 130 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 180 182 176 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c42">42</xref>
###xml 209 210 205 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c6">6</xref>
###xml 314 315 310 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f3">3</xref>
###xml 352 356 348 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 385 389 377 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 389 390 381 382 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 469 470 461 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f3">3</xref>
Swi6 also localizes to the centromere and silent mating-type loci. Therefore, we examined the nuclear localization of Swi6-GFP in arp6Delta cells by using fluorescence microscopy (42). As reported previously (6), wild-type cells expressing Swi6-GFP fusion protein contained 2-4 foci at the nuclear periphery (Fig. 3C). These foci were also observed in arp6Delta cells, indicating that arp6+ is dispensable for localization of Swi6 to the silenced heterochromatin (Fig. 3C).

###begin title 31
Arp6 localizes to nucleus
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 127 139 127 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 287 291 287 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GAL1</italic>
###xml 302 304 302 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c26">26</xref>
###xml 436 437 436 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f4">4</xref>
###xml 675 685 675 685 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 777 779 777 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c36">36</xref>
###xml 823 830 823 830 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 868 872 868 872 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">swi6</italic>
###xml 872 873 872 873 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 880 881 880 881 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f4">4</xref>
###xml 127 139 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 675 685 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 823 830 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
The ChIP assay revealed that Arp6 binds to telomere DNA. However, it is also possible that Arp6 localizes to other regions. In S.cerevisiae, Arp6 was reported to be present in both the cytoplasm and nucleus, but slightly concentrated in the nucleus, when Arp6-GFP was expressed from the GAL1 promoter (26). Therefore, we examined the cellular localization of Arp6-Myc by using indirect immunofluorescence microscopy. As shown in Figure 4, the localization of Arp6 coincided with the nuclear 4',6'-diamino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) staining. This result indicates that the localization of Arp6 is not confined to the telomere, but rather is distributed throughout the nucleus. In Drosophila, the localization pattern of Arp4/Arp6 is altered in cells expressing mutant forms of HP1 (36). In contrast, the localization pattern of S.pombe Arp6 was not affected by deletion of swi6+ (Fig. 4). This result further suggests that Swi6 and Arp6 function independently.

###begin title 33
DISCUSSION
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 0 10 0 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 195 207 195 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe arp6</italic>
###xml 207 208 207 208 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 294 295 294 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f1">1</xref>
###xml 363 367 363 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">swi6</italic>
###xml 367 368 367 368 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 375 376 375 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f2">2</xref>
###xml 459 463 459 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 463 464 463 464 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 471 472 471 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh234f3">3</xref>
###xml 562 572 562 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1096 1121 1096 1121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</italic>
###xml 1161 1165 1161 1165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">chp2</italic>
###xml 1227 1231 1227 1231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">swi6</italic>
###xml 1240 1242 1240 1242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c43">43</xref>
###xml 0 10 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 195 202 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 562 572 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 956 961 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1096 1121 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
Drosophila Arp4/Arp6 has been shown to play roles in heterochromatin. However, the exact roles of the Arp6 subfamily in chromatin organization have not been studied in detail. Here we found that S.pombe arp6+ is required for transcriptional silencing at telomeres, but not at centromeres (Fig. 1). The binding of Arp6 to telomeres was not affected by deletion of swi6+ (Fig. 2). Moreover, the localization of Swi6 to telomeres was not affected by deletion of arp6+ (Fig. 3). These two facts suggest that Swi6 and Arp6 function independently at telomere ends. In Drosophila, a physical interaction between Arp4/Arp6 and HP1 has been suggested. Therefore, we examined the interaction between Swi6 and Arp6-Myc by co-immunoprecipitation assays. Although we could detect both Swi6 and Arp6-Myc using antibodies against Swi6 and Myc-tag, respectively, we could not detect an interaction between these two proteins by co-immunoprecipitation assays using soluble yeast cell extracts (data not shown). These data are consistent with our genetic data suggesting that Swi6 and Arp6 function independently. Schizosaccharomyces pombe has another HP1 homolog, Chp2, and the chp2 mutant shows a larger silencing defect at telomeres than the swi6 mutant (43). Therefore, we examined the interaction between Chp2-myc and Arp6-TAP by co-immunoprecipitation assay. However, we could not detect an interaction between these two proteins (data not shown).
###end p 34

###xml 555 556 555 556 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 614 618 614 618 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 618 619 618 619 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 788 790 788 790 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c18">18</xref>
###xml 791 793 791 793 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c19">19</xref>
###xml 794 796 794 796 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c21">21</xref>
As telomere silencing is markedly affected by deletion of taz1+, we also examined a functional link between Taz1 and Arp6. However, the localization of Arp6 to telomeres was not affected by deletion of taz1+ (Fig. 2). Moreover, the localization pattern of Taz1-GFP was not affected by deletion of arp6+ (data not shown). These results suggest that Arp6 functions independently of Taz1. As Arp6 binds to telomere ends, it seemed possible that deletion of arp6+ might affect telomere length. However, the telomere length was not affected by deletion of arp6+ (data not shown). This finding suggests that deletion of arp6+ does not affect the localization of telomere-binding proteins such as Taz1, Rap1 and Rif1 that are required for both telomere length regulation and telomere silencing (18,19,21).
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 91 95 91 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 95 96 95 96 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 275 282 275 282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 284 286 284 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c44">44</xref>
###xml 287 289 287 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c45">45</xref>
###xml 369 371 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c44">44</xref>
###xml 372 374 372 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh234c46">46</xref>
###xml 393 397 393 397 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pof3</italic>
###xml 407 411 403 407 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad3</italic>
###xml 424 428 416 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arp6</italic>
###xml 275 282 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
Our results clearly demonstrate that Arp6 is required for telomere silencing. However, how arp6+ is involved in telomere silencing is still unknown. In addition to Swi6-based telomere silencing, several factors, including Rad3 and Pof3, are involved in telomere silencing in S.pombe (44,45). Rad3 and Pof3 are also required for DNA repair and/or DNA damage checkpoint (44,46). However, unlike pof3Delta and rad3Delta cells, arp6Delta cells were not sensitive to a DNA-damaging agent (methylmethane sulfonate), suggesting that Arp6 is not required for DNA repair or DNA damage checkpoint (data not shown).
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 145 152 145 152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 352 359 352 359 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 442 449 442 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 145 152 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 210 215 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 219 225 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 352 359 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 442 449 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
In summary, the data presented here indicate that an Arp6-mediated repression mechanism works side by side with Swi6-based telomere silencing in S.pombe. As the presence of the Arp6 subfamily is conserved from yeast to humans, it is tempting to speculate that the Arp6-mediated repression mechanism is conserved in other eukaryotes. Further studies of S.pombe Arp6 will help to clarify the mechanism of Swi6-independent telomere silencing in S.pombe, and possibly also in other eukaryotes.
###end p 37
