Adaptive expression responses in the Pol-gamma null strain of S. pombe depleted of mitochondrial genome

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 152 176 144 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 451 476 439 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</italic>
###xml 146 151 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 152 176 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 186 191 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 437 450 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 451 476 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
DNA polymerase gamma(Pol-gamma) has been shown to be essential for maintenance of the mitochondrial genome (mtDNA) in the petite-positive budding yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae. Budding yeast cells lacking mitochondria exhibit a slow-growing or petite-colony phenotype. Petite strains fail to grow on non-fermentable carbon sources. However, it is not clear whether the Pol-gamma is required for mtDNA maintenance in the petite-negative fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 44 48 44 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1</italic>
###xml 48 50 48 50 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 107 115 103 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe</italic>
###xml 131 141 127 133 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 241 251 233 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 326 336 314 320 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 402 412 386 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 504 514 484 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 107 115 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
We show that disruption of the nuclear gene pog1+ that encodes Pol-gamma is sufficient to deplete mtDNA in S. pombe. Cells bearing pog1Delta allele require substantial growth periods to form petite colonies. Mitotracker assays indicate that pog1Delta cells are defective in mitochondrial function and EM analyses suggest that pog1Delta cells lack normal mitochondrial structures. Depletion of mtDNA in pog1Delta cells is evident from quantitative real-time PCR assays. Genome-wide expression profiles of pog1Delta and other mtDNA-less cells reveal that many genes involved in response to stimulus, energy derivation by oxidation of organic compounds, cellular carbohydrate metabolism, and energy reserve metabolism are induced. Conversely, many genes encoding proteins involved in amino acid metabolism and oxidative phosphorylation are repressed.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 79 83 75 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1</italic>
###xml 83 85 79 81 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 244 252 240 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe</italic>
###xml 244 252 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
By showing that Pol-gamma is essential for mtDNA maintenance and disruption of pog1+ alters the genome-wide expression profiles, we demonstrated that cells lacking mtDNA exhibit adaptive nuclear gene expression responses in the petite-negative S. pombe.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 519 527 515 523 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 528 529 524 525 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 530 531 526 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 600 606 592 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POLG1 </italic>
###xml 619 624 611 616 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MIP1 </italic>
###xml 627 651 619 643 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 705 706 697 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 707 708 699 700 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 519 526 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 609 614 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 627 651 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
Mitochondria are essential organelles that generate ATP through the respiratory chain to provide energy for many biochemical reactions and cellular processes in eukaryotic cells. Mitochondria are one of the organelles that contain its own genetic material, the mitochondrial genome (mtDNA). It is thought that mitochondria evolved over millions of years after originating from bacteria that had invaded eukaryotic cells. It is thus not surprising that Pol-gamma has a lineage similar to prokaryotic DNA polymerase A in E. coli [1,2]. A nuclear-DNA encoded gene of the catalytic subunit of Pol gamma (POLG1 in human and MIP1 in Saccharomyces cerevisiae) has been shown to be essential for mtDNA stability [3,4].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 199 200 199 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 201 202 201 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 240 246 240 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POLG1 </italic>
###xml 355 357 355 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 363 376 363 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 596 610 588 602 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 611 613 603 605 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 791 817 783 809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe </italic>
###xml 851 853 843 845 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 960 962 952 954 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 116 121 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 225 230 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 363 376 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 596 609 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 791 816 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
The mitochondrial genomes in many mammalian systems are small when compared to their corresponding nuclear genomes. Human mtDNA, for example, is only ~16.6 kb in length and rodent mtDNA is ~16.3 kb [5-9]. Mutations in either human mtDNA or POLG1 have been implicated in a number of diseases such as Leigh's syndrome, Alpers syndrome, and ophthalmoplegia [10]. In S. cerevisiae, the size of mtDNA is relatively large, ~85.8 kb in size. Cells depleted of mtDNA (rhodegrees) have been isolated through the treatment with ethidium bromide which results in the formation of tiny or petite colonies in S. cerevisiae [11]. The petite mutants fail to grow on media containing non-fermentable carbon sources owing to a lack of mitochondrial functions. Petites are not readily isolated from wild type Schizosaccharomyces pombe using ethidium bromide treatment [12]. However, some unidentified nuclear mutations have facilitated the isolation of cells depleted of mtDNA [13]. The isolated petites exhibited severely retarded growth phenotypes that impeded further genetic analyses of these petite strains.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 45 49 45 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1</italic>
###xml 49 51 49 51 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 96 100 92 96 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1</italic>
###xml 100 101 96 97 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 115 119 111 115 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mip1</italic>
###xml 119 121 115 117 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 141 155 137 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 220 228 216 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe</italic>
###xml 297 307 293 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 422 432 414 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 969 978 957 966 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe </italic>
###xml 141 154 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 220 228 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
###xml 969 977 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
In this study, we show that the nuclear gene pog1+ (we designate the Pol-gamma-encoding gene as pog1+, because the mip1+ designation used in S. cerevisiae has already been used for a gene encoding a WD repeat protein in S. pombe) is essential for mtDNA maintenance. Significantly, cells bearing a pog1Delta allele are capable of forming petite colonies after extended incubation times under optimal growth conditions. The pog1Delta cells display multiple defects such as non-uptake of mitotracker dyes, impaired mitochondrial structures, and depletion of mtDNA. Genome-wide expression profiling of mtDNA-less cells reveals that many genes encoding proteins involved in response to stimulus, carbohydrate metabolism, and energy reserve metabolism are induced. On the other hand, many genes encoding proteins involved in amino acid metabolism and oxidative phosphorylation are repressed. The result indicates an adaptive expression response in petite-negative mtDNA-less S. pombe strains.
###end p 11

Results

We extracted protein sequences of the DNA polymerase gamma (SPCC24B10.22, designated as pog1+ for Pol-gamma) using the S. pombe genome in the public database [14]. BLAST analysis of the S. pombe Pol-gamma revealed its homolog in various other species such as Homo sapiens, Rattus norvegicus, Mus musculus, Caenorhabditis elegans, Candida albicans, and S. cerevisiae. Phylogenic analysis based on protein sequences showed that S. pombe Pol-gamma, like these in S. cerevisiae or C. albicans, is very similar to those in mammalian systems (Figure 1A).

###xml 1175 1185 1161 1167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 1234 1236 1216 1218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1D</xref>
###xml 1351 1359 1329 1337 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe</italic>
###xml 1351 1359 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
To investigate the function of the Pol-gamma, we performed gene deletion analysis using a PCR-mediated approach [15]. Diploid transformants that formed colonies on plates without uracil supplement were isolated for further validation of gene disruption. The genotype of diploid pog1+/pog1Delta strains was validated by PCR-assays using sequence-specific primers (Data not shown). To further examine whether pog1+ is essential for viability, we performed tetrad dissection analysis. Of ~15 asci/tetrads dissected on YES plates, all tetrads displayed a 2:2 segregation of normal growing spores versus non-growing spores after growth at 30degreesC for ~5 days, except for 1 tetrad in which one of the wild type spores was implanted deep into the agar and thus impeded its normal growth (figure 1B and 1C). This result indicates that pog1+ is essential for vegetative growth. Consistent with this, spores that formed normal-sized colonies were unable to grow on uracil-dropout plates (Data not shown). PCR assays confirmed that those spores possessed a wild type allele of pog1+, but not a deletion allele (Data not shown). However, after extended incubation terms, we found the pog1Delta spores were able to form petite colonies (Figure 1D). This result indicates that Pol-gamma is not essential for viability, but is important for normal growth rate in S. pombe.
###end p 16

To investigate phenotypes of the pog1Delta strain, the mutant cells were subjected to DAPI and aniline staining to visualize DNA and cell wall, respectively. Wild type cells were cylindrical shaped ~4-5 mum in diameter and ~12-14 mum in length (Figure 2A). The pog1Delta strain were typically dumbbell shaped (Figure 2A, see number 1 and 2), had abnormally thick septa (Figure 2A, see number 2 and 3), multiple septa (Figure 2A, see number 2) and anucleate cells (Figure 2A, see number 3). The multiple phenotypes of the pog1Delta strain are consistent with the notion that mitochondria are "power plants" that generate energy to support many biochemical reactions and cellular processes.

###xml 144 148 140 144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1</italic>
###xml 148 149 144 145 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>

We next investigated the Pol-gamma subcellular localization in wild type cells. For this reason, we constructed a strain bearing a sole copy of pog1+-GFP that exhibited no abnormal growth phenotypes, suggesting that C-terminus tagging of Pol-gamma does not affect its biological function. The GFP-tagged Pol-gamma showed the subcellular localization resembling to that of mitochondria previously reported [16,17] (Figure 2B).

###begin p 21
###xml 102 104 102 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>

###xml 1278 1280 1242 1244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2D</xref>
Functional mitochondria are capable of mitotracker uptake, a mitochondrion-selective fluorescent dye [18]. To test whether mitochondria were defective in pog1Delta cells, we stained the cells with mitotracker fluorescent dyes. As shown in Figure 2C, mitotracker fluorescent dyes aggregated to outline the functional mitochondrial structures in wild type cells. On the other hand, pog1Delta cells failed to show any fluorescent dye uptake, indicating that mitochondria are not functional in pog1Delta cells (Figure 2C). Cells with mitochondrial defects would fail to grow in media containing only non-fermentable carbon sources such as ethanol or glycerol [13]. To examine the growth ability of the pog1Delta strain on non-fermentable carbon sources, serial dilution of wild type and pog1Delta cultures were inoculated on plates containing either fermentable carbon source (i.e., glucose) or non-fermentable carbon source (i.e., glycerol). Both wild type and pog1Delta cells were capable of growing on glucose medium, although pog1Delta cells grew substantially slower than wild type cells (Figure 2D). On the other hand, pog1Delta cells failed to grow on plates containing glycerol, consistent with the conclusion that mitochondria are not functional in pog1Delta cells (Figure 2D).
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 56 66 56 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 246 256 242 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 272 274 264 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2E</xref>
###xml 365 374 357 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916;</italic>
###xml 450 452 438 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2E</xref>
To investigate whether the mitochondrial malfunction in pog1Delta cells was associated with disrupted mitochondrial structures, we thus performed electron microscopic (EM) analysis. EM images clearly showed the nuclear structure in wild type and pog1Delta strains (Figure 2E). However, normal mitochondrial structures were only apparent in wild type cells, but not pog1Delta, suggesting that the mitochondrial architecture had been disrupted (Figure 2E). These results indicate that disruption of Pol-gamma is sufficient to abolish normal mitochondrial structure and consequently mitochondrial function.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
###xml 33 43 33 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
Mitochondrial DNA is depleted in pog1Delta cells
###end title 23
###begin p 24
To investigate the role of Pol-gamma in maintaining the copy number of mtDNA, we performed real-time quantitative PCR (qPCR) using the Syber Green assay on whole DNA extracted from various strains. Due to different efficiencies of primer pairs in qPCR assay, the identical concentration of template DNA could give rise to different Ct-values (threshold cycle) from different primer pairs. For adequate estimation of the copy number of the samples, multiple pairs of primers specific to either mtDNA or nuclear genome were utilized in the Syber Green assays. The median of the Ct values from ~40 primer pairs specific to mtDNA was used to approximate the copy number of mtDNA. On the other hand, the median Ct value of ~60 primer pairs specific to nuclear DNA was used to estimate the copy number of the nuclear DNA. The higher the copy number of the sample, the sooner accumulated product would be detected in the real-time qPCR assay as a significant increase in fluorescence, and the lower the Ct value.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 139 141 139 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3A</xref>
###xml 333 336 333 336 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6.6</sup>
###xml 470 472 470 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3A</xref>
###xml 782 791 782 791 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe </italic>
###xml 792 794 792 794 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 815 824 815 824 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe </italic>
###xml 782 790 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
###xml 815 823 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
In wild type cells, the median Ct value for the mtDNA and nuclear DNA was 15.0 and 21.6, respectively, using the Syber Green assay (Figure 3A, left panel). That is, the median Ct value of mtDNA was 6.6-cycles less than that of the nuclear DNA. For easy comparison, 6.6-cycles difference could be converted into ~96.6 (equivalent to 26.6)-fold difference. In other words, the copy number of mtDNA was ~96.6-fold higher than that of nuclear DNA in wild type cells (Figure 3A, right panel). This result indicates that there are ~100 copies of mtDNA in a haploid G1-phase cell (a G1-cell would contain 1 copy of nuclear DNA). Given that the cell-cycle length was mostly occupied by G2-phase (a haploid G2-cell would contain two copies of nuclear DNA) under optimal growth conditions in S. pombe [19], an active growing S. pombe cell would thus contain ~100-200 copies.
###end p 25

###xml 684 694 668 674 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 793 803 773 779 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
We next investigated the copy number of mtDNA in pog1Delta cells. A majority of pog1Delta cells were found to possess 2C-DNA contents, as seen in wild type cells (Data not shown), suggesting no changes in nuclear DNA profiles. According to the real-time qPCR assay, the median Ct value of mtDNA and nuclear DNA was found to be 29.8 and 21.6, respectively, in pog1Delta cells (Figure 3B). That is to say, the median Ct value of mtDNA was 8.2-cycles more than that of the nuclear DNA, opposite to those in wild type cells (Figure 3A). Therefore, the copy number of mtDNA was ~1/284 (or 2-8.2)-fold less than that of the nuclear DNA in pog1Delta cells (Figure 3B). Approximately 150-300 pog1Delta cells would share one copy of mtDNA. This result indicates that mtDNA was dramatically depleted in pog1Delta cells.
###end p 27

It has been reported that no mtDNA could be detected by Southern blot analysis in ptp2-1 and ptp1-1 cells [13]. We thus applied both strains as negative control for the Syber Green assay. As shown in Figure 3C and 3D, the median Ct value of mtDNA was much higher than that of nuclear DNA in either ptp2-1 or ptp1-1 strains, indicating that the Syber Green assay is adequate for determination of mtDNA and nuclear DNA copy numbers. Based on the assay, it was estimated that ~25-50 ptp2-1 or ptp1-1 cells would share one copy of mtDNA, suggesting that mtDNA was depleted in ptp mutant cells.

Enzymes involved in TCA cycle and cellular respiration are required to function in mitochondria. Many of these enzymes are encoded by nuclear DNA. ###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 18 27 18 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe </italic>
###xml 133 147 133 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 148 150 148 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 225 234 225 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe </italic>
###xml 241 243 241 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 353 361 349 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe</italic>
###xml 380 390 376 382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 458 460 450 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1D</xref>
###xml 465 467 457 459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1E</xref>
###xml 497 507 489 495 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 556 557 544 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 601 605 589 593 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptp </italic>
###xml 621 622 609 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 651 660 639 648 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe </italic>
###xml 4 17 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 18 26 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
###xml 127 132 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 133 146 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 225 233 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
###xml 353 361 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
###xml 651 659 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
The fission yeast S. pombe is known as the petite-negative that can not tolerate the lost of mtDNA, in contrast to the budding yeast S. cerevisiae [12]. However, some nuclear mutations are known to allow growth of mtDNA-less S. pombe cells [13]. In this study, we show that an evolutionarily conserved Pol-gamma is essential for maintenance of mtDNA in S. pombe. Spores bearing a pog1Delta allele form petite colonies after extended incubation terms (Figure 1D and 1E). We have further shown that pog1Delta cells exhibit multiple growth phenotypes (Figure 2) and are depleted of mtDNA, similar to the ptp mutants (Figure 3). Thus, the petite-negative S. pombe mtDNA-less cells do not cease to grow on medium containing fermentable carbon courses, although the growth is substantially slow and exhibit severe growth phenotypes.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 13 23 13 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 155 157 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2E</xref>
###xml 255 263 251 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe</italic>
###xml 398 400 394 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 401 403 397 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 409 417 405 413 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe</italic>
###xml 533 535 529 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 686 696 682 688 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 816 818 808 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2C</xref>
###xml 255 263 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
###xml 409 417 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
We show that pog1Delta cells depleted of mtDNA possess no normal mitochondrial structures when compared with cells containing mtDNA in EM analyses (Figure 2E). This result suggests that mtDNA is required for maintaining normal mitochondrial structures in S. pombe. Mitochondrial respiratory complexes including cytochrome c oxidase and ATPase are encoded by both nuclear and mitochondrial genomes [26,27]. In S. pombe, mtDNA encodes at least 3 subunits of the cytochrome c oxidase (COX) complex and 3 subunits of the ATPase complex [25]. Our result suggests that without these COX and ATPase subunits cells fail to assemble membranes with mitochondrial potential. Consistent with this, pog1Delta cells fail to uptake mitochondria-specific dye or mitotracker due to a lack of mitochondrial membrane potential (Figure 2C).
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 123 125 123 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3A</xref>
###xml 242 244 242 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 272 274 272 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 280 290 280 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 370 377 366 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptp1-1 </italic>
###xml 380 387 376 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptp2-1 </italic>
###xml 442 444 438 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3C</xref>
###xml 449 451 445 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3D</xref>
###xml 687 696 683 692 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe </italic>
###xml 769 779 761 767 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 801 811 789 795 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 961 971 945 951 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 1011 1015 991 995 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptp </italic>
###xml 1071 1081 1051 1057 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 1089 1093 1065 1069 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3B&#8211;D</xref>
###xml 687 695 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
We show that, on average, each haploid cell contains ~100-200 copies of mtDNA based on the real-time qPCR analysis (Figure 3A). This is consistent with the observation of number of mitochondria-specific GFP foci reported by Matsuyama et al. [17] and in this study (Figure 2B). In pog1Delta cells, the mtDNA drops to ~1/300-1/150 copies per cell. Similarly, the mtDNA in ptp1-1 or ptp2-1 cells also drops to ~1/50-1/25 copies per cell (Figure 3C and 3D). The result supports the notion that these strains are depleted of mtDNA. Residues detected by mtDNA-specific primers in the real-time qPCR assay are probably represented the non-specific products; or other nuclear DNA polymerases in S. pombe could inefficiently rescue the lack of Pol-gamma by replicating mtDNA in pog1Delta cells. Alternatively, pog1Delta spores may carry over the mtDNA from the diploid mother cells during sproulation. The mtDNA copy number is substantially diluted during the growth of pog1Delta cells. However, it is not clear why the ptp mutant strains display higher mtDNA background than the pog1Delta (Figure 3B-D).
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 306 308 306 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4E</xref>
###xml 480 482 480 482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 483 485 483 485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 924 925 924 925 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 973 986 965 978 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 973 986 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
Genome-wide expression profiling of mtDNA-less cells revealed that the nuclear genes encoding carbohydrate metabolism and response to stimulus are induced by at least 3-fold. This result suggests that cells with impaired mitochondrial function attempt to maintain their growth competence potential (Figure 4E). In addition, cells lacking mtDNA appear to undergo extensive oxidative metabolism, consistent with the notion that respiratory deficiency induces peroxisome biogenesis [22,23]. On the other hand, genes encoding proteins involved in amino acids metabolism and energy production are down-regulated. This result suggested that mtDNA-less cells are adapted for slow growth and repress genes that are involved in mitochondrial function. Indeed, the majority of the nuclear genes encoding enzymes involved in TCA cycle and almost all genes involved in cytochrome c reductase and oxidase complexes are repressed (Figure 5), similar to those seen in rhodegrees cells in S. cerevisiae.
###end p 43
###begin title 44
Conclusion
###end title 44
###begin p 45
###xml 88 98 84 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 169 177 161 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe</italic>
###xml 203 213 195 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pog1&#916; </italic>
###xml 386 388 374 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 389 391 377 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 532 550 520 538 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eg., S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 573 586 561 574 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eg., S. pombe</italic>
###xml 169 177 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
###xml 351 356 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 361 366 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 537 550 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 578 586 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
We have demonstrated that Pol-gamma is essential for mtDNA maintenance. Cells bearing a pog1Delta allele are viable but grow substantially slower in the petite-negative S. pombe. Expression profiling of pog1Delta and other mtDNA-less mutant cells indicates that genes involved in glycolytic pathways and stress response are induced, common to budding yeast and human cell line systems [22-24]. Taken together, we propose that a common theme of adaptive expression responses to the disruption of mtDNA occur in both petite-positive (eg., S. cerevisiae) and petite-negative (eg., S. pombe) cells.
###end p 45

The tube was then subjected to a brief centrifugation for 30 sec at 12 Krpm in a microcentrifuge. The cells were resuspended in 0.5-ml sorbitol buffer (1.2 M sorbitol/0.1 M EDTA) containing Zymolyase (US Biological, Swampscott, MA) at a final concentration of 2 mg/ml and incubated at 37degreesC for 30 min. The spheroplasts were collected by centrifugation at 14 Krpm for 5 min and resuspended in 0.5-ml TES buffer (50 mM Tris/500 mM EDTA/100 mM NaCl, pH 8.0). 50 mul of both 10% SDS and 20 mg/ml proteinase K (Roche, Mannheim, Germany) were added into the spheroplasts and incubated at 65degreesC for 30 min with gentle mixing in a thermomixer (Eppendorf AG, Hamburg, Germany) at 500-600 rpm. The tube was transferred onto ice. 200-ml 5 M KCl was later added into the tube, the contents mixed, and the sample incubated on ice for additional 30 min. Bulky proteins were removed by centrifugation at 14 Krpm for 30 min at 4degreesC. DNA was washed with 80% cold ethanol and air dried. Resuspended DNA was subjected to DNase-free RNase (Roche) treatment and then extraction with phenol and chloroform and ethanol-precipitation. Resuspended DNA was quantified using a NanoDrop UV photo-spectrometer (NanoDrop Technologies, Wilmington, DE).
###end p 63
###begin title 64
RNA extraction
###end title 64
###begin p 65
###xml 290 293 290 293 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
Total RNA was extracted using acid phenol and treated with RNase-free DNase (Ambion Inc., Austin, TX) for 20 min at room temperature. For RT-qPCR analysis, the RNA was subsequently used as template to synthesize cDNA using reverse transcriptase (Invitrogen Co., Carlsbad, CA) with poly-d(T)20 primers according to manufacturer's guide. cDNA was cleaned by extraction with phenol and precipitation using ethanol. Then RNA pellet was resuspended in DEPC-treated distilled water and quantified.
###end p 65
###begin title 66
Real-time qPCR analysis
###end title 66
###begin p 67
###xml 259 260 259 260 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 372 376 372 374 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174; </sup>
Aliquots of ~20-ng total DNA were added to each well in a 96-well plate containing Syber Green master mix (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA). Triplicate PCR reactions for each gene-specific primer pairs [for a list of primer sequences, see Additional File 2] were carried out according to manufacturer's instruction using a quantitative real-time PCR machine (ABI PRISM(R) Sequence Detection System, Applied Biosystems) with an analytic software SDS2.2 (Applied Biosystems). In brief, it was programmed for 1 cycle at 95degreesC for 10 min, followed by 40 cycles at 95degreesC for 10 min and 60degreesC for 1 min. An additional cycle of 15 sec at 95degreesC, 60degreesC, and 95degreesC each was used to monitor the dissociation dynamics of PCR products. Standardized cycle threshold (Ct) of each PCR reaction was collected if the dissociation curve indicated no multiple products.
###end p 67
###begin title 68
Microarray fabrication and hybridization
###end title 68
###begin p 69
###xml 513 515 513 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
50-mer oligonucleotides (Proligo Biochemie GmbH, Hamburg, Germany) at a normalized concentration of 40 nM each in 96-well plates were mixed with equal volumes of 0.3x SSC buffer and transferred to 384-well plates using a BioMed FX liquid handling robot (Beckman Coulter Inc., Fullerton, CA). The oligonucleotides were subsequently spotted onto poly-Lysine coated glass slides at random positions using a spotting machine (GeneMachines, San Carlos, CA). Spotted slides were post-processed as described previously [19] before hybridization with fluorescent dye-labeled cDNA.
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 445 449 444 446 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174; </sup>
Approximately 20 mug of total RNA were used to synthesize cDNA in presence of fluorescent dye Cy3- or Cy5-dUTP using a reverse transcriptase kit (Invitrogen). Labeled cDNA was cleaned by washing on a microcon-YM30 column (Millipore, Billerica, MA). Equal amounts of Cy3- and Cy5-labeled cDNA was pooled and co-hybridized to a microarray slide using a EasyHyb buffer (Roche, Basel, Switzerland) in a hybridization chamber (GeneMachines) or a MAUI(R) mixer (BioMicro Systems, Salt Lake City, UT) at 42degreesC overnight. The slide was subsequently washed in a series of buffers containing various concentration of SSC (2x - 0.2x) with or without 1% SDS and dried by centrifugation before scanning.
###end p 70
###begin title 71
Microarray data acquisition and processing
###end title 71
###begin p 72
Microarray slides were scanned using a GenePix scanner (Axon Instruments, Union City, CA) at wavelengths 635 and 532 mum with a resolution of 10 mum controlled by GenePix Pro4 softwares (Axon Instruments). GPR (GenePix Result) files were generated and globally normalized based on a median of ratios by GenePix Pro. Ratios (i.e., median of ratios) and absolute intensity (i.e., median of absolute intensity) of each spot were collected only if the absolute intensity of spots (in either channel) was 2-fold or higher than the background to ensure good data quality.
###end p 72
###begin title 73
Intensity normalization
###end title 73
###begin p 74
###xml 68 72 68 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cy3 </italic>
###xml 121 122 121 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</italic>
###xml 253 255 253 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C </italic>
###xml 267 268 267 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">k</italic>
###xml 282 284 282 284 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C </italic>
###xml 333 335 333 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">i </italic>
###xml 377 379 377 379 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n </italic>
###xml 420 422 420 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n </italic>
###xml 478 480 476 478 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">k </italic>
###xml 522 524 520 522 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">k </italic>
###xml 575 577 573 575 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">10</sub>
###xml 577 580 575 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cy3</italic>
###xml 704 706 698 700 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">10</sub>
###xml 706 709 700 703 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cy3</italic>
###xml 776 778 770 772 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 798 803 792 797 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cy3' </italic>
###xml 805 813 799 807 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">k &#183; Cy3 </italic>
###xml 817 831 811 825 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cy5' = k &#183; Cy5</italic>
###xml 1004 1008 998 1002 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cy3 </italic>
Intensity normalization assumed that the average absolute intensity Cy3 of individual features in an array is a constant C, for a series of microarray hybridizations with a common reference (labeled with Cy3-dye). Each array is related to this constant C by a factor k, i.e., where C is set to 2500 (arbitrary unit) for convenience, i is an individual feature on an array, and n is a total number of available features (n </= 9858). To remove outliers during determining factor k for an array, selected features in factor k estimation were only those whose log-intensity (log10Cy3) are within the 2sigma range, i.e., where  and  are estimated mean and standard deviation, assuming that log intensity (log10Cy3) of individual features on an array followed normal distribution [30]. Thus, normalized Cy3' = k . Cy3 and Cy5' = k . Cy5. This intensity normalization does not change ratio of individual features. However, it permits establishment and validation of relationship between the absolute intensity Cy3 (arbitrary unit) of individual microarrays.
###end p 74
###begin title 75
Lowess normalization
###end title 75
###begin p 76
###xml 90 92 90 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 93 95 93 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 274 276 274 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M </italic>
###xml 281 282 281 282 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 283 288 283 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ratio</italic>
###xml 368 370 368 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 392 394 392 394 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M </italic>
###xml 400 401 400 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</italic>
###xml 422 423 422 423 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 424 430 424 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ratio'</italic>
###xml 437 438 437 438 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 439 444 439 444 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ratio</italic>
###xml 446 449 446 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">- L</italic>
###xml 450 451 450 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</italic>
###xml 460 461 460 461 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L</italic>
###xml 462 463 462 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</italic>
###xml 527 529 527 529 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">f </italic>
###xml 582 583 582 583 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n</italic>
Normalizations using locally weighted linear regression and smooth scatter plot (Lowess) [31,32] were performed for each individual array to remove intensity-dependent dye-bias. An MA-plot is used to facilitate detecting intensity-dependent patterns in the log ratio, where M = log2(Ratio) and . The Lowess smoother, implemented in the statistical software package R [33], locally normalizes M in an A-dependent manner log2(Ratio') = log2(Ratio) - L(A), where L(A) is the Lowess fit to the MA-plot. The user defined parameters f (fraction of data used for smoothing at each point), nsteps (number of iterations used in Lowess fit) are 0.5 and 3, respectively.
###end p 76
