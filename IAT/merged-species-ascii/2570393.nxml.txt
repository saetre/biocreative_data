###begin article-title 0
Forkhead Transcription Factors (FoxOs) Promote Apoptosis of Insulin-Resistant Macrophages During Cholesterol-Induced Endoplasmic Reticulum Stress
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 42 75 42 75 <email xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">senokuchi@fc.kuh.kumamoto-u.ac.jp</email>

###end p 1

###end p 2
###begin p 3
###xml 0 10 0 10 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OBJECTIVE&#8212;</bold>
OBJECTIVE-Endoplasmic reticulum stress increases macrophage apoptosis, contributing to the complications of atherosclerosis. Insulin-resistant macrophages are more susceptible to endoplasmic reticulum stress-associated apoptosis probably contributing to macrophage death and necrotic core formation in atherosclerotic plaques in type 2 diabetes. However, the molecular mechanisms of increased apoptosis in insulin-resistant macrophages remain unclear.
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 0 28 0 28 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RESEARCH DESIGN AND METHODS&#8212;</bold>
###xml 131 136 131 136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ob/ob</italic>
###xml 137 141 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
RESEARCH DESIGN AND METHODS-The studies were performed in insulin-resistant macrophages isolated from insulin receptor knockout or ob/ob mice. Gain- or loss-of-function approaches were used to evaluate the roles of forkhead transcription factors (FoxOs) in endoplasmic reticulum stress-associated macrophage apoptosis.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 0 8 0 8 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RESULTS&#8212;</bold>
RESULTS-Insulin-resistant macrophages showed attenuated Akt activation and increased nuclear localization of FoxO1 during endoplasmic reticulum stress induced by free cholesterol loading. Overexpression of active FoxO1 or FoxO3 failed to induce apoptosis in unchallenged macrophages but exacerbated apoptosis in macrophages with an active endoplasmic reticulum stress response. Conversely, macrophages with genetic knockouts of FoxO1, -3, and -4 were resistant to apoptosis in response to endoplasmic reticulum stress. FoxO1 was shown by chromatin immunoprecipitation and promoter expression analysis to induce inhibitor of kappaBepsilon gene expression and thereby to attenuate the increase of nuclear p65 and nuclear factor-kappaB activity during endoplasmic reticulum stress, with proapoptotic and anti-inflammatory consequences.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 0 12 0 12 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CONCLUSIONS&#8212;</bold>
CONCLUSIONS-Decreased Akt and increased FoxO transcription factor activity during the endoplasmic reticulum stress response leads to increased apoptosis of insulin-resistant macrophages. FoxOs may have a dual cellular function, resulting in either proapoptotic or anti-inflammatory effects in an endoplasmic reticulum stress-modulated manner. In the complex plaque milieu, the ultimate effect is likely to be an increase in macrophage apoptosis, plaque inflammation, and destabilization.

###begin p 9
###xml 276 277 276 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 278 279 278 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 627 628 627 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 629 630 629 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 929 930 929 930 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 1265 1266 1265 1266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 0 8 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Patients</span>
###xml 962 970 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Patients with type 2 diabetes have a strikingly increased incidence of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease. In part, this reflects diabetic dyslipidemia characterized by increased VLDL and reduced HDL levels secondary to peripheral insulin resistance and hyperinsulinemia (1,2). Vascular insulin resistance may also play a role in the acceleration of atherosclerosis in diabetes. Decreased insulin signaling and Akt activity in vascular endothelium impair vascular nitric oxide responses and are associated with increased expression of inflammatory genes, vascular cell adhesion molecule-1, and accelerated atherosclerosis (3,4). Moreover, diabetic individuals are susceptible to complications of atherosclerotic plaque formation, reflecting plaque instability. Apoptosis of macrophages, smooth muscle cells, and endothelial cells in advanced atherosclerotic lesions is thought to lead to plaque rupture and atherothrombosis (5). Atherosclerotic lesions from patients with type 2 diabetes showed increased areas of necrotic core and increased numbers of apoptotic macrophages even after normalization for total lesion area, suggesting that macrophage apoptosis and resulting necrotic core formation may specifically contribute to plaque instability in diabetes (5).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 528 529 528 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 530 531 530 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 684 685 684 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 686 687 686 687 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 688 690 688 690 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 960 962 960 962 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 1368 1370 1368 1370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 1522 1524 1522 1524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 511 517 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 522 526 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
A variety of different processes contribute to macrophage apoptosis in advanced lesions, including growth factor withdrawal, hypoxia, and oxidative and other cellular stresses. Several of these factors mediate apoptosis by activating the cellular endoplasmic reticulum stress response. Increased expression of C/EBP homologous protein (CHOP, also known as Ddit3/Gadd153) is a hallmark of endoplasmic reticulum stress, and CHOP is expressed in a subset of macrophages in advanced atherosclerotic lesions in both humans and mice (6-8). The endoplasmic reticulum stressors in atherosclerotic plaques include free cholesterol (FC), 7-oxysterols, oxidized phospholipids, and homocysteine (6,9-11). The unfolded protein response (UPR) is an adaptive response to endoplasmic reticulum stress that involves decreased translation of most proteins but increased expression of a subset of molecules involved in protein refolding or other endoplasmic reticulum functions (12). The UPR involves a balance or pro- and antiapoptotic factors and only leads to apoptosis when endoplasmic reticulum stress is overwhelming or accompanied by additional apoptotic stimuli. Endoplasmic reticulum loading of free cholesterol induces CHOP but is insufficient to cause apoptosis unless accompanied by an additional stimulus, such as ligation of the scavenger receptor A (SRA) by modified LDL (13). The UPR is also associated with an increase in Akt phosphorylation and activity, which is thought to represent a compensatory prosurvival mechanism (14).
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 17 22 17 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ob/ob</italic>
###xml 204 206 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 309 313 309 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 313 316 313 316 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 327 331 327 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ldlr</italic>
###xml 331 334 331 334 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 475 477 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 480 484 480 484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 484 487 484 487 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 606 610 606 610 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 610 613 610 613 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 723 725 723 725 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 23 27 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 317 321 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Macrophages from ob/ob mice have decreased levels of insulin receptors and show decreased phosphorylation of insulin receptor substrate 2 in response to insulin, indicating macrophage insulin resistance (15,16). To model the vascular effects of macrophage insulin resistance, we transplanted bone marrow from Insr-/- mice into Ldlr-/- recipients. This was associated with a dramatic increase in necrotic core formation, reflecting increased numbers of apoptotic macrophages (14). Insr-/- macrophages showed increased susceptibility to apoptosis induced by loading with free cholesterol or oxidized LDL. In Insr-/- macrophages, there was a marked blunting of Akt phosphorylation in response to endoplasmic reticulum stress (14). Insulin signaling leads to a variety of progrowth, antiapoptotic responses, notably phosphorylation and nuclear exclusion of the closely related transcription factors forkhead transcription factor 01(FoxO1), -3, and -4. The goal of the present study was to evaluate the hypothesis that decreased Akt signaling and increased activity of FoxOs during the endoplasmic reticulum stress response are responsible for the increased apoptotic response of insulin-resistant macrophages.

###begin title 14
Primary macrophage culture and free cholesterol loading.
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 124 125 124 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 238 244 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Peritoneal macrophages were harvested by peritoneal lavage 3 days after intraperitoneal injection of concanavalin A (ConA) (6). Macrophages were maintained in culture medium (Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum and 20% L-cell conditioned medium) until 90% confluent. Bone marrow-derived macrophages were differentiated from marrow cells by culture in the aforementioned medium for 10-12 days. FC was loaded by incubating cells in culture medium containing 10 mug/ml ACAT inhibitor 58035 (Sigma) plus 100 mug/ml acetylated LDL for the indicated time periods.

###begin title 30
RESULTS
###end title 30
###begin title 31
###xml 40 44 40 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 44 45 44 45 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 45 46 45 46 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">/</sup>
###xml 46 47 46 47 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 52 57 52 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ob/ob</italic>
Myr-Akt rescues the apoptotic defect in Insr-/- and ob/ob macrophages.
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 80 84 80 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 84 87 84 87 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 114 118 114 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 118 121 118 121 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+</sup>
###xml 362 366 362 366 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 366 369 366 369 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 390 394 390 394 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 394 397 394 397 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+</sup>
###xml 429 431 429 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 592 596 592 596 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 596 599 596 599 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+</sup>
###xml 604 608 604 608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 608 611 608 611 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 625 626 625 626 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</italic>
###xml 619 626 619 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1<italic>A</italic></xref>
###xml 1102 1107 1090 1095 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ob/ob</italic>
###xml 1183 1188 1171 1176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ob/ob</italic>
###xml 1272 1273 1260 1261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B</italic>
###xml 1266 1273 1254 1261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1<italic>B</italic></xref>
###xml 1645 1646 1633 1634 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</italic>
###xml 1639 1646 1627 1634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1<italic>C</italic></xref>
We previously showed reduced induction of Akt phosphorylation during the UPR in Insr-/- macrophages compared with Insr+/+ macrophages. To ascertain whether this might contribute to enhanced apoptosis, we used adenovirus to overexpress a constitutively active form of Akt (Myr-Akt). FC loading using AcLDL+ an ACAT inhibitor (58035) led to increased apoptosis in Insr-/- cells compared with Insr+/+ cells, as reported previously (14). Myr-Akt expression resulted in a major reduction in apoptosis in response to FC loading in both sets of cells and virtually eliminated the difference between Insr+/+ and Insr-/- cells (Fig. 1A). Given that transduction efficiency of adenovirus was approximately60%, this indicates a substantial reversal of the effect of FC loading and insulin receptor deficiency on apoptosis. Myr-Akt expression did not affect the uptake of AcLDL or FC accumulation in macrophages (not shown). To assess whether similar responses could be observed in cells with less severe defects in insulin signaling, we also audited the antiapoptotic impact of activated Akt in insulin-resistant ob/ob macrophages. Enhanced apoptosis in response to FC loading was also seen in ob/ob macrophages, and this response was partly rescued by Myr-Akt overexpression (Fig. 1B). To reinforce the relevance of the phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase-Akt signaling axis in this physiological process, we assessed the impact of inhibition of endogenous Akt activity in FC-loaded macrophages, using the phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase inhibitor LY-294002. This treatment resulted in a pronounced increase in apoptosis in FC-loaded wild-type macrophages (Fig. 1C). These data suggest that induction of Akt activity during FC-induced endoplasmic reticulum stress provides an antiapoptotic function that is otherwise impaired in insulin-resistant macrophages.
###end p 32
###begin title 33
FoxO1 and FoxO3 promote macrophage apoptosis during the UPR.
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 193 195 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 267 268 267 268 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 435 437 435 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r23">23</xref>
###xml 438 440 438 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
###xml 608 612 608 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 612 615 612 615 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 635 636 635 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</italic>
###xml 629 636 629 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2<italic>A</italic></xref>
###xml 660 661 660 661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B</italic>
###xml 654 661 654 661 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2<italic>B</italic></xref>
###xml 695 700 695 700 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ob/ob</italic>
###xml 857 858 857 858 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</italic>
###xml 851 858 851 858 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2<italic>C</italic></xref>
Insulin signaling via phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase leads to Akt-mediated phosphorylation of FoxO1 and FoxO3 on serine residues, causing nuclear exclusion and loss of transcriptional activity (22). Additionally, FC loading of macrophages leads to activation of Jun NH2-terminal kinase (JNK), and in other settings, JNK can phosphorylate related FoxOs (FoxO4) on different amino acids, resulting in nuclear localization and activation (23,24). We observed increased nuclear FoxO1 as a result of FC loading or insulin receptor deficiency and an additive effect of FC loading and insulin receptor deficiency in Insr-/- macrophages (Fig. 2A shows FoxO1-GFP; Fig. 2B shows endogenous nuclear FoxO1). ob/ob macrophages also showed increased nuclear FoxO1 compared with wild-type macrophages, and again, FC loading further increased levels of nuclear FoxO1 (Fig. 2C). Thus, both decreased insulin signaling and FC-induced endoplasmic reticulum stress are associated with increased nuclear FoxO1.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 422 423 422 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</italic>
###xml 416 423 416 423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3<italic>A</italic></xref>
###xml 428 429 428 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B</italic>
###xml 858 860 858 860 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>

###xml 1007 1010 1007 1010 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>

To evaluate the effect of constitutively nuclear forms of FoxOs on apoptosis, we transduced macrophages with FoxO1 or FoxO3 in which Akt target serine residues had been mutated to alanine or aspartic acid (FoxO1-ADA and FoxO3-AAA). Whereas in control macrophages, there was only a slight increase in apoptosis, the constitutively active FoxOs resulted in a robust increase in apoptosis in the context of FC loading (Fig. 3A and B). These findings suggest that FoxO1 and FoxO3 have a major role in promoting apoptosis in cells undergoing the UPR in response to FC loading but not in unstressed cells. To determine whether this was a general feature of the endoplasmic reticulum stress response, we subjected macrophages to a different form of endoplasmic reticulum stress by treating them with thapsigargin, an agent that inhibits the endoplasmic reticulum Ca2+ pump sarcoplasmic/endoplasmic reticulum calcium ATPase (SERCA). First, we showed that thapsigargin caused an increased amount of apoptosis in Insr-/- relative to Insr+/- cells, consistent with our previous demonstration that Insr-/- cells are more susceptible to apoptosis induced by a variety of endoplasmic reticulum stress responses (Fig. 3C). Second, we showed a dramatic increase in apoptosis in cells treated with both FoxO1-ADA and thapsigargin but not in cells treated with either alone (Fig. 3D).

###xml 1363 1370 1363 1370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3<italic>C</italic></xref>
We next considered three potential mechanisms to explain the effect of active FoxOs: 1) induction of UPR genes, 2) enhancement of the effects of SRA ligation in the context of the UPR (25), and 3) modification of the apoptotic response to the UPR. Previous studies have shown that FoxO1 induces CHOP expression during differentiation of preadipocytes into adipocytes (26), suggesting that FoxO1-ADA could be enhancing apoptosis by increasing the UPR. However, CHOP mRNA and protein were not further induced by FoxO1-ADA or FoxO3-AAA in control, FC-loaded, or thapsigargin-treated macrophages (supplementary Fig. S3A-C; data not shown). A combination of UPR induction and ligation of the SRA (e.g., by the SRA ligand fucoidan) is required to see an apoptotic response in FC-loaded macrophages, and this response is much more prominent in Insr-/- than in Insr+/+ macrophages (25). If FoxO1-ADA were activating the UPR in cells undergoing FC loading by incubation with Ac-LDL + 58035, it would be anticipated that fucoidan would increase apoptosis in cells expressing FoxO1-ADA. However, the combination of FoxO1-ADA and fucoidan did not increase apoptosis (supplementary Fig. S3D). Moreover, in thapsigargin-treated Insr-/- cells, addition of the SRA ligand Ac-LDL did not lead to a further increase in the level of apoptosis beyond that observed in Insr+/- cells (Fig. 3C). Together, the findings tend to exclude mechanisms 1 and 2.

To see whether the IkappaBepsilon gene might be a direct target of FoxOs, we overexpressed FoxO1-ADA and FoxO3-AAA in cells transfected with an IkappaBepsilon promoter-luciferase reporter gene. A construct containing 295 bp of upstream sequence showed transactivation by FoxOs, whereas a construct containing only 159 bp of upstream sequence did not (Fig. 7A and B). ChIP analysis revealed occupancy of the -159 to -295 bp region of the IkappaBepsilon promoter by FoxO1 (Fig. 7C, top panel). We identified three putative FoxO binding core sequences [AAA(C/T)A] in this region, one of which at -260 is conserved among rodents, humans, and nonhuman primates. Moreover, the ChIP analysis showed binding of FoxO1 to the IkappaBepsilon promoter containing mutations in all three potential FoxO binding sites (Fig. 7C, bottom panel). Thus, FoxO1 transactivates the proximal IkappaBepsilon promoter likely by interacting with other molecules that directly bind the DNA in the -159 to -295 region. These findings are consistent with previous studies showing that a subset of FoxO1/3 target genes is induced without direct binding of FoxOs to target promoters (18,37,38).
###end p 45
###begin title 46
###xml 74 78 74 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 78 81 78 81 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
Decreased expression of inflammatory and antiapoptotic genes in FC-loaded Insr-/- macrophages.
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 104 108 104 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 108 111 108 111 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 125 127 125 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 128 130 128 130 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r39">39</xref>
###xml 454 458 446 450 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 458 461 450 453 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 493 499 485 491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f8" ref-type="fig">Fig. 8</xref>
Previous reports have suggested either increased apoptosis or decreased inflammatory gene expression in Insr-/- macrophages (14,39). The finding of reduced NF-kappaB activity in these cells suggested a common mechanism to explain both increased apoptosis and reduced inflammatory responses. Consistent with this idea, we found decreased expression of several antiapoptotic genes (known targets of NF-kappaB) and some but not all proinflammatory genes in Insr-/- cells treated with FC loading (Fig. 8).
###end p 47
###begin title 48
DISCUSSION
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 640 641 626 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 805 807 787 789 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
Our findings are consistent with the hypothesis that a blunted Akt response in insulin-resistant macrophages promotes apoptosis during the endoplasmic reticulum stress response and show that a major underlying mechanism involves increased activity of FoxO transcription factors. FoxO1 binds the promoter and induces expression of IkappaBepsilon, blunting the p65/NF-kappaB response to endoplasmic reticulum stress, with proapoptotic and anti-inflammatory consequences. These studies are likely relevant to the mechanisms of increased macrophage apoptosis, necrotic core formation, and plaque destabilization in type 2 diabetic individuals (5) and suggest that insulin-sensitizing agents, such as peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-gamma agonists that reduce macrophage insulin resistance in vivo (15), could have beneficial effects in diabetic at herosclerosis by promoting plaque stabilization.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 255 257 255 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r40">40</xref>
###xml 461 463 461 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r41">41</xref>
###xml 1076 1080 1076 1080 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 1080 1083 1080 1083 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1251 1253 1251 1253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r42">42</xref>
###xml 1272 1275 1272 1275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bim</italic>
###xml 1395 1399 1395 1399 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 1399 1402 1399 1402 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
The closely related FoxO transcription factors FoxO1, -3, and -4 have a variety of different roles in modulating cell cycle control, apoptosis, and antioxidant responses with substantial redundancy and variable effects dependent on cell type and context (40). A recent study has revealed fundamental and overlapping roles of FoxO1, -3, and -4 in cell cycle control, induction of antioxidant molecules, and protection from apoptosis in hematopoietic stem cells (41). In contrast, in the present study, macrophages with knockout of all three FoxOs showed increased survival during endoplasmic reticulum stress induced by FC loading or growth factor withdrawal. Overexpression of FoxOs in various immortalized cell lines has been shown to induce apoptosis, and FoxO1 and/or FoxO3 have been found to directly target a variety of proapoptotic factors, such as Bim, Fas-ligand, and TRAIL (tumor necrosis factor-related apoptosis-inducing ligand). However, increased expression of these molecules did not appear to be involved in the increased apoptosis shown by primary cultures of Insr-/- macrophages (C.P.L., unpublished data). CHOP induction of Bim increases apoptosis in response to the UPR in a variety of cell types, including peritoneal macrophages (42). Measurements of Bim mRNA and protein showed increased expression in response to FC loading, but this response was not increased further in Insr-/- macrophages (supplementary Fig. S6). Importantly, although FoxO1/3 overexpression or knockdown had little effect on macrophage apoptosis in the basal condition, there were major effects during the endoplasmic reticulum stress response, defining a physiological role of these transcription factors in modulating macrophage apoptosis as part of the UPR. Thus, our findings suggest that the outcome of FoxO-dependent transcription is critically dependent on the local context and that the definition of FoxO proteins as pro- or antiapoptotic is an oversimplification.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 241 243 237 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r43">43</xref>
###xml 331 333 323 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 378 382 370 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 382 385 374 377 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 596 610 578 582 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I&#954;B&#603;</italic>
At least a part of the role of FoxOs in promoting apoptosis during endoplasmic reticulum stress involved an attenuation of the increased NF-kappaB response that is characteristic of the UPR and has an antiapoptotic function in this context (43). The mechanism of increased NF-kappaB activity during the UPR is not well understood (12). However, the blunting of this response in Insr-/- cells appears to involve a specific increase in levels of IkappaBepsilon that likely retains p65 in the cytoplasm. Our studies establish the underlying molecular mechanism to be transactivation of the proximal IkappaBepsilon promoter via an indirect binding of FoxO1/3. However, this is not necessarily the only mechanism by which FoxOs influence apoptosis during the endoplasmic reticulum stress response, and this area warrants additional investigation.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 251 253 251 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r44">44</xref>
###xml 254 256 254 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r45">45</xref>
###xml 347 348 347 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 509 511 509 511 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r45">45</xref>
###xml 512 514 512 514 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r46">46</xref>
###xml 829 831 829 831 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 1157 1159 1157 1159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r47">47</xref>
###xml 1043 1049 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 1121 1129 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Our findings are likely relevant to the increased complications of atherosclerosis suffered by diabetic individuals. Increased numbers of apoptotic macrophages associated with large necrotic cores and thin caps are characteristic of unstable plaques (44,45), and unstable plaques show evidence of increased endoplasmic reticulum stress responses (7). A number of different potential endoplasmic reticulum stressors, including an increased content of free cholesterol, are likely relevant in advanced plaques (45,46). Although most of our studies were performed using the FC loading method to induce apoptosis, the increase in apoptosis of insulin-resistant macrophages during the endoplasmic reticulum stress response appears to be generic, whether induced by FC loading, oxidized LDL, thapsigargin, or growth factor withdrawal (14), and thus is likely relevant to increased atherosclerotic complications in diabetic individuals. Interestingly, administration of an ACAT (acyl-coenzyme A: cholesterol acyltransferase) inhibitor (pactimibe) to humans led to a worsening of coronary atherosclerosis, especially in diabetic patients and subjects with BMI >30 (47), suggesting direct relevance of mechanisms defined here to diabetic atherosclerosis.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 68 72 64 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 72 75 68 71 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 509 511 501 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 512 514 504 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r39">39</xref>
###xml 756 758 748 750 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r45">45</xref>
###xml 806 810 798 802 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insr</italic>
###xml 810 813 802 805 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 899 905 891 897 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f8" ref-type="fig">Fig. 8</xref>
###xml 478 484 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
Diminished levels of nuclear p65 and NF-kappaB activity observed in Insr-/- cells during the endoplasmic reticulum stress response were associated with both increased apoptosis and decreased expression of some inflammatory genes, reflecting the well-known role of NF-kappaB in inducing these genes. This could perhaps explain previous findings of both increased apoptosis and decreased inflammation in different studies involving insulin receptor-deficient bone marrow cells in murine atherosclerosis models (14,39). However, in the complex milieu of advanced atherosclerotic plaques, it is likely that increased apoptosis of macrophages is associated with a clearance defect of apoptotic cells, leading to postapoptotic necrosis and inflammatory changes (45). Also, it is notable that in the basal state, Insr-/- macrophages showed increased expression of a number of different inflammatory genes (Fig. 8). Overall, our studies suggest that insulin-resistant macrophages are more likely to undergo apoptosis and to promote plaque inflammation.
###end p 53
