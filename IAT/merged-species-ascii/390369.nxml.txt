ESF1 is required for 18S rRNA synthesis in Saccharomyces cerevisiae
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 95 115 95 115 <email xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">t.hughes@utoronto.ca</email>
To whom correspondence should be addressed. Tel: +1 416 946 8260; Fax: +1 416 978 8528; Email: t.hughes@utoronto.ca
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 137 161 137 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 137 161 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
We report that Esf1p (Ydr365cp), an essential, evolutionarily conserved nucleolar protein, is required for the biogenesis of 18S rRNA in Saccharomyces cerevisiae. Depletion of Esf1p resulted in delayed processing of 35S precursor and a striking loss of 18S rRNA. Esf1p physically associated with ribosomal proteins and proteins involved in 18S rRNA biogenesis. Consistent with its role in 18S rRNA biogenesis, Esf1p also physically associated with U3 and U14 snoRNAs, but did not appear to be a core component of the SSU processome. These data indicate that Esf1p plays a direct role in early pre-rRNA processing.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 282 306 282 306 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 308 309 308 309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c1">1</xref>
###xml 310 311 310 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c2">2</xref>
###xml 665 666 665 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f1">1</xref>
###xml 102 107 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 111 117 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 276 281 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 282 306 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
Ribosome biosynthesis is one of the major metabolic activities in cells, and is highly conserved from yeast to humans. It occurs mainly in the nucleolus, a specialized compartment in the nucleus. Current knowledge of ribosome biogenesis is largely derived from studies of the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae (1,2). This process starts with the transcription of ribosomal RNA (rRNA) genes by RNA polymerase I and III into 35S pre-rRNA and 5S pre-rRNA. The 35S precursor is extensively modified and cleaved into the mature 18S, 5.8S and 25S rRNA through the coordinated action of a variety of endonucleases, exonucleases, RNA helicases and other protein factors (Fig. 1).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 121 122 121 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c1">1</xref>
###xml 123 124 123 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c2">2</xref>
###xml 183 184 183 184 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 187 188 187 188 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 194 195 194 195 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 197 198 197 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c1">1</xref>
###xml 199 200 199 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c3">3</xref>
###xml 501 502 501 502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c4">4</xref>
###xml 872 873 872 873 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c5">5</xref>
###xml 977 978 977 978 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c6">6</xref>
The U3 small nucleolar RNA (snoRNA), together with associated proteins, plays a central role in the 18S rRNA biogenesis (1,2) and is essential for the early cleavage events at sites A0, A1 and A2 (1-3). Recently, protein complexes that contain the U3 snoRNA and participate in the biogenesis of 40S ribosome subunits have been identified. Dragon and colleagues identified a large nucleolar ribonucleoprotein (RNP), called the small subunit (SSU) processome, required for 18S ribosomal RNA biogenesis (4). It contains the U3 snoRNA and at least 28 proteins, including 17 polypeptides (Utp1-17, for 'U three protein') that had not previously been shown to be associated with U3 or implicated in pre-rRNA processing events. We have recently discovered that the SSU processome consists of at least three distinct 'sub-complexes' and each physically associates with U3 snoRNA (5). Grandi and colleagues reported 90S pre-ribosomes formed at very early stages of ribosome biogenesis (6). The purified 90S pre-ribosomes contained not only the U3-specific proteins, but also other nucleolar proteins with a known role in 18S rRNA processing and 40S subunit assembly and additional uncharacterized proteins. However, seven components identified in the SSU processome (Snu13p, Utp3p, Utp5p, Utp7p, Utp11p, Utp14p and Utp16p) were not found in the isolated 90S pre-ribosomes.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 102 103 102 103 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 109 110 109 110 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 161 162 161 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c7">7</xref>
###xml 163 164 163 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c9">9</xref>
###xml 411 412 411 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c4">4</xref>
###xml 413 414 413 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c6">6</xref>
###xml 480 481 480 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c4">4</xref>
In addition to the U3 snoRNA, snoRNAs U14, snR10 and snR30 are required for early cleavages at sites A1 and A2 and hence also contribute to 18S rRNA biogenesis (7-9). However, the protein complexes containing these RNAs are less well-characterized than the U3-containing SSU processome. Furthermore, despite the application of several proteomic approaches to characterize early pre-ribosomal protein complexes (4-6) it is likely that not all proteins involved have been detected (4).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 137 139 137 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c10">10</xref>
###xml 163 170 163 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">YDR365c</italic>
###xml 271 273 271 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c11">11</xref>
###xml 352 354 352 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c10">10</xref>
###xml 533 535 533 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c12">12</xref>
###xml 536 538 536 538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c13">13</xref>
###xml 596 603 596 603 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">YDR365c</italic>
###xml 650 651 650 651 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 654 655 654 655 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 661 662 661 662 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 854 858 854 858 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESF1</italic>
###xml 88 93 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
We recently carried out a large-scale survey of non-coding RNA processing phenotypes in yeast mutants using a microarray-based approach (10). A mutated version of YDR365c, a gene that is co-regulated with established rRNA processing factors at the transcriptional level (11), displayed an array phenotype suggesting involvement in ribosome biogenesis (10). Ydr365cp has not been identified in the protein complexes described above, although it has been associated with different rRNA processing factors in other large-scale studies (12,13). Here, we provide genetic and biochemical evidence that YDR365c is required for the early cleavages at sites A0, A1 and A2 that lead to 18S rRNA synthesis, although it does not appear to be a core component of the SSU proces some. Owing to its apparently direct role in 18S rRNA biogenesis, we have named the gene ESF1 (Eighteen S rRNA Factor 1).
###end p 7

###begin title 15
Affinity purification and mass spectrometry analysis
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 58 59 58 59 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">7</sup>
###xml 491 492 491 492 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g</italic>
###xml 805 807 789 791 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c10">10</xref>
###xml 945 947 929 931 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c16">16</xref>
###xml 226 232 <span type="species:ncbi:13443">coffee</span>
Four liters of YNJK654 cells were grown in YPD to 1.5 x 107 cells/ml, then harvested. The cell pellets were washed twice with cold water and frozen with liquid nitrogen. Frozen cell pellets were broken with dry ice in a small coffee grinder (Krups), and 10 ml IPP150 buffer (10 mM Tris pH 8.0, 150 mM NaCl and 0.1% Triton X-100) plus 1 mM DTT and protease inhibitors were added to the lysed cells. The broken cells were subjected to centrifugation at 13 000 r.p.m. in an SS-34 rotor (20 200 g) for 1 h at 4degreesC. The lysates were mixed with 200 microl IgG agarose beads for 2 h at 4degreesC. After five washes with 0.5 ml IPP buffer, the IgG beads were collected and the RNA associated with the beads extracted with hot phenol and RNase-free DNase I was used to remove DNA in the samples as described (10). For the purification of protein complexes, the eluates from IgG columns were further purified with calmodulin as previously described (16). The purified proteins were separated by sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) on gels containing 10% polyacrylamide, visualized by silver staining and identified by MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry.

RESULTS
###end title 17
###begin title 18
Esf1p is an essential, evolutionarily conserved, nucleolar protein
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESF1</italic>
###xml 359 384 359 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</italic>
###xml 386 406 386 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana</italic>

###xml 359 384 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
###xml 386 406 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
###xml 408 431 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 433 450 <span type="species:ncbi:5141">Neurospora crassa</span>
###xml 452 475 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 477 489 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus musculus</span>
###xml 494 506 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Homo sapiens</span>
ESF1 encodes a protein of 628 amino acids with a calculated molecular weight of 72.4 kDa and pI of 4.99. The encoded Esf1 polypeptide has a coiled-coil region located between residue 426 and 492, and a bipartite nuclear localization signal near its N-terminus (from residue 447 to 464). We identified clear sequence counterparts in other organisms, including Schizosaccharomyces pombe, Arabidopsis thaliana, Drosophila melanogaster, Neurospora crassa, Drosophila melanogaster, Mus musculus and Homo sapiens. The multiple sequence alignment reveals what appear to be several discrete conserved regions (Fig. 2). In a systematic deletion analysis, Giaever and colleagues (20) reported that ESF1 is essential for viability. The protein has been localized to the nucleus, possibly enriched in the nucleolus, by three independent studies (21-23).

ESF1 is required for 18S rRNA synthesis and for cleavage of the pre-rRNA at sites A0, A1 and A2
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 49 53 49 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tetO</italic>
###xml 53 54 53 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">7</italic>
###xml 53 54 53 54 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>7</italic></sub>
###xml 55 59 55 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESF1</italic>
###xml 104 106 104 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c10">10</xref>
###xml 394 395 394 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f3">3</xref>
###xml 465 466 465 466 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 472 473 472 473 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 475 476 475 476 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 581 582 581 582 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 657 661 657 661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tetO</italic>
###xml 661 662 661 662 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">7</italic>
###xml 661 662 661 662 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>7</italic></sub>
###xml 663 667 663 667 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESF1</italic>
###xml 823 824 823 824 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 884 885 884 885 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 888 889 888 889 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 895 896 895 896 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 898 899 898 899 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c2">2</xref>
###xml 927 931 927 931 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESF1</italic>
###xml 978 979 978 979 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 982 983 982 983 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 989 990 989 990 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Our previous microarray study indicated that the tetO7-ESF1 mutant had a defect in 18S rRNA biogenesis (10). To investigate this further, we analyzed the steady-state levels of mature and precursor rRNA molecules by northern blotting. Probes specific for mature 18S and 25S rRNAs confirmed that the level of 18S rRNA was dramatically reduced upon depletion of Esf1p, relative to 25S rRNA (Fig. 3). Hybridizing the membrane with oligonucleotides complementary to D-A2 and A2-A3 regions (probes DA2 and A2A3) revealed that 20S pre-rRNA (the direct precursor to 18S rRNA) and the 27SA2 pre-rRNA (normally a precursor to the 25S rRNA), were not detected in the tetO7-ESF1 mutant. We also observed accumulation of 35S pre-rRNA and appearance of aberrant 23S pre-rRNA in the mutant. The 23S pre-rRNA is generated by cleavage at A3 in the 35S precursor without the early cleavages at sites A0, A1 and A2 (2). These data indicate that ESF1 is required for pre-rRNA processing at sites A0, A1 and A2 that lead to production of the mature 18S rRNA.

###xml 278 282 266 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESF1</italic>
###xml 581 582 569 570 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 614 615 602 603 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 701 702 689 690 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f4">4</xref>
###xml 763 764 751 752 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f3">3</xref>
To further confirm that depletion of Esf1p affects pre-rRNA processing, we carried out pulse-chase labeling of the pre-rRNA with [methyl-3H]methionine. In wild-type cells, the 35S precursor was processed into 25S and 18S rRNAs in approximately5 min (Fig. 4). In contrast, tetO7-ESF1 mutant contained very little 20S pre-rRNA and 18S rRNA at any time point examined (up to 15 min). The formation of 25S rRNA was delayed, but it was produced much more rapidly than 18S rRNA. Processing of the 35S precursor was also delayed in the Esf1p-depleted strain, perhaps because cleavage at A3 in the absence of cleavage at A2 is not the primary pathway. This would also explain the delay in 25S formation (Fig. 4), and the slight reduction in steady-state 27S levels (Fig. 3).
###end p 22
###begin title 23
Depletion of Esf1 does not affect the steady-state levels of box C/D snoRNAs U3, U14 and H/ACA snoRNAs snR10, snR30
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 135 136 135 136 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 139 140 139 140 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 145 146 145 146 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 148 149 148 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c3">3</xref>
###xml 150 151 150 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c7">7</xref>
###xml 152 153 152 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c9">9</xref>
###xml 215 219 215 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tetO</italic>
###xml 219 220 219 220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">7</italic>
###xml 219 220 219 220 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>7</italic></sub>
###xml 221 225 221 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESF1</italic>
###xml 259 260 259 260 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f5">5</xref>
###xml 266 273 266 273 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 302 306 302 306 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ESF1</italic>
To assess whether Esf1 is involved in the biogenesis or maintenance of the snoRNAs U3, U14, snR10 and snR30 [required for cleavage at A0, A1 or A2 (3,7-9)], the steady-state levels of each RNA were monitored in the tetO7-ESF1 mutant strain. As seen in Figure 5, the in vivo levels of these RNAs in the ESF1 mutant were similar to wild type. This suggests that ESF1 is not involved in biogenesis or stability of these snoRNAs.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Esf1p associates with ribosomal proteins, proteins required for small-subunit biogenesis and U3 and U14 snoRNAs
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 19 21 19 21 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c15">15</xref>
###xml 202 203 202 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f6">6</xref>
###xml 304 305 304 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c2">2</xref>
###xml 306 307 306 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c5">5</xref>
###xml 308 310 308 310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c24">24</xref>
###xml 311 313 311 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c26">26</xref>
Using the TAP tag (15), we affinity-purified Esf1p to ascertain which proteins and RNAs were physically associated. Esf1p-TAP co-purified with a number of proteins involved in ribosome biogenesis (Fig. 6), including Nsr1p, Krr1p, Nop1p, Utp22p and Puf6p, all of which are involved in 18S rRNA synthesis (2,5,24-26). In addition, Esf1p-TAP purified with a number of proteins that are components of the ribosome (Rps1p, Rps3p, Rps4p, Rps6p, Rps11p, Rpl2p, Rpl3p, Rpl4p, Rpl7p, Rpl10p, Rpl20p and Rpp0p).
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 238 239 238 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c2">2</xref>
###xml 240 241 240 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c4">4</xref>
###xml 242 243 242 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c5">5</xref>
###xml 371 372 371 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f7">7</xref>
###xml 663 664 663 664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c5">5</xref>
###xml 1057 1058 1057 1058 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c5">5</xref>
###xml 1125 1126 1125 1126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c4">4</xref>
To assay RNAs associated with Esf1p-TAP, RNA was phenol-extracted directly from washed IgG beads. As positive controls, we also affinity-purified Utp18p-TAP, Utp22p-TAP and Nop58p-TAP, which are known to be associated with the U3 snoRNA (2,4,5). Northern blotting revealed that Esf1p-TAP, as well as the three positive controls, co-purified with U3 and U14 snoRNAs (Fig. 7). The protein-RNA associations were stable after washes in 400 mM NaCl. In contrast, TAP-purifications of two other proteins (Krr1p and Nop14p), which are also involved in 18S rRNA biogenesis, did not contain U3 and U14 snoRNAs in the precipitates, in agreement with previous observations (5). Other negative controls (a wild-type strain with no tag and other unrelated proteins Ths1p-TAP, Pus1p-TAP and Gln1p-TAP) also failed to precipitate appreciable amounts of these RNAs. We also detected U14 snoRNA in the Utp18p-TAP and Utp22p-TAP pulldowns, even under very high stringent tandem affinity purifications in which a high-speed centrifugation step was added to the TAP procedure (5), which we note conflicts with previously published observations (4).
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 41 42 41 42 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 55 56 55 56 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 92 93 92 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f7">7</xref>
###xml 204 205 204 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c5">5</xref>
We also detected trace amount of 5'-ETS-A0 and 5'-ETS-A1 in the Esf1p-TAP precipitate (Fig. 7), which we have previously shown to associate with components of the SSU processome in the TAP purifications (5). However, we did not detect 20S precursor in the Esf1p immunoprecipitates (data not shown).

###begin title 29
DISCUSSION
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 167 168 167 168 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 219 220 219 220 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 253 254 253 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f3">3</xref>
###xml 389 390 389 390 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sub>
###xml 393 394 393 394 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 400 401 400 401 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 403 404 403 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c2">2</xref>
###xml 587 588 587 588 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 595 596 595 596 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f3">3</xref>
###xml 726 727 726 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f4">4</xref>
We present several lines of evidence that Esf1p is involved directly in 18S rRNA biogenesis. First, depletion of Esf1p led to a virtually complete loss of 20S and 27SA2 precursors, suggesting that the cleavage at site A2 was blocked in the mutant (Fig. 3). In addition, the aberrant 23S RNA species and 35S precursor accumulated in the mutant, indicating inhibition of cleavage events at A0, A1 and A2 (2). Although 18S rRNA biogenesis was affected in the mutant, 25S rRNA levels remained largely normal, suggesting that the 5.8S and 25S rRNAs are formed normally following cleavage at A3 (Fig. 3). Studies of kinetics of rRNA formation in the mutant also suggested that Esf1p mainly affected the biogenesis of 18S rRNA (Fig. 4).
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 167 168 167 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c4">4</xref>
###xml 226 227 226 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c2">2</xref>
###xml 316 318 316 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c24">24</xref>
###xml 360 361 360 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c5">5</xref>
###xml 402 403 402 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c5">5</xref>
###xml 505 506 505 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c6">6</xref>
###xml 514 515 514 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f7">7</xref>
###xml 559 561 559 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c25">25</xref>
###xml 562 564 562 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c26">26</xref>
###xml 691 693 691 693 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c25">25</xref>
###xml 694 696 694 696 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c26">26</xref>
###xml 754 755 754 755 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c5">5</xref>
###xml 756 758 756 758 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c10">10</xref>
###xml 821 823 821 823 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c27">27</xref>
###xml 953 954 953 954 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c5">5</xref>
###xml 932 937 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Among the proteins that co-purified with Esf1p, several are involved in 18S rRNA biogenesis. The nucleolar protein Nop1p is a component of the SSU processome complex (4). Nop1p is also a core component of all Box C/D snoRNPs (2), which include both U3 and U14 snoRNPs. Krr1p is required for 40S ribosome biogenesis (24) and subsequent export to the cytoplasm (5) and was found in the 90S pre-ribosome (5), although in our hands it does not co-purify with U3 or U14 RNAs, consistent with previous results (6) (Fig. 7). Nsr1p, a homolog of mammalian nucleolin (25,26), contains two RRMs (RNA recognition motifs) and a glycine/arginine-rich (GAR) domain. It is required for 18S rRNA synthesis (25,26). Utp22p is also involved in maturation of pre-18S rRNA (5,10) and has been described as a component of the SSU processome (27), although we have previously identified Utp22p in a distinct processome 'sub-complex' containing Rrp7p and yeast casein kinase (5). Aside from Utp22p and Nop1p, we did not detect association of Esf1p with any of the components of the SSU processome, indicating that it is not a core component of this large RNP.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 167 169 167 169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c21">21</xref>
###xml 261 262 261 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c2">2</xref>
###xml 263 264 263 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c4">4</xref>
###xml 265 267 265 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c28">28</xref>
###xml 268 270 268 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c29">29</xref>
###xml 338 342 338 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HCA4</italic>
###xml 370 372 370 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c28">28</xref>
###xml 424 426 424 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c29">29</xref>
###xml 509 510 509 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c4">4</xref>
###xml 511 512 511 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c6">6</xref>
###xml 567 568 567 568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c4">4</xref>
###xml 617 618 617 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c6">6</xref>
Hazbun and colleagues previously identified an Esf1p-associated complex which differs dramatically from ours, containing Bfr2p, Enp2p, Hca4p, Lcp5p, Nop58p and Utp9p (21). Among these proteins, Hca4p, Lcp5p, Nop58p and Utp9p are involved in 18S rRNA synthesis (2,4,28,29). Hca4p is a putative DEAD box RNA helicase and over-expression of HCA4 can suppress a U14 mutant (28). Lcp5p was found to be associated with U3 snoRNA (29) and Nop58p and Utp9p were found in both the SSU processome and 90S pre-ribosome (4,6), although Lcp5p was not found in the SSU processome (4). Bfr2p was also found in the 90S pre-ribosome (6). Hence, Hazbun and colleagues' data also support the general conclusion that Esf1p is involved in small-subunit biogenesis, although there is a complete lack of overlap with the specific associations we observed.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 159 160 159 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c4">4</xref>
###xml 161 163 161 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c27">27</xref>
###xml 171 172 171 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f6">6</xref>
###xml 242 243 242 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f7">7</xref>
###xml 435 440 435 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 443 445 443 445 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh518c21">21</xref>
###xml 947 948 947 948 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh518f2">2</xref>
We propose that Esf1p-containing complexes may be dynamic. Although Esf1p co-purifies with two individual components of the SSU processome (Utp22p and Nop1p) (4,27) (Fig. 6), as well as the U3 and U14 snoRNAs and the 5'-ETS of the rRNA (Fig. 7), all of the associations appear to be sub-stoichiometric, with the possible exception of the ribosomal proteins themselves. This is also clearly the case for the complex described in Hazbun et al. (21). Dynamic complexes might explain why different laboratories obtain quite different results from affinity purifications, since the associated proteins might depend upon the growth phase at harvest, the extraction procedure and the purification protocol used. The presence of Esf1p in multiple pre-ribosomal processing intermediates might also underlie its association with protein components of both the large and small ribosomal subunits. Analysis of mutants in the conserved features of Esf1p (Fig. 2) might help resolve the biochemical role of Esf1p in 18S biogenesis: presumably, they represent discrete domains that mediate physical interactions and/or catalytic functions.
###end p 33

 The major pre-rRNA processing pathway in S.cerevisiae. The primary transcript contains the sequences of mature 18S, 5.8S and 25S rRNA separated by two internal transcribed spacers (ITS1 and ITS2) and flanked by two external transcribed spacers (5'-ETS and 3'-ETS). Pre-ribosomal RNA processing is initiated by a cleavage in the 3'-ETS by the endoribonuclease Rnt1p. Following 3'-ETS processing, the 35S precursor is cleaved at A0 and A1, forming the 32S precursor. Cleavage at A2 then splits the 32S precursor into the 20S and 27SA2 precursors. The 20S precursor is exported to the cytoplasm and cleaved at site D, leading to the mature 18S rRNA. The 27SA2 precursor is processed into 27SB precursor. The 27SB precursor is then cleaved at sites C1 and C2 to generate the mature 25S rRNA and the 7S precursor, which is then processed by the exosome into mature 5.8S rRNA.
