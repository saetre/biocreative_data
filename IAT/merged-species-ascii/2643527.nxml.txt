###begin article-title 0
Hsp40 Couples with the CSPalpha Chaperone Complex upon Induction of the Heat Shock Response
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: NOA JEAB. Performed the experiments: SJG BB JP TN. Analyzed the data: SJG BB APB NOA JEAB. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: KEB XZ APB. Wrote the paper: JEAB.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 849 850 833 834 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">s</sub>
###xml 1195 1196 1155 1156 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">s</sub>
In response to a conditioning stress, the expression of a set of molecular chaperones called heat shock proteins is increased. In neurons, stress-induced and constitutively expressed molecular chaperones protect against damage induced by ischemia and neurodegenerative diseases, however the molecular basis of this protection is not known. Here we have investigated the crosstalk between stress-induced chaperones and cysteine string protein (CSPalpha). CSPalpha is a constitutively expressed synaptic vesicle protein bearing a J domain and a cysteine rich "string" region that has been implicated in the long term functional integrity of synaptic transmission and the defense against neurodegeneration. We have shown previously that the CSPalpha chaperone complex increases isoproterenol-mediated signaling by stimulating GDP/GTP exchange of Galphas. In this report we demonstrate that in response to heat shock or treatment with the Hsp90 inhibitor geldanamycin, the J protein Hsp40 becomes a major component of the CSPalpha complex. Association of Hsp40 with CSPalpha decreases CSPalpha-CSPalpha dimerization and enhances the CSPalpha-induced increase in steady state GTP hydrolysis of Galphas. This newly identified CSPalpha-Hsp40 association reveals a previously undescribed coupling of J proteins. In view of the crucial importance of stress-induced chaperones in the protection against cell death, our data attribute a role for Hsp40 crosstalk with CSPalpha in neuroprotection.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 356 359 356 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Bechtold1">[1]</xref>
###xml 361 364 361 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Chen1">[2]</xref>
###xml 1107 1110 1107 1110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Morano1">[3]</xref>
###xml 1794 1797 1794 1797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Muchowski1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1798 1801 1798 1801 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Brown1">[6]</xref>
###xml 2015 2018 2015 2018 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Brodsky1">[7]</xref>
In response to a range of stressful stimuli including hyperthermia and ischemia, an ancient evolutionarily conserved cellular program called the heat shock response is activated and the expression of several chaperones is induced to enhance cell survival to subsequent insults. The heat shock response also involves the translocation of several chaperones [1], [2]. Although the mechanistic basis of chaperone cytoprotection is not yet understood, the chaperone anti-apoptotic activity is thought to be due to the ability of chaperones to rid the cell of misfolded proteins. In addition to the stress-induced chaperones, many molecular chaperones are expressed constitutively and are widely held to have basic and indispensable functions in maintaining protein conformation. Constitutive and stress-induced chaperones maintain an important balance in the cell between protein refolding and protein elimination, however, while transient up-regulation of molecular chaperones is critical for cell survival, chronic up-regulation of chaperones (eg Hsp70) correlates with poor prognosis in some types of cancer [3]. Neurons are thought to be especially vulnerable to the risk of protein misfolding due to the conformational flexibility required of the cellular machinery underlying synaptic transmission. Furthermore, post-mitotic cells, such as neurons, cannot dilute aggregated proteins through cell division, leading to the potential accumulation of misfolded proteins. Huntington's, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's and Prion diseases are caused by defects in protein folding, underlining the biological importance of the problem of aberrant protein folding in neurons. In experimental models, molecular chaperones, either constitutive or stress-induced, are inhibitors of neurodegeneration (reviewed: [4]-[6]). Pharmacological modulators that directly regulate chaperone and stress-induced chaperone activity have been identified, emphasizing the potential of the heat shock response as a pharmaceutical target (Reviewed: [7]).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 626 629 618 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Braun1">[8]</xref>
###xml 630 634 622 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Natochin1">[11]</xref>
###xml 754 757 742 745 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Braun1">[8]</xref>
###xml 759 763 747 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Tobaben1">[10]</xref>
###xml 765 769 753 757 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Magga1">[12]</xref>
Cysteine string protein (CSPalpha) is a synaptic vesicle protein bearing a signature J domain and a cysteine rich string region that is implicated in the defense against neurodegeneration. The cytosolic proteins Hsc70 (heat shock cognate protein of 70 kDa), SGT (small glutamine rich tetratricopeptide repeat domain protein) and HIP (Hsc70 interacting protein) form a complex with CSPalpha which is tethered to the synaptic vesicle. Assembly of the chaperone components is thought to allow for localized activation of Hsc70, a cytosolic ATPase that couples energy from ATP hydrolysis to conformational work on target proteins [8]-[11]. The assembly of the CSPalpha chaperone complex is nucleotide sensitive, emphasizing the dynamic nature of the complex [8], [10], [12]. Why a specialized synaptic vesicle chaperone system evolved remains a mystery.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 720 724 704 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Braun2">[13]</xref>
###xml 726 730 710 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Bai1">[14]</xref>
###xml 1117 1125 1093 1101 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vitro</italic>
###xml 1209 1210 1177 1178 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">s</sub>
The existence of the heat shock response raises several questions. How do stress-induced chaperones protect synaptic transmission and prevent neurodegeneration? Is there crosstalk between stress-induced chaperones and constituitively expressed chaperones or do these chaperone machines have separate non-overlapping cellular tasks? As a first step toward testing the hypothesis that stress-induced chaperones are coupled to the CSPalpha chaperone system, we evaluated the components of the CSPalpha complex during transient expression of stress chaperones. CSPalpha expression levels do not increase in response to heat shock or geldanamycin treatment in neural cell lines. The previously reported 70 kDa CSPalpha dimer [13], [14] was reduced following heat shock. Suprisingly our findings demonstrate that the stress-induced J protein Hsp40 becomes a major component of the CSPalpha multimeric complex after heat shock. Following heat shock, Hsp40 expression is increased and Hsp40 localizes to the plasma membrane. Geldanamycin, like heat shock, triggers the assembly of Hsp40 with the CSPalpha multimeric complex. In vitro, the stimulatory effect of CSPalpha on the steady-state hydrolysis of GTP by Galphas is enhanced in the presence of Hsp40. Furthermore, transient transfection of CSPalpha or induction of the heat shock response increased isoproterenol-stimulated phosphorylation of synapsin.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
Our results suggest that the transient assembly of Hsp40 with the CSPalpha complex is important in the maintenance of synaptic function in the face of environmental stress, and emphasize the complexity and functional elegance of the J protein chaperone machines.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Results
###end title 8
###begin title 9
CSPalpha expression is not altered in response to heat shock
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 381 385 363 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Cordeiro1">[15]</xref>
###xml 386 390 368 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Yamada1">[17]</xref>
###xml 405 409 387 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Bowyer1">[18]</xref>
###xml 423 427 405 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Zhang1">[19]</xref>
###xml 729 733 703 707 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Braun2">[13]</xref>
###xml 735 743 709 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004595-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 123 128 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 540 543 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
To begin to test the role of CSPalpha in the heat shock response, the expression of CSPalpha and Hsp70 was examined in CAD mouse neuroblastoma cells before and after 40 min of conditioning heat shock at 42degreesC. CSPalpha is constitutively expressed in brain and exocrine/endocrine secretory tissues, however, its expression has been reported to be influenced by antidepressants [15]-[17], amphetamines [18] and diabetes [19]. The cellular mechanisms that underlie changes in CSPalpha expression are currently unknown. The cDNA clone for rat CSPalpha (594 bp open reading frame) contains 181 nucleotides of 5' untranslated region, 1.2 kb of 3' untranslated region and encodes a 35 kDa protein with extensive lipid modification [13]. Figure 1 shows that CSPalpha levels are not increased by heat shock. As expected, Hsp70 and Hsp40 were induced by heat shock and their induction was blocked by pretreatment with quercetin, an inhibitor of the heat shock response. Quercetin did not alter CSPalpha expression.

###xml 1599 1606 1543 1550 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1729 1732 1673 1676 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Brown1">[6]</xref>
We then evaluated the time course of expression of select chaperones in response to heat shock. Following a conditioning stress, Hsp70 is rapidly expressed in CAD, LAN1, and PC12 cell lines, consistent with Hsp70 induction in neurons [1]. Figure 2 demonstrates that Hsp70 protein expression is clearly increased approximately3 hours after heat shock. Although Hsp70 is detected in LAN1 cells prior to the conditioning stress, the levels of Hsp70 expression were still observed to increase in response to heat shock. The time course of Hsp70 expression showed slower onset in differentiated compared to undifferentiated CAD cells, however robust Hsp70 expression was observed at approximately6 hours in differentiated CAD cells. Figure 2 shows that Hsp25/27 expression also increased in CAD, LAN1, PC12 cell lines approximately5 hours after a conditioning heat shock. Although Hsp25/27 was expressed in unstressed LAN1 and PC12 cells, further increases were observed following a conditioning heat shock. Hsp40 levels increased approximately3 hours after a conditioning heat shock in CAD and LAN1 cells, however no increase was seen in PC12 cells which already had high control levels of Hsp40 pre-heat shock. In contrast to Hsp70, Hsp40 and Hsp25/27, the expression of actin was not altered in response to heat shock demonstrating the specificity of the cellular response to a conditioning stress. Following heat shock CSPalpha levels are not altered in CAD cells (Figure 1), however in PC12 and LAN1 cells reduced CSPalpha levels were observed (Figure 2). These data are consistent with those found in vivo where the severity of the stress required to trigger the heat shock response is reported to vary among neural populations [6].

Next we directly compared non-heat shocked (control) CAD, differentiated CAD, PC12, differentiated PC12, LAN1 and HEK cells for CSPalpha expression. Figure 3A shows that CSPalpha was present in all cell lines except HEK cells. The cell lines were then evaluated for basal (control) levels of heat shock chaperones including Hsp70, Hsp25/27 (heat shock protein 25/27) and Hsp40. Hsp70 was not detectable in non-heat shocked (control) CAD, differentiated CAD or PC12 cells, but was present at high levels in LAN1 and HEK cells and detectable in differentiated PC12 cells. Notably, some tumor cells have been reported to constitutively express high levels of the anti-apoptotic chaperone Hsp70. Furthermore, high Hsp70 levels have been reported to correlate with poor prognosis in some types of cancer [3], [7]. In contrast, Hsp25/Hsp27 was absent from CAD cells but detectable in HEK cells and abundant in LAN1 and PC12 cells. Hsp25 and Hsp27 are homologous proteins. Hsp25 is present in rat and mouse, while Hsp27 is present in humans. Anti-Hsp25 antibody does not cross-react with Hsp27 and vice versa. Actin is shown as a loading control (Figure 3, panel 5). The basal expression of select chaperones in unstressed rat brain is shown for comparison in Figure 3B. Hsp40 and Hsc70 are abundant in both the cytosolic (S) and membrane particulate (M) fractions of rat brain while the stress-inducible Hsp70 was not detected. Hsp25 was detected in the cytosolic fraction. Taken together, these observations indicate that while CSPalpha was present in all neural cell lines as expected, the background expression of stress-induced chaperones varied extensively between the cell lines.

Although CSPalpha is present in neural cell lines, its expression is approximately10 fold lower (10+/-1.7, n = 3, data not shown) than that found in rat brain homogenates. To conduct a more detailed analysis of the CSPalpha chaperone complex, CSPalpha was examined in CAD mouse neuroblastoma cells transiently transfected with CSPalpha in order to bring CSPalpha levels up to those found in adult rat brain. After transfection a 70 kDa CSPalpha immunoreactive band was observed (Figure 4). 70 kDa CSPalpha dimers have previously been reported in rat brain [13], [20], rat hippocampus [12], rat pancreas [13], PC12 cells transiently expressing CSPalpha [14], [21], and HEK293 cells transiently expressing CSPalpha [22]. The cellular role of the CSPalpha dimer is not known. Figure 4 shows that in CAD cells transfected with CSPalpha the 70 kDa CSPalpha dimer is stable, SDS-resistant and maintained after incubation in sample buffer at 80degreesC for 10 min. Figure 4 clearly demonstrates that following 40 minutes of conditioning heat shock at 42degreesC, there is a decline in the CSPalpha dimer detected. In contrast, quercetin increases the CSPalpha-CSPalpha complex in control and heat shocked cells (Figure 4). The upper panel in Figure 4 is an overexposure of the 70 kDa CSPalpha dimer demonstrating its presence in control but not heat shocked CAD cells. In transfected (Figure 4) but not untransfected (Figure 1) CAD cells, quercetin was observed to increase expression levels of the CSPalpha monomer. Taken together, these data show that the extremely stable CSPalpha dimer is regulated by quercetin as well as by a conditioning heat shock.

###xml 1511 1516 1467 1472 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1-198</sub>
###xml 1561 1566 1513 1518 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1-112</sub>
Two possible scenarios could mediate the anti-apoptotic activity of stress chaperones. It is possible that (1) stress inducible chaperones have evolved to perform the same function as constitutively expressed chaperones or that (2) they carry out specialized functions to specifically cope with physiological stress. In order to distinguish between these two possibilities, we examined the association between CSPalpha and heat shock proteins. Glutathione-S-transferase (GST) fusion proteins consisting of CSPalpha1-112 or CSPalpha1-198 (full length) were coupled to glutathione agarose beads and used in an in vitro binding assay. The beads were incubated with cell homogenate, washed, and bound proteins were eluted and evaluated by Western blot. We have previously shown that CSPalpha1-112 contains two binding sites for G proteins and has guanine nucleotide exchange (GEF) activity for Galphas[11]. Recombinant full length CSPalpha1-198 also contains both binding sites for G proteins, but only has GEF activity for Galphas in the presence of Hsc70 and SGT (small glutamine-rich tetratricopeptide repeat domain protein). Specifically, we have shown that CSPalpha regulates heterotrimeric GTP binding proteins (G proteins) by preferentially targeting the inactive GDP-bound form of Galphas and promoting GDP/GTP exchange which increases cAMP levels and downstream phosphorylation. In vitro, Hsc70 and SGT trigger a switch in CSPalpha from an inactive GEF to an active GEF. Therefore we examined both CSPalpha1-198 (requires Hsc70/SGT activation) and CSPalpha1-112 (active GEF) for their possible associations with stress-induced chaperones.

Chaperones homologous to those found in the CSPalpha complex are induced following a preconditioning heat shock (eg. Hsp70 (70 kDa heat shock protein) and Hsp40 (40 kDa heat shock protein)). Hsc70 and Hsp70 both associate with the J domain of CSPalpha [8], [9], [20]. Despite the robust induction of Hsp70 expression by heat shock and its significant homology (85%) with Hsc70 [23], a CSPalpha/Hsc70 complex was favored over a CSPalpha/Hsp70 complex in CAD cell homogenates (Figure 5). After a conditioning heat shock, Hsp70 was induced and associated weakly with the GST-CSPalpha1-112 and CSPalpha1-198 complex. These data confirm the relatively weaker CSPalpha association and ATPase activation of Hsp70 compared to Hsc70 utilizing yeast two hybrid and ATPase assay techniques [9], [20]. The ATPase Hsp90 weakly associated with the CSPalpha complex. Like Hsc70, Hsp70 association was increased in the presence of ATP. In contrast, Hsp25 was not found to associate with the CSPalpha complex either before or after heat shock.

If Hsp40 and CSPalpha have evolved to perform separate functions, then it is possible that induction of Hsp40 will reduce the assembly of CSPalpha with Hsc70. Given the homology between the J domains of CSPalpha and Hsp40, one might expect elevated levels of Hsp40 to disrupt the CSPalpha/Hsc70 complex and favor a Hsp40/Hsc70 complex by competing with CSPalpha for association with Hsc70 and Hsp70. To our surprise, however, Hsp40 was found to associate robustly with both GST-CSPalpha1-112 and CSPalpha1-198 after heat shock. Figure 5 shows that Hsp40 association with CSPalpha1-198 was nucleotide independent, however association of Hsp40 with CSPalpha1-112 was greater in the presence of either ATP or GDP. These data indicate that the components of the CSPalpha chaperone complex are altered in response to a conditioning stress to include the cytosolic stress-induced J protein Hsp40.

While these data are consistent with a direct interaction between Hsp40 and CSPalpha, it does not permit us to rule out the possibility that CSPalpha/Hsp40 interact indirectly. To investigate this possibility, we examined the ability of immobilized CSPalpha fusion proteins to interact with soluble Hsp40. As shown in Figure 6A, immobilized GST-CSPalpha1-198 and GST-CSPalpha1-112 were able to bind purified, soluble Hsp40 in a pull down assay, indicating that there is indeed a direct interaction between CSPalpha and Hsp40.

To further understand the structural requirements for assembly of Hsp40 with the CSPalpha complex, Hsp40 deletion and point mutants were constructed and the regions of Hsp40 required for its binding to CSPalpha were determined. In each assay, an equal amount of fusion protein was immobilized to sepharose beads as confirmed by Ponceau S staining. The presence of Hsp40 was analyzed by Western blotting. Hsp40 and CSPalpha belong to a large and diverse protein family [24]. Each member has a conserved J domain that functions to stimulate Hsc70/Hsp70 ATPase (eg CSPalpha stimulates Hsc70 ATPase [8]). There is no functional one to one correspondence between members of the J protein family and members of the Hsp70 family. J domains are a approximately70 amino acid region of homology comprised of four alpha helices with a highly conserved tripeptide of histidine, proline and aspartic acid (HPD motif) located between helices II and III. The structures of the J domain of CSPalpha (mouse) and Hsp40 (human) have been determined from nuclear magnetic resonance studies [25]. Figure S1 shows the comparison of the amino acid sequence between rat CSPalpha and Hsp40. The J domain (magenta) and cysteine string region (red) of CSPalpha and the J domain and the DnaJ C-terminal domain (cyan) of Hsp40 are indicated. CSPalpha and Hsp40 show only 16% identity over their entire amino acid sequences, but 51% identity within the J domains. Secondary structure predictions for helix 1 show a weak "beta sheet" (the probability is 0.4 sheet compared to 0.3 helix; 1 is high, 0 is low) for position 5-8 a.a. of Hsp40_Rat and 17-20 a.a. of CSPalpha_Rat, while predictions are stronger for helices 2, 3 and 4 (probability is 1, 1 and 0.7 respectively). Mutation of the highly conserved HPD tripeptide of Hsp40 (Hsp40HPD-->AAA) did not abolish binding to CSPalpha, indicating that CSPalpha/Hsp40 association is not dependent on this conserved motif. Furthermore, Hsp401-80 as well as Hsp401-80HPD-->AAA were not found to associate with CSPalpha1-198 or CSPalpha1-112 (Figure 6B&C). These data therefore define Hsp40 residues 81-340 as important for binding to CSPalpha.

###begin p 34
###xml 169 177 157 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004595-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 613 622 589 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004595-g005">Figures 5</xref>
###xml 623 624 599 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004595-g006">6</xref>
To understand better the nature of the CSPalpha/Hsp40 association, we evaluated the distribution of CSPalpha and Hsp40 in CAD cells transiently expressing myc-CSPalpha (Figure 7). In control cells (C), CSPalpha was observed to be concentrated at the cell-cell contact sites (indicated with an arrow) and, in contrast, Hsp40 was broadly expressed. Following heat shock (H.S.) we found CSPalpha and Hsp40 to be present primarily in the plasma membrane however reduced at the cell-cell contacts, indicating that Hsp40 as well as CSPalpha was relocalized. These observations are consistent with our biochemical data (Figures 5&6) demonstrating a CSPalpha:Hsp40 association following heat shock. Panel 4 clearly demonstrates that quercetin blocked the heat shock general redistribution of Hsp40 to the plasma membrane. Intracellular localization of CSPalpha and Hsp40 was increased in cells treated with quercetin (Q) as well as quercetin followed by heat shock (Q+HS). Plasma membrane co-localization of CSPalpha and Hsp40 in single differentiated cells is shown in yellow in the right hand panel (panel 5). Taken together, these biochemical and histochemical data demonstrate that following heat shock, cellular levels of Hsp40 are increased and both Hsp40 and CSPalpha undergo redistribution. These data indicate that changes in the expression levels of Hsp40 as well as changes in the cellular localization of Hsp40 and CSPalpha facilitate the assembly of Hsp40 with the CSPalpha chaperone complex.

Next we evaluated a series of agents to see if they altered either CSPalpha levels or the components of the CSPalpha complex (Figure S2A). The Hsp90 ATPase inhibitors geldanamycin, 17-AAG and novobiocin robustly induced the expression of Hsp70 in PC12 cells. Lithium ions have been reported to enhance CSPalpha expression [26] and we have previously shown that CSPalpha increased the response to the beta2 adrenergic agonist isoproterenol [11]. No Hsp70 was detected in PC12 cells treated with isoproterenol, LiCl or quercetin. In contrast to Hsp70 and Hsp25, the expression of actin was not altered in response to geldanamycin or 17AAG demonstrating the specificity of the cellular response to the Hsp90 inhibitors. Following geldanamycin treatment of PC12 cells, Hsp70 expression is detected as early as 5 hours and continues to increase (Figure S2B). To our surprise, geldanamycin did not trigger the expression of Hsp70 but did induce Hsp40 expression without increasing CSPalpha expression in CAD mouse neuroblastoma cells (Figures S2C&D). The molecular events that underlie the difference between geldanamycin-induced and heat shock-induced Hsp70 expression in CAD cells remains to be established, however, it provided us the opportunity to investigate the CSPalpha/Hsp40 association in the absence of Hsp70.

We then examined the CSPalpha complex in geldanamycin treated CAD cells. CSPalpha1-112 or CSPalpha1-198 fusion proteins were coupled to glutathione agarose beads and used in an in vitro binding assay. Following geldanamycin treatment, Hsp40-CSPalpha1-112 and Hsp40-CSPalpha1-198 complexes were abundant in the presence of ATP (Figure 8), similiar to that found after heat shock (Figure 5). Again, Hsp90 was observed to associate weakly with the CSPalpha complex. Thus the CSPalpha chaperone complex is dynamic, undergoing changes in components in the post-stress response and in response to Hsp90 inhibitors. Taken together, our results indicate that as the cellular levels of Hsp40 rise in response to a conditioning stress or after treatment with Hsp90 inhibitors, the CSPalpha complex becomes an Hsp40-CSPalpha complex.

Next we evaluated Hsp40's effect on the steady state hydrolysis of GTP by Galphas. We have previously demonstrated that the stimulation of GTPase activity by Galphas by CSPalpha requires Hsc70 and SGT [11]. Figure 9A shows that Hsp40 enhanced the CSPalpha-Hsc70-SGT-stimulated increase in GTP hydrolysis by Galphas. GTP hydrolysis by Galphas was not altered in the presence of Hsp40 alone, indicating that Hsp40 does not effect the hydrolysis of GTP by Galphas alone. Furthermore, Hsp40 does not hydrolyze GTP. Phosphorylation of synapsin in CAD cells serves as an independent readout of cellular signaling through Galphas. Moreover, we have recently reported that the expression of CSPalpha in CAD cells increases isoproterenol-mediated phosphorylation of the transcription factor CREB [27]. These results show that the heat shock response modulates signaling through Galphas pathways.

###begin title 46
Discussion
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 320 324 312 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Chandra1">[28]</xref>
###xml 325 329 317 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Zinsmaier1">[30]</xref>
###xml 996 1003 984 991 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1224 1227 1212 1215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Brown1">[6]</xref>
In this study, we demonstrate for the first time that the stress-induced J protein, Hsp40, physically couples with the constitutive CSPalpha chaperone complex. CSPalpha is a synaptic vesicle J protein that is essential for periods of extended neurotransmission and is implicated in the defense against neurodegeneration [28]-[30]. CSPalpha is constitutively expressed on synaptic vesicles and is thought to tether Hsc70 for conformational work at the synaptic vesicle site. Hsp40 is a cytosolic J protein that is rapidly and transiently induced in response to stress, including, but not exclusive to, heat shock and geldanamycin treatment. Stress-induced expression of several chaperones, including Hsp40, is widely thought to protect cells from the deleterious effects of subsequent stress. Interference with the heat shock response would be expected to have enormous cellular consequences and reduce cell survival. It is fully anticipated that transient expression of stress-induced chaperones in vivo occurs routinely in neurons to cope with the stresses rendered by various insults. Intriguingly, some neurons have a particularly higher threshold for the induction of the heat shock response compared with other neurons [6]. In this study, we provide evidence that Hsp40 specifically and directly associates with the constitutive CSPalpha synaptic vesicle chaperone complex following either a conditioning heat shock or geldanamycin treatment. Following heat shock, Hsp40 expression is increased and both CSPalpha and Hsp40 undergo redistribution. The association of Hsp40 with CSPalpha correlates with reduced CSPalpha dimerization, translocation of Hsp40 to the plasma membrane and enhanced CSPalpha-mediated augmentation of steady state GTP hydrolysis. Association with CSPalpha is mediated via C terminal binding sites of Hsp40 and does not involve the J domain. Since neurotransmitter release relies on complex interactions between multiple cellular components, we speculate that the cross-talk between CSPalpha chaperone machinery and the stress-induced Hsp40 is important in maintaining functionally competent synapses.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 468 491 452 475 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 491 495 475 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Zinsmaier1">[30]</xref>
###xml 496 500 480 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Barclay1">[32]</xref>
###xml 506 516 490 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 625 629 605 609 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Zinsmaier1">[30]</xref>
###xml 787 791 763 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-FernandezChacon1">[29]</xref>
###xml 793 797 769 773 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Chandra2">[33]</xref>
###xml 799 803 775 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Schmitz1">[34]</xref>
###xml 468 491 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 655 659 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Whether stress-induced chaperones typically perform independent cellular tasks or are coupled to constitutive chaperone machines, such as CSPalpha, is a current biological question. The notion that Hsp40 contributes to the folding activity of the CSPalpha chaperone complex after a conditioning stress is consistent with the neurodegeneration observed in CSPalpha deletion models. Deletion of the CSPalpha gene severely impairs central and presynaptic transmission in Drosophila melanogaster[30]-[32]. The Drosophila CSPalpha null mutants exhibit temperature sensitive paralysis and die as larvae or within days of adulthood [30]. Deletion of CSPalpha in mice causes blindness followed by progressive motor and sensorial impairment and neurodegeneration with no survival beyond 4 months [29], [33], [34]. Thus, these reports indicate that CSPalpha is important for the long term functional integrity of the synaptic machinery. It is possible, given the data presented here that the prevention of presynaptic neurodegeneration by the synaptic vesicle CSPalpha chaperone complex involves a transient interaction with the highly conserved stress induced protein Hsp40.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 127 131 123 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Natochin1">[11]</xref>
###xml 133 137 129 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Magga1">[12]</xref>
###xml 139 143 135 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Miller1">[35]</xref>
###xml 180 184 176 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Gundersen1">[36]</xref>
###xml 186 190 182 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Chen2">[37]</xref>
###xml 275 279 271 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Chandra1">[28]</xref>
###xml 281 285 277 281 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Nie1">[38]</xref>
###xml 287 291 283 287 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Swayne2">[39]</xref>
###xml 403 407 395 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Bronk1">[40]</xref>
###xml 409 413 401 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Ruiz1">[41]</xref>
###xml 461 465 453 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Jana1">[42]</xref>
###xml 466 470 458 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Wacker1">[46]</xref>
###xml 475 476 467 468 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sup>
###xml 476 480 468 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Rambold1">[47]</xref>
###xml 498 502 486 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Auluck1">[48]</xref>
###xml 518 522 506 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Chan1">[49]</xref>
###xml 524 528 512 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004595-Chai1">[50]</xref>
Candidates for the protein substrate(s) of the CSPalpha/Hsc70 system include: G proteins (heterotrimeric GTP binding proteins) [11], [12], [35], voltage sensitive calcium channels [36], [37] and SNAREs (soluble N ethylmaleimide-sensitive factor attachment protein receptors) [28], [38], [39]. Furthermore, CSPalpha has been shown to be critical for the normal calcium sensitivity of synaptic exocytosis [40], [41]. Several misfolded proteins such as huntingtin [42]-[46], PrPC[47], alpha-synuclein [48] and ataxin 1&3 [49], [50] are known substrates for the Hsp40/Hsc70 system. It is tempting to speculate that the assembly of Hsp40 with the CSPalpha chaperone complex may expand the client protein(s) targeted by the synaptic vesicle CSPalpha complex by shifting chaperone activity from dedicated to indiscriminant.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 349 357 337 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 445 446 425 426 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">s</sub>
In conclusion, our combined findings suggest a model in which heat shock alters the composition of the CSPalpha complex to include Hsp40. Understanding the composition of the CSPalpha chaperone complex, either basal or following a conditioning stress, is crucial to understanding the physiological role(s) of CSPalpha. Data presented here show that in vitro, Hsp40 increased the CSPalpha-induced increase in steady state GTP hydrolysis of Galphas. Furthermore, in CAD cells, induction of the heat shock response decreased CSPalpha dimerization and increased isoproterenol-stimulated phosphorylation of synapsin. In view of the crucial importance of stress-induced chaperones in protection against cell death, our data attribute a key role to the association of Hsp40 with the CSPalpha chaperone complex in neuroprotection.
###end p 50

Expression of GST fusion proteins was induced with 100 microM isopropyl-beta-D-thiogalactosidase (IPTG) for 5 hours at 37degreesC. Bacteria were suspended in PBS, 0.05% (v/v) Tween 20, 2 mM EDTA, 0.5 mM PMSF and 0.1% (v/v) beta-mercaptoethanol and lysed by two passages through a French press (Spectronics Instruments Inc.) GST fusion proteins were recovered by binding to glutathione-sepharose beads (GE Healthcare Biosciences). Beads were suspended as a 50% (v/v) slurry in 20 mM MOPS, 4.5 mM Mg acetate, 150 mM KCl, and 0.2% (v/v) Triton X-100. The concentrations of recombinant GST fusion proteins were estimated by Coomassie blue staining of SDS-polyacrylamide gels using bovine serum albumin (BSA) as a standard. The recombinant portion of the fusion protein was eluted from the beads with 50 mM Tris (pH 7.5), 150 mM NaCl, 2.5 mM CaCl2, 0.1% beta-mercaptoethanol and 3 microg/ml thrombin.

###begin title 61
In vitro 'Pull-down' Assays
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 104 105 104 105 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 116 117 116 117 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
For the 'pull down assays', cells were lysed end-over-end in 20 mM Tris (pH 7.4), 130 mM NaCl, 2 mM MgSO4, 2 mM NaVO4, 1% n-dodecyl-beta-D-maltoside, 1 mM PMSF and protease inhibitor (Sigma) for 60 min at 4degreesC. Lysates were centrifuged at 20,000xg for 30 minutes and the total detergent-solubilized cell lysate (supernatant) was collected. Protein concentration was determined using a Bradford reagent (BioRad). The detergent-solubilized cell lysates were incubated with bead-immobilized GST-tagged proteins in 20 mM MOPS, 4.5 mM MgAcetate, 150 mM KCl, 0.5% Tx100, and 2 mM ATP or 2 mM GDP in a final volume of 400 microl, for 1 hour at 37degreesC. Beads were washed twice with 200 microl of ice cold 20 mM MOPS, 4.5 mM MgAcetate, 150 mM KCl, 0.2% Tx100. Bound proteins were eluted in Laemmli sample buffer, fractionated by SDS-PAGE and analyzed by Western blotting.
