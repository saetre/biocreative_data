###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 0 14 0 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">achaete-scute </italic>
###xml 24 28 24 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C</italic>
###xml 299 304 299 304 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 382 387 382 387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 514 530 514 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila AS-C </italic>
achaete-scute complexe (AS-C) has been widely studied at genetic, developmental and evolutional levels. Genes of this family encode proteins containing a highly conserved bHLH domain, which take part in the regulation of the development of central nervous system and peripheral nervous system. Many AS-C homologs have been isolated from various vertebrates and invertebrates. Also, AS-C genes are duplicated during the evolution of Diptera. Functions besides neural development controlling have also been found in Drosophila AS-C genes.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 15 29 15 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">achaete-scute </italic>
###xml 39 42 39 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ASH</italic>
###xml 81 92 81 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx mori</italic>
###xml 376 384 376 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 398 405 398 405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH </italic>
###xml 409 425 409 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila AS-C </italic>
###xml 548 555 548 555 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH </italic>
###xml 81 92 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
We cloned four achaete-scute homologs (ASH) from the lepidopteran model organism Bombyx mori, including three proneural genes and one neural precursor gene. Proteins encoded by them contained the characteristic bHLH domain and the three proneural ones were also found to have the C-terminal conserved motif. These genes regulated promoter activity through the Class A E-boxes in vitro. Though both Bm-ASH and Drosophila AS-C have four members, they are not in one by one corresponding relationships. Results of RT-PCR and real-time PCR showed that Bm-ASH genes were expressed in different larval tissues, and had well-regulated expressional profiles during the development of embryo and wing/wing disc.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 15 29 15 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">achaete-scute </italic>
###xml 41 52 41 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx mori</italic>
###xml 84 89 84 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 167 172 167 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 41 52 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
###xml 146 154 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
There are four achaete-scute homologs in Bombyx mori, the second insect having four AS-C genes so far, and these genes have multiple functions in silkworm life cycle. AS-C gene duplication in insects occurs after or parallel to, but not before the taxonomic order formation during evolution.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 204 205 204 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 206 207 206 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 214 228 214 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">achaete-scute </italic>
###xml 237 241 237 241 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C</italic>
###xml 326 337 326 337 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 356 357 356 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 358 359 358 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
Transcriptional factors of the bHLH (basic helix-loop-helix) family play important roles in the development of metazoan, taking part in the regulation of neurogenesis, myogenesis, haemopoiesis and so on [1-5]. The achaete-scute complex (AS-C), a group of four bHLH genes, has been found to be involved in the determination of Drosophila neural precursors [6-9].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 119 121 119 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 198 208 198 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hunchback </italic>
###xml 209 211 209 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hb</italic>
###xml 214 216 214 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 222 232 222 232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">phyllopod </italic>
###xml 233 237 233 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">phyl</italic>
###xml 240 242 240 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 248 258 248 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 307 310 307 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ac </italic>
###xml 365 368 365 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ac </italic>
###xml 372 375 372 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sc </italic>
###xml 376 378 376 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
AS-C proteins interact with another bHLH protein Daughterless (Da) to form a dimer, and bind Class A E-boxes CAGG/CTG [10]. AS-C/Da heterodimers' binding sites were found in the upstream regions of hunchback (hb) [10] and phyllopod (phyl) [11]. In Drosophila, they were also found in the upstream region of ac itself, and required for auto- and cross-regulation by ac and sc [12].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 22 27 22 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 187 192 187 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 421 422 421 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 423 425 423 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>

###xml 431 455 431 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium castaneum ASH </italic>
###xml 456 462 456 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-ASH</italic>
###xml 468 497 468 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hydra vulgaris cnidarian ASH </italic>
###xml 498 504 498 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cn-ASH</italic>
###xml 586 597 586 597 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 598 600 598 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 601 603 601 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 670 681 670 681 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 700 708 700 708 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 769 771 769 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 810 815 810 815 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 843 846 843 846 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ac </italic>
###xml 977 979 977 979 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 982 985 982 985 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sc </italic>
###xml 1087 1090 1087 1090 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ac </italic>
###xml 1094 1099 1094 1099 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">l'sc </italic>
###xml 1118 1121 1118 1121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sc </italic>
###xml 1139 1141 1139 1141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1142 1144 1142 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 1147 1152 1147 1152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">l'sc </italic>
###xml 1262 1267 1262 1267 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">l'sc </italic>
###xml 1359 1361 1359 1361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 431 450 <span type="species:ncbi:7070">Tribolium castaneum</span>
###xml 468 482 <span type="species:ncbi:6087">Hydra vulgaris</span>
The main functions of AS-C genes are regulating the formation and patterning of precursors of central nervous system (CNS) and peripheral nervous system (PNS). During neural development, AS-C genes are expressed in one or more cells within the proneural clusters, which are committed to the neural precursor fate, and the remaining cells in the cluster differentiate to be epidermal cells or are eliminated by apoptosis [8,13,14]. Tribolium castaneum ASH (Tc-ASH) and Hydra vulgaris cnidarian ASH (Cn-ASH) show similar functions during the formation of sensory organs in the transgenic Drosophila [15,16]. Also, it was found that Cn-ASH protein formed heterodimers with Drosophila Da (Dm-Da) protein in vitro, and these dimers specially bound to the consensus E-boxes [15]. Besides regulation of neurogenesis, AS-C genes have other functions. ac takes part in the development of Malpighian tubule by expressing in the tip cell and regulating the sequential fates of the organ [17]. sc regulates sex determination by its different expression dosage between females and males, but neither ac nor l'sc can substitute for sc in this function [18-20]. l'sc expression is necessary for muscle founder cells segregating from the somatic mesoderm. Loss of expression of l'sc leads to a loss, whereas over expression causes a duplication of muscles and founder cells [21].

###xml 1289 1299 1289 1299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>

###xml 1375 1384 1375 1384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium</italic>
###xml 1411 1416 1411 1416 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Apis </italic>
###xml 1417 1419 1417 1419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 1420 1422 1420 1422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 774 778 <span type="species:ncbi:7460">bees</span>
###xml 797 813 <span type="species:ncbi:7070">red flour beetle</span>
###xml 846 855 <span type="species:ncbi:39708">P. coenia</span>
###xml 961 979 <span type="species:ncbi:7213">Ceratitis capitata</span>
###xml 997 1006 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">fruit fly</span>
According to the sequence and functional analysis in different species, AS-C genes are conserved during evolution. The four genes of Drosophila AS-C are achaete (ac), scute (sc), lethal of scute (l'sc) and asense (ase). The former three are defined as proneural genes and the last one as neural precursor gene, basing on their expression patterns and functions in proneural clusters. AS-C homologs have been isolated from various invertebrates and vertebrates [15,16,22-34]. They all have a conserved bHLH domain and some of them also have a conserved C-terminal motif. Gene duplication events caused a dramatic increase of AS-C gene number during Arthropoda evolution [16,30]. Within Insecta, AS-C homologs have been studied in Diptera (flies and mosquitoes), Hymenoptera (bees), Coleoptera (the red flour beetle) and Lepidoptera (the butterfly P. coenia). Each of the insects has one ase-like gene. Most of them have only one proneural gene, while the medfly Ceratitis capitata has two, and the fruit fly has three. The four genes in Drosophila were believed to have arisen from a single ancestral gene by three independent duplication events [16,30]. AS-C homologs in the insects studied are closely linked in the chromosome, for example, the four AS-C genes comprising about 100 Kb in Drosophila, and the two residing about 22 Kb apart in Anopheles, about 55 Kb apart in Tribolium, and about 40 Kb apart in Apis [16,34].
###end p 12

###xml 4 12 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 14 25 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
###xml 86 94 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 128 139 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
###xml 336 344 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 592 600 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
The silkworm, Bombyx mori, is a model organism of Lepidoptera. With the completion of silkworm genomic sequencing project [35], Bombyx mori is emerging as an important model lepidopteran. Only one AS-C homolog had been isolated from lepidopteran in previous studies [26]. In the present study, we found there were four AS-C homologs in silkworm, three proneural genes and an ase-like one. We detected the transcriptional activity of the genes by transient expression in Bm-N cells. Expression profiles of the genes in different tissues and expression changes during the development course of silkworm embryo and wing disc/wing were also studied.

###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
###xml 32 46 32 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">achaete-scute </italic>
###xml 58 69 58 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx mori</italic>
###xml 58 69 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
Isolation and identification of achaete-scute homologs in Bombyx mori
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 27 41 27 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">achaete-scute </italic>
###xml 168 179 168 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 327 332 327 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 459 462 459 462 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">th </sup>
###xml 567 575 567 575 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH1 </italic>
###xml 588 596 588 596 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH1 </italic>
###xml 207 215 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
It is well known that each achaete-scute homolog has a highly conserved bHLH domain which distinguishes them from other bHLH proteins. Using the amino acid sequence of Drosophila Ac (Genbank: ) to blast the silkworm EST database (see Materials and Methods section), we obtained an EST sequence (GenBank: ) encoding a conserved AS-C bHLH domain. Primers were designed based on the EST sequence, and RACE assay was carried out using the midgut total RNA of 3d 5th instar larva as the template. After sequencing and assembling, we gained a cDNA of 1,332 bp and named it Bm-ASH1 (Genbank: ). Bm-ASH1 gene contains a 582 bp ORF region (including the stop codon) and encodes a 193 aa protein.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 21 33 21 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx mori </italic>
###xml 123 128 123 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 233 240 233 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH1</italic>
###xml 725 733 725 733 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH2 </italic>
###xml 746 754 746 754 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH3 </italic>
###xml 770 777 770 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ase </italic>
###xml 803 811 803 811 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH2 </italic>
###xml 903 911 903 911 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH3 </italic>
###xml 1007 1014 1007 1014 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ase </italic>
###xml 21 32 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
Then we screened the Bombyx mori genome database using the 193 aa Bm-ASH1 protein sequence, and found genes with conserved AS-C bHLH region in four more contigs (Genbank: , ,  and ), besides the two (Genbank:  and ) corresponding to Bm-ASH1.  is part of . Each of them contained a deduced ORF region, and then we cloned the ORF regions by RT-PCR methods and they were sequenced. Primers for 3'-RACE were designed basing on the sequences of the ORF regions, and 3'-RACE assay was processed using the total RNA from 1 d pupal wing as the template. The segments gained by 3'-RACE were sequenced and assembled with each corresponding ORF sequence. The final cDNA sequences were 1,449 bp, 990 bp and 1,695 bp long, and were named Bm-ASH2 (Genbank: ), Bm-ASH3 (Genbank: ) and Bm-ase (Genbank: ) respectively. Bm-ASH2 gene contained a 720 bp OFR region (including the stop codon) and encoded a 239 aa protein, Bm-ASH3 gene contained a 726 bp OFR region (including the stop codon) and encoded a 241 aa protein, and Bm-ase gene contained a 1,215 bp OFR region (including the stop codon) and encoded a 404 aa protein.

###begin p 18
###xml 23 34 23 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 89 96 89 96 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH </italic>
###xml 116 121 116 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 211 212 211 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 427 429 427 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 430 432 430 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 600 601 600 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 633 640 633 640 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH1</italic>
###xml 642 650 642 650 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH2 </italic>
###xml 654 662 654 662 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH3 </italic>
###xml 686 693 686 693 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ase </italic>
###xml 696 703 696 703 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asense </italic>
Alignment results with Drosophila AS-C proteins showed that all four proteins encoded by Bm-ASH genes contained the AS-C characteristic one basic and two helix domains flanking a non-conserved loop domain (Fig. 1). Bm-ASH1, Bm-ASH2 and Bm-ASH3 also had the C-terminal 16-17 aa conserved motif, but Bm-Ase did not. There was a highly conserved short motif (SPxxS, x means uncertain acid amino) around the region of PEST domain [36-38]. In the bHLH region, Bm-ASH1 shares 74.6%, Bm-ASH2 shares 72.1% and Bm-ASH3 shares 72.1% amino acid identity with Dm-L'sc, and Bm-Ase shares 74.3% with Dm-Ase (Table 1). The data above indicate that Bm-ASH1, Bm-ASH2 and Bm-ASH3 are proneural genes and Bm-ase an asense homolog.

To understand the potential in vivo regulations of ASH genes, we detected the expression status of Bombyx ASH genes in different tissues of the 5th instar larvae on the 3rd and the 8th days (just after spinning) (Fig. 6). The data showed that Bm-ASH1 and Bm-ASH2 were expressed in organs derived from all the three cellular derms: ectoderm, mesoderm and endoderm; whereas Bm-ASH3 and Bm-ase were only expressed in tissues derived from ectoderm and mesoderm. Bm-ASH1 was expressed in all organs except silk glands (S-G) and hemocytes (He), and at a low level in 5 d and had no expression in 8 d fat body (FB). Bm-ASH2 was widely expressed in all the investigated samples. The expression of Bm-ASH3 and Bm-ase were more tissue specific, and the level of Bm-ase was relatively lower. Bm-ASH1 and Bm-ASH2 had high expression levels in gonads (G), while Bm-ASH2 and Bm-ASH3 had high expression levels in tracheal clusters (Tc). All of the data suggested that the four Bm-ASH genes might play various roles in the development of silkworm larva. We should note that all three proneural genes expressed highly in WD, and Bm-ase had a relatively higher expression level in 8 d WD. These implied that Bm-ASH genes might be important for the development of the wing disc.

###begin title 40
Discussion
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 40 45 40 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 57 68 57 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx mori</italic>
###xml 70 77 70 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH1</italic>
###xml 79 86 79 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH2</italic>
###xml 88 96 88 96 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH3 </italic>
###xml 100 106 100 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ase</italic>
###xml 241 249 241 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH2 </italic>
###xml 317 322 317 322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 353 354 353 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 356 357 356 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 440 441 440 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 558 559 558 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>
###xml 561 562 561 562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
###xml 57 68 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
###xml 410 418 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
Our results showed that there were four AS-C homologs in Bombyx mori: Bm-ASH1, Bm-ASH2, Bm-ASH3 and Bm-ase. The first three were proneural genes, and the last one was a neural precursor gene. The proneural genes enhanced the activity of the Bm-ASH2 promoter by binding its E-boxes in Bm-N cells, which was one of the AS-C homolog characteristics (Figs. 4 &5). The four genes had various expression profiles in silkworm larval tissues (Fig. 6), and further studies showed that they have important roles during the development of the embryo and the wing (Fig. 7 &8).
###end p 41
###begin title 42
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
AS-C genes duplication in insects occurs after or parallel but not before the taxonomic order formation during evolution
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 12 17 12 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 122 124 122 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 153 158 153 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 284 290 284 290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cn-ASH</italic>
###xml 296 301 296 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 439 442 439 442 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2,a</xref>
###xml 444 446 444 446 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2b</xref>
###xml 561 584 561 584 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 599 604 599 604 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 610 617 610 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">achaete</italic>
###xml 619 624 619 624 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">scute</italic>
###xml 626 642 626 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lethal of scute </italic>
###xml 646 653 646 653 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">asense </italic>
###xml 654 656 654 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 657 659 657 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 721 723 721 723 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 109 114 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 561 584 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
Homologs of AS-C genes exist in various animals from low-grade coelenterate to high-grade mammals, including human being [30]. It has been proposed that AS-C gene has several independent duplication events in Arthropoda, resulting in the plasticity of the gene number. In this model, Cn-ASH, the AS-C homolog in Hydra is supposed as the ancestral one. Parallel but independent duplication events occurred in insects and chelicerates (Fig. 2,a &2b). Within the Diptera two more duplication events happened during evolution. So the present most derived dipteran, Drosophila melanogaster, has the four AS-C genes achaete, scute, lethal of scute and asense [16,30]; while lower derived dipteran, such mosquito, has only two [31].
###end p 43

###xml 30 35 30 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 61 72 61 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx mori</italic>

###xml 628 629 628 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>

###xml 759 766 759 766 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx </italic>
###xml 793 804 793 804 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 847 853 847 853 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ac/sc </italic>
###xml 857 873 857 873 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH2/Bm-ASH3 </italic>
###xml 942 948 942 948 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ac/sc </italic>
###xml 953 958 953 958 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">l'sc </italic>
###xml 962 978 962 978 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH2/Bm-ASH3 </italic>
###xml 983 991 983 991 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH1 </italic>
###xml 51 59 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 61 72 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
###xml 446 454 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
We found that there were four AS-C homologs in the silkworm, Bombyx mori, a model lepidopteran. Also, Bombyx is the only insect besides Drosophila found with four AS-C genes so far. Among the three proneural AS-C genes in Drosophila, l'sc is the most original one, which is more identical to the proneural genes in other insects than ac and sc (Table 2). Like l'sc, Bm-ASH1 is more identical to non-lepidopteran insect proneural genes than other silkworm achaete-scute homologs (Table 2). What's more, l'sc and Bm-ASH1, and other sole proneural genes from other insects were sorted into one clade in the phylogenetic tree (Fig. 2). The data imply that Bm-ASH1 is the most original one in Bombyx. But homogeneous and phylogenetic analysis could not match each Bombyx proneural gene to those of Drosophila one by one, and phylogenetic result sorted ac/sc and Bm-ASH2/Bm-ASH3 into two independent branches. So we suggest that the generations of ac/sc from l'sc and Bm-ASH2/Bm-ASH3 from Bm-ASH1 are two parallel and independent events, that is, further duplication of proneural genes occurs after or parallel to but not before the split of Diptera and Lepidoptera during evolution.

The medfly Ceratitis capitata, a member of family Tephritidae, has three AS-C homolog genes Cc-sc, Cc-l'sc and Cc-ase. The bHLH domains of proteins coded by Ceratitis capitata acheate-scute homologs are highly conserved and display 95%, 91.5% and 90% identity with the Drosophila counterparts, respectively [28]. Only one AS-C homolog, B-ASH1, has been isolated from the Nymphalidae insect P. coenia [26]. B-ASH1 protein has a surprising identity with Bm-ASH2, 100% within the bHLH domains and 90.3% within the whole protein sequences. It is even higher than that between Bm-ASH2 and Bm-ASH3, 91.3% within the bHLH domains and 49.8% within the whole protein sequences (Table 1). Moths first appeared on earth between 100 and 190 million years ago, and butterflies appeared about 40 million years ago, based on fossilized evidence. All above suggest that there are at least three AS-C genes in P. coenia, corresponding with Bm-ASH1, Bm-ASH2 and Bm-ase, respectively, and Bm-ASH3 might be the most recent one among the silkworm proneural genes.

###begin title 48
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
AS-C genes have a broad expression distribution in insect tissues
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 438 448 438 448 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 450 455 450 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">l'sc </italic>
###xml 512 514 512 514 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 517 520 517 520 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sc </italic>
###xml 552 554 552 554 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 560 563 560 563 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ac </italic>
###xml 612 614 612 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 352 356 <span type="species:ncbi:7460">bees</span>
AS-C genes are important for the development of the nervous system, and have key roles in regulating the formation and patterning of neural precursors. They are specially expressed in most of the proneural clusters during the development of either the central (CNS) or peripheral nervous system (PNS) in arthropod, such as flies, butterflies, beetles, bees, spiders, chilopods, etc. Other functions of these genes have also been found in Drosophila. l'sc participates in the specification of muscle progenitors [21], sc functions in sex determination [18] and ac regulates the development of Malphigian tubules [17].
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 51 58 51 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH </italic>
###xml 523 534 523 534 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 535 537 535 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 656 663 656 663 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH </italic>
###xml 889 896 889 896 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH </italic>
###xml 929 930 929 930 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>
###xml 1186 1188 1186 1188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 1286 1294 1286 1294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH2 </italic>
###xml 75 83 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 242 250 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 677 685 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 1245 1253 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
In the present paper, we studied the expression of Bm-ASH genes in various silkworm larval tissues using RT-PCR methods. These genes are expressed in most organs derived from all three derms, showing they might have multiple functions during silkworm development. They have higher expression levels in the head, wing disc, tracheal cluster, body wall or gonad, and all of them are expressed in the organs though lower at some developmental points. This indicates that the genes might have overlapping functions, just as in Drosophila [42]. Expression profiles during the development of the embryo and the pupal wing also suggest co-operation characters of Bm-ASH genes. During silkworm embryo development, neurogenesis takes place around 3 d-old, and trachea, bristle and appendage occur on 5 d and 6 d. The correspondence between the two stages and those when the two expression peaks of Bm-ASH genes occure in the embryo (Fig. 7) suggests that these genes regulate the development of PNS and CNS. Expressions in wings of all the four genes are significantly higher on 1 d or 2 d after pupation than during other stages. Wing scale precursor cells form around the 2 d-old pupal stage [41], and our further studies on the scaleless wings mutant silkworm strongly proved the key role of Bm-ASH2 in the formation of wing scales (Zhou et al., unpublished).
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 51 55 51 55 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C</italic>
###xml 202 207 202 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 524 528 524 528 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ASH </italic>
###xml 572 593 572 593 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lithobius forficatus </italic>
###xml 594 600 594 600 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lf-ASH</italic>
###xml 617 636 617 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Archispirostreptus </italic>
###xml 637 644 637 644 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Asp-ASH</italic>
###xml 696 703 696 703 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-ASH </italic>
###xml 572 592 <span type="species:ncbi:7552">Lithobius forficatus</span>
Although we have known many important functions of AS-C, the analysis of the structures and evolution of these genes may suggest some of their unknown functions. We compared the protein sequences of 38 AS-C homologs from vertebrate and invertebrate. There are conserved domains besides the bHLH and C-terminal motifs, especially within vertebrates. This conservation even exists between vertebrate and invertebrate organisms. A ~20 amino acid conserved domain (corresponding to PEMRCKRRINFAQLGYNLPQ of Asp-ASH) was found in ASH genes from vertebrates and myriapod animals Lithobius forficatus (Lf-ASH, Genbank: ) and Archispirostreptus (Asp-ASH, Genbank: ). Together with the broad expression of Bm-ASH genes in tissues outside of the nervous system, they show that this gene family has other important functions waiting for exploration.
###end p 51
###begin title 52
Conclusion
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 43 57 43 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">achaete-scute </italic>
###xml 71 82 71 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx mori</italic>
###xml 92 104 92 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx mori </italic>
###xml 159 164 159 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 355 360 355 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AS-C </italic>
###xml 71 82 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
###xml 92 103 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
In this work, we isolate and identify four achaete-scute homologs from Bombyx mori. So far, Bombyx mori is the second insect which has been found to have four AS-C genes. During organism evolution, genes are duplicated with conserved domains to gain more special functions. Results of phylogenetic and gene expression analysis show that during evolution, AS-C genes duplication in insects occurs after or parallel to but not before the taxonomic order formation and functions of these genes are broad during insect development.
###end p 53
###begin title 54
Methods
###end title 54
###begin title 55
Insects and cell culture
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 391 393 373 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 4 12 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 187 195 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 307 313 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
The silkworm stock Jingsong x Haoyue was maintained in our laboratory. The insects were reared on an artificial diet at 25degreesC with 70%-80% relative humidity. Bm-N cells derived from silkworm were cultured at 27degreesC in TC-100 insect medium containing 10% heat-inactivated (56degreesC, 30 min) fetal bovine serum (Invitrogen). Cell culture details were the same as Summers and Smith [43].
###end p 56
###begin title 57
RNA isolation and RT-PCR
###end title 57
###begin p 58
###xml 231 233 231 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 0 8 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Silkworm</span>
Silkworm tissues were dissected out at different stages and the total RNA was extracted with TRIZOL Reagent (Invitrogen) according to the standard protocol, and whole embryo total RNA was extracted using the acid-guanidine method [44]. One microgramme of total RNA from each sample was used to synthesize first-strand cDNA using M-MLV Reverse Transcriptase (Promega) as the protocol described. PCR with proper program was performed using the reverse transcription product as template. Sequences of all primers used in this paper are available upon request.
###end p 58
###begin title 59
Rapid amplification of cDNA ends (RACE)
###end title 59
###begin p 60
One microgramme of total RNA was used for RACE cDNA synthesis (BD SMARTtrade mark RACE cDNA Amplification Kit, Clontech), according to the user's manual. PCR was performed with primer1 and Universal Primer A Mix (UPM, Clontech), then a nest PCR was processed with primer2 and NUP using the suitable diluted former PCR product as the template. Each PCR reaction was carried out under the following conditions: one cycle of pre-denaturing for 5 min at 95degreesC; and 30 cycles of 94degreesC for 40 s, 60degreesC for 40 s, 72degreesC for 3 min, then followed by 10 min incubation at 72degreesC.
###end p 60
###begin title 61
Database blast, protein sequences alignment and phylogenetic analysis
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 41 60 41 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila achaete </italic>
###xml 153 155 153 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 189 200 189 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bombyx mori</italic>
###xml 226 228 226 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 238 242 238 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ASH </italic>
###xml 288 290 288 290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 477 479 477 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 85 93 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 189 200 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">Bombyx mori</span>
We used the amino acid sequence coded by Drosophila achaete (Genbank: ) to blast the silkworm EST database with the blastx program on the NCBI web site [45], by limiting the organism with "Bombyx mori". As formerly described [46], insect ASH protein sequences were aligned with CLUSTALX [47] and revised manually with Gendoc software. Then a neighbor-joining (NJ) tree based on amino acid sequences was constructed using the PHYLIP software package (100 bootstrap replicates) [48].
###end p 62
###begin title 63
Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay
###end title 63
###begin p 64
###xml 116 120 116 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ASH </italic>
###xml 124 138 124 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila da </italic>
###xml 210 214 210 214 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IE1 </italic>
###xml 229 233 229 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hr3 </italic>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 322 324 322 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 723 727 715 717 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174; </sup>
###xml 862 864 843 845 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n </sup>
###xml 107 115 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
###xml 342 345 <span type="species:ncbi:12305?0.03296277145811789|species:ncbi:36410?3.8779731127197516E-4">CMV</span>
Promoter segments were cloned into Luciferase Reporter Vector pGL3-Basic separately, and the ORF region of silkworm ASH and Drosophila da (GenBank: ) genes were cloned into the modified pBacPAK8 vector with an IE1 promoter and a hr3 enhancer [40]. Then the plasmids were transfected into Bm-N cells as described formerly [49]. 0.1 mug of pRL-CMV Vector was co-transfected as an internal control reporter for each transfection. After incubating for 48 hours, the cells were collected by centrifugation at 10,000 rpm for 1 min at 4degreesC. Then the cell lysates were prepared using the passive lysis buffer, and 10 mug of each lysate was used for the dual-luciferase reporter assay according to the protocol (Dual-Luciferase(R) Reporter [DLRtrade mark] Assay System, Promega). Firefly luciferase activity and Renilla luciferase activity were determined with 20/20n Luminometer (Turner BioSystems, Inc., USA). Each treatment was repeated at least three times.
###end p 64
###begin title 65
Introduction of point mutation
###end title 65
###begin p 66
###xml 547 551 547 551 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfu </italic>
Primers P-F and P-R were designed at the terminals of the target segment sequence. Reversed primers m-R and m-F with the mutated bases were designed around the site where the point mutation would be introduced. PCRs were carried out with P-F pairing m-R or m-F paring P-R. After purification, the two PCR products were mixed, and denatured, renatured and extended for three cycles without any primers. Three cycles after running, P-F and P-R were added and the PCR was continued for 30 cycles of amplification. All of the PCRs were processed with pfu DNA polymerase.
###end p 66
###begin title 67
Quantitative real-time PCR
###end title 67
###begin p 68
###xml 52 56 52 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ASH </italic>
###xml 264 276 264 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bm-actin A3 </italic>
###xml 43 51 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silkworm</span>
Q-PCR was used to determine the changes of silkworm ASH genes expression during embryo development and wing development. Primers were designed based on the cDNA sequence and a segment around 200 bp would be specially amplified for each gene. The housekeeping gene Bm-actin A3 was used as the internal control. A 20 mul volume containing cDNA produced from about 10 ng of total RNA, 5 pmol of each primer, and 10 mul of SYBR Green Realtime PCR Master Mix (Toyobo Co., Ltd., Japan) was used for each PCR reaction. Then the PCR was processed on a Chromo4 Four-Color Real-Time System (Bio-Rad [formerly MJ Research]) under the following program: one cycle of 95degreesC for 3 min; then 40 cycles of 95degreesC for 15 sec, 60degreesC for 15 sec and 72degreesC for 30 sec. The melting curve was established from 60degreesC to 95degreesC. Three independent repeats were carried out for each reaction. Threshold cycle values were used for the further analysis.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 188 190 188 190 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sup>
###xml 195 197 195 197 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sup>
###xml 306 307 306 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F9">9</xref>
###xml 427 429 427 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F10">10</xref>
###xml 502 504 502 504 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sup>
Standard curves were constructed using serial dilutions of the ORF segments of the genes recombined into pMD18-T vectors (Takara, Japan). The log ranges of the standard curves were from 106 to 102 copies. The data curve and the melting curve showed the reliability and specificity of the PCR product (Fig. 9). Threshold cycles of each standard dilution point were used to generate a reliable standard curve for each gene (Fig. 10). Linear equations corresponding to each standard curve were made, and R2 for each standard curve was more than 0.99. This showed that the linearity of the standard curves was good.

The data curves (upper) and melting curves (lower) of Quantitative real-time PCR for Bm-actin A3 and Bm-ASH genes. These are the curves of amplifications for standard curve making. The plots show that the repeatability and specification of the Q-PCR are good.
