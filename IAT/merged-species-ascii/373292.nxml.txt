Drosophila RNase Z processes mitochondrial and nuclear pre-tRNA 3' ends in vivo
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###begin p 2
###xml 175 183 175 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 299 306 299 306 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 325 330 325 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-1</italic>
###xml 354 361 354 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 375 385 375 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 480 485 480 485 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-1</italic>
###xml 653 660 653 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
Although correct tRNA 3' ends are crucial for protein biosynthesis, generation of mature tRNA 3' ends in eukaryotes is poorly understood and has so far only been investigated in vitro. We report here for the first time that eukaryotic tRNA 3' end maturation is catalysed by the endonuclease RNase Z in vivo. Silencing of the JhI-1 gene (RNase Z homolog) in vivo with RNAi in Drosophila S2 cultured cells causes accumulation of nuclear and mitochondrial pre-tRNAs, suggesting that JhI-1 encodes both forms of the tRNA 3' endonuclease RNase Z, and establishing its biological role in endonucleolytic tRNA 3' end processing. In addition our data show that in vivo 5' processing of nuclear and mitochondrial pre-tRNAs occurs before 3' processing.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 225 226 225 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c1">1</xref>
###xml 481 482 481 482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c2">2</xref>
###xml 573 574 573 574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c3">3</xref>
Generation of functional tRNA molecules is an essential step in all organisms. Besides their central role in protein biosynthesis, tRNAs have other important functions in the cell, such as targeting proteins for degradation (1). tRNAs are first transcribed as precursors containing additional 5' and 3' sequences which have to be removed to yield functional tRNA molecules. Removal of the 5' leader sequence by the ubiquitous enzyme RNase P has been studied in detail (reviewed in 2). In contrast, we know little about the generation of the mature tRNA 3' end (reviewed in 3).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 81 97 81 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 159 160 159 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c4">4</xref>
###xml 174 191 174 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacillus subtilis</italic>
###xml 322 323 322 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c5">5</xref>
###xml 413 414 413 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c6">6</xref>
###xml 415 416 415 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c7">7</xref>
###xml 607 608 607 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c8">8</xref>
###xml 609 611 609 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c20">20</xref>
###xml 753 755 753 755 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c21">21</xref>
###xml 756 758 756 758 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c27">27</xref>
###xml 971 973 971 973 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c28">28</xref>
###xml 1129 1131 1129 1131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c29">29</xref>
###xml 81 97 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 174 191 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">Bacillus subtilis</span>
###xml 776 787 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">fruit flies</span>
###xml 1109 1114 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Maturation of pre-tRNA 3' ends in bacteria differs from organism to organism; in Escherichia coli, it is a multistep process involving endo- and exonucleases (4), whereas in Bacillus subtilis the mature tRNA 3' end of CCA-less pre-tRNAs is generated by a single endonucleolytic cut, catalysed by RNase Z (E.C. 3.1.26.11) (5). Archaeal tRNA precursors are also processed at the tRNA 3' end by an RNase Z activity (6,7). Most eukaryotic systems have been reported to employ endonucleases for cleaving pre-tRNAs directly 3' to the discriminator, although a few exonucleolytic pathways have also been reported (8-20). Exclusively endonucleolytic processing 3' to the discriminator has been observed in eukaryotic organelles (mitochondria and chloroplasts) (21-27). In mammals and fruit flies the mitochondrial DNA is transcribed into only two long primary transcripts (one for each strand) which contain the tRNA genes interspersed between the different mRNA and rRNA genes (28). Here tRNA processing is thus also vital for releasing functional mRNAs and rRNAs from the primary transcript, as first suggested for human mitochondria (29).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 182 184 182 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c30">30</xref>
In plants, nuclear RNase Z is not only required for tRNA maturation but also for snoRNA maturation, at least where a snoRNA is encoded downstream from a tRNA on a dicistronic locus (30), in which case the snoRNA is released from the primary transcript through endonucleolytic cleavage by RNase Z.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 182 183 182 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c7">7</xref>
###xml 285 287 285 287 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c31">31</xref>
The recent identification of the first RNase Z protein and gene sequences now allows the identification of orthologous proteins in all three kingdoms: bacteria, archaea and eukarya (7). These orthologous RNase Z proteins belong to a conserved group of proteins, the ELAC1/ELAC2 group (31). The shorter ELAC1 proteins (ranging from 250 to 350 amino acids) can be found in representatives of all three kingdoms, whereas the longer ELAC2 proteins (700-900 amino acids) are exclusively found in eukaryotic organisms.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 3 26 3 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 85 90 85 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-1</italic>
###xml 132 134 132 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c32">32</xref>
###xml 323 325 323 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c33">33</xref>
###xml 326 328 326 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c34">34</xref>
###xml 414 416 414 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c35">35</xref>
###xml 417 419 417 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c36">36</xref>
###xml 569 571 569 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c37">37</xref>
###xml 578 583 578 583 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-1</italic>
###xml 628 638 628 638 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 693 698 693 698 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-1</italic>
###xml 3 26 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
In Drosophila melanogaster, the sole RNase Z ortholog is an ELAC2 protein encoded by JhI-1, a juvenile hormone (JH) regulated gene (32). JH serves a number of important functions in insects, such as regulation of moulting during pre-adult development, and control of reproductive maturation and sexual behaviour in adults (33,34). At the cellular level, JH is implicated in cell proliferation and differentiation (35,36). The molecular mechanism of JH action is not yet known, and there have been few genes described so far whose expression is directly affected by JH (37). The JhI-1 gene is rapidly and specifically induced in Drosophila cultured cells in the presence of JH. Accumulation of JhI-1 transcripts requires the continuous presence of hormone, suggesting direct regulation.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 97 105 97 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 236 252 236 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila JhI-1</italic>
###xml 413 420 413 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
All data published to date concerning the eukaryotic tRNA 3' processing enzyme RNase Z have been in vitro studies. Here we show for the first time that the biological role of eukaryotic RNase Z is indeed tRNA 3' processing and that the Drosophila JhI-1 locus encodes the mitochondrial and nuclear tRNA 3' processing enzyme RNase Z. Furthermore, we find that tRNA 5' end processing precedes tRNA 3' end processing in vivo.
###end p 9

###xml 1925 1935 1925 1935 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A.thaliana</italic>
###xml 2142 2147 2142 2147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 30 35 <span type="species:ncbi:4565">wheat</span>
###xml 100 114 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D.melanogaster</span>
###xml 814 824 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A.thaliana</span>
###xml 1109 1133 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 1138 1152 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D.melanogaster</span>
###xml 1217 1229 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Homo sapiens</span>
###xml 1262 1272 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A.thaliana</span>
###xml 1901 1907 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 1925 1935 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A.thaliana</span>
###xml 2015 2020 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The isolation of RNase Z from wheat germ (7) allowed us to identify the sole homologous gene in the D.melanogaster genome (Fig. 1). Drosophila RNase Z is encoded by the JhI-1 gene, which was previously identified as JH inducible (32). Drosophila RNase Z contains two nuclear localization signals (405RPRK408 and 759RKRK762); additionally an N-terminal target sequence routes the protein to mitochondria according to two sorting servers (iPSORT, MitoProt). A second methionine residue is present at position 24 close to the predicted cleavage site of the N-terminal target sequence (position 26), suggesting that the JhI-1 gene may encode both a nuclear and a mitochondrial form of RNase Z. With 766 amino acids, the protein is almost three times as long as the originally identified RNase Z enzymes from the plant A.thaliana and the archaeon Methanococcus janaschii, and is thus a representative of the long form of RNase Z enzymes (ELAC2 proteins). Interestingly, the long RNase Z variants are only found in eukaryotic organisms and the relative distribution of ELAC1/ELAC2 proteins varies among eukaryotes. Saccharomyces cerevisiae and D.melanogaster contain only a single RNase Z protein (an ELAC2 protein), while Homo sapiens has one ELAC1 and one ELAC2 and A.thaliana encodes two ELAC1 and two ELAC2 proteins. ELAC1 and ELAC2 proteins belong to a single group since the C-terminal part of the ELAC2 proteins shows high sequence similarity to the ELAC1 proteins (including the highly conserved PDE domain (Fig. 1) (31). While it has been shown that the ELAC1 proteins and the C-terminal parts of the ELAC2 proteins have tRNA 3' processing activity (7,42), the function of the long N-terminal domain of the ELAC2 proteins is not clear. Long ELAC2 proteins appear to have evolved from duplication of the short ELAC1 proteins (31). Consistent with this theory, representatives of the ELAC1 proteins [E.coli RNase Z (43) and A.thaliana RNase Z (B.Spath and A.Marchfelder, unpublished data)] are homodimers, and the yeast ELAC2p N-terminal part (which possesses a slightly altered PDE domain) alone can process tRNAs at their 3' ends (B.Spath et al., in preparation).

###xml 869 879 869 879 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 919 924 919 924 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-1</italic>
###xml 933 941 933 941 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 943 953 943 953 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 528 537 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">fruit fly</span>
To analyse whether the JhI-1 gene indeed codes for a tRNA 3' processing endonuclease, we expressed the cDNA in insect Sf9 cells. The resulting recombinant protein has an apparent molecular weight of 92 kDa, somewhat larger than the calculated molecular weight of 83 kDa (Fig. 2A). Incubation with Drosophila nuclear pre-tRNAHis shows that the recombinant enzyme indeed cleaves the pre-tRNA to yield two products, the tRNA and the 3' trailer (Fig. 2B). Both products are the same size as the cleavage products generated with the fruit fly extract (Fig. 2B), which cleaves pre-tRNAs directly 3' to the discriminator (20,38,44). Thus recombinant Drosophila RNase Z is capable of converting a nuclear pre-tRNA into a 3' trailer and a 3' mature tRNA ready for CCA addition. Based on the sequence homology and tRNA 3' endonucleolytic activity, we conclude that JhI-1 encodes Drosophila RNase Z. We therefore re-designate the JhI-1 gene as dRNase Z, Drosophila tRNA 3'-processing endonuclease.

###xml 44 54 44 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>

Mitochondrial pre-tRNAs are also cleaved by Drosophila RNase Z in vitro
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 28 42 28 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D.melanogaster</italic>
###xml 101 103 101 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c45">45</xref>
###xml 260 270 260 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 313 323 313 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 459 469 459 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 516 519 516 519 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ile</sup>
###xml 552 553 552 553 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f2">2</xref>
###xml 28 42 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D.melanogaster</span>
The mitochondrial genome of D.melanogaster encodes 22 tRNAs which punctuate the mRNA and rRNA genes (45), suggesting that the tRNAs must be precisely excised from the long polycistronic bi-directional primary transcripts. Sorting servers predict that the lone Drosophila RNase Z is mitochondrially targeted, thus Drosophila RNase Z is likely to also be the mitochondrial tRNA 3' processing enzyme. To investigate whether mitochondrial tRNAs can be cleaved by Drosophila RNase Z, we incubated a mitochondrial pre-tRNAIle with recombinant dRNase Z (Fig. 2C). The mitochondrial pre-tRNA is efficiently converted into two products, the tRNA and the 3' trailer.

###begin title 30
###xml 23 31 23 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 23 31 23 31 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>dRNase Z</italic></bold>
Targeted disruption of dRNase Z expression in S2 cultured cells
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 87 95 87 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 185 192 185 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 351 361 351 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 414 422 414 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 426 432 426 432 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-26</italic>
###xml 434 440 434 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-26</italic>
###xml 457 467 457 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>

###xml 646 650 646 650 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rp49</italic>

###xml 1011 1019 1011 1019 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 1255 1261 1255 1261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-26</italic>
###xml 1323 1324 1323 1324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f3">3</xref>
###xml 1367 1373 1367 1373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-26</italic>
###xml 1395 1403 1395 1403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 1444 1452 1444 1452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 1470 1476 1470 1476 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">JhI-26</italic>
###xml 1541 1549 1541 1549 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
Since the recombinant dRNase Z displayed faithful tRNA 3'-end endonucleolytic activity in vitro, it was reasonable to investigate whether this enzyme is responsible for tRNA processing in vivo. We used RNAi, which in many instances has proven to be an efficient tool for disruption of targeted protein production. RNAi was carried out by transfecting Drosophila Schneider S2 (S2) cells with dsRNA corresponding to dRNase Z or JhI-26. JhI-26 is an unrelated Drosophila gene which, like dRNase Z, is expressed in S2 cells at a basal level and can be induced with JH (32). In the current experiment we used JhI-26 as a mock-transfection control and rp49 as a loading control. After transfection, cultures were allowed to grow for 1, 2 and 3 days prior to analysis. The efficiency of RNAi-mediated silencing was assessed by northern blot hybridization (Fig. 3). At all time points examined, the cells exposed to specific dsRNA fragments showed selective knockdown of the corresponding gene expression. The level of dRNase Z mRNA was significantly reduced in RNase Z RNAi cells after 1 day of treatment with dsRNA and the effect was even stronger when cells were exposed to dsRNA for up to 2-3 days, illustrating the efficiency of RNAi silencing. The level of JhI-26 mRNA was similarly reduced in JhI-26/Mock RNAi cells. Figure 3 also shows the specificity of RNAi, since JhI-26 dsRNA did not reduce dRNase Z mRNA levels, and dsRNA corresponding to dRNase Z had no effect on JhI-26 mRNA levels. RNAi can thus efficiently and specifically silence dRNase Z expression.
###end p 31
###begin title 32
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In vivo</italic>
In vivo tRNA 3' processing is catalysed by dRNase Z
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 120 123 120 123 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">His</sup>
###xml 190 198 190 198 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 255 258 255 258 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">His</sup>
###xml 298 299 298 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f4">4</xref>
###xml 345 348 345 348 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">His</sup>
###xml 390 393 390 393 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">His</sup>
###xml 428 430 428 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c20">20</xref>
###xml 431 433 431 433 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c44">44</xref>
###xml 568 571 568 571 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">His</sup>
###xml 830 838 830 838 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 1004 1012 1004 1012 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 1133 1140 1133 1140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
Next we tested RNAi cells for any defects in the tRNA processing pathway. We examined processing of nuclear encoded tRNAHis in control and mock-transfected cells versus cells with inhibited dRNase Z expression using a probe specific either for mature tRNAHis, the 5' leader or the 3' trailer (Fig. 4B). The 121 nt long primary transcript of tRNAHis consists of a 5' leader (13 nt), the tRNAHis (72 nt) and a 3' trailer (36 nt) (20,44). As expected, in control and mock-transfected cells, the 5'- and 3'-specific probes both revealed the 121 nucleotide unprocessed tRNAHis primary transcript. The dsRNA transfection did not change the pattern of hybridization with the 5'-specific probe: all samples displayed only the 121 nt band even after long exposures. In contrast, the 3'-specific probe detected an additional smaller RNA in dRNase Z RNAi cells, a 108 nt processing intermediate with a mature tRNA 5' end and a 36 nt extension at the 3' end. Importantly, the tRNA processing arrest was specific for dRNase Z RNAi cells: no processing intermediates accumulated in mock-transfected cells. We thus conclude that dRNase Z functions in vivo to process tRNA molecules at the 3' end.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 61 69 61 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 190 191 190 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f4">4</xref>
###xml 260 263 260 263 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">His</sup>
###xml 459 461 459 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c46">46</xref>
###xml 452 457 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Although the intermediate with a 3' extension accumulated in dRNase Z RNAi cells, the internal probe specific for mature tRNA revealed a 72 nt signal of equal intensity in all samples (Fig. 4B), indicating no alteration in the steady-state level of mature tRNAHis. Three explanations can be offered for this observation: (i) RNAi treatment was not fully effective, (ii) mature tRNA molecules are stable through several cell generations and (iii) as in yeast (46), there may be a back-up system for the endonucleolytic pathway.
###end p 34
###begin title 35
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
dRNase Z gene also encodes the mitochondrial tRNA 3' processing enzyme
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 166 176 166 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 192 202 192 202 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 369 370 369 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f1">1</xref>
###xml 501 511 501 511 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 536 544 536 544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 600 607 600 607 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 616 617 616 617 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f5">5</xref>
###xml 647 650 647 650 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ile</sup>
###xml 655 658 655 658 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Met</sup>
###xml 918 926 918 926 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 972 973 972 973 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f5">5</xref>
###xml 1008 1011 1008 1011 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ile</sup>
###xml 1154 1157 1154 1157 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ile</sup>
###xml 1179 1182 1179 1182 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Met</sup>
###xml 1215 1218 1215 1218 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ile</sup>
###xml 1376 1379 1376 1379 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ile</sup>
###xml 1384 1387 1384 1387 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Met</sup>
###xml 1501 1509 1501 1509 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 1568 1571 1568 1571 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ile</sup>
###xml 1622 1625 1622 1625 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ile</sup>
###xml 1723 1731 1723 1731 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 1785 1788 1785 1788 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ile</sup>
###xml 1850 1858 1850 1858 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 2224 2227 2224 2227 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Met</sup>
The dRNase Z gene may be suggested to encode the mitochondrial tRNA 3' processing enzyme for the following reasons. First, there is only a single RNase Z gene in the Drosophila genome; second Drosophila RNase Z contains a mitochondrial target sequence, and has a second methionine (amino acid 24) which is suspected to be the first methionine of the nuclear form (Fig. 1) close to the predicted target sequence cleavage site (amino acid 25), and finally, recombinant dRNase Z is capable of processing Drosophila mitochondrial pre-tRNAs in vitro. Thus we investigated whether it has the same activity in vivo. Figure 5A shows the mitochondrial tRNAIle-tRNAMet cluster, which is transcribed as part of a long precursor RNA in which the two tRNAs are separated by a 105 nt spacer. Northern blot hybridization with the 3' probe specific for the intergenic spacer revealed a 170 nt band that accumulated to a high level in dRNase Z RNAi cells, but not in mock RNAi cells (Fig. 5B). This band corresponds to a tRNAIle processing intermediate with a mature 5' end and a 105 nt extension at the 3' end. Thus RNase P efficiently produces the mature 5' end of tRNAIle and neighbouring tRNAMet, while 3' end processing of tRNAIle is inhibited by the dRNase Z knock-down. In conformity with this conclusion, in control and mock-transfected cells the 3' probe detected a stable 105 nt tRNAIle-tRNAMet intergenic spacer, whose steady-state level decreased with the increased level of the processing intermediate in dRNase Z RNAi cells. As expected, in all samples, the internal tRNAIle probe detected one major band, a 65 nt mature tRNAIle. Two important alterations were observed in the pattern of internal probe hybridization with the dRNase Z RNAi samples. First, the intensity of the mature tRNAIle band decreased progressively during 3 days of treatment with dRNase Z dsRNA, suggesting that RNAi against dRNase Z has a stronger effect on the processing of tRNAs encoded in mitochondria than in the nucleus. Second, the internal probe detected the same intermediate as the 3' probe. The corresponding band is more clearly visible after long exposure, but could not be detected with the internal probe against mature mitochondrial tRNAMet (data not shown). Again, this is consistent with our suggestion that the RNase Z knockdown abrogates cleavage at the 3' end, leading to the accumulation of tRNA processing intermediates with 3' extensions.

###begin p 37
###xml 183 186 183 186 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tyr</sup>
###xml 191 194 191 194 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cys</sup>
###xml 201 202 201 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f6">6</xref>

###xml 365 368 365 368 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Met</sup>
###xml 383 384 383 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f5">5</xref>
###xml 501 509 501 509 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 568 569 568 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f6">6</xref>
###xml 628 631 628 631 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tyr</sup>
###xml 681 684 681 684 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tyr</sup>
###xml 799 802 799 802 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tyr</sup>
###xml 920 930 920 930 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1025 1032 1025 1032 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1153 1155 1153 1155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c47">47</xref>
###xml 1221 1231 1221 1231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
To test whether the mitochondrial arrest of tRNA 3' end processing is a general outcome of dRNase Z knockdown, we also examined processing of a second mitochondrial tRNA cluster, tRNATyr-tRNACys (Fig. 6). These two genes are arranged in tandem with a 19 nt spacer and are transcribed as part of a mitochondrial polycistronic transcript analogous to the tRNAIle-tRNAMet cluster (Fig. 5A). With the 3' probe against the intergenic spacer, we detected accumulation of an 85 nt processing intermediate in dRNase Z RNAi cells, but not in wild-type or mock RNAi cells (Fig. 6), while the internal probe only detected 66 nt mature tRNATyr. The 85 nt intermediate corresponds to a pre-tRNATyr with a mature 5' end and a 19 nt extension at the 3' end. The progressive decrease in intensity of the mature tRNATyr band over 3 days of treatment with dRNase Z dsRNA was not observed in mock-transfected cells. Thus, we conclude that Drosophila RNase Z is required for processing of tRNA molecules encoded in both nucleus and mitochondria in vivo. Several tRNA modifying enzymes are known which are encoded by a single gene but routed to different cell compartments (47), and data presented here suggest that this is also the case for Drosophila RNase Z.

###begin title 38
Preferred reaction order in which cleavage by RNase P precedes RNase Z processing
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 138 146 138 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 203 205 203 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c48">48</xref>
###xml 245 252 245 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 279 282 279 282 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">His</sup>
###xml 515 516 515 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f4">4</xref>
###xml 594 601 594 601 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 659 667 659 667 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 714 716 714 716 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c20">20</xref>
###xml 840 841 840 841 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f5">5</xref>
###xml 846 847 846 847 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh182f6">6</xref>
###xml 681 690 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">fruit fly</span>
The preferred order of pre-tRNA 5' and 3' end processing has been examined with various substrates, organelles and organisms, principally in vitro, and results often favour first 5', then 3' processing (48). The present study favours this order in vivo. An 85 nt nuclear pre-tRNAHis with a 5' end leader and no 3' end trailer would be expected if 3' end processing preceded the RNase P reaction. Failure to detect the predicted 85 nt band in control and mock lanes with the 5' probe (even with long exposures; Fig. 4B and data not shown) suggests a preferred pre-tRNA processing reaction order in vivo (first RNase P, then RNase Z), in agreement with earlier in vitro results with fruit fly culture cell extracts (20). Furthermore, mitochondrial RNase P apparently cleaves efficiently when 3' end processing is impaired in RNAi cells (Figs 5 and 6).
###end p 39
###begin title 40
tRNA processing and protein biosynthetic capacity
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 4 14 4 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 178 180 178 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c32">32</xref>
###xml 208 216 208 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
The Drosophila gene encoding RNase Z was first detected by differential display of transcripts whose abundance is substantially elevated in cultured cells in the presence of JH (32). In flies, high levels of dRNase Z mRNA were found in adult females and in embryos although it is present at all developmental stages.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 48 56 48 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 242 250 242 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
###xml 503 513 503 513 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 622 624 622 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c49">49</xref>
###xml 1094 1102 1094 1102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dRNase Z</italic>
In adult females, we detected a large amount of dRNase Z transcripts produced in ovarian nurse cells and later transferred into the nascent oocyte (E.B.Dubrovsky, unpublished data). By the end of oogenesis, the egg accumulates a stockpile of dRNase Z mRNA. We suggest that maternally supplied dRNase Z contributes to the translational capacity of the developing embryo. Recent profiling of gene expression at the genomic level showed that more than 88% of genes whose expression is modulated during the Drosophila life cycle have a first peak of transcript abundance early in development, before the end of embryogenesis (49). At least 40% of these transcripts are maternally deposited; they encode proteins that are required for early embryonic events such as the set-up of pattern formation axes and the nuclear division cycles. To accommodate exceptionally high demand for protein synthesis during embryogenesis, the mature egg accumulates components of the translational machinery including the tRNA processing enzyme dRNase Z. Based on this hypothesis, it would be interesting to test the dRNase Z gene for a maternal effect.
###end p 42
###begin title 43
Do ELAC2 proteins have multiple functions?
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 4 9 4 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ELAC2</italic>
###xml 18 30 18 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 62 64 62 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c31">31</xref>
###xml 249 254 249 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ELAC2</italic>
###xml 287 288 285 286 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0</sup>
###xml 422 427 420 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ELAC2</italic>
###xml 18 30 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 76 81 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 261 266 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
###xml 416 421 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The ELAC2 gene in S.cerevisiae has been reported to be vital (31). Since in yeast cells there is a back-up system for the RNase Z-catalysed tRNA 3' processing pathway, the nuclear tRNA 3' processing function may not be the essential function of the ELAC2 gene. Yeast cells can become rho0, thus the mitochondrial tRNA 3' processing activity is also not essential. Consequently there could be another function of the yeast ELAC2 gene, loss of which leads to cell death.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 10 15 10 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ELAC2</italic>
###xml 83 85 83 85 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c31">31</xref>
###xml 242 244 238 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh182c50">50</xref>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 137 142 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 188 193 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The human ELAC2 gene is reportedly connected to the occurrence of prostate cancer (31). Recent reports showed that overexpression of the human ELAC2 protein delays the cell cycle and that human ELAC2 interacts with the gamma-tubulin complex (50). ELAC2 protein thus probably has an additional role in the cell besides tRNA 3' processing, perhaps involving cell cycle regulation.
