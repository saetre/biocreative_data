###begin article-title 0
NKX2-5 Regulates the Expression of beta-Catenin and GATA4 in Ventricular Myocytes
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: AR. Performed the experiments: AR JKT FA. Analyzed the data: AR SHZ. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: AR LKH JC BB GSVA. Wrote the paper: AR.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 342 347 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The molecular pathway that controls cardiogenesis is temporally and spatially regulated by master transcriptional regulators such as NKX2-5, Isl1, MEF2C, GATA4, and beta-catenin. The interplay between these factors and their downstream targets are not completely understood. Here, we studied regulation of beta-catenin and GATA4 by NKX2-5 in human fetal cardiac myocytes.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 346 358 343 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-catenin</italic>
###xml 363 368 357 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GATA4</italic>
Using antisense inhibition we disrupted the expression of NKX2-5 and studied changes in expression of cardiac-associated genes. Down-regulation of NKX2-5 resulted in increased beta-catenin while GATA4 was decreased. We demonstrated that this regulation was conferred by binding of NKX2-5 to specific elements (NKEs) in the promoter region of the beta-catenin and GATA4 genes. Using promoter-luciferase reporter assay combined with mutational analysis of the NKEs we demonstrated that the identified NKX2-5 binding sites were essential for the suppression of beta-catenin, and upregulation of GATA4 by NKX2-5.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 110 115 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
This study suggests that NKX2-5 modulates the beta-catenin and GATA4 transcriptional activities in developing human cardiac myocytes.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 387 390 384 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Brand1">[1]</xref>
###xml 392 395 389 392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Eisenberg1">[2]</xref>
###xml 605 608 602 605 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Kuo1">[3]</xref>
###xml 610 613 607 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Skerjanc1">[4]</xref>
###xml 627 630 624 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Bruneau1">[5]</xref>
###xml 721 724 715 718 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Kwon1">[6]</xref>
###xml 845 848 836 839 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Eisenberg1">[2]</xref>
###xml 850 853 841 844 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Schneider1">[7]</xref>
###xml 854 857 845 848 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Lickert1">[9]</xref>
###xml 1202 1206 1184 1188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Lin1">[10]</xref>
###xml 1551 1563 1530 1539 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-catenin</italic>
###xml 1568 1573 1544 1549 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GATA4</italic>
###xml 1730 1735 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 2305 2310 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
During early development myocardial cells within primary and secondary heart fields form under influence of bone morphogenetic proteins (BMPs), fibroblast growth factors (FGFs), and proteins from Wnt family, that initiate expression of a cascade of cardiac-associated transcription factors including NKX2-5, Isl1, MEF2C, GATA4, and beta-catenin and their downstream targets (reviewed in [1], [2]). The cardiac function of these transcription factors and their regulation is only partially understood. The importance of Nkx2-5, GATA4, and MEF2C in cardiac development has been demonstrated in many studies [3], [4], reviewed in [5]; the role of Wnt/beta-catenin pathway in cardiogenesis has recently begun to be unraveled [6]. Although the early studies were pointing at an inhibitory role for beta-catenin dependent Wnt pathway on cardiogenesis [2], [7]-[9], more recent studies have shown a biphasic role where beta-catenin is necessary at earlier stages of cardiomyogenesis and inhibitory at later stages of heart development. Furthermore, cardiac-specific deletion of beta-catenin has proved to be deleterious when beta-catenin is deleted in cardiac cells originated from the secondary heart fields [10], suggesting spatial difference in gene cascades that control cardiac myocyogenesis. Since NKX2-5 transcription factor is one of the earliest genes expressed in the heart cells we hypothesized that beta-catenin might be regulated by NKX2-5 in cardiac myocytes. Analysis of promoter regions identified candidate NKX2-5 binding elements (NKEs) in beta-catenin and GATA4 genes. To test if beta-catenin and GATA4 are regulated by NKX2-5, endogenous NKX2-5 expression was knocked down by expressing antisense NKX2-5 RNA (XKN) in human fetal ventricular myocytes. This study shows that beta-catenin and GATA4 transcription factors are regulated by NKE sequences in the promoter region of these genes. In addition, we confirm direct physical interactions between NKX2-5 and NKEs in the promoters of beta-catenin and GATA4 as demonstrated by electrophoretic mobility shift, chromatin immunoprecipitation, and luciferase promoter assays. This study supports the essential role of NKX2-5 in maintaining the cardiac gene expression program and suggests direct regulation of beta-catenin and GATA4 by NKX2-5 in human cardiomyocytes.

Identification of NKX2-5 binding sites in the promoters of beta-catenin and GATA4 genes
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 58 70 58 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-catenin</italic>

###xml 385 391 379 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005698-g001">Fig. 1</xref>

The genomic sequence surrounding the first exons of human beta-catenin and GATA4 genes were searched for NKX2-5-binding consensus sequence (NKE), TNAAGTG [11], using TFSEARCH. The 2 kb sequence immediately upstream of the first exon of human beta-catenin gene (CTNNB1) [12] was searched for candidate NKEs. Analysis of this sequence revealed candidate NKEs in positions -900 to -1400 (Fig. 1). Binding sites for USF (upstream stimulating factor) and other transcription factors such as SP-1, P300, ADR1, MyoD, and GATA1 were also found in this region (not shown). Similar analysis on the sequence surrounding the first exon of GATA4 gene was performed and a candidate NKEs in position -1540 was identified. The identified NKEs are located in the regions partially conserved between human and mouse when the promoter sequences are aligned using rVISTA (Fig. 1).

###begin title 15
NKX2-5 regulates the expression of beta-catenin and GATA4 in cardiac myocytes
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 184 191 177 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005698-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>
###xml 438 445 431 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005698-g002">Fig. 2B</xref>
###xml 693 700 680 687 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005698-g002">Fig. 2B</xref>
###xml 850 857 834 841 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005698-g002">Fig. 2C</xref>
###xml 1048 1055 1032 1039 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005698-g002">Fig. 2D</xref>
We further studied the regulation of beta-catenin and GATA4 by NKX2-5 in ventricular myocytes. The myocyte cultures were >90% alpha-MyHC positive as determined by immunocytochemistry (Fig. 2A). The myocyte cultures were treated with NKX2-5 antisense RNA produced from an adenovirus. The cells exposed to antisense RNA showed >95% reduction in NKX2-5 protein levels 48 hours post-treatment, while the level of PCNA control was unaffected (Fig. 2B). Antisense inhibition of NKX2-5 led to a significant increase in beta-catenin protein level suggesting that NKX2-5 negatively regulated beta-catenin, while expression of GATA4 and MEF2C was suppressed, suggesting a positive regulation by NKX2-5 (Fig. 2B). Furthermore, beta-catenin and GATA4 protein level changes were dependent on the concentration of antisense NKX2-5 (AdXKN) used in the experiments (Fig. 2C). Cardiomyocytes treated with AdXKN for 48 hrs demonstrated reduction in the expression of myosin heavy chain, an indication of disruption in cardiac cell sarcomeric structure and function (Fig. 2D). No other morphological differences were detected between the AdXKN-trandsfected cells and controls after 48 hrs.

###begin title 19
NKX2-5 binds to the beta-catenin and GATA4 promoters
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 41 53 41 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-catenin</italic>
###xml 58 63 55 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GATA4</italic>
###xml 243 247 240 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Riazi1">[13]</xref>

###xml 638 642 632 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Durocher1">[14]</xref>
###xml 702 704 696 698 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">th</sup>
###xml 713 725 707 716 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-catenin</italic>
###xml 850 855 841 846 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GATA4</italic>
###xml 860 872 851 860 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-catenin</italic>
###xml 905 912 893 900 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005698-g003">Fig. 3B</xref>
To study direct binding of NKX2-5 to the beta-catenin and GATA4 promoters through the putative NKEs, we performed electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) using nuclear extract from retrovirally-transduced C2C12 cells overexpressing NKX2-5 [13]. The binding of NKX2-5 to NKE1 within beta-catenin and to NKE-G within GATA4 promoter were examined and detected only in the presence of nuclear extract containing NKX2-5 protein (Fig. 3A). These results were further confirmed by chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) on cultured cardiac myocytes (Fig. 3B). A known NKX2-5-binding site in the atrial natriuretic factor (ANF) promoter region [14], was used as positive control, and primers amplifying the 4th exon of beta-catenin (CTNNB1-E) was used as negative control in ChIP analysis. The DNA harboring the candidate NKE sequences in the promoters of GATA4 and beta-catenin genes were immunopreceipitated (Fig. 3B). This further confirms physical interaction of NKX2-5 to the candidate NKEs in the promoters of GATA4 and beta-catenin.

Functional characterization of NKX2-5 binding sites in GATA4 and beta-catenin promoters

###xml 1316 1323 1300 1307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005698-g004">Fig. 4B</xref>
###xml 1614 1621 1598 1605 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005698-g004">Fig. 4B</xref>
###xml 674 679 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
In order to confirm that the identified sequences had either enhancer or repressor activity, we performed promoter-luciferase reporter assay. Regions surrounding the NKEs, the region between primers GF2 and GR2 for GATA4, and BF2 and BR2 for beta-catenin indicated in figure 1, were examined for repressor or enhancer activity, in COS7 cells. The region containing NKE in GATA4 promoter sequence led to an approximate 5 fold increase in the promoter activity, while the region surrounding NKEs in the beta-catenin promoter sequence repressed promoter activity by 2.5 fold, in the presence of NKX2-5 expressed from an adenovirus (Fig. 4A). Similar results were obtained when mouse NKX2-5 was expressed from a plasmid in COS7 cells carrying the promoter-luciferase constructs (data not shown). To further confirm that the candidate NKEs in the beta-catenin and GATA4 promoters were involved in binding to Nkx2-5, we mutated or deleted the candidate NKE sequences (Fig.1 and 4B). Introduction of base changes in NKE at position -912 (mNKE1) and deletions of the region encompassing the two NKEs in the 5' half of the sequence (mNKEDelta) in beta-catenin upstream sequence completely inactivated NKX2-5 repression, while base changes at positions -948 (mNKE2) and -1065 (mNKE3) had less effect on the promoter activity (Fig. 4B). Deletion of 10 bases in the 5' half not containing an NKE did not significantly change the promoter activity. Introducing base changes to the NKE in GATA4 promoter, reduced promoter activity by almost 50% when cells were treated with a high concentration of adenovirus expressing NKX2-5 (Fig. 4B).

###begin title 27
Nkx2-5 modulates Wnt/beta-catenin pathway in cardiomyocytes

###begin title 35
Discussion
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 108 111 108 111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Bruneau1">[5]</xref>
###xml 113 117 113 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Kasahara1">[17]</xref>
###xml 119 123 119 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Harvey1">[18]</xref>
###xml 333 338 333 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GATA4</italic>
###xml 343 355 343 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-catenin</italic>
###xml 926 929 917 920 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Eisenberg1">[2]</xref>
###xml 1151 1155 1139 1143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Lin1">[10]</xref>
###xml 1361 1365 1346 1350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Qyang1">[19]</xref>
###xml 1583 1587 1565 1569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Ueno1">[20]</xref>
###xml 1786 1789 1762 1765 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Lickert1">[9]</xref>
###xml 1931 1934 1907 1910 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Marvin1">[8]</xref>
###xml 2266 2270 2239 2243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Chen1">[11]</xref>
###xml 2272 2276 2245 2249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Kasahara1">[17]</xref>
###xml 2750 2754 2720 2724 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Smagulova1">[21]</xref>
###xml 2756 2763 2726 2733 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vivo</italic>
###xml 2923 2931 2887 2895 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 3071 3075 3032 3036 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Liu1">[22]</xref>
###xml 1087 1092 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1370 1375 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1732 1736 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1903 1910 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 2431 2437 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
Nkx2-5 transcription factor regulates multiple aspects of cardiac cell structure, function, and development [5], [17], [18]. Identification of genes downstream of Nkx2-5 is therefore crucial in understanding the transcriptional network that regulates cardiac myogenesis. Following identification of candidate NKEs in the promoter of GATA4 and beta-catenin genes; using EMSA and ChIP, we demonstrated that Nkx2-5 binds to the region surrounding identified sequences. Furthermore, gene reporter assay revealed that the regions encompassing NKEs were functional and that base change or deletion of the NKEs resulted in either reduction or complete loss of activation or repression of the promoter activity when Nkx2-5 was co-expressed in COS7 cells. The data presented here indicates that NKX2-5 negatively regulates beta-catenin in cardiac myocytes. beta-catenin activation is the hallmark of canonical Wnt pathway (reviewed in [2]). Both Wnts and Wnt inhibitors are expressed in the developing heart suggesting a requirement for regulated Wnt signaling. At early stages of development in mouse embryos beta-catenin directly regulates Islet1 expression [10], a gene essential for the development of cardiac cells in the secondary heart field, and therefore, beta-catenin is essential for development of cardiac cells that originate from the secondary heart field [19]. In mouse embryonic stem cells Wnt/beta-catenin appears to have a biphasic role as it enhances cardiogenesis at early stages of differentiation while conversely inhibiting cardiogenesis at later stages of development [20]. The inhibitory function of Wnt/beta-catenin in cardiogenesis is supported by several lines of evidence such as formation of multiple hearts in mice in which beta-catenin is ablated in the endoderm [9], and the blockage of cardiogenesis when Wnt-3A or Wnt-8C are ectopically expressed in the precardiac mesoderm of chicken and xenopus embryos [8]. Our results add to these findings and suggest that beta-catenin expression is repressed in cardiac myocytes at least at the gestational ages tested and that this transcriptional repression is mediated by Nkx2-5. Previous studies have suggested the presence of a transcriptional repressor domain at the carboxyl-terminus of Nkx2-5 [11], [17] that may mediate the repressor activity of Nkx2-5 on beta-catenin gene expression shown in this study. Repression of genes such as connexins 40 and 43 in murine cardiomyocytes overexpressing Nkx2-5 has been shown in other studies but the molecular nature and the possible co-factors involved are not identified. Transcriptional repression has also been demonstrated for other cardiac-associated factors such as GATA4/FOG2 complex directly repressing Lhx9 gene in the heart [21]. In vivo, examination of beta-catenin RNA level in Nkx2-5 heterozygous hearts (reduced Nkx2-5) revealed an increase in beta-catenin RNA which was in agreement with our in vitro data. A recent study has shown that activation of Wnt/beta-catenin downregulates Hdac1, a suppressor of Nkx2-5 and GATA4 in cardiomyocytes [22]. Therefore, in Nkx2-5 heterozygous hearts, higher levels of beta-catenin could increase Nkx2-5 and GATA4 via downregulation of Hdac1. It is note worthy that in Nkx2-5 heterozygous hearts the level of Nkx2-5 is not exactly half as compared to the wild type hearts. Further study is required to delineate the regulation of beta-catenin in cardiac myocytes originated from primary (e.g. left ventricle) or secondary (e.g. right ventricle) heart fields and at different developmental stages.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 168 171 168 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Kuo1">[3]</xref>
###xml 173 176 173 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Bruneau1">[5]</xref>
###xml 178 182 178 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Molkentin1">[23]</xref>
###xml 279 283 279 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Zeisberg1">[24]</xref>
###xml 370 376 370 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Nkx2-5</italic>
###xml 386 390 386 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Schwartz1">[25]</xref>
###xml 392 396 392 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Brown1">[26]</xref>
###xml 417 422 417 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Gata4</italic>
###xml 450 456 450 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Nkx2-5</italic>
###xml 556 560 556 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Brewer1">[27]</xref>
###xml 574 582 574 582 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 922 925 922 925 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1163 1167 1163 1167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Brewer1">[27]</xref>
###xml 74 79 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Moreover, our study indicated the direct regulation of GATA4 by NKX2-5 in human fetal cardiac myocytes. The role of GATA4 in early cardiogenesis is clearly established [3], [5], [23]. GATA4 inactivation results in myocardial thinning and disruption of myocyte cell proliferation [24]. The presence of high affinity GATA4 binding sites in cardiac enhancer regions of the Nkx2-5 promoter [25], [26] have suggested that Gata4 is genetically upstream of Nkx2-5. Accordingly, the expression of GATA4 is upregulated prior to Nkx2-5 in P19CL6 cardiomyocyte model [27].Treatment of in vitro cultured ventricular myocytes with antisense NKX2-5 in this study, however, led to a reduction in GATA4 level suggesting that NKX2-5 positively regulates the expression of GATA4, at least in cultured cardiac myocytes, and therefore, not only does GATA4 regulate NKX2-5, but the converse regulation exists as well. GATA4 RNA level in Nkx2-5+/- embryos, however, was unexpectedly upregulated. We reason that this upregulation represents a complex regulation of GATA4 transcription factor during development. Since GATA4 begins to express in cardiac progenitor cells prior to Nkx2-5 [27], its expression may be controlled by unknown factors that are upregulated in the Nkx2-5 heterozygous hearts due to a compensatory mechanism, and therefore, higher expression of GATA4 in the Nkx2-5 heterozygous hearts may be essential to maintain Nkx2-5 above a threshold level necessary for the normal development of the heart.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 148 156 145 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005698-g007">figure 7</xref>
###xml 162 170 159 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 307 311 304 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Montgomery1">[28]</xref>
Overall, our study proposes a model where Nkx2-5 negatively regulates beta-catenin and positively regulates GATA4 in cardiomyocytes, as depicted in figure 7. The in vitro cell culture model of fetal cardiac myocytes, such as the one used in this study, in combination with antisense RNA or siRNA technology [28] helps to elucidate the transcriptional regulatory network in cardiac myogenesis.

###begin title 41
Materials and Methods
###end title 41
###begin title 42
Cardiac myocyte culture
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 790 791 778 779 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 791 792 779 780 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">.</sup>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Human fetal hearts at gestational ages 20-22 (n = 5) weeks were collected in IMDM+20% FBS. Ventricles were separated and the fat and connective tissues were removed before mincing. The tissue was then digested by incubating in enzyme solution (1 mg/ml collagenase type II, 0.125% trypsin, 0.03% glucose) for 10 min at 37degreesC and passed through a filter. These steps were repeated 2-3 times and the cells were pelleted by centrifugation. The cell mixture was resuspended in the media and the number of live cells was counted. Fibroblasts were separated from other cardiac cells using anti-fibroblast antibody-conjugated magnetic beads purchased from Miltenyi Biotech. Inc., according to manufacturer's instruction. Cells were cultured in IMDM+20% FBS for 3-5 days at 37degreesC and 5% CO2.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
Mouse neonatal cardiomyocytes were prepared using similar protocol. Isolated cells obtained after tissue digestion were pre-plated for 1 hr to allow attachment of fibroblasts to the plate. Cells not adhered were then seeded on plates coated with 0.1% gelatin and cultured in DMEM+15% FBS for 24-48 hrs before they were used in experiments.

Mouse neonatal cardiomyocytes were prepared as described above. Cells were transfected with adenovirus expressing mouse Nkx2-5 at 1 MOI and cultured in growth media for 24 hrs. ###xml 201 205 201 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005698-Zaidi1">[29]</xref>
###xml 308 338 305 335 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="gene">tataagaattaacctgcagacagcgctctg</named-content>
###xml 358 388 355 385 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="gene">agaagaaaccctaagtgtgtcgcccccagc</named-content>
###xml 142 147 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
EMSA was performed using Lightshift Chemiluminescent EMSA kit (Pierce). Nuclear extracts were prepared from C2C12 myoblasts expressing either human Nkx2-5 or GFP [13] using method previously described [29]. Complementary oligonucleotides encompassing the putative NKX2-5 binding sites in beta-catenin (NKE1: tataagaattaacctgcagacagcgctctg) and GATA4 (NKE-G: agaagaaaccctaagtgtgtcgcccccagc) promoters were synthesized and annealed. The annealed oligonucleotides were biotinylated using 3' end DNA labeling kit (Pierce) and incubated with 5 microg of nuclear extracts for 20 min to allow binding. The mixtures were analyzed by gel electrophoresis on 5% PAGE in 0.5x TBE buffer. The membrane was hybridized with HRP-conjugated straptavidin (Pierce) for detection of DNA-protein complex according to the manufacturer's instruction.

ChIP was performed according to a published protocol [30] with some modifications. In brief, cultures of cardiac myocytes were fixed using formaldehyde. The cells were lysed with RIPA buffer and sonicated to fragment DNA. DNA-protein complexes were immunoprecipitated with 1 microg of Nkx2-5 antibody (Santa Cruz) and protein G (Roche), according to the manufacturer's instruction. The immunoprecipitated DNA was used as template in PCR using primers: ANF: (agtaagaatgcggctcttgc), (gagacagaaccctccccatt), CTNNB1-P: (tcgacaaacgtcaattttgc), (tcgattaagcagcctccaat), GATA4-P: (cccttcagccttaagttcc), (gcgacacacttagggtttcttc), and CTNNB1-E (cacccggaggtgatttacaca), (gcctggctctgatctccatgt). Amplification condition was 94degreesC- 4 min, 94degreesC- 30 sec, 50degreesC-30 sec, 72degreesC- 30 sec, for 35 cycles.
