###begin article-title 0
Gsalpha signalling suppresses PPARgamma2 generation and inhibits 3T3L1 adipogenesis
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 678 681 667 670 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
###xml 759 763 748 752 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 891 895 880 884 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 930 933 919 922 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
###xml 1094 1098 1071 1075 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 1192 1195 1169 1172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
###xml 1374 1377 1351 1354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
###xml 1446 1449 1423 1426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
###xml 1703 1712 1676 1681 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PPAR&#947;</italic>
###xml 1783 1793 1752 1758 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PPAR&#947;2</italic>
Since TSH receptor (TSHR) expression increases during adipogenesis and signals via cAMP/phospho-cAMP-response element binding protein (CREB), reported to be necessary and sufficient for adipogenesis, we hypothesised that TSHR activation would induce preadipocyte differentiation. Retroviral vectors introduced constitutively active TSHR (TSHR*) into 3T3L1 preadipocytes; despite increased cAMP (RIA) and phospho-CREB (western blot) there was no spontaneous adipogenesis (assessed morphologically, using oil red O and QPCR measurement of adipogenesis markers). We speculated that Gbetagamma signalling may be inhibitory but failed to induce adipogenesis using activated Gsalpha (gsp*). Inhibition of phosphodiesterases did not promote adipogenesis in TSHR* or gsp* populations. Furthermore, differentiation induced by adipogenic medium with pioglitazone was reduced in TSHR* and abolished in gsp* expressing 3T3L1 cells. TSHR* and gsp* did not inactivate PPARgamma (PPARG as listed in the HUGO database) by phosphorylation but expression of PPARgamma1 was reduced and PPARgamma2 undetectable in gsp*. FOXO1 phosphorylation (required to inactivate this repressor of adipogenesis) was lowest in gsp* despite the activation of AKT by phosphorylation. PROF is a mediator that facilitates FOXO1 phosphorylation by phospho-Akt. Its transcript levels remained constantly low in the gsp* population. In most measurements, the TSHR* cells were between the gsp* and control 3T3L1 preadipocytes. The enhanced down-regulation of PREF1 (adipogenesis inhibitor) permits retention of some adipogenic potential in the TSHR* population. We conclude that Gsalpha signalling impedes FOXO1 phosphorylation and thus inhibits PPARgamma transcription and the alternative promoter usage required to generate PPARgamma2, the fat-specific transcription factor necessary for adipogenesis.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Introduction
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 176 181 176 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 169 187 169 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">Drolet <italic>et al</italic>. 2008</xref>
###xml 190 198 190 198 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vitro</italic>
###xml 562 567 562 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 556 573 556 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib3">Boone <italic>et al</italic>. 1999</xref>
###xml 735 740 735 740 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 728 746 728 746 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">Reusch <italic>et al</italic>. 2000</xref>
###xml 326 332 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
Adipose tissue expands via two mechanisms, hypertrophy of individual adipocytes and hyperplasia due to the proliferation and differentiation of preadipocyte precursors (Drolet et al. 2008). In vitro protocols to induce preadipocyte differentiation (adipogenesis) use agents to increase cAMP. Furthermore, studies in the 3T3L1 murine preadipocytes, the cell line that has been central in unravelling the complex mechanisms driving adipogenesis, indicated that treatment with forskolin or isobutylmethylxanthine (IBMX) alone suffices to trigger the process (Boone et al. 1999) and it has been shown subsequently that cAMP-response element binding protein (CREB) activation is both necessary and sufficient to induce adipogenesis (Reusch et al. 2000).
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 250 271 250 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Vassart &amp; Dumont 1992</xref>
###xml 339 344 339 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 329 350 329 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Haraguchi <italic>et al</italic>. 1996</xref>
###xml 753 758 753 758 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 742 764 742 764 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib24">Vanderpump <italic>et al</italic>. 1995</xref>
Many G protein-coupled receptors, including the thyrotropin receptor (TSHR), which is the main regulator of thyroid function and growth, signal predominantly via the cAMP/protein kinase A (PKA) pathway, ultimately leading to phosphorylation of CREB (Vassart & Dumont 1992). TSHR expression is also increased during adipogenesis (Haraguchi et al. 1996). Does this suggest a role for TSHR activation in adipocyte biology? The question is clinically relevant since thyroid dysfunction is common, affecting up to 2% of the population, and the majority of these individuals will have overactivation of the TSHR either by supraphysiological concentrations of TSH in hypothyroidism or from thyroid stimulating antibodies in Graves' hyperthyroidism (Vanderpump et al. 1995).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 297 305 293 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 414 436 410 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Paschke &amp; Ludgate 1997</xref>
###xml 487 492 483 488 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 480 498 476 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">Fuhrer <italic>et al</italic>. 2003</xref>
###xml 836 841 828 833 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 830 847 822 839 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib30">Zhang <italic>et al</italic>. 2006</xref>
###xml 1809 1813 1782 1786 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 1831 1836 1804 1809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1823 1842 1796 1815 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Ludgate <italic>et al</italic>. 1999</xref>
###xml 1930 1938 1899 1907 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 2125 2129 2086 2090 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 585 590 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 675 683 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1062 1068 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
On the basis of the known signalling pathways following from TSHR activation, we hypothesise that it should trigger adipogenesis spontaneously or at least enhance that induced by known differentiating agents such as synthetic PPARgamma (PPARG as listed in HUGO database) agonists. We have used an in vitro model in which naturally occurring constitutively active mutant forms of TSHR, found in e.g. toxic nodules (Paschke & Ludgate 1997), are introduced using retroviral vectors (Fuhrer et al. 2003). Our previous studies used the model to investigate the effect of TSHR activation on human orbital primary preadipocytes (of relevance to the eye disease occurring in Graves' patients). We demonstrated spontaneous induction of early, but inhibition of the later stages of adipogenesis, even in the presence of PPARgamma agonists (Zhang et al. 2006). Orbital preadipocytes are a particular fat depot, being derived from the neural crest, and this may explain the differences between our results following activation of the cAMP pathway and those reported using a murine preadipocyte cell line. In the current study, we have applied the model (activating mutant TSHR-L629F) to 3T3L1. To our surprise, despite modestly increasing intracellular cAMP and elevating phospho-CREB levels, TSHR activation did not induce adipogenesis and this was not changed by adding a phosphodiesterase inhibitor. Furthermore, adipogenesis induced by PPARgamma agonists was significantly reduced in the mutant TSHR expressing cells compared with the non-modified population. Since TSHR activation liberates two functional moieties of the Gs protein, alpha (which acts via PKA) and betagamma (signals via PI3K), we suggested that the inhibitory effects were due to the latter. Thus, we have introduced constitutively active Gsalpha (gsp*), Q227L (Ludgate et al. 1999), which yields only the alpha subunit. Again there was no spontaneous adipogenesis and in vitro induced differentiation was completely abolished. Further investigations revealed a reduction in PPARgamma1 and the complete absence of PPARgamma2 proteins, the fat-specific isoform, in gsp* expressing 3T3L1.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Materials and Methods
###end title 6
###begin title 7
Reagent source; cell culture and adipogenesis protocols
###end title 7
###begin p 8
All chemicals were obtained from Sigma-Aldrich and tissue culture media and serum from BioWhittaker-Lonza (Verviers, Belgium) unless otherwise stated. The 3T3L1 cell line was purchased from the ATCC (Atlanta, GA, USA).
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 390 395 387 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 384 401 381 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib30">Zhang <italic>et al</italic>. 2006</xref>
###xml 6 12 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
3T3L1 murine preadipocytes were routinely cultured in DMEM/F12 10% FCS (complete medium, CM). Adipogenesis was induced in confluent cells by replacing with differentiation medium (DM) containing 5% FCS, biotin (33 muM), panthothenate (17 muM), tri-iodothyronine (1 nM), dexa-methasone (100 nM), thiazolidinedione (1 muM) and insulin (500 nM), for 10-12 days, as previously described (Zhang et al. 2006).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 68 71 64 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>

###xml 18 23 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 48 51 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 549 554 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 564 567 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Activating mutant human TSHR, L629F (TSHR*) and rat Gsalpha, Q227L (gsp*), were introduced using retroviral vectors, previously produced in our laboratory (Ivan et al. 1997, Fuhrer et al. 2003). All experiments were performed on the mixed pools of cells that resulted from geneticin selection. Furthermore, at least two independent mixed pools were generated for each cell population, with results being comparable. Initial characterisation of the G418 resistant pools of 3T3L1 employed RT-PCR and direct sequencing to demonstrate the expression of human TSHR and rat Gsalpha in the appropriate populations as previously described (Ivan et al. 1997, Fuhrer et al. 2003). In many of our experiments we also included 3T3L1 populations transduced with the wild-type (WT) and a second mutant form of TSHR, M453T. Results obtained (basal levels of cAMP/pCREB, transcript measures for PPARgamma and GPDH, etc.) indicated that there was no significant difference in the behaviour of WT compared with non-modified or M453T compared with L629F. In the interests of brevity, the results are reported for the non-modified, L629F and gsp* expressing populations.

###begin title 11
Effect on cAMP levels
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 50 53 50 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
###xml 83 84 83 84 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sup>
###xml 178 180 178 180 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;4</sup>
###xml 299 301 299 301 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;5</sup>
###xml 525 530 525 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 518 536 518 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">Fuhrer <italic>et al</italic>. 2003</xref>
###xml 655 656 655 656 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sup>
###xml 250 256 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
The non-modified 3T3L1 and populations expressing gsp* or TSHR* were plated at 5x104/well in 12-well plates. The following day they were incubated for 4 h in medium containing 10-4 M IBMX with no further addition (basal conditions), or plus 10 mU/ml bovine TSH. Forskolin (in addition to IBMX) at 10-5 M was included as a positive control in all populations. cAMP was extracted in 0.1 M HCl and measured using an in-house RIA capable of detecting femtogram quantities of the second messenger, as previously described (Fuhrer et al. 2003). Coulter counting of adjacent wells provided an accurate cell number for results to be expressed as picomoles cAMP/104 cells.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Western blotting
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 453 454 453 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 453 454 453 454 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>g</italic></bold>
The three different 3T3L1 populations were propagated in duplicate in 6-well plates in CM or DM. Proteins were extracted, at various time points, in Laemmli buffer containing 1 mM sodium orthovanadate and 1 mM phenylmethylsulphonyl fluoride. To prepare nuclear and cytosolic fractions, cells were harvested in ice-cold PBS, centrifuged and resuspended in HEPES buffer containing protease inhibitors. The supernatant produced by centrifugation at 12 000 g provided the cytosolic and high-salt extraction of the pellet, the nuclear fractions respectively.
###end p 14

###xml 143 165 142 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib1">Al-Khafaji <italic>et al</italic>. 2005</xref>

Samples (containing 20 mug protein) were separated by 10% SDS-PAGE and then the gel electroblotted onto PVDF membrane as previously described (Al-Khafaji et al. 2005). To investigate various signalling pathways, the blots were probed with the following rabbit antibodies (all from Cell Signalling Technology unless otherwise stated): anti phospho-CREB (Ser 133, 1:2000 overnight; 4 degreesC); anti total-CREB (1:1000, room temperature, 1 h; Santa CruzBiotechnology, Santa Cruz, CA, USA); anti phospho-Akt (Thr 308, 1:1000, 4 degreesC, overnight); anti total-Akt (1:1000, room temperature, 1 h, Santa Cruz); and anti phospho-SYK (Tyr 352, 1:5000, overnight, 4 degreesC, BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA). To investigate transcription factors, rabbit antibodies to phospho-PPARgamma (Ser 82, 1:1000 overnight, 4 degreesC, Upstate), anti total-PPARgamma (1:1000, overnight, 4 degreesC), anti total-FOXO1 (1:1000, overnight, 4 degreesC) and anti phospho-FOXO1 (Ser 256, 1:1000, overnight, 4 degreesC) were employed. In all cases proteins were detected using either an anti-mouse IgG-HRP conjugate or an anti-rabbit IgG-HRP conjugate (1:5000, room temperature for 1 h, GE Healthcare, Amersham, UK) and visualised by enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL Plus, GE Healthcare).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
Films were analysed using the Alpha Imager 1200 digital imaging system (Alpha Innotech Corp., San Leandro, CA, USA). The blots were initially probed with the phospho-specific antibodies; they were then stripped and reprobed with antibodies that recognise total proteins.

###begin title 27
TSHR* and gsp* increase unstimulated cAMP levels
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 74 75 72 73 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sup>
###xml 106 112 102 108 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">s.e.m.</sc>
###xml 256 259 252 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
###xml 282 283 276 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
###xml 306 307 298 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
###xml 490 493 480 483 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
###xml 613 618 603 608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 603 624 593 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Haraguchi <italic>et al</italic>. 1996</xref>
In non-modified 3T3L1, the unstimulated level of cAMP was 397+/-18 pmol/104 cells (results are the mean+/-s.e.m. of three experiments all performed in at least triplicate). Unstimulated cAMP levels were significantly increased in cells expressing TSHR* or gsp* reaching 129+/-3.9% (P<0.02) and 140+/-5.9% (P<0.01) respectively of the non-modified population. TSH (1 mU/ml) elicited a cAMP response in cells expressing mutant TSHR (650+/-218% of unstimulated) but not in the non-modified or gsp* 3T3L1 cells, in keeping with the very low level of endogenous TSHR in these cells prior to differentiation (Haraguchi et al. 1996). There was no significant difference in the response to forskolin, which induced a robust >20-fold increase in cAMP in all the three populations.

###begin title 29
Phosphorylated CREB levels are increased in TSHR* and gsp* expressing cells and are modulated during adipogenesis
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 560 563 560 563 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
###xml 662 663 660 661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
###xml 674 677 672 675 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
###xml 692 693 688 689 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
###xml 790 796 786 792 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Fig. 1</xref>
Western blots to investigate activation of CREB by phosphorylation are usually performed following acute stimulation of the cells followed by analysis in the minutes after exposure to the stimulant. By contrast, we have examined the basal levels of phospho-CREB in the three populations of 3T3L1 to assess the effects of chronic activation via the various mutants. We have used a value of 1 for the phosphorylated:total CREB ratio of the non-modified preadipocytes. Based on at least seven experiments using two independently generated populations of TSHR* or gsp* expressing cells, there was a modest, but significant increase in the ratio in L629F (1.5+/-0.58 P<0.02) and gsp* (1.19+/-0.22 P<0.02), reflecting chronic activation of this pathway. A representative western blot is shown in Fig. 1.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 382 383 382 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
###xml 577 580 577 580 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
To investigate whether CREB phosphorylation is affected by the adipogenic process, we have measured the phosphorylated:total CREB ratios in samples obtained from the different 3T3L1 populations at various time points following addition of DM. In the non-modified cells there is a slight reduction in the ratio in mid-differentiation, but a significant increase in the later stages (P<0.05), to achieve levels 150-300% of those on day 0 (prior to addition of DM). Apart from the higher basal levels of phospho-CREB, a similar pattern of expression was apparent in the TSHR* and gsp* expressing cells through adipogenesis (data not shown).
###end p 31
###begin title 32
The increased phospho-CREB levels do not produce spontaneous adipogenesis
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 150 153 150 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
In CM, even when the cells had been confluent for up to 10 days, there were no morphological changes consistent with adipogenesis in cells expressing gsp* or TSHR* when compared with the control 3T3L1. Occasional cells containing small lipid vacuoles were observed in all populations.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 72 77 72 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PREF1</italic>
###xml 79 88 79 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PPAR&#947;</italic>
###xml 92 96 88 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GPDH</italic>
###xml 126 129 122 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
Furthermore, we did not see a consistent change in transcript levels of PREF1, PPARgamma or GPDH in cells expressing TSHR* or gsp* when compared with the non-modified population (data not shown).
###end p 34
###begin p 35
Since chronic stimulation of the cAMP pathway might induce up-regulation of e.g. phosphodiesterases, we included a phosphodiesterase inhibitor (IBMX) in the culture medium. Cells were allowed to reach confluence in CM and 0.5 mM IBMX was added for various time periods, but there was still no morphological evidence for adipogenesis and the transcript levels for markers of differentiation were not significantly changed (data not shown).
###end p 35
###begin title 36
TSHR* and gsp* inhibit induced adipogenesis by affecting PPARgamma isoforms
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 261 265 257 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>

PPARgamma agonist induced differentiation of the non-modified cells produced the expected change in morphology and similar signs of adipogenesis were also present in the L629F expressing cells, but at a reduced level compared with the control; by contrast, the gsp* population were completely devoid of differentiating cells, as illustrated in Fig. 2, by the absence of oil red O stained cells.

###begin p 38

###xml 1098 1099 1082 1083 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
###xml 1217 1221 1201 1205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 1235 1236 1219 1220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
###xml 1338 1342 1320 1324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 1396 1401 1378 1383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PREF1</italic>
We compared transcripts for markers of adipogenesis in non-modified, L629F and gsp* expressing cells; statistical analyses of the results reported as fold changes are shown in Table 2. Figure 3 is a representative experiment illustrating that PPARgamma agonist induced differentiation of non-modified 3T3L1 resulted in sustained and significant increases in PPARgamma and GPDH (P<0.05 and 0.02 respectively). The L629F and gsp* populations displayed an attenuated increase in PPARgamma (although not significantly different from non-modified) and in GPDH (both significantly less than non-modified) being more severe in the gsp* expressing cells in keeping with their morphological appearance. By contrast, the reduction to 89+/-0.07% of pre-treatment values in the gsp* population is not significantly different from day 0 PREF1 transcripts in CM in these cells.

###begin p 39
###xml 72 73 72 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</italic>
###xml 247 251 245 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 355 356 351 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
###xml 379 380 373 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
###xml 573 577 567 571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
Coulter counting of the cells in the first 3 days after addition of DM (n=3) revealed that the non-modified 3T3L1 had a 2.4+/-0.9-fold increase in cell number on day 3 compared with day 0, indicating mitotic clonal expansion (MCE). Both L629F and gsp* populations displayed significantly increased proliferation compared with the non-modified, 4.9+/-0.5 (P<0.005) and 3.3+/-0.7 (P<0.01) fold respectively, suggesting no influence of either mutation on MCE. In the following seven days, there was no further increase in either the non-modified or L629F cell number, but the gsp* population continued to proliferate, in keeping with the absence of differentiation.

###xml 83 88 79 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 80 94 76 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib10">Hu <italic>et al</italic>. 1996</xref>
###xml 222 228 214 220 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Fig. 4</xref>

We were unable to detect any phosphorylated PPARgamma either in total cell lysates or nuclear extracts. However, as shown in Fig. 4, the expression of PPARgamma1 is greatly reduced (at all time points) in L629F and gsp* and PPARgamma2 is completely absent from the latter, in contrast to the non-modified 3T3L1.

PPARgamma1 expression continues to increase throughout adipogenesis, but PPARgamma2 expression is at the limit of detection during the MCE stage, but then resumes in the terminal stages of differentiation.

###begin title 42
Reduced FOXO1 phosphorylation may explain the lack of PPARgamma2
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 25 30 25 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FOXO1</italic>
###xml 93 98 85 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 86 104 78 96 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Armoni <italic>et al</italic>. 2006</xref>
###xml 206 211 198 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 200 217 192 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Nakae <italic>et al</italic>. 2003</xref>
###xml 238 244 230 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig5">Fig. 5</xref>
###xml 476 480 468 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
The transcription factor FOXO1 represses the promoters for PPARgamma1 and PPARgamma2 (Armoni et al. 2006); it is inactivated by phosphorylation when it translocates from the nucleus to the cytoplasm (Nakae et al. 2003). As can be seen in Fig. 5, in the non-modified cells total FOXO1 protein expression increases in the first 24 h after addition of DM in the nucleus, where it is highly phosphorylated. It is essentially absent from the cytoplasm during the MCE phase. In the gsp* population, total FOXO1 protein expression is higher in basal conditions, its expression is increased in DM and we observe its translocation from the cytoplasm to the nucleus between days 1 and 3. The main difference is the considerably decreased level of phosphorylation from day 3 onwards compared with the non-modified 3T3L1. The TSHR* cells' behaviour is midway between that of the other two populations.
###end p 43
###begin title 44
What accounts for the behaviour of FOXO1?
###end title 44
###begin p 45
###xml 108 113 108 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 101 119 101 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Sakaue <italic>et al</italic>. 1998</xref>
###xml 258 264 258 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig6">Fig. 6</xref>
###xml 359 362 359 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp</italic>
Phosphorylation-dependent inactivation of FOXO1 depends chiefly on the PI3K and phospho-Akt pathway (Sakaue et al. 1998). We investigated and observed an early increase in the proportion of phospho-Akt in all populations of 3T3L1 cells after addition of DM (Fig. 6), indicating the need for an alternative explanation for the reduced FOXO1 phosphorylation in gsp*.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 174 198 174 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Fritzius &amp; Moelling 2008</xref>
###xml 372 377 368 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 367 383 363 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Park <italic>et al</italic>. 2008</xref>
###xml 561 564 557 560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ID2</italic>
###xml 580 584 576 580 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 653 659 649 655 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig7">Fig. 7</xref>
###xml 661 665 657 661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PROF</italic>
###xml 809 813 805 809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 871 875 867 871 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
Two novel modulators of adipogenesis have recently been described. PROF is a mediator between AKT and FOXO1 whose expression is transiently up-regulated during adipogenesis (Fritzius & Moelling 2008). ID2 is a small molecule, whose transcription also increases during adipogenesis and interacts with as yet to be identified factors to stimulate PPARgamma expression (Park et al. 2008). We investigated their expression and found a transient increase in ID2 TCN in all three populations in the first 4-8 h following addition of DM, although transcript levels of ID2 were lowest in gsp* cells at all time points (data not shown). By contrast, as shown in Fig. 7, PROF transcription was increased in the non-modified and, to a lesser extent, in the L629F populations, but remained at a constant low level in the gsp* cells. Thus, despite the abundance of phospho-Akt in the gsp* population, the paucity of PROF prevents inactivation of FOXO1 by phosphorylation, PPARgamma2 is not expressed and adipogenesis does not occur.

###begin title 47
Discussion
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 161 168 161 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ex vivo</italic>
###xml 333 338 333 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 325 344 325 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">Starkey <italic>et al</italic>. 2003</xref>
###xml 949 956 949 956 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1117 1122 1117 1122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1109 1128 1109 1128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Ludgate <italic>et al</italic>. 1999</xref>
###xml 1137 1142 1137 1142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1130 1148 1130 1148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">Fuhrer <italic>et al</italic>. 2003</xref>
###xml 194 202 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Our initial aim was to investigate the effects of TSHR activation on 3T3L1 preadipocytes, following our previous studies demonstrating increased adipogenesis in ex vivo fat samples from Graves' patients, in whom thyroid stimulating antibodies (as opposed to gain-of-function mutation) produce chronic activation of the TSHR (Starkey et al. 2003). From reports of a pro-adipogenic effect of agents capable of elevating cAMP, we hypothesised that spontaneous adipogenesis would arise in preadipocytes expressing naturally occurring (e.g. in toxic thyroid adenoma) gain-of-function TSHR mutations (which like thyroid stimulating antibodies, activate adenylate cyclase/PKA), but this was not the case. It should be noted that the increase in cAMP in preadipocytes using retroviral vectors to express the mutant receptor is considerably lower than that obtained following overexpression by transfection of COS cells. The model more closely resembles the in vivo situation, in which modest elevation of cAMP is sufficient to generate a thyroid toxic nodule, and concurs with our previous findings in thyroid cells (Ludgate et al. 1999, Fuhrer et al. 2003). The constitutively active mutant TSHR responded further when stimulated with TSH, in common with other such mutants, although its TSH responsiveness was less than the WT TSHR (data not shown) as reported by others .
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 391 396 387 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 384 402 380 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">Reusch <italic>et al</italic>. 2000</xref>
We have then used a gain-of-function mutation in a second component of the PKA pathway, Gsalpha subunit, to achieve activation of CREB. Neither the receptor, nor its downstream G protein, induced spontaneous adipogenesis (even in the presence of IBMX) as would be expected from previous reports indicating that CREB activation is 'necessary and sufficient for adipogenesis in 3T3L1' (Reusch et al. 2000).
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 72 76 72 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 80 88 80 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 242 247 242 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 236 253 236 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib30">Zhang <italic>et al</italic>. 2006</xref>
###xml 366 371 362 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 361 379 357 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib27">Wang <italic>et al</italic>. (1992)</xref>
###xml 506 524 502 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">Wang &amp; Malbon 1999</xref>
###xml 947 951 939 943 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 199 204 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Experiments were then conducted to investigate the effects of TSHR* and gsp* on in vitro induced adipogenesis. The reduced differentiation in 3T3L1 expressing TSHR* mirror the results we obtained in human primary orbital preadipocytes (Zhang et al. 2006). Our demonstration of an inhibitory role for Gsalpha in adipogenesis confirms and extends the findings of Wang et al. (1992) who demonstrated enhanced differentiation in 3T3L1 cells treated with oligomers antisense to the protein. Subsequently, they (Wang & Malbon 1999) reported that Gsalpha repression of adipogenesis is mediated by the tyrosine kinase Syk, but how this may impact adipogenesis is not clear and we are not aware of further studies to address this question. Our investigations of the pathway confirmed their findings, i.e. an early and transient increase in phospho-SYK in the first day following induced adipogenesis, but only in the non-modified population. The L629F and gsp* expressing cells showed a decrease in phospho-SYK levels (data not shown).
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 64 68 64 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 243 248 235 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 234 254 226 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Tontonoz <italic>et al</italic>. 1995</xref>
Our experiments illustrate that the failure to differentiate in gsp* is due to reduced expression of PPARgamma1 and interference in the alternate promoter usage required to generate PPARgamma2, the isoform specific to adipose tissue (Tontonoz et al. 1995).
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 601 606 589 594 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 593 612 581 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib19">Saladin <italic>et al</italic>. 1999</xref>
There is some controversy concerning how PPARgamma2 expression changes following addition of DM. Some authors report that it is induced, others that it is up-regulated. In our differentiation protocol; PPARgamma2 protein expression increases in the first 24 h, but is then repressed until day 5 post-induction. Its reappearance seems to coincide with the start of the terminal differentiation phase and was a consistent finding in all experiments, but contrasts with the results obtained by others reporting that PPARgamma2 expression steadily increases through all stages of differentiation (Saladin et al. 1999).
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 88 93 80 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 81 99 73 91 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Armoni <italic>et al</italic>. 2006</xref>
###xml 648 653 640 645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 642 661 634 653 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Nakae <italic>et al</italic>. (2003)</xref>
###xml 1185 1189 1177 1181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
FOXO1 represses transcription from both the PPARgamma1 and PPARgamma2 promoters (Armoni et al. 2006) and so we investigated its participation in our different 3T3L1 populations. We observed a rapid increase in FOXO1 protein expression and its subsequent translocation to the nucleus during the first 3 days post-induction in non-modified 3T3L1. At later time points, the nuclear FOXO1 sustained a high degree of phosphorylation. The antibody we have used detects phosphorylation of FOXO1 at serine 256, located in the forkhead domain and indicative of inactivation of the transcription factor; there are two additional phosphorylation sites. Nakae et al. (2003) transduced cells with a tagged FOXO1 and were able to demonstrate a cytoplasmic location for phosphorylated FOXO1. In our experiments investigating endogenous FOXO1, it is predominantly cytoplasmic, then nuclear and then present in both fractions, in agreement with this work. Any phosphorylated endogenous FOXO1 in our nuclear extracts is probably partially phosphorylated (on serine 256, the preferred target for the kinase) and thus inactive. Our experiment also revealed that phosphorylation of FOXO1 is impeded in the gsp* population from day 3 post-induction onwards and so provides some explanation for their lack of adipogenesis.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 113 117 113 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 434 458 434 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Fritzius &amp; Moelling 2008</xref>
###xml 461 470 461 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In silico</italic>
###xml 704 709 704 709 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 698 715 698 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib29">Zhang <italic>et al</italic>. 2005</xref>
###xml 864 868 864 868 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
At no time point is signalling via phospho-Akt compromised and yet FOXO1 phosphorylation had remained low in the gsp* cells. Investigation of two recently described modulators of adipogenesis, ID2 and PROF suggested they may have a role in explaining this anomaly. PROF, also known as WDFY2, is a member of the WD-repeat propeller-FYVE protein family, which is a binding partner for phospho-Akt and facilitates FOXO1 phosphorylation (Fritzius & Moelling 2008). In silico analysis of its proximal promoter reveals the presence of a potentially functional half-site cAMP response element; however, it lacks a 'tata' box, generally thought to be required for robust transcriptional induction by cAMP (Zhang et al. 2005). Our investigations demonstrate that its expression level, although higher in basal conditions, remained fairly constant at all time points in the gsp* cells following addition of DM, in contrast to the marked increase in the non-modified 3T3L1.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 66 70 62 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 322 327 300 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 317 333 295 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib6">Ford <italic>et al</italic>. 1998</xref>
We also considered the possibility that the excess Gsalpha in the gsp* expressing cells might sequester free Gbetagamma subunits generated during adipogenesis and thus impede their downstream effects. However, the mutation in alpha renders it permanently in the GTP-bound form and thus less likely to bind betagamma (Ford et al. 1998).
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 105 109 105 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
###xml 316 321 305 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 307 327 296 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib28">Zaballos <italic>et al</italic>. 2008</xref>
###xml 401 406 386 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 397 412 382 397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib12">Kim <italic>et al</italic>. 2007</xref>
###xml 561 567 546 552 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig8">Fig. 8</xref>
###xml 671 675 656 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gsp*</italic>
In the majority of our experiments, the behaviour of the TSHR* population is mid-way between that of the gsp* expressing and parent cell lines, indicating that the inhibitory effects of Gsalpha are abrogated, presumably by the beta/gamma subunits, which in the thyroid have been reported to signal by PI3K (Zaballos et al. 2008). PREF1 inhibits adipogenesis by preventing induction of PPARgamma2 (Kim et al. 2007). The significantly enhanced down-regulation of PREF1 in the TSHR* expressing cells may contribute to the rescue mechanism. The drawing depicted in Fig. 8 summarises the mechanism we propose to explain the difference in adipogenic potential of the TSHR* and gsp* expressing 3T3L1 populations.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 143 153 139 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PPAR&#947;2</italic>
In conclusion, our results identify an important role for Gsalpha signalling in inhibiting the alternative promoter usage necessary to produce PPARgamma2 transcripts.
