###begin article-title 0
Androgen Receptor Copy Number Variation and Androgenetic Alopecia: A Case-Control Study
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: JEC SJW JAE. Performed the experiments: JEC SJW. Analyzed the data: JEC SJW JAE. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: JEC SJW SBH JAE. Wrote the paper: JEC SJW SBH JAE.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 96 98 96 98 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 395 397 395 397 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 518 520 518 520 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 596 598 596 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 182 185 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 206 209 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 377 380 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 521 524 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 574 577 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
The functional polymorphism that explains the established association of the androgen receptor (AR) with androgenetic alopecia (AGA) remains unidentified, but Copy Number Variation (CNV) might be relevant. CNV involves changes in copy number of large segments of DNA, leading to the altered dosage of gene regulators or genes themselves. Two recent reports indicate regions of CNV in and around AR, and these have not been studied in relation to AGA. The aim of this preliminary case-control study was to determine if AR CNV is associated with AGA, with the hypothesis that CNV is the functional AR variant contributing to this condition.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 84 86 84 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 140 142 140 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 259 261 259 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 359 361 359 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 72 75 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 149 152 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">men</span>
###xml 252 255 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 362 365 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
Multiplex Ligation-dependent Probe Amplification was used to screen for CNV in five AR exons and a conserved, non-coding region upstream of AR in 85 men carefully selected as cases and controls for maximal phenotypic contrast. There was no evidence of CNV in AR in any of the cases or controls, and thus no evidence of significant association between AGA and AR CNV.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 58 60 58 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
The results suggest this form of genomic variation at the AR locus is unlikely to predispose to AGA.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 59 61 59 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 192 195 192 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Ellis1">[1]</xref>
###xml 222 224 222 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 283 286 283 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Hayes1">[2]</xref>
###xml 287 290 287 290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-LevyNissenbaum1">[4]</xref>
###xml 441 443 441 443 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 526 529 526 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Ellis2">[5]</xref>
The importance of the gene encoding the androgen receptor (AR; NCBI NM_000044) in the development of androgenetic alopecia (AGA; OMIM %300710) is now well established. Our initial observation [1] of genetic association of AR with AGA has been replicated in three independent studies [2]-[4]. Although association with several single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) has been demonstrated, none have proven causative. We have shown that known AR coding sequence polymorphisms such as triplet repeats are not associated with AGA [5], and mutation screening has identified no other relevant coding sequence mutations. Other forms of variation that might affect gene expression should now be considered.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 95 98 95 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Redon1">[6]</xref>
###xml 99 102 99 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-McCarroll1">[8]</xref>
###xml 318 321 318 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Redon1">[6]</xref>
###xml 323 326 323 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Beckmann1">[9]</xref>
###xml 23 26 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 265 268 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
Copy Number Variation (CNV) has been recognised recently as a common form of genomic variation [6]-[8] in which DNA segments (>1 kb) are altered in copy number compared to a reference genome. Recent reports have suggested that up to 12% of the genome is subject to CNV, accounting for at least 1% of genetic variation [6], [9].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 39 41 39 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 41 44 41 44 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Redon1">[6]</xref>
###xml 46 49 46 49 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-McCarroll1">[8]</xref>
###xml 119 122 119 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Redon1">[6]</xref>
###xml 329 331 329 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 0 3 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 115 118 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 127 130 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 340 343 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">men</span>
CNV has been reported in the region of AR[6], [8] and SNPs can be co-inherited in linkage disequilibrium (LD) with CNV [6]. As CNV might result in variable copies of genetic regulatory regions or copies of the gene itself, such variation might underpin the tissue- and developmental stage-specific abnormalities in expression of AR seen in men predisposed to AGA.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 21 23 21 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 129 131 129 131 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 24 27 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 118 121 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 187 190 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
We hypothesised that AR CNV could be associated with AGA. Therefore, our approach was to first search for evidence of CNV at the AR locus and then, if present, to test for association of CNV with AGA.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Materials and Methods
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Subjects
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 227 231 227 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Harrap1">[10]</xref>
###xml 340 344 340 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Taylor1">[11]</xref>
###xml 132 143 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participant</span>
###xml 360 363 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">men</span>
###xml 517 520 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">men</span>
The study was approved by the Ethics Review Committee of the Alfred Hospital Melbourne, and informed consent was obtained from each participant. Subjects were drawn from the population-based Victorian Family Heart Study cohort [10]. AGA phenotypes were gathered by way of direct assessment by trained observers and validated questionnaires [11]. Cases were 34 men aged 20-30 years (mean 27 years) with cosmetically significant baldness (Hamilton-Norwood types III vertex to VII, median type IV), and controls were 51 men aged 54-68 years (mean 56 years) with no indication of baldness (Hamilton-Norwood type I).
###end p 15
###begin title 16
MLPA analysis
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 216 218 216 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 253 255 253 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 336 343 336 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0005081-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 348 356 348 356 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005081-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 454 461 454 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0005081-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 554 559 554 559 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 559 563 559 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-White1">[12]</xref>
###xml 691 695 691 695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-White1">[12]</xref>
###xml 779 783 779 783 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Lalic1">[13]</xref>
###xml 205 208 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 317 320 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
Peripheral blood was collected from each subject, and DNA was extracted using standard phenol-chloroform methods. Multiplex Ligation-Dependent Probe Analysis (MLPA) was performed to detect the presence of CNV around AR. Six probes were selected to test AR exons and one upstream non-coding region for the presence of CNV (probes 1 - 6; Table 1 and Figure 1). Two further probes (7 and 8) in unlinked autosomal loci were used as reference point controls (Table 1). Peak heights were analysed for the eight probes for each DNA sample, as outlined in White et al[12]. Briefly, peak heights for each probe were standardized to control peaks and the median ratio for each probe normalised to 1.0 [12]. We set threshold values for duplication and deletions of 1.5 and 0.5 respectively [13]. All samples were analysed at least twice.
###end p 17

###begin title 22
Results
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 0 8 0 8 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005081-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 40 42 40 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 128 130 128 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 339 341 339 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 434 437 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
Figure 2 shows data for each of the six AR test probes in all case-control subjects. The standard deviation for each of the six AR test probes was within 15%, except for probes 2 and 6 which were slightly higher, possibly suggesting less reliable performance of the probes. Nevertheless, across the remaining samples, good coverage of the AR region was achieved by the higher performing probes, and the results provide no evidence of CNV in this region, either as deletion or duplication, in cases or controls.

###begin title 26
Discussion
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 38 40 38 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 208 212 208 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Stranger1">[14]</xref>
###xml 366 368 366 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 20 23 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 84 87 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 340 343 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 477 480 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
This study examined CNV in and around AR and their potential relationship with AGA. CNV may contribute to differences in gene expression through duplications or deletions of genes or gene regulatory elements [14], and may in part account for genetic predisposition to common complex conditions such as AGA. However, we found no evidence of CNV in the regions of the AR that we examined. Insofar as our original hypothesis, we could therefore find no evidence of association of CNV with AGA.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 126 128 126 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 277 282 277 282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 327 329 327 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 345 349 345 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-McCarroll2">[15]</xref>
###xml 201 204 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 316 319 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
Although data generated by probes 2 and 6 showed relatively high standard deviations, casting doubt on their reliability, the AR gene was well covered by the remaining probes. The lack of evidence for CNV at this locus supports the findings of a very recent study by McCarroll et al which has demonstrated a lack of CNV in the AR coding regions [15].
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 188 196 188 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005081-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 472 476 472 476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005081-Bruder1">[16]</xref>
###xml 626 628 626 628 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 700 702 700 702 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 81 84 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 398 401 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 499 502 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 629 632 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
###xml 685 688 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
This study examined a locus on chromosome x in males, meaning that if changes in CNV dosages were detected, they would be expected to be at either a 0 (deletion) or 2 (duplication) level. Figure 2 demonstrates that the results for some samples showed normalised ratios of around 0.5 or 1.5, consistent with possible mosaicism within the cells of the blood samples from which the DNA was extracted. CNV mosaicism has been demonstrated in blood samples in monozygotic twins [16], and it is known that CNV may be variable between cell types within the same individual. Although the results presented here indicate no evidence of AR CNV in DNA derived from lymphocytes, we cannot rule out CNV around the AR in DNA contained in the cells of the hair follicles themselves.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 137 139 137 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 436 438 436 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 274 277 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
Our preliminary investigation did not thoroughly investigate the downstream non-coding flanking region and other more distant sites from AR. However, we focussed on a region that we have already shown to be strongly associated with AGA and it would be likely that causative CNV, if they exist, would be in the region examined. Sample sizes used were relatively small, however power is augmented by the fact that association of AGA with AR is well-established and that the cases and controls were selected to represent high phenotypic contrast.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 30 32 30 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 83 85 83 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 199 201 199 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">AR</italic>
###xml 33 36 <span type="species:ncbi:12143?0.0219256434699714">CNV</span>
In the absence of evidence of AR CNV involvement in AGA, other forms of non-coding AR sequence variation, and also epigenetic mechanisms at this locus, should be considered in clarifying the role of AR as a genetic risk locus for AGA.
###end p 31
