###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 482 488 478 484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 75 80 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
alphaA-crystallin is highly expressed in the embryonic, neonatal and adult mouse lens. Previously, we identified two novel distal control regions, DCR1 and DCR3. DCR1 was required for transgenic expression of enhanced green fluorescent protein, EGFP, in lens epithelium, whereas DCR3 was active during "late" stages of lens primary fiber cell differentiation. However, the onset of transgenic EGFP expression was delayed by 12-24 hours, compared to the expression of the endogenous Cryaa gene.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 143 149 143 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 226 232 226 232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 268 281 264 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
###xml 517 523 501 507 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 758 771 734 747 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
###xml 819 829 787 797 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa/EGFP</italic>
###xml 849 860 817 828 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa/EGFP </italic>
###xml 137 142 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Here, we used bacterial artificial chromosome (BAC) and standard transgenic approaches to examine temporal and spatial regulation of the mouse Cryaa gene. Two BAC transgenes, with EGFP insertions into the third coding exon of Cryaa gene, were created: the intact alphaA-crystallin 148 kb BAC (alphaA-BAC) and alphaA-BAC(DeltaDCR3), which lacks approximately 1.0 kb of genomic DNA including DCR3. Expression of EGFP in the majority of both BAC transgenics nearly recapitulated the endogenous expression pattern of the Cryaa gene in lens, but not outside of the lens. The number of cells expressing alphaA-crystallin in the lens pit was higher compared to the number of cells expressing EGFP. Next, we generated additional lines using a 15 kb fragment of alphaA-crystallin locus derived from alphaA-BAC(DeltaDCR3), 15 kb Cryaa/EGFP. A 15 kb region of Cryaa/EGFP supported the expression pattern of EGFP also in the lens pit. However, co-localization studies of alphaA-crystallin and EGFP indicated that the number of cells that showed transgenic expression was higher compared to cells expressing alphaA-crystallin in the lens pit.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 205 216 197 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa/EGFP </italic>
###xml 515 521 495 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 529 544 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
We conclude that a 148 kb alphaA-BAC likely contains all of the regulatory regions required for alphaA-crystallin expression in the lens, but not in retina, spleen and thymus. In addition, while the 15 kb Cryaa/EGFP region also supported the expression of EGFP in the lens pit, expression in regions such as the hindbrain, indicate that additional genomic regions may play modulatory functions in regulating extralenticular alphaA-crystallin expression. Finally, deletion of DCR3 in either alphaA-BAC(DeltaDCR3) or Cryaa (15 kb) transgenic mice result in EGFP expression patterns that are consistent with DCR's previously established role as a distal enhancer active in "late" primary lens fiber cells.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 292 293 292 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 453 454 453 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 455 456 455 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 838 839 838 839 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 840 841 840 841 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 1099 1100 1099 1100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 1101 1102 1101 1102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
Gene regulation during embryonic development is regulated at the DNA level by a number of proximal (promoters) and distal (enhancers, silencers, insulators and locus control regions) regulatory regions that are organized by a variety of histone and non-histone proteins present in chromatin [1]. Ocular lens development has been extensively studied to address the molecular mechanisms that control tissue-specific, temporal and spatial gene regulation [2,3]. The mammalian lens is composed of two cell types, the anterior proliferating lens epithelial cells and the posterior differentiating lens fiber cells, both of a single progenitor origin. Lens fiber cells are characterized by a high concentration of crystallins, a family of proteins which constitute 90% of the water soluble proteins, and act as structural proteins in the lens [4-6]. These crystallins play key roles in maintaining lens transparency and in generating its refractive index. The relative simplicity of the lens embryonic origin, and its structure, make it an ideal model system to study the complexities of gene regulation [2,3].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 179 180 175 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 181 182 177 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 389 390 381 382 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 431 432 423 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 454 467 442 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
###xml 506 507 494 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 508 510 496 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 533 539 521 527 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 633 635 621 623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 688 689 676 677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 690 692 678 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 715 721 703 709 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 873 875 857 859 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 876 878 860 862 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 879 881 863 865 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 982 984 962 964 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 1136 1138 1112 1114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 1139 1141 1115 1117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 157 162 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 167 172 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 774 780 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Lens fiber cell differentiation is accompanied by the accumulation of high levels of alphaA-crystallin, representing 20-40% of the crystallin content of the human and mouse lens [4-6]. In addition to its role as a structural protein, alphaA-crystallin is also a member of the small heat shock protein family, thereby functioning as a molecular chaperone that prevents protein aggregation [7], and possesses an autokinase activity [8]. Disruption of alphaA-crystallin results in the formation of cataracts [9,10] (see MGI website for Cryaa phenotypic alleles), and has also been shown to result in apoptosis of lens epithelial cells [11], illustrating its vital role for lens homeostasis [9,10] (see MGI website for Cryaa phenotypic alleles). Furthermore, genetic studies in humans have shown that numerous alphaA-crystallin missense mutations result in cataract formation [10,12-19]. More recent studies have shown that alphaA-crystallin binds to and suppresses caspase 6 activity [20]. Although alphaA-crystallin is expressed predominantly in the lens, low levels of its expression have been reported in the retina, spleen and thymus [21,22]. However, no specific function of alphaA-crystallin has been shown in these tissues.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 44 57 40 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
###xml 162 164 158 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 252 265 244 257 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
###xml 663 667 647 651 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cis </italic>
###xml 873 886 849 862 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
###xml 1034 1052 1010 1024 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;A-crystallin </italic>
Our recent studies have identified two alphaA-crystallin lens-specific enhancers, DCR1 (located ~-8 kb) and DCR3 (located ~+3 kb, following the last Cryaa exon) [23] as novel distal regulatory regions involved in transcriptional regulation of the alphaA-crystallin gene. Conventional transgenic approaches utilizing DCR1 and DCR3 fused to the 1.9 kb alphaA-crystallin promoter were successful in expressing EGFP in both lens epithelium and fiber cells. However, while endogenous alphaA-crystallin expression was first apparent in the lens pit at E10.5, these transgenics expressed EGFP one day later in the lens vesicle, leading us to hypothesize that additional cis regulatory elements were required to recapitulate endogenous alphaA-crystallin expression. In this study we utilized BAC transgenics as a means to dissect further the transcriptional regulation of the alphaA-crystallin gene. Our main goal was to narrow down the genomic region(s) that harbour lens-specific essential regulatory regions required for the initiation of alphaA-crystallin gene expression in the lens pit.
###end p 11

Results
###end title 12
###begin title 13
###xml 43 48 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Endogenous alphaA-crystallin expression in mouse eye
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 83 91 79 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in situ </italic>
###xml 107 109 103 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 217 219 209 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 230 232 222 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 297 299 289 291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1C</xref>
###xml 396 398 388 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1D</xref>
###xml 453 458 445 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1E, F</xref>
###xml 598 600 586 588 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1F</xref>
Consistent with previous reports of alphaA-crystallin mRNA expression monitored by in situ hybridizations [24], endogenous alphaA-crystallin protein expression is first apparent in the lens pit at E10.5 (compare Fig. 1A with Fig. 1B). Expression is up-regulated at E11.5 in the lens vesicle (Fig. 1C) and continues to be highly expressed in the differentiating primary fiber cells at E14.5 (Fig. 1D), and within the fiber cells of the newborn lens (Fig 1E, F). Lower levels of alphaA-crystallin are expressed in lens epithelium compared to lens fiber cells of the postnatal day 1 (PND1) lens (Fig. 1F).

Expression pattern of EGFP driven by 148 kb Cryaa BAC, alphaA-BAC
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 73 75 73 75 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 210 212 206 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 390 392 382 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 556 569 544 557 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
###xml 584 586 572 574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 689 691 677 679 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 703 705 691 693 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 1100 1101 1084 1085 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Prior studies identified two distal control regions, DCR1 and DCR3 (Fig. 2A), that, in combination with a 1.9 kb promoter fragment, recapitulate most aspects of transcriptional regulation of alphaA-crystallin [23]. However, in these studies, the onset of EGFP expression was between E11-E11.5, which is at least 12 hours after the onset of endogenous alphaA-crystallin expression (see Fig. 1B). This finding suggests other regulatory regions could be required. To address this, we generated a BAC transgenic by modifying a 148 kb BAC encompassing the alphaA-crystallin gene (see Fig. 2A) through an in frame insertion of EGFP into the third exon, by homologous recombination according to [25] (see Fig. 2B). EGFP was inserted after V146, close to the C-end of the 173 amino acid alphaA-crystallin. Four founders were generated, but only three were studied due to breeding difficulties encountered with the fourth founder. All founders had visually green eyes, and transgene integrations were subsequently confirmed by genotyping. The copy number of the transgenic lines was determined by qPCR (Table 1) as described in Methods.

Embryonic EGFP expression analysis revealed that all three lines first express EGFP at E10.5 (Fig. 3A, F), which is temporally congruent with the onset of endogenous alphaA-crystallin expression (Fig. 1). ###begin title 23
Removal of a 1.0 kb region containing DCR3 does not affect early alphaA-crystallin/EGFP expression in alphaA-BAC(DeltaDCR3)
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 47 53 47 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 128 134 128 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 235 237 235 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2C</xref>
###xml 389 391 389 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 498 499 498 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
To test if DCR3 is essential for expression of Cryaa gene, we generated a BAC transgene which lacks approximately 1.0 kb of the Cryaa genomic region, including DCR3 downstream of the third exon (nucleotides +3,171 to +4,197) (see Fig. 2C). This results from a genomic deletion that occurs simultaneously with the insertion of EGPF into the third exon using the pLD53.SC-AB shuttle vector [26]. Three lines are generated which express EGFP and contain two to five copies of the transgene (see Table 1).
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 87 92 83 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4A, F</xref>
###xml 206 211 194 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4B, G</xref>
###xml 300 305 288 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4C, H</xref>
###xml 402 407 390 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4D, I</xref>
###xml 425 427 413 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1C</xref>
###xml 445 450 433 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4E, J</xref>
###xml 468 470 456 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1D</xref>
###xml 563 568 551 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1C, D</xref>
###xml 606 607 594 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 612 613 600 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
Similar to alphaA-BAC, EGFP expression is first evident in the lens pit at E10.5 (Fig. 4A, F) in one of the three alphaA-BAC (DeltaDCR3) lines. Expression of EGFP is also detected in the lens vesicle (Fig. 4B, G) and is upregulated considerably in the differentiating lens fiber cells at E12.5 (Fig. 4C, H). ###begin title 42
Discussion
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 30 43 26 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
###xml 189 191 181 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 192 194 184 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 239 245 231 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 329 331 321 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 571 572 559 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 752 753 736 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 812 818 796 802 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 871 877 855 861 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 908 918 892 902 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">EGFP/polyA</italic>
###xml 1001 1006 985 990 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5A, F</xref>
###xml 1012 1014 996 998 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7K</xref>
###xml 1113 1119 1097 1103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 1147 1153 1131 1137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 1239 1241 1223 1225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 19 24 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 233 238 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 312 327 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 806 811 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 980 985 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Initial studies of mouse alphaA-crystallin gene expression have shown that the alphaA-crystallin promoter fragment -366 to +46 supports expression of a linked CAT gene only in lens fibers [27,28]. Two novel regulatory regions of the mouse Cryaa locus, DCR1 and DCR3, have been recently identified and studied in transgenic mice [23]. Using the DCR1/1.9 kb promoter/EGFP reporter system, we detect initial EGFP expression in the lens vesicle (E11.5) although the onset of endogenous alphaA-crystallin commences in the invaginating lens placode/lens pit around E10.5 (Fig. 1). This finding suggests that other regulatory regions are required for the proper onset of alphaA-crystallin expression as the lens placode invaginates to form the lens pit (Fig. 1). Here, we find that a modified 14 kb region of the mouse Cryaa locus (a 15 kb transgene containing 14 kb of genomic Cryaa region with a 1.0 kb insert of EGFP/polyA) can support robust EGFP expression in the lens pit of E10.5 mouse embryos (Figs. 5A, F, and 7K), and, thus, represents a reasonable source from which to identify the "earliest" enhancer of the Cryaa locus. Moreover, the 148 kb Cryaa BAC is insufficient for appreciable EGFP expression in the retina, spleen and thymus [22].
###end p 43
###begin title 44
###xml 59 77 59 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;A-crystallin </italic>
Novel insights into the temporal and spatial regulation of alphaA-crystallin gene expression in lens and extralenticular tissues
###end title 44
###begin p 45
###xml 521 526 509 514 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7A, B</xref>
###xml 641 642 629 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 643 645 631 633 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 670 676 658 664 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 713 719 701 707 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
All three alphaA-BAC transgenic lines display both temporal and spatial expression patterns of EGFP that are qualitatively comparable to those of the endogenous alphaA-crystallin in lens. EGFP expression is first detected at E10.5 in the lens pit, and subsequently expressed in both the lens epithelium and fiber cells of the developing and newborn lens. The only significant difference between EGFP and alphaA-crystallin expression is much lower number of cells co-expressing both proteins between E10.5 and E11.5 (Fig. 7A, B). The most likely reason for this difference is the position-dependent distinct epigenetic regulatory mechanisms [1,29] between the endogenous Cryaa locus and randomly integrated 148 kb Cryaa BACs.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 153 155 153 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 231 242 223 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa/EGFP </italic>
###xml 424 425 412 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 546 548 534 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 549 551 537 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 595 601 583 589 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 720 721 708 709 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
Previous studies conducted with traditional transgenics suggested that DCR3 plays a specific role during "late" primary lens fiber cell differentiation [23]. Herein, the deletion of DCR3 in both alphaA-BAC(DeltaDCR3) and the 15 kb Cryaa/EGFP trangenes did not have any prevailing effect on the onset of EGFP expression in the lens, compared to the expression of alphaA-BAC transgenes, as 5 out of 7 independent lines (Table 1) showed EGFP expression in the lens pit. The number of lines analyzed here is comparable to similar transgenic studies [30-32]. Though DCR3 constituted a portion of the Cryaa 3'-UTR, its deletion seemed not to affect the average expression of EGFP mRNA obtained from both BAC transgenes (Table 2). Nevertheless, the possible function of DCR3 in alphaA-crystallin mRNA processing and stability will require additional experimentation.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 68 74 68 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 81 83 81 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 129 135 129 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 147 149 147 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 240 251 240 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa/EGFP </italic>
###xml 547 548 543 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 658 660 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7K</xref>
###xml 996 998 972 974 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 1242 1243 1214 1215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 62 67 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 123 128 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
A lens-specific chromatin domain between DCR1 and DCR3 of the mouse Cryaa locus [23] may represent the minimal size of the mouse Cryaa locus (Fig. 2A). Since DCR3 appears to act as a "late" fiber-cell specific enhancer, we examined a 15 kb Cryaa/EGFP fragment without DCR3. From our results, it was evident that the genomic regions required for early temporal alphaA-crystallin expression in the developing lens are present within this 15 kb modified genomic fragment, as EGFP expression initiated in the lens pit at E10.5 in all four lines (Fig. 5). In fact, it appears that more cells in the lens pit expressed EGFP than endogenous alphaA-crystallin (Fig. 7K). This observation requires additional comments, as the alphaA-crystallin antibody is expected to recognize both alphaA-crystallin and alphaA/EGFP fusion proteins. For example, it is possible that the epitopes recognized by the polyclonal antiserum could be masked during the presumptive formation of alphaA-crystallin/EGFP oligomers [33]. It is also possible that the alphaA-crystallin/EGFP fusion proteins precipitate with other proteins to interfere with antibody recognition consistent with a range of lens opacities found in all transgenic lenses studied (see Additional file 2). Even if the protein remains unblocked, a constant, but unknown, fraction of the protein is bound by antibody under experimental conditions, whereas all of the transgene-derived protein is EGFP-tagged. Moreover, the relative fluorescence produced by each tag is different, so, although relative location can be estimated, relative amounts of two proteins within any sample should never be compared by this methodology. It is quite possible that the fluorescence intensity elicited from EGFP at sufficient concentration will always obscure the immunofluorescence signal from alphaA-crystallin under the experimental conditions used.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 184 185 180 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 186 187 182 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 188 190 184 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 191 193 187 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 521 532 513 524 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa/EGFP </italic>
###xml 587 588 579 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 675 686 664 675 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">crystallin </italic>
###xml 764 766 753 755 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 828 834 817 823 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 1000 1006 989 995 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U2af1 </italic>
###xml 1081 1088 1070 1077 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Snf1lk </italic>
###xml 1187 1243 1176 1232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U2 small nuclear ribonucleoprotein auxiliary factor gene</italic>
###xml 1245 1250 1234 1239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U2af1</italic>
###xml 1319 1321 1308 1310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 1491 1493 1476 1478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 659 669 <span type="species:ncbi:10141">guinea pig</span>
In the past, alphaA-crystallin was referred to either as a lens-specific, or as a lens-preferred protein with high expression in lens, and low expression in retina, spleen and thymus [3-5,12,13]. More recent high-throughput data, available through the MGI and Unigene websites (see Methods), expanded the previously established extralenticular regions of alphaA-crystallin expression to the heart, hindbrain, midbrain, cerebellum, pancreas, pituitary and a few other tissues. Thus, expression of EGFP driven by the 15 kb Cryaa/EGFP in regions such as the hindbrain from E10.5-14.5 (Fig. 6) may reflect its naturally low expression in this tissue. Analysis of guinea pig zeta-crystallin transcriptional control indeed identified a brain-specific regulatory region [34]. Interestingly, expression of EGFP, supported by the 148 kb Cryaa BAC, was not detected by qRT-PCR in spleen or thymus. One intriguing possibility is that expression in these tissues originates via elements shared with the adjacent U2af1 (expressed highly in thymus relative to other tissues such as the eye) and Snf1lk (expressed highly in spleen compared to other tissues) genes, but not present in the 148 kb BAC. A U2 small nuclear ribonucleoprotein auxiliary factor gene, U2af1, also carried in this BAC, encodes a heterodimeric splicing factor [35]. The alphaA-BAC transgenic lines also exhibit elevated U2af1 (data not shown), while expression of Snf1lk was not tested due to its absence in the 148 kb BAC (see Fig. 2A).
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 161 167 157 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 357 359 353 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 366 383 362 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">interferon-&#947; </italic>
###xml 469 482 461 474 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Th2 cytokine </italic>
###xml 506 508 498 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 646 648 638 640 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 649 651 641 643 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
Thus, the present data suggest that the genomic regions required for alphaA-crystallin expression in the retina, spleen and thymus are not present in the 148 kb Cryaa BAC and may be located in genomic regions adjacent to this BAC clone, or, as in the case of genes such as olfactory receptors, these regulatory regions may be present on another chromosome [36]. The interferon-gamma locus on chromosome 10 is similarly regulated by elements within a locus encoding the Th2 cytokine genes on chromosome 11 [30]. The complexity of the genome in relationship to the individual genes and their regulation is far more complex than previously thought [37,38].
###end p 49
###begin title 50
Transgene positional/copy number effects and lens structural integrity
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 121 123 121 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 339 341 331 333 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 621 623 613 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 703 705 695 697 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
Although the general concept is that large transgenes, such as BACs and YACs, are not susceptible to positional effects [31], the lack of EGFP expression at E10.5 in 2 out of 3 alphaA-BAC(DeltaDCR3) transgenic lines suggests otherwise. Previous reports have indicated that generally one to five copes of BACs are inserted into the genome [32], however recent studies have suggested that this is not always the case. In fact, analyses conducted by Chandler et al., 2007 reveal that, while 50 % of transgenes are inserted as copies ranging between 1-5, BACs can be inserted with much greater copies, even up to almost 100 [32]. Additional BAC studies have reported copy numbers to be also in excess of 5 [39].
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 87 98 87 98 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa/EGFP </italic>
###xml 149 150 149 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 195 206 191 202 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa/EGFP </italic>
###xml 421 427 417 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
###xml 438 440 434 436 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 206 221 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 334 339 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 395 400 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 405 410 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Analysis of the structural integrity of the adult lens reveals that both BAC and 15 kb Cryaa/EGFP transgenics display opacities (see Additional file 2). In fact, some of the alphaA-BAC and 15 kb Cryaa/EGFP transgenic mice begin to show signs of lens opacities upon eye opening (data not shown). Morphologically these adult transgenic mouse lenses appear to display nuclear cataracts, resembling human and mouse models of Cryaa cataracts [40]. Given the overpression of alphaA-crystallin/EGFP, one can speculate plausible mechanisms contributing to lens opacities. Firstly, it is possible that excess of alphaA-crystallin EGFP fusion proteins interferes with the stoichiometric ratio of alphaA and alphaB-crystallins in the alpha-crystallin heteroaggregate complex, leading to the misfolding and precipitation of proteins. Expression of the alphaA-crystallin/EGFP fusion protein may also play a role in disrupting other normal interactions of lens proteins, resulting in protein aggregation precipitation and cataract formation.
###end p 52
###begin title 53
Conclusion
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 73 84 73 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa/EGFP </italic>
###xml 355 357 351 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
The present studies have shown that a modified genomic fragment of 15 kb Cryaa/EGFP is sufficient to direct the earliest onset of alphaA-crystallin/EGFP expression in the lens pit (E10.5). The leading candidate regions to harbour this early enhancer activity are four exons (1-3 and ins), and evolutionarily conserved regions in introns 1 and 2 (see Fig. 2A).
###end p 54
###begin title 55
Methods
###end title 55
###begin title 56
###xml 13 18 13 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">EGFP </italic>
###xml 30 36 30 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryaa </italic>
Insertion of EGFP into 148 kb Cryaa BAC
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 15 28 11 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
###xml 131 133 127 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 315 323 307 315 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1278 1282 1231 1235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DpnI</italic>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 82 87 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 315 322 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The mouse alphaA-crystallin BAC clone RP-23-465G4 was obtained from screening the mouse RPCI 23 library as we described elsewhere [23]. alphaA-BAC was generated through a homologous recombination, according to Lee et al., 2001. Briefly, the BAC clone RP-23-465G4 was first transformed by electroporation into SW105 E. coli cells harbouring a defective lambda prophage (kindly provided by Dr. Neil Copeland, NCI, Frederick, MD). A 2.4 kb linear targeting cassette containing EGFP-pA, FRT-Kan-FRT sequences was PCR amplified from pCS2+MTe-GFP-FRT-kan-FRT plasmid (kindly provided by Dr. Jim Lauderdale, University of Georgia, Athens, GA) with primers bearing 50 bp of homology to alphaA-crystallin and the plasmid. This targeting vector was PCR amplified with primers: 5'-CTCTCCTGCTCCCTGTCTGCGGATGGCATGCTGACCTTCTCTGGCCCCAAGGTCATGGTGAGCAAGGGCGAG-3' and 5'-CTCCCGTGACACAGGAATGGCCCTCTCGCTGTGGCCAGCATCCAAACCGGACTGTATTCCAGAAGTAGTGAG-3' using the following conditions: 95degreesC, 2 min for 1 cycle; 95degreesC, 30 sec; 60degreesC, 30 sec; 72degreesC, 2 min 30 sec for 30 cycles; 72degreesC, 7 min for 1 cycle. The PCR product was separated by gel electrophoresis followed by purification with the Qiaquick gel extraction kit (Qiagen) and the plasmid template removed by digestion with DpnI. Homologous recombination was conducted by electroporating 300 ng of the targeting vector into SW105 competent cells containing BAC clone RP-23-465G4, with a Bio-Rad gene pulser (1.75 kV, 25 muF, 200 Omega). Recombinants were verified by PCR using primers (5'-TTTGGCGCGCCAAGCCAGTTCCATACCCTGA-3' and 5'-CGGCGAGCTGCACGCTGCCGTCC-3'), and a single colony was used for FLP induction with 10% L (+) arabinose. Colonies were plated on chloramphenicol LB plates, and modified BAC's were verified by sequencing and restriction analysis.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 120 132 116 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin</italic>
###xml 181 183 169 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 226 239 210 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
###xml 518 528 502 512 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AscI/SmaI </italic>
###xml 532 542 516 526 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PacI/FseI </italic>
###xml 654 667 633 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin </italic>
The BAC clone RP-23-465G4 was modified, using the shuttle vector pLD53.SCAEB to insert EGFP into the third exon of alphaA-crystallin, to generate alphaA-BAC(DeltaDCR3) transgenics [26]. Briefly, two regions homologous to alphaA-crystallin were PCR amplified using the following primers: Homologous region 1, 5'-TTTGGCGCGCCAAGCCAGTTCCATACCCTGA-3' and 5'-GTTTCTCCTCCCGTGACACA-3'; homologous region 2, 5'-TTAATTAAACACCACGGAACATACCACA-3' and 5'-CCGGCCGGGCTTGGACATCCAGGAACAG-3'. The homologous regions were cloned into the AscI/SmaI and PacI/FseI sites of the pLD53.SCAEB shuttle vector, respectively. 1 mug of the shuttle vector was electroporated into alphaA-crystallin BAC competent cells, using a Bio-rad gene pulser (1.8 kV, 25 muF, 200 Omega), and cointegrates were selected on LB plates containing chloramphenicol (20 mug/ml) and ampicillin (50 mug/ml). Cointegrates were analyzed by PCR with the following primers: 5'-GATGAGGAAGCTGGGTGGTA-3' and 5'-CGGCGAGCTGCACGCTGCCGTCC-3', and a correct integrate was subsequently resolved by growing in LB containing chloramphenicol and ampicillin and plated on LB + chloramphenicol + sucrose plates. Resolved colonies were analyzed by PCR with the following primers: 5'-CCTACGGCGTGCAGTGCTTCAGC-3' and 5'-CAAAGACAGCTCCATGCTGA-3', and modified clones were verified by sequencing.
###end p 58
###begin title 59
###xml 21 44 21 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;A-crystallin/EGFP </italic>
Isolation of a 15 kb alphaA-crystallin/EGFP fragment
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 15 33 11 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A-crystallin/EGFP </italic>
###xml 97 102 85 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">XmaI </italic>
###xml 106 110 94 98 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SpeI</italic>
###xml 203 207 191 193 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174; </sup>
###xml 535 540 521 526 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">XmaI </italic>
###xml 544 548 530 534 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SpeI</italic>
The 15 kb alphaA-crystallin/EGFP construct was generated by digesting alphaA-BAC(DeltaDCR3) with XmaI and SpeI. The digests were run on a 0.8% agarose gel (Seaplaque GTG) overnight, and stained with SYBR(R) Gold (Molecular Probes). A 15 kb band was excised from the gel and electroeluted in TAE buffer. The DNA was precipitated with 100% ethanol and sodium acetate (pH 5.2; 300 mM), then washed with 70% ethanol. The 15 kb fragment containing 14 kb genomic DNA and 1.0 kb of EGFP/poly A was cloned into pBluescript SK II vector sites, XmaI and SpeI.

Generation of transgenic mice

###begin p 62
###xml 465 480 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>

alphaA-BAC and alphaA-BAC(DeltaDCR3) DNAs for pronuclear injection were isolated using the Qiagen endonuclease-free mega purification kit (Qiagen) according to the manufacturer's instructions, with the exception that the volume of P1, P2 and P3 was increased three times.
