Reduced diversity and increased virulence-gene carriage in intestinal enterobacteria of coeliac children
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 302 321 302 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 366 374 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 387 395 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
Coeliac disease is an immune-mediated enteropathology triggered by the ingestion of cereal gluten proteins. This disorder is associated with imbalances in the composition of the gut microbiota that could be involved in its pathogenesis. The aim of the present study was to determine whether intestinal Enterobacteriaceae populations of active and non-active coeliac patients and healthy children differ in diversity and virulence-gene carriage, so as to establish a possible link between the pathogenic potential of enterobacteria and the disease.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 0 19 0 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 230 247 230 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 389 393 389 393 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fimA</italic>
###xml 408 412 408 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">papC</italic>
###xml 427 433 427 433 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sfaD/E</italic>
###xml 455 459 455 459 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">draA</italic>
###xml 474 478 474 478 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hlyA</italic>
###xml 493 497 493 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">neuB</italic>
###xml 512 516 512 516 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">KfiC</italic>
###xml 534 538 534 538 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iutA</italic>
###xml 107 115 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 141 149 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 230 246 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Enterobacteriaceae clones were isolated on VRBD agar from faecal samples of 31 subjects (10 active coeliac patients, 10 symptom-free coeliac patients and 11 healthy controls) and identified at species level by the API 20E system. Escherichia coli clones were classified into four phylogenetic groups A, B1, B2 and D and the prevalence of eight virulence-associated genes (type-1 fimbriae [fimA], P fimbriae [papC], S fimbriae [sfaD/E], Dr haemagglutinin [draA], haemolysin [hlyA], capsule K1 [neuB], capsule K5 [KfiC] and aerobactin [iutA]) was determined by multiplex PCR.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 15 34 15 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 60 68 60 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 171 179 171 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 461 468 461 468 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E coli </italic>
###xml 668 672 668 672 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">papC</italic>
###xml 687 693 687 693 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sfaD/E</italic>
###xml 711 715 711 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hlyA</italic>
###xml 737 745 737 745 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 60 67 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 114 122 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 139 147 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 171 178 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 222 230 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 254 262 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 388 396 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 451 459 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 553 561 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 737 744 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 789 797 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
A total of 155 Enterobacteriaceae clones were isolated. Non-E. coli clones were more commonly isolated in healthy children than in coeliac patients. The four phylogenetic E. coli groups were equally distributed in healthy children, while in both coeliac patients most commensal isolates belonged to group A. Within the virulent groups, B2 was the most prevalent in active coeliac disease children, while D was the most prevalent in non-active coeliac patients. E coli clones of the virulent phylogenetic groups (B2+D) from active and non-active coeliac patients carried a higher number of virulence genes than those from healthy individuals. Prevalence of P fimbriae (papC), capsule K5 (sfaD/E) and haemolysin (hlyA) genes was higher in E. coli isolated from active and non-active coeliac children than in those from control subjects.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusion
###end title 8
###begin p 9
This study has demonstrated that virulence features of the enteric microbiota are linked to coeliac disease.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Background
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 78 81 78 81 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">14 </sup>
###xml 415 416 415 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 417 418 417 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 841 857 841 857 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bifidobacterium </italic>
###xml 861 874 861 874 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lactobacillus</italic>
###xml 925 937 925 937 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacteroides </italic>
###xml 941 957 941 957 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 1003 1004 1003 1004 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 1005 1006 1005 1006 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 290 295 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 620 628 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 768 776 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 941 957 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
The human gastrointestinal tract is a complex ecosystem integrated by up to 1014 bacteria. These microorganisms may belong to more than 500 different bacterial species, although 99% of the total community consists of only 30-40 species. The intestinal microbiota plays an important role in human health as they contribute to inhibiting pathogen colonization, boosting the immune system, and metabolising nutrients [1-3]. Alterations in the intestinal microbiota have also been linked to inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD), such as ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease, as well as to other immune-related disorders. IBD patients have been shown to carry higher concentrations of mucosa-associated bacteria than control groups. In addition, the gut microbiota of these patients was characterized by a decrease in protective bacteria, such as Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus, and an increase in harmful bacterial groups like Bacteroides and Escherichia coli. This phenomenon has been named "dysbiosis" [4,5].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 0 17 0 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 295 303 295 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 479 487 479 487 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 599 600 599 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 759 767 759 767 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 853 854 853 854 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 981 989 981 989 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1031 1032 1031 1032 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 1035 1043 1035 1043 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1153 1154 1153 1154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1155 1157 1155 1157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 1206 1214 1206 1214 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 0 16 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 68 73 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 295 302 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 459 464 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 479 486 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 759 766 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 981 988 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1035 1042 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1206 1213 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Escherichia coli was the first bacterial specie to be identified in human gastrointestinal samples. This widespread commensal Gram-negative bacterium found in the intestinal tract of healthy individuals can occasionally cause intestinal and extraintestinal diseases. The pathogenic potential of E. coli stains could be influenced by host endogenous factors, as well as by the genetic structure and ecological distribution of the strains in a particular host. Human infections by E. coli strains commonly occur in immuno-compromised individuals or when the normal gastrointestinal barrier is broken [6], enabling opportunistic pathogens to evade host surveillance mechanisms. Phylogenetic studies based on the analysis of 38 metabolic enzymes, have shown that E. coli strains fall into four main phylogenetic groups, denominated "A", "B1", "B2" and "D" [7]. Strains causing extra-intestinal infections mainly belong to group B2, and to a lesser extent to group D, whereas commensal E. coli strains mostly belong to A and B1 groups [8]. E. coli strains belonging to B2 and D groups carry more virulence-associated genes than strains from A and B1 groups [9,10]. Therefore, the presence or absence of certain E. coli strains or an altered distribution of their relative abundance can enhance the pathogenic potential of the enteric population in predisposed individuals.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 316 318 316 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 622 630 622 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 734 736 734 736 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 837 839 837 839 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 84 89 <span type="species:ncbi:4565">wheat</span>
###xml 121 124 <span type="species:ncbi:4550">rye</span>
###xml 126 132 <span type="species:ncbi:112509|species:ncbi:4513">barley</span>
###xml 148 152 <span type="species:ncbi:4498|species:ncbi:50455">oats</span>
###xml 332 340 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 622 629 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 707 715 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Coeliac disease is an immune-mediated enteropathology triggered by the ingestion of wheat-gluten and similar proteins of rye, barley and, probably, oats. Coeliac disease can be present at any age with a variety of clinical features; however, typical cases manifest in early childhood with gastrointestinal symptoms [11]. In coeliac patients, the inflammatory milieu caused by gluten antigens could lead to imbalances in gut microbial composition, characterised by higher numbers of Gram-negative bacteria and decreased numbers of beneficial Gram-positive bacteria, as compared to healthy individuals. In previous studies, E. coli proportions were significantly higher in duodenal biopsies of active coeliac patients than in controls [12]; moreover, a similar trend was detected in faeces although the differences were not so remarkable [13]. However, little is known about the possible association between enterobacterial population and coeliac disease pathogenesis.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 65 84 65 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 129 137 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 150 158 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
The aim of the present study was to determine whether intestinal Enterobacteriaceae populations of active and non-active coeliac patients and healthy children differ in diversity and virulence-gene carriage, so as to establish a possible link between the pathogenic potential of enterobacteria and the disease.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Methods
###end title 15
###begin title 16
Subjects and sampling
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 11 19 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 67 75 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 321 329 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 460 468 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 557 565 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 901 909 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
Thirty-one children were included in this study, 10 active coeliac patients, on a normal gluten-containing diet, showing clinical symptoms of the disease, positive coeliac serology markers and signs of severe enteropathy by duodenal biopsy examination (mean age 3.86 years, range 1.0-8.86 years); 10 symptom-free coeliac patients (non-active coeliac), who had been on a gluten-free diet for 1-2 years (mean age 6.2 years, range 1.0-12.0 years); and 11 healthy children without known food intolerance (mean age 3.51 years, range 0.1-7.75 years). None of the children included in the study had been treated with antibiotics for at least 1 month before the sampling time. Faecal samples were collected and immediately stored at 4degreesC, under anaerobic conditions (AnaeroGen, Oxoid, Hampshire, UK), and analysed in less than 12 hours. The study protocol was approved by the local Ethics Committee, and children were enrolled in the study after written informed consent was obtained from their parents.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
###xml 32 51 32 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
Isolation and identification of Enterobacteriaceae clones from faeces
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 441 459 435 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae</italic>
###xml 705 724 693 712 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 985 993 973 981 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1008 1016 996 1004 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 985 992 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1008 1015 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Faecal samples (2 g wet weight) were 10-fold diluted in phospahate-buffered saline (PBS, 130 mM sodium chloride, 10 mM sodium phosphate, [pH 7.2]), and homogenized in a Lab Blender 400 Stomacher (Seward Medical, London, UK). Serial dilutions were made in PBS and aliquots were plated on Violet Red Bile Dextrose Agar, VRBD (Scharlau, Barcelona, Spain) and incubated under aerobic conditions at 37degreesC for 48 hours. Then, five individual Enterobacteriaceae-like colonies differing in size, shape, colour or mucoid appearance on VRBD agar were isolated and subcultured in Plate Count Agar (Scharlau, Barcelona, Spain) under aerobic conditions at 37degreesC for 24 hours. The identity of the presumptive Enterobacteriaceae isolates was confirmed by conventional microbiological methods, including colony and cellular morphology and Gram staining. The selected isolates were further identified at species level by using the API20E system (BioMerieux, Lyon, France), in order to select E. coli clones and non-E. coli clones.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
###xml 70 78 70 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 70 77 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Phylogenetic classification and virulence-associated gene carriage of E. coli isolates
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 0 17 0 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 146 148 146 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 223 224 222 223 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 266 277 264 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ChuA, YjaA </italic>
###xml 281 288 279 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TspE4C2</italic>
###xml 848 850 821 823 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 854 855 827 828 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 871 872 844 845 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 876 877 849 850 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-</sup>
###xml 892 894 865 867 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">- </sup>
###xml 901 903 874 876 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 917 919 890 892 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">- </sup>
###xml 926 928 899 901 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">- </sup>
###xml 0 16 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Escherichia coli isolates were assigned to one of the four phylogenetic groups A, B1, B2 or D by a triplex PCR technique as previously described [14]. The 25 mul PCR mixture consisted of 10 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.3), 2.5 mM MgCl2, 1 muM of each primer pair for the genes ChuA, YjaA and TspE4C2, 200 muM, dNTPs and 2.5 U of Taq polymerase (Ecotaq, ECOGEN, Spain). A piece of a bacterial colony was collected directly from a plate and added to the reaction mixture as a source of DNA. The PCR programme was as follows: initial denaturalization at 94degreesC for 4 min; and 30 cycles of 5 s at 94degreesC, 10 s at 59degreesC; and a final extension step of 5 min at 72degreesC. The PCR products were separated in a 2% agarose gel electrophoresis and visualised by ethidium-bromide staining. The isolates were assigned to phylogenetic groups as follows: ChuA+ YjaA+, group B2; ChuA+YjaA-, group D; ChuA- TspE4C2+ group B1; ChuA- TspE4C2- group A.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 113 115 113 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 199 204 199 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fimA </italic>
###xml 223 228 223 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">papC </italic>
###xml 242 249 242 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sfaD/E </italic>
###xml 266 271 266 271 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">draA </italic>
###xml 353 358 353 358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hlyA </italic>
###xml 372 377 372 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">neuB </italic>
###xml 391 396 391 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">KfiC </italic>
###xml 413 418 413 418 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iutA </italic>
###xml 516 518 515 517 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 561 563 560 562 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
The same isolates were also characterized by two sets of multiplex PCR as described by Nowrouzian et al. (2001) [15]. The first PCR detected the presence of the following virulence-associated genes: fimA (type-1 fimbriae), papC (P fimbriae), sfaD/E (S fimbriae) and draA (Dr haemagglutinin). The second PCR detected the presence of the following genes: hlyA (haemolysin), neuB (capsule K1), KfiC (capsule K5) and iutA (aerobactin). The 25 mul PCR reaction mixture consisted of 10 mM Tris HCl (pH 8.3), 1.5 mM of MgCl2 for the first multiplex PCR and 2.0 mM MgCl2 for the second multiplex PCR, 0.45 muM of each primer, 100 muM dNTPs and 2.5 U of Taq polymerase (Ecotaq, ECOGEN, Spain). A piece of a bacterial colony was used as a source of DNA as indicated above.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
Statistical analyses
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 374 376 374 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P </italic>
Differences in prevalence of different species, phylogenetic groups or virulence genes were established by applying the chi-square test, and when appropriate by the two-tailed Fisher's exact test and Kolmogorov-Smirnof test. In each case, analyses were carried out with the Statgraphics software (Manugistics, Rockville, MD), and statistical differences were established at P value below 0.05.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Results
###end title 25
###begin title 26
###xml 36 44 36 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>

###xml 76 84 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
Diversity of Enterobacteriaceae and E. coli clones from coeliac and healthy children
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 68 87 68 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 106 113 106 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>

###xml 365 373 365 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 468 470 468 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P </italic>
###xml 106 113 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 228 235 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 262 269 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 345 353 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 365 372 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 422 430 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 458 466 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Of the 31 individuals analyzed in this study, only 14 (45%) carried Enterobacteriaceae species other than E. coli. The latter species was always recovered in healthy individuals and in eight of them (73%) at least one other non-E. coli species was detected. Non-E. coli species were detected in only four (40%) active and two non-active coeliac patients (20%). Non-E. coli clones were recovered more abundantly in healthy children than in non-active coeliac patients (P = 0.03).

###begin title 38
Discussion
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 86 105 86 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 109 117 109 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 109 116 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 178 186 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Significant differences were revealed in the diversity and virulence-gene carriage of Enterobacteriaceae and E. coli clones isolated from faeces of active and non-active coeliac patients and healthy controls, suggesting that this bacterial group could be primarily or secondarily involved in coeliac disease.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 42 61 42 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 246 265 246 265 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 286 293 286 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 337 345 337 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 348 367 348 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 432 434 432 434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 549 557 549 557 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 577 596 577 596 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 632 634 632 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 688 696 688 696 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 0 7 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 82 87 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 134 142 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 218 226 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 286 293 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 337 344 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 397 405 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 489 497 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 549 556 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 688 695 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
E. coli was the most common member of the Enterobacteriaceae family isolated from human faecal samples of healthy and coeliac disease children. This result is consistent with a previous study of 49 coeliac and healthy children, in which the main Enterobacteriaceae species detected was E. coli. In the aforementioned study, the ratio of E. coli to Enterobacteriaceae was greater in active coeliac children than in healthy controls [13]. Furthermore, biopsy specimens taken from paediatric patients with inflammatory bowel disease also revealed that E. coli was the most common Enterobacteriaceae species detected in MacConkey agar [16]. Thus, a reduction in the relative abundance of non-E. coli species is associated with coeliac disease and could favour the predominance of harmful clones in the gut ecosystem.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 17 36 17 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 479 481 479 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 407 412 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Although several Enterobacteriaceae colonies were often selected from a sample, because they differed morphology and were suspected to represent different strains, they often turned out to be identical regarding their phylogenetic classification and virulence-associated gene patterns, usually some of them represented the same clone. This can be explained by the fact that only one clone usually dominates human gastrointestinal gut so that the other clones are not detectable [17].
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 204 212 204 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 444 452 444 452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 696 704 696 704 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1142 1150 1142 1150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1160 1162 1160 1162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1453 1455 1453 1455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 2014 2016 2014 2016 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 126 134 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 204 211 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 255 263 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 444 451 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 481 489 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 696 703 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 743 751 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 822 830 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 856 864 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1142 1149 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1183 1191 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1512 1519 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">infants</span>
###xml 1623 1630 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">infants</span>
###xml 1742 1750 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1991 1999 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The phylogenetic groups A, B1, B2 and D were found in different proportions in healthy, active coeliac and non-active coeliac patients. This fact also suggests links between lower diversity of intestinal E. coli population and coeliac disease. In healthy children, isolates of each of the four groups were found in equal proportions. By contrast, isolates belonging to groups A and B2, followed by those of group D dominated the composition of E. coli microbiota in active coeliac children, while integrants of group B1 were not isolated; group D was the most common in non-active coeliac disease, followed by groups A, B2, and B1. These results reflect imbalances in the composition of the four E. coli phylogenetic groups in coeliac disease children, revealing the most remarkable differences between non-active coeliac patients and healthy controls. In patients with IBD, such as Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis, B2 and D phylogenetic groups were more prevalent than in the control group. Moreover, a relationship was established between the presence of serine protease autotransporter proteins (SPATE) or adhesins in B2+D-positive E. coli isolates [18]. In active coeliac patients, the dominance of group B2 virulent clones could provide pathogenic inflammatory factors either causative or consequence of the inflammatory status associated with gluten intake in the active phase of this disorder creating a vicious circle. Nowrouzian et al. [19] concluded that group D strains preferentially colonize infants with less complex intestinal microbiota, since this group is more abundant than others in one-year-old infants. Both genetic make-up and gluten-free diet could contribute to changes in the gut milieu of non-active coeliac patients, leading to a predominant colonization of the virulent D group in detriment to the others. In fact, lower total bacterial populations have been detected in duodenal samples taken from this population group when compared with active coeliac patients and controls [12].
###end p 42

Overall, E coli clones of the virulent phylogenetic groups (B2+D) isolated from active and non-active coeliac patients, carried a higher number virulent genes than those from healthy controls. A higher prevalence of P fimbriae gene (papC) was found in coeliac patients as compared to healthy children, regardless of the phase of this disorder; however, the association of type-1 fimbriae (fimA) and S fimbriae (sfaD/E) gene carriage with coeliac disease was not so evident. Significant differences in the carriage of type-1 fimbriae (fimA) gene were not detected in the group of children under study but E. coli clones from active and non-active coeliac disease isolates tended to carry fewer type-1 fimbriae (fimA) genes than those from healthy children. Previous studies have indicated that the proportion of type-1 fimbriated E. coli is lower in IgA-deficient subjects than in control individuals [20,21] and this may also be the case for coeliac disease patients since this disease is associated with IgA deficiency. E. coli adhesins, including P fimbriae, confer mannose-resistant (MR) adherence to intestinal epithelial cells. MR adhesions are well-know virulence factors in urinary-tract infection, septicaemia and meningitis. Strains that persist in the human intestinal microbiota (resident strains) are more often P fimbriated, whereas S fimbriated E. coli are not associated with long-term persistence in the gut of healthy individuals [20,22]. Thus, the significantly higher prevalence of P fimbriae E. coli clones in treated and untreated coeliac patients constitutes a novel link between gut microbiota and coeliac disease, and revealed that gut health may be compromised in these subjects, even when subjected to a gluten-free diet.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 41 45 41 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kfiC</italic>
###xml 50 58 50 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 596 598 596 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 599 601 599 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 50 57 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 216 224 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
A higher prevalence of capsular K5 gene (kfiC) of E. coli clones belonging to the virulent groups was associated with active coeliac disease, while a similar but less remarkable trend was found in non-active coeliac patients, as compared to controls. Capsular polysaccharides are known to render bacterial surfaces hydrophilic and negatively charged, making the bacterium resistant to entrapment in mucus. In addition, capsules contribute to virulence by protecting bacteria from phagocytosis and possibly from serum killing, in part by blocking activation of the alternative complement pathway [23,15].
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 49 53 49 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hlyA</italic>
###xml 63 70 63 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E coli </italic>
###xml 240 248 236 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 385 393 381 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 476 478 472 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 479 481 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 139 147 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 240 247 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 385 392 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Moreover, a higher prevalence of the haemolysin (hlyA) gene in E coli clones of the virulent groups was associated with non-active coeliac patients in particular. alpha-Haemolysin is the most common cytolytic protein secreted by haemolytic E. coli strains. Haemolysin activity might contribute to persistence by attacking enterocytes and releasing nutrients for the bacteria. In fact, E. coli appears to use membrane lipids as its main nutrient source in the large intestine [23,24].
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 32 40 32 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 202 206 202 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">iutA</italic>
###xml 234 240 234 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sfaD/E</italic>
###xml 258 262 258 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">papC</italic>
###xml 277 281 277 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kfiC</italic>
###xml 299 303 299 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hlyA</italic>
###xml 348 356 348 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 554 555 554 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 556 558 556 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 583 591 583 591 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 709 711 709 711 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 712 714 712 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 715 717 715 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 32 39 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 348 355 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 583 590 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 987 995 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
It has also been confirmed that E. coli clones of the virulent groups (B2 + D) carried more virulent-associated genes than those of commensal groups (A + B1), with the exception of the aerobactin gene (iutA). Remarkably, genes for S (sfaD/E) and P fimbriae (papC), K5 capsule (kfiC) and haemolysin (hlyA) were significantly more common in virulent E. coli clones than in commensal strains of our population groups. In agreement, genes coding for virulence-associated genes have been found more frequently in pathogenic strains than in commensal strains [8,23]. In addition, virulent E. coli isolates (B2 + D) have previously been shown to carry virulence-associated genes more commonly than commensal clones [10,25,26]. Virulence-associated genes are usually encoded on pathogenicity islands (PAIs) providing a mechanism for coordinated horizontal transfer of virulence genes, thus favouring dissemination of pathogenic determinants, as could be the case in the gut ecosystem of coeliac patients [27].
###end p 46
###begin title 47
Conclusion
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 39 58 39 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae </italic>
###xml 112 120 112 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 478 486 478 486 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 112 119 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 149 157 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 478 485 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
This study has demonstrated changes in Enterobacteriaceae diversity and increases in virulence-gene carriage in E. coli clones isolated from coeliac patients when compared to those from healthy controls. Thus, the results support the hypothesis that dysbiosis may constitute a virulence factor contributing to pathogenesis and full expression of coeliac disease. Further studies should be carried out to correlate the virulence gene carriage with specific pathogenic roles that E. coli clones could play in this disorder.
###end p 48
