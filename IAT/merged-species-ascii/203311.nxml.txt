The complete chloroplast DNA sequence (122 890 bp) of the moss Physcomitrella patens has been determined. The genome contains 83 protein, 31 tRNA and four rRNA genes, and a pseudogene. Four protein genes (rpoA, cysA, cysT and ccsA) found in the liverwort Marchantia polymorpha and the hornwort Anthoceros formosae are absent from P.patens. The overall structure of P.patens chloroplast DNA (cpDNA) differs substantially from that of liverwort and hornwort. Compared with its close relatives, a 71 kb region from petD to rpoB of P.patens is inverted. To investigate whether this large inversion and the loss of rpoA usually occur in moss plants, we analyzed amplified cpDNA fragments from four moss species. Our data indicate that the large inversion occurs only in P.patens, whereas the loss of the rpoA gene occurs in all mosses. Moreover, we have isolated and characterized the nuclear rpoA gene encoding the alpha subunit of RNA polymerase (RNAP) from P.patens and examined its subcellular localization. When fused to green fluorescent protein, RpoA was observed in the chloroplasts of live moss protonemata cells. This indicates that chloroplast RNAP is encoded separately by chloroplast and nuclear genomes in the moss. These data provide new insights into the regulation and evolution of chloroplast transcription.

Chloroplasts evolved from cyanobacteria through endosymbiosis, and possess independent genomes (1). To date, complete chloroplast DNA (cpDNA) sequences from two dozen land plants and algae have been determined (2,3). Algal cpDNAs range from 89 (Codium fragile) to over 1500 kb (Acetabularia), and their gene content and organization vary from species to species (2). In contrast, land plant cpDNAs are relatively uniform in size, from 120 to 160 kb, and their gene content and organization are well conserved (3). Bryophytes consist of three classes (liverworts, hornworts and mosses) and have been identified as the earliest land plants (4-6). Among the bryophytes, the cpDNA (121 024 bp) of the liverwort Marchantia polymorpha was the first to be sequenced (7), and the hornwort Anthoceros formosae cpDNA sequence (161 162 bp) has recently been published (8). The larger size of the cpDNA genome of A.formosae results from a larger inverted repeat (IR) sequence compared with the liverwort M.polymorpha. The overall gene organization is highly conserved between the two bryophytes. Single introns, however, are inserted into the 23S rRNA genes (rrn23) of A.formosae (8) but not into those of M.polymorpha (7), the charophyte Chaetosphaeridium globosum (9) or vascular plants (3). Over 900 RNA editing sites have been identified in the hornwort A.formosae chloroplast (10). In contrast, no RNA editing site has been found in the cpDNA of the liverwort M.polymorpha (11). Therefore, RNA editing events differ substantially between bryophyte species compared with the high degree of conservation of editing sites in the chloroplasts of vascular plants (12). Significant divergence in RNA editing events among the bryophytes supports bryophyte paraphyly, as suggested by molecular phylogenetic studies (13-15).

The remaining bryophyte group, the mosses, has been reported to be most closely related to the early lycopod land plants, the ferns, and the vascular plant lineage (4-6,15). The exact phylogenetic status of this group, however, remains unresolved. To answer this question, molecular data on moss chloroplast genes are required. There is little information on the gene structure (16-18) or RNA editing (19) of the moss P.patens. Therefore, we have determined the complete nucleotide sequence of the P.patens cpDNA. Moreover, we have isolated a nuclear rpoA gene encoding an alpha subunit of RNA polymerase (RNAP) and discuss the relocation of rpoA from the chloroplast to the nucleus.

A P.patens genomic library was screened using the tobacco ndhD sequence (21) as a probe, as described previously (22), and the genomic clone lambda-D5 was isolated. The lambda-D5 insert DNA was amplified by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) using primers designed from lambdafix-II vector sequences, AGCTCTAATACGACTCACTATAGGGCGTCGA and GAGCTCAATTAACCCTCACTAAAGGGAGTCGA. The reaction of long and accurate (LA) PCR was performed with 30 cycles of 10 s denaturation at 98degreesC and 15 min annealing and extension at 68degreesC using LA Taq DNA polymerase (Takara Shuzo). The amplified DNA contained a 12 640 bp region from rpl32 to trnV-GAC. To further amplify cpDNA fragments, total cellular DNA (90 ng) was subjected to LA PCR under the same conditions as above using primers designed from either nucleotide sequences deposited in DNA databases or sequences determined in this study as follows: for amplification of a 19 379 bp DNA region from psbD to rbcL, LA1 (TTAGGAGGTCTATGGACTTTCGTTGCTCTT, AB013655) and LA2 (AGTCATCACGAAGTAAGTCAACAAACCCTA, AB066297); for a 14 473 bp DNA region from psbB to rbcL, LA3 (TAAGTAAAAAAAAAAGATGATGGAAAA, this study) and LA4 (AGGTTCTGTTACTAACTTATTTACTTCTATTG, AB066297); for a 13 774 bp DNA region from psbB to rrn16, LA5 (AGTATTGCTGCTGTATTGTTTGCTTTT, this study) and LA6 (TTTGAGTTTCATTCTTGCGAACGTACTCCC, this study); for a 17 834 bp DNA region from rpl21 to rrn4.5, LA7 (TGTACGCTATAATTGAAACCGGAGGTGAAC, this study) and LA8 (TTTATCFATCACGATAGGTGCCAAGTGGAAG, this study); for a 15 083 bp DNA region from rpoC1 to rrn16, LA9 (GATGATTTTTAATTGTTAGTATGTATAGTCC, AB013657) and LA10 (TTTGAGTTTCATTCTTGCGAACGTACTCCC, this study); for a 9799 bp DNA region from psbA to psbD, LA11 (TGTAGGTATTTGGTTTACTGCTTTAGGTATC, X04465) and LA12 (ACCAACTACTCCAATAGCACTCATCCATAAA, AB013655); for a 19 156 bp DNA region from trnH to rpoC1, LA13 (AAATAATAAAAAATGGGCGAACGACGGGAAT, this study) and LA14 (AAAATCATCAAGGTATCTATGGTAATAAAAA, AB013657); for an 11 365 bp DNA region from rps7 to chlL, LA15 (GTTTCTTCTTTTTTTCGTATTGCTTCTCCAC, this study) and LA16 (GTAAATAACATCTTCAGGCCAAACATCTTCAT, this study). ###begin title 15
Sequence analysis
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 615 623 615 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P.patens</italic>
###xml 639 641 639 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c19">19</xref>
###xml 642 644 642 644 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c23">23</xref>
###xml 1101 1103 1101 1103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c19">19</xref>
###xml 615 623 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
The amplified cpDNA fragments were sheared, cloned into pUC18, and shotgun-sequenced using Shimadzu RISA384 sequencers by the Department of Genomic Research, Shimadzu Co., Japan. Nucleotide sequence files were assembled using the Phrap program (Phil Green, University of Washington, Seattle, WA, USA) and the resulting sequences were analyzed with Genetyx-Mac v.9.0 (Software Development) and Sequencer software (Hitachi Software Engineering). When the nucleotide sequences of either protein- or RNA-coding regions contained ambiguous bases or apparent small gaps, they were corrected by sequencing of re-amplified P.patens DNA fragments (19,23). Fifteen cpDNA regions with small gaps and 33 protein- or RNA-coding regions were amplified from total cellular DNA, and subcloned into pGEM-T Easy (Promega). At least three independent clones were picked up and sequenced using the DYEnamic ET Terminator Cycle Sequencing Kit (Amersham Bioscience) and an ABI PRISM 3100 DNA sequencer (Perkin-Elmer, Applied Biosystems). Database searches and sequence data analyses were performed as described previously (19).

The circular cpDNA of P.patens is 122 890 bp long, and its gene map is shown in Figure 1. Overall GC content of the cpDNA is 28.5%, which is closer to those of the liverwort M.polymorpha (28.8%) (7) and the hornwort A.formosae (32.9%) (8), the charophyte Chaetosphaeridium (29.6%) (9) and the algae (30.4-33%) (2), than to the GC content of the vascular plants (38-39%) (3). This quadripartite structure of cpDNA is well conserved in the bryophytes (7,8), a charophyte (9) and most vascular plants (3). BglII, ClaI and SacI restriction maps predicted from the complete nucleotide sequence coincide with the physical maps constructed previously (25).

Relative to hepatic bryophytes (M.polymorpha and A.formosae), an extensive rearrangement of the P.patens cpDNA is evident within the LSC region. A large inversion is present that encompasses a 71 kb region from petD to rpoB, its apparent endpoints lying between rps11 and rpoB at one end and petD and trnC-GCA at the other (Fig. 1, arrows). To investigate whether this large inversion occurs in other moss species, we used PCR with different combinations of primers on the DNAs of C.purpurea, P.euryphyllum, H.splendens and B.pomiformis. The combinations of primers P1 and P2, and primers P3 and P4 amplified the expected DNA fragments of 1002 and 1235 bp, respectively, from P.patens DNA (data not shown). In contrast, these primer combinations failed to amplify any DNA fragments from the DNA of other mosses. Eighteen genes for six tRNAs and 12 proteins contain introns, as shown in Figure 1 and Table 1. The intron of the trnL gene encoding tRNALeu-UAA is classified into the group I-type intron and the remaining introns of the 17 genes are categorized into the group II intron (8,21). A maturase-like polypeptide is encoded within the longest intron (2168 bp) of trnK coding for tRNALys-UUU. The gene for ribosomal protein S12 is divided into 5'-rps12 and 3'-rps12, and each gene segment can be transcribed independently and the transcripts trans-spliced. clpP and ycf3 contain two introns, as in the corresponding tobacco and rice genes, and the other intron-containing genes have a single intron. Anthoceros formosae ycf3 also has two introns, whereas M.polymorpha ycf3 has only the first of these introns. ycf66 is absent in A.formosae, but present in P.patens and M.polymorpha. The intron of rrn23 is present in A.formosae as well as in the green algae Chlorella (26) and Chlamydomonas (36), but not in P.patens, M.polymorpha (8) or the prasinophytes Mesostigma (32) and Nephroselmis (27), which constitute the most ancient green-plant lineage (5).

###begin title 32
###xml 8 12 8 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
Loss of rpoA from moss cpDNA
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 4 8 4 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 96 101 96 101 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl23</italic>
###xml 105 110 105 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps11</italic>
###xml 135 140 135 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps11</italic>

###xml 184 197 184 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P.patens rpoB</italic>
###xml 225 230 225 230 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps11</italic>

###xml 374 385 374 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C.purpureus</italic>
###xml 387 391 387 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">petD</italic>
###xml 419 424 419 424 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps11</italic>
###xml 442 446 442 446 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 479 480 479 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg726f2">2</xref>
###xml 540 544 540 544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 619 623 619 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 184 192 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
###xml 329 340 <span type="species:ncbi:53007">H.splendens</span>
###xml 342 355 <span type="species:ncbi:90297">P.euryphyllum</span>
###xml 357 369 <span type="species:ncbi:52976">B.pomiformis</span>
###xml 374 385 <span type="species:ncbi:3225">C.purpureus</span>
The rpoA gene is normally present immediately downstream from a ribosomal-protein gene cluster (rpl23 to rps11) and is located between rps11 and petD in most land plants. In contrast, P.patens rpoB is located downstream from rps11, and rpoA is completely absent from the cpDNA (Fig. 1). Interestingly, in the other moss species, H.splendens, P.euryphyllum, B.pomiformis and C.purpureus, petD is located downstream from rps11, and there is no rpoA gene located between them (Fig. 2B). This observation strongly suggests that the loss of the rpoA gene is a general occurrence in mosses. However, the possibility that the rpoA gene is present at some other locus on the cpDNA cannot be ruled out for other mosses.

###begin title 34
###xml 30 34 30 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
Identification of the nuclear rpoA gene
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 15 19 15 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 76 80 76 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 153 157 153 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 506 522 502 518 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 546 548 538 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c37">37</xref>
###xml 619 631 611 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M.polymorpha</italic>
###xml 663 669 655 661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 690 691 678 679 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg726f3">3</xref>
###xml 752 758 740 746 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PpRpoA</italic>
###xml 778 786 766 774 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P.patens</italic>
###xml 916 922 900 906 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PpRpoA</italic>
###xml 506 522 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 609 618 <span type="species:ncbi:3197">liverwort</span>
###xml 619 631 <span type="species:ncbi:3197">M.polymorpha</span>
###xml 663 669 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 778 786 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
The absence of rpoA in the moss cpDNA strongly suggests the transfer of the rpoA gene from the chloroplast to the nuclear genome. To isolate the nuclear rpoA counterpart, we searched the DNA databases and identified an EST (GenBank accession no. BI740521) encoding the C-terminal 57 amino acid residues of the alpha subunit. We next obtained and sequenced the full-length cDNA. The predicted protein contains 450 amino acid residues with the characteristic arrangement of protein domains identified in the Escherichia coli alpha subunit of RNAP (37) and shows 50% amino acid identity with chloroplast-encoded liverwort M.polymorpha RpoA and 29% identity with the E.coli alpha subunit (Fig. 3). Therefore, we designated this protein and gene PpRpoA and PpRpoA, respectively. The P.patens alpha homolog contains an N-terminal extension, which could be a transit peptide for targeting this protein to chloroplasts. The PpRpoA gene consists of two exons, which are separated by a 215 bp intron. The first exon encodes only the 94 amino acid N-terminal extension that is predicted to target PpRpoA to the chloroplast. Genomic Southern analysis showed that PpRpoA is encoded by a single copy gene (data not shown).
###end p 35
###begin title 36
Chloroplast localization of the moss PpRpoA
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 144 146 144 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c38">38</xref>
###xml 245 253 245 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P.patens</italic>
###xml 380 390 380 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PpRpoA-gfp</italic>
###xml 462 463 462 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg726f4">4</xref>
###xml 245 253 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
The N-terminal sequence of PpRpoA was predicted to specify chloroplast targeting (a score of 0.911) by the TargetP program for protein sorting (38). To localize PpRpoA in cells we further investigated the cellular localization in protoplasts of P.patens protonemata through the use of a chimeric protein comprised of the PpRpoA N-terminal 94 amino acid residues fused to GFP. The PpRpoA-gfp construct produced GFP fluorescence associated with chloroplasts (Fig. 4). This clearly demonstrated that the N-terminal extension of the PpRpoA functions as a chloroplast-targeting signal. This strongly suggests that the nuclear-encoded PpRpoA is a component of chloroplast RNAP.
###end p 37
###begin title 38
DISCUSSION
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 66 74 66 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P.patens</italic>
###xml 102 114 102 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M.polymorpha</italic>
###xml 129 137 129 137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P.patens</italic>
###xml 180 184 180 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cysA</italic>
###xml 189 193 189 193 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cysT</italic>
###xml 209 213 209 213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ycf3</italic>
###xml 304 312 304 312 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P.patens</italic>
###xml 66 74 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
###xml 102 114 <span type="species:ncbi:3197">M.polymorpha</span>
###xml 129 137 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
###xml 257 266 <span type="species:ncbi:3197">liverwort</span>
###xml 304 312 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
The size and quadripartite structure of the chloroplast genome of P.patens is very similar to that of M.polymorpha. The cpDNA of P.patens, like the vascular plants, however, lacks cysA and cysT and contains a ycf3 gene with two introns, whereas that of the liverwort does not. This implies that the moss P.patens diverged from the hepatic bryophytes and is more closely related to the vascular plants.

###xml 1297 1299 1297 1299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c44">44</xref>
###xml 51 59 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
###xml 465 473 <span type="species:ncbi:4031">geranium</span>
###xml 475 495 <span type="species:ncbi:4031">Pelargonium hortorum</span>
###xml 532 551 <span type="species:ncbi:4177">Epifagus virginiana</span>
###xml 615 636 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
###xml 675 683 <span type="species:ncbi:4031">geranium</span>
###xml 878 888 <span type="species:ncbi:4031">P.hortorum</span>
###xml 898 910 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P.falciparum</span>
The most striking feature of the cpDNA of the moss P.patens is the absence of rpoA. After the divergence of the mosses from the hepatic bryophytes, rpoA was lost from the cpDNA, together with ycf5, cysA and cysT. The loss of rpoA gene appears to be typical in mosses, and poses the question as to whether moss chloroplasts possess a transcription system unique among the green land plants. rpoA is already known to be absent from cpDNAs of the photosynthetic plant geranium (Pelargonium hortorum) (39), the parasitic vascular plant Epifagus virginiana (40), and from the plastid-like genome of the malaria parasite Plasmodium falciparum (41). The retained 60% of rpoA in the geranium cpDNA is extensively fragmented but an intact rpoA gene was not detected in the nuclear DNA by Southern hybridization analysis (39). In contrast, rpoB, C1 and C2 are most likely intact genes in P.hortorum (39) and P.falciparum (41). This suggests that rpoA gene was preferentially lost from the cpDNA, rather than rpoB, C1 and C2. The loss of single genes or groups of functionally related genes occurred similarly in specific clades, e.g. the loss of ycf1, ycf2 and accD in monocotyledonous plants (42,43), the loss of all ndh genes in the gymnosperm Pinus (29) and the loss of infA during angiosperm evolution (44).
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 43 47 43 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 161 167 157 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 206 207 202 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg726f3">3</xref>
###xml 258 262 254 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 378 382 374 378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 392 396 388 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 456 476 452 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana</italic>
###xml 478 480 474 476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c45">45</xref>
###xml 492 494 488 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c46">46</xref>
###xml 527 531 523 527 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 161 167 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 456 476 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>

In this study we have identified a nuclear rpoA gene encoding a protein predicted to contain 450 amino acids with high sequence identity to the alpha subunit of E.coli RNAP and the cpDNA-encoded RpoA (Fig. 3). Our findings clearly indicate that a functional rpoA has been transferred from the chloroplast to the nucleus. This is the first evidence for the presence of a nuclear rpoA. Nuclear rpoA genes with a chloroplast transit sequence are not found in Arabidopsis thaliana (45) and rice (46), whose cpDNAs retain an intact rpoA.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 73 85 73 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M.polymorpha</italic>
###xml 176 182 176 182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PpRpoA</italic>

###xml 377 379 377 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c18">18</xref>
###xml 443 460 443 460 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M.polymorpha rpoA</italic>
###xml 472 480 472 480 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P.patens</italic>
###xml 553 557 553 557 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 753 764 753 764 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C.purpureus</italic>
###xml 823 831 823 831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P.patens</italic>
###xml 917 921 917 921 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoA</italic>
###xml 63 72 <span type="species:ncbi:3197">liverwort</span>
###xml 73 85 <span type="species:ncbi:3197">M.polymorpha</span>
###xml 362 370 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
###xml 443 455 <span type="species:ncbi:3197">M.polymorpha</span>
###xml 472 480 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
###xml 753 764 <span type="species:ncbi:3225">C.purpureus</span>

There are 171 identical amino acid residues between PpRpoA and liverwort M.polymorpha RpoA, of which 98 are encoded by codons that differ at synonymous positions. For example, PpRpoA preferentially uses GC residues at the third position in Leu, and generally uses the codons TTC for Phe, AAG for Lys and GAG for Glu. These codons are also preferentially used in P.patens ESTs (18). In contrast, A or T residues are preferentially used for the M.polymorpha rpoA as well as P.patens chloroplast protein genes. This indicates that the transfer of the moss rpoA to the nucleus was not a recent event and may have occurred immediately after the divergence of mosses from the hepatic bryophytes. There is also an EST (AW098196) in the databases from the moss C.purpureus which is predicted to encode a polypeptide similar to the P.patens PpRpoA. This indicates that mosses probably possess in general a transferred nuclear rpoA gene.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 101 107 97 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>

###xml 653 655 633 635 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c20">20</xref>
###xml 656 658 636 638 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg726c50">50</xref>
###xml 744 752 724 732 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P.patens</italic>
###xml 101 107 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 312 320 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
###xml 396 402 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 528 536 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
###xml 744 752 <span type="species:ncbi:3218">P.patens</span>
The alpha subunit is well characterized to be essential for RNAP assembly and basal transcription in E.coli (47,48). Therefore, in the moss chloroplast, the alpha subunit is indispensable for the function of plastid-encoded plastid RNAP (PEP), and the PpRpoA probably functions as the alpha subunit of PEP. Many P.patens chloroplast genes have canonical -10 and -35 sequences similar to those of E.coli promoter sequences (49). Moreover, cDNAs encoding sigma-like transcription initiation factor for PEP have been isolated from P.patens, and the encoded transit peptide of sigma-like factor has been shown to function as a chloroplast-targeting signal (20,50). Therefore, there is no doubt that PEP also transcribes photosynthetic genes in the P.patens chloroplasts. Our data indicate that the biosynthesis and assembly of the core subunits of PEP are controlled cooperatively by the chloroplast and nuclear genomes, and a relatively more complicated transcription system operates in mosses than in higher plants.
