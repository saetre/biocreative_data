###begin article-title 0
Role of alphaBI5 and alphaBT162 residues in subunit interaction during oligomerization of alphaB-crystallin
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
This is an open-access article distributed under the terms of the Creative Commons Attribution License, which permits unrestricted use, distribution, and reproduction in any medium, provided the original work is properly cited.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Purpose
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 43 44 43 44 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
To determine whether the residues in the NH2- and COOH-terminal extensions interact with one another during oligomerization of alphaB-crystallin.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 143 159 135 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 143 159 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Site-directed mutagenesis was used to mutate alphaBI5 and alphaBT162 residues to Cys. The recombinant I5C and T162C proteins were expressed in Escherichia coli cells and purified using chromatographic techniques. These proteins were analyzed by SDS-PAGE and mass spectrometry and characterized by multi-angle light scattering and circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy methods. Fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) assay was used to determine the interaction between the subunits.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Dimer formation was observed in both alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C in storage at 4 degreesC. During air oxidation at room temperature, alphaBT162C formed dimers to a greater extent than alphaBI5C. The average molar masses, secondary structures, and chaperone-like activities of the reduced forms of I5C and T162C were comparable to that of wild type alphaB-crystallin. The oligomeric assembly of reduced forms of I5C and T162C appeared homogenous under JEOL 1200EX Electron microscope whereas the oxidized proteins appeared as irregular aggregates. FRET assay demonstrated interactions between alphaBI5C-alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C-alphaBT162C. However, there was no evidence of an interaction between alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C residues during oligomerization.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusions
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 45 46 45 46 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 286 287 282 283 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
This study suggests that residues from the NH2- and COOH-terminal regions in alphaB-crystallin interact with residues from the corresponding regions of another subunit, but there exists no interaction between the residues at the COOH-terminal extension region and the residues at the NH2-terminal region.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Introduction
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 46 47 42 43 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 153 154 149 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 206 207 198 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 396 397 384 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 502 503 486 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 504 505 488 489 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 830 831 807 808 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 1144 1145 1113 1114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 1146 1147 1115 1116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Human alphaB-crystallin, also known as HspB5 [1], is a small heat-shock protein (sHSP) that contains 175 amino acids and has a molecular mass of 20 kDa [2]. alphaB-Crystallin has chaperone-like properties [3], is expressed in various tissues, and responds to thermal, environmental, and chemical stressors. Upregulation of alphaB-crystallin has been reported in Alzheimer and Parkinson diseases [4], and mutant alphaB-crystallin has been associated with cataract formation and desmin-related myopathy [5,6]. alphaBeta-Crystallin is an intensively studied lens protein yet its three-dimensional structure remains unknown. Its polydispersity is the main obstacle to the acquisition of X-ray crystallographic data. Further, the crystallin oligomer is too bulky for structure determination by nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy [7]. To circumvent these limitations, several solution-based interaction studies and low-resolution electron microscopy (EM)-based structural studies have been conducted but have provided limited clues to the structural organization of the alphaB-crystallin oligomer and its hetero-oligomer with alphaA-crystallin [8,9].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 200 201 193 194 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 233 235 226 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 236 238 229 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 304 328 297 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Methanococcus jannaschii</italic>
###xml 491 493 480 482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 494 496 483 485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 640 642 625 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 643 645 628 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 304 328 <span type="species:ncbi:2190">Methanococcus jannaschii</span>
###xml 343 348 <span type="species:ncbi:4565">wheat</span>
Sequence comparison with other sHSPs shows that alphaB-crystallin has an evolutionarily conserved sequence with a crystallin domain of several beta strands in an immunoglobulin-like fold flanked by NH2- and COOH-terminal extensions [10,11]. The X-ray crystal structures of related proteins, Mj sHSP16.5 (Methanococcus jannaschii sHSP16.5) and wheat sHSP16.9, suggest that there exist common structural features that can be attributed to the alpha-crystallin domain region in these proteins [12-14]. In alphaB-crystallin, residues 19-71, 75-82, 131-138, 141-148, and 155-164 have been implicated in oligomerization and subunit interactions [15-19].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 134 135 134 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 136 138 136 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 139 141 139 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 295 297 291 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 386 388 382 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 440 442 436 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 458 460 454 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r23">23</xref>
###xml 556 558 548 550 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 726 728 718 720 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 1005 1007 986 988 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
###xml 1326 1328 1299 1301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 1472 1474 1445 1447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 1475 1477 1448 1450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r26">26</xref>
###xml 1496 1497 1469 1470 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1657 1659 1622 1624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r27">27</xref>
###xml 1799 1800 1764 1765 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1900 1901 1865 1866 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1987 1988 1952 1953 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 2092 2094 2057 2059 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 2178 2180 2139 2141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 2212 2213 2169 2170 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 2365 2366 2322 2323 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 2535 2537 2484 2486 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
###xml 2538 2540 2487 2489 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r30">30</xref>
###xml 2651 2653 2596 2598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r31">31</xref>
The COOH-terminal of sHSPs is largely unstructured, contains many charged residues, and is believed to play a role in solubilization [7,11,14]. The results obtained from the mammalian two-hybrid system has shown that the COOH-terminal region was important for alphaB-crystallin oligomerization [20], and the results were confirmed by other studies based on peptide-protein interaction [21] and change in oligomer size following truncation [22] or mutations [23]. The COOH-terminal 157-164 region of alphaB-crystallin has been shown to be a chaperone site [17], and deletion of this region resulted in poor solubility and larger aggregate formation, which suggests that this region is critical for the function of the protein [19]. Site directed mutagenesis studies of conserved IXI/V motif in the COOH-terminal extension of alphaB-crystallin have shown that this region comes in the proximity of the beta8-strand in the alpha-crystallin domain and also interacts with the same motif from another subunit [24]. However, the involvement of residues COOH-terminal to the IXI/V motif in oligomerization is not fully understood. Lys150 of alphaB-crystallin was found to cross-link with Lys166 of either the same or another molecule of alphaB-crystallin when 3.3'-dithiobis(sulfosuccinimidyl propionate) was used as a cross-linker [25]. It is not clear to what extent the cross-linker length was a determining factor in this presumed interaction. Protein cross-linking studies [25,26] have not shown NH2-terminus interaction between alphaB-crystallin subunits. A pin array study showed a weak interaction between alphaB-crystallin and immobilized residues 11-18 [27], but this has not been confirmed by other studies. In sHSP 16.9, on the basis of crystal structure, it has been concluded that only the NH2-terminal region of six subunits in an oligomer has some structure whereas the other six subunits NH2-terminal regions are presumed disordered. Further, it has also been shown that the NH2-terminal residues 7-10 in HSP 16.9 interact with residues 108-110 in the subunit and act as a "patch" [13]. Residues 7-10 of HSP 16.9 are analogous to residues 21- 24 in alphaB-crystallin [13] since the alphaB-crystallin NH2-terminal region has an additional 14 residues compared to HSP 16.9. Previous mutagenesis or two-hybrid system studies have not addressed the role of NH2-terminal residues 1-19 in homo-oligomerization of alphaB-crystallin. The alphaB-crystallin oligomer is a dynamic molecule that constantly dissociates and reassociates [28-30]. Since the COOH-terminal truncation of alphaB-crystallin has been observed during aging and diabetic lenses [31], it has been proposed that this modification might be affecting the oligomer nature and function of alpha-crystallin in vivo.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 156 157 156 157 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 456 458 448 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 459 461 451 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 661 662 649 650 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 852 853 836 837 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
Oxidation of single cysteine proteins leads to dimerization when the reactive cysteines are in close proximity. Therefore, to investigate the role of the NH2-terminal region in alphaB-crystallin oligomerization in the present study, we converted the I5 residue to Cys and characterized the recombinant protein. The I5 residue is outside the alphaB-crystallin sequence that is homologous to sHSP 16.5 and sHSP 16.9, of which the crystal structure is known [12,13]. We also prepared a T162C mutant of alphaB-crystallin to determine the role of COOH-terminal extension outside the conserved IXI/V motif in oligomerization. The extent of interactions between the NH2-terminal and COOH-terminal regions of mutant alphaB-crystallin was investigated using the fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) assay. Our study showed that the I5 residue in the NH2-terminal region is involved in subunit interaction to a lesser extent than the T162C residues in the COOH-terminal extension. Additionally, there was no evidence of interactions between I5C and T162C residues in the subunits.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Methods
###end title 15
###begin title 16
Expression and purification of recombinant proteins
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 383 399 371 387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 509 511 497 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r32">32</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 383 404 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">Escherichia coli BL21</span>
Human alphaB-crystallin cDNA was cloned in pET-23d(+) vector (Novagen-EMD Biosciences, Madison, WI) and used as the template to introduce cysteine mutations at alphaBI5 or alphaBT162 by site-directed mutagenesis (Quik-Change site-directed mutagenesis kit, Stratagene, La Jolla, CA). Mutation was confirmed by automated DNA sequencing. Wild-type and mutant proteins were expressed in Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) pLysS cells (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) and purified according to the method described previously [32]. Purity of the recombinant proteins was evaluated by SDS-PAGE, and molecular mass was confirmed by Matrix-Assisted Laser Desorption/Ionization Time-of Flight (MALDI-TOF) Mass Spectrometry.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Structural characterization of recombinant proteins
###end title 18
###begin p 19
The wild-type and mutant alphaB-crystallins were characterized by spectroscopic methods. All assays were performed in 50 mM phosphate buffer containing 150 mM NaCl and 0.02% sodium azide at pH 7.2.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Tryptophan fluorescence
###end title 20
###begin p 21
The intrinsic fluorescence spectra of wild-type and mutant alphaB-crystallin proteins were analyzed using a Jasco spectrofluorimeter FP-750 (JASCO Corporation, Tokyo, Japan). Protein samples at 0.2 mg/ml were excited at 295 nm, and the emission spectra were recorded between 300 and 400 nm.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
1,1'-bi(4-anilino) naphthalene-5,5'-disulfonic acid fluorescence
###end title 22
###begin p 23
The surface hydrophobicity of wild-type and mutant alphaB-crystallin proteins was compared using the probe, 1,1'-bi(4-anilino)naphthalene-5,5'-disulfonic acid (bis-ANS; Molecular Probes Inc., Eugene, OR). Bis-ANS (10 mul of 1 mM) was added to a 0.2 mg/ml sample and excited at 385 nm, and the emission spectra were recorded from 400 nm to 600 nm.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Circular dichroism studies
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 531 533 531 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
Changes in protein secondary structure were evaluated by far- and near-ultraviolet (UV) circular dichroism (CD) spectra in a JASCO J-815 CD spectrometer (JASCO Inc., Easton, MD). A protein concentration of 0.2 mg/ml was used for far-UV CD measurements, and a concentration of 3 mg/ml was used for near-UV CD measurements. At least six scans in 5 mm path lengths were recorded for each sample and then averaged. Secondary structural elements were determined according to a computer software program derived from Sreerama and Woody [33].
###end p 25
###begin title 26
Molecular size determination by multi-angle light scattering
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 221 223 213 215 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">XL</sub>
###xml 665 667 657 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r34">34</xref>
The quaternary structure of the recombinant alphaB-crystallin and its mutants was determined by multi-angle light scattering measurements. Protein samples (100 microg in phosphate buffer) were passed through a TSK G5000PWXL gel (Tosoh Bioscience, Montgomeryville, PA) size-exclusion column connected to a HPLC system with refractive index detector (Shimadzu Scientific Instruments, Inc.,Columbia, MD) and a multiangle light scattering and quasi-elastic light scattering detectors (Wyatt Technology Corp., Santa Barbara, CA). The molar mass, hydrodynamic radius and polydispersity of the samples were estimated using ASTRA (5.1.5) software, as described previously [34].
###end p 27
###begin title 28
Electron microscopic study
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 323 324 319 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
The oligomeric structure of the mutant alphaB-crystallin proteins were examined under the JEOL 1200EX electron microscope (JEOL, Ltd. Tokyo, Japan). A drop of protein (1 mg/ml) was applied to carbon-coated grids and negatively stained with 2% uranyl acetate, and the specimens were examined under different magnifications [8].
###end p 29
###begin title 30
Functional characterization of recombinant proteins
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 476 477 469 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 478 480 471 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r32">32</xref>
For aggregation studies, 250 mug of alcohol dehydrogenase (Biozyme Laboratories, San Diego, CA) was incubated at 37 degreesC in 1 ml of 50 mM phosphate buffer (pH 7.4), which contained 150 mM NaCl. Aggregation was initiated by the addition of 100 mM EDTA in the absence or presence of mutant or wild-type proteins. The extent of aggregation was measured by monitoring the light scattering at 360 nm in a Shimadzu UV-VIS spectrophotometer (Shimadzu Corporation, Kyoto, Japan) [3,32].
###end p 31
###begin title 32
Subunit interaction study by FRET assay
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 89 91 81 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 194 195 178 179 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5</sub>
###xml 229 230 213 214 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5</sub>
###xml 559 561 543 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 785 786 759 760 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</sub>
###xml 788 789 762 763 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">0</sub>
###xml 826 828 800 802 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r35">35</xref>
###xml 898 900 872 874 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
The interaction between alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C was measured using the FRET technique [15]. The cysteines in alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C were labeled with thiol reactive dyes. Alexa Fluor 488 (C5-maleimide) and Alexa Fluor 555 (C5-maleimide) were used as donor and acceptor fluorophores, respectively. Additionally, in some experiments, the proteins were labeled with amine reactive dyes, Alexa Fluor 350 (carboxylic acid TFP ester) as the donor fluorophore and Alexa Fluor 488 (carboxylic acid TFP ester) as the acceptor fluorophore as described previously [15]. For FRET assays, 50 microg of each labeled protein was incubated at 37 degreesC. Subunit exchange interactions were monitored by measuring the ratio of acceptor fluorescence intensity at baseline and at timed intervals (Ft/F0), as described in a previous study [35]. The subunit exchange rate was calculated according to Bova et al. [28].
###end p 33
###begin title 34
Mass spectrometry analysis
###end title 34
###begin p 35
The interaction between alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C was also studied by nanospray Quadrupole  Time of  Flight  Mass  Spectrometry. Reduced I5C and T162C proteins (25 mug each) were mixed together and incubated at room temperature for 3 h. Reducing agents were removed by the dialysis method, and the proteins were allowed to form dimers at room temperature. The sample was desalted using C-18 spin columns (Pierce Biotechnology, Rockford, IL), eluted in 70% acetonitrile, and analyzed by nanospray mass spectrometry to identify hetero-dimer mass.
###end p 35
###begin title 36
Results
###end title 36
###begin title 37
Expression, purification and characterization of the alphaB-crystallin mutants
###end title 37

Gel chromatography by TSK 5000 column in the presence of the buffer that contained reducing agent TCEP and analysis of the eluted proteins by multi-angle light scattering methods revealed that both wild-type and mutant proteins exhibit similar elution profiles with only a minor difference. The wild-type and mutant forms of alphaB-crystallin showed an average oligomeric mass of 700+/-130 kDa at the peak apex (Figure 2), indicating that the mutation did not significantly affect the homo-oligomerization of the proteins. This suggested that on average the oligomers were composed of 35 subunits. In contrast, in the absence of the reducing agent in the buffer, both alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C mutants formed larger oligomers, presumably due to the cysteine-cysteine cross-linking of subunits or oligomers. The TSK 5000 column profile shown in Figure 3 for oxidized and reduced alphaBT162C crystallin indicates the heterogeneity in the oligomers. Under similar elution conditions, alphaBI5C stored without reducing agents was found to elute as a broad peak (not shown), which suggests that the oligomers further aggregate. Under electron microscopy, mutant alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C proteins stored in the absence of TCEP appeared as aggregates with irregular shapes whereas the oligomeric assembly of the reduced forms of alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C was more homogenous (Figure 4) with a diameter of about 10-15 nm, which is comparable to that of wild-type alphaB-crystallin [9].

Intrinsic tryptophan fluorescence and bis-ANS fluorescence of the alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C proteins under reducing conditions were similar to those of wild-type alphaB-crystallin (Figure 5). We also investigated whether the alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C mutations altered the chaperone-like function of alphaB-crystallin. ###begin title 48
Subunit interactions on fluorescence energy transfer studies
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 106 108 98 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r37">37</xref>
###xml 242 244 230 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r37">37</xref>
###xml 700 709 676 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f8">Figure 8A</xref>
###xml 772 774 740 742 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;5</sup>
###xml 778 780 746 748 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 984 986 948 950 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;6</sup>
###xml 990 992 954 956 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 1585 1594 1541 1550 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f8">Figure 8B</xref>
###xml 1946 1948 1894 1896 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;5</sup>
###xml 1952 1954 1900 1902 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 1956 1964 1904 1912 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f9">Figure 9</xref>
Fluorescence labeling has been used previously to investigate alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin interactions [37]. Previous studies have shown that the labeling of alphaB-crystallin with Alexa Fluor does not affect the properties of the protein [37]. Therefore, we used a similar approach to investigate alphaB-wild-type and mutant interactions. In these studies, the reaction mixtures of Alexa Fluor 488-alphaBT162C and Alexa Fluor 555-alphaBT162C (both labeled at Cys) showed a time-dependant decrease in the emission intensity of donor fluorophores at 519 nm and a relative increase in the emission intensity of acceptor fluorophores at 565 nm, indicating close proximity of the two labeled residues (Figure 8A). The interaction rate for alphaBT162C-alphaBT162C was 5.16x10-5 sec-1. A similar assay of alphaBI5C labeled with Alexa Fluor 488 as donor and Alexa Fluor 555 as acceptor revealed a measurable interaction between the subunits, and the rate of subunit interaction was 1.66x10-6 sec-1. The difference in the calculated rate in subunit interaction between I5C and T162C mutant proteins is likely a reflection of the proximity of Cys groups involved in interaction. The T162C mutant, which showed greater propensity to form dimers upon oxidation, also gave a better value of interaction during FRET assay. Surprisingly, in an experiment in which we mixed oligomers of alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C labeled with Cys-reacting fluorophores, Alexa Fluor 488 and Alexa Fluor 555, respectively, negligible fluorescence resonance energy transfers occurred between the two labeled proteins (Figure 8B). It appeared as though there was no subunit interaction between the two sets of oligomers. However, when the interactions were reexamined using amine-labeled alphaBI5C (donor) and alphaBT162C (acceptor), the transfer of energy was robust from the donor to the acceptor. The calculated subunit interaction rate for amine-labeled experiments was 6.5x10-5 sec-1 (Figure 9), higher than observed for cysteine-labeled alphaBI5C or alphaBT162C. The absence of interaction between I5C and T162C of alphaB-crystallin was further confirmed by mass spectrometric analysis of a mixture of the two proteins. Nanospray QqTOF MS analysis of a mixture of alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C showed a molecular mass of 20149.6 and 40299.45, corresponding to the monomers and dimers of alphaBI5C, respectively, and molecular mass of 20160.8 (monomer) and 40323.08 (dimer) of alphaBT162C. However, there was no evidence of a hetero-dimer having a mass of 40308.43, which corresponds to the mass of alphaBI5C-alphaBT162C dimer, suggesting that the two cysteine mutants do not form dimers involving cysteines from hetero subunits.

###begin title 52
Discussion
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 125 127 121 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r38">38</xref>
###xml 170 172 166 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r39">39</xref>
###xml 201 203 197 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r40">40</xref>
###xml 248 249 244 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 287 289 283 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r41">41</xref>
###xml 317 319 313 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r42">42</xref>
###xml 602 604 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 626 628 614 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 756 758 740 742 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 779 781 763 765 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 956 958 932 934 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r43">43</xref>
###xml 959 961 935 937 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r44">44</xref>
###xml 610 615 <span type="species:ncbi:4565">wheat</span>
###xml 764 769 <span type="species:ncbi:4565">wheat</span>
Several models of the quaternary structure of alpha-crystallin have been proposed including a dynamic protein micelle model [38], a cubic and rhombic dodecahedron model [39], a pitted-flexiball model [40], a chaperone-like annulus or toroid model [7], an octomer disc-like shaped model [41], and an open-loose model [42]. No general agreement has been reached for a consensus model that incorporates the dynamic and polydispersed nature of this heteromeric protein. However, due to their sequence similarity to alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin, the three-dimensional structures of a sHSP from Mj HSP16.5 [12] and wheat HSP 16.9 [13] make it possible to model the quaternary structure of alpha-crystallin. On the basis of crystal structure data on Mj HSP16.5 [12] and wheat HSP16.9 [13] and cryo-EM data on alphaB-crystallin, it has been proposed that alphaB-crystallin subunits assemble to form different molecular mass oligomers with a large central cavity [43,44]. We indeed observed under electron microscopy a less dense central region in both alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C crystallin oligomers, suggesting the presence of a lightly packed cavity. While the significance of the cavity in the alpha-crystallin oligomer is not known, the arrangement of subunits with a central cavity offers the flexibility to accommodate a varying number of subunits in a dynamic oligomer.
###end p 53

###xml 1568 1576 1534 1542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 1597 1605 1563 1571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 1169 1174 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The flexible COOH-terminal hydrophilic tail is likely to be important for keeping the protein in soluble form [7]. The more hydrophobic region of the NH2-terminal domain would favor multimerization of alphaB-crystallin [11,14]. Electron paramagnetic resonance studies combined with site-directed spin labeling studies revealed that alphaB-crystallin is composed of flexible building units with an extended surface area where beta4-beta8 strands of the protein are involved in subunit-subunit interaction during complex assembly [10,44]. However, those studies did not include the extent of the interaction between two NH2-terminal extensions  or  two COOH-terminal regions extension of alphaB-crystallin during oligomerization. In a study where Hsp16.5 was converted to a polydisperse oligomer by insertion mutagenesis [44], the COOH-terminal was found to tether between the dimers. Further, the NH2-terminal of the engineered protein was more exposed and available for interaction with denaturing proteins [44]. We used the Cys-Cys cross-linking approach to understand how the NH2- and COOH-terminal regions in alphaB-crystallin interact during oligomerization. Since human alphaB-crystallin is a Cys-free protein, we introduced a single Cys at the COOH-terminal and NH2-terminal regions to obtain alphaBT162 and alphaBI5C mutants. The introduction of Cys at these two positions did not affect chaperone activity (Figure 7), overall hydrophobicity (Figure 5A), or intrinsic tryptophan fluorescence (Figure 5B). The Cys mutants showed only minimal structural changes (Figure 6) and oligomer size (Figure 2). Together, these findings suggest that the structure-function of the mutants did not change significantly from that of the wild-type protein.

###xml 335 344 319 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f8">Figure 8A</xref>
###xml 461 463 445 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r45">45</xref>

###xml 1077 1078 1041 1042 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1442 1444 1394 1396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 1616 1618 1540 1542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r27">27</xref>
###xml 1915 1917 1823 1825 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 1918 1920 1826 1828 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r26">26</xref>
###xml 2091 2093 1991 1993 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r26">26</xref>
###xml 2286 2288 2186 2188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 2536 2538 2424 2426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 2890 2892 2762 2764 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 2893 2895 2765 2767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r26">26</xref>
###xml 2914 2915 2786 2787 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 3084 3086 2944 2946 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 3171 3172 3031 3032 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 3212 3214 3068 3070 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 3725 3726 3573 3574 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
The labeling of alphaBI5C and  alphaBT162C  mutants  with fluorescent probes Alexa Fluor 488 and Alexa Fluor 555,  respectively, did not disrupt the oligomer size and function of the proteins. The results of the FRET assay suggest that the alphaBT162C residue of a subunit comes into close proximity of alphaBT162C of another subunit (Figure 8A). The distance between donor and acceptor molecules should remain less than 100 A for a successful energy transfer [45]. Similarly, FRET studies showed that 5C of one subunit comes in close proximity of 5C from another alphaB subunit during oligomerization. In contrast, the FRET assay demonstrates no energy transfer between alphaB5C (donor) and alphaB162C (acceptor), suggesting that the I5 residues at the NH2-terminal region of a subunit and the T162 residues at the COOH-terminal region of another subunit do not interact during oligomerization. However, the alphaBI5C protein labeled at Lys residues and the alphaBT162C crystallin readily exchange subunits during oligomerization. This was evident from the FRET between the NH2-labeled I5C (donor) and labeled alphaBT162C (acceptor), and the rate of subunit exchange was maximal, reflecting unhindered interaction and fluorescence energy transfer. The data further confirm that the mutation did not affect the subunit exchange. There are 10 lysine residues in alphaB-crystallin, and the majority of these are in the alpha-crystallin domain [11]. Among these residues, alphaBK82, alphaBK90, alphaBK92, alphaBK103, alphaBK119, and alphaBK150 are at the subunit interaction regions in alphaB-crystallin homo-oligomer [27]. It is likely that the labeling at these sites might have led to maximal energy transfer between amine-labeled alphaBI5C and alphaBT162C proteins. Chemical cross-linking has been used previously to determine the interactions between alphaA-crystallin and alphaB-crystallin in a hetero-oligomer [25,26]. In one study, alphaA-crystallin Lys166 cross-linked to alphaB-crystallin Lys175 when 3.3'-dithiobis[sulfosuccinimidyl propionate] (DTSSP) was used as the cross-linker [26]. In another study, however, the same residues were not cross-linked by DTSSP, but it was suggested that Lys150 might be cross-linking with Lys166 either intramolecularly or intermolecularly [25]. The discrepancy between the results of these two studies may be due to differences in experimental conditions and sample preparations. Additionally, since alpha-crystallin is composed of alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin in approximately 3:1 ratio [11], it is likely that the mosaic nature of the heteroaggregate does not permit cross-linking between alphaB-crystallin subunits. In support of this view, we observed that when alphaA-crystallin was mixed with alphaBT162C in equal ratio, the cross-linking of alphaB-crystallin mutants decreased significantly (not shown). Previous cross-linking studies [25,26] have not shown NH2-terminus interactions between alphaB-crystallin subunits, but Lys11 of alphaA-crystallin was suggested to be involved in interactions with Lys121 of alphaB-crystallin [25]. A two-hybrid study was also performed to determine the involvement of COOH- and NH2-terminal regions of alphaB-crystallin [20]. Contrary to the observation made in our study, the two-hybrid system assay showed that only the COOH-terminal domain was important for self aggregation of alphaB-crystallin. Since it is also known that alphaB-crystallin interacts with other proteins through its chaperone site, it is not clear to what extent the chaperone action was involved in the two-hybrid system interaction studies interpreted as subunit interactions. Additionally, it is not known whether the hybrid proteins used in the study with NH2- or COOH-terminal domains of alphaB-crystallin folded the same way as in the native protein where the subunit interaction sites are fully accessible.

###begin p 56
###xml 171 172 171 172 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 284 285 280 281 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 513 515 505 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 516 518 508 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 655 657 639 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 747 748 727 728 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 892 893 872 873 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1005 1007 981 983 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 1045 1047 1021 1023 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 1075 1076 1051 1052 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1238 1239 1206 1207 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
For the first time, our study of Cys-substituted mutants, which did not involve the use of cross-linking agents, demonstrated the involvement of residue(s) close to the NH2-terminus of alphaB-crystallin with corresponding residue(s) from another subunit during oligomerization. The NH2-terminal region comprising of 5C residues in the alphaB-crystallin mutant that we identified as the interacting region does not have an equivalent sequence in HSPs 16.5 and 16.9, the two proteins with known crystal structures [12,13]. Therefore, the homology model study of alphaB-crystallin has only provided structural insight to the alphaB-crystallin 20-170 region [21]. The results of the present study suggest that in alphaB-crystallin, a residue in the NH2-terminal 1-20 region has a role in oligomerization of this sHSP. Previous mutagenesis studies involving the conserved Phe-rich region in the NH2-terminal domain suggested that residues may be important for both structure and function of alphaA-crystallin [16]. Limited trypsin digestion studies [22] also suggested that the NH2-terminal region is involved in oligomerization of alphaB- and alphaA-crystallins. However, those studies did not indicate the role of specific residues in the NH2-terminal domain.

###begin p 57
###xml 170 172 166 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 717 718 705 706 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1234 1235 1214 1215 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1315 1317 1291 1293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 1318 1320 1294 1296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 1384 1385 1360 1361 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1561 1562 1533 1534 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1591 1593 1563 1565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 1890 1891 1858 1859 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1990 1998 1950 1958 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f4">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 2425 2427 2377 2379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r46">46</xref>
###xml 2732 2734 2680 2682 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r46">46</xref>
The involvement of the COOH-terminal T162 region of alphaB-crystallin in oligomerization was earlier identified by pin array studies as one of the interactive sequences [19]. However, the same study did not show the I5 region as an interacting site during alphaB-crystallin oligomerization. The pin array study also did not identify the region in alphaB-crystallin responsible for interactions with the peptide(s) in array. It should be noted that the lack of three dimensional (3D) conformation of peptides in a pin array does not offer a recognizable binding site similar to that presented by a folded protein. The interactions between the COOH-terminal extensions appear to be more pronounced than those between NH2-terminal regions because Cys oxidation that induced dimer formation was about three times more prevalent for T162C mutants than for I5C mutants under identical storage conditions. The mass spectroscopic study also suggested that the T162C forms a dimer more readily than the I5C. However, the mass spectroscopic studies did not demonstrate the interaction between I5C and T162C mutants of alphaB-crystallin through Cys residues. Previous studies have shown that alphaB-crystallin subunit interaction involves the NH2-terminal domain, the alpha-crystallin domain, and the COOH-terminal extension [15-19]. However, none of the studies found interaction between the NH2-terminal domain and COOH-terminal extensions, but there is evidence for the interaction between residues 108 and 110 in the alpha-crystallin domain and residues 7-10 in the NH2-terminal domain of HSP16.9 [13]. As discussed above, FRET studies involving I5C and T162C did not show any interactions between the epitopes carrying these mutations. Therefore, one can conclude that even though there are multiple interactions between the alphaB-crystallin subunits, they do not involve interactions between NH2-terminal residues 1-19 and COOH-terminal extensions. The aggregation of alphaBI5C or alphaBT162C (Figure 4) following oxidation suggests that forced dimerization of alphaB-crystallin subunits leads to aggregation and precipitation. Therefore, it is likely that cross-linking of subunits following age-related modifications such as glycation may result in the formation of light scattering aggregates in vivo. Recently, it was shown that the COOH-terminal truncation of alphaB-crystallin causes protein insolubilization and myopathy [46], and it was shown that the truncated proteins aggregate in vivo. Our study shows that the COOH-terminal residues play a role in oligomerization, and disruption of this interaction may be a causative factor in the destabilization and uncontrolled aggregation of the alphaBQ151X mutant described earlier [46]. Further, it is likely that age-related truncation of alphaB-crystallin may result in oligomers that are not governed by the physiologically significant COOH-terminal interactions. Instead, the crystallin subunits may form large oligomers that scatter light.
