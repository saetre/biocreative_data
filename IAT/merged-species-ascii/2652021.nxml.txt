###begin article-title 0
alpha-Crystallin distribution in retinal pigment epithelium and effect of gene knockouts on sensitivity to oxidative stress
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
This is an open-access article distributed under the terms of the Creative Commons Attribution License, which permits unrestricted use, distribution, and reproduction in any medium, provided the original work is properly cited.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Purpose
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 105 109 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To investigate the susceptibility of retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) from alphaA (-/-) and alphaB (-/-) mice to oxidative stress, and the subcellular changes of alphaA and alphaB-crystallins under oxidative stress.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 34 35 34 35 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 36 37 36 37 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 177 178 169 170 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 179 180 171 172 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 410 411 402 403 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 412 413 404 405 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 111 115 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 284 289 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
The effect of hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) on apoptosis in RPE from alphaA (-/-), alphaB (-/-), and wild type (wt) mice was assessed by TUNEL and AnnexinV/Propidium Iodide assays. H2O2-induced changes in caspase-3 activity and mitochondrial permeability transition (MPT) were determined. Human RPE in early passages (2-4) were starved in 1% FBS-containing Dulbecco's modified Eagle medium (DMEM) and treated with H2O2 for 24 h. Gene expression was quantitated by real time PCR. Confocal microscopy was used to examine alpha-crystallin compartmentalization. Whole cell and mitochondrial alpha-crystallin protein amounts were examined by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and Western blot analysis.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 101 102 93 94 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 103 104 95 96 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 186 187 178 179 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 188 189 180 181 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 336 337 324 325 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 338 339 326 327 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 36 40 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 170 175 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
RPE from alphaA (-/-), alphaB (-/-) mice exhibited increased susceptibility to apoptosis induced by H2O2, increased caspase-3 activation, and increased MPT. Treatment of human RPE with H2O2 resulted in a dose-dependent decrease in alphaB-crystallin mRNA expression. Confocal microscopy and subcellular fractionation of RPE showed that H2O2 treatment decreased cytosolic and mitochondrial pools of alphaB-crystallin but caused no change in alphaA-crystallin content. TEM confirmed changes in expression of alphaA and alphaB-crystallins with oxidative stress.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusions
###end title 8
###begin p 9
Lack of alpha-crystallins renders RPE cells more susceptible to apoptosis from oxidative stress. Mitochondrial alpha-crystallins may play an important role in the protection from increased susceptibility of RPE in oxidative stress.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Introduction
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 106 107 102 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 318 319 306 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 320 321 308 309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 370 371 358 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 533 534 517 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 756 757 724 725 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 939 940 899 900 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
alpha-Crystallins belong to the family of small heat shock proteins (sHSPs) that include Hsp25 and Hsp27 [1]. While original studies on alpha-crystallins dealt with their abundant expression and role in the lens, it is now generally accepted that alpha-crystallins are proteins with entirely different non-lens roles [2,3] and expressed in multiple tissues of the body [4]. The two forms of alpha-crystallins, A and B, share an amino acid sequence homology of about 57% and are found in heterogeneous aggregates of the two proteins [5]. While the distribution of alphaA and alphaB-crystallins are different-alphaA is found predominantly in the lens while alphaB is ubiquitous-both have been demonstrated to protect cells from thermal and metabolic stress [6]. Furthermore, their ability to prevent apoptosis by inhibiting caspases implies that alphaA and alphaB-crystallins may provide crucial physiological functions in non-lens tissues [7].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 212 213 197 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 411 412 388 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 583 585 552 554 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 700 702 661 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 52 57 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
An analysis of the expression of crystallins in the mouse retina showed that alphaA, alphaB, beta, and gamma-crystallins were found in the inner and outer nuclear layers and the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) [8]. In another study, RPE cells overexpressing alphaB-crystallin showed resistance to apoptosis, suggesting that alpha-crystallins may play a beneficial role in preventing stress-induced cell death [9]. While both alphaA and alphaB-crystallins offer cell protection, the relative potencies and mechanisms of cell protection have not been fully delineated. Andley et al. [10] found that the antiapoptotic activity of alphaA is greater than alphaB-crystallin in the lens, while Mao et al. [11] have shown that alphaA and alphaB-crystallins display similar degrees of protection against apoptosis in both lens and non-lens tissues. Information on the subcellular localization of alphaA and alphaB-crystallin within RPE, which has hitherto not been studied in detail, may offer clues for similarities/dissimilarities in alpha-crystallin function.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 291 293 271 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 540 542 508 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 637 639 601 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 889 891 845 847 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 1024 1026 976 978 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 1293 1295 1241 1243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 1296 1298 1244 1246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 1467 1469 1411 1413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 1651 1653 1595 1597 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 1654 1656 1598 1600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 14 18 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 210 214 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 400 404 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 687 691 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 752 757 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 782 786 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 883 887 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 931 935 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1173 1178 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Generation of mice lacking alphaA (alphaA(-/-)) and alphaB (alphaB(-/-))-crystallin has provided valuable insights into the functional roles of these proteins in the lens. Lenses of alphaA-crystallin deficient mice appeared structurally normal, but developed opacification quickly with age [12]. The presence of dense alphaB-crystallin inclusion bodies in the central lens fiber cells of alphaA(-/-) mice was also observed, suggesting that alphaA-crystallin may be necessary for maintaining the solubility of other crystallins in the lens [12]. It was also found that the absence of alphaA increases cell death during the mitotic phase [13]. On the other hand, lenses in the alphaB(-/-) mice developed normally and were remarkably similar to wild-type mouse lenses, but alphaB(-/-) mice showed skeletal muscle degeneration, spine curvature and a life span one half that of wild type mice [14]. Further, lens cells from alphaB(-/-) mice exhibited a greater tendency for hyperproliferation in culture and genomic instability [15]. Recent studies have addressed the link between crystallin expression and progression of retinal diseases. The level of alphaB-crystallin in rd1 mouse retina increased significantly at 15 days postnatal, which correlated with the stage of maximal rod degeneration [16,17]. Further, an increase in alphaB-crystallin was also found in various retinal degenerations which varied according to the severity, type and onset of the degeneration [18]. Oxidative stress could cause alterations in crystallin content of the retina as has been observed in brain and Alzheimer disease, but the evidence is lacking at the present time [19,20].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 197 199 197 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 509 511 501 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 961 963 951 953 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r23">23</xref>
###xml 278 286 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
In an attempt to better understand the role of drusen in age-related macular degeneration (AMD), Crabb et al. performed a proteomic analysis of drusen preparations from AMD and non-AMD donor eyes [21]. They found that crystallins were detected in all drusen preparations in AMD patients, and were inconsistently observed in non-AMD samples. The results indicated that alphaA and alphaB-crystallins accumulated in Bruch membrane and choroidal connective tissue to a greater degree in AMD than in normal aging [22]. It was suggested that accumulation of crystallins was a stress response manifested during the presence of AMD, and that crystallins may be involved in trapping damaged proteins and preventing their aggregation. Recent drusen analyses on primates have identified an elevated titer for mu-crystallin antibodies in macular degeneration animals, suggesting that autoantibodies against mu-crystallin may be involved in the pathogenesis of the disease [23].
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 242 244 230 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
###xml 245 247 233 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 266 268 254 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r26">26</xref>
###xml 662 664 634 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r27">27</xref>
The compartmental localization of alpha-crystallins in ocular tissues and its significance are topics of great interest. alpha-Crystallins are predominantly cytosolic proteins but nuclear localization of alphaB-crystallins has been reported [24,25]. Deretic et al. [26] had shown that alphaA and alphaB-crystallins are expressed in frog retinal cells in soluble form and associated with post-Golgi membrane of photoreceptors that participate in the transport of newly synthesized rhodopsin. Recently, studies in U373MG cells, which express appreciable amounts of alphaB-crystallin, showed that alphaB-crystallin localized in the perinuclear region of the Golgi [27]. The function of crystallins in the Golgi and the effects of physical and chemical stress on their reorganization in Golgi as well as other compartments has not been fully explored.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 251 256 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In the present study we have investigated the effect of the absence of alpha-crystallins on the susceptibility of RPE cells to apoptotic stimuli. The influence of oxidative stress on the expression and subcellular distribution of alpha-crystallins in human RPE is also examined.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Methods
###end title 17
###begin title 18
Chemicals and materials
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 278 280 270 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 281 283 273 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 93 97 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 198 202 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 286 291 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 327 332 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Hydrogen peroxide was obtained from Sigma Aldrich (St. Louis, MO). The 129S6/SvEvTac control mice were purchased from Taconic Farms (Germantown, NY), while the alphaA and alphaB-crystallin knockout mice in 129S6/SvEvTac background were obtained from the National Eye Institute [12,14]. Human RPE cells were isolated from fetal human donor eyes obtained from Advanced Bioscience Resources, Inc., (Alameda, CA). Eight donors were used for experiments, and donor to donor variation had a negligible effect on the results.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
###xml 92 96 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Isolation and characterization of primary cultured retinal pigment epithelium from knockout mice
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 962 963 946 947 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 964 965 948 949 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 642 648 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 874 878 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
All animal studies were conducted with adherence to the ARVO animal statement. The cornea, lens, vitreous, and retina were removed from eyes soaked in phosphate buffered saline (PBS) containing 5% penicillin/streptomycin (Sigma). The choroid/sclera tissue was then placed into a 2% dispase solution in PBS for 20 min at 37 degreesC. After the incubation, the tissue was rapidly pipetted up and down for 30 s. The dispase solution containing the RPE cells was passed through a 70 mu filter followed by a 40 mu filter after which the cells were spun down and resuspended in Ham's F-12 Media (Cellgro, Herndon, VA) containing at least 25% fetal bovine serum (FBS; Irvine Scientific, Santa Ana, CA). The RPE were then grown on laminin coated plates (Becton Dickinson and Company, Franklin Lakes, NJ), and primary passages of wild type RPE controls, alphaA(-/-), and alphaB(-/-) mice were then incubated in medium containing 1% FBS overnight prior to treatment with H2O2 (100 muM -200 muM) for 24 h. RPE cell cultures were characterized by immunoreactivity for cytokeratin and RPE65, established markers of RPE.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
###xml 31 36 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Cell culture and treatment for human retinal pigment epithelium
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 336 338 335 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
###xml 523 524 522 523 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 525 526 524 525 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 572 573 571 572 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 574 575 573 574 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 778 779 777 778 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 780 781 779 780 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 23 28 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Studies using cultured human RPE were approved by the Institutional Review Board of the University of Southern California and adhere to the Declaration of Helsinki. Primary RPE cells were cultured in DMEM with 2 mM L-glutamine, 100 U/ml penicillin, 100 mug/ml streptomycin (Sigma), and 10% heat-inactivated FBS as previously described [28]. Third to fourth passage cells at >90% confluency were used in all experiments. Prior to treatment, cells were changed to 1% FBS DMEM for 16 h and then treated with varying doses of H2O2 for 24 h. Initial studies on time course of H2O2 treatment established 24 h as an optimal time point for all studies. Experiments in 1% serum were repeated in serum-free media, and subsequent results revealed similar trends. Cell viability following H2O2 treatment protocol in our experiments was verified by Trypan Blue (Cellgro), and Annexin V staining (Annexin V FLUOS staining kit; Roche Applied Science, Indianapolis, IN).
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Apoptosis assays
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 69 74 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 177 181 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1055 1059 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To determine whether knockout of alpha-crystallin gene expression in mouse RPE cells would cause an increase in apoptosis, DNA cleavage of alphaA and alphaB-crystallin knockout mice and wild type control cells were measured by TdT-mediated dUTP nick-end labeling (TUNEL; In Situ Cell Death Detection Kit, Fluorescein; Roche). After treatment, floating and adherent cells were collected and pooled together. The cells were then fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS (pH 7.4) followed by permeabilization with 0.1% Triton X-100 in 0.1% sodium citrate. To label DNA strand breaks, cells were incubated with 50 mul TUNEL reaction mixture containing TdT and fluorescein-dUTP in the binding buffer and incubated for 90 min at 37 degreesC in a humidified chamber. Cells were then washed and analyzed by flow cytometry. Further differentiation between apoptotic versus necrotic mechanisms of cell death was achieved by Annexin V and propidium iodide (PI) staining (Annexin V FLUOS staining kit; Roche) of RPE isolated from wild type and alpha-crystallin knockout mice at the various doses of oxidative stress. Cells were analyzed using flow cytometry. Cells positive for Annexin V staining, but not PI were gated as early apoptotic cells. AnnexinV/PI double positive cells indicated either late stage apoptosis or necrosis.
###end p 25
###begin title 26
Caspase-3 activation
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 51 53 51 53 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TM</sup>
Caspase-3 activation was determined using a CaspACETM FITC-VAD-FMK In Situ Marker (Promega, Madison, WI), a FITC-conjugated caspase inhibitor. Cells were collected and incubated with FITC-VAD-FMK for 60 min, then measured by flow cytometry using the FL-1 setting. Ten thousand events were recorded in each analysis.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
Mitochondrial membrane permeability transition
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 401 405 <span type="species:ncbi:262676">Mito</span>
The loss of mitochondrial membrane potential by apoptosis is marked by increased mitochondrial permeability transition (MPT) thought to occur through the formation of pores in the mitochondria by dimerizing apoptotic proteins. During this process, the electrochemical gradient across the mitochondrial membrane collapses. To assess the mitochondrial membrane potential, a cell permeable cationic dye (Mito Flow; Cell Technology, Mountain View, CA) is added to the cells for 30 min at culture conditions and then analyzed by flow cytometry using FL-3 setting. Ten thousand events were recorded in each analysis. Healthy cells retain the reagent, while apoptotic cells exhibit a lower fluorescence signal due to less accumulation of the dye.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
###xml 43 44 39 40 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 45 46 41 42 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
Expression of alpha-crystallin genes with H2O2 treatment
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 291 292 281 282 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 792 794 777 779 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">15</sub>
###xml 829 832 <span type="species:ncbi:11866?0.5863095238095238|species:ncbi:12321?0.21726190476190477">AMV</span>
###xml 1182 1187 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Initial relative gene expression levels of alphaA and alphaB-crystallin was examined by semi-quantitative reverse transcriptase polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR). cDNA from harvested RNA was transcribed using oligo(dT) and 5 mug of total RNA. 1 mul of cDNA was added to a master mix of MgCl2, 10X buffer, dNTP, primers (see below), and Taq polymerase (Qiagen, Valencia, CA). After 30 cycles, samples were run on an agarose gel with ethidium bromide. Quantitative expression of alpha-crystallin was examined using real-time PCR (LightCycler; Roche). Trizol (GIBCO BRL, Rockville, MD) was used to extract and isolate RNA, while contaminating genomic DNA was removed with a DNAase kit (DNA-free; Ambion, Austin, TX). One mug of total RNA, measured by a spectrophotometer, was added to oligo(dT)15 primer (Promega, Madison, WI) and AMV reverse transcriptase (Promega) for the reverse transcriptase reaction. After a 1:10 dilution of the cDNA, 2 mul of the cDNA was added to 2 mul of green fluorescent dye with Taq DNA polymerase in a 20 mul PCR mix (LightCycler FastStart DNA Master SYBR Green I, Roche). Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) served as an internal control. Human primers were designed using Primer Express software (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA) and were only selected if the pair spanned across exons to minimize non-specific products (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA) and purchased from Qiagen (Valencia, CA): alphaA 5'-GAG ATC CAC GGA AAG CAC AAC-3' (nucleotide position 52-72) and 5'- GGT AGC GGC GGT GGA ACT-3' (107-127); alphaB 5'-TCC CCA GAG GAA CTC AAA GTT AAG-3' (278-301) and 5'-GGC GCT CTT CAT GTT TCC A-3' (327-347); GAPDH 5'-CCA CAT CGC TCA GAC ACC AT-3' (85-104) and 5'-GGC AAC AAT ATC CAC TTT ACC AGA GT-3' (150-169).
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 159 160 159 160 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T</sub>
###xml 253 254 245 246 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T</sub>
Product formation detection was set in the center of the linear portion of PCR amplification, and the cycle at which each reaction reached the set threshold (CT) was determined. Relative change in mRNA expression was calculated to obtain the DeltaDeltaCT values. Four separate sets of RNA were isolated and examined, and each set was tested in duplicate. Levels were normalized relative to GAPDH mRNA and reported as fold change over controls.

###begin title 33
Isolation and fractionation of proteins
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 454 460 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
For crude cell lysates, trypsinized cells were pelleted and washed once with PBS. Fifty microliters of Mammalian Protein Extraction buffer (Pierce, Rockford, IL), along with a 1:100 dilution of proteinase inhibitor mix (Sigma), was added to each pellet. After incubation for 1 h at 4 degreesC, cell debris was pelleted at 14,000xg for 10 min. The remaining supernatant, containing the soluble cell proteins, was measured for protein concentration, using bovine serum albumin as a standard.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
To separate mitochondrial proteins from cytosolic proteins, mitochondria were first isolated using a Mitochondria/Cytosol Fractionation Kit (BioVision Inc., Mountain View, CA). To check the efficiency of homogenization, suspensions were observed under a microscope. A shiny ring around the cell indicated intact cells, and 35 strokes with a dounce homogenizer lysed 90% of the cells. The lysate was first spun for 10 min at 700xg to remove cellular debris and then at 10,000xg for 30 min to pellet the mitochondria. The resulting supernatant was saved as the cytosol portion while the pellet, containing whole mitochondria, was lysed with a mitochondria-specific buffer supplied with the kit. The purity of the fractions was checked by Western blot analysis with a polyclonal antibody against prohibitin, a mitochondrial marker (Abcam, Cambridge, MA) and GW182, a cytoplasmic resident protein (Novus Biologicals, Littleton, CO). Nuclear fractions were isolated using a Nuclear/Cytosol Fractionation Kit (BioVision Inc.). Harvested samples were examined by Western blot analysis.

###begin title 36
Protein analysis
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 450 456 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 606 610 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Protein expression was examined using Western blot techniques. Concentration of harvested proteins was examined by the Bradford-Lowry assay. Equal amounts of protein lysate (15-50 mug) were resolved on 12.5-15% Tris-HCl polyacrylamide gels (Ready Gel; Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA) at 120 V and then transferred to a PVDF blotting membrane (Millipore, Bedford, MA). Each membrane was blocked with 5% blotting grade non-fat dry milk (Biorad), incubated with rabbit anti-alphaA crystallin or anti-alphaB crystallin antibody (Stressgen, San Diego, CA). Specificity of antibodies was confirmed by testing in knockout mice; alphaA showed no immunoreactive band in alphaA(-/-) and alphaB showed no immunoreactive band in alphaB(-/-) retina. After incubation with the secondary antibody (Vector Laboratories, Burlingame, CA), protein bands were detected by chemiluminescence (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech, Cleveland, OH). To verify equal loading of proteins, gels were stained with Coomassie stain (Bio-Safe Coomassie Stain, BioRad) or PVDF membranes were stripped with Tris-buffered saline with 0.1% Tween for 30 min at 60 degreesC and then incubated with GAPDH antibody (Ambion).

Confocal microscopy
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 793 797 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>

RPE cells, grown to >90% confluency on chamber slides (Lab-Tek, Naperville, IL) and exposed to oxidative stress, were examined for alpha-crystallin expression and for mitochondrial localization. To visualize the mitochondria, mitochondria tracker (Molecular Probes, Eugene, OR) was added to samples for 30 min, prior to fixation with 4% paraformaldehyde. ###end p 39
###begin title 40
Transmission electron microscopy
###end title 40
###begin p 41
Trypsinized cells were pelleted and fixed in 2% paraformaldehyde and 0.1% glutaraldehyde in phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) for 1 h at room temperature. The fixed pellets were dehydrated through an ethanol (EtOH) dilution series up to 100% EtOH, infiltrated in an 1:1 EtOH / LR White mixture overnight, embedded in 100% LR White acrylic resin (Ted Pella Inc., Redding CA) in beam capsules, and incubated overnight at 60 degreesC. The blocks were then ultra thin sectioned (75 nm in thickness) and placed on parlodian coated nickel grids. Sections on grids were etched with 0.5% sodium metaperiodate 10 min at room temperature to remove excess resin, and then washed 5 times for 10 min each by drops of 0.025 M Tris buffer (pH 7.4). Sections were placed in a blocking solution of 5% BSA in 0.025 M Tris buffer for 15 min at room temp. After addition of the primary antibody diluted with 5% BSA and 0.025 M Tris for 2 h at 37 degreesC, the grids were then incubated in secondary antibody conjugated to 10 nm gold (Ted Pella Inc.) in 0.025 M Tris buffer at 37 degreesC for 45 min and counterstained with saturated uranyl acetate and lead citrate. Analysis was performed on a Zeiss EM 10 electron microscope (Zeiss).

###begin title 42
Statistical analysis
###end title 42
###begin p 43
Statistical analyses were performed using SAS Proc GLM procedure (SAS version 9.1, SAS Institute, Cary, NC). Different statistical strategies of multiple comparisons were used to test the differences among experimental groups. Specifically, multiple comparisons were performed on TUNEL data with Bonferroni correction and AnnexinV/PI cell death data with Tukey tests. All other data were analyzed using Dunnett's tests. Accepted level of significance for all tests was p<0.05.

The effect of H2O2 on alphaA and alphaB-crystallin distribution and expression in human RPE was further examined by confocal microscopy (Figure 4). RPE cells seeded on chamber slides were treated with H2O2 doses of 25 muM, 150 muM, and 350 muM. Only traces (<1%) of inter-organelle contaminants were found by densitometry of the Western blots. The purified fractions were then used for subsequent experiments. GAPDH, which is less abundant in mitochondria compared to cytoplasm [29], also served to verify fraction purity and protein quantity in mitochondria and cytosolic fractions. The results of Western blot analysis showed that H2O2 at doses of 150 mM and 350 mM H2O2 did not affect the levels of alphaA-crystallin significantly as compared to controls (Figure 5C), while alphaB-crystallin exhibited a dose-dependent decrease in mitochondrial levels in the dose range of 25-350 muM H2O2 (Figure 5D).
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 289 290 276 277 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>

###begin title 63
Discussion
###end title 63
###begin p 64
###xml 164 165 160 161 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 166 167 162 163 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 84 88 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 354 358 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 501 506 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our studies have demonstrated the following: (1) RPE from alpha-crystallin knockout mice show increased apoptotic sensitivity in response to oxidative stress from H2O2 exposure, and the extent of sensitivity to alphaA and alphaB-crystallin knockouts is similar despite marked differences in their cellular levels; (2) RPE from alphaA-crystallin knockout mice show increased caspase-3 activation and mitochondrial membrane permeability transition ; (3) alphaB-crystallin expression and distribution in human RPE changes with oxidative stress while that of alphaA was unchanged; (4) gene expression of alphaB-crystallin followed the same trend as that of protein while alphaA-crystallin mRNA showed an increase in expression; and (5) oxidative stress results in decreased alphaB-crystallin in mitochondria while alphaA-crystallin remains unaltered. These results reveal that both alphaA and alphaB-crystallins are critical for protection from oxidative insult but the mechanisms of action may vary between the two.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 133 135 133 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r30">30</xref>
###xml 355 357 355 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r31">31</xref>
###xml 398 400 398 400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 604 606 596 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 594 602 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Small heat shock protein dysfunction is implicated in many diseases such as cataracts, myopathies, and a number of neuropathologies [30]. In the retina, a major function of crystallins may be to protect retinal neurons from damage by metabolic or environmental stress as seen by evidence of elevated crystallin expression in light damaged photoreceptors [31] and in models of retinal degeneration [18]. Crystallins may be important in the development of, or in response to, AMD since both alphaA and alphaB-crystallins were found to be accumulated in drusen and Bruch membrane tissues from AMD patients [22].
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 201 203 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 553 555 521 523 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r32">32</xref>
###xml 690 692 654 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
###xml 693 695 657 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r34">34</xref>
###xml 1047 1049 991 993 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r32">32</xref>
###xml 1050 1052 994 996 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
###xml 754 758 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The magnitude and mechanisms of cellular protection from alphaA and alphaB-crystallins remain a subject of debate and has for the most part been studied in lens cells. Studies by Andley et al. (2000) [10] suggest that alphaA-crystallin is more potent in preventing apoptosis than alphaB-crystallin. In other studies, alphaA and alphaB-crystallin offered similar protection against programmed cell death; however, the two crystallins can promote cell survival by different mechanisms: alphaA-crystallin through the AKT pathway and alphaB by RAF/MEK/ERK [32]. Additional antiapoptotic mechanisms have been suggested in recent studies demonstrating interactions of alpha-crystallins with p53 [33,34]. It is intriguing that in our study RPE from alphaA(-/-) mice were just as susceptible as alphaB(-/-) RPE to oxidative stress, despite the relatively low abundance of alphaA-crystallin in RPE. Recent articles have suggested that alphaA-crystallin may be rendering similar degrees of protection to alphaB-crystallin by different signaling mechanisms [32,33].
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 195 197 187 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 226 228 218 220 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r27">27</xref>
###xml 491 493 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r35">35</xref>
###xml 690 692 670 672 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r36">36</xref>
###xml 815 820 791 796 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">R120G</sup>
###xml 1010 1012 986 988 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r37">37</xref>
###xml 821 825 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1095 1099 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
alpha-Crystallins are considered to be soluble cytoplasmic proteins but have also been described in association with subcellular organelles. alphaB-Crystallin was shown to reside in the nucleus [25] and the perinuclear Golgi [27], and is known to associate with cellular membranes. In some instances, we found that alpha-crystallin expression appeared to be filamentous in appearance. A closely related protein to alphaB-crystallin, HSPB2, has been shown to associate with the mitochondria [35]. In this study we demonstrated the localization of alpha-crystallins within mitochondria and their regulation by oxidative stress. This finding is supported by a recent report by Maloyan et al. [36] showing a direct association of normal or mutant (R120G) alphaB-crystallin with heart mitochondria in wild type and CryABR120G mice. These authors further found that mitochondrial dysfunction is one of the earliest detectable events in the development of R120G-mediated cardiac myopathy. Furthermore, Kadono et al. [37] found that myocyte mitochondria from alphaB-crystallin and HSPB2 double knockout mice have increased apoptosis due to ischemia reperfusion which was accompanied by induction of MPT.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 92 93 92 93 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 94 95 94 95 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 145 147 145 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r38">38</xref>
###xml 238 240 238 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r39">39</xref>
###xml 401 402 393 394 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 403 404 395 396 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 134 139 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 425 430 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The mitochondrion is the main organelle in which oxygen metabolism occurs, and stress from H2O2 treatment increases ROS generation in human RPE [38]. We found by confocal microscopy that production of ROS was increased in both wild type [39] and alphaA(-/-) RPE and was localized, in part, to mitochondria (data not shown). The depletion of alphaB-crystallin in mitochondria with supra-physiological H2O2 doses (>300 muM) in human RPE may reflect the consumption of alphaB-crystallin for scavenging large amounts of ROS generated.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 132 133 124 125 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 134 135 126 127 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 389 390 373 374 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 391 392 375 376 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 476 477 460 461 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 478 479 462 463 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 596 597 576 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 622 623 601 602 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 624 625 603 604 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 906 907 881 882 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 908 909 883 884 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1024 1025 999 1000 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1026 1027 1001 1002 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1074 1076 1049 1051 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r40">40</xref>
###xml 1077 1079 1052 1054 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r41">41</xref>
###xml 1129 1130 1104 1105 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1131 1132 1106 1107 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1207 1211 1182 1186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bcl2</italic>
###xml 1229 1230 1204 1205 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1231 1232 1206 1207 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1338 1340 1309 1311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r42">42</xref>
###xml 1201 1206 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1254 1260 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
We found that alphaA-crystallin protein expression remained stable, while alphaB-crystallin expression increased upon low doses of H2O2 and returned back to control levels with high doses. alphaA-Crystallin mRNA expression showed approximately a two-fold induction with both low and high doses of oxidative stress. alphaB-Crystallin mRNA expression was also upregulated with low doses of H2O2, but interestingly, decreased dramatically at higher doses. The concentrations of H2O2 in this study are in the range previously used by others to examine alphaB-crystallin gene expression. Alge et al. [9] reported that 200 muM H2O2 resulted in an upregulation of alphaB-crystallin mRNA. The maximal increase occurred at 6 h in their study, while the conditions we have chosen are intended to mimic chronic oxidative stress more frequently seen in vivo. The upregulation of several genes with sublethal doses of H2O2 and a down-regulation with higher doses has been reported in a number of recent studies supporting the idea that H2O2 may act as a signaling molecule at low doses [40,41]. The exact signaling mechanism associated with H2O2 remains to be elucidated. It is worth noting that it was found that human bcl2 gene attenuates H2O2-induced apoptosis in rabbit lens epithelial cells through down-regulation of the alphaB-crystallin gene [42].
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 188 189 180 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 329 330 313 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 686 687 650 651 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 688 689 652 653 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 934 936 882 884 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 937 939 885 887 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r43">43</xref>
###xml 940 942 888 890 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r45">45</xref>
###xml 214 219 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 580 584 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Studies on crystallins have focused predominantly in the lens, where both alphaA and alphaB-crystallin are expressed in equal amounts and comprise a huge portion of total soluble protein [2]. On the other hand, in mouse retina, alphaB-crystallin is 15 to 30 fold lower than alphaA-crystallin, but distributed in the same layers [8]. While the information on alpha-crystallin expression in the RPE is scarce, data from our current study indicate a much higher expression of alphaB-crystallin as compared to alphaA-crystallin. RPE isolated from alphaA or alphaB-crystallin knockout mice showed an increased susceptibility to apoptosis via oxidative stress. The extent of cell death from H2O2 was similar in both alphaA(-/-) and alphaB(-/-) RPE, but it is interesting that the utilization of antiapoptotic mechanistic pathways could vary among the three prominent members of the sHSP family, namely Hsp27, alphaA and alphaB-crystallins [11,43-45].
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 298 299 294 295 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 300 301 296 297 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 384 386 380 382 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r46">46</xref>
###xml 622 623 614 615 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 624 625 616 617 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 930 931 914 915 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 194 198 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The BCL-2 family contributes to the regulation of the swelling of the mitochondria and the opening of the permeability transition pore. Our studies have revealed that RPE cells from alphaA(-/-) mice show an increased mitochondrial membrane permeability transition compared to wildtype cells under H2O2 insult. While BCL-2 family members are thought to be the sentinels of cell death [46], crystallins may be acting as chaperones or regulators, possibly by interaction at the BH3 domain of these proteins. alpha-Crystallins may also be directly and/or indirectly affecting downstream death effectors at the caspase level. H2O2-treated RPE cells from alphaA(-/-) show increased caspase-3 activation compared to wildtype RPE. Studies done by others show that alphaB-crystallin negatively regulates cyt c and caspase-8-independent activation of caspase 3 by inhibiting its autoproteolytic maturation, which may provide further clues [7]. Further work will be needed to assess the contribution of mitochondrial (and extramitochondrial) pathways of apoptosis in RPE cells under oxidative stress.
