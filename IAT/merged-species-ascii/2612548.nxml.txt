###begin article-title 0
Protective role of membrane tumour necrosis factor in the host's resistance to mycobacterial infection

###begin p 3
###xml 199 218 191 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium bovis</italic>
###xml 374 379 362 367 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 434 437 422 425 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 473 478 461 466 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 505 513 493 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. bovis</italic>
###xml 771 797 759 785 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 883 888 871 876 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 199 218 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">Mycobacterium bovis</span>
###xml 273 277 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 396 400 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 479 483 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 505 517 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
###xml 771 797 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 896 911 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
Tumour necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) plays a critical role in the recruitment and activation of mononuclear cells in mycobacterial infection. The role of membrane TNF, in host resistance against Mycobacterium bovis bacille Calmette-Guerin (BCG), was tested in knock-in mice in which the endogenous TNF was replaced by a non-cleavable and regulated allele (Delta1-12, TNFtm/tm). While 100% of mice with complete TNF deficiency (TNF-/-) succumbed to infection, 50% of TNFtm/tm mice were able to control M. bovis BCG infection and survived the experimental period. Membrane expressed TNF allowed a substantial recruitment of activated T cells and macrophages with granuloma formation and expression of bactericidal inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS). Using virulent Mycobacterium tuberculosis infection we confirm that membrane TNF conferred partial protection. Infection in TNFtm/tm double transgenic mice with TNF-R1 or TNF-R2 suggest protection is mediated through TNF-R2 signalling. Therefore, the data suggest that membrane-expressed TNF plays a critical role in host defence to mycobacterial infection and may partially substitute for soluble TNF.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Introduction
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 23 49 23 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 254 255 246 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1">1</xref>
###xml 256 257 248 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2">2</xref>
###xml 432 447 416 431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 477 478 461 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">3</xref>
###xml 479 480 463 464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6">6</xref>
###xml 23 49 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 69 75 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 348 352 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 432 447 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Protective immunity to Mycobacterium tuberculosis infection, both in humans and experimental animals, is regulated by T cells, macrophages and cytokines, which include interferon-gamma (IFN-gamma), interleukin-12 (IL-12) and tumour necrosis factor (TNF).1,2 IFN-gamma derived from T and natural killer (NK) cells has been shown to be essential, as mice with a disruption of the IFN-gamma system are unable to restrict the growth of M. tuberculosis and succumb to the infection.3-6
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 114 115 114 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">3</xref>
###xml 116 117 116 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8">8</xref>
###xml 286 287 275 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9">9</xref>
###xml 462 464 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10">10</xref>
###xml 547 548 522 523 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b5">5</xref>
###xml 549 550 524 525 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8">8</xref>
###xml 589 591 564 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b11">11</xref>
###xml 673 675 645 647 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b12">12</xref>
###xml 676 678 648 650 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b13">13</xref>
###xml 109 113 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
A critical role for TNF in mycobacterial defence is inferred from neutralization and deletion experiments in mice.3-8 In addition, the TNF-related cytokine lymphotoxin-alpha, (LTalpha, previously known as TNF-beta), has also been shown to be required to control mycobacterial infection.9 Secreted TNF and LTalpha signal both through the p55 and p75 TNF receptors, TNFR1 and TNFR2 respectively, while the cell bound LTalphabeta heterotrimer recognize the LTbetaR.10 TNFR1 signalling appears to be critical for the control of mycobacterial infection5,8 while a role of TNFR2 is not excluded.11 LTbetaR signalling was similarly found to protect against mycobacterial infection12,13
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 138 140 138 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14">14</xref>
###xml 141 143 141 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b15">15</xref>
###xml 367 369 363 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b16">16</xref>
TNF is produced by macrophages, but is expressed by a variety of cells and is a major regulator of inflammation and leucocyte trafficking.14,15 Although a key role of TNF in controlling intracellular bacterial infections is uncontested, the function of membrane TNF, which is subsequently cleaved by the metalloproteinase-disintegrin TACE (TNF-alpha converting enzyme16) into the secreted trimeric TNF is, in mycobacterial host resistance, less clear.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 228 230 228 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b17">17</xref>
###xml 231 233 231 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19">19</xref>
###xml 292 299 292 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 338 353 338 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 363 365 363 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b20">20</xref>
###xml 366 368 366 368 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b22">22</xref>
###xml 719 721 719 721 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b23">23</xref>
###xml 834 836 830 832 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b24">24</xref>
###xml 857 859 849 851 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b25">25</xref>
###xml 1086 1101 1074 1089 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 1101 1103 1089 1091 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b26">26</xref>
###xml 1104 1106 1092 1094 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b27">27</xref>
###xml 1111 1133 1099 1121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Listeria monocytogenes</italic>
###xml 1144 1146 1132 1134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b28">28</xref>
###xml 1192 1208 1176 1192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">L. monocytogenes</italic>
###xml 1274 1276 1258 1260 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b25">25</xref>
###xml 338 353 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 601 616 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 1111 1133 <span type="species:ncbi:1639">Listeria monocytogenes</span>
###xml 1192 1208 <span type="species:ncbi:1639">L. monocytogenes</span>
Several biological functions of membrane TNF have been described, such as cytotoxicity, polyclonal activation of B cells, and induction of IL-10 by monocytes and intracellular adhesion molecule-1 expression on endothelial cells.17-19 The transgenic expression of membrane TNF demonstrated an in vivo role including the control of BCG and M. tuberculosis infection20-22 but this approach is imperfect as the transgenic expression of high levels membrane TNF is artificial and may cause altered immune responses and non-physiological pathology. As an example the spontaneous development of arthritis in transgenic mice expressing mutant membrane TNF was directly attributed to the deregulation of membrane TNF production.23 Two novel mutants have been generated where the endogenous TNF was either replaced by a Delta1-9,K11E TNF allele24 or Delta1-12 allele,25 which represent a major advance allowing interesting insights in the role of membrane TNF in lymphoid structure development and inflammation. We and others demonstrated that the Delta1-9,K11E mutant has enhanced resistance to M. tuberculosis26,27 and Listeria monocytogenes infection.28Delta1-12 TNF mutant is resistant to low-dose L. monocytogenes infection, but no data are available on mycobacterial infections.25
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 51 53 47 49 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b25">25</xref>
###xml 155 174 151 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium bovis</italic>
###xml 238 243 234 239 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 363 371 359 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. bovis</italic>
###xml 423 438 419 434 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 550 552 542 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b24">24</xref>
###xml 102 106 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 155 178 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">Mycobacterium bovis BCG</span>
###xml 208 212 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 244 248 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 363 375 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
###xml 423 438 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 537 542 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Here, we demonstrated that the Delta1-12 TNF mutant25 is partially resistant to infection with 50% of mice that succumb to the non-virulent vaccine strain Mycobacterium bovis BCG, while 100% of TNF-deficient mice succumb to infection. TNFtm/tm mice were able to recruit and activate macrophages and T cells, and generate mycobactericidal granuloma in response to M. bovis BCG infection. However, resistance to the virulent M. tuberculosis strain was less pronounced, suggesting substantial differences between this and the Delta1-9,K11E mouse mutant.24
###end p 9

###xml 273 277 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>

Adult, 8- to 10-week-old female mice of the wild type (WT) strain C57BL/6, homozygous Delta1-12 TNF mutant25 (TNFtm/tm), TNFR1-/-, TNFR2-/- and TNF-/-29 strains, all on a C57BL/6 genetic background were bred and maintained under specific pathogen-free conditions. TNFtm/tm mice were crossed on TNFR1-/- mice30 or TNFR2-/- mice31 in order to study the signalling of membrane TNF. The genotypes of the mouse populations were confirmed by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) analysis of tail biopsies. PCR primers: Delta1-12 TNF mutant - 5'-TAATGGGCAGGGCAAGGTGG-3' and 5'-TTCACTTCCGGTTCCTGCACCCT-3'; TNFR1 - 5'-CTCTCTTGTGATCAGCACTG -3', 5'-TCCCGCTTCAGTGACAACGTC-3' and 5'-AGAAATCTCAAGACAATTCTCTGC-3'; TNFR2 - 5'-CCTCTCATGCTGTCCCGGAAT-3', 5'-AGCTCCAGGCACAAGGGCGGG-3', 5'-CGGTTCTTTTTGTCAAGAC-3' and 5'-ATCCTCGCCGTCGGGCATGC-3'. All protocols employed in this study were approved by the University of Cape Town Research Ethics Committee.

###begin title 16
Mycobacterial infection
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 7 12 7 12 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 101 102 101 102 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 31 35 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 131 135 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 177 181 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 257 261 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
WT, TNFtm/tm and TNF-deficient mice were injected intravenously (i.v.) at an infective dose of 2 x 106 BCG. Groups consisted of 10 mice and the experiments were repeated twice. Mice were killed at 2, 4 and 8 weeks after infection. For the aerosol infection mice were infected with 30 viable CFU of H37Rv via the aerosol route using an inhalation exposure system (Glas-Col, Terre Haute, IN).

Lipopolysaccharide (LPS)/d-galactosamine (d-Gal)-induced septic shock

###begin title 20
Histology and immunohistochemistry
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 405 406 398 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b7">7</xref>
###xml 436 442 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 705 708 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 714 720 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
Lungs were removed and weighed. The organs were fixed in 4% buffered formalin, or frozen on dry ice and maintained at -80degrees. Two to 5 mum-thick sections of the lungs were cut and stained with either haematoxylin and eosin or Ziehl-Neelsen for acid-fast bacilli. Formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded sections were de-paraffinized and re-hydrated through decreasing concentrations of alcohol as described.7 Sections were stained with a rabbit antimouse antibody specific for inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS; obtained from J. Pfeilschifter, University of Frankfurt, Germany). Sections were then washed in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and incubated for 30 min at room temperature with biotinylated rat anti-rabbit serum followed by Vectastain ABC system (Vector Laboratories Inc., Burlingame, CA) and 3,3'-diaminobenzidine (DAB) substrate. Finally, sections were mounted in Immunomount (Shandon, Pittsburgh, PA).
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Colony-forming units (CFU)
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 58 62 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Bacterial loads in the lung, liver and spleen of infected mice were evaluated at 2, 4 and 8 weeks post-infection. Organs were weighed and defined aliquots were homogenized in saline containing 0.04% Tween-80. Tenfold serial dilutions of organ homogenates were plated in duplicates onto Middlebrook 7H10 agar plates containing 10% OADC (Difco). Plates were incubated at 37degrees for 19-21 days and colonies counted. Data are expressed as mean CFU per organ.

###begin title 24
Bronchoalveolar lavage
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 213 214 212 213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 213 214 212 213 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>g</italic></bold>
###xml 561 562 553 554 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 561 562 553 554 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>g</italic></bold>
Under anaesthesia a 20-gauge catheter (Introcan, B. Braun, Melsungen AG, Germany) was inserted into the exposed trachea. The lungs were lavaged with two volumes of 300 mul PBS. Lavage fluid was centrifuged at 405 g for 5 min, the supernatant was removed, aliquoted and stored at -80degrees for cytokine and chemokine analysis. Lungs were subsequently lavaged with four volumes of 800 mul PBS. To determine whether membrane-bound TNF contributes to protective immunity, a comparative study was performed to determine the susceptibility of TNFtm/tm, TNF-/- and WT mice to mycobacterial infection. ###begin title 52
Membrane TNF allows the expression of pulmonary granuloma
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 267 268 267 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">3</xref>
###xml 352 360 352 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. bovis</italic>
###xml 352 364 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
The establishment of granulomas is the manifestation of a vigorous cell-mediated immune response, which is crucial for inhibiting mycobacterial growth. A critical role of TNF for initiating and maintaining the structural integrity of granulomas has been demonstrated.3 We therefore asked whether membrane TNF is sufficient for granuloma formation upon M. bovis BCG infection.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 206 213 206 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig04">Fig. 4a</xref>
###xml 550 558 550 558 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. bovis</italic>
###xml 33 38 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 305 309 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 530 534 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 550 562 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
Pulmonary pathology in all three mouse strains at 2 weeks post-infection was characterized by areas that had normal alveolar and focal thickened septa, but the alveolar spaces were free from infiltrations (Fig. 4a) and there was no difference between the groups. At 4 weeks post-infection the lungs of WT mice displayed well-defined granulomatous lesions that were characterized by foamy epithelioid-like macrophages with surrounding and interspersed lymphocytes and prominent perivascular lymphocytic infiltration. At 8 weeks WT mice, which control M. bovis BCG infection, showed evidence of resolving granulomatous lesions.
###end p 54

As expected, TNF-/- mice showed further thickening of septae containing inflammatory cells at 4 weeks, but did not develop granulomatous lesions and only sparse perivascular lymphocytic infiltration. At 8 weeks, a prominent perivascular and peribronchial lymphocyte infiltration was found in TNF-/- mice. There was, however, no structural organization of the infiltrating lymphocytes and macrophages into a well-structured granuloma, and an increased recruitment of neutrophils was apparent.
###end p 56

###xml 16 21 16 21 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>

In contrast, TNFtm/tm mice displayed typical, but small granulomas at 4 weeks post-infection, which were less well demarcated than those found in WT mice. Epitheloid macrophages and lymphocytes were distinguishable and perivascular lymphocyte recruitment was evident. These early granulomas developed into enlarged pulmonary lesions at 8 weeks post-infection.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 76 81 76 81 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>

###xml 203 207 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In addition, Ziehl-Neelsen staining revealed that bacilli in both WT and TNFtm/tm mice are less abundant and predominantly confined to granulomas at 4 weeks and 8 weeks post-infection, whereas in TNF-/- mice with the absence of properly defined granulomas the bacilli were dispersed in the tissue and predominantly extracellular (Fig. 4b). Therefore, in contrast to complete TNF deficiency the sole presence of membrane-bound TNF allowed a substantial granulomatous response with bactericidal properties.

As the T-cell recruitment was reduced in BAL, we asked whether the inflammatory response in the lungs of BCG-infected mice differed in the total or partial absence of TNF. ###end p 79
###begin p 80
###xml 129 144 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
Therefore, the sole expression of membrane TNF provided partial protection against H37Rv infection and the data using the double transgenic mice indicate that membrane TNF signalling through TNF-R2 confers the protective effect of membrane-bound TNF.
###end p 80
###begin title 81
Discussion
###end title 81
###begin p 82
###xml 51 53 47 49 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b25">25</xref>
###xml 159 167 155 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. bovis</italic>
###xml 278 283 274 279 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 453 468 449 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 580 582 572 574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b24">24</xref>
###xml 640 642 628 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b24">24</xref>
###xml 795 810 779 794 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 820 822 804 806 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b26">26</xref>
###xml 823 825 807 809 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b27">27</xref>
###xml 830 838 814 822 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Listeria</italic>
###xml 849 851 833 835 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b28">28</xref>
###xml 159 171 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
###xml 184 188 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 248 252 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 284 288 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 453 468 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 567 572 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 795 810 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Here we demonstrated that the Delta1-12 TNF mutant,25 which expresses only membrane TNF is partially resistant to infection to the non-virulent vaccine strain M. bovis BCG with 50% of mice surviving the experimental period, while all TNF-deficient mice succumb to infection. TNFtm/tm mice were able to recruit and activate macrophages and T cells and generate mycobactericidal granuloma in response to BCG infection. However, resistance to the virulent M. tuberculosis strain was less pronounced, suggesting substantial differences between this and the Delta1-9,K11E mouse mutant.24 This TNF mutant differs from the Delta1-9,K11E TNF allele24 in several aspects - the lymphoid structure especially is abnormal. We and others demonstrated that the Delta1-9,K11E mutant has enhanced resistance to M. tuberculosis infection26,27 and Listeria infection.28
###end p 82
###begin p 83
###xml 124 125 124 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b4">4</xref>
###xml 126 127 126 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b7">7</xref>
###xml 128 130 128 130 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b35">35</xref>
###xml 245 246 245 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b5">5</xref>
###xml 302 304 302 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b11">11</xref>
###xml 413 416 413 416 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 434 437 434 437 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 504 506 504 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b37">37</xref>
###xml 438 442 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
A critical role of TNF for the effective control and resolution of mycobacterial infection has been demonstrated previously.4,7,35 Further, TNFR1-mediated signalling is required for generating protective immunity against mycobacterial infection,5 which is probably more important than TNFR2 signalling.11 TNF provided by recombinant BCG expressing TNF may reconstitute granuloma formation and host response in TNF-/-, but not in TNFR1-/- mice, demonstrating the critical role of TNF and TNFR1 signalling.37
###end p 83
###begin p 84
###xml 47 52 47 52 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 367 375 367 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 375 377 375 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19">19</xref>
###xml 378 380 378 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b38">38</xref>
###xml 385 392 385 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 392 394 392 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b21">21</xref>
###xml 395 397 395 397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b38">38</xref>
###xml 398 400 398 400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b39">39</xref>
###xml 731 733 731 733 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b40">40</xref>
###xml 53 57 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 407 422 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
The partial protection that is generated in TNFtm/tm mice could indicate local cell-to-cell TNF signalling by membrane expressed TNF on T cells or macrophages at the site of infection leading to a partial activation of the immune cells. Several biological functions of membrane TNF have been reported previously and a preferential TNFR2 signalling has been suggested in vitro19,38 and in vivo21,38,39 using transgenic mice expressing membrane TNF. The present data therefore suggest a functional role of TNFR2 in host protection. Furthermore, membrane TNF has been shown to be involved in reverse (outside-to-inside) signalling. Upon ligation of its receptor membrane-bound TNF-expressing cells are activated to express E-selectin.40 Thus, membrane-bound TNF, at least in T cells, might function as a bipolar positive regulator of inflammation either transmitting signals as a ligand to target cells or receiving signals through membrane TNF itself into T cells.
###end p 84
###begin p 85
###xml 277 279 277 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b21">21</xref>
###xml 280 282 280 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b41">41</xref>
###xml 283 285 283 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b43">43</xref>
###xml 372 377 372 377 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 454 457 454 457 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 503 504 503 504 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 508 509 508 509 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 517 518 517 518 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 522 523 522 523 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 639 641 639 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14">14</xref>
###xml 718 723 718 723 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 885 886 885 886 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1026 1027 1026 1027 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1">1</xref>
###xml 1049 1050 1049 1050 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1109 1111 1109 1111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b44">44</xref>
###xml 378 382 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 458 462 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 724 728 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1021 1025 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Although the exact mechanism of how protection is acquired through membrane TNF is unclear, membrane TNF on activated T cells might be sufficient for partial activation of macrophages; this results in the upregulation of iNOS expression, which is crucial for bacterial killing.21,41-43 Membrane TNF expressed on T cells might replace secreted TNF to form granulomas in TNFtm/tm mice. Macrophage and lymphocyte recruitment was significantly reduced in TNF-/- mice, with a significant reduction of the CD3+ CD4+ and CD3+ CD8+ T-cell subsets in the absence of TNF, which might be related to reduced chemokine production in the absence of TNF.14 As the recruitment of the different cell types was partially restored in TNFtm/tm mice, membrane TNF on T cells or other cells might provide a partial signal for chemokine induction and hence increased cell trafficking. The contribution of CD4+ T-cells in cell-mediated immunity against mycobacteria has been investigated by the use of neutralizing antibodies and gene-deficient mice.1 In the absence of CD4+ typical granuloma formation in T cells was largely absent.44
###end p 85
###begin p 86
###xml 410 412 410 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b24">24</xref>
###xml 398 403 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
However, in view of the only partial activation and protection, which is likely caused by direct cell-cell contact, secreted TNF and hence distal signalling, appears to be required for a full protective host response. This finding concurs with the recent data demonstrating reduced severity of autoimmune encephalomyelitis, but little effect on inflammation in the spinal cord in a similar genetic mouse model.24
###end p 86
###begin p 87
###xml 43 48 43 48 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 61 64 61 64 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 106 111 106 111 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 119 122 119 122 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 160 165 160 165 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 173 176 173 176 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 191 196 191 196 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm/tm</sup>
###xml 65 80 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 123 127 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 177 181 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 197 201 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 345 350 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Lastly, the infection studies in double TNFtm/tmx TNFR1 or R2-/- transgenic mice rescued protection in TNFtm/tm x TNFR2-/- mice and comparable protection in TNFtm/tm x TNFR1-/- mice as in TNFtm/tm mice suggest that membrane signalling though TNFR2 likely provides host protection. Our data in this infection model using the Delta1-12 TNF mutant mouse shows a reduced protective immune response during mycobacterial infection when compared to previous findings in which a membrane TNF transgenic mutant or the Delta1-9,K11E mutant were used. Our findings indicate that membrane TNF, in the Delta1-12 TNF mutant may preferentially induce protection through TNFR2. Receptor signalling in membrane TNF transgenic mutant or the Delta1-9,K11E mutant may be mediated through both TNFR1 and TNFR2 different, accounting for the differences observed in the degree of protection afforded by membrane TNF in the different mutant strains.
###end p 87
###begin p 88
The data suggest that sparing membrane expressed TNF in therapeutic interventions neutralizing TNF such as in rheumatoid arthritis might reduce infectious complications. In summary, we show that membrane TNF participates in cell-mediated immunity against mycobacterial infection. The protective effect of membrane-bound TNF is likely mediated through TNFR2 signalling.
