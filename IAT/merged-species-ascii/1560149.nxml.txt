The DNA polymerase activity of Pol epsilon holoenzyme is required for rapid and efficient chromosomal DNA replication in Xenopus egg extracts

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
DNA polymerase epsilon (Pol epsilon) is involved in DNA replication, repair, and cell-cycle checkpoint control in eukaryotic cells. Although the roles of replicative Pol alpha and Pol delta in chromosomal DNA replication are relatively well understood and well documented, the precise role of Pol epsilon in chromosomal DNA replication is not well understood.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 18 26 18 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 270 278 258 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
This study uses a Xenopus egg extract DNA replication system to further elucidate the replicative role(s) played by Pol epsilon. Previous studies show that the initiation timing and elongation of chromosomal DNA replication are markedly impaired in Pol epsilon-depleted Xenopus egg extracts, with reduced accumulation of replicative intermediates and products. This study shows that normal replication is restored by addition of Pol epsilon holoenzyme to Pol epsilon-depleted extracts, but not by addition of polymerase-deficient forms of Pol epsilon, including polymerase point or deletion mutants or incomplete enzyme complexes. Evidence is also provided that Pol epsilon holoenzyme interacts directly with GINS, Cdc45p and Cut5p, each of which plays an important role in initiation of chromosomal DNA replication in eukaryotic cells.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 147 155 141 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 345 351 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeasts</span>
These results indicate that the DNA polymerase activity of Pol epsilon holoenzyme plays an essential role in normal chromosomal DNA replication in Xenopus egg extracts. These are the first biochemical data to show the DNA polymerase activity of Pol epsilon holoenzyme is essential for chromosomal DNA replication in higher eukaryotes, unlike in yeasts.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 217 218 189 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 219 220 191 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 324 325 282 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 398 399 356 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 209 215 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeasts</span>
###xml 308 313 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 317 322 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Three structurally and functionally distinct DNA polymerases, known as DNA polymerases alpha, delta, and epsilon (Pol alpha,-delta, and -epsilon, respectively), are required for chromosomal DNA replication in yeasts [1-3]. The complex structure of each Pol alpha, -delta, and -epsilon is well conserved from yeast to human [4], thus their function inside the cell is also believed to be conserved [4].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 322 323 310 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 324 325 312 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 366 374 354 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 445 446 429 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 597 598 577 578 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 590 595 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
DNA primase initiates DNA replication by synthesizing a short oligo-ribonucleotide primer which is immediately elongated by Pol alpha to form short RNA-DNA fragments on both leading and lagging strand of DNA. Pol delta elongates the short RNA-DNA fragment initiated by Pol alpha-primase to make a mature Okazaki fragment [2,5]. To carry out processive DNA synthesis in vitro, Pol delta requires PCNA and its loader, Replication Factor-C (RF-C) [5]. In cooperation with Fen1p (Rad27p), Dna2p and RPA, Pol delta also plays a crucial role in processing RNA-linked Okazaki-fragments in budding yeast [5].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 41 49 35 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 146 147 140 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 148 149 142 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 150 151 144 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 238 239 226 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 240 241 228 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 415 420 397 402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2 </italic>
###xml 528 529 504 505 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 604 605 580 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 611 616 587 592 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2 </italic>
###xml 670 671 646 647 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 672 673 648 649 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 728 733 704 709 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POL2 </italic>
###xml 737 742 713 718 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POL3 </italic>
###xml 809 811 781 783 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 812 814 784 786 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1158 1159 1108 1109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 1160 1162 1110 1112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 1168 1173 1118 1123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol3 </italic>
###xml 1230 1231 1180 1181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1480 1482 1424 1426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 1608 1610 1552 1554 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 1860 1865 1804 1809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc2 </italic>
###xml 1907 1914 1847 1854 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol3-01</italic>
###xml 2011 2013 1945 1947 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 2014 2016 1948 1950 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 222 236 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeasts</span>
###xml 369 374 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
###xml 1312 1317 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Although the precise role of Pol epsilon in vivo is still unclear, it has been implicated in DNA replication, repair, recombination, and mitosis [1,2,4]. Pol epsilon has at least one essential function in both budding and fission yeasts [3,6], and several lines of evidence suggest that Pol epsilon plays an essential catalytic role during chromosomal DNA replication. Yeast cells harboring a temperature-sensitive pol2 allele are temperature sensitive for growth and express a thermolabile Pol epsilon DNA polymerase activity [7]. Furthermore, Pol2p is associated with replication forks during S phase [8] and pol2 mutants fail to complete chromosomal DNA replication [7,9]. Furthermore, 3'-5' exonuclease-deficient mutants of POL2 and POL3 (Pol delta) accumulate strand-specific lesions in chromosomal DNA [10-13]. These observations support models for chromosomal DNA replication in which Pol epsilon and Pol delta play leading strand- and lagging strand-specific roles during chromosomal DNA replication, respectively. Pol epsilon has been proposed as the leading strand DNA polymerase because Pol epsilon is a highly processive polymerase without PCNA [1,14] and pol3 mutants have defects in maturation of Okazaki fragments [5]. Nevertheless, it has been reported that the amino-terminal portion of budding yeast Pol epsilon (Pol2p), which includes motifs required for DNA polymerase and exonuclease activities, is dispensable for DNA replication, DNA repair, and viability [15]. However, this conclusion is controversial, because the polymerase active domain mutant (polymerase-dead-mutant) is lethal [16] and our studies showed that the deletion mutant confers temperature-sensitivity for growth, a defect in DNA elongation, premature senescence, and short telomeres. Furthermore, this pol2p deletion is lethal in combination with temperature-sensitive cdc2 and with exonuclease-deficient Pol delta (pol3-01). These results suggest that Pol epsilon plays a crucial role in maintaining genomic integrity [17,18].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 77 90 71 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 192 194 180 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 195 197 183 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 355 363 337 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 377 379 359 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 380 382 362 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 533 541 509 517 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 912 914 878 880 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 1037 1045 997 1005 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 77 90 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
In higher eukaryotes, the function of Pol epsilon is much less clear than in S. cerevisiae, although some reports showed that Pol epsilon is required for cellular chromosomal DNA replication [19-21]. In order to understand how Pol epsilon involves in higher eukaryotic chromosomal DNA replication, we have been characterizing DNA replication catalyzed by Xenopus egg extracts [22,23]. We have shown previously that both the initiation and elongation steps of chromosomal DNA replication are markedly impaired in Pol epsilon-depleted Xenopus egg extracts, resulting in significant reduction of the overall DNA synthesis as well as accumulation of small replication intermediates. Moreover, despite the decreased DNA synthesis, excess amounts of Pol alpha are loaded onto the chromatin template in Pol epsilon-depleted extracts, indicative of the failure of proper assembly of DNA synthesis machinery at the fork [22]. Although these experiments clearly demonstrate that Pol epsilon is required for a normal chromosomal DNA replication in Xenopus egg extracts, they did not give any answer whether DNA polymerase activity of Pol epsilon directly involves in chromosomal DNA replication process.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 66 74 66 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 211 219 199 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 600 608 558 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 672 680 624 632 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
In the present report we show that the reconstituted and purified Xenopus Pol epsilon holoenzyme, which is proficient in DNA polymerase activity, fully complements DNA replication defect of Pol epsilon-depleted Xenopus egg extracts, but neither DNA polymerase domain deleted Pol epsilon holoenzyme, DNA polymerase-dead-Pol epsilon holoenzyme, the catalytic subunit of Pol epsilon, nor other subunits of Pol epsilon holoenzyme complements the defect at all. These results clearly prove that the DNA polymerase activity of Pol epsilon holoenzyme directly participates in chromosomal DNA replication in Xenopus egg extracts and that the products made by Pol epsilon-depleted Xenopus egg extracts are not fully replicated DNA.
###end p 13

A previous study reported cloning of a partial cDNA for the catalytic subunit (p260) of Xenopus Pol epsilon (xPol epsilon) [24], and in this report, the corresponding full-length cDNA was cloned by 5' Rapid amplification of cDNA ends (5' Race) (see Additional file 1). The fact that the full length cDNA encodes a 2285 amino acid residue protein whose predicted amino acid sequence is 81% identical to human p260 [25] and 62% identical to S. cerevisiae Pol2p [6] within the polymerase domain (see Additional file 2 and Additional file 3) confirms the identity of the cloned fulllength cDNA. Full length cDNA encoding p17 and p12 of xPol epsilon were also cloned using 3' RACE, Xenopus ovary mRNA and partial cDNA clones 4783571(5') (GenBank ACC# ) and PBX0037B02(5') (GenBank ACC# ), respectively. The predicted amino acid sequences of p12 and p17 showed very high similarity to p12 and p17 of human Pol epsilon, respectively [26](see Additional file 4), confirming the identity of the cloned cDNAs. cDNA encoding p60 of xPol epsilon holoenzyme was cloned previously [22]; thus, with this report, full length cDNA clones have been isolated for all four subunits of xPol epsilon holoenzyme.

###begin title 17
The C-terminal Zinc finger domain of p260 is required for binding p60 but not p12-p17
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 268 270 256 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 460 462 436 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>

###xml 1210 1212 1166 1168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1C</xref>
###xml 1313 1315 1269 1271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1C</xref>
###xml 1620 1623 1560 1563 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
###xml 1754 1755 1694 1695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>

Deletion mutants of xPol epsilon p260 were constructed and used to identify protein regions involved in the interfaces between p260 and the other subunits of Pol epsilon holoenzyme. For these experiments, p260 deletion mutants were fused to the FLAG epitope tag (Fig. 1A) and the following mutants were generated: (a) FLAG-p260full, (b) FLAG-p260DeltaC2157, (c) FLAG-p260DeltaC1054, (d) FLAG-p260D860N, and (e) FLAG-p260DeltaCat (equivalent to yeast Pol2-16p [15,16,27]). The p260 mutants were co-expressed with xPol epsilon p60, p12 and p17 using a baculovirus insect cell expression system and immunoprecipitated with anti-Flag antibodies. Figure 1B shows that anti-Flag antibodies co-immunoprecipitated p260, p60, p12, and p17 from crude extracts of insect cells expressing wild type p260, p60, p12, and p17. In contrast, in cells expressing p260DeltaC2157, p12 and p17 co-immunoprecipitated with the catalytic subunit but p60 did not, and in cells expressing p260DeltaC1054, no other subunits of Pol epsilon holoenzyme co-immunoprecipitated with the p260 mutant protein. These results suggest that p60, but not p12 or p17, interacts with p260 residues 2157 to 2285, which includes Zinc-finger motifs (Fig. 1C), and that p12 and p17 interact with a motif or region between residues 1054 and 2157 of p260 (Fig. 1C). Both p260D860N (a DNA polymerase-dead-point mutant) and p260DeltaCat did not interfere with formation of the xPol epsilon holoenzyme, because all four Pol epsilon subunits co-immunoprecipitated from cells expressing these p260 mutants. In cells expressing histidine-tagged p60 (H-p60), p12, and p17, Ni2+ -chelating beads only precipitated H-p60, indicating that H-p60 does not interact directly with p12 or p17, or both subunits (Fig. 3), although it was known that both subunits interact each other and form a 1:1 complex in insect cells (data not shown) as S. cerevisiae Dpb3p-Dpb4p complex [28].

###begin title 21
###xml 84 92 78 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
DNA polymerase activity of xPol epsilon holoenzyme is required for DNA synthesis in Xenopus egg extracts
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 363 365 351 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 366 368 354 356 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>

###xml 469 470 451 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 584 601 566 583 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro Xenopus </italic>

###xml 1059 1060 991 992 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>

Previous studies suggested that the amino-terminal portion of yeast Pol epsilon is dispensable for DNA replication, DNA repair, and viability. Because this portion of the enzyme includes all known DNA polymerase and exonuclease motifs, these results suggest that the DNA polymerase activity of Pol epsilon is dispensable for chromosomal DNA replication in yeast [15,16,27]. To investigate this possibility further, the xPol epsilon mutant complexes described in Figure 2 as well as partial holoenzyme complexes lacking specific subunits were purified from insect cells and used in an in vitro Xenopus chromosomal DNA replication system with Pol epsilon-depleted Xenopus egg extracts. The following xPol epsilon holoenzyme complexes were used: (a) wild type r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme, (b) Pol epsilon-DN containing p260D860N, (c) Pol epsilon DeltaCat containing p260DeltaCat, (d) p260p60 sub-complex of xPol epsilon, (e) p260-p12-p17 sub-complex of xPol epsilon, and (f) p260 (Fig. 2 and 3). The specific activity of each enzyme complex is summarized in Table 1. Although we did not measure the specific activity of p260-p60 and p260-p12-p17 sub-complexes, they were similarly purified as r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme and p260 and might be the same specific activity as that of p260 alone. Pol epsilon-DN and Pol epsilon DeltaCat holoenzymes do not have any significant DNA polymerase activity as predicted, although a slight contaminating DNA polymerase activity could be detected (Table 1). This may be due to contamination of insect DNA polymerase activity. It should be noted here that recombinant xPol epsilon holoenzyme complexes (r-xPol epsilon) purified from insect cells had minor contaminants, and all enzyme preparations except Flag-tagged p260 included p60, p12, and p17 in amounts that were detected by immunoblotting.

Figure 4 shows that the extent and rate of DNA synthesis were greatly reduced in Pol epsilon-depleted Xenopus extracts, and this defect was fully complemented by addition of purified native (n-xPol epsilon)(data not shown, and [22]) or wild type recombinant xPol epsilon (r-xPol epsilon). ###begin title 27
xPol epsilon holoenzyme interacts with xCdc45p, xCut5p, and xGINS
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 196 204 186 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 218 219 208 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>

###xml 950 952 904 906 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5C</xref>
###xml 1131 1133 1073 1075 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5B</xref>
###xml 1197 1206 1133 1142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 1212 1214 1148 1150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5C</xref>
###xml 1333 1335 1269 1271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5C</xref>
###xml 1544 1546 1474 1476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5D</xref>
###xml 186 191 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Previous studies suggested that Cdc45p, Cut5p, and GINS are critical for loading Pol alpha and Pol epsilon onto DNA replication origins and for initiating chromosomal DNA replication in yeast and Xenopus egg extracts [2,22,24,29-31]. However, those studies did not show any direct interaction between these initiation proteins and Pol epsilon holoenzyme. Therefore, in this study, we tested this possibility by measuring physical interactions between r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme and xCdc45p, xCut5p, and xGINS. The experiments were performed by incubating r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme with these proteins and immunoprecipitating protein complexes with anti-FLAG antibody. Figure 5A shows that xGINS was co-immunoprecipitated with Pol epsilon holoenzyme from reactions containing r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme and recombinant xGINS [29]. Addition of xCdc45p, but not xCut5p [31], slightly disrupted the complex between r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme and xGINS (Fig 5C), and incubation of xCdc45, xCut5p, and xGINS in the absence of r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme resulted in formation of a heterotrimeric complex lacking xPol epsilon holoenzyme (Fig. 5B). xCdc45p also interacts directly with xPol epsilon holoenzyme in vitro (Fig. 5C), and the amount of this complex decreased slightly in the presence of xGINS, but not in the presence of xCut5p (Fig. 5C). These results suggest that the trimeric xCdc45/xCut5p/xGINS complex interacts with xPol epsilon holoenzyme, and this interaction may involve direct contacts with xCdc45p and xGINS but not with xCut5p (Fig. 5D).

###begin title 33
Discussion
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 121 122 115 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 123 124 117 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 125 126 119 120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 323 325 311 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 326 328 314 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 329 331 317 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 442 459 424 441 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro Xenopus </italic>
###xml 613 621 583 591 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 703 705 667 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 877 878 819 820 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 1193 1194 1125 1126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 1337 1345 1263 1271 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 1500 1501 1416 1417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 1717 1718 1621 1622 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 1724 1726 1628 1630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 28 33 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 277 282 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Previous genetic studies in yeast suggested that Pol epsilon plays an important role during chromosomal DNA replication [1,7-9]. However, because the amino-terminal portion of Pol epsilon, that is required for its DNA polymerase- and exonuclease activities, is dispensable for yeast DNA replication, repair, and viability [15,16,27], the role of Pol epsilon during DNA replication has remained obscure. This study explores this role using an in vitro Xenopus DNA replication system and wild type and mutant forms of r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme. Here we show that the DNA replication defect in xPol epsilon-depleted Xenopus egg extracts is readily corrected by native (n-xPol epsilon) (data not shown and [22]) or recombinant xPol epsilon (r-xPol epsilon) holoenzyme or the p260-p60 Pol epsilon sub-complex, but not by p260DeltaCat holoenzyme, p260 DN, p260 or p260-p12-p17 (Fig. 4). Because the former enzymes are polymerase proficient, while p260DeltaCat holoenzyme and p260 DN are polymerase-deficient, although these preparations contained a small amount of DNA polymerase activity, and the last two sub-complexes do not contain the second essential subunit of xPol epsilon holoenzyme (Table 1), these results clearly demonstrate that the DNA polymerase activity of Pol epsilon holoenzyme is required for chromosomal DNA replication in Xenopus egg extracts. Note that both p260DeltaCat and p260 DN holoenzymes partially complements DNA replication defect of xPol epsilon-depleted egg extracts (Fig. 4). However, these DNA synthesis activities never reach to the levels of either mock-depleted-, n-xPol epsilon holoenzyme-, or r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme-supplemented egg extracts even after long time incubation (Fig. 4 and [22]), thus we conclude that those residual activity of DNA synthesis upon addition of catalytically dead polymerases is not significant to the key issue of this paper.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 162 163 150 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 261 269 243 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 284 286 266 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 297 298 279 280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
We do not know why n-xPol epsilon holoenzyme preparations always exhibit higher specific activity of DNA polymerase than that of r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme (Table 1), although both polymerase holoenzymes equally complement the defect of the Pol epsilon-depleted Xenopus egg extracts ([22] and Fig. 4). However, we noticed that the reconstituted r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme is much more volatile than n-xPol epsilon holoenzyme during purification (Shikata K and Sugino A, unpublished results). Thus, it is possible that r-xPol epsilon holoenzyme purified from insect cells would not be fully activated yet and it might be activated during incubation with xPol epsilon-depleted egg extracts by unknown mechanism(s) and function in the egg extracts as does n-xPol epsilon holoenzyme.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 113 120 113 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol2-16</italic>
###xml 322 327 322 327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc2 </italic>
###xml 328 337 328 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol &#948;</italic>
###xml 403 410 395 402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pol3-01</italic>
###xml 653 655 633 635 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 656 658 636 638 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 748 756 722 730 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 961 962 919 920 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 1101 1103 1053 1055 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 1104 1106 1056 1058 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1107 1109 1059 1061 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 1286 1293 1232 1239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus</italic>
###xml 1368 1382 1314 1328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 1386 1394 1332 1340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe</italic>
###xml 35 40 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 802 808 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeasts</span>
###xml 1086 1091 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1368 1381 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 1386 1394 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
The previous studies showed that a yeast strain consisting of a deletion of the amino-terminal portion of Pol2p (pol2-16) is temperature-sensitive for its growth, has a defect in elongation of DNA replication, has a short cell senescence, has a shorting telomere length, and is synthetic lethal with temperature-sensitive cdc2 (pol delta) mutations and with the 3'-5' exonuclease minus Pol delta mutant pol3-01, suggesting that Pol epsilon is required primarily for maintenance of genome integrity and that the DNA polymerase activity of Pol epsilon might be substituted by the remaining polymerase(s), if the polymerase domains are completely missing [16-18]. However, the requirement of xPol epsilon holoenzyme for chromosomal DNA replication in Xenopus egg extracts is much more strict than that of yeasts and Pol epsilonDeltaCat holoenzyme cannot restore DNA synthesis activity of Pol epsilon-depleted extracts to the levels of mock-depleted extracts (Fig. 4) and residual DNA synthesis observed in Pol epsilon-depleted extracts is not authentic chromosomal DNA replication, unlike yeast systems [15-18,27]. Therefore, we conclude that the DNA polymerase activity of Pol epsilon holoenzyme is strictly required for normal chromosomal DNA replication in higher eukaryotes, including Xenopus, but it may not be absolutely required for chromosomal DNA replication in S. cerevisiae and S. pombe, which have a relative small chromosomal DNA.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 307 309 295 297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 435 442 423 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus</italic>
###xml 448 459 436 447 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 460 462 448 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 463 465 451 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 825 833 795 803 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 923 925 893 895 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 966 974 936 944 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 1094 1095 1056 1057 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 1096 1097 1058 1059 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1098 1100 1060 1062 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 428 433 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 448 458 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 579 584 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
If the above analysis is correct, then it is important to determine whether Pol epsilon holoenzyme participates in leading or lagging strand DNA synthesis. Although the present study does not answer this question, previous studies are consistent with a role for Pol epsilon in leading strand DNA synthesis [23]. Furthermore, Cdc45p and GINS associate directly with Mcm2-7p, a DNA helicase that works at the replication fork, in yeast, Xenopus, and Drosophila [33-35], and xPol epsilon also associates directly with xGINS, xCdc45p, and xCut5p, as shown in this study. xGINS, like yeast GINS, also stimulates DNA synthesis catalyzed by xPol epsilon. Thus, it is very likely that the DNA polymerase activity of xPol epsilon holoenzyme, plus xCdc45p, xGINS and xMcm2-7p, perform a coordinated function at the replication fork in Xenopus egg extracts. We propose that this function is required for leading strand DNA synthesis [32], while lagging strand DNA synthesis in Xenopus may be carried out by Pol delta, PCNA, RF-C and Pol alpha-primase as well as Fen1p, Dna2p, RPA, DNA ligase and RNase H [4,5,36].
###end p 37
###begin title 38
Conclusion
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 147 155 135 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 572 580 530 538 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 802 808 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeasts</span>
This work shows that the DNA polymerase activity proficient xPol epsilon holoenzyme complements DNA replication defect of the Pol epsilon-depleted Xenopus egg extracts. However, neither the DNA polymerase-domain-deleted xPol epsilon, DNA polymerase-dead-xPol epsilon, the catalytic subunit of Pol epsilon nor other subunits of Pol epsilon without the catalytic subunit complements the defect at all. Furthermore, we show that xPol epsilon holoenzyme directly interacts with xCdc45, xCut5, and xGINS, which are required for the initiation of chromosomal DNA replication in Xenopus egg extracts. These results are the first, direct and biochemical evidence that the DNA polymerase activity of xPol epsilon holoenzyme is absolutely required for chromosomal DNA replication in higher eukaryotes, unlike in yeasts.
###end p 39

###begin title 49
Expression, purification, and reconstitution of Xenopus Pol epsilon Complex
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 36 38 36 38 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sup>
###xml 560 561 530 531 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1038 1046 995 1003 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>
###xml 1093 1101 1038 1046 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus </italic>

###xml 1304 1306 1245 1247 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">10</sub>
###xml 1315 1319 1256 1260 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">400 </sub>
For expression of proteins, 150 x 106 log phase sf21 insect cells were grown in suspension tissue culture flasks. The cells were infected with viral supernatant at a multiplicity of infection of 10 for p260 (a catalytic subunit of xPol epsilon complex), 5 for p60 (the second subunit of xPol epsilon complex), 5 for p12 (the third subunit of XPol epsilon complex), and 5 for p17 (the fourth subunit of xPol epsilon complex). After a 2-day incubation at 27 degreesC, cells were harvested, washed once with cold 1 x Tris-buffered saline, flash frozen in liquid N2, and stored at -80 degreesC. The frozen cells were thawed once on ice and then resuspended in 5 ml of buffer A (50 mM Tris, 150 mM NaCl, 5 mM EDTA, 20% glycerol, pH 7.5) containing 20 mug/ml leupeptin. After lysis, cells were kept on ice for 15 min and centrifuged at 12,000 rpm in a microcentrifuge for 10 min, and supernatant was recovered. Pol epsilon complex was purified from insect cell extracts with DEAE sepharose column, followed by anti-FLAG antibody column. Native Xenopus Pol epsilon (n-xPol epsilon) was purified from Xenopus egg extract by 4 sequential column chromatographies as published [22]. Throughout the purification, column fractions were assayed for DNA polymerase activity with the use of [alpha-32P]dTTP and oligo(dT)10/poly(dA)400 (1:19; 0.04 mM nucleotides) as a primer/template and were analyzed by Western blotting. One unit of Pol epsilon supported the incorporation of 1 nmol of dTMP under the conditions described above.
