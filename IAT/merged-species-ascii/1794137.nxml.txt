###begin article-title 0
###xml 61 71 61 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
Mapping and identification of candidate loci responsible for Peromyscus hybrid overgrowth
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 66 76 66 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 148 149 148 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 151 162 151 162 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">maniculatus</italic>
###xml 184 185 184 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 187 197 187 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">polionotus</italic>
###xml 485 489 485 489 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 652 656 652 656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 823 827 823 827 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 832 836 832 836 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 874 878 874 878 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 936 940 936 940 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 1091 1095 1091 1095 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 1124 1132 1124 1132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 148 162 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">P. maniculatus</span>
###xml 184 197 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">P. polionotus</span>
###xml 559 570 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">house mouse</span>
Crosses between two recently diverged rodent species of the genus Peromyscus result in dramatic parent-of-origin effects on growth and development. P. maniculatus females crossed with P. polionotus males yield growth-retarded conceptuses, whereas the reciprocal cross results in overgrowth and lethality. These hybrid effects are particularly pronounced in the placenta. We previously detected linkage to two regions of the genome involved in the overgrowth effects. One locus, termed Peal, is a paternally expressed autosomal locus mapping to a domain whose house mouse equivalent contains several clusters of imprinted genes. The other locus, termed Mexl, maps to a gene-poor region of the X chromosome. Here we use an advanced intercross line to verify and narrow the regions of linkage and identify candidate genes for Mexl and Peal. While we have previously shown that Mexl affects both pre-and postnatal growth, we show here that Peal affects only prenatal growth. Utilizing criteria such as mutant phenotypes and allelic expression, we identify the loci encoding the homeobox protein Esx1 and the zinc-finger protein Pw1/Peg3 as candidates. Both loci exhibit expression changes in the hybrids.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Introduction
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 21 31 21 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 87 109 87 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus maniculatus</italic>
###xml 290 311 290 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">Dewey and Dawson 2001</xref>
###xml 354 368 354 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. maniculatus</italic>
###xml 487 500 487 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. polionotus</italic>
###xml 87 109 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">Peromyscus maniculatus</span>
###xml 115 125 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">deer mouse</span>
###xml 354 368 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">P. maniculatus</span>
###xml 487 500 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">P. polionotus</span>
###xml 503 517 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">oldfield mouse</span>
Rodents of the genus Peromyscus are the most common indigenous North American mammals. Peromyscus maniculatus, the deer mouse, is the most widely distributed of the group, ranging from Alaska to Mexico City, from sea level to 14,000 feet elevation, and from the Pacific to Atlantic Oceans (Dewey and Dawson 2001). Despite near ubiquity in North America, P. maniculatus is largely excluded from the southeast United States. This region is inhabited by a recently diverged sister species, P. polionotus ("oldfield mouse").
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 35 49 35 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. maniculatus</italic>
###xml 59 72 59 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. polionotus</italic>
###xml 129 140 129 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">Dawson 1965</xref>
###xml 142 164 142 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR31">Rogers and Dawson 1970</xref>
###xml 35 49 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">P. maniculatus</span>
###xml 51 53 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 59 72 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">P. polionotus</span>
###xml 74 76 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 174 176 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 198 200 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
Crosses between captive strains of P. maniculatus (BW) and P. polionotus (PO) yield striking parent-of-origin effects on growth (Dawson 1965; Rogers and Dawson 1970). Female BW animals crossed with PO males (bw x po) produce animals that exhibit prenatal growth retardation relative to the parental strains. The bw x po growth effects are particularly pronounced in the placenta, which average half the size of those of either parental genotype. The undergrowth persists through the life of the bw x po offspring, which are fertile and healthy.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 531 549 531 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">Dawson et al. 1993</xref>
###xml 558 568 558 568 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 32 34 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 56 58 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 66 68 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 71 73 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 164 166 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 169 171 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 460 462 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 465 467 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
Conversely, offspring of female PO animals crossed with BW males (PO x BW) exhibit placental and somatic overgrowth. A variable suite of phenotypes accompanies the PO x BW overgrowth; these range from conceptuses where there is no embryo proper (i.e., only placental and extra-embryonic tissues are present) to those that survive to late gestation. Typically the latter are deformed and oversized to the point that the mother dies at parturition. The very few PO x BW that have survived postnatally have been oversized and female (Dawson et al. 1993). These Peromyscus crosses represent a rare case in which the genetics of mammalian reproductive isolation may be examined.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 42 43 42 43 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 349 367 349 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">Dawson et al. 1993</xref>
###xml 567 583 567 583 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">Reik et al. 2003</xref>
The parent-of-origin effects seen in the F1 crosses suggested that genes whose expression is dependent on parental origin might underlie the hybrid dysgenesis. Such loci include mitochondrial, sex chromosome-linked, and genes subject to genomic imprinting. Mitochondrial-nuclear genotype interactions have been ruled out as a source of the effects (Dawson et al. 1993). Imprinted genes are autosomal genes that show preferential expression of one parental allele over the other. Many imprinted genes are involved in growth pathways and are expressed in the placenta (Reik et al. 2003).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 132 144 132 144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus musculus</italic>
###xml 182 204 182 204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR33">Takagi and Sasaki 1975</xref>
###xml 206 221 206 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR38">Xue et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 325 341 325 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">Khil et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 343 360 343 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">Moore et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 126 130 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 132 144 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus musculus</span>
###xml 150 156 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
The X chromosome also shows parental gene expression bias. For example, the paternal copy is preferentially silenced in house mice (Mus musculus) and bovine extra-embryonic tissues (Takagi and Sasaki 1975; Xue et al. 2002). Moreover, the X chromosome is known to contain a disproportionately large number of placental genes (Khil et al. 2004; Moore et al. 2005).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 120 121 120 121 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 156 157 156 157 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 244 245 244 245 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 259 260 259 260 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 348 349 348 349 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 145 147 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 160 162 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 219 221 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 253 255 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 339 341 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 344 346 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 386 388 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 412 414 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
To investigate the genetics of the overgrowth, we initially performed two backcrosses. In the first backcross we mated F1 females (bw x po) with BW males (F1 x BW hereafter). The second cross series consisted of female PO animals crossed with F1 males (PO x F1). We expected both backcrosses to mimic the overgrowth phenotypes seen in the PO x BW F1cross, because both could contribute PO alleles maternally and BW alleles paternally.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 156 159 156 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1</italic>
###xml 160 164 160 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peg3</italic>
###xml 202 219 202 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">Vrana et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 295 305 295 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 336 339 336 339 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
We mapped two loci associated with overgrowth through genotypic analysis of the offspring from the two crosses: one linked to the paternally expressed gene Pw1/Peg3, and one linked to the X chromosome (Vrana et al. 2000). We showed that X chromosome loci are imprinted (maternally expressed) in Peromyscus extra-embryonic tissues as in Mus. Thus, both regions of linkage were consistent with the parent-of-origin effects.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 34 38 34 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 83 91 83 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 105 109 105 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 199 203 199 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 236 240 236 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 186 188 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 223 225 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
We have termed the X-linked locus Mexl (Maternal Expressed X-linked Locus) and the Pw1/Peg3-linked locus Peal (Paternal Expressed Autosomal Locus). Both backcrosses could contribute the PO allele of Mexl maternally and the BW allele of Peal paternally to produce overgrowth. The linkage to each locus, however, is revealed in only one of the two backcrosses, depending on whether the paternal or maternal genome is varied in that cross.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 46 47 46 47 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 69 70 69 70 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 88 105 88 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">Vrana et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 198 199 198 199 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 203 204 203 204 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 287 291 287 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 296 300 296 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 422 426 422 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 477 481 477 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 419 421 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 474 476 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
We also previously performed a cross between F1 (bw x po) females x F1 (bw x po) males (Vrana et al. 2000). As expected, this cross yielded small, normal, and oversized offspring. Furthermore, the F1 x F1 offspring genotypes suggested a negative interaction between the two mapped loci (Mexl and Peal). That is, when both parental genotypes varied, we saw a significant reduction in those that had a maternally derived PO Mexl allele and those that had a paternally derived BW Peal allele.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 30 31 30 31 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 37 38 37 38 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 110 111 110 111 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 144 145 144 145 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 330 347 330 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">Vrana et al. 1998</xref>
###xml 520 521 520 521 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 24 26 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 41 43 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 104 106 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 179 181 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 184 186 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 524 526 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
While both backcrosses (PO x F1 and F1 x BW) did in fact yield both normal and oversized offspring, the PO x F1 cross better recapitulated the F1 phenotypes. The offspring of the PO x BW cross exhibited extreme placental and somatic overgrowth, multiple developmental abnormalities, and loss of imprinting (LOI) of certain genes (Vrana et al. 1998). LOI is characterized by a switch from monoallelic to biallelic expression, indicating a perturbation in control of genomic imprinting. In contrast, the offspring of the F1 x BW cross exhibited mild placental/somatic overgrowth, little embryonic lethality or morphologic abnormalities, and no discernible LOI.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 130 149 130 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">Duselis et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 449 453 449 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 454 458 454 458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 192 194 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
We examined the disparity between these two crosses and have provided genetic evidence for a maternal effect involved in the LOI (Duselis et al. 2005). This maternal effect appears to require PO homozygosity in mothers of affected offspring and to be due to a small number of loci. The maternal effect appears to be a separate mechanism contributing to the hybrid overgrowth. Thus, we hypothesize that the combination of the maternal effect and the Mexl-Peal interaction are responsible for the full hybrid overgrowth and suite of associated characteristics.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 175 185 175 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 409 417 409 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 422 426 422 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 486 490 486 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 495 499 495 499 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
To further explore the genetics of the hybrid overgrowth, we have confirmed and refined the areas of linkage through a combination of backcrosses and intercrosses. Because no Peromyscus genome is yet mapped, we developed additional genetic markers for these regions. We surveyed these areas for candidate loci and investigated several through sequence identity and gene expression. We focused particularly on Pw1/Peg3 and Esx1 because they were the first candidate genes identified for Peal and Mexl (respectively) and because they remained candidates through the genetic analyses.
###end p 14

Materials and methods
###end title 15
###begin title 16
Animal breeding/husbandry
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 40 50 40 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 239 240 239 240 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 289 291 289 291 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">10</sub>
###xml 334 336 334 336 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">13</sub>
###xml 449 459 449 459 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ad libitum</italic>
###xml 13 15 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 20 22 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 84 86 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 100 102 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
We purchased BW and PO animals from the Peromyscus Genetic Stock Center (). We bred BW females with PO males as the basis of the advanced intercross line. These hybrid animals were then interbred for 2 more generations in the case of the G3 animals, 9 more generations in the case of the G10s, and 12 more generations in the case of G13s. Animals picked to breed for the next generation were chosen randomly. Animals were housed with food and water ad libitum on a 16:8 light:dark cycle.

###begin title 21
Development of markers and genotyping
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 80 83 80 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 88 92 88 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Homo</italic>
###xml 320 322 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 327 329 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
Genes used as markers were chosen based on their location in the linkage areas. Mus and Homo sequences were obtained from ENSMBL () and aligned. Areas in the 5' or 3' untranslated regions (UTRs) or small exons and introns that had a high level of conservation were used to design primers. Sequence was obtained from the PO and BW strains by PCR and cloned into the pCR 2.1-TOPO vector (Invitrogen Life Technologies, Carlsbad, CA). Several clones of each allele were sequenced to obtain the most accurate sequence. Sequences were then examined for restriction fragment length polymorphisms (RFLPs) using Vector NTI or TACG ().
###end p 22

###begin title 25
Gene expression
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 67 70 66 67 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174;</sup>
###xml 144 153 141 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/ Peg3</italic>
###xml 155 159 152 156 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>

###xml 293 303 290 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
RT-PCR was performed on 1.0 mug of DNase I-treated RNA using TaqMan(R) RT-PCR reagents (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA). TaqMan assays for Pw1/ Peg3, Esx1, and an endogenous control, Rpl32, were obtained through the Assays-by-Design service offered by Applied Biosystems after submitting Peromyscus sequence for these genes. In each reaction approximately 75 ng of cDNA was used along with 2.5 mul of 20 x  assay mix and TaqMan Universal Mastermix-No UNG (Applied Biosystems) for a total reaction volume of 50 mul. Reactions were done in triplicate in 96-well plates and run on an ABI 7900 real-time PCR instrument. Two or more samples were analyzed for each parental strain and each reciprocal hybrid. Data were analyzed and expression levels calculated as per the manufacturer's recommendations. Northern analysis was performed by standard methods. Quantitation and visualization was conducted on an Typhoon 9400 (GE Healthcare Bio-Sciences Corp., Piscataway, NJ).

###begin title 27

###begin title 28
Crosses used to localize Mexl
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 76 77 76 77 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 165 188 165 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">Darvasi and Soller 1995</xref>
###xml 237 239 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
To refine the area of linkage on the X chromosome, we first bred (bw x po) F1 animals together for multiple generations to create an advanced intercross line (AIL) (Darvasi and Soller 1995). Advanced intercross females were then bred to BW males. We reasoned that such "advanced backcrosses" would enable us to localize the area of the X chromosome responsible for the overgrowth without potential confounding effects of allelic variation at interacting paternal loci.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 522 532 522 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 563 566 563 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 114 116 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 119 121 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 123 125 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 143 145 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
Our prior analyses were based largely on weight of mature placentas as the result of several factors. First, some PO x BW, PO x AIL, and AIL x BW matings resulted in conceptuses that consisted of only placentas and associated extra-embryonic tissues. Second, those crosses that produced severe overgrowth frequently resulted in maternal lethality. Third, it was very difficult to ensure exact time of embryonic development and thus compare weights of the embryo proper. In contrast, maximum placental weight is reached in Peromyscus at the equivalent stage as in Mus, at approximately 14.5 days of gestation (E14.5).
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 156 157 156 157 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 220 221 220 221 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 368 372 368 372 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plp1</italic>
###xml 374 377 374 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 462 466 462 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xist</italic>
###xml 473 476 473 476 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 496 513 496 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">Vrana et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 167 169 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 237 239 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
We had previously undertaken two crosses in which maternal genotype varied. We established X linkage for both adult body weight and placental weight in an F1 female x BW male cross. We then bred a third generation AIL (G3) female x male BW cross resulting in 87 offspring. Placental overgrowth in these embryos had cosegregated most closely with a marker for the gene Plp1 (Mus position = 56 cM). The next closest marker in the initial analysis was that for the Xist gene (Mus position = 42 cM) (Vrana et al. 2000).
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 13 20 13 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in situ</italic>
###xml 80 83 80 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 118 128 118 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 318 335 318 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">Vrana et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 460 464 460 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 528 532 528 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 534 537 534 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 557 561 557 561 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Irs4</italic>
###xml 573 578 573 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Prdx4</italic>
###xml 594 599 594 599 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Glra2</italic>
Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) analysis has confirmed that the whole Mus X chromosome is syntenic with the Peromyscus X chromosome (Dr. Rachel O'Neill, University of Connecticut, personal. communication) Furthermore, gene order on the X chromosome appears to be generally conserved between the two species (Vrana et al. 2000). We first developed additional markers corresponding to genes on the distal portion of the X chromosome, where we believed Mexl to lie. These markers included genotyping assays for the genes Esx1 (Mus position 57.0 cM), Irs4 (58.0 cM), Prdx4 (67.7 cM), and Glra2 (72.0 cM).
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 205 209 205 209 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 247 251 247 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plp1</italic>
###xml 64 66 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 365 367 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
After the previously published work, we next genotyped the G3 x BW offspring with the additional distal X chromosome markers in an attempt to refine the area of phenotypic linkage. In this cross, however, Esx1 genotypes were identical to those of Plp1 for all placentas larger than the parental range. We therefore bred AIL females of greater generation numbers to BW males in the expectation that their offspring would provide greater numbers of recombinant offspring.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 32 34 32 34 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">10</sub>
###xml 40 42 40 42 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">13</sub>
###xml 294 295 294 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="Tab1" ref-type="table">1</xref>
###xml 69 71 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
Specifically, we utilized both G10 and G13 females in backcrosses to BW males. The two crosses yielded 86 conceptuses of age E14.5 or later. These conceptuses yielded placentas with a spectrum of weights from those within the parental weight range to those exhibiting extreme overgrowth (Table 1). Note that the parental strains show the same mean weight of approximately 0.18 g. We also included eight younger conceptuses whose placental weight already exceeded the maximum weight seen in both parental strains. Thus, we genotyped 94 total offspring of these crosses with the distal X chromosome markers and compared the results with placental weights.
###end p 34

Esx1 maternal genotypes showed the greatest correlation with the largest placentas (Table 2), suggesting this is the closest marker to the Mexl locus. The distribution of maternal Esx1 genotypes by placental weight over all the offspring AIL female x BW male cross is shown in Fig. 1. These data reveal that the PO Esx1 genotypes are also found in normal-sized placentas and that they constitute greater than 92% (87/94) of the total maternal genotypes. We had previously found substantial evidence for allele fixation at multiple autosomal loci in the AIL (Duselis et al. 2005): seven of nine loci tested yielded chi-squared values corresponding to p values of 0.05 or lower. In this case, approximately 31 of the 94 maternal Esx1 alleles are predicted to be of the PO genotype (one third of the original intercross X alleles were a PO genotype). Analysis of this disparity results in a chi-squared score of 101 (p < 0.0005), suggesting that the Esx1 genotypes in the AIL are extremely skewed. Gene order in this distal portion of the X chromosome appears to be different than that in Mus, where the gene order is (proximal-distal) Xist, Plp1, Esx1, Irs4, Prdx4, Glra2. An analysis of the data in Table 2 using the Map Manager software (Manly and Olson 1999) suggests the gene order in Peromyscus is Prdx4, Xist, Esx1, Plp1, Irs4, with Glra2 either proximal or distal to the other loci.

###begin title 40
Crosses used to localize Peal
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 67 71 67 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 433 452 433 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">Duselis et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 207 209 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 316 318 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 461 463 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
To localize the paternally expressed gene affecting hybrid growth (Peal), we undertook crosses in which the paternal genome was varied but the maternal genome remained constant. These crosses therefore were PO females crossed with AIL males. Crosses in which the paternal genome is varied but the maternal genome is PO are more difficult to undertake than the reciprocal because of the maternal effect on genomic imprinting control (Duselis et al. 2005). These PO x AIL crosses always result in offspring with some degree of imprinting perturbation and also often in maternal lethality. Resorptions are also common in these crosses, resulting in our harvesting embryos from approximately one quarter of matings.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 39 40 39 40 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 46 48 46 48 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">13</sub>
###xml 130 131 130 131 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 254 255 254 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="Tab1" ref-type="table">1</xref>
###xml 28 30 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 124 126 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
We obtained 16 litters from PO x AIL (G3 and G13) crosses, resulting in 96 mature placentas for genetic analysis. As in the PO x F1 cross, these offspring displayed mild to severe placental and somatic overgrowth with no obvious sexual dimorphism (Table 1).
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 70 78 70 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>

###xml 253 271 253 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">Dawson et al. 1999</xref>
###xml 399 402 399 402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 459 462 459 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ckm</italic>
###xml 464 467 464 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 491 495 491 495 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gpi1</italic>
###xml 586 594 586 594 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 596 601 596 601 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Myod1</italic>
###xml 617 620 617 620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H19</italic>
###xml 93 95 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 524 526 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
We had previously detected linkage of the overgrowth phenotype to the Pw1/Peg3 locus using a PO female x F1 male cross. Pw1/Peg3 is located on Mus Chromosome 7 at 6.5 cM. Mus whole Chromosome 7 FISH probes hybridize entirely to Peromyscus Chromosome 1 (Dawson et al. 1999), suggesting that gene content in this region is conserved. Therefore, we designed genotyping assays for other loci located on Mus proximal Chromosome 7. The new assays include those for Ckm (Mus location = cM 4.5) and Gpi1 (cM 11.0). We genotyped the PO x AIL offspring with these as markers as well as those for Pw1/Peg3, Myod1 (cM 23.5), and H19 (cM 69.0). The latter, however, did not show any genotype-phenotype association (data not shown).

To estimate how great an increase in resolution these crosses provided, we compared recombination rates between the F1 and AIL backcrosses. The PO x F1 cross produced four recombinants between Pw1/Peg3 and Myod1 in 109 animals for a distance of 3.7 cM. In contrast, there were 13 recombinants between these two loci in the 103 PO x AIL offspring (12.6 cM). As expected, the majority of the recombinants were found in the later generation AIL crosses. Identification of Mexl and Peal candidate loci

###begin p 56
###xml 97 100 97 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 242 252 242 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>

We examined the Mouse Genome Informatics Database () for known genes between 50 and 80 cM of the Mus X chromosome. Our criteria for candidate gene identification were (1) loci in the region that show mutant phenotypes similar to those of the Peromyscus hybrids; (2) genes that exhibit great divergence between mouse and human (suggesting rapid evolution that might lead to the interspecific negative interaction); (3) genes thought to be directly involved in growth pathways (e.g., by cell culture assays or paralogy); and (4) genes highly expressed in the placenta.

###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 49 52 49 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 146 154 146 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 321 325 321 325 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 386 403 386 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">Vrana et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 585 590 585 590 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Usp29</italic>
###xml 592 598 592 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zfp264</italic>
###xml 604 612 604 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 49 52 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
In contrast to the distal X chromosome, proximal Mus Chromosome 7 is gene dense. For example, there are at least 40 distinct genes within 1 cM of Pw1/Peg3. An additional criterion that may be used to identify candidate genes in this region is their allelic expression. That is, our genetic analyses strongly suggest that Peal is normally expressed exclusively from the paternal allele (Vrana et al. 2000). Like other autosomal imprinted domains, only a subset of genes in this region exhibit monoallelic paternal expression. The known paternally expressed genes in this domain include Usp29, Zfp264, and Pw1/Peg3 ().
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 201 205 201 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zim2</italic>
###xml 212 220 212 220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 270 274 270 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zim2</italic>
###xml 293 301 293 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 351 366 351 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR15">Kim et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 369 373 369 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zim2</italic>
###xml 388 391 388 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 422 430 422 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 464 467 464 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 526 530 526 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zim1</italic>
###xml 551 555 551 555 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zim2</italic>
###xml 560 568 560 568 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 619 623 619 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zim1</italic>
###xml 679 683 679 683 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ast1</italic>
###xml 712 727 712 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">Kim et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 95 108 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">Domestic cows</span>
###xml 110 116 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 122 126 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 343 349 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 388 391 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
###xml 464 467 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
###xml 579 584 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 593 605 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">domestic cow</span>
Comparative genomic analyses, however, suggest that this imprinted domain is rapidly evolving: Domestic cows, humans, and mice show differences in both gene content and imprinting status. For example, Zim2, like Pw1/Peg3, is a paternally expressed zinc-finger gene. The Zim2 gene overlaps the Pw1/Peg3 gene (sharing several small 5' exons) in humans (Kim et al. 2000). Zim2 is present in Mus but does not share exons with Pw1/Peg3 and is maternally expressed. The Mus genome has another maternally expressed zinc-finger gene, Zim1, which lies between Zim2 and Pw1/Peg3. Both the human and the domestic cow genomes lack Zim1; the latter has an unrelated, nonimprinted gene termed Ast1 in the equivalent position (Kim et al. 2004).
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 75 83 75 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 375 393 375 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR29">Relaix et al. 1998</xref>
###xml 395 399 395 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">2000</xref>
###xml 434 442 434 442 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 553 571 553 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">Curley et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 573 587 573 587 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">Li et al. 1999</xref>
###xml 681 683 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 686 688 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
The only imprinted gene in this region to be functionally characterized is Pw1/Peg3. This gene was originally discovered in a screen for novel myogenic regulatory factors. Further biochemical and cell-culture assays have revealed that the gene product acts in the tumor necrosis factor (TNF)/NFkappaB signal transduction pathway and has a key role in p53-mediated apoptosis (Relaix et al. 1998, 2000). The gene-targeting reduction in Pw1/Peg3 product resulted in animals with both placental and somatic growth retardation as well as behavioral defects (Curley et al. 2004; Li et al. 1999). Thus, one might predict that overexpression of the gene resulting from the LOI seen in the PO x BW hybrids would lead to overgrowth.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 100 121 100 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR26">Panichkul et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 439 453 439 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">Li et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 455 467 455 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR32">Szulman 1987</xref>
###xml 674 692 674 692 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">Judson et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 52 58 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Another locus that maps to the equivalent region in humans, 19q13.4, also affects placental growth (Panichkul et al. 2005). This locus influences susceptibility to biparental complete hydatidiform moles (BiCHM), which are characterized by the presence of hyperplastic trophoblast villi and lack of a fetus. Hydatidiform moles typically have either no maternal genome or have two paternal genomes (e.g., due to fertilization by two sperm) (Li et al. 2002; Szulman 1987). Recent studies, however, have confirmed the existence of hydatidiform moles that have normal biparental genomic contributions (BiCHM). The BiCHM tissues exhibit altered DNA methylation suggestive of LOI (Judson et al. 2002).
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 72 80 72 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 166 171 166 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NALP7</italic>
###xml 337 356 337 356 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">Murdoch et al. 2006</xref>
###xml 363 366 363 366 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 363 366 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
While the familial BiCHM susceptibility locus has been localized to the Pw1/Peg3 domain, it does not correlate with coding region polymorphisms of the gene. The gene NALP7, known to be involved in apoptotic and inflammatory pathways, has emerged as the primary candidate for those BiCHM whose susceptibility locus maps to Chromosome 19 (Murdoch et al. 2006). The Mus genome, however, apparently lacks an ortholog in this region.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 57 67 57 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 139 147 139 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 251 254 251 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 391 394 379 382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 251 254 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
###xml 391 394 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
While we do not yet know the structure of this domain in Peromyscus, Northern analysis suggests that there are no genes sharing exons with Pw1/Peg3. We hybridized placental and embryonic RNA with multiple probes derived from regions equivalent to the Mus upstream exons and the large (approximately5 kb) final exon. All tests revealed only the approximately 9-kb transcript also observed in Mus (data not shown).
###end p 65
###begin title 66

###end title 66
###begin p 67

Upon identifying Esx1 and Pw1/Peg3 as candidate genes for Mexl and Peal (respectively), we asked whether expression levels varied between the two species and if perturbations occurred in the hybrid offspring. We tested expression levels in both genes via Northern analysis and quantitative PCR analysis.

###begin p 70
###xml 22 26 22 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 309 313 309 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 516 538 516 538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR31">Rogers and Dawson 1970</xref>
###xml 540 557 540 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR37">Vrana et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 585 589 585 589 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
It is unclear whether Esx1 is a viable candidate gene for the genetic incompatibilities underlying the hybrid growth retardation. While preliminary evidence suggests the involvement of the X chromosome in the genetics of the bw x po hybrid phenotypes, it is not yet clear whether the same region is involved. Esx1 is predominantly expressed in the labyrinth region of the placenta. The other major layer of the muroid placenta, the spongiotrophoblast or junctional zone, is greatly reduced in the bw x po placentas (Rogers and Dawson 1970; Vrana et al. 2001). Therefore, the increased Esx1 expression in bw x po may be a consequence of a relative overabundance of the expressing cells.
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 4 7 4 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H19</italic>
###xml 82 92 82 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>

###xml 240 257 240 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">Vrana et al. 1998</xref>
###xml 300 304 300 304 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
The H19 gene is also predominantly transcribed in the labyrinthine portion of the Peromyscus placenta (Vrana et al. 2001). However, total H19 expression levels in the bw x po placenta are no higher than that seen in either parental strain (Vrana et al. 1998). Thus, it is possible that the increased Esx1 expression plays a causal role in the growth-retarded hybrid placentas.
###end p 71

###begin title 74
Discussion
###end title 74
###begin p 75
###xml 6 16 6 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 256 260 256 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 265 269 265 269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 324 328 324 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 396 404 396 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 503 507 503 507 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 626 630 626 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 678 682 678 682 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 718 722 718 722 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plp1</italic>
###xml 727 731 727 731 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 739 740 739 740 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="Tab2" ref-type="table">2</xref>
###xml 789 792 789 792 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 844 854 844 854 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 789 792 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
These Peromyscus species and their hybrids offer a novel system for studying the role of natural genetic variation on epigenetic regulation, reproductive isolation, growth, and development. Here we have refined the areas of the genome containing two loci (Mexl and Peal) contributing to the hybrid parent-of-origin effects. Peal remains unbounded on one side. The combination of tight linkage to Pw1/Peg3 in combination with requirement for paternal expression, however, will likely result in few other Peal candidate loci. As most X-linked loci are maternally expressed in rodent placentas, it would be difficult to identify Mexl candidates on the basis of allelic expression. Mexl, however, appears to be bounded by Plp1 and Esx1 (Table 2). These two loci lie only about 300 kb apart in Mus (). Thus, a comprehensive survey of this region in Peromyscus should not be difficult.
###end p 75
###begin p 76
###xml 50 54 50 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 59 63 59 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 68 76 68 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 81 85 81 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
We have identified candidate genes for both loci, Esx1 for Mexl and Pw1/Peg3 for Peal. Genotypes of both candidate loci are completely concordant with placental hypertrophy in their respective backcrosses. While both genes show equivalent expression levels in mature placentas of the parental strains, this does not rule out interspecific regulatory changes. For example, the genes may show different temporal and/or spatial expression patterns.
###end p 76
###begin p 77
###xml 117 121 117 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 145 153 145 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 95 97 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 123 125 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
Despite the apparent requirement of these alleles for hypertrophy, those particular genotypes (PO maternal allele of Esx1, BW paternal allele of Pw1/Peg3) did not always result in overgrowth. Our approach of crossing AIL animals to a parental strain rules out incomplete penetrance because of the necessity of genotypes at interacting loci inherited from the other parent.
###end p 77
###begin p 78
###xml 158 166 158 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 216 217 216 217 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 325 326 325 326 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 425 433 425 433 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 444 461 444 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">Vrana et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 530 531 530 531 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 649 657 649 657 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 210 212 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 319 321 <span type="species:ncbi:42413">PO</span>
###xml 541 543 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">BW</span>
###xml 673 677 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">Rice</span>
It seems likely, however, that other alleles from the varying parental genome may act as modifiers to the phenotypes. For example, the lack of correlation of Pw1/Peg3 genotypes with postnatal overgrowth in the PO x F1 cross suggests the involvement of other loci contributing to the hybrid overgrowth. Furthermore, the PO x F1 placental analysis revealed a class of mildly overgrown placentas that were also not dependent on Pw1/Peg3 genotype (Vrana et al. 2000). Another unidentified locus affecting postnatal overgrowth in the F1 female x BW backcross has been linked to the autosomal platinum coat-color allele, which does not exhibit linkage to Pw1/Peg3 (W. Dawson, J. Rice, J. Weston, C. Ramsdell, and M. Dewey; submitted).
###end p 78
###begin p 79
###xml 4 12 4 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pw1/Peg3</italic>
###xml 82 86 82 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 467 486 467 486 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">Morison et al. 2005</xref>
The Pw1/Peg3 region is gene dense, potentially rendering further genetic tests of Peal difficult. A first approach would be to characterize the genes in the region and their allelic expression, i.e., nonpaternally expressed genes could be ruled out. A systematic study of this domain has not yet been performed in any species. Furthermore, comparative studies to date suggest one cannot assume that imprinting status in this region necessarily applies to other taxa (Morison et al. 2005).
###end p 79
###begin p 80
###xml 159 163 159 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 332 353 332 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">Li and Behringer 1998</xref>
###xml 355 369 355 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">Li et al. 1997</xref>
In contrast, the paucity of genes on the distal X chromosome of muroid rodents examined to date makes potential testing of candidate loci more feasible. While Esx1 looks promising as a candidate, it is unclear how its gene product might regulate postnatal growth, given its lack of embryonic and postnatal expression (save testis) (Li and Behringer 1998; Li et al. 1997).
###end p 80
###begin p 81
###xml 45 48 45 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 73 84 73 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus spretus</italic>
###xml 155 158 155 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 267 277 267 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus</italic>
###xml 291 294 291 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 380 384 380 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Esx1</italic>
###xml 397 416 397 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR40">Zechner et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 485 488 485 488 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus</italic>
###xml 498 517 498 517 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR39">Zechner et al. 1997</xref>
###xml 45 48 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
###xml 73 84 <span type="species:ncbi:10096">Mus spretus</span>
###xml 155 158 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
###xml 291 294 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
###xml 485 488 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus</span>
Crosses between the mixed genetic background Mus strain C57BL/6 (B6) and Mus spretus also yield reciprocal parent-of-origin effects on placental size. The Mus crosses, however, do not yield significant reciprocal effects on embryo or postnatal growth. Similar to the Peromyscus crosses, the Mus hybrid placental growth effects show genetic linkage to the X chromosome and altered Esx1 expression (Zechner et al. 2002). No genetic role for any autosomal locus has yet been found in the Mus hybrids (Zechner et al. 1997).
###end p 81
###begin p 82
###xml 239 244 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The greater inquiries underlying this work are whether these allelic variants of imprinted genes and imprinting regulatory genes have undergone positive selection, whether similar changes underlie other cases of reproductive isolation and human disease, and what the commonalities are in the biological roles of imprinted genes.
###end p 82
###begin p 83
###xml 156 172 156 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">Reik et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 279 301 279 301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">Carter and Enders 2004</xref>
###xml 303 320 303 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">Cross et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 592 614 592 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">Fisher and Hodges 2003</xref>
###xml 616 636 616 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">Reik and Walter 2001</xref>
###xml 707 727 707 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">Oudejans et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 755 771 755 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">Reik et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 554 559 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
It is clear that a major role of imprinted gene products is to modulate prenatal growth. The placenta, a key endocrine organ, expresses many of these loci (Reik et al. 2003). Eutherian placentas, however, vary tremendously in overall morphology, cell types, and gene expression (Carter and Enders 2004; Cross et al. 2003). Numerous associations between altered imprinted gene expression and placental phenotypes suggest at least a role for imprinted genes in this aspect of mammalian diversity. Evidence suggests roles for imprinted genes in a number of human growth and placental disorders (Fisher and Hodges 2003; Reik and Walter 2001). Included in the latter group are the two most common, preeclampsia (Oudejans et al. 2004) and gestational diabetes (Reik et al. 2003). Thus, it seems likely that natural variation at imprinted loci can contribute to mammalian speciation. This work represents a significant advance in isolating such variation.
###end p 83
###begin p 84
###xml 48 52 48 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mexl</italic>
###xml 57 61 57 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peal</italic>
###xml 126 148 126 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peromyscus maniculatus</italic>
###xml 333 342 333 342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">Dice 1949</xref>
###xml 344 352 344 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">Liu 1954</xref>
###xml 467 477 467 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">Vrana 2006</xref>
###xml 126 148 <span type="species:ncbi:10042">Peromyscus maniculatus</span>
An opportunity exists to generally test whether Mexl and Peal play a role in reproductive isolation in other populations. The Peromyscus maniculatus species complex contains a number of partially isolated populations. Furthermore, preliminary studies suggest parent-of-origin effects in hybrids between several of these populations (Dice 1949; Liu 1954). Because very little is known about genetic and epigenetic variation at imprinted loci in any mammalian species (Vrana 2006), the present work provides a preliminary framework to test hypotheses about this aspect of mammalian biology.
###end p 84
