###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 274 299 247 272 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 268 273 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 274 298 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
The carbonic anhydrases (CAs) are involved in inorganic carbon utilization. They have been classified into six evolutionary and structural families: alpha-, beta-, gamma-, delta-, epsilon-, zeta- CAs, with beta-CAs present in higher plants, algae and prokaryotes. The yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae encodes a single copy of beta-CA Nce103/YNL036W.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
We determined the crystal structure of Nce103 in complex with a substrate analog at 2.04 A resolution. It assembles as a homodimer, with the active site located at the interface between two monomers. At the bottom of the substrate pocket, a zinc ion is coordinated by the three highly conserved residues Cys57, His112 and Cys115 in addition to a water molecule. Residues Asp59, Arg61, Gly111, Leu102, Val80, Phe75 and Phe97 form a tunnel to the bottom of the active site which is occupied by a molecule of the substrate analog acetate. Activity assays of full length and two truncated versions of Nce103 indicated that the N-terminal arm is indispensable.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
The quaternary structure of Nce103 resembles the typical plant type beta-CAs of known structure, with an N-terminal arm indispensable for the enzymatic activity. Comparative structure analysis enables us to draw a possible tunnel for the substrate to access the active site which is located at the bottom of a funnel-shaped substrate pocket.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 24 34 24 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">EC 4.2.1.1</italic>
###xml 153 154 153 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 155 156 155 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 205 207 205 207 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 226 230 226 230 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">vivo</italic>
###xml 302 303 302 303 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 309 310 309 310 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 310 312 310 312 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">- </sup>
###xml 349 350 349 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 529 530 529 530 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 679 680 655 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 774 776 746 748 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 968 969 937 938 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 1038 1065 1003 1030 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Methanosarcina thermophila </italic>
###xml 1066 1067 1031 1032 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 1086 1087 1047 1048 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 1100 1101 1058 1059 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1199 1201 1151 1153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 1038 1064 <span type="species:ncbi:2210">Methanosarcina thermophila</span>
Carbonic anhydrase (CA; EC 4.2.1.1) is a zinc containing enzyme which catalyzes a reversible reaction to form bicarbonate from carbon dioxide and water [1,2]. By facilitating the interconversion between CO2 and bicarbonate in vivo, CA activity was proposed to be required to ensure adequate level of CO2or HCO3- as the substrates for other enzymes [3]. Therefore, CAs have been found to play an essential role in a series of fundamental biological processes such as photosynthesis, respiration, pH homeostasis and ion transport [4]. Based on the origins and structural features, CAs are divided into six classes, termed alpha, beta, gamma, delta, epsilon and zeta, respectively [5]. The alpha-CAs were found in mammals, algae and prokaryotes to facilitate the exchange of CO2 in the respiratory cycle. The beta-CAs were characterized in higher plants, algae and prokaryotes, and those in higher plants have been reported to play a critical role during photosynthesis [6]. The gamma-class was first identified in the thermophilic archaeon Methanosarcina thermophila [7], whereas delta- [8] and zeta- [9] class were found in diatom only. Notably, the epsilon-CAs from bacteria can form a carboxysome [10].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 445 446 420 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 447 448 422 423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 449 451 424 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 452 454 427 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 963 965 932 934 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
The beta-CAs of known structure form a dimer, tetramer or octamer of identical subunits, with the active site located at the interface of two subunits. Each subunit consists of three parts, an N-terminal arm composed of two or three projecting alpha-helices, a conserved zinc-binding core consisting of alpha-beta-alpha units exhibiting a Rossmann fold and an anti-parallel beta-sheet and a C-terminal domain that is dominated by alpha-helices [3,5,11-15]. The N-terminal arm of one subunit makes significant contacts with another subunit via domain swapping. The overall structure of the zinc-binding core is relatively conserved, but the zinc-coordinating residues differ in various classes of CA. In beta-CA, the zinc ion binds to two cysteines and a histidine, in addition to a water molecule or an aspartate as the fourth ligand. Based on the divergence of the active site, beta-CAs were further grouped into two subclasses, the plant type and the cab type [16].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 18 43 18 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 96 97 96 97 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n</underline>

###xml 290 292 290 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>

###xml 386 388 382 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 424 431 416 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NCE103 </italic>
###xml 519 521 511 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 572 574 561 563 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 615 617 604 606 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sup>
###xml 618 621 607 610 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1 </sup>
###xml 644 646 633 635 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">7 </sup>
###xml 647 650 636 639 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1 </sup>
###xml 651 653 640 642 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1</sup>
###xml 654 656 643 645 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 12 17 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 18 42 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
The budding yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae encodes a single copy of CA, termed Nce103 after the non-classical export pathway where it was identified for the first time [17]. Deletion of NCE103 leads to a growth-defect phenotype under aerobic conditions, but not under anaerobic conditions [18]. The DeltaNCE103 strain exhibits an enhanced sensitivity to H2O2 compared to the wild type [19]. However, the restoration of DeltaNCE103 is due to the recovery of carbonic anhydrase activity rather than antioxidant activity [18]. Nce103 is an efficient beta-CA who shows high CO2 hydrase activity, with a kcat of 9.4 x 105 s-1 and kcat/Km of 9.8 x 107 M-1 s-1[20].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 139 140 136 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
Nce103 contains all conserved residues of the active site in the plant type beta-CAs, and a relatively divergent N-terminal region (Figure 1). To verify if this N-terminal region is critical for the CA activity, we overexpressed and purified three versions of Nce103, full length, Nce103DeltaN13 and Nce103DeltaN50. Moreover, we determined the crystal structure of Nce103DeltaN13 at 2.04 A resolution. Structural analysis in combination with comparative activity assays revealed the N-terminal region comprising two alpha-helices is indispensable for controlling the substrate tunnel.
###end p 12

###begin title 14
Results and discussion
###end title 14
###begin title 15
Overall structure
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 275 276 275 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
We overexpressed, purified and crystallized the full length Nce103, but failed optimizing the crystal to diffraction quality. After performing a multiple sequence-alignment, we deduced the first non-conserved 13 residues of Nce103 might interfere the crystal packing (Figure 1). Thus, we constructed the truncated version Nce103DeltaN13 which enabled us to determine its crystal structure at 2.04 A resolution.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 83 93 76 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. sativum</italic>
###xml 106 108 99 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 111 123 104 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. purpureum</italic>
###xml 136 138 129 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 141 148 134 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 162 163 155 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>

###xml 252 265 245 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. neoformans</italic>
###xml 284 286 277 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 489 491 482 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 657 659 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 820 822 803 805 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 83 93 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">P. sativum</span>
###xml 111 123 <span type="species:ncbi:35688">P. purpureum</span>
###xml 141 148 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 171 184 <span type="species:ncbi:727">H. influenzae</span>
###xml 201 216 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 252 265 <span type="species:ncbi:5207">C. neoformans</span>
The structure of Nce103DeltaN13 resembles that of other plant type beta-class CAs (P. sativum, PDB code: [11]; P. purpureum, PDB code: [12]; E. coli, PDB codes: [3] and ; H. influenzae, PDB code: [5]; M. tuberculosis Rv3588c, PDB codes: [13] and [14], C. neoformans, PDB codes:  and [15]). It comprises all the conserved active site residues (Gln48, Phe75, and Tyr/Phe97) as found in the plant type CAs. The overall structure of Nce103 contains two molecules in an asymmetric unit (Figure 2A). The missing electron density of the N-terminal 6x His-tag, residues His14-Ser16 and Pro41-Gln48 of each subunit indicates their high degree of flexibility (Figure 2B). Similar to other beta-CAs, the Nce103 monomer consists of three components: an N-terminal arm, a conserved alpha/beta core and a C-terminal subdomain (Figure 2B). The N-terminal arm is composed of two alpha-helices that extend away from the rest of the molecule, making extensive contacts with the other molecule in the asymmetric unit. The alpha/beta core consists of a parallel four-strand beta-sheet ordered beta2-beta1-beta3-beta4, with a fifth anti-parallel strand beta5 connected by a short turn. The C-terminal subdomain contains three projecting alpha-helices running on the surface of the alpha/beta core.
###end p 17

###begin p 19
###xml 75 76 75 76 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 579 581 567 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
Two subunits form a very tight homodimer with a buried interface of 3,027 A2, bringing the two zinc binding pockets close to each other. The juxtaposition of the two beta2 strands at an angle of 120degrees results in the formation of an eight-strand beta-sheet running through the core of the homodimer. Via domain swapping, the N-terminal arm of one molecule reaches both the zinc binding core and the C-terminal subdomain of the second molecule in the asymmetric unit, creating a broad and deep groove. The overall structure of the homodimer is reminiscent of a saddle (Figure 2A).
###end p 19
###begin title 20
The zinc binding site
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 384 386 381 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3B</xref>
The active site is located at the inner side of the parallel beta-sheet, thus largely sequestered from the solvent. Each subunit contains a zinc ion that resides at the interface between two subunits, with three coordinating residues (Cys57, Cys115 and His112) from the same subunit, and a water molecule Wat263A/Wat260B as the fourth to complete the tetrahedral coordination (Figure 3B). The average coordination bonds of the two zinc ions are 2.29 +/- 0.05, 2.05 +/- 0.01, 2.32 +/- 0.07 and 2.12 +/- 0.05 A for Cys57Sgamma, His112Nepsilon, Cys115Sgamma and Wat263A/Wat260B, respectively. The four zinc ligands are stabilized by the surrounding residues via a number of hydrogen bonds: His112Ngamma~Thr113O (carbonyl oxygen); His112Nepsilon~Wat267O; Cys115Sgamma~Gly117N and Cys57Sgamma~Asp59N. In addition, two conserved residues Asp59 and Arg61 form two hydrogen bonds: Asp59Odelta1~Arg61NH1 and Asp59Odelta2~Arg61NH2.
###end p 21

###begin title 23
An acetate molecule in the substrate binding pocket
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 260 262 260 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3A</xref>
###xml 337 339 337 339 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 496 498 496 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3B</xref>
###xml 637 639 637 639 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3B</xref>
A hydrophilic tunnel that passes a bottleneck consisting of Phe97, Leu102, Gly111 and Asp59 is the only access to the active site of Nce103. In the substrate binding pocket, there is a flat trilobed piece of density, reminiscent of an acetate molecule (Figure 3A), which is an analog of the substrate bicarbonate as reported previously [11]. In both active sites of the two subunits, the acetate molecule is finely complementary to the pocket from both the viewpoints of shape and charge (Figure 3B). Moreover, the acetate is stabilized by five hydrogen bonds with the amide nitrogens of residue Gly117 and three water molecules (Figure 3B). On the surface of the homodimer, there are four flat but slightly longer pieces of electron density which are well fitted with four molecules of ethylene glycol. Both acetate and ethylene glycol were introduced during crystallization.

###begin title 25
Comparison between the active sites of Nce103 and beta-CAs from other species
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 80 91 77 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. sativum </italic>
###xml 111 119 108 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 142 156 139 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. neoformans </italic>
###xml 432 434 425 427 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4A</xref>
###xml 812 814 805 807 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4A</xref>
###xml 1146 1147 1139 1140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1256 1258 1249 1251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 80 90 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">P. sativum</span>
###xml 111 118 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 142 155 <span type="species:ncbi:5207">C. neoformans</span>
The crystal structure of Nce103 was superimposed on three beta-CA homologs from P. sativum (PSCA; PDB code: ), E. coli (ECCA; PDB code: ) and C. neoformans (Can2; PDB code: ), giving a root mean squared deviation (RMSD) of 1.58 A, 1.96 A and 1.95 A, respectively, for 197 Calpha-atoms. Moreover, the zinc-coordinating residues (Cys57, His112, Cys115) of Nce103 and those of PSCA (Cys160', His220', Cys267') superimpose well (Figure 4A). Besides in Nce103, an acetate molecule was also found in the active sites of PSCA and Can2, respectively. Compared to that in PSCA (or Can2), the acetate molecule (ACT) in Nce103 is far away from the zinc ion (3.53 vs 2.65 A). The fourth zinc ligand Wat263 (Wat260 in subunit B) of Nce103 occupies the position of ACT'-O1' in PSCA, putting ACT away from the zinc ion (Figure 4A). The distance between the acetate molecule and the zinc ion in Nce103 (3.53 A & 3.99 A) and that in subunit A in PSCA (2.65 A) are different, reminding us of the moving path of the substrate/product. In addition, a bicarbonate molecule was found in the noncatalytic binding site of ECCA, which is at the opposite of the zinc ion [5]. As another notable difference, the density map of the residues Pro41 to Gln48 in Nce103 is absent (Figure 2B), whereas the counterparts in most beta-CAs of known structure are well ordered. However, superposition of these structures indicated that the loop adopts different conformations.

###begin title 28
A proposed tunnel of substrate
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 344 346 344 346 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4B</xref>
###xml 442 444 442 444 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 541 543 541 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4C</xref>
###xml 749 751 749 751 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 1062 1064 1059 1061 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4D</xref>
###xml 1189 1191 1186 1188 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 1218 1219 1215 1216 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-</sup>
###xml 1418 1419 1412 1413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
Using the program CAVER, we added hydrogen atoms to the Nce103 structure and calculated a solvent accessible pathway starting from the acetate ion to the outside bulky solvent. The tunnel profile is a graph of cross section radius (radius of maximally inscribed ball) versus tunnel length measured from its deepest place to the outside (Figure 4B). We speculate that this calculated pathway can mimic the possible substrate diffusion tunnel [21]. The two tunnels in the homodimer point to the opposite directions towards the surface (Figure 4C). It is worth noting that this tunnel is funnel-shaped, widely open toward the solvent but has a narrow gorge (cross section radius ~2.1 A) in the vicinity of the acetate ion, suggesting that the linear CO2 (with a length of ~3.3 A) molecule can freely diffuse toward the active sites, consistent with the diffusion-control catalytic mechanism of beta-CAs. The gorge of the funnel is lined by several hydrophobic residues (Phe75, Phe97 and Leu102) on one side and polar residues (Asp59 and Arg61) on the other (Figure 4D). The distribution of hydrophobic and polar residues creates an ideal microenvironment for the diffusion of both nonpolar CO2 and negatively charged HCO3-. Furthermore, all these residues are highly conserved among beta-CAs from other species, suggesting a potential role of these residues in assisting the substrate approaching the active site (Figure 1).
###end p 29
###begin title 30
Activity assays indicated that the N-terminal arm of Nce103 is indispensable
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 25 48 25 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cryptococcus neoformans</italic>
###xml 140 142 133 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 423 425 416 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 562 563 555 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 25 37 <span type="species:ncbi:89924">Cryptococcus</span>
###xml 25 48 <span type="species:ncbi:5207">Cryptococcus neoformans</span>
In the pathogenic fungus Cryptococcus neoformans, the N-terminal domain of the beta-CA (Can2) is formed by four antiparallel alpha-helices [15]. It is reported that the N-terminal extension has a strong interaction with a surface groove which is located on the top of the active site of an adjacent monomer within the dimer. So, the N-terminal extension might mediate a regulation mechanism or protein/protein interaction [15]. The shorter N-terminal extension of Nce103 does not contain those residues necessary to interact with the active site in Can2 (Figure 1). To further validate the putative contribution of the N-terminal arm to the activity of Nce103, we constructed another truncated version of Nce103 which contains residues His51-Lys221, designated as Nce103DeltaN50. During purification, both Nce103DeltaN13 and Nce103DeltaN50 were not stable at pH 8.5 (data not shown), despite Nce103DeltaN50 remained dimeric in solution. Therefore, the Nce103 homodimer is mainly sustained by the interactions of the hydrophobic groups of the zinc binding core, instead of the swapped N-terminal arms.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 88 90 80 82 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 143 145 135 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 298 299 282 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
The activity of Nce103, Nce103DeltaN13 or Nce103DeltaN50 to catalyze the hydration of CO2 at pH 7.5 was determined using a colorimetric assay [22]. Nce103DeltaN13 shows almost the same activity as the full length Nce103, whereas the activity of Nce103DeltaN50 has been completely abolished (Figure 5). These results indicate that the first 50 residues are indispensable for the stability and activity of Nce103.
###end p 32

###begin title 34
Conclusion
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 114 128 114 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 114 127 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
Carbonic anhydrase is an important enzyme in inorganic carbon utilization. Here we provide the X-ray structure of S. cerevisiae beta-carbonic anhydrase Nce103 at 2.04 A resolution. The quaternary structure of Nce103 resembles the typical beta-CAs of known structure. Comparative structure analysis enabled us to find a possible moving path of the substrate analog acetate toward the active site, and to calculate a funnel-shaped tunnel for the diffusion of the substrate/product. Furthermore, the activity assays indicated the N-terminal arm is indispensable for the enzymatic activity.
###end p 35

###begin title 37
Cloning, expression and purification
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 30 45 30 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NCE103/YNL036W </italic>
###xml 96 110 96 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 330 334 330 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nco </italic>
###xml 340 344 340 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Not </italic>
###xml 421 438 415 432 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli Rossetta </italic>
###xml 589 596 582 589 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 nm </sub>
###xml 937 940 930 933 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
###xml 96 109 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 421 428 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The open reading frame of the NCE103/YNL036W gene was amplified by PCR using the genomic DNA of S. cerevisiae strain S288C as the template. An additional sequence coding for a six-histidine tag was introduced at the 5' end of the gene during PCR amplification. Then the PCR product was cloned into a pET29a-derived vector between Nco I and Not I restriction sites. Expression was done at 37degreesC using the transformed E. coli Rossetta (DE3) strain and 2x YT medium (OXOID LTD.) supplemented with 30 mug/ml kanamycin and chloramphenicol, respectively. When the cell culture reached an OD600 nm of 0.6, protein expression was induced with 0.2 mM IPTG (BBI) and the cells were grown for a further 4 hr. Cells were collected by centrifugation, resuspended in 30 ml buffer containing 200 mM NaCl, 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.5. Cells were lysed by three cycles of freezing/thawing followed sonication. His-tagged proteins were purified using a Ni2+ affinity column. Eluted protein was further purified by gel filtration using a Superdextrade mark 75 column (GE Healthcare Bioscience) equilibrated in 200 mM NaCl, 20 mM beta-mercaptoethanol and 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.5. The purity of the pooled fractions was checked by SDS-PAGE.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 14 21 14 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NCE103 </italic>
The truncated NCE103 without the sequence coding for the N-terminal 13 or 50 residues (Nce103DeltaN13 and Nce103DeltaN50) were amplified, respectively. PCR products were purified using the DNA gel extraction kit (V-gene, China) and inserted into pET29a-derived vector. The mutant proteins (Nce103DeltaN13 and Nce103DeltaN50) were overexpressed and purified as described above, except that Nce103DeltaN13 for crystallization was purified in 20 mM acetate buffer, pH 4.6.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
Crystallization of Nce103DeltaN13
###end title 40
###begin p 41
Crystals of Nce103DeltaN13 were obtained by the hanging drop vapor diffusion method at 16degreesC. In each drop of crystallization, 2 mul protein sample at 4 mg/ml in the buffer of 40 mM NaCl, 20 mM sodium acetate, pH 4.6 and 20 mM beta-mercaptoethanol was mixed with 1 mul reservoir solution (20% PEG4000, 0.1 M sodium citrate, pH 5.6, 0.1 M sodium acetate, 20% ethylene glycol) and equilibrated against 0.5 ml reservoir solution. Crystals at a maximal size of 100-200 mum appeared within 6 days.

Enzymatic activity measurements
