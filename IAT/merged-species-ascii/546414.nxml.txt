Modulation of epithelial sodium channel (ENaC) expression in mouse lung infected with Pseudomonas aeruginosa

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 34 57 34 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 445 446 434 435 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 448 449 437 438 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 34 56 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
###xml 88 93 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 146 151 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The intratracheal instillation of Pseudomonas aeruginosa entrapped in agar beads in the mouse lung leads to chronic lung infection in susceptible mouse strains. As the infection generates a strong inflammatory response with some lung edema, we tested if it could modulate the expression of genes involved in lung liquid clearance, such as the alpha, beta and gamma subunits of the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC) and the catalytic subunit of Na+-K+-ATPase.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 0 23 0 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 186 187 186 187 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 189 190 189 190 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 0 22 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
###xml 137 142 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Pseudomonas aeruginosa entrapped in agar beads were instilled in the lung of resistant (BalB/c) and susceptible (DBA/2, C57BL/6 and A/J) mouse strains. The mRNA expression of ENaC and Na+-K+-ATPase subunits was tested in the lung by Northern blot following a 3 hours to 14 days infection.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 685 686 656 657 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 688 689 659 660 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 691 692 662 663 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 31 36 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 878 883 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The infection of the different mouse strains evoked regulation of alpha and beta ENaC mRNA. Following Pseudomonas instillation, the expression of alphaENaC mRNA decreased to a median of 43% on days 3 and 7 after infection and was still decreased to a median of 45% 14 days after infection (p < 0.05). The relative expression of betaENaC mRNA was transiently increased to a median of 241%, 24 h post-infection before decreasing to a median of 43% and 54% of control on days 3 and 7 post-infection (p < 0.05). No significant modulation of gammaENaC mRNA was detected although the general pattern of expression of the subunit was similar to alpha and beta subunits. No modulation of alpha1Na+-K+-ATPase mRNA, the catalytic subunit of the sodium pump, was recorded. The distinctive expression profiles of the three subunits were not different, between the susceptible and resistant mouse strains.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusions
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 24 36 24 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
These results show that Pseudomonas infection, by modulating ENaC subunit expression, could influence edema formation and clearance in infected lungs.

Background
###end title 10
###begin p 11

###xml 404 405 393 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 406 407 395 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 426 427 415 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 428 430 417 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 456 458 445 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 459 461 448 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 470 472 459 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 489 491 478 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 524 526 513 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 714 716 703 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 717 719 706 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 747 749 736 738 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 852 854 841 843 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 855 857 844 846 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 997 999 986 988 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 1166 1168 1151 1153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 1173 1175 1158 1160 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 1240 1242 1225 1227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 1243 1245 1228 1230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 270 284 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus laevis</span>
###xml 1082 1086 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The epithelial sodium channel (ENaC) is expressed in epithelial cells of several tissues involved in salt and water reabsorption. The channel is composed of three related subunits (alpha, beta, gamma) that are able to reconstitute a functional channel when expressed in Xenopus laevis oocytes [1,2]. ENaC is expressed in a wide range of tissues, including the kidney [1,3-5], distal colon [1,3,5], lung [6-8], ear epithelium [9,10], papilla of the tongue [11-13], eyes [14], chondrocytes [15] and differentiating epithelia [16]. ENaC synthesis and activity are highly regulated by hormones, such as aldosterone, vasopressin and catecholamines, by intracellular pH, feedback inhibition and extracellular proteases [17,18]. In the lung, vectorial Na+ transport from the alveoli to the interstitium is the main force that drives water out of the alveoli [19,20]. This transport mechanism plays a crucial role late in gestation and at birth when sodium transport is involved in lung liquid clearance [21]. Its importance at birth has been shown unambiguously in alphaENaC gene knockout mice, where the inability to clear lung water rapidly leads to hypoxemia and death [22]. Na+ transport is also important in adults for lung liquid clearance [19,23].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 89 97 89 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 212 214 209 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 215 217 212 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 323 324 316 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 325 326 318 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 327 329 320 322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 373 375 366 368 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 376 378 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 467 469 460 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 470 472 463 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 765 767 758 760 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 768 770 761 763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 911 913 904 906 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 949 951 942 944 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 952 954 945 947 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 1105 1107 1098 1100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 1108 1110 1101 1103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 872 880 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Increased ENaC expression has been detected in the lung and in alveolar epithelial cells in vitro, following stimulation with steroids, beta-agonists, catecholamines, and agents that increase cAMP concentration [24-27]. alphaENaC expression in the lung is modulated at birth when considerable liquid clearance is required [3,6,27]. It is also upregulated during hyperoxia [28,29] and downregulated during hypoxia, which could explain high altitude lung edema (HALE) [30,31]. Several lines of evidence suggest that up-regulation or downregulation of ENaC activity in the lung could be associated with lung infection. In type I pseudohypoaldosteronism, a recessive genetic disease leading to a non-functional ENaC, susceptibility to lung infection has been reported [32-34]. Although ENaC is not the primary defect associated with cystic fibrosis (CF), airway cells from CF patients show a 2-3-fold increase in Na+ transport compared to normal cells [35,36]. This sodium hyperabsorption results from the inability of cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator (CFTR) in CF cells to downregulate ENaC activity [37,38].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 0 23 0 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 165 167 165 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 324 326 324 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 329 341 329 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 409 411 409 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 466 468 466 468 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 558 560 558 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 568 591 568 591 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 652 654 652 654 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 0 22 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
###xml 279 286 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">persons</span>
###xml 296 304 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 436 444 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 548 556 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 568 590 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
###xml 642 650 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is a bacterium occuring naturally in a wide range of environments such as in soil, fresh and seawater, plants and decomposing organic matter [39]. Although not usually pathogenic, this common bacterium can evoke opportunistic infections in immunodeficient persons, such as patients with severe burns [39]. Pseudomonas can promote nosocomial lung infection after artificial ventilation [40] and is also present in patients with bronchiectasis [39]. Chronic lung infections are the major cause of morbidity and mortality in CF patients [41] where Pseudomonas aeruginosa is the main source of chronic lung infection in CF patients [42].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 16 39 16 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 276 278 276 278 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 381 395 381 395 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 561 563 561 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 564 566 564 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 569 581 569 581 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 679 681 679 681 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 884 885 884 885 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 887 888 887 888 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 16 38 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
###xml 68 75 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbits</span>
###xml 338 343 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 381 394 <span type="species:ncbi:287">P. aeruginosa</span>
###xml 460 465 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Instillation of Pseudomonas aeruginosa in the lung of anaesthetised rabbits has been reported to promote acute pneumonia, resulting in alveolar epithelial injury, loss of epithelial barrier integrity, lung edema, pleural empyema and pleural effusions within 8 h of infection [43]. A more chronic pneumonia model has been developed in the mouse by the intratracheal instillation of P. aeruginosa entrapped in agar beads. In this model, the lungs of susceptible mouse strains develop severe lung infection with a strong inflammatory response and some lung edema [44,45]. Pseudomonas by itself has been shown to inhibit active sodium absorption in cultured airway epithelial cells [46]. Here, we studied its impact on the expression of genes involved in the modulation of liquid absorption in alveolar and airway epithelium, namely the three ENaC subunits and the catalytic subunit of Na+-K+-ATPase.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 0 12 0 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 194 196 179 181 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 198 199 183 184 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 201 202 186 187 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 125 130 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Pseudomonas entrapped in agar beads was instilled in the lung of resistant (BalB/c) and susceptible (DBA/2, C57BL/6 and A/J) mouse strains, and the expression of alpha, beta, gammaENaC and alpha1 Na+-K+-ATPase mRNA was studied by Northern blotting in lungs infected between 3 hours and 14 days.

###begin title 16
Methods
###end title 16
###begin title 17
###xml 23 36 23 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. aeruginosa</italic>
###xml 13 17 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 23 36 <span type="species:ncbi:287">P. aeruginosa</span>
Infection of mice with P. aeruginosa
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 23 37 23 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 184 186 183 185 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sup>
###xml 195 197 194 196 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sup>
###xml 343 345 342 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 346 348 345 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 23 36 <span type="species:ncbi:287">P. aeruginosa</span>
###xml 243 247 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Clinical strain 508 of P. aeruginosa (provided by Dr. Jacqueline Lagace, Universite de Montreal, Montreal, Canada) was entrapped in agar beads, and 50-mul suspensions containing 2 x 105 to 1 x 106 CFU/ml were instilled intratracheally in male mice of resistant (BALB/c) or susceptible (DBA/2, C57BL/6 and A/J) strains as described previously [44,45].
###end p 18
###begin title 19
Macrophage and polymorphonuclear (PMN) counts in bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL)
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 68 70 68 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 112 114 112 114 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 156 170 156 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 86 90 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 156 169 <span type="species:ncbi:287">P. aeruginosa</span>
BAL were performed as described elsewhere with a few modifications [44]. The infected mice were sacrificed by CO2 inhalation at different time points after P. aeruginosa instillation in the lungs. The trachea was cannulated, and the lungs were washed seven times with 1 ml PBS. Total cell counts were conducted in a hemacytometer. Differential cell counts were made by Diff-Quick staining (American Scientific Products) of Cytospin preparations. Number of animals: day 1, n = 6; day 4, n = 16; day 6, n = 6; day 14, n = 3.
###end p 20
###begin title 21
Northern blotting
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 184 186 184 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 461 462 460 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 543 545 542 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 666 667 650 651 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 669 670 653 654 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 672 673 656 657 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 855 856 831 832 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 1063 1064 1032 1033 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 1076 1077 1041 1042 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 1079 1080 1044 1045 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1082 1083 1047 1048 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1346 1348 1307 1309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 1496 1498 1453 1455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 1607 1609 1564 1566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 24 28 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 809 814 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 874 877 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1310 1313 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1419 1425 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 1584 1587 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1803 1807 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The lungs from infected mice were harvested between 3 h to 14 days after infection, homogenized in 5 ml of 4 M guanidine isothiocyanate, and centrifuged on a cesium chloride gradient [44]. Fifteen to 20 mug of total RNA purified from the lungs were electrophoresed on 1% agarose-formaldehyde gel and transferred to Nytran membranes (Schleicher & Schuell, Keene, NH, USA) by overnight blotting with 10 X SSC. Hybridization was performed, as reported previously [3], in Church buffer (0.5 M Na phosphate, pH 7.2, 7% SDS (w/v), 1 mM EDTA, pH 8) [47]. The nylon membranes were hybridized successively with different cDNA probes. (alphaENaC, betaENaC and gammaENaC, alpha1Na+-K+-ATPase, glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GADPH) or 18S rRNA). To detect alphaENaC mRNA, the blots were hybridized with 764-bp mouse alphaENaC cDNA (His-445 to stop codon) [3]. The probes for rat beta and gammaENaC cDNA were gifts from Dr. B.C. Rossier (Institut de pharmacologie et de toxicologie de l'Universite de Lausanne, Lausanne, Switzerland) and coded for the entire cDNA [2]. The alpha1Na+-K+-ATPase probe was a gift from Dr. J. Orlowski (Physiology Department, McGill University, Montreal, Quebec, Canada) and consisted of a NarI-StuI 332-bp fragment coding from nucleotide 89 to 421 (from the 5'UTR to Arg-61) of the rat kidney and brain alpha isoform [48]. For quantitative study, alphaENaC mRNA expression was normalized to murine GADPH with a 455 bp cDNA probe cloned between nucleotide 146 and 601 [44] or with 18S rRNA, using a 640-bp cDNA probe between nucleotidet 852 and 1492 of the rat 18S rRNA sequence [26]. The blots were exposed to Kodak Xar-film with an intensifying screen, or to a PhosphorImager (Molecular Dynamics, Sunnyvale, CA, USA) for densitometric analysis. Because different strains of mice were investigated in this study (BalB/c, DBA/2, C57BL/6 and A/J), the expression of the different mRNA was calculated at each time point as the % of expression relative to an untreated control from the same strain. The data from the different strains were pooled and subjected to statistical analysis.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
Between each round of hybridization, the membranes were stripped by treatment with 0.1 X SSC, 1% SDS and 2.5 mM EDTA at 95degreesC. The blots were allowed to cool gradually with agitation for 30 min at room temperature. The membranes were then rinsed with 5 X SSC and rehybridized. Number of animals: n = between 6 and 8 animals for each time point and each mRNA studied.
###end p 23

Results

###begin title 27
###xml 16 20 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 35 48 <span type="species:ncbi:287">P. aeruginosa</span>
Inflammation in mice infected with P. aeruginosa
###end title 27

Pseudomonas embedded in agar beads was administered intratracheally in resistant (BALB/c) and susceptible strains of mice (DBA/2, C57BL/6, A/J) as described previously [44,49]. alpha, beta and gammaENaC expression in infected lungs was measured by Northern blot hybridization (Fig. 2). Expression of the three subunits was highly modulated in time after lung infection, but showed a similar pattern between the four mouse strains tested. The BALB/c strain that is resistant to Pseudomonas infection [45], as well as the DBA/2, C57BL/6 and A/J susceptible strains, showed increased alpha, beta and gammaENaC expression at 24 h, followed by a decrease on day 3 post-infection. The GADPH standard gene did not manifest any modulation of its expression. Densitometric quantitative analyses of the Northern blots were performed for the four mouse strains. The relative expression at each time point was determined relative to uninfected animals of the same strain and the data from the 4 strains were pooled for analysis (Fig. 3). alphaENaC mRNA expression presented a significant decline to a median of 43% on days 3 and 7 post-infection, and was still decreased to a median of 45% on day 14 post-infection compared to uninfected controls (p < 0.05, Fig. 3). betaENaC mRNA expression was increased to a median of 241% of uninfected control values, 24 h post-infection (p < 0.05), and was followed by a decrease to medians of 42% and 54% on day 3 and 7 post-infection (p < 0.05) (Fig. 3). Although the expression of gamma ENaC mRNA showed an expression pattern very similar to the alpha and betaENaC subunits, with an increased expression at 24 h (median of 171%) followed by a decreased expression on day 3 (median of 53%) and 7 (median of 66%) of infection, these changes failed however to reach significance (Fig. 3). No modulation of alpha, beta or gammaENaC mRNA was detected when the lungs were instilled with agarose beads only (data not shown). We also investigated the expression of alpha1 Na+-K+-ATPase mRNA coding for the catalytic domain of the sodium pump, but could not find any significant change during infection (Fig. 3).

###begin title 34
Discussion
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 20 32 20 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 114 116 114 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 117 119 117 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 138 140 138 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 185 199 185 199 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 356 358 356 358 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 359 361 359 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 526 537 526 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas</italic>
###xml 565 567 565 567 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 616 628 616 628 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 669 671 669 671 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 790 792 790 792 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 813 825 813 825 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 934 936 934 936 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 1030 1032 1030 1032 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 1033 1035 1033 1035 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 1078 1090 1078 1090 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 1276 1287 1276 1287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas</italic>
###xml 1528 1539 1528 1539 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas</italic>
###xml 185 198 <span type="species:ncbi:287">P. aeruginosa</span>
###xml 248 253 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 341 346 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 550 555 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1346 1351 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1469 1474 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The instillation of Pseudomonas enmeshed in agarose beads in the lung is a good model to study lung inflammation [44,45] and lung injury [43] secondary to an infection. For this study, P. aeruginosa enmeshed in agarose beads was instilled into the mouse lung because the model allows the development of chronic lung infection in susceptible mouse strains [44,45]. The infection leads to cellular infiltration and alveolar edema that stand on day 3 post-infection and that can be still demonstrated on day 14 post-infection in Pseudomonas-susceptible mouse strains [45]. Because the lung inflammation associated with Pseudomonas infection is accompanied by lung injury [43], and because we have shown recently that ENaC expression can be modulated under conditions that promote lung injury [50], we tested here if Pseudomonas was affecting the mRNA expression level of the three ENaC subunits as well as the catalytic subunit of the Na+ pump since these elements are involved in lung liquid balance across the alveolar epithelium [19,23]. The results reported here indicate that Pseudomonas infection modulated the expression of the three ENaC mRNA with a characteristic pattern. There was no significant difference, however, in the expression profile of ENaC mRNA between the Pseudomonas-resistant (Balb/C) and -susceptible (DBA/2, C57BL/6, A/J) mouse strains. Modulation of ENaC expression is therefore most likely not a genetic marker linked to the susceptibility of mouse strains to establishment of a chronic infection with Pseudomonas.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 0 12 0 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 494 506 490 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 707 715 699 707 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 892 894 880 882 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 1027 1029 1011 1013 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 1255 1257 1239 1241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 836 841 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Pseudomonas infection affected ENaC mRNA with a pattern consisting of increased expression at 24 h, followed by a marked decrease on day 3 post-infection. The change in ENaC mRNA was related to bacterial infection, since agarose beads alone failed to evoke any modulation of these RNA. The three ENaC subunits were modulated with a similar profile, with some noticeable differences, however. Although alphaENaC mRNA expression tends to increase by day 1, the most noticeable feature brought by Pseudomonas infection to alphaENaC mRNA was the significant decreases after 3 days, 7 days and 14 days post-infection. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report demonstrating that bacterial infection in vivo can lead to modulation of ENaC mRNA expression. Recently, alphaENaC mRNA expression was found to be downregulated in the mouse lung after 7 and 14 days of adenoviral infection [51]. Furthermore, there is some evidence that alphaENaC expression is also decreased in other models of lung injury. Folkesson et al. [52] reported a decline in ENaC expression following subacute lung injury, 10 days after intratracheal administration of bleomycin. More recently, we recorded a decrease in ENaC expression after ischemia-reperfusion lung injury [50]. All these results, and the results reported in the present report, suggest that the modulation of ENaC expression associated with lung infection could be a widespread mechanism, not specific to a given pathogen or injury process, but a general response of the lung to inflammation and injury.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 44 56 41 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 340 342 322 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>

###xml 511 512 489 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 580 581 558 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 624 639 602 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus laevis </italic>
###xml 679 681 657 659 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 726 727 700 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 918 920 877 879 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 921 923 880 882 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 1220 1222 1175 1177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 1299 1301 1250 1252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 1347 1349 1295 1297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 1484 1486 1421 1423 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 1576 1578 1513 1515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
###xml 2023 2035 1956 1968 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 2245 2247 2170 2172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
###xml 2248 2250 2173 2175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B63">63</xref>
###xml 2319 2321 2244 2246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B63">63</xref>
###xml 2416 2418 2341 2343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>
###xml 624 638 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus laevis</span>
###xml 1106 1110 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1293 1297 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 2145 2149 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The beta ENaC subunit was also modulated by Pseudomonas infection. There was a significant increase in the mRNA expression on day 1 post-infection, followed, as for alphaENaC, by a decreased expression on day 3 and day 7 post-infection. Different stoichiometries have been proposed for ENaC. One model suggests a 2alpha, beta, gamma ratio [53,54] whereas others postulate an octomeric [55] or nonameric structure [55,56]. Although the expression of the alpha subunit alone is sufficient to allow ENaC activity [1], the three subunits are needed to get a fully functional channel [2]. The expression of the three subunits in Xenopus laevis oocytes increases amiloride-sensitive Na+ current by 100% compared to alphaENaC alone [2]. The alpha, beta and alpha, gamma channels are 20 times less effective in driving amiloride-sensitive current than the native channels and show differences in their biophysical properties [57,58]. Gene inactivation or over-expression of the different ENaC subunits has revealed important differences in the role each subunit plays in lung liquid management. alphaENaC knockout mice develop respiratory distress and die within 40 h from birth because of their inability to clear lung liquid [22]. Lung liquid clearance at birth is also slower in gammaENaC knockout mice [59], but is not affected in betaENaC knockouts [60]. Increased transgenic expression of betaENaC targeted in the airway epithelia, but not alpha or gamma subunits, showed an increase Na+ transport across the airway epithelium and a reduced height of the airway surface liquid [61]. For all these reasons, it is difficult to predict how the modulation of ENaC mRNA expression and its effect on the ratio of the three subunits, would have an impact on ENaC activity. In addition, ENaC mRNA content also does not necessarily reflect the amount of active channel at the membrane. One thing seems clear however, because of its prominence in the lung, the diminution of alphaENaC expression that we detected in the lung following Pseudomonas infection, could certainly influence amiloride-sensitive current and lung liquid clearance as in alphaENaC KO mice rescued by transgenic expression of alphaENaC that has a lower expression of ENaC in the lung [62,63]. In such model, there is a reduced ENaC current in tracheal cells [63], and a much slower lung liquid clearance following thiourea or hyperoxia-induced lung edema [64].
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 232 244 232 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 353 355 353 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B65">65</xref>
###xml 356 358 356 358 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B66">66</xref>
###xml 454 456 454 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B66">66</xref>
###xml 600 612 600 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 284 288 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 714 722 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The general biphasic modulation of ENaC mRNA expression with an increase at 24 h followed by a decrease thereafter is an interesting finding that could explain some contradictory reports concerning ENaC expression in lung following Pseudomonas infection. Acute bacterial pneumonia in rats has been shown to increase alveolar epithelial fluid clearance [65,66] when in late pneumonia, there is a decrease in the lung liquid clearance ability of the lung [66]. These contradictory results could be well explained by the modulation of ENaC expression reported here. The long term ENaC downregulation by Pseudomonas infection could be of potential clinical significance to understand the slow improvement in some ARDS patients.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 41 43 30 32 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 45 46 34 35 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>

###xml 716 717 693 694 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 970 972 943 945 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 974 975 947 948 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 977 978 950 951 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 987 999 960 972 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 1022 1024 995 997 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 1107 1108 1080 1081 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1110 1111 1083 1084 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
In contrast to alpha and beta ENaC, alpha1 Na+-K+-ATPase mRNA was unaffected in the course of lung infection. This is similar to what has been reported during adenovirus lung infection [51] where alphaENaC, aquaporin 1 (AQP1) and AQP5 mRNA show decreased expression, but not alpha1 Na+-K+-ATPase. In ischemia reperfusion injury, there was also no modulation of alpha1 Na+-K+-ATPase expression despite significant ENaC downregulation [50]. These results, as well as the data reported here, suggest that the inflammatory process seems to selectively affect, and not in a non-specific way, some elements of lung liquid clearance. It would be difficult at this time to speculate on the reasons for this modulation. Na+-K+-ATPase is an important element in lung liquid clearance, however, by being one of the key generator of membrane potential, the enzyme also affects other channels and ion transport process. It is possible that by modulating ENaC expression and not alpha1 Na+-K+-ATPase, Pseudomonas infection alters the Na+ transport system but does not change other important cell functions meditated by Na+-K+-ATPase. Furthermore, despite a similar mRNA expression level, there could be a fall in protein content or activity of the sodium pump. Additional experiments are necessary to answer this question.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 71 73 71 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B67">67</xref>
###xml 74 76 74 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B68">68</xref>
###xml 91 93 91 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B69">69</xref>
###xml 117 119 117 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B70">70</xref>
###xml 120 122 120 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B71">71</xref>
###xml 201 203 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B70">70</xref>
###xml 204 206 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B72">72</xref>
###xml 239 241 225 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B73">73</xref>
###xml 257 259 240 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B74">74</xref>
###xml 519 521 498 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 412 416 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Several studies report that in lung epithelial cells, viral infection [67,68], mycoplasma [69], bacterial infection [70,71], and inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha) [70,72], interleudin-1beta (IL-1beta) [73], or TGF-beta [74] decrease the expression of water channels, such as AQP1 and AQP5 and reduce the short circuit current generated by cells. Adenoviral lung infection in mice results in pulmonary inflammation and lung edema with lowered expression of AQP1, AQP5 and alphaENaC [51]. All these data, including the results presented here, suggest that lung inflammation, by decreasing the expression of alphaENaC and water channels, could hamper the liquid clearance ability of the lungs and favour edema formation.
###end p 40
###begin title 41
Conclusions
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 34 46 34 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 193 204 193 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas</italic>
###xml 423 435 419 431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 122 127 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We have shown in this report that Pseudomonas infection modulates ENaC mRNA expression. This modulation is independent of mouse strain susceptibility to establishment of chronic infection with Pseudomonas. Although there is an elevation of ENaC expression after 24 h, the most important feature is probably the long-lasting decrease of alphaENaC transcripts on days 3 and 7 post-infection. The lung inflammation induced by Pseudomonas infection therefore seems to favour a reduction in the expression of an essential element involved in lung liquid clearance as well as the regulation of airway surface liquid volume.
###end p 42
