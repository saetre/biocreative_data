###begin article-title 0
Pdx-1 or Pdx-1-VP16 protein transduction induces beta-cell gene expression in liver-stem WB cells
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
This is an open access article distributed under the terms of the Creative Commons Attribution License (), which permits unrestricted use, distribution, and reproduction in any medium, provided the original work is properly cited.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 32 37 32 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1</italic>
###xml 42 53 42 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1-VP16 </italic>
###xml 92 101 92 101 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 101 104 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Pancreatic duodenal homeobox-1 (Pdx-1) or Pdx-1-VP16 gene transfer has been shown to induce in vitro rat liver-stem WB cell conversion into pancreatic endocrine precursor cells. High glucose conditions were necessary for further differentiation into functional insulin-producing cells. Pdx-1 has the ability to permeate different cell types due to an inherent protein transduction domain (PTD). In this study, we evaluated liver-to-pancreas conversion of WB cells following Pdx-1 or Pdx-1-VP16 protein transduction.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 287 314 284 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1, Ngn3, Nkx2.2, Kir6.2</italic>
###xml 428 469 422 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3, NeuroD, Pax4, Nkx2.2, Nkx6.1, Pdx-1</italic>
###xml 495 518 486 509 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Glut-2, Kir6.2, insulin</italic>
WB cells were grown in high glucose medium containing Pdx-1 or Pdx-1-VP16 recombinant proteins for two weeks. beta-like cell commitment was analysed by RT-PCR of pancreatic endocrine genes. We found that WB cells in high glucose culture spontaneously express pancreatic endocrine genes (Pdx-1, Ngn3, Nkx2.2, Kir6.2). Their further differentiation into beta-like cells expressing genes related to endocrine pancreas development (Ngn3, NeuroD, Pax4, Nkx2.2, Nkx6.1, Pdx-1) and beta-cell function (Glut-2, Kir6.2, insulin) was achieved only in the presence of Pdx-1(-VP16) protein.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 85 94 85 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
These results demonstrate that Pdx-1(-VP16) protein transduction is instrumental for in vitro liver-to-pancreas conversion and is an alternative to gene therapy for beta-cell engineering for diabetes cell therapy.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 300 301 294 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
The difficulties encountered in obtaining sufficient supply of transplantable beta-cells is a major problem in cell therapy of type I diabetes. Liver may be a potential source of cells for beta-cell engineering. Indeed, liver and pancreas derive from the same endodermal region during embryogenesis [1] and hepatocytes and beta-cells share similar built-in glucose-sensing systems.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 185 186 182 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 187 188 184 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 311 319 302 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">insulin </italic>
###xml 342 351 333 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 415 421 406 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1 </italic>
###xml 506 507 497 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 508 509 499 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 563 578 554 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Herpes simplex </italic>
###xml 668 669 659 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 670 672 661 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 688 694 679 685 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1 </italic>
###xml 697 708 688 699 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1-VP16 </italic>
###xml 837 839 828 830 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 840 842 831 833 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 897 906 888 897 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 682 687 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 750 753 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Among transcription factors involved in pancreatic beta-cell specification, Pdx-1 plays a central role. All progenitors of the endocrine as well as the exocrine pancreas express Pdx-1 [2,3]. In the adult, Pdx-1 expression is mainly restrained to beta-cells where it regulates important beta-cell functions like insulin transcription. Several in vitro studies, using viral or stable plasmid gene transfer, show that Pdx-1 expression in hepatic cells results in reprogrammation into insulin producing cells [4-7]. Fusion of Pdx-1 to the VP16 activation domain from Herpes simplex virus (Pdx-1-VP16) leads to more efficient liver-to-pancreas conversion than Pdx-1 alone [8-12]. Stable mouse Pdx-1 or Pdx-1-VP16 gene transfection initiates conversion of rat epithelial liver stem-like WB cell line into pancreatic endocrine precursor cells [13,14]. In these cells, long-term high glucose (HG) culture in vitro is necessary for further pancreatic endocrine differentiation.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 397 399 397 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 400 402 400 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 557 559 557 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 661 669 661 669 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 417 422 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 493 496 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
Safety of gene therapy remaining a prime concern, protein transduction offers a more secure alternative to induce stem cell differentiation. Indeed, protein transduction domains (PTD) allow proteins to translocate across the cytoplasmic membrane. Due to an Antennapedia-like PTD in its structure, Pdx-1 protein can permeate different cell types and induces insulin expression in pancreatic ducts [15,16]. However, in human embryonic stem cells, the adjunction of a PTD domain derived from the HIV TAT protein (TAT) is necessary for Pdx-1 cell transduction [17]. Up to now, no study using protein transduction has achieved liver-to-endocrine pancreas conversion in vitro.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
Here, we evaluate if Pdx-1(-VP16) proteins have the capacity to induce a pancreatic endocrine shift in WB cells. To achieve this, we treat WB cells with Pdx-1 or Pdx-1-VP16 proteins, containing their own PTD, or fused to the PTD of TAT.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Methods
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Recombinant proteins synthesis
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 18 24 18 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1 </italic>
###xml 28 39 28 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1-VP16 </italic>
###xml 247 252 247 252 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1</sub>
###xml 529 530 529 530 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 12 17 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 126 129 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
Full-length mouse Pdx-1 and Pdx-1-VP16 open reading frames were cloned into pET28b-TAT-v2-1 expression plasmid containing the HIV TAT protein PTD (kind gift from S. Dowdy), in order to construct Pdx-1, Pdx-1-VP16, TAT-Pdx-1 and TAT-Pdx-1-VP16. PTDPdx-1-eGFP was constructed by fusing the PTD of Pdx1 (RHIKIWFQNRRMKWKK) to eGFP and subsequent cloning into pET28b-TAT-v2-1. TAT-eGFP was constructed by insertion of eGFP into pET28b-TAT-v2-1. eGFP was cloned into pET21a(+) expression vector (Novagen, WI, USA). See Additional data 1 for more details.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 84 86 84 86 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 117 120 117 120 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+ </sup>
Recombinant proteins were produced according to Studier's method of auto-induction [18]. Proteins were purified by Ni2+ affinity chromatography on Protino Ni-TED resin (Macherey-Nagel, France), diafiltrated on Centricon-Plus-20 centrifugal filter devices (Millipore, France), and stored at -20degreesC in PBS/pH8.0/25%glycerol.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Luciferase assay
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 146 150 146 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">luc2</italic>
###xml 94 97 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
The RIP2-reporter gene was constructed by cloning the [-683 bp, +11 bp] 5' flanking region of rat insulin-II gene into the EcoRV-site of pGL4.10 [luc2] (firefly luciferase) (Promega, France).
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 7 9 7 9 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sup>
###xml 96 97 96 97 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
18 x 103 HepG2 cells/well were seeded onto a 96-well plate and grown 24 h in 10% FCS DMEM (5% CO2, 37degreesC). 0,12 mug of RIP2-reporter and 0,13 mug of HSV-TK-hRluc control vector (renilla luciferase, pGL4-74, Promega) were co-transfected in these cells using JetPEI reagent (Polyplus-Transfection, France). 12 h after transfection, the medium was replaced with medium containing 5 muM protein or storage buffer for negative controls. 36 h later, cells were assayed for luciferase activities using Dual-Glo-Assay-System (Promega) and a scintillation counter (MicroBeta-Trilux, Wallac/Perkin-Elmer, France).
###end p 19
###begin title 20
WB cell culture and protein treatment
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 45 47 45 47 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 149 150 149 150 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
WB-F344 cells, kindly provided by N. Malouf [19], were grown in low glucose (LG) medium: 10% FCS RPMI-1640 (11 mM glucose, Invitrogen, France) (5% CO2, 37degreesC).
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 44 46 44 46 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sup>
###xml 175 180 174 179 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1</sub>
To evaluate transduction efficiency, 50 x 103 WB cells/well were seeded onto a 24-well plate. 24 h later, the medium was replaced with fresh medium containing 15 muM eGFP, PTDPdx-1-eGFP, or TAT-eGFP proteins and incubated for another 24 h before confocal microscopy analysis.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 53 55 53 55 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sup>
To evaluate effects of Pdx-1(-VP16) proteins, 25 x 103 WB cells/well (passage 15) were seeded onto a 96-well plate with medium adjusted to HG concentration (25 mM D-glucose, Sigma-Aldrich). 12 h later, the medium was replaced by HG medium containing 1 muM protein (Pdx-1 (n = 4), TAT-Pdx-1 (n = 4), Pdx-1-VP16 (n = 5), or TAT-Pdx-1-VP16 (n = 5)) or storage buffer for HG control cells (n = 5). The medium was replaced every 3-4 days. Control cells in LG medium (n = 5) were also grown. To favour differentiation rather than proliferation, WB cells were treated for 2 weeks without being trypsined. Then mRNAs were collected and analysed by RT-PCR. Each sample (n) represents one culture well.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Laser scanning confocal microscopy
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 174 178 169 173 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">exc </sub>
###xml 192 195 182 185 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">em </sub>
###xml 230 234 215 219 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">exc </sub>

###begin title 28
Results
###end title 28
###begin title 29
Pdx-1 and TAT PTDs transduced HepG2 and WB cells
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 13 18 13 18 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1</sub>
###xml 72 80 72 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 253 258 253 258 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1</sub>
###xml 317 319 317 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1a</xref>
###xml 392 397 392 397 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1</sub>
###xml 457 459 457 459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1b</xref>
###xml 547 549 547 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1b</xref>
###xml 706 711 706 711 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1</sub>
To assess PTDPdx-1- or TAT-mediated protein transduction in liver cells in vitro, we evaluated the ability of Pdx-1 and TAT PTDs to deliver fusion proteins into WB and HepG2 cells. To achieve this, we produced three recombinant eGFP fusion proteins: PTDPdx-1-eGFP, TAT-eGFP and eGFP alone as a negative control (Fig. 1a). Intracellular eGFP fluorescence was detected in cells treated with PTDPdx-1-eGFP or TAT-eGFP - as punctuated or diffused signals (Fig. 1b). No cellular uptake was detected in cells treated with eGFP protein lacking PTD (Fig. 1b). These results indicated clearly (i) that the efficiency of HepG2 and WB cell transduction was dependant on the presence of a PTD domain, and (ii) that PTDPdx-1efficiently delivered fusion proteins into cells.
###end p 30

###begin title 32
Pdx-1(-VP16) protein transduction increased insulin promoter activity
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 159 161 159 161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2a</xref>
###xml 282 284 282 284 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2b</xref>
###xml 600 606 597 603 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1 </sub>
###xml 164 167 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Rat</span>
In order to convert WB cells into insulin producing, cells we produced and purified four Pdx-1 proteins: Pdx-1, Pdx-1-VP16, TAT-Pdx-1 and TAT-Pdx-1-VP16 (Fig. 2a). Rat insulin-II promoter activity was significantly increased by treatment with any of the Pdx-1(-VP16) proteins (Fig. 2b). These results showed that these four Pdx-1(-VP16) proteins transduced hepatic cells and, in part, went to the nucleus and activated a beta-cell specific promoter. Fusion of TAT to these proteins, which consequently contain two PTDs in their structure, increased insulin promoter activity in an equal manner as PTDPdx-1 alone. Pdx-1 fusion to VP16 activation domain did not further enhance insulin promoter activity.

###xml 771 774 762 765 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GK </italic>
Taking advantage of the commitment of WB cells towards an endocrine phenotype in HG culture, Pdx-1(-VP16) protein treatment was performed on WB cells grown in HG medium. Proteins induced the expression of a wide panel of beta-cell genes after two weeks of culture: NeuroD, Pax4, Nkx6.1, insulin 1, Glut-2. Expression of Pancreatic Polypeptide (PP), a non-beta endocrine hormone, was also detected. Among different samples, some expressed all of these beta-cell genes (Ngn3, NeuroD, Pax4, Nkx2.2, Nkx6.1, Pdx-1, insulin 1, Glut-2, Kir6.2) and others only some of them. Although insulin 1 was expressed, insulin 2 expression was not induced by Pdx-1(-VP16) protein treatment. Glucokinase (GK) was not detected despite using primers amplifying pancreatic as well as hepatic GK isoforms.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
No pancreatic endocrine gene expression was observed at earlier time points (day 6 and 9 of treatment, data not shown).
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 19 27 19 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Albumin </italic>
The hepatic marker Albumin was absent in all conditions tested, including LG control cells.
###end p 40
###begin title 41
Discussion
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 152 160 149 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
In the present study, we demonstrate the aptitude of Pdx-1 or Pdx-1-VP16 protein transduction to reprogram hepatic stem-like cells into beta-like cells in vitro.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 556 558 556 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 612 614 612 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 615 617 615 617 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
First, we verified that the PTD of Pdx-1 and TAT allow transduction into hepatic WB and HepG2 cell lines. Intracellular localization of fusion proteins revealed by confocal microscopy analysis indicate a true uptake of proteins and not mere adherence to the cell surface. Furthermore, transduced recombinant Pdx-1, TAT-Pdx-1, Pdx-1-VP16 and TAT-Pdx-1-VP16 proteins exert biological activity on an insulin promoter reporter system. Pdx-1 fusion to VP16 activation domain does not further enhance insulin promoter activity consistent with previous findings [10]. Our results confirm reports of Pdx-1 transduction [15,20] and are the first demonstration of Pdx-1-VP16 protein transduction.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 164 165 164 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 166 167 166 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 168 170 168 170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 171 173 171 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 174 176 174 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 199 212 199 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1(-VP16) </italic>
###xml 524 544 524 544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3, Nkx2.2, Pdx-1 </italic>
###xml 548 554 548 554 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kir6.2</italic>
###xml 587 594 587 594 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al. </italic>
###xml 727 729 727 729 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 732 739 732 739 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kir6.2 </italic>
###xml 789 796 789 796 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kir6.2 </italic>
###xml 836 842 836 842 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1 </italic>
###xml 845 856 845 856 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1-VP16 </italic>
###xml 903 905 903 905 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 963 965 963 965 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 983 988 983 988 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pax4 </italic>
###xml 1003 1005 1003 1005 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 956 961 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Pancreatic differentiation experiments were conducted in an HG environment. In fact, long-term HG culture furthers liver cell commitment towards a pancreatic fate [4,6,13,14,21]. Previous studies of Pdx-1(-VP16) expression in WB cells do not distinguish between respective contributions of transgene expression and HG culture on differentiation. Here, we show that HG culture alone converts WB cells into pancreatic endocrine precursor cells. In contrast to LG cultures, HG cultures express four pancreatic endocrine genes: Ngn3, Nkx2.2, Pdx-1 and Kir6.2. These results concur with Yang et al. study, where confluent culture of hepatic oval stem cells for 2 months in HG medium induces conversion into insulin-producing cells [21]. Kir6.2 expression is at odds with previous reports where Kir6.2 was detected in WB cells overexpressing Pdx-1 or Pdx-1-VP16 genes only (i) after 2-3 months in HG culture [14], (ii) 40 days post-transplantation into diabetic mouse [13], or (iii) after Pax4 co-expression [22].
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 86 106 86 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NeuroD, Pax4, Nkx6.1</italic>
###xml 108 118 108 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">insulin 1 </italic>
###xml 122 129 122 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Glut-2 </italic>
###xml 703 710 703 710 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al. </italic>
###xml 854 856 854 856 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 972 978 972 978 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1 </sub>
###xml 1044 1054 1044 1054 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">insulin 1 </italic>
###xml 1058 1062 1058 1062 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pax4</italic>
###xml 1163 1172 1163 1172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 1206 1212 1206 1212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1 </italic>
###xml 1215 1226 1215 1226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1-VP16 </italic>
###xml 1268 1270 1268 1270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 1287 1297 1287 1297 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">insulin 1 </italic>
###xml 1317 1327 1317 1327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">insulin 2 </italic>
###xml 1331 1343 1331 1343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glucokinase </italic>
###xml 1455 1457 1452 1454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 1470 1475 1467 1472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3 </italic>
###xml 1554 1563 1551 1560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">insulin 1</italic>
Pdx-1, TAT-Pdx-1, Pdx-1-VP16, or TAT-Pdx-1-VP16 proteins further induce expression of NeuroD, Pax4, Nkx6.1, insulin 1 and Glut-2 after two weeks of treatment. Some samples express just a part of these markers or display RT-PCR expression patterns similar to HG controls. Quantitative analysis would help to further nuance pancreatic gene expression in-between these samples, in particular up-regulation of the endogenous Pdx-1 gene. The heterogeneity between samples may be a consequence of: (i) infrequent liver to endocrine pancreas conversion, leading to few pancreatic gene positive cells which may be difficult to detect, (ii) different kinetics of gene expression between wells. According to Tang et al. study, our experiments do not reveal more efficient differentiation following Pdx-1-VP16 protein treatment compared to Pdx-1 protein treatment [14]. TAT-mediated transduction does not lead to more advanced differentiation suggesting that containing two PTDs (PTDPdx-1 and TAT) does not increase transduction efficiency. Surprisingly, insulin 1 and Pax4, two of the pancreatic genes expressed after our protein treatments, are not detected in a previous in vitro study on WB cells transduced with Pdx-1 or Pdx-1-VP16 genes, even after 3 months of HG culture [14]. Here, despite insulin 1 expression, neither insulin 2 nor glucokinase are detected pointing at the possible need for long-term culture in HG medium to obtain mature beta-like cells [14]. Moreover, Ngn3 expression in wells scoring positive for all other pancreatic genes, including insulin 1, suggests the presence of remaining subpopulations of immature pancreatic precursor cells.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 18 24 18 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1 </italic>
###xml 27 38 27 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pdx-1-VP16 </italic>
###xml 123 124 123 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 125 126 125 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 127 128 127 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 129 131 129 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 132 134 132 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 227 229 227 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PP</italic>
###xml 242 251 242 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glucagon </italic>
###xml 383 390 377 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Amylase</italic>
###xml 488 490 482 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 405 408 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Overexpression of Pdx-1 or Pdx-1-VP16 genes in hepatic cells leads to exocrine as well as a range of endocrine cell types [4,7,8,13,14]. In our study, Pdx-1(-VP16) or TAT-Pdx-1(-VP16) protein treatments result in expression of PP, but not of glucagon suggesting that Pdx-1(-VP16) transduction in hepatic stem-like cells may lead to endocrine beta-cell and non-beta-cell phenocopies. Amylase, expressed in rat liver or exocrine pancreatic cells, is detected in all conditions tested here [23].
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 15 22 15 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al. </italic>
###xml 325 327 319 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 548 556 539 547 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 77 81 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 265 269 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Recently, Koya et al. demonstrated that Pdx-1 protein delivery into diabetic mice restores euglycemia mainly through pancreatic beta-cell regeneration. The authors observed beta-cell gene expression and insulin synthesis in the pancreas and in the liver of treated mice suggesting hepatic insulin contribution to euglycemia [20]. In complement, our findings provide the first direct evidence that Pdx-1(-VP16) protein transduction in conjunction with HG culture reprograms hepatic stem-like cells into cells displaying similarities with beta-cells in vitro. At a point where strategies for targeted beta-cell differentiation begin to surface, our study illustrates how simple exposition to Pdx-1(-VP16) protein in the surrounding medium triggers short-term pancreatic endocrine conversion. This study may contribute to the development of protein transduction therapy, a new concept to induce beta-cell differentiation.
