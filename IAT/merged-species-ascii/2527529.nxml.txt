Interaction of C-Terminal Truncated Human alphaA-Crystallins with Target Proteins
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: AK EA. Performed the experiments: AK. Analyzed the data: AK. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: AK. Wrote the paper: AK.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 254 259 243 248 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;172</sub>
###xml 283 288 268 273 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;168</sub>
###xml 299 304 280 285 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;162</sub>
###xml 44 49 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Significant portion of alphaA-crystallin in human lenses exists as C-terminal residues cleaved at residues 172, 168, and 162. Chaperone activity, determined with alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and betaL-crystallin as target proteins, was increased in alphaA1-172 and decreased in alphaA1-168 and alphaA1-162. The purpose of this study was to show whether the absence of the C-terminal residues influences protein-protein interactions with target proteins.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 364 369 360 365 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;172</sub>
###xml 426 431 414 419 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;168</sub>
###xml 452 457 436 441 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;162</sub>
###xml 543 548 520 525 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;172</sub>
###xml 602 607 571 576 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;168</sub>
###xml 618 623 583 588 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;162</sub>
###xml 710 715 671 676 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;172</sub>
###xml 792 797 745 750 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;168</sub>
###xml 841 846 786 791 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;162</sub>
Our hypothesis is that the chaperone-target protein binding kinetics, otherwise termed subunit exchange rates, are expected to reflect the changes in chaperone activity. To study this, we have relied on fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) utilizing amine specific and cysteine specific fluorescent probes. The subunit exchange rate (k) for ADH and alphaA1-172 was nearly the same as that of ADH and alphaA-wt, alphaA1-168 had lower and alphaA1-162 had the lowest k values. When betaL-crystallin was used as the target protein, alphaA1-172 had slightly higher k value than alphaA-wt and alphaA1-168 and alphaA1-162 had lower k values. As expected from earlier studies, the chaperone activity of alphaA1-172 was slightly better than that of alphaA-wt, the chaperone activity of alphaA1-168 was similar to that of alphaA-wt and alphaA1-162 had substantially decreased chaperone activity.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Cleavage of eleven C-terminal residues including Arg-163 and the C-terminal flexible arm significantly affects the interaction with target proteins. The predominantly hydrophilic flexible arm appears to be needed to keep the chaperone-target protein complex soluble.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 355 358 328 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-VanDerOuderra1">[1]</xref>
###xml 359 362 332 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-DeJong1">[3]</xref>
###xml 449 452 414 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Ingolia1">[4]</xref>
###xml 563 566 528 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Horwitz1">[5]</xref>
###xml 567 570 532 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Rao1">[7]</xref>
###xml 862 865 819 822 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Wistow1">[8]</xref>
The major proteins in the vertebrate eye lens are alpha-, beta-, and gamma-crystallins the predominant one being the alpha-crystallin. alpha-Crystallin consists of two nearly homologous subunits, namely, alphaA- and alphaB-crystallins and both having a molecular mass of 20 kDa in the monomer form and contain 173 and 175 amino acid residues respectively [1]-[3]. Both alphaA- and alphaB-crystallins belong to the class of small heat shock proteins [4] and function as molecular chaperones having the ability to prevent aggregation of partially unfolded proteins [5]-[7]. The model structure of alpha-crystallin consists of a globular N-terminal domain and a C-terminal domain containing an exposed C-terminal arm rich in hydrophilic amino acids, whereas the C-terminal stretch of 80-100 residues known as the 'alpha-crystallin/sHsp domain' are highly conserved [8].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 97 100 93 96 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Takemoto1">[9]</xref>
###xml 101 105 97 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Thampi1">[15]</xref>
###xml 234 237 226 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Takemoto1">[9]</xref>
###xml 239 243 231 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Takemoto2">[14]</xref>
###xml 245 249 237 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Thampi1">[15]</xref>
###xml 460 464 444 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Thampi1">[15]</xref>
###xml 471 476 455 460 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 476 480 460 464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Aziz1">[16]</xref>
###xml 654 659 630 635 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;172</sub>
###xml 774 779 746 751 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;168</sub>
###xml 895 900 855 860 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;162</sub>
###xml 1495 1499 1444 1448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Aziz1">[16]</xref>
###xml 1501 1505 1450 1454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Shroff1">[17]</xref>
###xml 165 170 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 328 333 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 593 598 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
C-terminal cleavage of alphaA-crystallin at residues 162, 168, and 172 has been reported earlier [9]-[15]. The major post-translational modification which occurs in human alphaA-crystallin is the loss of the C-terminal serine residue [9], [14], [15]. Enhanced cleavage of the C-terminal residue of alphaA-crystallin in diabetic human lenses has been reported in our earlier study, the average level of the truncated alphaA-crystallin increased from 30% to 50% [15]. Aziz et al[16] have recently reported modification in the oligomeric structure and chaperone function of the various truncated human alphaA-crystallins. Interestingly, the truncated alphaA1-172 exhibited significant increase in its oligomeric size as well as chaperone activity. The oligomeric size of alphaA1-168 was similar to that of alphaA-wild type (alphaA-wt) whereas the chaperone activity was moderately decreased. alphaA1-162, on the other hand, showed substantial decrease in the oligomeric size as well as the chaperone activity. If indeed chaperone to target protein binding is an essential step for alphaA-crystallin to operate as a molecular chaperone, characterization of the interaction of the truncated alphaA-crystallins with target proteins should show why their chaperone function is altered as a result of the truncation. In this study, we have used fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) to study chaperone - target protein interaction using ADH and betaL-crystallin, two widely used target proteins [16], [17], and recombinant alphaA-wt and C-terminal truncated alphaA-crystallins.

###xml 891 896 868 873 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;168</sub>
###xml 904 909 877 882 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;162</sub>
###xml 429 434 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The level of subunit exchange between two different proteins was determined by FRET. In order to determine the in vitro FRET level two fluorescent dyes with overlapping fluorescence spectra are necessary. SITS and LYI are widely used fluorescent dyes with spectral overlaps and safe from structural alterations due to fluorescent tags [18]-[20]. So, in the present study the amine specific fluorescent probe SITS was attached to human alphaA-wt and the C-terminal truncated alphaA-crystallins and the cysteine specific LYI fluorescent probe was attached to the target proteins ADH and betaL-crystallin. The level of labeling was determined spectrophotometrically using molar extinction coefficients of 47,000 mol-1 cm-1 at 336 nm for SITS and 11,000 mol-1 cm-1 at 426 nm for LYI. The level of labeling was about 0.8, 1.12, 1.17, 1.04 and 7.1 and 1.56 moles for alphaA-wt, alphaA1-172, alphaA1-168, alphaA1-162, ADH and betaL-crystallin respectively. Differences in the level of labeling of the two different fluorophores were taken into account while computing the data.

The interaction between the SITS -labeled human alphaA-wt and the C-terminal truncated alphaA-crystallins and the LYI- labeled target protein ADH was initiated by mixing equimolar concentration of the proteins in 20 mM MOPS buffer with 100 mM NaCl and 10 mM EDTA at 37degreesC. EDTA was used for unfolding ADH so that alphaA-crystallin will bind to ADH under the same condition as used for the chaperone assay. The rate of subunit exchange with ADH was determined by FRET analysis. Figure 1 shows the fluorescence spectra showing the time dependent decrease in SITS emission intensity at 426 nm and a concomitant increase in LYI fluorescence at 515 nm. After 30 min at 37degreesC, there was no remarkable change in the emission intensity at 515 nm due to the achievement of stable equilibrium (data not shown). The fluorescence emission spectra illustrate that the highest increase in the acceptor spectrum at 515 nm was observed in alphaA-wt when compared to the C-terminal truncated alphaA-crystallins (Fig. 1). We have calculated the rate of subunit exchange from the increase in acceptor fluorescence intensity after taking into account differences in the levels of tagging of the various proteins by the probes. Figure 2A shows the plot of Ft/ F0 of LYI at 515 nm as a function of time, where Ft and F0 are the emission intensities at time t and zero respectively. The rate constant was obtained by fitting the data to the exponential function Ft/F0 = A1+A2 e-kt, where A1 and A2 are constants and k is the rate constant for subunit exchange. The increase in the relative fluorescence intensity at 515 nm is due to fluorescence resonance energy transfer from donor SITS-labeled human alphaA-wt and C-terminal truncated alphaA-crystallins' fluorophore to the acceptor LYI-labeled ADH during the interaction. The maximum relative fluorescence intensity was seen with alphaA-wt whereas alphaA 1-172 had lower fluorescence intensity, alphaA1-168 showed further decrease in the fluorescence intensity, and alphaA1-162 showed the lowest. The subunit exchange rates or the k values are summarized in Table 1 which is in agreement with the above observation. For instance, alphaA-wt had the highest k value (2.073) whereas the k values of alphaA1-172, alphaA1-168, and alphaA1-162, respectively, were 6, 24, and 43% lower. Figure 2B illustrates the decrease in the relative fluorescence at 426 nm when the fluorescence resonance energy is transferred to the acceptor target protein. The rate constant (k) values (Table 1) were higher when collected and calculated at donor energy (at 426 nm) than when calculated at acceptor energy (at 515 nm). This is believed to be was due to fluorescence quenching because no direct transfer of fluorescence energy occurs where there is no sufficient contact between the residues carrying the two probes. The amount of loss due to quenching is comparatively lower in both alphaA-wt and alphaA1-172 than in alphaA1-168 and alphaA1-162.

###begin title 45
Discussion
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 186 190 186 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Aziz1">[16]</xref>
###xml 731 736 719 724 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1&#8211;172</sub>
We have shown earlier that cleavage of 1, 5, and 11 C-terminal residues affects or improves chaperone activity depending on the number of residues cleaved and the chaperone assay system [16]. Chaperone activity of the truncated alphaA-crystallins can vary by two major factors: accessibility to the chaperone sites which are responsible for the chaperoning process or due to enhanced or decreased affinity to the substrate which is being unfolded. The present study focused on the latter aspect by studying the binding of the wild-type and the truncated alphaA-crystallins with the putative target proteins. The data show that the binding kinetics (k value) reflect the chaperone activity differences with the exception that alphaA1-172 showed higher chaperone activity, but, lower k value. Thus, we can conclude that the binding affinity to target proteins does not always concur with the chaperone activity data. However, it is noteworthy that all the three truncated alphaA-crystallins have shown decreased target protein binding.

###xml 265 270 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In an earlier study, we have determined the oligomeric size, secondary and tertiary structures and chaperone activity of recombinant human alphaA-wt and the various C-terminal truncated alphaA-crystallins [16]. alphaA1-172, which is the major form of the truncated human alphaA-crystallin, had a molecular mass of 866 kDa as compared to 702 kDa for alphaA-wt, when the molecular mass was determined by dynamic light scattering. The chaperone activity of alphaA1-172 was higher than that of alphaA-wt, when ADH, insulin and betaL-crystallin were used as target proteins and the alphaA-crystallin : target protein ratio varied between 1:1 and 1:20. In this study, we have used equal amounts of alpha-crystallin and target protein, ie, 1:1 ratio, in all the assays to avoid artificially exaggerated differences between alphaA-wt and the truncated alphaA-crystallins. As shown in Figures 5 and 6, alphaA1-172 had slightly better chaperone activity than alphaA-wt, which confirms our previous observation [16]. However, alphaA-crystallin-target protein binding studies gave conflicting results. For instance, the relative fluorescence intensity due to FRET and the k values were lower in alphaA1-172 (Figs. 2 & 4; Tables 1 & 2). Both the present study and the earlier study [16] have shown alphaA1-168 having nearly the same chaperone activity as alphaA-wt. however, the FRET studies gave contradicting results as the relative fluorescence intensity as well as the k values were significantly lower (Figs. 2 & 4; Tables 1 & 2). With both the ADH and the betaL-crystallin target protein methods, alphaA1-162 showed the lowest chaperone activity (Figs. 5 & 6) as shown earlier. Interestingly, alphaA1-162 also showed the lowest level of relative fluorescence intensity and k value during FRET studies. Thus, the cleavage of 11 C-terminal residues of alphaA-crystallin, which is known to affect its secondary and tertiary structures [16], severely affects its binding to target proteins which in turn affect its ability to function as a molecular chaperone.

###xml 881 885 833 837 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Aziz1">[16]</xref>
###xml 962 966 910 914 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003175-Shroff2">[21]</xref>
###xml 107 112 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
By using FRET analysis, we have recently investigated the effect of cleavage of the C-terminal residues of human alphaA-crystallin on subunit exchange with alphaB-crystallin forming heteroaggregates [21]. The subunit exchange rate or the k value for alphaA1-172 interacting with alphaB-wt was decreased by 50% compared to alphaA-wt interacting with alphaB-wt. Likewise, the k value was decreased 40% when alphaA1-168 interacted with alphaB-wt, whereas interaction of alphaA1-162 with alphaB-wt showed 84% decrease in the k value. Thus, cleaving 11 residues including lysine-163 had shown the most effect. The importance of lysine-163 in maintaining the oligomeric structure of alphaA-crystallin has been demonstrated in a recent study [23]. In fact, in alphaA1-162 the secondary and tertiary structures were significantly altered and the molecular mass was substantially decreased [16]. Such changes affect binding to alphaB-crystallin and heterooligomerization [21] and also seem to affect binding to target proteins decreasing the chaperone activity.
