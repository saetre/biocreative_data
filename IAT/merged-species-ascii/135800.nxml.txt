The fission yeast pfh1+ gene encodes an essential 5' to 3' DNA helicase required for the completion of S-phase

###xml 80 93 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 94 119 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
###xml 542 566 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 775 782 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 873 898 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
The Cdc24 protein plays an essential role in chromosomal DNA replication in the fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe, most likely via its direct interaction with Dna2, a conserved endonuclease-helicase protein required for Okazaki fragment processing. To gain insights into Cdc24 function, we isolated cold-sensitive chromosomal suppressors of the temperature-sensitive cdc24-M38 allele. One of the complementation groups of such suppressors defined a novel gene, pfh1+, encoding an 805 amino acid nuclear protein highly homologous to the Saccharomyces cerevisiae Pif1p and Rrm3p DNA helicase family proteins. The purified Pfh1 protein displayed single-stranded DNA-dependent ATPase activity as well as 5' to 3' DNA helicase activity in vitro. Reverse genetic analysis in S.pombe showed that helicase activity was essential for the function of the Pfh1 protein in vivo. Schizosaccharomyces pombe cells carrying the cold-sensitive pfh1-R20 allele underwent cell cycle arrest in late S/G2-phase of the cell cycle when shifted to the restrictive temperature. This arrest was dependent upon the presence of a functional late S/G2 DNA damage checkpoint, suggesting that Pfh1 is required for the comple tion of DNA replication. Furthermore, at their permissive temperature pfh1-R20 cells were highly sensitive to the DNA-alkylating agent methyl methanesulphonate, implying a further role for Pfh1 in the repair of DNA damage.

###begin title 4
INTRODUCTION
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 118 142 118 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>

###xml 118 142 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>

###xml 302 308 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeasts</span>
###xml 473 480 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
The study of chromosomal DNA replication in eukaryotic cells has greatly benefited from the amenability of the yeasts Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Schizosaccharomyces pombe to genetic analysis. Collections of conditional-lethal cell division cycle (cdc) mutants generated by random mutagenesis of both yeasts have played an important role in facilitating the identification of a large number of protein factors essential for different stages of the replication process. In S.pombe, for example, genes encoding all three essential replicative DNA polymerases have been identified in this way (reviewed in 1).
###end p 5

###xml 3 10 3 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>

###xml 3 10 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
In S.pombe the cdc24-M38 mutant was initially isolated as one of the cdc mutants (2). Cells carrying this temperature-sensitive mutation halt cell cycle progression when shifted to the restrictive temperature, becoming arrested in late S-phase with incompletely replicated DNA (2-4). The cdc24+ gene encodes an essential 501 amino acid protein with no apparent homologs in other species (3,4).
###end p 6

###xml 320 321 308 309 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 326 330 314 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pcn1</italic>

###xml 385 389 373 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdm1</italic>
###xml 389 390 377 378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 389 390 377 378 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 498 507 482 491 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc24-M38</italic>
###xml 518 519 502 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c7">7</xref>
Two lines of evidence indicate possible interactions between Cdc24 and DNA polymerase delta (Pol delta). First, Cdc24 physically and genetically interacts with two accessory proteins for DNA polymerase delta: the large subunit of replication factor C (RF-C) and proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA), encoded by rfc1+ and pcn1+, respectively (4-6). Secondly, overexpression of the cdm1+ gene, encoding a non-essential subunit of Pol delta, suppresses the temperature-sensitive lethality of the cdc24-M38 mutation (7).
###end p 7

###xml 259 266 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 306 313 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>

###xml 499 504 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The dna2+ gene was also identified as a multicopy suppressor of a temperature-sensitive cdc24 mutant (3). dna2+ interacts genetically, not only with cdc24+, but also with cdc1+ and cdc27+ (encoding the essential small subunits of Pol delta), cdc17+ (encoding S.pombe DNA ligase I), and rad2+ (encoding the S.pombe homolog of the nuclease Fen-1) (8). Fen-1 is required for the Okazaki fragment processing in the mammalian SV40 in vitro DNA replication system (9). Dna2 binds to Cdc24 strongly in the yeast two-hybrid assay system (8). This helicase-endonuclease is required for DNA replication, but not for the bulk DNA synthesis (8). This phenotype is the same as those of cdc24 and cdc17.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 50 52 50 52 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c10">10</xref>
###xml 53 55 53 55 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c11">11</xref>
###xml 139 141 139 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c12">12</xref>
###xml 152 160 152 160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 281 283 281 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c13">13</xref>
###xml 284 286 284 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c14">14</xref>
###xml 300 302 300 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c15">15</xref>
###xml 66 71 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In addition to having weak DNA helicase activity (10,11), budding yeast Dna2p also has single-stranded DNA-specific endonuclease activity (12). Indeed, in vitro studies have suggested that Dna2 acts as a nuclease in Okazaki fragment maturation during lagging strand DNA synthesis (13,14, reviewed in 15). Taken together, these genetic and biochemical results suggest the most likely function of Cdc24 is in the process of Okazaki fragment maturation, perhaps as a regulator of Dna2 activity, or as an adaptor molecule, linking Dna2 to Pol delta.
###end p 9

###xml 125 127 125 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c16">16</xref>

###xml 1315 1317 1315 1317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c29">29</xref>
###xml 1413 1415 1413 1415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c29">29</xref>
###xml 1615 1617 1615 1617 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c26">26</xref>
###xml 1713 1725 1713 1725 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 144 149 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 700 712 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 1599 1604 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1608 1613 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>

DNA helicase molecules play important roles in various DNA transactions including DNA replication, recombination and repair (16-18). In budding yeast, in addition to Dna2p, at least three other DNA helicases appear to be involved in DNA replication. The MCM helicase complex has DNA helicase activity and is proposed to act by unwinding duplex DNA at replication origins (19). In addition, this complex moves with replication fork after initiation of DNA synthesis (20), and is also required for the elongation phase of DNA synthesis (21). The MCM protein complex is therefore a good candidate for the replicative helicase that unwinds DNA duplex ahead of the moving replication fork. Two additional S.cerevisiae DNA helicases, Pif1p and Rrm3p, are also likely to have functions in the DNA replication (reviewed in 22). Pif1p is 5' to 3' DNA helicase (23,24) that functions both in the nucleus and in mitochondria. In the nucleus, Pif1p inhibits both de novo telomere formation and telomere elongation (25,26) and in this way protects cells against gross chromosome rearrangements (27). In the mitochondria, Pif1p is involved both in mtDNA repair and recombination (23,28). Rrm3p, a putative DNA helicase protein highly homologous to Pif1p, is suggested to be a replicative DNA helicase specific for ribosomal DNA (29). Interestingly, Pif1p and Rrm3p have opposite functions in replication fork movement at rDNA (29). As a result Pif1p and Rrm3p are thought to have similar substrate specificities but to exert different effects on them. The Pif1p/Rrm3p subfamily of DNA helicases is conserved from yeast to human (26). However there are no reports of detailed analysis of Pif1p subfamily helicases other than in S.cerevisiae.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 36 43 36 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 43 47 43 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 47 48 47 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 47 48 47 48 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 161 170 161 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc24-M38</italic>
###xml 282 294 282 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 568 575 568 575 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 282 294 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 568 575 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
Here we report the isolation of the S.pombepfh1+ gene, which encodes a DNA helicase homologous to Pif1p subfamily DNA helicases, as a suppressor mutation of the cdc24-M38 mutant. We present evidence that Pfh1 DNA helicase is essential for cell viability (in contrast to the case of S.cerevisiae homologs Pif1p and Rrm3p) and that Pfh1 is required for cell cycle progression in late S-phase and for the proper response to DNA-damaging agents. These results lead us to propose that Pfh1 performs essential functions in both chromosomal DNA replication and DNA repair in S.pombe.

###begin title 33
Purification of the recombinant Pfh1 protein
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 0 16 0 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 345 350 331 336 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600nm</sub>

The preparative medium (500 ml of LB, 50 microg/ml ampicillin) was inoculated with 1 ml of the seed culture. The column was washed with 10 ml of buffer T300, and eluted with a linear gradient (300-700 mM) of NaCl in buffer T (15 ml). Fractions containing the DNA-dependent ATPase activity were pooled and concentrated 10-fold. Aliquots (0.4 ml) were loaded onto a Superdex 200 HR 10/30 column (24 ml; Pharmacia Biotech). ###begin title 35
Preparation of helicase substrates
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 229 231 227 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c40">40</xref>

DNA substrates used to measure helicase activity of Pfh1 were constructed by hybridizing an oligonucleotide (5'-CGG ACG CTC GAC GCC ATT AAT AAT GTT TTC-3') to PhiX174 single-stranded circular (ssc) DNA and purified as described (40). To analyze Pfh1 function by using pfh1 mutant cells, we first generated pfh1-R20 and pfh1-R23 single mutants by genetic crossing and tetrad dissection. These two single pfh1 mutants were still cold sensitive for growth (Fig. 1). To analyze the phenotype of these mutants, exponentially growing cells at 34degreesC were shifted down to 18degreesC and analyzed by DAPI staining. To investigate whether pfh1+ is required for the completion of S-phase, we attempted to analyze the phenotype of a pfh1rad1 double mutant. Rad1 is required for DNA replication/damage checkpoint function (41,42): the rad1-1 mutant fails to arrest the cell cycle when DNA is unreplicated or damaged. If pfh1+ is required for the completion of DNA replication, pfh1 mutant cells would be expected to enter mitosis when combined with the rad1-1 mutation. To test this, we generated pfh1-R20 rad1-1 double mutants by tetrad analysis. However, we found that pfh1-R20 rad1-1 cells grew very slowly, forming only microcolonies at 34degreesC. These cells were streaked on YE plates and incubated at 18degreesC for 24 h and examined by DAPI staining. Cells displaying nuclear abnormalities suggestive of inappropriate entry into mitosis were frequently observed (Fig. 2C). These phenotypes were also observed in cells grown even at a permissive temperature of 34degreesC. Similar results were obtained from the analysis of pfh1-R23 rad1-1 cells (data not shown). These results strongly suggest that pfh1+ is required for S-phase completion. To investigate this further, we constructed pfh1 double mutants with Deltachk1 and Deltacds1 mutants (43-45). Both Chk1 and Cds1 are downstream kinases of the DNA replication/damage checkpoint pathway. By the analysis of these mutant cells at 18degreesC, we found that checkpoint arrest required Chk1 but not Cds1: elongated cells were not observed in pfh1-R20 Deltachk1 or in pfh1-R23 Deltachk1, whereas pfh1-R20 Deltacds1 and pfh1-R23 Deltacds1 cells were elongated at 18degreesC (data not shown). This result suggested that loss of Pfh1 function triggers a chk1+-dependent late S/G2 DNA damage checkpoint pathway. Similar checkpoint dependencies were reported with other S-phase cell division cycle mutants, such as pol3 (46) and cdc17 (43-45).

To analyze further the function of pfh1+, we tested the sensitivity of pfh1-R20 and pfh1-R23 mutants to the DNA replication inhibitor HU and the DNA-damaging agents MMS and UV. This was achieved by spotting cells onto YE plates containing HU or MMS, or by irradiating cells spotted onto YE plates with UV. There are two possible explanations for the sensitivity to HU. The mutant cells may be inherently checkpoint defective, i.e. entry into mitosis may result in the presence of HU, with lethal consequences. Alternatively, the cells may arrest normally in HU, but be unable to recover from the arrest to resume normal growth. In order to distinguish between these possibilities, we exposed pfh1 mutant cells to HU transiently and investigated their viability and morphology over a period of time (Fig. 3A-C). As shown in Figure 3A, cell number increase in the pfh1-R20 and pfh1-R23 cultures ceased following HU exposure: the elongated morphology of pfh1-R20 and pfh1-R23 cells at 6 h incubation in HU indicated that checkpoint function was intact in these cells, in contrast to rad1-1 cells which showed cut phenotype without cell elongation (Fig. 3C). These results indicated that HU sensitivity of pfh1 mutant cells arose not from a checkpoint defect but from a problem in the recovery process.

To isolate the pfh1+ gene, both S.pombe genomic DNA and SV40 promoter-driven cDNA libraries were screened for clones that could suppress the cold sensitivity of pfh1-R20 and pfh1-R23 cells. We isolated three cDNA clones and one genomic clone that rescued the mutants (Fig. 4A). All three cDNA clones contained the same ORF, and this ORF was also present in the genomic DNA clone. The ORF was identical to a gene located on the S.pombe chromosome II cosmid clone SPBC887 (EMBL accession no. AL033388) at nucleotides 28298-30756. DNA sequencing revealed that the pfh1+ gene encodes an 805 amino acid protein with a calculated molecular mass of approximately90 kDa. To determine the nature of the mutation in pfh1-R20 and pfh1-R23, these alleles were amplified by PCR. One feature of the pfh1+ gene is the presence of two MluI cell cycle box (MCB) sequences 382 and 637 bp upstream of the start of the pfh1+ ORF. To examine the expression of pfh1+ during the cell cycle, we synchronized S.pombe cells by the cdc25-22 block and release method (Fig. 6A). RNA samples were prepared every 20 min following return to the permissive temperature and analyzed by northern hybridization using probes specific for pfh1+, cdc18+ (previously shown to peak at the G1/S boundary) and ura4+ (known to remain constant through the cell cycle). ###begin title 70
ATPase and helicase activities of Pfh1
###end title 70
###begin p 71
###xml 109 110 109 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f8">8</xref>

With purified wild-type NusA-Pfh1, we were able to detect ssDNA-dependent ATPase activity as shown in Figure 8A. The amount of ATP hydrolyzed was proportional to the amounts of enzyme added, resulting in 233, 625 and 1505 pmol of ATP hydrolysis with 30, 90 and 270 ng, respectively, of enzyme. We next examined whether NusA-Pfh1 possesses helicase activity and found that wild-type NusA-Pfh1 was able to unwind the 30 bp partial duplex DNA present in PhiX174 sscDNA, whereas the mutant NusA-Pfh1K338E failed to do so. Since the S.cerevisiae Pif1p helicase unwinds DNA in the 5' to 3' direction (23,24), we tested whether S.pombe Pfh1 unwinds duplex DNA in the same direction as Pif1p. As shown in Figure 8C, NusA-Pfh1 utilized a partial duplex substrate with a 5' tail only, but not the substrate with a 3' tail, demonstrating that Pfh1 translocates and unwinds duplex DNA in the 5' to 3' direction like S.cerevisiae Pif1p. It is noteworthy that unwinding by Pfh1 was more efficient with oligonucleotide-based substrates than with PhiX174-based substrate. For example, >80% of substrate was unwound with 30 ng of Pfh1 (Fig. 8C) with oligonucleotide-based substrate, while >270 ng of Pfh1 was required for comparable unwinding of the PhiX174-based substrate (Fig. 8B). This is likely to be due to the presence of bulk ssDNA in PhiX174 sscDNA that may sequester a large fraction of enzyme.
###end p 72
###begin title 73
###xml 50 57 50 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
Catalytic activity is essential for Pfh1 function in vivo
###end title 73
###begin p 74
###xml 58 65 58 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 235 243 235 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1-R20</italic>
###xml 330 331 330 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f4">4</xref>
###xml 393 401 393 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1-R20</italic>
###xml 461 470 457 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1/pfh1</italic>
###xml 470 471 466 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 470 471 466 467 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 653 657 645 649 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
To examine the essentiality of catalytic activity of Pfh1 in vivo, we constructed plasmid pREP81-pfh1K338R expressing Pfh1-K338R, which contains a mutation in the ATP-binding motif. pREP81-pfh1 and pREP81-pfh1K338R were transformed to pfh1-R20 cells and we examined the suppression ability of cold sensitivity. As shown in Figure 4B, pREP81-pfh1 was able to complement the cold sensitivity of pfh1-R20 cells, whereas pREP81-pfh1K338R was not. In addition, Deltapfh1/pfh1+ diploid cells transformed with these plasmids were sporulated and examined for the viability. Similarly, pREP81-pfh1 but not pREP81-pfh1K338R could complement the lethality of Deltapfh1 cells (data not shown). Thus, catalytic activity of Pfh1 DNA helicase is essential for cell viability.
###end p 74
###begin title 75
Pfh1 is a nuclear protein
###end title 75
###begin p 76
###xml 127 139 127 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 188 195 188 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 276 280 276 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 280 281 280 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 280 281 280 281 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 314 318 314 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 318 319 318 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 318 319 318 319 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 534 535 534 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f9">9</xref>
###xml 796 803 796 803 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 912 913 912 913 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f9">9</xref>
###xml 949 956 949 956 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 32 37 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 127 139 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 188 195 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 796 803 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 949 956 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
As described above, the budding yeast Pfh1 homolog Pif1p has been shown to function both in the nucleus and in mitochondria in S.cerevisiae. To determine the intracellular localization of S.pombe Pfh1, we constructed a strain that expressed a Pfh1-GFP fusion protein from the pfh1+ genomic locus, driven by native pfh1+ promoter (see Materials and Methods). Exponentially growing Pfh1-GFP expressing cells were then analyzed by fluorescence microscopy. The fluorescent signal of the Pfh1-GFP protein was observed in the nucleus (Fig. 9), consistent with the notion that the Pfh1 protein functions in chromosomal DNA replication. Although some fluorescence could also be detected in the cytoplasm of Pfh1-GFP expressing cells (in ribbon-like patterns reminiscent of previously published images of S.pombe mitochondria), these were also seen, at similar intensity, in wild-type cells lacking the GFP protein (Fig. 9). Unlike Pif1p, therefore, Pfh1 in S.pombe appears to be exclusively nuclear.
###end p 76
###begin title 77
DISCUSSION
###end title 77
###begin p 78
###xml 145 150 145 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc24</italic>
###xml 317 318 317 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c2">2</xref>
###xml 319 320 319 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c4">4</xref>
###xml 335 340 335 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc24</italic>
###xml 340 341 340 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 340 341 340 341 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 476 483 468 475 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 505 509 497 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dna2</italic>
###xml 509 510 501 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 509 510 501 502 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 617 621 609 613 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pcn1</italic>
###xml 621 622 613 614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 621 622 613 614 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 624 628 616 620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rfc1</italic>
###xml 628 629 620 621 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 628 629 620 621 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 633 637 625 629 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdm1</italic>
###xml 637 638 629 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 637 638 629 630 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 785 789 773 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dna2</italic>
###xml 789 790 777 778 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 789 790 777 778 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 854 859 842 847 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc24</italic>
###xml 876 877 864 865 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c3">3</xref>
###xml 878 879 866 867 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c4">4</xref>
###xml 880 881 868 869 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c7">7</xref>
###xml 1148 1149 1136 1137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c4">4</xref>
###xml 1226 1227 1214 1215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c8">8</xref>
###xml 76 89 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 476 483 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 653 666 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 1152 1157 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
The Cdc24 protein plays an important role in chromosomal DNA replication in fission yeast. Cells carrying temperature-sensitive mutations in the cdc24 gene undergo cell cycle arrest when shifted to the restrictive temperature, becoming arrested with incompletely replicated chromosomes prior to the onset of mitosis (2-4). Previously, cdc24+ has been shown to interact with three genes encoding subunits of the DNA polymerase delta holoenzyme complex (Pol delta-PCNA-RF-C) in S.pombe, as well as with the dna2+ gene which encodes a nuclease-helicase required for Okazaki fragment processing. Overexpression of either pcn1+, rfc1+ or cdm1+ (encoding the fission yeast PCNA protein, the large subunit of replica tion factor C, and the D-subunit of DNA polymerase delta, respectively) or dna2+ is sufficient to rescue the temperature-sensitive phenotype of cdc24 mutant alleles (3,4,7). In the case of Pcn1, Rfc1 and Dna2, these genetic interactions reflect physical interactions between the encoded proteins. Co-immunoprecipitation experiments have shown physical associations, direct or indirect, between Cdc24 and PCNA, and between Cdc24 and Rfc1 (4). Yeast two-hybrid analysis suggests that Cdc24 and Dna2 interact directly (8).
###end p 78
###begin p 79
###xml 53 58 53 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc24</italic>
###xml 58 59 58 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 58 59 58 59 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 143 152 143 152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc24-M38</italic>
###xml 168 169 168 169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c2">2</xref>
###xml 296 300 296 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 300 301 300 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 300 301 300 301 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 336 343 336 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 348 356 348 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 478 490 478 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 504 506 504 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c22">22</xref>
###xml 478 490 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
In order to isolate novel factors that interact with cdc24+, we identified cold-sensitive chromosomal suppressors of the temperature-sensitive cdc24-M38 mutant allele (2). Amongst 37 such mutants, we mapped five to a single complementation group. We cloned the corresponding gene, which we named pfh1+, and here describe the results of in vivo and in vitro analysis of the function of Pfh1, an 805 amino acid protein homologous to Pif1p and Rrm3p DNA helicase family members in S.cerevisiae (reviewed in 22).
###end p 79
###begin p 80
###xml 17 24 17 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 37 41 37 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 41 42 41 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 41 42 41 42 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 75 83 75 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1-R20</italic>
###xml 88 96 88 96 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1-R23</italic>
###xml 118 122 118 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 191 192 191 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f1">1</xref>
###xml 218 219 218 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f2">2</xref>
###xml 221 222 221 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f3">3</xref>
###xml 227 228 227 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f5">5</xref>
###xml 253 254 253 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f6">6</xref>
###xml 294 295 294 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f8">8</xref>
###xml 328 335 328 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 342 343 342 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f4">4</xref>
###xml 379 380 379 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f9">9</xref>
###xml 538 539 538 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f2">2</xref>
###xml 545 546 545 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f5">5</xref>
To elucidate the in vivo function of pfh1+, we analyzed the cold-sensitive pfh1-R20 and pfh1-R23 cells as well as the pfh1-disrupted cells. All the data, including genetic interactions (Fig. 1), mutant phenotype (Figs 2, 3 and 5), mRNA expression (Fig. 6), 5' to 3' DNA helicase activity (Fig. 8), essentiality of this activity in vivo (Fig. 4B), and cellular localization (Fig. 9) suggest that Pfh1 has an essential function in chromosomal DNA replication. However, like Cdc24 and Dna2, Pfh1 is not required for bulk DNA synthesis (Figs 2B and 5E).
###end p 80
###begin p 81
###xml 91 97 91 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 223 224 223 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf590f8">8</xref>
###xml 482 490 482 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1-R20</italic>
###xml 500 504 496 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 580 588 576 584 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1-R20</italic>
###xml 617 621 609 613 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 666 670 658 662 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 670 671 662 663 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 670 671 662 663 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 91 97 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
To understand the enzymatic function of Pfh1, we purified recombinant protein expressed in E.coli to near homogeneity and demonstrated that Pfh1 protein has ssDNA-dependent ATPase and 5' to 3' DNA helicase activities (Fig. 8). In addition, we introduced a point mutation at a lysine residue in the ATP-binding pocket predicted to be essential for ATPase activity, demonstrated that the recombinant mutant protein did indeed lack such activity, and introduced the mutant allele into pfh1-R20 and Deltapfh1 cells. Since this mutant gene could not complement the cold sensitivity of pfh1-R20 cells nor lethality of Deltapfh1 cells, we conclude that the essentiality of pfh1+ is based on its catalytic activity.
###end p 81
###begin p 82
###xml 410 412 410 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c49">49</xref>
###xml 532 534 532 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c49">49</xref>
###xml 569 576 569 576 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 956 958 952 954 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c15">15</xref>
###xml 1037 1045 1033 1041 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 293 298 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 426 431 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 569 576 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 679 692 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
What is the cellular function of Pfh1? One fascinating possibility is that Pfh1 functions in Okazaki fragment processing together with the Dna2-Cdc24 complex. Conceivably, the Pfh1 helicase could cooperate with Dna2. Recently, we found that DNA helicase and endonuclease activities of budding yeast Dna2p are tightly coupled to ensure efficient removal of the flap during the Okazaki fragment maturation step (49). In budding yeast, Dna2p helicase activity assists this process by removing secondary structures present in the flap (49). In this context, since purified S.pombe Dna2 lacks any detectable ATPase (helicase) activity (H. Y. Kang and Y. S. Seo, unpublished results), fission yeast Dna2 may require a coupled DNA helicase to execute complete removal of the flap in the Okazaki fragment maturation step. Pfh1 may therefore unwind DNA secondary structures present in the flaps formed by displacement synthesis by DNA polymerase delta (reviewed in 15). Further characterization of the biochemical properties of the Pfh1 helicase in vitro will doubtless prove highly informative.
###end p 82
###begin p 83
###xml 161 176 161 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">region-specific</italic>
###xml 188 190 188 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c22">22</xref>
###xml 382 389 382 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 611 615 611 615 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 615 616 615 616 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 615 616 615 616 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 626 630 626 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PIF1</italic>
###xml 635 639 635 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RRM3</italic>
###xml 664 676 664 676 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 103 108 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 382 389 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 664 676 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 694 699 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 791 796 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
A further issue to be addressed concerns the extent of Pfh1 function throughout the genome. In budding yeast, the Pfh1 homologs Pif1p and Rrm3p have been termed region-specific helicases (22): Pif1p is believed to function in mtDNA, telomere DNA and rDNA whereas Rrm3p is involved in rDNA replication (where its function opposes that of Pif1p). This raises the possibility that, in S.pombe, the function of Pfh1 (and by implication, perhaps that of Cdc24 also) might be confined to certain regions of the genome only, such as the rDNA repeats on chromosome III. However, it is worth noting that, in contrast to pfh1+, neither PIF1 nor RRM3 is an essential gene in S.cerevisiae. Indeed, budding yeast cells mutated for both genes are viable. Thus, there are clear differences between the two yeast systems.
###end p 83
###begin p 84
###xml 137 141 137 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
In addition to its role in chromosomal DNA replication, Pfh1 also appears to have an essential role to play in the repair of DNA damage: pfh1 mutant cells are highly sensitive to exposure to the DNA-alkylating agent MMS. Whether Pfh1 is required to cope with the effects of MMS-induced damage specifically in S-phase remains to be seen.
###end p 84
###begin p 85
###xml 125 129 125 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 129 130 129 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 129 130 129 130 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 215 217 215 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf590c50">50</xref>
###xml 306 318 306 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 376 380 372 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 380 387 376 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 597 601 593 597 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfh1</italic>
###xml 601 602 597 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</italic>
###xml 601 602 597 598 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>+</italic></sup>
###xml 306 318 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
Finally, during the preparation of this manuscript, Zakian and co-workers reported the isolation and characterization of the pfh1+ gene on the basis of the protein sequence similarity between Pfh1, Pif1p and Rrm3p (50). For the most part, their results mirror ours. However, in an echo of the situation in S.cerevisiae, these authors also observed telomere shortening in Deltapfh1S.pombe cells, consistent with a role for Pfh1 in telomere length regulation. Still, these authors also demonstrated that telomere length regulation was not the sole essential function of Pfh1 protein by showing that pfh1+ was essential for cell viability even in a strain with circular chromosomes lacking telomeric sequences. Clearly, further work is required to unambiguously assign a function to Pfh1.
###end p 85
