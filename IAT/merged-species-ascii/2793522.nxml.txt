CX3CR1 Is Expressed by Human B Lymphocytes and Meditates CX3CL1 Driven Chemotaxis of Tonsil Centrocytes
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 14 15 14 15 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 52 53 52 53 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 108 109 108 109 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 222 223 222 223 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 254 259 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Fractalkine/CX3CL1, a surface chemokine, binds to CX3CR1 expressed by different lymphocyte subsets. Since CX3CL1 has been detected in the germinal centres of secondary lymphoid tissue, in this study we have investigated CX3CR1 expression and function in human naive, germinal centre and memory B cells isolated from tonsil or peripheral blood.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 109 110 109 110 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 290 291 290 291 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 356 357 356 357 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 385 386 385 386 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 524 525 524 525 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 528 529 528 529 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 638 639 638 639 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 678 679 678 679 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 902 903 902 903 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 975 976 975 976 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 979 980 979 980 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 981 982 981 982 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 988 989 988 989 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 992 993 992 993 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 994 995 994 995 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 50 55 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 884 890 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 953 957 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 996 1000 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1078 1082 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We demonstrate unambiguously that highly purified human B cells from tonsil and peripheral blood expressed CX3CR1 at mRNA and protein levels as assessed by quantitative PCR, flow cytometry and competition binding assays. In particular, naive, germinal centre and memory B cells expressed CX3CR1 but only germinal centre B cells were attracted by soluble CX3CL1 in a transwell assay. CX3CL1 signalling in germinal centre B cells involved PI3K, Erk1/2, p38, and Src phosphorylation, as assessed by Western blot experiments. CX3CR1+ germinal centre B cells were devoid of centroblasts and enriched for centrocytes that migrated to soluble CX3CL1. ELISA assay showed that soluble CX3CL1 was secreted constitutively by follicular dendritic cells and T follicular helper cells, two cell populations homing in the germinal centre light zone as centrocytes. At variance with that observed in humans, soluble CX3CL1 did not attract spleen B cells from wild type mice. OVA immunized CX3CR1-/- or CX3CL1-/- mice showed significantly decreased specific IgG production compared to wild type mice.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 145 146 145 146 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 27 32 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We propose a model whereby human follicular dendritic cells and T follicular helper cells release in the light zone of germinal centre soluble CX3CL1 that attracts centrocytes. The functional implications of these results warrant further investigation.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 2 3 2 3 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 27 28 27 28 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 190 193 190 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Umehara1">[1]</xref>
###xml 197 198 197 198 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 335 338 335 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Foussat1">[2]</xref>
###xml 340 343 340 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Bazan1">[3]</xref>
###xml 345 348 345 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Imaizumi1">[4]</xref>
###xml 350 353 350 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Papadopoulos1">[5]</xref>
CX3C chemokine ligand 1 (CX3CL1) is a multidomain molecule consisting of a chemokine domain linked to a transmembrane domain via an extended mucin-rich stalk, and of an intracellular domain [1]. CX3CL1, that exists as membrane-anchored and soluble forms, is constitutively expressed in many hematopoietic and non-hematopoietic tissues [2], [3], [4], [5].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 2 3 2 3 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 29 30 29 30 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 52 53 52 53 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 204 205 204 205 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 216 217 216 217 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 241 244 241 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Imai1">[6]</xref>
###xml 282 283 282 283 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 293 294 293 294 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 373 374 373 374 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 384 385 384 385 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 422 423 422 423 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 426 427 426 427 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 442 443 442 443 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 465 466 465 466 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 568 571 568 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Fong1">[7]</xref>
###xml 591 592 591 592 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 658 661 658 661 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Umehara1">[1]</xref>
###xml 663 666 663 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Bazan1">[3]</xref>
###xml 691 692 691 692 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 695 696 695 696 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 170 175 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
CX3C chemokine receptor 1 (CX3CR1), the exclusive CX3CL1 receptor, is a pertuxis toxin (PTX)-sensitive seven-transmembrane G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) expressed on human NK cells, monocytes, Th1 CD4+ cells, CD8+ T cells and mast cells [6]. Two types of interactions between CX3CL1 and CX3CR1 have been reported, one occurring between the membrane anchored forms of CX3CL1 and CX3CR1, the other one between surface CX3CR1+ and soluble CX3CL1. Membrane-bound CX3CL1 induces firm adhesion of leukocytes under static and flow conditions without activating integrins [7], whereas soluble CX3CL1 released from the cell surface following proteolytic cleavage [1], [3] induces chemotaxis of CX3CR1+ cells.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 36 37 36 37 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 75 78 75 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Imai1">[6]</xref>
###xml 80 83 80 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Nishimura1">[8]</xref>
###xml 85 88 85 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Andreasson1">[9]</xref>
###xml 98 99 98 99 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 148 151 148 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Foussat1">[2]</xref>
###xml 311 312 311 312 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 400 401 400 401 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 523 524 523 524 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 55 60 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 421 426 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Previous studies failed to detect CX3CR1 expression in human B lymphocytes [6], [8], [9]. Since CX3CL1 is expressed in secondary lymphoid follicles [2], we reasoned that this chemokine could be involved in the local B cell trafficking and decided to re-investigate with different approaches the expression of CX3CR1 in the major B cell subsets from tonsil and peripheral blood. We show that indeed CX3CR1 is expressed by human naive, memory and germinal center (GC) B cells, that are the only subset attracted by soluble CX3CL1 in chemotaxis assays.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 172 176 172 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-MacLennan1">[10]</xref>
###xml 490 494 490 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-MacLennan1">[10]</xref>
###xml 1024 1028 1024 1028 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Allen1">[11]</xref>
###xml 1030 1034 1030 1034 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Hauser1">[12]</xref>
###xml 1036 1040 1036 1040 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Allen2">[13]</xref>
###xml 1042 1046 1042 1046 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Schwickert1">[14]</xref>
###xml 1048 1052 1048 1052 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Hauser2">[15]</xref>
The GC is the site where antigen activated naive B cells migrate, proliferate and undergo class switch recombination, antibody gene diversification and affinity maturation [10]. GC have two distinct zones called dark and light zones based upon histological appearance. B cells in the dark zone, called centroblasts, proliferate and somatically hypermutate antibody variable genes, then move to the light zone where they are selected based on the affinity of the B cell receptor for antigen [10]. Recent studies have delineated two alternative models for B cell trafficking in the GC, i) the cyclic re-entry model, whereby B cells that have migrated from the dark to the light zone and have been selected subsequently return to the dark zone for further proliferation and, ii) the intrazonal recirculation model, whereby most B cells stay in dark or light zones and selection in these areas operates independently. This latter model keeps into account the low frequency of GC B cells recirculating from one zone to the other [11], [12], [13], [14], [15].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 54 58 54 58 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Allen1">[11]</xref>
###xml 60 64 60 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Hauser1">[12]</xref>
###xml 66 70 66 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Schwickert1">[14]</xref>
###xml 72 76 72 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Hauser2">[15]</xref>
###xml 78 82 78 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Allen3">[16]</xref>
###xml 137 141 137 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-KomaiKoma1">[17]</xref>
###xml 143 147 143 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Corcione1">[18]</xref>
###xml 41 46 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 109 114 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
These mechanisms have been identified in mouse models [11], [12], [14], [15], [16], whereas less is known on human GC B cell trafficking [17], [18]. This study adds a new piece of information on the latter issue.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
###xml 29 30 29 30 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 37 42 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Expression and Function of CX3CR1 in Human Tonsil B Cells
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 24 25 24 25 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 209 210 209 210 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 405 407 405 407 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">vs</italic>
###xml 59 64 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We first investigated CX3CR1 expression in highly purified human B cells from tonsil and peripheral blood by flow cytometry. Ten independent experiments demonstrated that B cells from both sources expressed CX3CR1 (tonsil B cells median 46%, range 35-70; median MRFI 5.2, range 4.0-8.0; blood B cells median 52%, range 36-59; median MRFI 6.0, range 4.0-7.0). In spite of a trend to a higher MRFI in blood vs tonsil B cells the difference was not statistically significant.

###begin title 70
Discussion
###end title 70
###begin p 71
###xml 25 33 25 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 56 60 56 60 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-KomaiKoma1">[17]</xref>
###xml 71 75 71 75 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Corcione1">[18]</xref>
###xml 331 338 330 337 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 631 635 630 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Allen1">[11]</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 141 147 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Murine</span>
Human GC B cells migrate in vitro to anti-Ig antibodies [17] or CXCL12 [18] only following CD40 mAb and IL-4 mediated rescue from apoptosis. Murine GC B cells overexpressing Bcl-2 under control of the Ig mu enhancer and therefore resistant to spontaneous apoptosis show different homing properties regulated by chemokine gradients in vivo. In particular, centroblasts express high levels of CXCR4 and home to the dark zone where the CXCR4 ligand CXCL12 is abundantly expressed. Centrocytes expressing high levels of CXCR5 accumulate in the light zone of the GC where the CXCR5 ligand CXCL13 is expressed at elevated concentrations [11]. Thus, current evidence indicates that only GC B cells rescued from apoptosis are enabled to migrate along chemokine gradients.
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 58 59 58 59 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 297 300 297 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Imai1">[6]</xref>
###xml 302 305 302 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Nishimura1">[8]</xref>
###xml 307 310 307 310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Andreasson1">[9]</xref>
###xml 32 37 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Here we show unambiguously that human B cells expressed CX3CR1 mRNA and protein as assessed by quantitative PCR, flow cytometry and competition binding. The discrepancy between these and previous results is likely related to differences in the antibodies and the techniques for mRNA analysis used [6], [8], [9].
###end p 72
###begin p 73
###xml 2 3 2 3 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 43 44 43 44 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 143 144 143 144 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 147 150 147 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Imai1">[6]</xref>
###xml 185 186 185 186 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 265 269 265 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Kansra1">[34]</xref>
###xml 271 275 271 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Cambien1">[35]</xref>
###xml 380 381 380 381 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 393 395 393 395 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">vs</italic>
###xml 487 488 487 488 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 633 637 633 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-DellaRocca1">[36]</xref>
###xml 639 643 639 643 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Luttrell1">[37]</xref>
CX3CR1 triggered GC B cell chemotaxis to CX3CL1, that was abolished by PTX pre-treatment, as expected from the well known PTX sensitivity of CX3CR1[6]. Signal transduction induced by CX3CL1 in GC B cells was similar to that previously reported for other cell types [34], [35]. In particular, p-PI3K, p-Erk1/2, p-p38 and p-Akt, as well as p-Src, were found to be up-regulated in CX3CL1 treated vs untreated GC B cells. Inhibition of PI3K, Erk1/2, p38 or Src, but not of Akt, suppressed CX3CL1 induced GC B cell migration. Thus, the PI3K and Src pathways integrated signals originating from either scaffold, as reported for other GPCR [36], [37].
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 2 3 2 3 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 6 7 6 7 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 57 58 57 58 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 398 399 398 399 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
CX3CR1+ naive and memory B cells were not attracted by CX3CL1 and showed a limited signalling pattern, i.e. p-Akt and p-Src were up-regulated, whereas p-PI3K, p-p38, and p-ERK1/2 were not. These results indicate that i) signalling and chemotaxis are dissociated in non-GC B cells, ii) lack of p-PI3K activation is consistent with the crucial role of this pathway in GC B cell migration, and iii) CX3CL1 driven signalling in non-GC B cells suggests functions of the chemokine alternative to chemotaxis that warrant investigation.
###end p 74

###xml 496 497 496 497 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>

###xml 603 604 603 604 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
CX3CR1+ GC B cells contained two major subsets expressing CD23 or CD27. CD23+ cells contained CD44+, IgM+ and Bcl2+ cells, that may represent antigen-activated naive B cells that have recently accessed the GC [38]. CD27+ cells were enriched for cells expressing IgG, IgM, Bcl2 and CD95 but not CD44. Only the CX3CR1+, CD27+ GC B cell subset was attracted by CX3CL1. Further characterization of migrated GC B cells showed that they displayed a predominant CX3CR1+, CD27+, Bcl-2+, IgG+, CD77-, CD44-, CD23- immunophenotype, consistent with that of centrocytes. Therefore, these experiments suggest that CX3CL1 participates in the control of centrocyte trafficking within the light zone of the GC.
###end p 77
###begin p 78
###xml 12 14 12 14 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FH</sub>
###xml 78 82 78 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Vinuesa1">[33]</xref>
###xml 242 246 242 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-MacLennan1">[10]</xref>
###xml 292 294 292 294 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FH</sub>
###xml 550 554 550 554 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Allen2">[13]</xref>
In the GC, TFH cells promote centrocyte differentiation to Ig-secreting cells [33], and contact between centrocytes and FDC results into positive selection of centrocytes expressing high affinity B cell receptors for antigen displayed on FDC [10]. Recent studies have suggested that FDC and TFH cells may collaborate in B cell selection. Thus, B cells that have captured, processed and presented high amounts of antigen to T cells would receive help from the latter cells at the expenses of B cells that have captured lower concentrations of antigen [13].
###end p 78
###begin p 79
###xml 17 18 17 18 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 94 97 94 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Foussat1">[2]</xref>
###xml 336 337 336 337 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
In this study, CX3CL1 was abundantly expressed in the GC, as assessed by immunohistochemistry [2]. Such expression was detected not only in the GC, but also in the FM and SE area. These findings are consistent with our unpublished results (Corcione A et al.) showing that a half of GC, naive and memory B cells express constitutively CX3CL1 on the cell surface.
###end p 79
###begin p 80
###xml 2 3 2 3 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 39 41 39 41 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FH</sub>
###xml 125 129 121 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Ludwig1">[30]</xref>
###xml 165 172 161 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 172 176 168 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Andersson1">[39]</xref>
###xml 234 236 230 232 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FH</sub>
###xml 339 340 335 336 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 366 370 362 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008485-Hundhausen1">[40]</xref>
###xml 379 381 375 377 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FH</sub>
###xml 455 456 451 452 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 474 481 470 477 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 503 504 499 500 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 507 508 503 504 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 530 531 526 527 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 559 561 555 557 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FH</sub>
###xml 495 500 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
CX3CL1 was released constitutively by TFH cells and FDC, whose production was increased by stimulation with TNF or IFN-gamma [30], two cytokines produced in tonsils in vivo[39]. In this respect, our preliminary experiments show that TFH cells and FDC express constitutively ADAM10, a metalloprotease involved in constitutive shedding of CX3CL1 from the cell surface [40]. Since TFH cells and FDC reside in the light zone of GC, they may control through CX3CL1 release local in vivo migration of human CX3CR1+ centrocytes. Thus, CX3CL1 would cluster together TFH cells, FDC and centrocytes and promote differentiation of the latter cells to Ig-secreting cells.
###end p 80
###begin p 81
###xml 37 38 37 38 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 64 65 64 65 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 116 117 116 117 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
Mouse B cells, although expressing CX3CR1, did not migrate to CX3CL1, indicating that surface rather than soluble CX3CL1 may be involved in cellular interactions leading to antigen specific IgG production.
###end p 81
###begin p 82
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vivo</italic>
###xml 103 104 103 104 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 107 108 107 108 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 109 110 109 110 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 116 117 116 117 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 120 121 120 121 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 122 123 122 123 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 124 128 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In vivo experiments demonstrated that OVA-specific IgG production was significantly impaired in both CX3CR1-/- or CX3CL1-/- mice, in the absence of changes in microarchitecture, organization of lymphoid follicles and lymphocyte subset distribution. Further studies are required to understand what mechanisms underlie defective antibody production and where they operate, i.e. in secondary lymphoid organs or at periphery.
###end p 82
###begin p 83
###xml 73 78 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 152 157 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 162 167 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
In conclusion we have identified a novel chemokine candidate to regulate human centrocyte trafficking in secondary lymphoid organs. Differences between human and mouse models highlight the difficulties at comparing the results obtained in these systems.

The membranes were stripped with RestoreTM Western Blot Stripping Buffer (Pierce, Rockford, IL, USA) and re-probed with mAbs to detect total p38, Akt, and ERK-1/2 (Santa Cruz Biotechnology). After incubation with horse radish peroxidase (HRP) antibodies, blots were developed with Enhanced Chemiluminescence Detection. In kinase assay, lysates (300 microg) were pre-cleared with rabbit serum and Protein A-agarose immunoprecipitated overnight at 4degreesC with 1 microg Hck mAb (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Santa Cruz, CA, USA). Each immunoprecipitate sample was washed twice with lysis buffer and twice with kinase buffer (30 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 10 mM MgCl2, 1 mM CaCl2, 5 mMCl2). After washing, cells were incubated for 15 minutes at 30degreesC in 30 microl kinase buffer 10 microM ATP (Sigma), and 5 microCi gamma32P ATP (ICN Biomed) in presence or in absence of 10 microg of acid-denaturated enolase (muscle rabbit enolase, Sigma, St. Louis, MO, USA). The reaction was stopped by the addition of 4X Laemmli sample buffer and boiled for 5 min. Samples were separated by electrophoresis on 12% SDS-polyacrylamide gel. Kinase activity was detected by autoradiography.

###xml 904 905 898 899 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
The competition binding assay was performed according to Wang et al. [44]. Briefly, cells were suspended in binding buffer (PBS pH 7.4 containing 2% BSA) supplemented with 1 nM 125I-CX3CL1 (Perkin Elmer, specific activity 2200/Ci mmol) in the absence or presence of 1, 10 and 100 nM cold CX3CL1 for 2 h at 4degreesC. Non specific binding was determined in parallel through cell incubation with 125I-CX3CL1 in the presence of 100-fold excess unlabeled CX3CL1 and subtracted from total binding to yield specific binding. After incubation, medium was removed, cells were washed with binding buffer and radioactivity counted. Percentage of binding inhibition by unlabeled CX3CL1, calculated as ratio between cell-bound cpm in the presence of unlabeled ligand and cell-bound cpm in the absence of unlabeled ligand multiplied by 100, was used as a measure for competition between 125I-labeled and unlabelled CX3CL1. Some experiments were performed using 100 nM cold CXCL8 (BioSource International, Flynn, Camarillo, Ca, USA) as negative control.
