###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 50 72 50 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plasmodium falciparum </italic>
###xml 256 261 256 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
###xml 331 334 331 334 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.2</sub>
###xml 29 34 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 50 71 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
In its effort to survive the human immune system, Plasmodium falciparum uses several parasite-derived antigens most of which are expressed at the surface of the parasitized red blood cells (pRBCs). Recently SURFINs, a new family of antigens encoded by the surf multi-gene family, has been reported. One member of the family, SURFIN4.2, was found present both at the pRBC-surface and at the merozoite apex.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 46 50 46 50 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 149 153 149 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 153 157 153 157 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 196 201 196 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
###xml 334 338 334 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 338 342 338 342 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 361 365 361 365 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 409 413 409 413 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
The presence of a second SURFIN member, SURFIN4.1 (PFD0100c, PFD0105c) is reported here. Bioinformatic tools were used to study the structure of the surf4.1 gene. To investigate the expression of surf genes PCR and real-time quantitative PCR (Rt-QPCR) were employed and Northern and Western blots were used to confirm the size of the surf4.1 gene and the SURFIN4.1 protein respectively. Localization of SURFIN4.1 was determined using immunofluorescence assays.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 4 8 4 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 8 12 8 12 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 166 170 166 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 170 174 170 174 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 466 470 454 458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 470 474 458 462 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 542 545 518 521 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1</sub>
###xml 699 703 675 679 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 703 707 679 683 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 783 787 759 763 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
The surf4.1 gene was found present in one copy by Rt-QPCR in some parasites (3D7AH1, 3D7S8, 7G8) whereas six copies of the gene were identified in FCR3 and FCR3S1.2. surf4.1 was found transcribed in the late asexual stages of the parasite beginning approximately32 hours post invasion and throughout the schizont stages with the level of transcription peaking at late schizogony. The levels of transcript correlated with the number of gene copies in FCR3 and 3D7S8. surf4.1 was found to encode a polypeptide of approximatelyMw 258 kDa (SURFIN4.1) present within the parasitophorous vacuole (PV), around free merozoites as merozoite-associated material, but not at the pRBC-surface. Despite multiple surf4.1 gene copies in some parasites this was not reflected in the levels of SURFIN4.1 polypeptide.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusion
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 6 10 6 10 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
SURFIN4.1 is a member of the SURFINs, present in the PV and on the released merozoite. The results suggest different SURFINs to be expressed at different locations in the parasite and at distinct time-points during the intra-erythrocytic cycle.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Background
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 31 53 31 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plasmodium falciparum </italic>
###xml 475 497 475 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plasmodium falciparum </italic>
###xml 678 682 678 682 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 720 724 708 712 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 766 767 754 755 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 768 769 756 757 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 998 999 986 987 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 31 52 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
###xml 475 496 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">Plasmodium falciparum</span>
The erythrocytic life cycle of Plasmodium falciparum is characterized by complex interactions of parasite derived surface exposed antigens with host receptors and immune defence proteins. At the surface of the the parasitized red blood cells (pRBCs), parasite antigens provide adhesive properties and at the same time allow for limited parasite resistance to the host immune response by virtue of great sequence variability. In case of the well-characterized surface antigen Plasmodium falciparum erythrocyte membrane protein 1 (PfEMP1), a multi-adhesive variable ligand of 200-400 kDa, this is achieved by clonal exchange, "switching" of the mutually expressed PfEMP1 encoding var gene. A unique set of approximately60 var genes is present in each parasite genome [1-3]. With the release of merozoites from the host cell, a number of additional parasite proteins are exposed to the immune system, which, in response to the host immune pressure, show polymorphisms or are differentially expressed [4].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 45 58 45 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum</italic>
###xml 228 229 228 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 230 231 230 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 394 396 394 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 616 618 616 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 619 621 619 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 726 728 726 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 729 731 729 731 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 817 818 817 818 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 824 837 824 837 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum</italic>
###xml 860 864 860 864 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rh1 </italic>
###xml 1157 1159 1157 1159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 1160 1162 1160 1162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 45 58 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
###xml 824 837 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
The merozoite, which is the invasive form of P. falciparum, invades erythrocytes in a cascade of events in which multiple receptor-ligand interactions facilitate parasite attachment, reorientation, junction formation and entry [5-9]. The machinery employed in this process is complex and involves proteins at the merozoite surface and in specialized secretory organelles at the merozoite apex [10]. Triggered by the initial parasite attachment to the host cell, micronemes and/or rhoptries release their contents. The Duffy binding-like (DBL) family (EBA-175 and its paralogues EBA-140 (BAEBL) and EBA-181 (JESEBL) [11,12]) in micronemes and the reticulocyte binding like (RBL) family (PfRh1, PfRh2a, PfRh2b, PfRh3 and PfRh4 [13-16]) in the neck of rhoptries have been shown to be relevant for merozoite infectivity [4]. In P. falciparum, several copies of Pf Rh1 gene were reported in FCR3, W2mef, and FCB1 (13, 3 and 4 respectively) when compared to 3D7. Thus, apart from the different protein antigens expressed on the surface of the merozoite, the complexity of the malaria parasite is increasing by the introduction of gene duplications and deletions [17-19].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 124 141 124 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plasmodium vivax </italic>
###xml 204 206 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 231 240 231 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. vivax </italic>
###xml 255 275 255 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plasmodium knowlesi </italic>
###xml 304 307 304 307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var</italic>
###xml 365 369 365 369 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.2 </sub>
###xml 637 641 637 641 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 1396 1398 1396 1398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 1449 1460 1449 1460 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Plasmodium </italic>
###xml 1491 1493 1491 1493 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pk</italic>
###xml 1497 1501 1497 1501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 1615 1617 1615 1617 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 124 140 <span type="species:ncbi:5855">Plasmodium vivax</span>
###xml 231 239 <span type="species:ncbi:5855">P. vivax</span>
###xml 255 274 <span type="species:ncbi:5850">Plasmodium knowlesi</span>
SURFINs are a small family of large (200-300 kDa) proteins, which are composed of domains structurally related to PvSTP1 of Plasmodium vivax but also other exported and surface exposed parasite proteins [20], including PfEMP1, the P. vivax VIR family and Plasmodium knowlesi variant surface antigen (SICAvar). In accordance with this structural relationship, SURFIN4.2 was found co-transported with PfEMP1 to the pRBC-surface. The protein is also observed on the merozoite as merozoite-associated material (MAM) at the apical end of the released merozoites. In the 3D7 parasite SURFINs are encoded by 10 surface-associated interspersed (surf) genes, which are located close or within the subtelomeric region of five chromosomes. Frequent recombination events in this area are responsible for the considerable variability of proteins encoded in this region, also observed in SURFINs. N-terminally, SURFINs contain a cysteine rich domain (CRD) defined by six conserved cysteine residues, five of which are found positionally conserved in PvSTP1. The CRD precedes a region of high size and sequence variability, followed by a putative transmembrane (TM) region. In contrast to the variable N-terminal region, the C-terminal domain of SURFINs is relatively conserved and characterized by tryptophane rich domains (WRD) of approximately 150 amino acid residues, separated by less conserved sequences [20]. The WRDs establish a structural link to several Plasmodium surface proteins, i.e. PfEMP1, PkSICAvar and also the giant erythrocyte membrane protein 332 (Pf332), of which recent data also suggests surface exposure [21].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 48 52 48 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 52 56 52 56 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 202 206 202 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 206 210 206 210 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 241 245 241 245 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
The objectives of the study were to analyze the surf4.1 gene structure to determine whether it is a pseudo gene, as previously annotated, or a complete functional gene. The transcription pattern of the surf4.1 gene and localization of SURFIN4.1 on the protein level were also investigated.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Methods
###end title 15
###begin title 16
Parasites and cultures
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 127 129 127 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 130 132 130 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
Parasites 3D7AH1, 3D7S8, FCR3, FCR3S1.2, 7G8 and TM180 were maintained in continuous culture according to standard procedures [22,23].
###end p 17
###begin title 18
###xml 12 17 12 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
Analysis of surf gene presence and sequence heterogeneity in various parasite lines
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 149 154 149 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
###xml 285 286 285 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 291 292 291 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 569 574 569 574 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
Based on the 3D7 sequences of the annotated SURFINs, two sets of forward and reverse primers (Primer set 1 and Primer set 2), were designed for each surf gene. Some of the primers were designed to cross introns while others were within the open reading frames (ORFs) (Additional Files 1 and 2). Genomic DNA was extracted from trophozoite stages of 3D7S8, NF54, 7G8, DD2, FCR3, FCR3S1.2, TM284, TM180, R29, UAM25, UKS03, and UKS05 using the DNeasy Blood and Tissue kit according to the manufacturer's instructions (Qiagen, USA). Using two sets of independently designed surf gene specific primers, DNA was PCR amplified from each parasite. The following were the PCR conditions 35 cycles of 94degreesC for 30 sec, 55degreesC for 30 sec, and 68degreesC 60 sec.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 4 8 4 8 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
surf4.1 transcript analysis
###end title 20
###begin p 21
Highly synchronized 3D7S8 and FCR3 parasites were harvested post invasion (p.i.) at early ring (10 h), late ring (20 h), early trophozoite (30 h), and schizont (40 h) parasite stages. The parasites were resuspended in pre-warmed TRIzol (Invitrogen, USA) (37degreesC) and incubated at 37degreesC for 5 minutes. Total RNA was extracted using the manufacturer's instructions (Invitrogen, USA) and DNAse treated (Ambion, USA). The RNA (100 ng) was reverse transcribed (RT+) using MuLV reverse transcriptase enzyme and random hexamers (Applied Biosystems, USA) according to manufacturer's instructions. For each RT+ experiment, one RT- reaction (with reverse transcriptase omitted) was performed to ensure that there was no DNA contamination in the RNA.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 4 8 4 8 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>

###xml 576 580 576 580 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 581 584 581 584 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1</sub>
###xml 809 813 791 795 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 813 817 795 799 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 867 871 849 853 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 872 875 854 857 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1</sub>
###xml 881 884 863 866 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1</sub>
###xml 938 939 920 921 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
surf4.1 is currently present as PFD0100c in GeneDB, and PlasmboDB v5.4 but it was previously indicated as a truncated gene in the PlasmoDB v4.3 malaria database and was then suggestively comprised two distinct genes PFD0105c and PFD0100c. Primers (surfC4.1F/R and Sintra4.1 F/R) were consequently designed on opposite sides of the intergenic region of the two previously annotated genes (yielding amplification products of 1160 bp and 450 bp respectively, see Additional File 2). cDNA from ring stages, trophozoite stages and schizont stages was amplified using surfC4.1F and surfC4.1R. The RT-PCR cycling conditions were as follows: 35 cycles of 94degreesC for 30 sec, 55degreesC for 30 sec, and 68degreesC 60 sec. The cDNA from the different parasite stages was also amplified using primers specific to the surf4.1 gene sections, these included 5CS-1F/R, 5CS-2F/R, surfC4.1F/R, S4.1F/R, 1C-S1F/R, 1C-S2F/R and 1C-S3F/R (Additional File 2).
###end p 22
###begin title 23
Identification of the PFD0105c intron location
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 32 36 32 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 37 40 37 40 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1</sub>

###begin title 27
Sera and specific antibodies
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 14 19 14 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
###xml 19 23 19 23 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>

###xml 711 718 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbits</span>

A fragment of surf 4.1 (from the non-cytoplasmic part of the protein), SURFIN4.1-C1 was PCR amplified from 3D7S8 using primers 5'-atcgagctcTTGGATAATACAGGTGAT-3' and 5'-gatctcgagTTCCTTATGATGTTTTGGT-3'. The amplified DNA was cloned into TOPOII vector and sequenced. The SURFIN4.1-C1 fragment was subsequently cloned into the PQE Trisystem -His strep 2 expression vector (Qiagen, USA) which adds a stretch of 6 histidine residues to the C terminus and 8 streptavidin residues at the N-terminus. The fusion protein was purified on His-Trap columns (Amersham Biotec, Sweden) charged with Nickel ions (1 M, NiCl2, SIGMA, USA) according to manufacturer's instructions. The purified fusion protein was used to immunize rabbits (500 mug/injection) and rats (100 mug/injection) in Freund's complete adjuvant (SIGMA, USA) and boosted three times at one-month intervals with the same protein concentration in Freund's incomplete adjuvant. The animals were bled 10 days after the last boost.

###begin p 30
###xml 112 114 100 102 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">7 </sup>

Real-time quantitative PCR analysis of surf4.1 copy numbers and transcription levels

###xml 913 917 913 917 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 1043 1045 1043 1045 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
The data presented here shows a revised gene structure of surf4.1, which combines two previously identified open reading frames (PFD0100c and PFD0105c) (plasmodb.org v4.3 and Figure 1A). This was obtained by bioinformatic analysis and confirmed by PCR, which shows that surf4.1 is one complete gene with three exons separated by two introns (Figure 1B). In PlasmoDB, surf4.1 gene was previously annotated as two individual open reading frames, PFD0100c and PFD0105c separated by a 410 bp intergenic region (hereby referred to as intergenic region). However, RT-PCR amplification using primers designed to amplify the fragment surrounding this intergenic region, showed a single band from cDNA indicating that the annotation was incorrect and that these two predicted open reading frames compose a single open reading frame. This led to the recent re-annotation of the gene in PlasmoDB v5.4. In this study the surf4.1 transcript was shown to be a single open reading frame in 3D7S8, FCR3, FCR3S1.2, 3D7AH1, 7G8 and TM180 parasite lines (Figure 1B).

###begin title 66
Discussion
###end title 66
###begin p 67
###xml 11 24 11 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum</italic>
###xml 41 46 41 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
###xml 168 170 168 170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 223 227 223 227 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 344 348 344 348 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 366 370 366 370 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 370 374 370 374 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 399 403 399 403 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.2 </sub>
###xml 482 495 482 495 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum</italic>
###xml 11 24 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
###xml 482 495 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
SURFINs of P. falciparum, encoded by the surf multi gene family, compose a new family of surface proteins. Not much is known about the protein family and its function [20]. This paper reports on the identification of SURFIN4.1 a member of the SURFINs present in the parasitophorous vacuole (PV) and in part at the level of the merozoite. SURFIN4.1 is encoded by the surf4.1 gene, which, as is SURFIN4.2 (the only member of the family thoroughly studied), located on chromosome 4 of P. falciparum.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 40 45 40 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
###xml 103 116 103 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum</italic>
###xml 152 157 152 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
###xml 390 391 390 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S3">3</xref>
###xml 566 567 566 567 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S3">3</xref>
###xml 103 116 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
In order to investigate the presence of surf genes in both laboratory strains and clinical isolates of P. falciparum, PCR was carried out on all the 10 surf genes using two different primer sets for each gene. Two independent primer sets were used in order to reduce bias in gene amplification, which may arise with the use of only one primer set due to sequence variation (Additional File 3). The outcome showed that all the genes were amplified in either laboratory adapted or clinical isolates, but not every gene was amplified in every parasite (Additional File 3).
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 23 27 23 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 27 31 27 31 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 121 122 121 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S3">3</xref>
###xml 353 354 353 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
###xml 378 382 378 382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 382 386 382 386 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 427 428 427 428 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
###xml 494 498 494 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 498 502 498 502 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
In the same manner the surf4.1 gene was also found amplified in all parasite lines, but for isolate R29 (Additional File 3). Single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) were observed in laboratory strains (HB3, DD2, D10, 7G8, IT, RO33, K1, FCR3, and FCC-2), some clinical isolates from Uganda (UAM25, UKS03 and UKS05) and GHANA 1 from Ghana (Additional File 4). The sequence data on surf4.1 from HB3, DD2, D10, 7G8 (Additional File 4) further confirms the existence of sequence heterogeneity of the surf4.1 gene.
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 46 50 46 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 50 54 50 54 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 153 155 153 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 158 160 158 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 403 407 403 407 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 407 411 407 411 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 469 483 469 483 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum </italic>
###xml 534 537 534 537 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1</sub>
###xml 627 631 627 631 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.2 </sub>
###xml 651 653 651 653 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1D</xref>
###xml 469 482 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
A detailed analysis of the gene revealed that surf4.1 is one complete gene comprised of three exons with one continuous open reading frame (ORF) (Figure 1A) [20]. Differences in length of the first intron were observed between FCR3 and 3D7S8 parasite strains with FCR3 having a longer intron. It is not established whether the difference in intron length and or presence of SNPs could contribute to the surf4.1 gene maintaining a complete open reading frame in certain P. falciparum strains. The gene encodes a 258 kDa protein, SURFIN4.1, which is of a slightly smaller molecular weight than the previously characterised SURFIN4.2 (286.4 kDa) (Figure 1D).
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 24 28 24 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 28 32 28 32 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 325 327 325 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 346 350 346 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 350 354 350 354 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 934 948 934 948 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum </italic>
###xml 949 951 949 951 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 934 947 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
With the knowledge that surf4.1 exists as one complete gene, copy number polymorphisms were analysed in different parasite isolates. FCR3 and its daughter clone FCR3S1.2 (cloned by micromanipulation) were found to have six copies of the gene, in concordance with copy number estimates performed previously using microarrays [18]. The increase in surf4.1 gene copy numbers correlated with a five-fold increase in RNA transcription observed in FCR3 compared to the 3D7S8 parasite strain. However, the increase in copy number of the gene was not reflected on level of protein, rather that the protein was present a similar levels in the two parasites. This might be due to sequence differences in the protein in FCR3, resulting in poorer recognition by the antibodies raised using a protein construct from 3D7S8 And another possibility may be that there is a certain level of translational repression as has been previously described in P. falciparum [26].
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 40 44 40 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 44 48 44 48 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 101 105 101 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 105 109 105 109 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 282 286 270 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 286 290 274 278 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 322 326 310 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 326 330 314 318 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.2 </sub>
###xml 498 502 486 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 502 506 490 494 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 608 609 596 597 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 644 648 632 636 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
The temporal transcriptional profile of surf4.1 was analysed in the 3D7S8 and FCR3 parasite strains. surf4.1 is transcribed during the late stages from approximately32 h post invasion peaking at the late schizont stages (44-48 hrs). The difference in transcription patterns between surf4.1 (late trophs and schizonts) and surf4.2 (transcribed from early rings to the schizont stages) may suggest differences in function of the proteins encoded by the respective genes. The transcription profile of surf4.1 correlated well with the protein expression, observed in both FCR3 and 3D7S8 parasite lysates (Figure 3). No detectable protein for SURFIN4.1 was observed in early parasite stages.
###end p 72
###begin p 73
###xml 32 36 32 36 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.2 </sub>
###xml 69 72 69 72 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1</sub>
###xml 220 224 220 224 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 405 409 405 409 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 448 452 448 452 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.2 </sub>
###xml 556 560 556 560 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.2 </sub>
###xml 654 658 654 658 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 697 701 697 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4A&#8211;B</xref>
In the trophozoite stage, SURFIN4.2 is present in the PV as is SURFIN4.1, which appears during the late trophozoite or early schizont stages and is observed in segmented schizonts carrying newly formed merozoites. SURFIN4.1 is also observed as amorphous material (MAM) around the merozoites, which could suggest that it plays a role in the merozoite invasion process. It is interesting to note that SURFIN4.1 partially co-localises with both SURFIN4.2 and EBA175 in the intact schizont but the proteins differentiate in the segmented schizonts. Both SURFIN4.2 and EBA175 are observed at the apical end of merozoites in the segmented schizont while SURFIN4.1 is spread around the merozoite (Figure 4A-B).
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 6 10 6 10 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 92 95 92 95 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1</sub>
###xml 242 246 242 246 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 405 408 405 408 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1</sub>
###xml 468 472 468 472 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 538 542 538 542 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.2 </sub>
SURFIN4.1 seems not to be exposed on the pRBC surface as shown by failure of the anti-SURFIN4.1-C1 serum to agglutinate IE in FCR3 and 3D7S8. This observation is in line with the IFA on air-dried monolayer results, which indicates that SURFIN4.1 remains within the PV and is not present in the pRBC cytoplasm or at the surface of IE. Immunofluorescence assay on live cells showed no reactivity with SURFIN4.1-C1 serum. Still both SURFINs have PEXEL-like motifs: SURFIN4.1 has a PEXEL-like motif (RNVFE) located at aa 25-29 while in SURFIN4.2 the PEXEL motif is RKIFE though at the same aa position. The motifs differ but whether this difference could affect the differential transport of SURFINs to the IE is to be elucidated.
###end p 74
###begin p 75
###xml 22 26 22 26 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 136 139 136 139 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.2</sub>
###xml 200 204 200 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 204 207 204 207 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1.2</sub>
###xml 264 266 264 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 290 294 290 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 294 298 294 298 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 463 465 463 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
The function of SURFIN4.1 is not yet known, but other members of the SURFINs have been associated with putative functions such as SURFIN4.2, which might be involved in erythrocyte invasion. PFA0650w (surf1.2) has been suggested to be involved in surface adhesion [27] and PFD0100c (part of surf4.1 gene) has been shown to share Myb-protein domains and GO annotations with PFI1480w and PFL0815w, which have putative functions regarding transcriptional regulation [28].
###end p 75
###begin p 76
###xml 80 84 80 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">var </italic>
###xml 97 99 97 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 100 102 100 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 109 114 109 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
###xml 182 183 182 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 456 461 456 461 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf </italic>
Antigenic variation has been reported in some multi-gene families including the var gene family [29-31]. The surf multi-gene family shows differential expression of its genes (Table 1). Some of the genes are expressed simultaneously during the entire blood stage parasite cycle; some are expressed throughout the life cycle while one third is expressed during the later stages of the cycle. It is not yet established whether differential expression of the surf genes could suggest different functions.
###end p 76
###begin title 77
Conclusion
###end title 77
###begin p 78
###xml 12 16 12 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 16 20 16 20 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 64 78 64 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. falciparum </italic>
###xml 176 180 176 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">surf</italic>
###xml 180 184 180 184 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 205 209 205 209 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.1 </sub>
###xml 64 77 <span type="species:ncbi:5833">P. falciparum</span>
In summary, surf4.1 is one complete, and not truncated, gene in P. falciparum parasites, with gene-copy number polymorphisms existing amongst different clones and strains. The surf4.1 gene encodes a SURFIN4.1 protein of Mw approximately 258 kDa present in the PV and associated with the released merozoite.
###end p 78
