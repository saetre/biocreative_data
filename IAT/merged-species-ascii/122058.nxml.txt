Comparative transcription map of the wobbler critical region on mouse chromosome 11 and the homologous region on human chromosome 2p13-14
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Background
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 56 63 56 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
###xml 65 67 65 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 41 46 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 98 103 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 119 124 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
To support the positional cloning of the mouse mutation wobbler (wr) the corresponding regions on human Chr2p13-14 and mouse Chr11 were analyzed in detail and compared with respect to gene content, order, and orientation.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Results
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 180 183 180 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rel</italic>
###xml 194 199 194 199 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rab1a</italic>
###xml 447 454 447 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
###xml 494 501 494 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
###xml 681 685 681 685 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Otx1</italic>
###xml 779 781 779 781 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 248 253 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 384 387 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
###xml 392 397 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The gene content of the investigated regions was highly conserved between the two species: 20 orthologous genes were identified on our BAC/YAC contig comprising 4.5 Mb between REL/Rel and RAB1A/Rab1a. Exceptions were pseudogenes ELP and PX19 whose mouse counterparts were not located within the analyzed region. Two independently isolated genomic clones indicate an inversion between man and mouse with the inverted segment being identical to the wobbler critical interval. We investigated the wobbler critical region by extensive STS/EST mapping and genomic sequencing. Additionally, the full-length cDNA sequences of four newly mapped genes as well as the previously mapped gene Otx1 were established and subjected to mutation analysis. Our data indicate that all genes in the wr critical region have been identified.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Conclusion
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 133 135 133 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
Unexpectedly, neither mutation analysis of cDNAs nor levels of mRNAs indicated which of the candidate genes might be affected by the wr mutation. The possibility arises that there might be hitherto unknown effects of mutations, in addition to structural changes of the mRNA or regulatory abnormalities.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Background
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 359 360 359 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 662 663 662 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
The conservation of genomic structure between two or more species is the basis of comparative genomics. During the process of evolution the distribution of genes on chromosomes is rearranged by illegitimate recombination and translocation. Species sharing a recent common ancestor tend to have fewer chromosomal rearrangements than distantly related species [1]. Orthologous genes serve as landmarks for the definition of related chromosomal regions. Orthologous segments are defined by at least one pair of orthologous genes; conserved synteny relates to at least two pairs of orthologous genes located on the same chromosomal segment in two different species [2].
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 133 134 133 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
With increasing sequence data comparative genomics is no longer restricted to genes, as evolutionary conserved intergenic sequences [3] can now also be taken into account. In addition, comparison of genomic sequence data of two different species facilitates the identification of novel genes and conserved regulatory elements.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 122 134 122 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler (wr)</italic>
###xml 198 199 198 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 230 231 230 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 384 386 384 386 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 107 112 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 246 251 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 283 288 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 343 348 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We use comparative genomics for a positional cloning approach in order to identify candidate genes for the mouse mutation wobbler (wr) which causes muscle weakness due to motor neuron degeneration [4] and a spermiogenesis defect [5]. The wobbler mouse is used as an animal model for human spinal muscular atrophies (SMAs) although there is no human SMA known to be located within the wr homologous region.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 4 6 4 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 60 61 60 61 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 211 217 211 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rab1a </italic>
###xml 218 219 218 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 227 231 227 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mor2</italic>
###xml 237 240 237 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rel</italic>
###xml 250 254 250 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Otx1</italic>
###xml 255 256 255 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 265 271 265 271 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peli1 </italic>
###xml 272 273 272 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 285 290 285 290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cct4 </italic>
###xml 291 293 291 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 39 44 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 93 98 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The wr mutation is located on proximal mouse chromosome 11 [6] within a region homologous to human Chr2p13-14. Some genes of that region have previously been mapped to our Chr11 / Chr2p13-14 physical map: RAB1A/Rab1a [7], MDH1/Mor2, REL/Rel and OTX1/Otx1[8], PELI1/Peli1 [9], and CCT4/Cct4 [10].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 230 237 230 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
###xml 93 98 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 110 115 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 174 179 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Here we present a detailed transcript map of the orthologous chromosomal regions on proximal mouse Chr 11 and human Chr 2p13-14 with newly mapped genes and newly established mouse cDNA sequences serving as candidate genes for the wobbler mutation. The genomic regions analyzed are highly conserved in gene content, but two independent genomic clones indicate that the gene order is disrupted by an inversion.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Results
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 216 217 216 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 269 270 269 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 20 25 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 235 240 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 315 320 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Our physical map of mouse proximal Chr 11 is based on 68 BAC and 11 YAC clones covering 3 Mb. The present mostly BAC-based contig has been established by STS/EST mapping improves the previously described YAC contig [8]. Our homologous human YAC contig (twelve clones, [8]) spans approximately 4.5 MB, exceeding the mouse contig on the distal side.
###end p 14

###xml 698 703 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 818 823 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The human pseudogenes endozepine like peptide (ELP) [16] and px19-like protein (PX19) [17] are localized in the human genome sequence of the interval under study and their positions were confirmed by STS-mapping on our human physical map. However, their counterparts were not detected on the mouse contig. Murine Elp was radiation hybrid mapped but located far distally (at 40 cM) on Chr11 (Fig 1). The product of Elp is a testis specific isoform of the ubiquitous acyl-CoA binding protein (ACBP) which is highly expressed in the haploid stages of male germ cell development [16]. PX19 was first identified in chicken as a cDNA with a LEA (late embryogenesis abundant) motif [17]. By searching the human draft genome sequence we detected more than 5 intronless copies of this gene, and also in the limited data of the mouse genome several copies were present (data not shown).

Murine genomic clones, YAC clone ymWIBR100H6 (500 Kb) and BAC clone 135B4 (200 kb), comprised sequences from both, Kiaa0903 and Hspc159; this indicated a different gene order of mouse and man in the wobbler critical region (Fig. 1). Within each segment order and orientation of genes are conserved between man and mouse, but the central one is inverted. The distal segment is defined by Rel and Murr1, the proximal one by Hspc159 and Rab1a and the central one by Kiaa0903 and Peli1.

###begin title 21
Segregation analysis of the wr candidate interval
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 30 37 30 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
###xml 79 80 79 80 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 118 130 118 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus musculus</italic>
###xml 140 142 140 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 145 167 145 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mus musculus castaneus</italic>
###xml 118 130 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mus musculus</span>
###xml 145 167 <span type="species:ncbi:10091">Mus musculus castaneus</span>
The candidate interval of the wobbler mutation was refined in comparison with [8] by using the interspecies backcross Mus musculus C57BL/6J-wr x Mus musculus castaneus.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 187 189 187 189 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 252 257 252 257 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Murr1</italic>
###xml 262 264 262 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 402 410 402 410 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kiaa0903</italic>
###xml 416 421 416 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Murr1</italic>
New polymorphisms between the two strains were established all over the candidate region using BAC-end or intron sequences. Two recombinations were detected between BAC-end 147N22rev and wr; and four recombinations were detected between anonymous cDNA Murr1 and wr. Thus the candidate region of the wobbler mutation was narrowed down to the interval between the two loci 147N22rev (located distally to Kiaa0903) and Murr1.
###end p 23

###xml 207 208 207 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 211 216 211 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mor2 </italic>
###xml 217 218 217 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 225 231 225 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peli1 </italic>
###xml 232 233 232 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 562 569 562 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
###xml 289 294 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 465 470 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Interestingly, the apparently inverted region seems to be identical to the wobbler critical interval. It contains the newly mapped genes Hcc8, Ugp2, Kiaa0903, Homoloc13 and the previously mapped genes Otx1 [8], Mor2 [8], and Peli1 [9]. Our high resolution physical maps in addition to the human draft sequence suggests that all orthologous genes have been identified. Because no additional genes were found by BLAST search using genomic sequences or by analysis of mouse sequences with exon prediction programs we believe that the set of candidate genes for the wobbler mutation is complete.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 54 58 54 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hcc8</italic>
###xml 60 64 60 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ugp2</italic>
###xml 66 85 66 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kiaa0903, Homoloc13</italic>
###xml 117 121 117 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Otx1</italic>
###xml 406 413 406 413 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
###xml 415 417 415 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 418 420 418 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 562 569 562 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
###xml 441 445 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>

All genes were subjected to mutation analysis by comparative sequencing of the coding regions, and parts of 3' and 5' UTRs, but no mutation was detected so far. Using RNAs from testis and spinal cord (the affected organs) of adult wobbler (wr/wr) and wildtype (+/+) mice, transcript levels of the canididate genes were analyzed by Northern blotting and RT-PCR, but no difference between wobbler and wildtype mice was found (data not shown).

###begin title 29
Discussion
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 503 508 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Knowledge of gene order and content is crucial for the identification of disease genes. During the draft phase of genome projects sequence information has to be used with caution since multiple gaps lead to incorrect orientation and order of the sequence fragments. High-resolution maps based on contigs of genomic clones as presented in this work help to overcome this problem. For example, the positions of genes UGP2 and HCC8, whose 5' ends are less than 2 kb apart, were shown to be inverted in the human draft version relative to the correct orientation, and most of the UGP2 gene was not available. The correct gene order was resolved with the high resolution map presented here. Furthermore, genes are missing from the chromosome maps if their transcripts have not been described although the protein is known. For example, OTX1 was not annotated in the draft sequence because its cDNA sequence was not present in the database.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 784 786 784 786 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 808 809 808 809 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 106 111 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 122 127 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 286 291 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 341 346 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 469 474 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 634 639 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 644 647 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
###xml 750 755 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 771 776 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
By the identification of 20 orthologous gene pairs we were able to show that the gene content of proximal mouse Chr11 and human Chr2p13-14 is highly conserved. On the other hand, the gene order appears disrupted by an inversion which comprises 1.5 Mb. Despite the fact that the present mouse draft sequence suggests an orientation as in the human genome our results indicate an inversion. The exact inversion breakpoints are not known, and despite extensive screens no mouse BACs spanning the distal breakpoint were detected. Low complexity and reiterative sequences may be responsible for both, the chromosomal rearrangement between mouse and man and the difficulty to clone these regions. A comparable inversion of about 1 Mb has been reported for human Chr19p13.3 and mouse Chr10 [22]. Carver and Stubbs [1] suggest that continuous regions of conserved syntenies without insertions, deletions and inversions are rather the exception than the rule. Further small-scale rearrangements within regions of conserved synteny are expected to be discovered with high-resolution maps and availability of sequence data.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 327 329 327 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 387 390 387 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Elp</italic>
###xml 921 929 921 929 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Homoloc2</italic>
###xml 130 135 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 353 358 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 380 386 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Murine</span>
###xml 641 646 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 903 908 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1064 1067 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
###xml 1072 1077 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The only exceptions from the conservation of gene content are PX19 and ELP which are probably pseudogenes that have integrated on human Chr2p after the divergence of rodents and primates. Although some cDNA clones are listed in the databases, no expression of ELP could be detected and the sequences contain several mutations [23]. We conclude that the human ELP is a pseudogene. Murine Elp probably arose by retroposon-mediated gene duplication and secondarily acquired a testis-specific promotor and thus a limited expression. The Chr2p copy of PX19 is truncated compared to the functional PX19 cDNA, with the 3' UTR partially missing. No human EST corresponding to the Chr2p copy was detected, implying it to be a pseudogene. The polyA tail in the genomic sequence suggests that it may have arisen by the integration of an alternatively polyadenylated PX19 transcript. Although first identified as a human cDNA clone, Homoloc2 neither has an open reading frame nor could a transcript be detected by RT-PCR. The high conservation of its genomic sequence between man and mouse suggests a regulatory function.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 65 72 65 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
Establishing full-length cDNA sequences for all genes within the wobbler critical region was the basis for an extensive comparative sequencing effort aimed to detect a mutation. So far, no mutation and no difference of transcript levels was found.
###end p 33
###begin title 34
Conclusions
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 122 124 122 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 464 471 464 471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
###xml 16 21 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 22 25 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">man</span>
###xml 199 204 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 274 279 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The comparative mouse-man genomics approach has proven a valuable tool to find new candidate genes for disease mutations [24]. However, inversions and insertions have to be taken into account if the human genome is to be used as a framework for the sequence assembly of the mouse. Besides providing new candidates comparative genomics leads to the identification of conserved noncoding sequences, which might have important biological functions. Mutations such as wobbler for which all candidate genes have been excluded might affect a conserved noncoding sequence with a cis-acting effect on genes that might be located outside the critical region as defined by recombination.
###end p 35
###begin title 36
Methods
###end title 36
###begin title 37
BAC and YAC clones
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 76 77 76 77 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 8 13 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 41 46 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
For the mouse BAC and YAC clones and the human YAC clones see Resch et al. [8]. The contigs were established by extensive STS/EST mapping and genomic sequencing.
###end p 38
###begin title 39
STS/EST mapping
###end title 39
###begin p 40
Standard PCR reactions were: 12.5 mul reaction volume of Qiagen (Hilden, Germany) Taq Mastermix Kit with 12.5 pmoles of each primer. PCR was performed on all BACs and YACs using 50 ng of template DNA.
###end p 40
###begin title 41
Search for orthologous ESTs
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 257 258 257 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 12 17 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 34 39 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Orthologous mouse ESTs for mapped human ESTs were identified via BLAST search. For ESTs with greater than 80% homology to ESTs of other species, PCR primers were generated. Subsequently, physical mapping was carried out by STS and radiation hybrid mapping [8].
###end p 42
###begin title 43
Preparation of RNA and RT-PCR
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 28 35 28 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wobbler</italic>
###xml 37 39 37 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 40 42 40 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wr</italic>
###xml 118 120 118 120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
###xml 89 93 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 123 128 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Mouse RNA from wildtype and wobbler (wr/wr)was prepared from various tissues of C57Bl/6J mice by standard procedures [23]. Human RNA was purchased from Clontech (Palo Alto, USA). One mug of total RNA was reverse transcribed using the Omniscript RT-Kit from Qiagen (Hilden, Germany).
###end p 44
###begin title 45
Expansion of ESTs to full length cDNAs
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 138 147 138 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in silico</italic>
In order to expand EST sequences (length usually < 500 bp), overlapping sequences of cDNA clones belonging to a single EST were assembled in silico. To verify the assembled sequences, primers were generated for each end, and the transcript was amplified by RT-PCR, cloned and sequenced.
###end p 46
