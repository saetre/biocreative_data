###begin p 3
###xml 0 10 0 10 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OBJECTIVE&#8212;</bold>
###xml 109 114 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
OBJECTIVE-To document the transcriptome of the pancreatic islet during the early and late development of the mouse pancreas and highlight the qualitative and quantitative features of gene expression that contribute to the specification, growth, and differentiation of the major endocrine cell types. A further objective was to identify endocrine cell biomarkers, targets of diabetic autoimmunity, and regulatory pathways underlying islet responses to physiological and pathological stimuli.
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 0 28 0 28 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RESEARCH DESIGN AND METHODS&#8212;</bold>
###xml 143 147 143 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 154 158 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
RESEARCH DESIGN AND METHODS-mRNA expression profiling was performed by microarray analysis of e12.5-18.5 embryonic pancreas from neurogenin 3 (Ngn3)-null mice, a background that abrogates endocrine pancreatic differentiation. The intersection of this data with mRNA expression in isolated adult pancreatic islets and pancreatic endocrine tumor cell lines was determined to compile lists of genes that are specifically expressed in endocrine cells.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 0 8 0 8 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RESULTS&#8212;</bold>
###xml 386 390 383 387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZnT8</italic>
RESULTS-The data provided insight into the transcriptional and morphogenetic factors that may play major roles in patterning and differentiation of the endocrine lineage before and during the secondary transition of endocrine development, as well as genes that control the glucose responsiveness of the beta-cells and candidate diabetes autoantigens, such as insulin, IA-2 and Slc30a8 (ZnT8). The results are presented as downloadable gene lists, available at , stratified by predictive scores of relative cell-type specificity.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 0 12 0 12 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CONCLUSIONS&#8212;</bold>
CONCLUSIONS-The deposited data provide a rich resource that can be used to address diverse questions related to islet developmental and cell biology and the pathogenesis of type 1 and 2 diabetes.

###begin p 10
###xml 220 221 220 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 357 358 357 358 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 359 360 359 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 704 708 704 708 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 841 842 841 842 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 941 942 941 942 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 949 953 949 953 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 953 954 953 954 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 954 955 954 955 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">/</sup>
###xml 955 956 955 956 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1041 1042 1041 1042 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 1141 1146 1141 1146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ptf1a</italic>
###xml 1151 1156 1151 1156 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Prox1</italic>
###xml 1249 1250 1249 1250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 725 729 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 957 961 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The major endocrine and exocrine cell types of the pancreas share a common embryological origin from the foregut endoderm and possess common phenotypic characteristics related to the regulated secretion of polypeptides (1). Microarray-based gene expression analysis of the genes that are related by ontology and common pathways of metabolism and signaling (2-4) in the tissue is hampered by difficulty of separating endocrine from exocrine tissue at early embryological ages except by laborious procedures based on cell sorting that may distort the expression profiles. An alternative approach adopted here is to compare gene expression in fetal pancreas from E12.5-18.5 from wild-type and neurogenin 3 (Ngn3) knockout (-/-) mice: animals in which the ductal and exocrine programs proceed normally but islet endocrine cells fail to develop (5). Ngn3 is expressed transiently around E14.5 in a small population of endocrine progenitor cells (6), and Ngn3-/- mice fail to express key islet transcription factors Isl1, Pax4, Pax6, and NeuroD1 (5). Nevertheless, pancreatic exocrine cell lineage is correctly specified through the expression of Ptf1a and Prox1, and only minor changes in acinar cellular polarity and zymogen granule accumulation occur (5).
###end p 10

###xml 43 47 43 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 47 50 47 50 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 168 169 168 169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 264 268 264 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 268 271 268 271 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 296 303 296 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Slc30a8</italic>
###xml 305 309 305 309 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZnT8</italic>
###xml 366 367 366 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 417 418 417 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 108 113 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Data expression profiles from pancreata of Ngn3-/- versus wild-type animals were compared with normal adult mouse pancreatic islets and islet-derived tumor cell lines (7) to provide information on endocrine cell-type specificity of genes that are downregulated in Ngn3-/- animals. The example of Slc30a8 (ZnT8), a gene that has been associated with type 2 diabetes (8) and that encodes a type 1 diabetes autoantigen (9) is highlighted.

###begin title 12
RESEARCH DESIGN AND METHODS
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 84 88 84 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 88 89 88 89 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 89 91 89 91 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 627 629 614 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 62 67 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 438 442 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 551 555 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Embryonic pancreata were harvested from time-mated CD1 strain mouse intercrosses of Ngn3+/- females and males and stored individually in RNAlater (Ambion) at 4degreesC until genotyping by PCR (30 cycles; Ngn3 forward, 5'-cctcttctggctttcactac-3' and reverse, 5'-ggagcgagagtttgatgtgg-3'; neomycin forward, 5'-gtcttgtcgatcaggatgatctc-3' and reverse, 5'-caatatcacgggtagccaacgc-3'). Pancreatic islets were prepared from 6-month-old female CD1 mice by collagenase digestion and isolated by Ficoll/Histopaque gradients and handpicking (each preparation: six mice/1,000 islets). alphaTC1-6, betaTC3', MIN6-, and mPAC-tumor cell lines (10) were grown to 60% confluency in RPMI medium. Total RNA was extracted from tissues with TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA), purified by RNeasy columns (Qiagen, Valencia, CA). Biotin-labeled cRNA was synthesized by the Affymetrix protocol (Affymetrix, Santa Clara, CA) and hybridized to MGU74Av2 and/or MOE 430 2.0 microarrays. Raw microarray datasets are available through EPCON db ().
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 184 185 184 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 247 248 247 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 58 65 <span type="species:ncbi:28980">Redwood</span>
Data were analyzed with GeneSpring 7.2 (Silicon Genetics, Redwood City, CA) and GC Robust Multi-array Average (GC RMA) used for normalization. ANOVA was applied to cell line data, and P values were corrected by Benjamini and Hochberg's procedure. P < 0.05 were considered significant. NetAffx (Affymetrix, Santa Clara, CA; ) was used to extract a common probe set between MOE430 2.0 and MGU74Av2 platforms. Gene classification was performed with FatiGO ().
###end p 14
###begin p 15
Quantitative RT-PCR (qRT-PCR) was performed on cDNA (5 ng) derived from total RNA by iScript cDNA synthesis kits (Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA). Assays used FAM dye-labeled Taqman MGB probes and an ABI 7000 PCR instrument (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA) and were normalized to glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase. Triplicate cycle threshold values were normalized to samples.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 438 442 437 441 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZnT8</italic>
###xml 454 455 453 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
###xml 309 319 <span type="species:ncbi:10141">guinea pig</span>
###xml 334 339 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 377 380 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 426 432 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
Mouse pancreata were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde and embedded in optimal cutting temperature compound (Tissue Tek; Sakura Finetek, Torrance, CA) for immunofluorescence microscopy. Sections (6 mum) were blocked in tyramide signal amplification blocking solution (Zymed, Carlsbad, CA) incubated overnight with guinea pig anti-insulin, mouse anti-glucagon (Sigma, St Louis, MO), rat anti-somatostatin (Abcam, Cambridge, MA), or rabbit anti-ZnT8 aa268-369 (9). Secondary antibodies conjugated to Alexa 488, 1-amino-1-methyl-3(4)-aminomethylcyclohexane, cyanine 5, and cyanine 3 fluorophores (Jackson Immunoresearch Laboratories, West Grove, PA) were applied for 60 min and sections mounted in glycerol-based media. Images were acquired with an Olympus 1X70 microscope equipped with a Photometrics Quantix cooled monochromatic CCD camera (Kodak chip KAF1400).
###end p 16
###begin title 17
RESULTS
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 63 67 63 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 67 70 67 70 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 187 191 187 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 216 218 216 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 219 221 219 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 494 498 494 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 632 636 632 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 636 639 636 639 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 688 695 688 695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 71 75 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Pancreatic microarray analyses were performed on wild-type and Ngn3-/- mice at E12.5, E15.5, and E18.5. The majority of the endocrine fate allocations occur between E13.5 and E15.5, when Ngn3 is maximally expressed (11,12); endocrine markers (insulin and glucagon) subsequently appear along with neuroendocrine markers, e.g., prohormone convertases and chromogranins. Thus, comparison of E12.5 with E15.5 identifies transcripts that characterize precursor cells, responses to the activation of Ngn3 expression, and the transition to the differentiated endocrine fate. At E12.5, 203 gene transcripts were downregulated >threefold in Ngn3-/- versus wild-type littermates and, at E15.5, 54 (Table 1 and supplementary Tables 2 and 3, available in an online appendix at ). Fewer genes were upregulated: 53 at >threefold in E12.5 and 17 in E15.5 (data not shown).
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 56 60 56 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 60 63 60 63 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 262 266 262 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZnT8</italic>
###xml 327 331 327 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 331 334 331 334 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 523 527 523 527 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZnT8</italic>
###xml 585 592 585 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="t2">Table 2</xref>
At E18.5, 645 transcripts were downregulated >3-fold in Ngn3-/- versus wild-type animals (supplementary Table 4), 290 >4-fold, and 35 >10-fold. Validation of select transcripts by qRTPCR demonstrated that glucagon (down 1,400-fold), insulin (down 400-fold), and ZnT8 (down 18-fold) were readily detectable in wild-type but not Ngn3-/- animals with 40 cycles of PCR (data not shown). Of the transcripts that were downregulated >fourfold, 46 mapped to transcripts highly expressed in adult islets (raw value >2,000 including ZnT8) and 28 to transcripts moderately expressed (500-2,000) (Table 2).
###end p 19

###xml 384 387 363 366 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>

###xml 778 779 736 737 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D</italic>
###xml 772 779 730 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1<italic>D</italic></xref>
###xml 1093 1094 1041 1042 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E</italic>
###xml 1087 1094 1035 1042 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1<italic>E</italic></xref>
###xml 1231 1235 1175 1179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZnT8</italic>
###xml 1388 1389 1325 1326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F</italic>
###xml 1382 1389 1319 1326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1<italic>F</italic></xref>
###xml 1419 1423 1356 1360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZnT8</italic>
###xml 388 392 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Comparison of alphaTC1.6- and betaTC3-cell transcripts identified 1,554 transcripts that were >2-fold enriched in betaTC3 versus alphaTC1.6 and 403 that were >10-fold enriched (supplementary Table 5). Conversely, 1,202 transcripts were >2-fold enriched in alphaTC1.6- versus betaTC3-cell lines and 212 >10-fold (supplementary Table 6). Of the genes downregulated >fivefold in the Ngn3-/- mice, 74 were enriched in alphaTC-cells and 108 in betaTC-cell lines (supplementary Tables 7 and 8). Another 1,396 transcripts were expressed in both alphaTC- and betaTC-cell lines and 774 not detected. qRT-PCR analyses of islets and cell lines (Fig. 1A-F) showed that insulin transcripts were enriched in adult islets versus betaTC- and not expressed in alphaTC- or mPAC-cell lines (Fig. 1D). By microarray analysis, insulin expression in islets and beta-cell lines did not differ since signals were saturated (>25,000 arbitrary units). Glucagon transcript levels by qRTPCR were higher in islets than in alphaTC-cells and present at a low level in betaTC3- and MIN6-cells but absent in mPAC-cells (Fig. 1E). By microarray analysis, the glucagon signals saturated in islets and alphaTC-cells but otherwise showed the same trend of expression. ZnT8 transcript levels by qRTPCR were 4- to 5-fold higher in beta-cell lines than in alphaTC1.6-cells and 50- to 100-fold higher than in ductal cells (Fig. 1F). Microarray analysis showed ZnT8 expression in islets and alpha- and beta-cell lines but not in ductal cells.
###end p 20

###xml 36 40 36 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZnT8</italic>
###xml 319 325 315 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="f2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 334 338 330 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 338 341 334 337 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 375 379 371 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZnT8</italic>
Immunohistochemical localization of ZnT8 in wild-type adult pancreas showed its expression in the majority of islet cells and absence in acinar and ductal tissue. It was principally colocalized with insulin and also found in a subpopulation of somatostatin-positive cells. Expression in alpha-cells was not detectable (Fig. 2). E18.5 Ngn3-/- pancreas tissue did not show any ZnT8, insulin, glucagon, or somatostatin expression (data not shown).

###begin title 22
DISCUSSION
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 51 55 51 55 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 55 58 55 58 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 697 701 697 701 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IAPP</italic>
###xml 703 708 703 708 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ChgrA</italic>
###xml 710 715 710 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ChgrB</italic>
###xml 717 720 717 720 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Npy</italic>
###xml 722 725 722 725 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pyy</italic>
###xml 727 732 727 732 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PCSK1</italic>
###xml 734 739 734 739 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PCSK2</italic>
###xml 745 755 745 755 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGRP/G6pc2</italic>
###xml 859 865 859 865 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NeuroD</italic>
###xml 867 871 867 871 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MafB</italic>
###xml 873 878 873 878 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Insm1</italic>
###xml 880 884 880 884 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Myt1</italic>
###xml 886 890 886 890 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pax6</italic>
###xml 896 900 896 900 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Isl1</italic>
###xml 59 63 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The loss of expression of genes in the pancreas of Ngn3-/- mice provides a model to define the transcriptome of the endocrine pancreas and offers insight into the transcriptional and morphogenetic factors responsible for the patterning and differentiation of the endocrine lineage. Genes responsive to paracrine signaling from endocrine cells adjacent to exocrine or ductal cells might also have been revealed, although there was little evidence of this. As previously observed (5), expression of the pancreatic islet hormones (insulin, glucagon, somatostatin, pancreatic polypeptide, and ghrelin) were markedly diminished alongside genes known to be expressed in islets but not in acinar tissue (IAPP, ChgrA, ChgrB, Npy, Pyy, PCSK1, PCSK2, and IGRP/G6pc2). Several candidate transcriptional regulators of islet cell lineage development were likewise absent (NeuroD, MafB, Insm1, Myt1, Pax6, and Isl1).
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 156 161 156 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Robo1</italic>
###xml 197 207 197 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 250 263 250 263 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">9430047L24Rik</italic>
###xml 336 341 336 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Robo1</italic>
###xml 456 458 456 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 459 461 459 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 577 579 577 579 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 197 207 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
At E12.5, there are a few endocrine cells that are immunoreactive to glucagon and occasional insulin and glucagon double-positive cells. During this phase, Robo1 (Dutt1 protein, orthologous to the Drosophila roundabout) and an expressed sequence tag 9430047L24Rik encoding Unc5c homolog are the two most endocrine-enriched transcripts. Robo1 encodes a molecule of the neural-cell adhesion molecule family that interacts with the extracellular ligand Slit (13-15) that has been implicated in migration of axons, myoblasts, and leukocytes in vertebrates and in lung development (16).
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 45 49 45 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 49 52 49 52 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 67 71 67 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Myt1</italic>
###xml 73 89 73 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">crystallin &#946;2</italic>
###xml 91 111 88 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">secretogogin EF-hand</italic>
###xml 117 130 114 127 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4930568N03Rik</italic>
###xml 312 316 309 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Myt1</italic>
###xml 334 338 331 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ngn3</italic>
###xml 366 367 363 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 368 370 365 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 495 499 492 496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nol4</italic>
At E15.5, four transcripts notably absent in Ngn3-/- pancreas were Myt1, crystallin beta2, secretogogin EF-hand, and 4930568N03Rik (Genbank accession no. ). Microarray data defining the pancreatic development kinetics from E12.5 to E18.5 (K. Juhl, S. Sarkar, J. Jensen, J. Hutton, unpublished data) suggest that Myt1 is downstream of Ngn3, as previously documented (3,17). At E18.5, somatostatin and pancreatic polypeptide cells appear and islet formation is initiated. Genes notably absent in Ngn3-/- pancreas included MafB, Nkx6.1, Wbscr14, Nnat, Syt13, and Pcsk1n-genes that function in mature islet cells as transcription factors (MafB, Nkx6.1, and Wbscr14) (18) or in stimulus secretion coupling. Comparison of alphaTC and betaTC expression data with the list of 2,352 genes that were downregulated at any age in Ngn3-/- mice revealed 74 alpha-cell candidates including glucagon, Arx, Brn4, Spp1, Irx2, Rbp4, MafB, Car2, Tfpi, Vegfc, and Fev-pending and 108 beta-cell candidates including Ins1&2, IAPP, Neuronatin, Pdx1, G6Pcrs, Npy, Prcad, Sepp1, Sytl4, Hpca, and Atp2a3. The above microarray datasets can be downloaded from an open access Web site: . While the current study focuses on embryological development of the pancreas, these datasets can potentially be analyzed in other ways to provide an understanding of islet function in health and disease. Given the current interest in identifying biomarkers that could be used to isolate beta-cells and their precursors or to image islet mass, it was of interest to review how effective the selection criteria used here to ascertain beta-cell specificity would be in identifying a beta-cell marker of lower abundance. The example is the insulin granule zinc transporter ZnT8 (Slc30a8), a type 1A diabetes autoantigen (9) and a gene associated with type 2 diabetes susceptibility (8). ZnT8 transcripts were decreased 18-fold in Ngn3-/- mice pancreas at E18.5 (Table 2), in betaTC3- and MIN6-cells, and to a lesser extent in alphaTC-cells (Fig. 1A and F) but not in a mouse pancreatic ductal cell line (mPAC). Immunofluorescence microscopy showed colocalization with insulin and with a minor islet cell population that coincides with the delta-cell. A lower level of expression in the alpha-cell was indicated by qRTPCR and other studies (22) but could not be confirmed by immunohistochemistry. A similar strategy could apply the current datasets for the discovery of novel regulatory processes involved in islet metabolism, stimulus secretion coupling, gene transcription, and intracellular and intracellular signaling.
