###begin article-title 0
Sclera-related gene polymorphisms in high myopia
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Purpose
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 326 335 320 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TGF-&#946;2</italic>
###xml 337 341 328 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bFGF</italic>
###xml 347 351 338 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FMOD</italic>
Transforming growth factor-beta2 (TGF-beta2), basic fibroblast growth factor (bFGF), and fibromodulin (FMOD) are important extracellular matrix components of the sclera and have been shown to be associated with the development of high myopia. Our aim was to examine the association between myopia and the polymorphisms within TGF-beta2, bFGF, and FMOD.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Methods
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 558 567 558 564 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TGF-&#946;2</italic>
###xml 577 581 574 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bFGF</italic>
###xml 595 599 592 596 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FMOD</italic>
###xml 29 37 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The study group comprised of patients (n=195; age range: 17-24 years) with a spherical equivalent of -6.5 diopters (D) or a more negative refractive error. The control group comprised of individuals (n=94; age range: 17-25 years) with a spherical equivalent ranging from -0.5 D to +1.0 D. The subjects with astigmatism over -0.75 D were excluded from the study. High resolution melting (HRM) genotyping and restriction fragment length polymorphism (RFLP) genotyping were used to detect single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs). The polymorphisms detected were TGF-beta2 ( and ), bFGF ( and ), and FMOD (). Moreover, a stepwise logistic regression procedure was used to detect which of the significant SNPs contributed to the main effects of myopia development.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Results
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 88 98 88 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TGF-&#946;2 </italic>
###xml 201 210 198 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TGF-&#946;2</italic>
###xml 340 349 334 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TGF-&#946;2</italic>
There were significant differences in the frequency of the A allele and A/A genotype in TGF-beta2 (; p=0.0178 and 0.03, respectively). Moreover, the haplotype distribution of haplotype 2 (Ht2; A/A) of TGF-beta2 differed significantly between the two groups (p=0.014). The results of the stepwise logistic regression procedure revealed that TGF-beta2 () contributed significantly to the development of high myopia.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Conclusions
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 95 105 92 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TGF-&#946;2 </italic>
TGF-beta2 is an important structure of sclera and might contribute to the formation of myopia. TGF-beta2 () polymorphisms, A allele and A/A genotype, had a protective role against the development of high myopia.

###begin title 9
Introduction
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 105 106 105 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 152 153 152 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 154 155 154 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 523 524 523 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 652 653 652 653 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
The prevalence of myopia ranges from 20% to 30% in North American, European, and Australian populations [1] and is as high as 90% in Asian populations [2,3]. Low to moderate degrees of myopia present a relatively minor inconvenience as the symptoms are easily corrected using spectacles or contact lenses. High degrees of myopia, while also correctable using these optical approaches, are of major concern because of the increased risk of myopia-related pathology such as chorioretinal degeneration and retinal detachment [4]. The ocular pathology associated with high myopia is among the leading causes of registered blindness in the developed world [5].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 155 156 155 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 256 257 256 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 490 491 490 491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 719 720 719 720 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 951 952 942 943 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 1133 1134 1115 1116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 1135 1137 1117 1119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
Many studies have suggested that myopia is a complex disease with multiple causes, including the interaction of multiple genes with environmental stimuli [6]. Therefore, it is necessary to take both genes and environment into account to understand myopia [6]. As for pathogenesis of high myopia, studies of high myopia in animal models have demonstrated that increasing eye size facilitated by remodeling of the sclera was one of the most important etiologies in the progression of myopia [7]. The sclera consists of fibrous connective tissue comprised largely of heterologous collagen fibrils, which are in turn composed mainly of type I collagen with small amounts of other fibrillar and fibril-associated collagens [8]. An important candidate in the search for factors involved in scleral remodeling in myopia is transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-beta). Transforming growth factor-beta is known to initiate myofibroblast formation and fibrosis [9]. Although five members of the TGF-beta family have currently been identified, only TGF-beta isoforms 1, 2, and 3 have been detected in eyes and TGF-beta2 is the predominant form [9,10].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 396 398 393 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 631 633 628 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 792 794 789 791 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 824 833 821 827 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TGF-&#946;2</italic>
###xml 841 845 835 839 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bFGF</italic>
###xml 886 895 880 886 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TGF-&#946;2</italic>
###xml 901 901 892 892 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"/>
###xml 902 906 893 897 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bFGF</italic>
In addition to TGF-beta, basic fibroblast growth factors (bFGFs) are also involved in the remodeling of sclera. bFGFs with their receptors (FGFRs) and signaling cascades are involved in a diverse range of cellular processes including proliferation, apoptosis, cell survival, chemotaxis, cell adhesion, motility, and differentiation. bFGF-2 is a potent mitogen for fibroblasts and myofibroblasts [11]. Moreover, the major extracellular matrix components of the fibrous mammalian sclera are collagen types I and III and the small leucine rich proteoglycans (SLRPs), which include decorin, biglycan, lumican, and fibromodulin (FMOD) [12]. There is considerable evidence that FMOD is involved in regulating the formation of the network of collagen fibrils that makes up the extracellular matrix [13]. Among the polymorphisms,  (TGF-beta2) and  (bFGF) are found in the promoter region and  (TGF-beta2) and (bFGF) are found in the 3'-UTR region. Polymorphisms in 5'-UTR and 3'-UTR might have effects on the translation and stability of the genes. We selected these polymorphisms as candidates in this study.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Methods
###end title 13
###begin title 14
###xml 0 8 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Patients</span>
Patients
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 172 179 172 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 97 109 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
###xml 859 867 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Patients</span>
###xml 1193 1201 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1326 1331 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
From February to November 2004, we measured the refractive error in 3,000 volunteers. All of the participants were medical students, unrelated and Taiwan-born Han Chinese (Table 1). The included subjects had a visual acuity with distance correction of 0.2 logMAR (logarithm of the minimal angle of resolution; 20/32) or better. The included subjects did not have any systemic diseases. They also did not receive ocular surgery before. Refractive error was measured in diopters (D) and determined by the mean spherical equivalent (SE) of the two eyes of each individual after administering one drop of cycloplegic drug (1% mydricyl; Alcon, Berlin, Germany). Individuals with -6.5 D or a more negative refractive error (both eyes) were included in the study group, and those with myopia between -0.5 D and +1.0 D (both eyes) were included in the control group. Patients with astigmatism with a refractive error more negative than -0.75 D were excluded from the study. Other volunteers did not meet the criteria to be included in either the study or control groups, and they were excluded from our study. Our study was reviewed by the ethics committee, and informed consent was obtained from all patients and control subjects. The study was performed according to the tenets of the Declaration of Helsinki for research involving human subjects. Comprehensive ophthalmic examinations and blood collection were performed. As with all data collection procedures, auto-refraction (Auto-refractor/auto-keratometer; ARK 700A; Nikon, Tokyo, Japan) was taken on both eyes by experienced optometrists who were trained and certified on study protocols. Refractive data, sphere(s), negative cylinder(c), and axis measurements were analyzed by calculating refractive error with SE.

The single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) were selected based on three criteria. (1) The selected SNP had a frequency in the CHB (Han Chinese in Beijing, China) or JPNJapanese) population as documented in the  project, and the heterozygosity should be more than 10%. (2) The selected SNP should be in the region of the promoter, 5'-UTR, exons, and 3'-UTR of the gene. We excluded any intron SNP because it is hard to predict the outcome of the polymorphism. (3) In exons, only non-synonymous SNPs were selected. Based on these criteria, only a few SNPs could be selected. For FMOD, the selected SNP was based on sequencing experiments. The exons of FMOD were sequenced among 50 randomly selected individuals to reveal the SNPs in FMOD. Only one SNP was detected. As for selecting haplotype,  genotypes were analysed within  and tag SNPs were selected using . Four tag SNPs were selected for each gene with r2>/=0.80 to capture 80% of genotype information in the region. The average tag SNP was with r2=0.901.

###begin title 28
Statistical analysis
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 126 127 124 125 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
###xml 353 354 349 350 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
Genotypes were obtained by direct counting with subsequent calculation of allele frequencies. Data were analyzed using the chi2 test, and p values were calculated using the Minitab program (Minitab Inc., San Jose, CA). A p value less than 0.05 was considered significant. Adherence to the Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE) constant was tested using a chi2 test with one degree of freedom. The odds ratios (OR) and the corresponding 95% confidence intervals (CI) were calculated with reference to the allele and genotype. Correction for multiple comparisons was performed by Bonferroni correction. For the SNPs that were found to be significant after multiple testing corrections, stepwise logistic regression was used to determine if any of these SNPs could account for the effects of the other positive SNPs. STATA package (version 8.2; Stata Corp., College Station, TX) was used to perform the stepwise logistic regression.

###xml 481 483 478 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 468 476 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 711 717 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">people</span>
Some genes have been found to be closely related to high myopia such as matrix metalloproteinases [15], paired box 6 [16], myocilin [17], TGF-beta1 [18], Paired box gene 6 (PAX-6) [19], and Collagen, type I, alpha 1 (COL1A1) [20]. These genes all indicate an evident predisposition for the development of high myopia. However, none of these genes have been found to be solely responsible for the development of myopia in different ethnic groups or to cover all myopia patients [15-21]. The other difficulty in myopic study is the uncertainty of what are the surrounding environmental influences that promote the progression of myopia. Individuals with a higher education have a higher prevalence of myopia than people in the general population, which is why students in a medical school were chosen as candidates in this study. The medical school's students in this study decreased the bias of environmental influence.

In this study, we found that the distribution of the TGF-beta2 () A/A genotype and A allele differed significantly between the myopia and control groups. The A/A genotype and A allele of TGF-beta2 () had a protective effect against myopia (Table 4). In the analysis of haplotypes, we found that Ht 2 (A/A) of TGF-beta2 was significantly different in the incidence of high myopia. The frequency of the Ht 2 (A/A) of TGF-beta2 was higher in the control group then in the myopia group (p=0.014), indicating that Ht 2 of TGF-beta2 polymorphisms had a protective effect against myopia. In TGF-beta2, combining the analyses of allelic and haplotype study, we could indicate that the A allele of TGF-beta2 () had a protective effect against high myopia, and its effect would increase when the subjects had the A allele in TGF-beta2 (; Ht2 [A/A] of TGF-beta2; Table 3). As for bFGF, the frequencies of the haplotypes were all without significant differences between the two groups (Table 3). According to our study, the genetic polymorphisms in bFGF did not play any important role in the suffering of high myopia. Besides, the results of stepwise regression procedure demonstrated that S1 (TGF-beta2 []) contributed significantly to the development of myopia (p=0.04) (Table 5). There were no significant effects when S5 (FMOD []), S4 (bFGF []), S3 (bFGF []), and S2 (TGF-beta2 []) were eliminated from the model (Table 5). This also reinforced the importance of TGF-beta2 () in the development of high myopia. In the check of the Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE), all SNPs in this study obeyed HWE except bFGF (; Table 4). To validate our findings, we repeated the genotyping analysis several times and consistent results were obtained. The errors in genotyping were kept to a minimum. We tried to trace the ancestry background of the controls and patients to check the probability of population stratification. According to the paper published by Pan et al. [22], they mentioned that on the SNP profiles in the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) region (6p21.3), the composition of our population is as follows: 85% are Minnan descendants, 5% are Hakka descendants, and the remaining 10% are mixed population of Minnan, Hakka, and Canton descendants. There was no significant difference in the MHC region among these groups, this indicated that the population in this study was homogeneous and population stratification should not affect this study. Moreover, the deviations from HWE might be a sign of mutation. This disequilibrium seems to also support the association of the polymorphisms in TGF-beta2 with high myopia.

###xml 2364 2366 2290 2292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
###xml 2462 2467 2385 2390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TGFb2</italic>
The expression of TGF-beta1 and TGF-beta2 were different in myopia. A study by Hayashi et al. [23] revealed that there was no significant relationship between high myopia and TGF-beta1 among the Japanese [23]. Furthermore, Kusakari et al. [24] reported that TGF-beta1 expression was reduced in the sclera, choroid, and retina during myopia development in an isoform- and time-specific manner. However, it has been shown that the TGF-beta2 content increased in the retina, choroid, and sclera during myopia development [25]. In this study, we found that expression of TGF-beta2 was different from the result of TGF-beta1 among the Japanese. The different expressions of TGF-beta2 and TGF-beta1 in myopia could be understood by the different behavior of the two isoforms in sclera during the progression of myopia. Moreover, our other study has found that the CC genotype in the TGF-beta1 codon 10 polymorphism () contributes to the genetic predisposition of high myopia [18]. The SNP we detected (TGF-beta1 []) was not the purpose in the study of Hayashi et al. [23], which is about the relation of TGF-beta1 and myopia in the Japanese. To refine the role of TGF-beta1 in myopia, it is still necessary to study the expression of TGF-beta1 in other populations. Studies on the TGF-beta-induced factor gene(TGIF) [26] had revealed that TGIF 657 T-->G was a useful marker for high myopia. The target genes of these studies were related to TGF-beta1 and not TGF-beta, which was surveyed in this study. These data may be collected together for mapping high myopia in the future. TGF-beta is one of the growth factors released from photoreceptor and retina pigment epithelium layer in the retina [27], TGF-beta demonstrated a different broad range of function [28]. A previous study had proposed that the concentration of TGF-beta mRNA and active form of the TGF-beta protein will decrease in the form-deprived myopia eyes compared with the control group [29]. Consequently, TGF-beta may mediate the control of ocular axial elongation then influence the progression of high myopia [29]. A recent study about TGF-beta revealed that reduced scleral TGF-beta is a major contributor to the extracellular matrix remodeling in the myopic eye. In the progression of myopia, remodeling of scleral and producing a larger population of contractile cells are the important process [28]. These findings all revealed that important role of TGF-beta in myopia. The function assay of TGFb2- and the genetic regulation the SNP is worthy of further investigation.

###begin p 45
###xml 601 603 601 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r30">30</xref>
Myopia is considered to be a complex and multigenic disease involving several overlapping signaling pathways, each one mediated by a group of distinct genetic profiles. Therefore, it is important to study the polymorphisms involved in the development of myopia to further clarify the relationship between genes and myopia. In this study we prevented the effects of environment by selected the study and control groups from medical school students. Nevertheless, the "environment" effects cannot be completely prevented. Moreover, the incidence of myopia in our country increased in the past 20 years [30]; these all indicated that the effective of environment would not be over-emphasized. Although the results of this study could give us the genetic information of myopia, the prevention of high myopia still needs the efforts of eye doctors and the association of public health.
