###xml 1476 1478 1451 1453 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
Ni2+ inhibits current through calcium channels, in part by blocking the pore, but Ni2+ may also allosterically affect channel activity via sites outside the permeation pathway. As a test for pore blockade, we examined whether the effect of Ni2+ on CaV3.1 is affected by permeant ions. We find two components to block by Ni2+, a rapid block with little voltage dependence, and a slow block most visible as accelerated tail currents. Rapid block is weaker for outward vs. inward currents (apparent Kd = 3 vs. 1 mM Ni2+, with 2 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+) and is reduced at high permeant ion concentration (110 vs. 2 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+). Slow block depends both on the concentration and on the identity of the permeant ion (Ca2+ vs. Ba2+ vs. Na+). Slow block is 2-3x faster in Ba2+ than in Ca2+ (2 or 110 mM), and is approximately10x faster with 2 vs. 110 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+. Slow block is orders of magnitude slower than the diffusion limit, except in the nominal absence of divalent cations (approximately3 muM Ca2+). We conclude that both fast and slow block of CaV3.1 by Ni2+ are most consistent with occlusion of the pore. The exit rate of Ni2+ for slow block is reduced at high Ni2+ concentrations, suggesting that the site responsible for fast block can "lock in" slow block by Ni2+, at a site located deeper within the pore. In contrast to the complex pore block observed for CaV3.1, inhibition of CaV3.2 by Ni2+ was essentially independent of voltage, and was similar in 2 mM Ca2+ vs. Ba2+, consistent with inhibition by a different mechanism, at a site outside the pore.

###begin title 5
INTRODUCTION
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 413 432 413 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib1">Almers et al., 1984</xref>
###xml 434 454 434 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib6">Hess and Tsien, 1984</xref>
###xml 674 691 674 691 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Yang et al., 1993</xref>
###xml 693 717 693 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Obejero-Paz et al., 2004</xref>
Many ions block channels by binding within the pore, preventing flux of permeant ions. In particular, several di- and trivalent cations bind with high affinity to the ion selectivity filter of voltage-dependent cation channels. Indeed, that is how calcium itself blocks permeation of monovalent cations, introducing calcium selectivity into a channel that has a high intrinsic conductance for monovalent cations (Almers et al., 1984; Hess and Tsien, 1984). High affinity blockers, which can block not only flux of monovalent cations but also current carried by calcium ions, have been especially useful as probes for the functional architecture of the calcium channel pore (Yang et al., 1993; Obejero-Paz et al., 2004).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 79 80 79 80 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 250 252 250 252 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 361 383 361 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">Narahashi et al., 1987</xref>
###xml 385 406 385 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">Hagiwara et al., 1988</xref>
###xml 483 484 483 484 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 516 518 516 518 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 520 536 520 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib11">Lee et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 583 584 583 584 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 613 615 613 615 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 617 635 617 635 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">Soong et al., 1993</xref>
###xml 676 678 676 678 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 867 887 867 887 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">Zamponi et al., 1996</xref>
###xml 890 912 890 912 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib10">Lacinova et al. (2000)</xref>
###xml 929 931 929 931 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 962 963 962 963 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 1024 1042 1024 1042 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Diaz et al. (2005)</xref>
###xml 1118 1120 1118 1120 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
Although the fundamental mechanism of ion selectivity is conserved among the CaV family of voltage-dependent calcium channels, it has long been recognized that there are significant quantitative differences in selectivity for permeation and block. Ni2+ has received particular attention. Originally thought to be a selective blocker of T-type calcium channels (Narahashi et al., 1987; Hagiwara et al., 1988), it is now clear that only one of the three cloned mammalian T-channels, CaV3.2, is strongly inhibited by Ni2+ (Lee et al., 1999), while one high voltage-activated channel, CaV2.3, is quite sensitive to Ni2+ (Soong et al., 1993). However, it has been suggested that Ni2+ acts in part by binding to a site on the extracellular side of the channel, affecting current through the channel allosterically, not only by binding within the permeation pathway itself (Zamponi et al., 1996). Lacinova et al. (2000) reported that Ni2+ accelerated deactivation of CaV3.1, interpreted as a modification of activation gating, but Diaz et al. (2005) concluded instead that this effect resulted from time-dependent block by Ni2+.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 75 77 75 77 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 83 84 83 84 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 182 199 182 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Kang et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 274 275 274 275 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 296 298 296 298 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50</sub>
###xml 322 323 321 322 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 361 362 360 361 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 404 406 403 405 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50</sub>
###xml 476 478 474 476 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 526 543 524 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Kang et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 648 649 646 647 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 658 660 656 658 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 695 697 693 695 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 703 704 701 702 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 744 761 742 759 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Diaz et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 763 780 761 778 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Kang et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 866 867 864 865 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 890 891 888 889 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 896 912 894 910 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib11">Lee et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 914 934 912 932 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Serrano et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 936 960 934 958 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Obejero-Paz et al., 2004</xref>
Recently, mutational analysis has found that high affinity inhibition by Ni2+ of CaV3.2 requires a histidine near the extracellular side of the S4 transmembrane segment of domain I (Kang et al., 2005). Replacement of histidine with glutamine (the corresponding residue in CaV3.1) increased the IC50 from 5 to 300 muM in CaV3.2. The reverse mutation (Q172H in CaV3.1) had a partial effect, changing the IC50 from 300 to 60 muM. This suggests that high affinity inhibition by Ni2+ results from binding to an extracellular site (Kang et al., 2005). That result also reopens the question of the mechanism of the relatively low affinity inhibition of CaV3.1 by Ni2+. One clue is that the effect of Ni2+ on CaV3.2 is essentially voltage independent (Diaz et al., 2005; Kang et al., 2005), while pore block by di- and trivalent cations is typically voltage dependent for CaV channels, including CaV3.1 (Lee et al., 1999; Serrano et al., 2000; Obejero-Paz et al., 2004).
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 53 55 53 55 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 72 73 72 73 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 350 367 350 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Yang et al., 1993</xref>
###xml 405 407 405 407 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 443 445 443 445 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 461 463 461 463 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 470 471 470 471 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 510 512 510 512 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 519 521 519 521 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 544 564 544 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Serrano et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 663 665 663 665 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3+</sup>
###xml 684 686 684 686 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 693 695 693 695 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 730 754 730 754 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Obejero-Paz et al., 2004</xref>
We examine here not only the voltage dependence of Ni2+ inhibition of CaV3.1, but also the dependence on the chemical nature and the concentration of the permeant ion. The potency of pore block is strongly influenced by competitive interactions among ions in the pore; for example, block is much stronger when monovalent ions are the charge carrier (Yang et al., 1993). We have also found that block by Mg2+ is considerably stronger in 2 mM Ba2+ than in 2 mM Ca2+ for CaV3.1, even though currents carried by Ba2+ and Ca2+ are quite comparable (Serrano et al., 2000). The nature of the ionic effects may vary among blocking ions, however, as the rate of block by Y3+ is sensitive to Ca2+ vs. Ba2+ for closed but not open channels (Obejero-Paz et al., 2004).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 22 24 22 24 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 57 58 57 58 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 299 301 299 301 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 414 416 414 416 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 426 427 426 427 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 523 524 523 524 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
We report here that Ni2+ has two inhibitory effects on CaV3.1 channels, a rapid inhibition that is ion dependent but weakly voltage dependent, and a slower block (reflected in tail current acceleration) that is both ion and voltage dependent. Both effects are most consistent with interactions of Ni2+ with the ion conduction pathway, as opposed to an extracellular allosteric site (see Fig. S1, available at ). Ni2+ blocks CaV3.1 by mechanisms fundamentally different from the voltage-independent high-affinity block of CaV3.2.
###end p 10

MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 11

###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 19 20 19 20 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 187 188 186 187 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 181 184 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Experiments with CaV3.2 used transiently transfected HEK 293 cells. Cells were transfected at 80% confluence in 35-mm dishes using FuGENE6 (Roche) with 1.5 mug plasmid DNA encoding rat CaV3.2 and 0.5 mug CD8 pcDNA (gifts from E. Perez-Reyes [University of Virginia, Charlottesville, VA] and E. Ficker [Case Western Reserve University], respectively). One day after transfection, cells were dispersed with trypsin and transferred to coverslips. On the day of the experiment, usually 2 d after plating, transfected cells were identified using 1 mul Dynabeads CD8 (Dynal Biotech).
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 205 207 171 173 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 258 259 224 225 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>

Access resistances in the whole-cell configuration were 5 +/- 1 MOmega and were compensated by approximately90% ("correction") and approximately50% ("prediction"). Ni2+ was added in the concentrations indicated (as NiCl2) without other change in the extracellular solution.

###begin p 15
###xml 13 15 13 15 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 525 527 525 527 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 718 720 718 720 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 720 721 720 721 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 727 729 727 729 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 729 730 729 730 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 926 928 926 928 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 928 929 928 929 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 948 954 948 954 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig9" ref-type="fig">Fig. 9</xref>
Effects of Ni2+ were examined by two basic protocols: the I-V protocol, where currents were recorded directly upon depolarization from a holding potential of -100 mV to the voltages indicated, and the IIV protocol, where the voltage was varied following a 2-ms step to +200 mV to maximally activate channels with minimal inactivation. These two protocols, taken together, help separate effects of voltage and ions on gating vs. permeation. Both protocols were delivered sequentially before, during, and after recovery from Ni2+ exposure, to assess current rundown. Two versions of the I-V protocol were run, brief (5 ms) voltage steps from -90 to +200 mV, and longer (40 ms) steps from -90 to 0 mV (+30 mV in 110 mM Ca2+o or Ba2+o), since channel activation is not complete in 5 ms at some negative voltages. I-V relations shown in the figures are the peak current (from either the 5 or 40 ms protocol), except in nominally Ca2+o -free conditions (Fig. 9), where the current at the end of 5-ms steps was used.
###end p 15

Fig. 1 shows effects of 1 mM Ni2+ on CaV3.1 using the IIV protocol, where channels are first activated by strong depolarization (2 ms at +200 mV) and then repolarized to a wide range of potentials (+100 to -150 mV). Several effects are apparent. First, currents at +200 mV are slower to activate, and are slightly smaller after 2 ms. Second, the "instantaneous" current measured following repolarization is reduced, only slightly at +100 mV, but more substantially at -150 mV. Third, tail currents are faster in Ni2+, especially at the more negative voltages. Fourth, the effect of Ni2+ appears to be greater with Ba2+ than with Ca2+, especially on the time course of the inward tail currents.

The calculated rates of Ni2+ block exhibit several interesting features. Block was rather strongly voltage dependent, with rates varying e-fold for 32 mV, corresponding to an apparent electrical distance delta = 0.4 for the transition state for Ni2+ entry into the pore. The bimolecular rate constants were several orders of magnitude lower than expected for diffusion-limited entry of Ni2+ into the pore, at -80 mV (1.0 +/- 0.1) x 106 M-1s-1 in Ca2+ (n = 12, 0.3-3 mM Ni2+) and (3.1 +/- 0.3) x 106 M-1s-1 at -80 mV in Ba2+ (n = 11, 0.1-1 mM Ni2+). Does the faster entry of Ni2+ into the pore in Ba2+ produce a more potent block? This was examined using I-V relationships, measured as the effect on peak inward or outward current at each voltage (Fig. 4). With this protocol, inward currents were very strongly inhibited in both Ca2+ and Ba2+, while outward currents were much less affected (see Fig. S3 for sample current records). Inhibition by Ni2+ could be roughly approximated by a Woodhull (1973) model with Kd = 0.2 mM for Ba2+ and 0.3 mM for Ca2+ (at 0 mV), with weak voltage dependence (e-fold for 64 mV, corresponding to delta = 0.19 for the site of Ni2+ block) (Fig. 4, C and F). The Woodhull analysis assumes that the voltage dependence of inhibition by Ni2+ results from voltage-dependent pore block, vs. (e.g.) a shift in channel gating to more positive voltages. This issue (and interpretation of delta values) will be considered further in the Discussion.

The time constant for the current ratios accelerated with depolarization (Fig. 7, B and E). That time constant (tauNi) should depend on both the on and off rates (Ni2+ entry and exit):where kB is the pseudo first-order rate constant for block by Ni2+, and k-B is the rate constant for exit of Ni2+ from the pore. Assuming that the Ni2+/control ratio at 5 ms (r5) (Fig. 7, A and D) reflects steady-state occupancy of Ni2+ at each voltage,Eqs. 1 and 2 give two equations for two unknowns (kB and k-B). Calculated k-B values are shown in Fig. 7 C (in Ca2+) and Fig. 7 F (in Ba2+). Ni2+ exit was faster with depolarization, as expected for relief of pore block. The other noteworthy effect is that Ni2+ exit was slower in 3 mM Ni2+ than in lower concentrations of Ni2+ (Fig. 7 C). The final level of inhibition at +50 to +100 mV (Fig. 7, A and D) agrees reasonably well with that measured with the IIV protocol (Fig. 2 C). It is especially noteworthy that strong depolarization did not fully reverse inhibition by 3 mM Ni2+ (Fig. 6 C and Fig. 7 A), even though the kinetics were fast (tau < 1 ms, Fig. 7 B). Taken together, these results demonstrate that inhibition of CaV3.1 by Ni2+ cannot be explained by simple voltage-dependent pore block alone. The kinetics of block (from tail currents at negative voltages) and relief of block (from activation at positive voltages) are consistent with a contribution of slow pore block. Slow block can also explain the observation that block is much stronger with the I-V protocol than with the IIV. However, the instantaneous block observed with the IIV and the incomplete relief of block by depolarization to +200 mV in 3 mM Ni2+ suggest a faster, lower-affinity process with different ion and voltage dependence (Fig. S1 A). Is the low-affinity site in the pore, or an allosteric site, perhaps related to the site that binds Ni2+ with high affinity in CaV3.2?

###begin title 53
DISCUSSION
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 18 20 18 20 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 34 35 34 35 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 144 146 144 146 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 439 441 439 441 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
We propose that Ni2+ blocks the CaV3.1 pore at two sites, a relatively superficial low affinity site, and a site deeper within the pore where Ni2+ entry is strongly affected by permeant ions (Fig. S1 B). We will first describe how this hypothesis can explain the major features of our results, as well as a substantial number of subtle features. We next consider alternative interpretations, and relate our findings to previous views of Ni2+ action on calcium channels.
###end p 54
###begin title 55
###xml 16 18 16 18 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 56 57 56 57 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
Two sites for Ni2+ Block in the Permeation Pathway of CaV3.1
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 2 4 2 4 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 121 123 121 123 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 189 191 189 191 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 221 222 221 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 222 223 222 223 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sub>
###xml 244 246 244 246 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 252 254 252 254 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 368 383 368 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2, C and F</xref>
###xml 416 418 416 418 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 424 426 424 426 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 478 480 478 480 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 486 488 486 488 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 490 498 490 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig8" ref-type="fig">Fig. 8 C</xref>
###xml 522 524 522 524 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 530 532 530 532 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 797 799 797 799 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 864 872 864 872 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Fig. 7 C</xref>
###xml 1023 1025 1023 1025 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1027 1035 1027 1035 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig6" ref-type="fig">Fig. 6 C</xref>
Ni2+ inhibited currents measured "instantaneously" following strong depolarization. This suggests a site accessible to Ni2+ at near the diffusion-limited rate. However, this component of Ni2+ block was low affinity, with Kd = 1-3 mM (in 2 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+). Fast block had a weak voltage dependence, suggestive of a primary dependence on the direction of current flow (Fig. 2, C and F). Fast block was similar with Ca2+ or Ba2+ as the charge carrier, but was reduced in 110 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+ (Fig. 8 C). The effect of high Ca2+ or Ba2+, and the voltage or current dependence, suggests that this site is in the ion permeation pathway (as opposed to a completely extracellular site that affects ion flow allosterically). This is supported by the observation that relief of slow block is inhibited by Ni2+ at the high concentrations that produce significant fast block (Fig. 7 C), suggesting a weak "lock-in" effect. That effect should be weak, since the "steady-state" outward current is inhibited by <50% at >+150 mV in 3 mM Ni2+ (Fig. 6 C), implying <50% occupancy of the fast block site.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 150 152 150 152 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 285 287 285 287 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 402 404 402 404 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
The most plausible location for the fast block site is therefore near the outer end of the permeation pathway, perhaps in an outer vestibule, which Ni2+ might be able to access readily (perhaps even without dehydrating). If that site is outside the electrical field of the membrane, Ni2+ occupancy would not be directly voltage-dependent, but it seems plausible that an outward current could relieve Ni2+ block at this superficial site.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 16 18 16 18 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 199 201 199 201 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 203 219 203 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Lux et al., 1990</xref>
###xml 224 226 224 226 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 228 248 228 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Serrano et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 256 258 256 258 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3+</sup>
###xml 260 284 260 284 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Obejero-Paz et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 301 303 301 303 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 358 360 358 360 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 373 375 373 375 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 422 424 422 424 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3+</sup>
###xml 497 499 493 495 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3+</sup>

###xml 538 540 534 536 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 673 675 665 667 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 815 817 803 805 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 850 852 838 840 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 860 861 848 849 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
###xml 863 865 851 853 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 866 868 854 856 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 882 899 870 887 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">Khan et al., 2008</xref>
###xml 905 907 893 895 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3+</sup>
###xml 911 912 899 900 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">9</sup>
###xml 914 916 902 904 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 917 919 905 907 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 931 955 919 943 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Obejero-Paz et al., 2004</xref>
Slow block by Ni2+ shares several properties of block by other di- and trivalent cations, but there are interesting differences as well. Block is relieved by strong depolarization, as observed for Ca2+ (Lux et al., 1990), Mg2+ (Serrano et al., 2000), and Y3+ (Obejero-Paz et al., 2004). The rate of Ni2+ entry is voltage dependent, and is much slower with Ca2+ than with Ba2+, as observed for block of closed channels by Y3+. However, the apparent electrical distance (delta) is 0.4, vs. 0.1 for Y3+ (Obejero-Paz et al., 2004), in 2 mM Ca2+. Note that this delta value, estimated from the entry rate, is the apparent electrical distance to the energy barrier that limits Ni2+ entry, not the distance to the actual binding site. Curiously, delta varied with ionic conditions (see below). The bimolecular rates for Ni2+ entry are much lower than for Ca2+ (3 x 108 M-1s-1 near -60 mV; Khan et al., 2008) or Y3+ (109 M-1s-1 at -80 mV; Obejero-Paz et al., 2004).
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 60 62 60 62 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 95 97 95 97 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 101 103 101 103 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 422 424 422 424 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 562 563 562 563 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 655 657 655 657 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 691 693 691 693 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3+</sup>
###xml 733 735 733 735 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 741 743 741 743 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 775 777 775 777 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 842 844 842 844 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 850 852 850 852 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 925 927 925 927 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 994 996 994 996 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 999 1001 999 1001 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1045 1047 1045 1047 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
Taken together, the most plausible site for slow block by Ni2+ is the selectivity filter for Ca2+. Ni2+ appears to have considerable difficulty in entering this site, even though it is thought to be physically near the extracellular side of the pore. This may involve both slow dehydration and competitive ion-ion interactions. These two factors may be related, since compared with many other di- and trivalent cations, Ni2+ entry might require more complete replacement of waters of hydration with the side chains of the glutamate and aspartate residues of a CaV3 selectivity filter, which could increase the ability of ions within the pore to prevent Ni2+ entry. Perhaps some ions (e.g., Y3+) can aggressively push any competing Ca2+ or Ba2+ ions through the pore, while Ni2+ must wait patiently in the low-affinity fast-block site until Ca2+ or Ba2+ ions permeate. In that view, most ions pass quickly by the site where Ni2+ occupancy produces a fast block. That could explain the lack of Ca2+-Ba2+ selectivity for effects on fast block by Ni2+.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 23 25 19 21 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 318 320 306 308 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 452 453 436 437 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 496 498 468 470 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 504 506 476 478 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
Why is the delta for Ni2+ entry so large, and so ion dependent? Those two features together suggest that the large delta is a consequence of strong interactions with other ions within the pore, rather than an accurate measure of an electrical location within the pore. The ion dependence predicts that the delta for Ni2+ entry should approach the "true" value in the absence of competition, which would explain the observation that delta is lower in Na+ (0.16, vs. approximately0.4 in 2-110 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+).
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 253 254 249 250 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 279 281 271 273 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 287 289 279 281 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 320 322 308 310 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 328 330 316 318 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 384 386 372 374 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 537 539 525 527 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 598 600 586 588 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 658 660 642 644 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 690 692 674 676 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
It is noteworthy that the extent of block, measured from I-V relations, was less ion dependent than the rate of block. Furthermore, the apparent voltage dependence of block depended in the opposite direction on ion concentration, with delta = 0.45 in Na+, delta = 0.19 in 2 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+, and delta = 0.15 in 110 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+. This can be explained in part by the low-affinity Ni2+-binding site, which is relatively insensitive to ionic conditions and to voltage. That site would make the largest contribution to the net block by Ni2+ when "high-affinity" slow block is the weakest (in high Ca2+). In addition, the measured delta could be in error if Ni2+ affects gating, or if slow Ni2+ block has not reached steady state at the time of peak current.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 40 42 40 42 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 112 114 112 114 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 409 411 409 411 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
This two-site model for pore block by Ni2+ provides an attractive qualitative explanation for the behavior of Ni2+ block under a variety of ionic conditions, and a wide range of voltages. However, we are well aware that this will need to be confirmed by molecularly based modeling of ion permeation in calcium channels, at a sufficient level of detail to account for the complex organometallic chemistry of Ni2+ ions.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 6 8 6 8 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 85 87 85 87 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 94 96 94 96 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 171 188 171 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">Khan et al., 2008</xref>
The Ni2+ entry rate was sensitive to the identity of the permeant divalent cation (Ca2+ vs. Ba2+), even at 110 mM concentrations where flux through the pore is saturated (Khan et al., 2008). It is often assumed that saturation of flux implies saturation of binding site(s) within the pore, but that is not necessarily true.
###end p 63
###begin title 64
###xml 33 35 33 35 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
Alternative Interpretations of Ni2+ Action
###end title 64
###begin p 65
###xml 98 100 98 100 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
As for any pharmacological agent, there are several possible mechanisms by which an ion such as Ni2+ can affect the behavior of a voltage-dependent ion channel, including pore block, shifts in gating by screening or binding to surface charge, and allosteric modulation by binding to extra-pore sites.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 38 40 38 40 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 191 193 191 193 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 197 199 197 199 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 207 209 207 209 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 277 278 277 278 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 287 288 287 288 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 360 362 360 362 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 368 370 368 370 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 446 448 434 436 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 493 495 481 483 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 499 501 487 489 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 509 511 497 499 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 528 530 516 518 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 537 539 525 527 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
There is good reason to expect that Ni2+ will shift channel activation to more positive potentials, at least in part by a surface charge mechanism. Effects of three other divalent cations (Ca2+, Ba2+, and Mg2+) on gating were well described by screening a surface charge of 1 e- per 98 A2, which predicts (e.g.) that addition of 1 mM divalent cation to 2 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+ would shift channel activation by approximately3 mV. It is possible that Ni2+ affects surface charge more strongly than Ca2+, Ba2+, and Mg2+; for example, Fe2+ and Mn2+ shift gating as expected for both screening and low-affinity binding to surface charge (Lopin, K.V., I.P. Gray, C.A. Obejero-Paz, and S.W. Jones. 2007. Biophysical Society meeting. Abstract 2867; Jamieson, Q., C.A. Obejero-Paz, and S.W. Jones. 2008. Biophysical Society meeting. Abstract 3129).
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 16 18 16 18 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 262 264 262 264 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 322 324 322 324 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 326 334 326 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 B</xref>
###xml 348 350 348 350 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 357 365 357 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig9" ref-type="fig">Fig. 9 D</xref>
###xml 462 464 462 464 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 509 511 509 511 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 518 520 518 520 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 762 764 762 764 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 772 787 772 787 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3, C and D</xref>
###xml 789 797 789 797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig8" ref-type="fig">Fig. 8 F</xref>
###xml 803 811 803 811 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig9" ref-type="fig">Fig. 9 E</xref>
###xml 839 841 839 841 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
The effect of Ni2+ on tail kinetics is in the direction expected for a surface charge effect: that is, faster tail currents (measured at a particular voltage) could in principle result from a positive shift along the voltage axis. However, where the effect of Ni2+ on tail current time constants was the strongest (2 mM Ba2+, Fig. 3 B; nominally Ca2+ free, Fig. 9 D), the effect was not a pure parallel shift along the voltage axis. Furthermore, the effect of Ni2+ to speed tail currents more profoundly in Ba2+ vs. Ca2+ would not be expected from a surface charge mechanism. Finally, this interpretation would imply that it is merely coincidental that the effect on tail kinetics is well approximated by bimolecular kinetics with a voltage-dependent rate for Ni2+ block (Fig. 3, C and D, Fig. 8 F, and Fig. 9 E). Thus, we conclude that Ni2+ speeds tail currents primarily by voltage- and time-dependent channel block, not by effects on channel gating.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 69 71 69 71 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 125 140 125 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Fig. 4, C and F</xref>
###xml 142 150 142 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig8" ref-type="fig">Fig. 8 I</xref>
###xml 156 164 156 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig9" ref-type="fig">Fig. 9 I</xref>
###xml 223 225 223 225 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 415 430 415 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib24">Woodhull (1973)</xref>
###xml 490 492 490 492 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 923 925 923 925 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1027 1030 1027 1030 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O,r</sub>
###xml 1109 1111 1109 1111 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1295 1319 1295 1319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Th&#233;venod and Jones, 1992</xref>
###xml 1321 1339 1321 1339 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib3">Block et al., 1998</xref>
###xml 1341 1360 1341 1360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Babich et al., 2007</xref>
Several figures in this paper present the fractional inhibition by Ni2+ of the peak current measured from I-V relationships (Fig. 4, C and F, Fig. 8 I, and Fig. 9 I). Inhibition can result from voltage-dependent block by Ni2+ within the ion permeation pathway, but a positive shift in channel activation would also lead to a voltage-dependent reduction in current amplitudes. This complicates interpretation of the Woodhull (1973) fits to the data from the I-V protocol. Specifically, if Ni2+ shifts activation to more positive voltages (even by a few mV), inhibition at more negative voltages would reflect a combination of pore block and a reduction in the probability that a channel is open. It is important to recognize that a positive shift in the I-V curve (or derived measures such as the G-V) could result either from a true shift in channel activation, or from voltage-dependent block. For a fast blocker (e.g., Mg2+) pore block can be measured as a reduction in the IIV, and shifts in gating from the I-V/IIV ratio (PO,r), but separation of the two effects is difficult for a slow blocker such as Ni2+. If block is very slow, the block observed at the time of peak current with the I-V protocol may not fully reflect steady-state block, as noted previously for other channel blockers (Thevenod and Jones, 1992; Block et al., 1998; Babich et al., 2007).
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 275 293 275 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Kuo and Hess, 1992</xref>
###xml 295 313 295 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib3">Block et al., 1998</xref>
In principle, surface charges could also affect ion permeation, if the charges are close enough to the permeation pathway to affect the local concentration of permeating and blocking cations. However, this effect appears to be minimal for voltage-dependent calcium channels (Kuo and Hess, 1992; Block et al., 1998).
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 61 63 61 63 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 98 99 98 99 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 151 153 151 153 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 203 204 203 204 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 219 221 219 221 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 305 331 305 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib12">Li-Smerin and Swartz, 1998</xref>
###xml 333 348 333 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">McDonough, 2007</xref>
###xml 535 537 535 537 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 543 545 543 545 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 580 582 580 582 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 630 632 630 632 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 822 823 822 823 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 878 885 878 885 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig11" ref-type="fig">Fig. 11</xref>
###xml 943 960 943 960 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Diaz et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 962 979 962 979 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Kang et al., 2005</xref>
One possible explanation of the low-affinity inhibition by Ni2+ of the IIV relationship is that CaV3.1 retains a low-affinity extracellular site for Ni2+ block, homologous to the high-affinity site in CaV3.2. Perhaps Ni2+ traps a voltage sensor in the "down" position, analogous to voltage sensor toxins (Li-Smerin and Swartz, 1998; McDonough, 2007). That could slow activation, and/or keep some channels from opening at all. We cannot fully exclude this, but that mechanism would not easily explain the reduced fast block in 110 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+. Furthermore, concentrations of Ni2+ that occupy that low-affinity site "lock in" Ni2+ block at the deep pore site, suggesting a more direct interaction (which we propose to be at the extracellular side of the permeation pathway). Note also that high-affinity inhibition of CaV3.2 is not reversed even by depolarization to +200 mV (Fig. 11), consistent with simple voltage-independent inhibition (Diaz et al., 2005; Kang et al., 2005).
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 187 189 187 189 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 548 550 548 550 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 557 559 557 559 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 631 632 631 632 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 656 658 656 658 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 665 667 665 667 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 788 790 788 790 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 797 799 797 799 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1124 1146 1124 1146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib10">Lacinova et al. (2000)</xref>
###xml 1164 1166 1164 1166 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1202 1203 1202 1203 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 1266 1268 1266 1268 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1275 1277 1275 1277 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1364 1366 1364 1366 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
This study illustrates how the basic I-V and IIV protocols, combined with an analysis of ion dependence, can go a long way toward determining the mechanisms of action of an ion such as Ni2+ on a calcium channel. A discrepancy between the extent of inhibition measured from the I-V and IIV protocols can be explained either by time-dependent block, or by modified channel activation. Acceleration of tail currents may result either from time-dependent open-channel block or from a shift in activation, but differential effects on tail kinetics in Ca2+ vs. Ba2+ strongly suggest a pore block mechanism. That is especially true for CaV3.1, where changes in Ca2+ and Ba2+ concentration shift the voltage dependence of gating in the same manner, implying similar "surface charge" effects of Ca2+ and Ba2+ on channel activation. Analysis of tail current acceleration in terms of pore block can test that hypothesis by comparison to expectations for a bimolecular reaction, with voltage-dependent rates. Conversely, analysis based solely on effects on I-V relationships may not be sufficient for mechanistic analysis. For example, Lacinova et al. (2000) concluded that Ni2+ modified activation kinetics for CaV3.1, by slowing channel deactivation (more strongly in 20 mM Ba2+ vs. Ca2+). Their experimental results are consistent with ours, but we conclude instead that Ni2+ accelerates tail currents primarily by time- and voltage-dependent block, vs. a true modulation of the kinetics of channel closing.
###end p 71
###begin title 72
###xml 36 38 36 38 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
Comparison to Previous Studies of Ni2+ Block of Calcium Channels
###end title 72
###begin p 73
###xml 18 20 18 20 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 32 33 32 33 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 189 191 189 191 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 226 246 226 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Winegar et al., 1991</xref>
###xml 257 259 257 259 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 409 411 409 411 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 439 440 439 440 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sup>
###xml 442 444 442 444 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 445 447 445 447 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 468 470 468 470 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 472 492 472 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Winegar et al., 1991</xref>
###xml 515 516 515 516 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sup>
###xml 518 520 518 520 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 521 523 521 523 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 530 531 530 531 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 541 549 541 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig8" ref-type="fig">Fig. 8 F</xref>
###xml 643 663 643 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Winegar et al., 1991</xref>
###xml 726 728 726 728 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 960 962 960 962 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1016 1018 1016 1018 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
We propose that Ni2+ block of CaV3.1 involves a low affinity site with rapid kinetics, and a high affinity site within the "deep" pore. This agrees quite well with single-channel data on Ni2+ block of L-type calcium channels (Winegar et al., 1991), where Ni2+ not only reduced the apparent single channel conductance, but also produced discrete blocking events. The rate for discrete block of L channels by Ni2+ was also quite slow, 6 x 104 M-1s-1 at 0 mV in 110 mM Ba2+ (Winegar et al., 1991), compared with 3 x 104 M-1s-1 for CaV3.1 (from Fig. 8 F). In addition, the rates of block and relief of block both increased with hyperpolarization (Winegar et al., 1991), consistent with our observation that the rate of block by Ni2+ was more voltage dependent than the steady-state block (which depends on the off/on rate ratio). We do not have direct measurements of the exit rate, but note that an exit rate that increases with hyperpolarization suggests that Ni2+ can exit the pore to the intracellular site (i.e., Ni2+ is a weakly permeant ion).
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 38 54 38 54 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib11">Lee et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 56 73 56 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Kang et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 103 123 103 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">Zamponi et al., 1996</xref>
###xml 125 150 125 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">McFarlane and Gilly, 1998</xref>
###xml 152 176 152 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Magistretti et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 196 198 196 198 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 331 332 331 332 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 447 448 447 448 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 479 480 479 480 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 485 502 485 502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Kang et al., 2005</xref>
Many previous studies, on T channels (Lee et al., 1999; Kang et al., 2005) and other calcium channels (Zamponi et al., 1996; McFarlane and Gilly, 1998; Magistretti et al., 2001), concluded that Ni2+ modified channel gating, most likely by an extra-pore site. Our results do not provide compelling evidence for such an effect for CaV3.1. This is consistent with the interpretation that the high-affinity site near the extracellular side of S4 in CaV3.2 is essentially absent in CaV3.1 (Kang et al., 2005).
###end p 74
