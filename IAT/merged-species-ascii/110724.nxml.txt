A functional assay in Escherichia coli to detect non-assisted interaction between galactose repressor dimers
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
To whom correspondence should be addressed. Tel: +33 1 42 11 51 24; Email: amouyal@igr.fr
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 10 26 10 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 544 547 544 547 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 10 26 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Among the Escherichia coli operons repressed from multiple sites on DNA, the galactose operon is unique: its repression requires an auxiliary protein, HU, to assist cooperative repressor binding to two distant DNA sites. Here we show that GalR can still mediate repression from distant sites in an artificial and simplified regulatory region which totally disturbs the organisation of the natural interactions. This simple and unexpected cooperation of a protein incapable of self-association in solution might be involved in regulation of the gal operon. Furthermore, the assay may be generalised to detection of rather weak cooperative interactions between DNA-bound proteins.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 4 20 4 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 65 67 65 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oi</italic>
###xml 72 74 72 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oe</italic>
###xml 106 107 106 107 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd511f1">1</xref>
###xml 187 188 187 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c1">1</xref>
###xml 382 383 382 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c1">1</xref>
###xml 413 419 413 419 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 559 562 559 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 570 578 570 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 609 610 609 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c2">2</xref>
###xml 611 612 611 612 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c4">4</xref>
###xml 800 801 800 801 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c5">5</xref>
###xml 4 20 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 413 419 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The Escherichia coli GalR repressor binds to two operator sites, Oi and Oe, in the galactose operon (Fig. 1A). Both of these DNA sequences are required for full repression of the operon (1). It was originally expected that the observed cooperation would be the result of a simple bridging of the two operator sites by the repressor protein, with looping out of the intervening DNA (1), as observed for some other E.coli operons. However, according to the most recent studies, an auxiliary protein (histone-like protein HU) must assist the interaction between gal dimers in vitro, along with DNA supercoiling (2-4). Two other proteins (the CRP activator and RNA polymerase) also bind to the sequence between the two operator sites and may possibly affect interaction between DNA-bound GalR molecules (5 and references therein).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 122 125 122 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 276 277 276 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c2">2</xref>
###xml 278 279 278 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c6">6</xref>
###xml 351 352 351 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c6">6</xref>
###xml 492 493 492 493 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c7">7</xref>
###xml 580 581 580 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c7">7</xref>
###xml 590 597 590 597 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 700 703 700 703 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
However, some observations suggested to us that non-assisted GalR-GalR interaction might be possible: (i) derepression of gal operon expression is not total in cells depleted of the two genes encoding HU and, moreover, derepression differs in such strains, from 2- to 8-fold (2,6); (ii) gyrase inhibitors cause total derepression of galP2 expression (6); (iii) strains depleted of both HU subunits are rather sickly and spontaneously accumulate secondary mutations (e.g. in the gyrase genes; 7); (iv) HU might stimulate gyrase activity as well as decrease topoisomerase activity (7). Thus, in vivo DNA supercoiling, rather than HU, is an essential component of DNA looping-mediated repression of the gal operon.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 17 20 17 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hup</italic>
###xml 218 221 218 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
Since the use of hup mutants does not lead to clear conclusions, we have used a complementary approach. To determine to what extent HU, as well as the other auxiliary proteins, affect the interaction between DNA-bound gal dimers we have analysed GalR-mediated repression at artificial promoter derivatives.
###end p 6

###xml 0 16 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Escherichia coli strain CSH50Delta1 [ara, (lac pro)Delta, (galR lysA)Delta, strA] cannot produce beta-galactosidase nor the gal repressor. When necessary, this strain was co-transformed with a pLacZ plasmid derivative expressing beta-galactosidase under control of the desired combination of gal operators and the pGalR plasmid producing the repressor. Repression of beta-galactosidase synthesis can then be followed for this construct. Repression of the lacz gene was followed with this method when the gene was under control of the lac operators and lac repressor (8,9).

The operator sites included in the appropriate plasmids result from annealing of sense and antisense synthetic oligonucleotides (Genosphere, Evry). The minimal 16 bp sequence which was used by Haber and Adhya (1) is flanked by two additional base pairs on each side: 'Oe' sequence, 5'-AA.GTGTAAAC.GATTCCAC.TT-3'; 'Oi ' sequence, 5'-AA.GTGGTAGC.GGTTACAT.TT-3'.

###xml 535 538 535 538 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Spe</italic>
The pLacZ plasmid is a pACYC184 derivative. It is a low copy number plasmid with less than 15 copies (9,10). It is tetracycline resistant. Its promoter region is detailed in Figure 1B. When required, the 20 bp Oi operator sequence was inserted into the XbaI restriction site and the 20 bp Oe sequence into the SpeI restriction site. The number of operator sequences inserted into either the XbaI site or the SpeI site was determined by PCR with two 'external' primers, generating a fragment larger than the region between the XbaI and SpeI sites. The orientation of the inserted operator was determined with another set of primers, the sense or antisense operator oligonucleotide and one of the external primers. Presence of the expected PCR fragment for either the sense or antisense operator primer led to the orientation assignment.

The gal repressor coding sequence was copied and amplified from the genome of E.coli strain DH5alpha by PCR. This operation preserves the ampicillin resistance gene and removes the tetracycline gene in pBR322. The resulting plasmid is compatible with the pLacZ derivative. Its copy number is greater than 25 (10). It produces sufficient gal repressor to occupy part or all of the gal operator sites carried by the pLacZ derivative since repression of beta-galactosidase synthesis is detectable.

###begin title 16
RESULTS
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 55 58 55 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 135 138 135 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 196 197 196 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c9">9</xref>
###xml 279 282 276 279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 305 308 302 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 398 399 395 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd511f1">1</xref>
The artificial constructs that we have used to analyse gal repressor-mediated repression were derived from a previous study related to lac repressor-mediated repression and oriented heterodimers (9). In these constructs the expression of beta-galactosidase was controlled by two lac operators. One of the lac operators was inserted adjacent to the -10 sequence, the other one 102 bp upstream (Fig. 1B). beta-Galactosidase expression cannot be activated by CRP protein since there is no binding site for this protein in the promoter region. Furthermore, in these constructs RNA polymerase cannot bind simultaneously to the repressor in a pre-formed repressor-DNA loop since the operators overlap the promoter region.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 31 34 31 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 48 51 48 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 78 82 78 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacz</italic>
###xml 122 125 122 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 274 278 271 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LacZ</italic>
###xml 396 397 393 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c8">8</xref>
###xml 398 399 395 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c9">9</xref>
###xml 401 402 398 399 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sc>

In the present study, for each combination of operators beta-galactosidase activity was determined in cells carrying both plasmids, the pLacZ (pACYC184) derivative and the pGalR plasmid (pBR322 derivative), in the absence and presence of the inducer (compare 8,9) d-galactose (40 mM). The repression data are shown in the first and second columns of Table 1.

###xml 924 928 924 928 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">galR</italic>
###xml 954 955 950 951 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd511tb1">1</xref>
###xml 960 961 956 957 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd511tb2">2</xref>
###xml 1011 1012 1007 1008 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sc>
Repression was not totally relieved by inducers, neither d-galactose up to 100 mM nor d-fucose up to 20 mM (data not shown), for the promoter derivatives carrying two DNA sites for GalR (Table 1, lines 6-9, second column, and Table 2, lines 1-3, second column). Similarly, high levels of lac repressor did not allow full induction of the lac operon or of its artificial derivatives (11,12). d-Galactose forms a ternary complex with the gal repressor and gal DNA and the repressor remains on the promoter (13). High levels of gal repressor do not prevent clearance of the promoter from one site (13; our data). Under the same conditions, the repressor is not cleared from two sites (our data), probably because the higher order complex of GalR-GalR-DNA and inducer is more stable than the ternary complex. Therefore, as in previous studies with the lac repressor (compare 8,9), we employed a vector which encodes a defective galR gene, pGalRDelta (Tables 1 and 2, third column), instead of plasmid pGalR and the d-galactose inducer.
###end p 19
###begin p 20
###xml 100 102 100 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oi</italic>
###xml 119 120 119 120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd511tb1">1</xref>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oe</italic>
###xml 262 263 262 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd511tb1">1</xref>
###xml 486 487 486 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd511tb1">1</xref>
As expected, activity was only weakly repressed by GalR on the two promoter constructs carrying the Oi operator (Table 1, lines 2 and 3). Moreover, repression was not significantly affected by GalR on the two promoter derivatives carrying the Oe operator (Table 1, lines 4 and 5). However, if an additional operator was inserted into the single operator derivatives, repression was strongly increased. Depending on the orientation of the operators, repression was 10- to 15-fold (Table 1, lines 6-9).
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 28 30 28 30 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c14">14</xref>
###xml 227 229 227 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oi</italic>
###xml 333 335 333 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oi</italic>
In fact, Lanzer and Bujard (14) have shown that the efficiency of repression is mainly determined by the position of an operator in the promoter region and the kinetic parameters of the RNA polymerase-promoter interaction. The Oi site overlaps the start site of transcription. Therefore, it is not surprising that the orientation of Oi can have an effect on repression as well as on promoter strength.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 68 75 68 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 148 151 148 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 178 185 178 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 546 553 546 553 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 607 615 607 615 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 674 676 674 676 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c15">15</xref>
###xml 677 679 677 679 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c17">17</xref>
###xml 732 734 732 734 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c18">18</xref>
###xml 827 830 827 830 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
At this stage, we have shown that cooperation between the two sites in vivo does not require HU nor the CRP activator or the RNA polymerase. If the gal repressor is tetramerised in vivo, leading to loop formation and repression of transcription, insertion of a half-integral number of helical turns between the two operators is expected to impede tetramerisation and relieve repression. In contrast, insertion of a spacer with an integral number of helical turns will restore repression. Several studies have shown that the helical repeat of DNA in vivo differs from the helical repeat of unconstrained DNA in vitro (10.5 bp/helix turn) and varies between 11.1 and 11.7 bp (15-17). DNA supercoiling is in part responsible for this (18). We reasoned that the two operator sites were favourably positioned for DNA looping in the gal operon, i.e. aligned on the same face of the DNA. If this is the case, the two sites are separated by an integral number of helical turns. Since the two sites are separated by 114 bp, the resulting helical pitch would be 11.4 bp.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 341 343 339 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oe</italic>
###xml 346 348 342 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oi</italic>
###xml 443 445 437 439 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oe</italic>
###xml 451 453 443 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oi</italic>
###xml 463 464 455 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd511tb2">2</xref>
###xml 687 689 677 679 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oe</italic>
###xml 695 697 683 685 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oi</italic>
###xml 887 889 873 875 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oe</italic>
###xml 895 897 879 881 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oi</italic>
If the sites are aligned ('in phase') for 114 bp, they should be out of phase when adding or removing half of the helical repeat, i.e. 5.7 bp, to 114 bp (giving 108.3 and 119.7 bp) and again in phase when adding or removing the helical repeat, 11.4 bp, to 114 bp (giving 102.6 and 125.4 bp). The distance between the two operators in the <--Oe<--Oi construct is 102 bp (close to the calculated 102.6 bp distance). This construct is renamed <--Oe102<--Oi in Table 2. For technical reasons (see Materials and Methods) we inserted 8 bp between the operators of this construct. The distance between the operators becomes 110 bp (or 9.6 turns). The two sites should be out of phase in the <--Oe110<--Oi construct. Insertion of the minimal 14 bp self-complementary oligonucleotide leads to 116 bp (10.1-10.2 turns for a helical repeat of 11.4 bp). The sites should again be in phase in the <--Oe116<--Oi construct.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 28 29 28 29 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd511tb2">2</xref>
###xml 517 519 517 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c15">15</xref>
###xml 520 522 520 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c17">17</xref>
The data are shown in Table 2. When the sites are separated by 102 bp the level of cooperativity is high. Repression is effectively relieved for 110 bp and restored for 116 bp. Thus, there is a clear effect of orientation of the two sites on the DNA. The local helical repeat which fits with the observed data (a high level of cooperativity for the natural distance of 114 bp and the artificial 102 bp and 116 bp distances; loss of cooperativity for 110 bp) is ~11.5 bp, in the range of values found by other groups (15-17).
###end p 24
###begin title 25
DISCUSSION
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 67 69 67 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oi</italic>
###xml 74 76 74 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oe</italic>
###xml 101 103 101 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oe</italic>
###xml 251 253 251 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oi</italic>
###xml 322 324 322 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c19">19</xref>
###xml 466 469 466 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 522 524 522 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c19">19</xref>
###xml 592 599 592 599 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 679 680 679 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c1">1</xref>
###xml 696 699 696 699 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 735 738 735 738 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 763 766 763 766 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 771 777 771 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">per se</italic>
###xml 848 850 848 850 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c20">20</xref>
The wild-type galactose operon is repressed at two operator sites, Oi and Oe. The upstream operator, Oe, was discovered first. The question of how this operator could repress transcription upstream of the operon was resolved when the second operator, Oi, was found, located within the first structural gene of the operon (19). To explain the involvement of the two sites in repression, Adhya and co-workers postulated several mechanisms. Interaction between the two gal dimers bound at the operator sites was one of them (19). The two operators are required for full repression of the operon in vivo and function synergistically, as shown by successive destruction of the sites (1). In fact, two lac operators were substituted for the gal operators, showing that gal DNA per se does not impede DNA looping, as observed by Brenowitz and co-workers (20).
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 24 27 24 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 85 93 85 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 159 162 159 162 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 251 258 251 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
However, interaction of gal dimers bound at the operator sites could never be proved in vitro in the absence of auxiliary proteins. Our data indicate that the gal repressor can act cooperatively outside the context of the wild-type galactose promoter in vivo. The promoters have totally arbitrary sequences and exclude site-specific protein binding. In particular, insertion of spacers between the operator sites, with subsequent modification of the helical phasing of the operators and an effect on repression, supports the existence of protein-protein interactions.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 37 40 37 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 100 101 100 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c3">3</xref>
###xml 167 168 167 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c3">3</xref>
###xml 273 276 273 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
More precisely, when HU binds to the gal promoter, a complex with a precise architecture is formed (3). HU is specifically located in the -10 to +30 promoter segment (3). It is unlikely that such a complex is formed with our constructs. The precise geometry of the GalR-HU-gal DNA complex would have to adapt to the various arbitrary sequences and distances found in these constructs.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 79 82 79 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 116 119 116 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 257 258 257 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd511f1">1</xref>
Apart from HU, other proteins have been said to affect the interaction between gal repressors. Transcription of the gal operon is initiated from two overlapping promoters, P1, in the presence of the activating complex cAMP/CRP, and P2, in its absence (Fig. 1A).
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 14 17 14 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 56 57 56 57 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c5">5</xref>
###xml 102 105 102 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 176 179 176 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 250 253 250 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 310 313 310 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 372 375 372 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 518 521 518 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
As far as the gal operon is concerned, several studies (5 and references therein) have shown that the gal repressor does not inhibit CRP/cAMP and RNA polymerase binding to the gal regulatory regions. Thus, regulation of open complex formation at the gal promoters would not result from competition between the gal repressor, CRP/cAMP and RNA polymerase for binding at the gal operon regulatory region, but would instead result from interactions of the three proteins during formation of a nucleoprotein complex on the gal DNA fragment. This nucleoprotein complex would be favoured by DNA bending induced by the constituent proteins.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 99 106 99 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 200 203 200 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 276 281 276 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO1</italic>
###xml 286 291 286 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO3</italic>
###xml 391 392 391 392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c8">8</xref>
###xml 445 447 445 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c21">21</xref>
###xml 479 481 479 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c22">22</xref>
Our data indicate that CRP or RNA binding to the loop are not essential for GalR dimer cooperation in vivo. With the same constructs we have previously shown that the observed cooperation between two lac operators closely positioned in the promoter region, like the wild-type lacO1 and lacO3 operators, is effectively due to their interaction and not to some indirect effect of CRP protein (8), exclusion of the repressor from its binding site (21) or assistance by CRP protein (22).
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 102 105 102 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 111 112 111 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c2">2</xref>
###xml 113 114 113 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c5">5</xref>
###xml 307 308 307 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c3">3</xref>
###xml 442 444 442 444 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c23">23</xref>
###xml 473 476 473 476 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 505 506 505 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c3">3</xref>
Various models have been proposed to explain how the auxiliary proteins facilitate GalR repression on gal DNA (2,5). From the most recent works by Adhya and co-workers it appears that the structure which is formed is closer than initially thought to that found in related systems: HU does not contact GalR (3) and the two dimers are capable of specific interactions (as we have found), since mutations in the GalR gene abolish cooperativity (23). Since HU does not bind to gal DNA in the absence of GalR (3), HU binding may be dependent on direct GalR-GalR cooperation and may stabilise an interaction which is weaker in its absence.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 455 466 455 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Trypanosoma</italic>
###xml 542 544 542 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c24">24</xref>
###xml 545 547 545 547 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c25">25</xref>
###xml 641 643 641 643 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c26">26</xref>
In this case it is possible that the system that we have used, where the repression loop cannot be destabilised by CRP or RNA polymerase binding, allows such a weak interaction to be detected. We also cannot exclude that the sequence between the operators in our constructs induces DNA bending or some DNA deformation that brings the two sites into close proximity. T tracts, such as those present in the sequence between operators, are characteristic of Trypanosoma minicircle kinetoplast DNA and are well-known determinants of DNA bending (24,25). This sequence might replace the requirement for HU binding, since HU is known to bend DNA (26).
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 307 309 307 309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c20">20</xref>
###xml 345 348 345 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 377 385 377 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 413 416 413 416 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 504 506 504 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c27">27</xref>
###xml 620 630 620 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli lac</italic>
###xml 632 634 632 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c28">28</xref>
###xml 635 637 635 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c29">29</xref>
###xml 643 646 643 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">deo</italic>
###xml 659 661 659 661 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c30">30</xref>
###xml 662 664 662 664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c31">31</xref>
###xml 671 677 671 677 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 702 704 702 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c32">32</xref>
###xml 730 732 725 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c33">33</xref>
###xml 776 779 771 774 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sp1</italic>
###xml 781 783 776 778 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c34">34</xref>
###xml 789 792 784 787 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p53</italic>
###xml 794 796 789 791 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c35">35</xref>
###xml 620 626 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 671 677 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Clearly, the interaction between the two dimers is not highly stable. In the absence of auxiliary proteins, cooperation between the two dimers is only seen at low temperatures on linear DNA, as revealed by quantitative analysis of repressor binding to DNA by gel retardation assay and DNase I footprinting (20). Furthermore, not only do the two gal dimers not readily interact in vitro when bound to DNA, but the gal dimer also does not show any oligomerisation in solution, even at high concentrations (27). This is an exception among the transcription factors which control regulation from multiple sites, such as the E.coli lac (28,29) and deo repressors (30,31), the E.coli arabinose AraC protein (32) and lambdacI repressor (33 and references therein) and the eukaryotic Sp1 (34) and p53 (35) proteins.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 72 80 72 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 85 92 85 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 98 106 98 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 179 180 179 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c2">2</xref>
###xml 290 291 290 291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c2">2</xref>
###xml 292 293 292 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c4">4</xref>
###xml 294 295 294 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c6">6</xref>
###xml 672 673 672 673 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c6">6</xref>
DNA supercoiling stabilises the weak interaction between the two dimers in vitro and in vivo: (i) in vitro transcriptional repression by GalR is only observed on supercoiled DNA (2); (ii) HU, which is supposed to facilitate interaction between the two dimers, only acts on supercoiled DNA (2-4,6); (iii) GalR-GalR DNA complexes are not formed on linear DNA, except at low temperatures, however, a non-negligible number of GalR-GalR complexes are formed on supercoiled minicircles, as revealed by electron microscopy (25% of the number of loops formed with HU); (iv) relaxation of DNA following inhibition of DNA gyrase activity by coumermycin totally relieves repression (6).
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 131 139 131 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In vitro</italic>
###xml 159 162 159 162 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 276 281 276 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacOi</italic>
###xml 289 291 289 291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c36">36</xref>
###xml 445 453 445 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 455 457 455 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c37">37</xref>
###xml 458 460 458 460 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c39">39</xref>
###xml 466 473 466 473 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 475 477 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c37">37</xref>
###xml 550 552 550 552 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c40">40</xref>
DNA superhelicity is also an essential component of DNA looping in related systems. It is not associated with HU in these systems. In vitro DNA looping of the lac repressor was first characterised on linear DNA. However, the assays were performed with high affinity consensus lacOi sites (36; M.Kramer, M.Amouyal and B.Muller-Hill, Berlin Summer School, September 1986). When the natural components were retained, DNA supercoiling was necessary in vitro (37-39) and in vivo (37). DNA supercoiling is also essential for DNA looping with AraC protein (40).
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 87 94 87 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 127 134 127 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 159 164 159 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacWM</italic>
###xml 191 192 191 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c9">9</xref>
###xml 244 247 244 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 309 317 309 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 410 417 410 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 463 468 463 468 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO3</italic>
###xml 469 474 469 474 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO3</italic>
###xml 488 490 488 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c41">41</xref>
###xml 527 535 527 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
Finally, this is not the only example of an interaction that can only be characterised in vivo. For example, we have described in vivo cooperation between two lacWM sites in a previous work (9). These sites are nearly equivalent to half-sites. lac repressor binds with such a low specificity to a single site in vitro that repression of transcription is not observed when the site overlaps the promoter region in vivo. This situation has been reproduced with the lacO3-lacO3 interaction (41), which has never been demonstrated in vitro.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 59 62 59 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 88 93 88 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacOi</italic>
###xml 156 159 156 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gal</italic>
###xml 293 298 293 298 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO1</italic>
###xml 299 304 299 304 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO3</italic>
###xml 329 331 329 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c37">37</xref>
###xml 343 348 343 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO2</italic>
###xml 349 354 349 354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO3</italic>
###xml 425 426 425 426 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
###xml 428 430 428 430 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 432 434 432 434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c20">20</xref>
###xml 476 478 476 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c27">27</xref>
###xml 501 504 501 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 533 538 533 538 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO1</italic>
###xml 559 561 559 561 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">10</sup>
###xml 568 570 568 570 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">11</sup>
###xml 572 574 572 574 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 576 578 576 578 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c42">42</xref>
###xml 630 635 630 635 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacOi</italic>
###xml 660 662 660 662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c43">43</xref>
###xml 681 686 681 686 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO2</italic>
###xml 691 696 691 696 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacO3</italic>
Along the same lines, the highly stable interaction of the lac repressor with consensus lacOi sites has been taken as a positive control for DNA looping in gal studies. However, with regard to affinities, the GalR interaction is better compared to less stable interactions, for example to the lacO1-lacO3 loop (in particular see 37) or to the lacO2-lacO3 interaction. Indeed, the affinity of GalR for its sites is only 3 x 108 M-1 (20) and it is equivalent for the two sites (27). The affinity of the lac repressor for the wild-type lacO1 site lies between 1010 and 1011 M-1 (42). The affinity of this repressor for the consensus lacOi site is 10-fold higher (43) and that for the lacO2 and lacO3 pseudo-operators is 10-fold and 100- to 1000-fold lower, respectively.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 189 196 189 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 318 319 318 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd511c9">9</xref>
###xml 366 376 366 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli gal</italic>
###xml 366 372 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
In conclusion, our observations support the hypothesis that GalR dimers are capable of non-assisted interactions in a system which allows the detection of weak protein-protein interactions in vivo. Previously we had shown that the same system could detect the cooperativity generated by weak DNA-protein interactions (9). This finding implies that repression of the E.coli gal operon may, at least partly, operate in this manner under wild-type conditions and in strains which have compensated for the deletion of HU by secondary mutations.
