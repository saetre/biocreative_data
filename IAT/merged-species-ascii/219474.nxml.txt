The complete nucleotide sequence and RNA editing content of the mitochondrial genome of rapeseed (Brassica napus L.): comparative analysis of the mitochondrial genomes of rapeseed and Arabidopsis thaliana

###xml 45 59 <span type="species:ncbi:3708">Brassica napus</span>
###xml 104 124 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
The entire mitochondrial genome of rapeseed (Brassica napus L.) was sequenced and compared with that of Arabidopsis thaliana. The 221 853 bp genome contains 34 protein-coding genes, three rRNA genes and 17 tRNA genes. This gene content is almost identical to that of Arabidopsis. However the rps14 gene, which is a pseudo-gene in Arabidopsis, is intact in rapeseed. On the other hand, five tRNA genes are missing in rapeseed compared to Arabidopsis, although the set of mitochondrially encoded tRNA species is identical in the two Cruciferae. RNA editing events were systematically investigated on the basis of the sequence of the rapeseed mitochondrial genome. A total of 427 C to U conversions were identified in ORFs, which is nearly identical to the number in Arabidopsis (441 sites). The gene sequences and intron structures are mostly conserved (more than 99% similarity for protein-coding regions); however, only 358 editing sites (83% of total editings) are shared by rapeseed and Arabidopsis. Non-coding regions are mostly divergent between the two plants. One-third (about 78.7 kb) and two-thirds (about 223.8 kb) of the rapeseed and Arabidopsis mitochondrial genomes, respectively, cannot be aligned with each other and most of these regions do not show any homology to sequences registered in the DNA databases. The results of the comparative analysis between the rapeseed and Arabidopsis mitochondrial genomes suggest that higher plant mitochondria are extremely conservative with respect to coding sequences and somewhat conservative with respect to RNA editing, but that non-coding parts of plant mitochondrial DNA are extraordinarily dynamic with respect to structural changes, sequence acquisition and/or sequence loss.

###begin title 4
INTRODUCTION
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 632 635 632 635 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cis</italic>
###xml 641 646 641 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trans</italic>
Mitochondria are semiautonomous organelles whose universally recognized function is to produce cellular ATP by the process of oxidative phosphorylation. This mitochondrial function is conserved in all eukaryotic cells, i.e. animal, fungi and plant cells, but nevertheless, the mitochondrial genomes of higher plants exhibit a number of unique features compared to their counterparts in animals or fungi. Higher plant mitochondrial genomes are not only larger in size, but they also contain structural rearrangements due to homologous intra- or inter-molecular recombination events. Moreover, specific modes of gene expression (e.g. cis- and trans-splicing, RNA editing, etc.) complicate the analysis of the information encoded by plant mitochondrial genomes.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 93 107 93 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Brassica hirta</italic>
###xml 164 165 164 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c1">1</xref>
###xml 245 246 245 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c2">2</xref>
###xml 418 419 418 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c2">2</xref>
###xml 770 790 770 790 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana</italic>
###xml 807 808 807 808 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c3">3</xref>
###xml 809 810 809 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c4">4</xref>
###xml 838 839 838 839 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c5">5</xref>
###xml 93 107 <span type="species:ncbi:3728">Brassica hirta</span>
###xml 202 211 <span type="species:ncbi:3656">muskmelon</span>
###xml 337 345 <span type="species:ncbi:3659">cucumber</span>
###xml 770 790 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
###xml 795 805 <span type="species:ncbi:3555">sugar beet</span>
###xml 832 836 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
The sizes of mitochondrial genomes vary widely even among higher plant species. With 208 kb, Brassica hirta has the smallest mitochondrial genome in higher plants (1), while the mitochondrial genome of muskmelon is estimated to be over 2400 kb (2). This size variation can occur relatively rapidly in evolution as exemplified within the cucumber family, where the mitochondrial genome size varies by more than 6-fold (2). Similarly, the plant mitochondrial genome organization is known to be very dynamic. It would be of great interest to understand such rapid evolutionary changes in size and structure, and their consequences with respect to gene content. To date, the complete mitochondrial genome sequences in higher plants have been determined in two dicot plants, Arabidopsis thaliana and sugar beet (3,4) and in one monocot, rice (5). Although significant structural differences are observed among the mitochondrial genomes of these three plant species, most of the genes are conserved aside from the occasional transfer of a mitochondrial gene to the nuclear genome in one angiosperm clade or the other. However, these species are only distantly related to each other, and not suitable for investigating rapid evolutionary changes over the entire mitochondrial genome.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 25 39 25 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Brassica napus</italic>
###xml 197 208 197 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 301 312 301 312 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 436 437 436 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c6">6</xref>
###xml 476 487 476 487 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 498 499 498 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c3">3</xref>
###xml 617 625 617 625 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Brassica</italic>
###xml 25 39 <span type="species:ncbi:3708">Brassica napus</span>
In this study, rapeseed (Brassica napus L.) was chosen for the analysis of the tempo and mode of evolutionary changes for several reasons. First, rapeseed belongs to Cruciferae, the same family as Arabidopsis. It occupies a good phylogenetic position for making comparisons with the already sequenced Arabidopsis mitochondrial genome. Second, physical mapping revealed that the mitochondrial genome in rapeseed is about 220 kb in size (6), which is only two-thirds of that in Arabidopsis (367 kb) (3). This implies that these two plants can be used to study the evolution of genome size. Third, it is well known that Brassica species, including rapeseed, have the smallest mitochondrial genomes among higher plants. Investigation of the rapeseed mitochondrial genome can thus reveal the minimum sequence requirement for the mitochondrial genome of higher plants.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 103 117 103 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Brassica napus</italic>
###xml 163 174 163 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A.thaliana.</italic>
###xml 103 117 <span type="species:ncbi:3708">Brassica napus</span>
###xml 163 173 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">A.thaliana</span>
Here the complete nucleotide sequence and RNA editing content of the mitochondrial genome in rapeseed (Brassica napus L.) are presented and compared with those of A.thaliana.

###begin title 9
MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 9
###begin title 10
Mitochondrial DNA and RNA isolation
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 198 199 198 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c7">7</xref>
Mitochondria were isolated from green leaves of 8-week-old rapeseed plants (cv. Wester). Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) and mitochondrial RNA (mtRNA) were prepared using procedures described previously (7).
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Sequencing procedures
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 52 53 52 53 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c6">6</xref>
Based on the physical map of rapeseed mitochondria (6) and available sequences of rapeseed mitochondrial genes, 28 oligonucleotides were synthesized. Using mitochondrial DNA as a template, long PCR was carried out using these nucleotides as primers to amplify 14 parts of the mitochondrial genome. The amplified fragments were completely digested with BamHI, EcoRI and HindIII, or were partially digested with Sau3AI. A recombinant DNA library containing BamHI, EcoRI, HindIII and Sau3AI fragments in pBluescript II SK+ vector (Stratagene, USA) provided templates for sequencing. PCR-derived amplification products were also used to close gaps between restriction fragments. Each base of the rapeseed mtDNA was covered at least three times (6.8 times on average). The DNA sequencer used was a Li-COR 4200L2 (Li-COR, USA).
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Data analysis
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 220 221 220 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c8">8</xref>
###xml 292 293 292 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c9">9</xref>
###xml 53 56 <span type="species:ncbi:37162?0.34174508126603936">MAC</span>
Sequences were evaluated and assembled using GENETYX-MAC ver11.2.3 (Software Development, Japan). A database search was done at the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) using the BLAST network service (; 8). A tRNA gene search was carried out with the tRNA scan-SE service (; 9). The Pairwise BLAST program on our local server was used for the comparison between the whole genome sequences of rapeseed and the mitochondrial genomes of other plants.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
RNA editing determination
###end title 16
###begin p 17
Mitochondrial RNA was reverse transcribed with random hexamers using Superscript II reverse transcriptase (Invitrogen, USA). PCR amplification was done with primer pairs specific to each open reading frame (ORF), and the resultant PCR products were sequenced directly. Fluorescent charts for cDNA and genomic sequences were compared to determine RNA editing sites.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
###end title 18
###begin title 19
Gene organization
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 92 93 92 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f1">1</xref>
###xml 123 134 123 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 155 156 155 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c3">3</xref>
###xml 236 247 236 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 497 501 497 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrn5</italic>
###xml 503 508 503 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrn18</italic>
###xml 510 515 510 515 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrn26</italic>
###xml 544 545 544 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg795tb1">1</xref>
###xml 550 551 550 551 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg795tb2">2</xref>
###xml 708 709 708 709 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f1">1</xref>
###xml 792 803 792 803 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 860 865 860 865 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps14</italic>
###xml 874 875 874 875 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg795tb1">1</xref>
###xml 928 933 928 933 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps14</italic>
###xml 959 970 959 970 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 972 974 972 974 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c10">10</xref>
###xml 1023 1034 1023 1034 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1036 1041 1036 1041 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmFN</italic>
###xml 1085 1091 1085 1091 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmFN1</italic>
###xml 1096 1102 1096 1102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmFN2</italic>
###xml 1105 1106 1105 1106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c3">3</xref>
###xml 1107 1109 1107 1109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c11">11</xref>
###xml 1238 1239 1238 1239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c5">5</xref>
###xml 1360 1361 1360 1361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg795tb2">2</xref>
###xml 1459 1470 1459 1470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1817 1828 1817 1828 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1950 1955 1950 1955 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps12</italic>
###xml 1994 1999 1994 1999 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl16</italic>
The rapeseed mtDNA sequence forms a circle of 221 853 bp (accession number: AP006444) (Fig. 1), two-thirds the size of the Arabidopsis mtDNA (366 924 bp) (3). The overall G + C content of rapeseed mtDNA (45.2%) is comparable to that of Arabidopsis mtDNA (44.8%). Homology searches using the BLASTN and BLASTX programs and a tRNA gene search using the tRNA scan-SE program detected a total of 54 genes in the rapeseed mitochondrial genome: 34 known protein-coding genes, three ribosomal RNA genes (rrn5, rrn18, rrn26), and 17 tRNA genes (Tables 1 and 2), which altogether account for 17.4% (38 662 bp) of the genome. The positions of these genes in the rapeseed mitochondrial genome are illustrated in Figure 1. The content of protein-coding genes is completely identical between rapeseed and Arabidopsis with the exception of a gene for ribosomal protein S14 (rps14) (Table 1). Due to a stop codon and a deleted nucleotide, the rps14 gene is a pseudo-gene in Arabidopsis (10) but an intact ORF in rapeseed. In rapeseed and Arabidopsis, ccmFN genes are divided into two reading frames (ccmFN1 and ccmFN2) (3,11), a known peculiarity of the mitochondrial genomes of these two Cruciferae species, which distinguishes them from other plants (5). Seventeen tRNA genes specifying 15 species of amino acids were identified in the rapeseed mitochondrial genome (Table 2). Of these, 11 tRNAs are of mitochondrial origin and six are considered to be of plastid origin. Arabidopsis mtDNA encodes 22 tRNA genes, five more than rapeseed mtDNA. However, in both plant species the genes for at least six tRNAs (for amino acids G, A, V, L, T and R) are absent from the mitochondrial genome and these tRNAs have to be imported from the nucleus. The DNA sequences of the genes described above are highly conserved between rapeseed and Arabidopsis. Most of the protein-coding genes show more than 99% sequence similarity; the lowest similarity (97.3%) is found between rps12 genes and the highest (99.9%) between rpl16 genes.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 319 330 319 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 483 494 483 494 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
In addition to previously identified genes, 45 ORFs larger than 100 codons in size were annotated in the rapeseed mitochondrial genome. None of these ORFs could be assigned a function based on sequence similarity at either the nucleotide or protein level. Moreover, none of these rapeseed ORFs has a counterpart in the Arabidopsis mitochondrial genome, in which 85 ORFs larger than 100 codons were described, although limited similarity is observed between some ORFs of rapeseed and Arabidopsis. Also, most ORFs do not show any significant homology to other genes found in the nuclear or plastid genome, although some have similarity to nuclear transposon or plastid sequences. However, all of these ORFs of possible transposon or plastid origin seem to be non-functional because of their fragmented and/or truncated nature.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
In summary, despite their large size difference, the mtDNAs of the two species share nearly the same set of functional genes (protein genes, rRNA genes and tRNA genes). The additional ORFs are not shared between these two closely related plants, which means that these ORFs could not code for additional important protein information and may instead, although it seems unlikely, fulfil a species-specific function.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
Large repeat sequences for tripartite structure
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 139 140 139 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c6">6</xref>
###xml 229 233 229 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cox2</italic>
###xml 245 246 245 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f1">1</xref>
###xml 288 292 288 292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cox2</italic>
###xml 421 427 421 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cox2-1</italic>
###xml 436 437 436 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f1">1</xref>
###xml 482 486 482 486 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cox2</italic>
###xml 509 515 509 515 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cox2-2</italic>
###xml 524 525 524 525 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f1">1</xref>
###xml 722 723 722 723 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c6">6</xref>
In this study, a 2427 bp sequence was found to be present as a direct repeat in the rapeseed mitochondrial genome, as reported previously (6). This sequence included the first exon, the intron, and part of the second exon of the cox2 gene (Fig. 1). Due to this duplication, two copies of cox2 genes exist in rapeseed, although these copies diverge from each other at the point 55 bp upstream of the stop codon. One copy (cox2-1 in Fig. 1) is homologous to other plant mitochondrial cox2 genes, but the other (cox2-2 in Fig. 1) has an extension that shows no homology to any other sequences. The presence of these 2427 bp repeats implies a tricircular structure for the rapeseed mitochondrial genome, as postulated before (6). Via these 2427 bp repeats, the rapeseed mitochondrial genome could recombine into two subgenomic circles (124 908 and 96 945 bp circles).
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 45 56 45 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 137 138 137 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c3">3</xref>
###xml 139 140 139 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c4">4</xref>
###xml 254 258 254 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp6</italic>
###xml 263 269 263 269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">orf139</italic>
###xml 281 292 281 292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 294 299 294 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrn26</italic>
###xml 326 330 326 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cox2</italic>
###xml 542 544 542 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c12">12</xref>
###xml 577 584 577 584 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B.hirta</italic>
###xml 586 587 586 587 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c1">1</xref>
###xml 793 804 793 804 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 61 71 <span type="species:ncbi:3555">sugar beet</span>
###xml 310 320 <span type="species:ncbi:3555">sugar beet</span>
###xml 577 584 <span type="species:ncbi:3728">B.hirta</span>
Large repeat sequences are also found in the Arabidopsis and sugar beet mitochondrial genomes (6.5 and 4.2 kb, and 6.2 kb, respectively; 3,4), and have been suggested to be active in intramolecular recombination. However, the sequences of these repeats (atp6 and orf139 regions in Arabidopsis, rrn26 region in sugar beet, and cox2 region in rapeseed) are completely different from each other. Intramolecular recombination to generate the complexity of the plant mitochondrial genome is a common feature of higher plant mitochondrial genomes (12), except for one known example, B.hirta (1). However, the DNA sequences involved in the intramolecular recombination are highly species-specific, and different sequences act as recombination repeats even within related species such as rapeseed and Arabidopsis.
###end p 25
###begin title 26
Sequences shared among rapeseed and other plant genomes
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 122 133 122 133 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 156 157 156 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c3">3</xref>
###xml 193 194 193 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c4">4</xref>
###xml 197 208 197 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 225 227 225 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c13">13</xref>
###xml 272 274 272 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c14">14</xref>
###xml 326 329 326 329 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;10</sup>
###xml 160 170 <span type="species:ncbi:3555">sugar beet</span>
Using the Pairwise BLAST software, the whole sequence of the rapeseed mitochondrial genome was compared with those of the Arabidopsis mitochondrial genome (3), sugar beet mitochondrial genome (4), Arabidopsis plastid genome (13) and rapeseed mitochondrial linear plasmid (14). The E (expectation) value threshold was set at 1e-10 for these comparative analyses among rapeseed and other genomes.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 65 76 65 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 403 414 403 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 431 432 431 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c4">4</xref>
###xml 551 562 551 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 753 764 753 764 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1277 1288 1277 1288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 254 264 <span type="species:ncbi:3555">sugar beet</span>
###xml 419 429 <span type="species:ncbi:3555">sugar beet</span>
###xml 608 618 <span type="species:ncbi:3555">sugar beet</span>
When the mitochondrial genome of rapeseed is compared to that of Arabidopsis, the sequences shared by these two related species total 143 126 bp, which represents 64.5% of the rapeseed genome. On the other hand, the sequences shared between rapeseed and sugar beet total 64 972 bp, representing 29.3% of the rapeseed genome. It has been reported that 78 057 bp of the mtDNA sequences are shared between Arabidopsis and sugar beet (4). These values are in good agreement with the phylogenetic relationship among these three plant species: rapeseed and Arabidopsis belong to the same family, Cruciferae, while sugar beet is a member of another family, Chenopodiaceae. However, one-third (about 78.7 kb) and two-thirds (about 223.8 kb) of the rapeseed and Arabidopsis mitochondrial genomes, respectively, were not aligned with each other. These non-homologous sequences are scattered through the rapeseed mitochondrial genome, and a total of 31 such regions longer than 1000 bp were identified. These data suggest that after divergence of these two species, in addition to the multiple recombination events, the mitochondrial genomes of both plants were very quickly modified by sequence acquisition and/or sequence loss encompassing up to two-thirds of the genome in the case of Arabidopsis. The large size variation between these two plants might be due partly to such rapid processes of sequence acquisition and/or sequence loss.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 250 261 250 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
A BLAST search was carried out for the 31 non-homologous regions longer than 1 kb (a total of 51 517 bp) to explore the origins of these sequences. Of these sequences, 13.2% showed similarity to sequences in other plant mitochondrial genomes (not to Arabidopsis sequences) and 5.9% appeared to be of plastid origin. The fraction of sequences showing similarity to nuclear genome sequences was only 0.3%. The remaining sequences (80.6%) did not show any similarity to the registered sequences in the DNA sequence databases. Identifying the origin of these non-homologous sequences is of great importance and interest for understanding the evolution of higher plant mitochondrial genomes.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 60 71 60 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 248 251 248 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cis</italic>
###xml 76 86 <span type="species:ncbi:3555">sugar beet</span>
The mtDNA sequences shared by three dicot plants, rapeseed, Arabidopsis and sugar beet, totaled 61 870 bp, which corresponds to 27.9% of the rapeseed genome. This value is comparable to the sum total (68 343 bp) of coding sequences (38 662 bp) and cis-splicing introns (29 681 bp) of the rapeseed mitochondrial genome. These data suggest that the gene-coding sequences may be highly conserved among the mitochondrial genomes of higher plants, but other regions, i.e. non-coding sequences, may have diverged very rapidly during evolution, as they can no longer be aligned with each other.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 202 213 202 213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 445 449 445 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbcL</italic>
###xml 454 458 454 458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoB</italic>
###xml 644 655 644 655 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 723 727 723 727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psaA</italic>
###xml 732 736 732 736 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ycf2</italic>
###xml 767 778 767 778 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 823 827 823 827 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psbD</italic>
###xml 832 836 832 836 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ndhB</italic>
###xml 850 861 850 861 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
In the rapeseed mitochondrial genome, 14 stretches of plastid-like sequences ranging from 43 to 2181 bp were identified. These sequences totaled 7950 bp, comprising 3.6% of the mitochondrial genome. In Arabidopsis, about 1% of the mitochondrial genome contains sequences imported from the plastid. In rapeseed, nine of the 14 plastid sequences found contain tRNA genes, seven of which are well conserved between the two plants. The fragments of rbcL and rpoB are also conserved in both plant mitochondria. Thus, these sequences may have been transferred from the plastid genome to the mitochondrial genome before the divergence of rapeseed and Arabidopsis. However, the rapeseed mitochondrial genome contains pieces of the psaA and ycf2 genes, which are not found in Arabidopsis mtDNA. On the other hand, the pieces of the psbD and ndhB genes in the Arabidopsis mitochondrial genome are not present in rapeseed mtDNA. These results also suggest that functional sequences may be highly conserved, but sequence acquisition and/or sequence loss may be very frequent for non-functional gene pieces. Indeed, plastid tRNA genes in the mitochondrial genomes are intact and most likely functional, but protein-coding gene pieces seem to have no function in the mitochondrial genome of either plant.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 122 124 122 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c14">14</xref>
###xml 598 609 598 609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 711 722 711 722 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 760 770 <span type="species:ncbi:3555">sugar beet</span>
It has been reported that a rapeseed mitochondrial linear plasmid had no sequence similarity to the mitochondrial genome (14). However, a 19 bp sequence, CTCTYCTTTCAGTYGAGTT, was found to be common to these two DNA molecules. This sequence is located at three positions in the upstream regions of ORF2, 3 and 4 of the linear plasmid but is scattered at 41 positions in the rapeseed mitochondrial genome. Since there are no known functions (promoter, binding, etc.) for this sequence motif, it is unclear what role it has in rapeseed mitochondria. Interestingly, this motif is found 22 times in the Arabidopsis mitochondrial genome, although there have been no reports showing the presence of linear plasmids in Arabidopsis mitochondria. On the other hand, the sugar beet mitochondrial genome contains no such sequence motif. This sequence may be related to the co-evolution of the mitochondrial linear plasmid and mitochondrial genome specific to the Cruciferae species.
###end p 32
###begin title 33
Potential promoters
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 178 180 178 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c15">15</xref>
###xml 525 530 525 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 533 535 533 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c16">16</xref>
By comparing sequences surrounding transcription start sites of plant mitochondria, the conserved nonanucleotide motif (CNM) has been identified in dicotyledonous plant species (15). Since no experimental information about the 5' termini of primary transcripts is available for rapeseed, identification of potential promoter regions was carried out by screening of the rapeseed mitochondrial genome sequence with the CNM motif. In this search, only six CNM sequence variations were used, following the criteria of Dombrowski et al. (16).
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 467 474 467 474 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 579 584 579 584 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrn18</italic>
###xml 590 601 590 601 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 649 654 649 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrn18</italic>
###xml 787 789 787 789 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c17">17</xref>
###xml 833 838 833 838 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrn18</italic>
###xml 904 921 904 921 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis rrn18</italic>
###xml 1048 1052 1048 1052 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp8</italic>
###xml 1069 1073 1069 1073 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">orfB</italic>
###xml 1076 1078 1076 1078 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c18">18</xref>
###xml 1082 1086 1082 1086 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp9</italic>
###xml 1088 1093 1088 1093 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rrn26</italic>
###xml 1181 1192 1181 1192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1250 1261 1250 1261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1330 1334 1330 1334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps7</italic>
###xml 1336 1340 1336 1340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl2</italic>
###xml 1449 1460 1449 1460 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1569 1580 1569 1580 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
Twenty-nine CNM motifs were found in the rapeseed mitochondrial genome. The number of potential promoters was reduced to 21 by specifying other characteristics of promoter sequences of dicot plants: the presence of an AT-box upstream of CNM and at least one purine at nucleotide positions +3 and +4. Fifteen promoters are located within a 1 kb region upstream of known genes and ORFs. These potential promoters may promote the transcription of the coding information in vivo. For instance, two of these potential promoters are found in the upstream region of the 18S rRNA genes (rrn18). In Arabidopsis, the transcription initiation site upstream of rrn18 was experimentally investigated by primer extension analysis, and the CNM was found to reside at the transcription initiation site (17). Potential promoter sequences of rapeseed rrn18 were identified at the same position in the homologous region of Arabidopsis rrn18, except that the rapeseed sequence is duplicated. In total, seven potential promoters upstream of identified genes, including atp8 [formerly named orfB; (18)], atp9, rrn26 and some tRNA genes, are located at the same positions as in the homologous regions of Arabidopsis. These genes may be transcribed in the same manner as in Arabidopsis using the same promoters. However, the four potential promoters for rps7, rpl2 and two tRNA genes are either absent or present at a different position in the corresponding regions of the Arabidopsis mitochondrial genome. For these genes, there may be different transcriptional controls between rapeseed and Arabidopsis.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 113 124 113 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 173 177 173 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nad6</italic>
###xml 182 186 182 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp1</italic>
###xml 291 302 291 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 339 343 339 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nad6</italic>
###xml 348 352 348 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp1</italic>
Some of the genes without a CNM in their 5' UTR show only limited similarity to the corresponding regions of the Arabidopsis mitochondrial genome. For example, the rapeseed nad6 and atp1 5' UTR sequences diverged 2 and 53 bp upstream of the initiation codon, respectively, compared with the Arabidopsis sequences. The transcription of the nad6 and atp1 genes is likely to be initiated from different non-CNM promoters in the two plants. The actual number of promoters in the rapeseed mitochondrial genome should be investigated experimentally.
###end p 36
###begin title 37
Introns and RNA editing sites
###end title 37
###begin p 38
Before translation, mitochondrial messages in higher plant mitochondria may undergo RNA splicing and/or RNA editing, two post-transcriptional processing events that may critically alter the coding message.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 208 211 208 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cis</italic>
###xml 318 323 318 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trans</italic>
###xml 364 367 364 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nad</italic>
###xml 375 379 375 379 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nad1</italic>
###xml 381 385 381 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nad2</italic>
###xml 387 391 387 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nad5</italic>
###xml 463 474 463 474 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 497 498 497 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c3">3</xref>
In the rapeseed mitochondrial genome, 24 introns ranging in size between 839 and 3448 bp were identified. All of these introns belong exclusively to group II, as in other higher plants. Nineteen introns were cis-spliced sequences, and comprised 29 681 bp or 13.4% of the rapeseed mitochondrial genome. A total of five trans-spliced introns were found in the three nad genes, nad1, nad2, nad5. The intron numbers and positions are completely identical to those of Arabidopsis mitochondrial genome (3). Intron structures are also highly conserved between these two species.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 199 210 199 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 289 300 289 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 323 325 323 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c19">19</xref>
###xml 677 678 677 678 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg795tb3">3</xref>
###xml 705 711 705 711 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">orf222</italic>
###xml 730 736 730 736 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">orf224</italic>
###xml 798 800 798 800 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c20">20</xref>
RNA editing leads to change of a C to a U residue, sometimes creating an initiation or termination codon, but more often creating an internal codon with strong functional relevance. The editotype of Arabidopsis, that is, all editing sites found in the systematic transcriptome analysis of Arabidopsis, has been determined (19) and shown to consist of 441 sites scattered throughout the mitochondrial genome. There are 34 known protein-coding genes in rapeseed mitochondria as stated above. In total, 24 genes and nine ORFs longer than 150 amino acids were subjected to the editing analysis in this study. The remaining 10 genes had already been analyzed for RNA editing (Table 3). In addition, editing of orf222, the homologue of orf224 associated with Polima male sterile cytoplasm, was detected (20).
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 126 127 126 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg795tb3">3</xref>
###xml 254 260 254 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">orf188</italic>
###xml 374 385 374 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 387 389 387 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c19">19</xref>
###xml 556 567 556 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 601 602 601 602 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkg795tb3">3</xref>
###xml 734 745 734 745 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
In summary, 427 C to U RNA editing modifications were detected in genes and ORFs of rapeseed mitochondrial transcripts (Table 3). None of nine unidentified ORFs examined had editing sites in their transcripts, except for one partial editing event in the orf188 transcript. The total number of editing sites (427 sites) in rapeseed is nearly identical to that (441 sites) in Arabidopsis (19). However, the number of editing sites shared by both plant mitochondria was only 358, which correspond to 83.8% and 81.2% of the total editing sites in rapeseed and Arabidopsis transcripts, respectively (Table 3). These percentages seem to be quite low considering that the average similarity in the primary DNA sequences between rapeseed and Arabidopsis is 99.2% for protein coding regions. This means that the tempo of diversification of RNA editing is more rapid than that of coding information.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 63 67 63 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps3</italic>
###xml 72 77 72 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl16</italic>
###xml 165 170 165 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl16</italic>
###xml 229 236 229 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Petunia</italic>
###xml 241 258 241 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis rpl16</italic>
###xml 272 274 272 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c21">21</xref>
###xml 275 277 275 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c22">22</xref>
###xml 300 305 300 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl16</italic>
###xml 375 380 375 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl16</italic>
###xml 399 408 399 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oenothera</italic>
###xml 413 423 413 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Marchantia</italic>
###xml 514 516 514 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c23">23</xref>
###xml 517 519 517 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c24">24</xref>
###xml 757 762 757 762 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl16</italic>
Two RNA editing sites are located within the overlap region of rps3 and rpl16. The second of these two edits creates a stop codon 21 amino acids downstream from the rpl16 ATG codon. This RNA editing event is also observed in the Petunia and Arabidopsis rpl16 transcripts (21,22), which suggests that rpl16 gene might be a pseudo-gene. However, no ATG codon is encoded in the rpl16 reading frames of Oenothera and Marchantia mitochondria, and a valine GTG codon is postulated to be used for translation initiation (23,24). This GTG codon is conserved in rapeseed mitochondria, 15 amino acids downstream from the stop codon introduced by the editing event. If GTG is used for translation initiation, RNA editing would have no effect on the translation of the rpl16 gene.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 13 18 13 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps14</italic>
###xml 54 59 54 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps14</italic>
###xml 178 183 178 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps14</italic>
###xml 239 248 239 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oenothera</italic>
###xml 258 260 258 260 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c25">25</xref>
###xml 261 263 261 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c27">27</xref>
###xml 278 283 278 283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps14</italic>
###xml 324 335 324 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 353 355 353 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c28">28</xref>
###xml 356 358 356 358 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c30">30</xref>
###xml 401 416 401 416 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oenothera rps14</italic>
###xml 430 432 430 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c26">26</xref>
###xml 566 577 566 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 581 586 579 584 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps14</italic>
###xml 643 652 641 650 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oenothera</italic>
###xml 665 667 663 665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c26">26</xref>
###xml 746 751 744 749 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps14</italic>
###xml 815 824 813 822 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oenothera</italic>
###xml 868 869 866 867 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f2">2</xref>
###xml 1031 1032 1029 1030 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f2">2</xref>
###xml 1090 1095 1088 1093 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps14</italic>
###xml 1235 1240 1233 1238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rps14</italic>
###xml 227 237 <span type="species:ncbi:3906">broad bean</span>
###xml 253 256 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">pea</span>
###xml 337 342 <span type="species:ncbi:4577|species:ncbi:381124">maize</span>
###xml 347 351 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
The rapeseed rps14 gene transcript is not edited. The rps14 gene is one example of the gene-transfer events from the mitochondrion to the nucleus often found in plants. In fact, rps14 is located in the mitochondrial genomes of broad bean, Oenothera and pea (25-27). In contrast rps14 has been translocated to the nucleus in Arabidopsis, maize and rice (28-30). Two RNA editing events were observed in Oenothera rps14 transcripts (26). The first editing event altered a codon from TCC (Ser) to TTC (Phe), and the second one modified a CCT (Pro) triplet to TCT (Ser). Arabidopsis Psirps14 transcripts have retained the first editing site in the Oenothera transcript (26). Alignment of the deduced amino acid sequences from rapeseed and other plant rps14 genes allowed us to determine that the second editing event in Oenothera occurred at a non-conserved position (Fig. 2). However the first RNA editing event, leading to the change of a Ser residue to a conserved Phe, seems to be essential for producing mature RPS14 protein (Fig. 2). It is possible that the rapeseed mitochondrial copy of rps14 might be non-functional, although it is an intact reading frame and is transcribed. Further work is needed to examine whether a functional rps14 gene exists in the rapeseed nuclear genome.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 96 100 96 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmC</italic>
###xml 239 243 239 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmC</italic>
###xml 264 265 264 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f3">3</xref>
###xml 301 302 301 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f3">3</xref>
###xml 392 393 392 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f3">3</xref>
###xml 453 457 453 457 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmC</italic>
###xml 470 471 470 471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f3">3</xref>
###xml 506 510 506 510 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmC</italic>
###xml 651 655 651 655 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmC</italic>
###xml 786 802 786 802 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis ccmC</italic>
###xml 831 835 831 835 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmC</italic>
###xml 850 861 850 861 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 915 916 915 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f3">3</xref>
###xml 1038 1042 1038 1042 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmC</italic>
###xml 1280 1285 1280 1285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmFN</italic>
###xml 1335 1337 1335 1337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c11">11</xref>
###xml 1344 1349 1344 1349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmFN</italic>
###xml 1415 1421 1415 1421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmFN1</italic>
###xml 1426 1432 1426 1432 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmFN2</italic>
###xml 1443 1444 1443 1444 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg795f1">1</xref>
###xml 1572 1574 1572 1574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c31">31</xref>
###xml 1828 1833 1828 1833 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmFN</italic>
###xml 1565 1570 <span type="species:ncbi:4565">wheat</span>
During the systematic analysis of RNA editing sites in rapeseed mitochondria, it was found that ccmC cDNA could not be amplified using the primer pair ccmC-F and ccmC-R, which are located before the start codon and after the stop codon of ccmC, respectively (Fig. 3A for the primer positions and Fig. 3B for the PCR products). Two other 3'-primers, ccmC-R1 and ccmC-R2 were also tested (Fig. 3A). Only one primer pair, ccmC-F and ccmC-R1, could amplify ccmC cDNAs (Fig. 3B). These results suggest that the ccmC transcripts are interrupted within the 50 bp-sequence before the stop codon. Twenty-five RNA editing sites were identified in the truncated ccmC transcript (ccmC-R1-primed cDNA); however, no editing creating a stop codon has been detected. This situation is also observed in Arabidopsis ccmC transcripts. The end of the ccmC transcript in Arabidopsis was mapped between primers ccmC-R1 and ccmC-R2 (Fig. 3A) (S.Binder and P.Giege, personal communications). There are no data demonstrating the presence of a nuclear copy of the ccmC gene or the presence of the CcmC protein itself in rapeseed mitochondria at present. However, the present finding raises the question of the existence of CcmC and its mode of translation. Previously we reported that the structure of the ccmFN gene in rapeseed mitochondria was quite unusual (11). The ccmFN sequence is divided into two parts, which are about 74 kb apart (ccmFN1 and ccmFN2, see Fig. 1). These two parts are transcribed and their transcripts are edited in a similar manner as the homologous transcripts of wheat (31). However, no mature transcript covering the whole coding region has been detected, a result which excludes a trans-splicing event. No other copy of this gene was found in either the nuclear or mitochondrial genome. Nevertheless, the protein product of ccmFN was identified in rapeseed mitochondria by western blot analysis. The mode of expression of these CcmFN and CcmC proteins in rapeseed remains mysterious.
###end p 44
###begin title 45
CONCLUSIONS
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 246 247 246 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg795c6">6</xref>
###xml 297 305 297 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Brassica</italic>
###xml 556 567 556 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
The comparative analysis presented here allows a more comprehensive understanding of mitochondrial genome evolution in higher plants. It had previously been noted that plant mitochondrial DNA evolves rapidly in structure, but slowly in sequence (6), which was concluded based on comparisons among Brassica species. Palmer and Herbon found numerous internal rearrangements of mitochondrial genomes by homologous recombination events through short dispersed repeats as the points of crossover. This picture is still true in part. In this study, rapeseed and Arabidopsis were shown to have nearly the same sets of genes for mitochondrial proteins, rRNAs and tRNAs, although the gene orders are different. The intron contents and locations are also completely conserved in the two plants. These results support the idea that internal genome rearrangement is one of the major driving forces for the mitochondrial genome evolution of higher plants.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 211 222 211 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 520 531 520 531 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1040 1051 1040 1051 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1056 1066 <span type="species:ncbi:3555">sugar beet</span>
However, this study showed that large parts of mitochondrial genomes in higher plants are species-specific and show no homology to other plant mitochondrial genomes. One-third and two-thirds of the rapeseed and Arabidopsis mitochondrial genomes, respectively, were not aligned with each other. This means that after divergence of these species, the mitochondrial genomes of both plants were very quickly modified by sequence acquisition and/or sequence loss, accounting for up to two-thirds of the genome in the case of Arabidopsis. At this level, rapid sequence transfer, sequence acquisition and/or sequence loss is thought to have been a more important factor in the evolution than internal genome rearrangement. Parts of these non-alignable sequences originated from plastid or nuclear genomes, but most of them show no homology to registered sequences. It will be necessary to clarify the origin of these sequences and how they were transferred to mitochondria. Furthermore, the mtDNA sequences shared by three dicot plants, rapeseed, Arabidopsis and sugar beet, are limited to the coding sequences and introns. These data suggest that the plant mitochondrial genome is very dynamic and fluid, except for the primary gene information.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 67 78 67 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 484 495 484 495 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
Some potential promoter regions are conserved between rapeseed and Arabidopsis, but the upstream regions of some genes are completely divergent from each other. On the other hand, RNA editing sites shared by both plant mitochondria constitute about 80% of the total editing sites. The remaining 20% of editing sites are species-specific. This percentage is quite high considering that the primary DNA sequences for the protein-coding regions are highly conserved between rapeseed and Arabidopsis. These data suggest that the evolutionary speed is higher at the level of gene regulation than at the primary gene sequence level.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 61 65 61 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmC</italic>
###xml 175 180 175 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ccmFN</italic>
An additional notable finding is that the transcripts of the ccmC gene are truncated before the stop codon. Previously we reported the unusual structure and expression of the ccmFN gene in rapeseed mitochondria. Although there are no precise data about the expression of CcmC protein in rapeseed mitochondria so far, the present finding raises another question about expression of Ccm proteins in rapeseed.
###end p 49
