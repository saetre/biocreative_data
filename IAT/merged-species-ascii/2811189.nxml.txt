###begin article-title 0
Roles of the Src Tyrosine Kinases Lck and Fyn in Regulating gammadeltaTCR Signal Strength
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: RML SMH. Performed the experiments: RML SMH. Analyzed the data: RML SMH. Wrote the paper: RML SMH.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 597 601 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Lck and Fyn, members of the Src family of tyrosine kinases, are key components of the alphabetaTCR-coupled signaling pathway. While it is generally accepted that both Lck and Fyn positively regulate signal transduction by the alphabetaTCR, recent studies have shown that Lck and Fyn have distinct functions in this signaling pathway, with Lck being a positive regulator and Fyn being a negative regulator of alphabetaTCR signal transduction. To determine whether Lck and Fyn also differentially regulate gammadeltaTCR signal transduction, we analyzed gammadelta T cell development and function in mice with reduced Lck or Fyn expression levels. We found that reducing Lck or Fyn levels altered the strength of the gammadeltaTCR signaling response, with low levels of Lck weakening gammadeltaTCR signal strength and low levels of Fyn augmenting gammadeltaTCR signal strength. These alterations in gammadeltaTCR signal strength had profound effects not only on alphabeta/gammadelta lineage choice, but also on gammadelta thymocyte maturation and gammadelta T cell effector function. These results indicate that the cellular levels of Lck and Fyn play a role in regulating the strength of the gammadeltaTCR signaling response at different stages in the life of the gammadelta T cell.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 455 458 455 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Palacios1">[1]</xref>
###xml 460 463 460 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Salmond1">[2]</xref>
Signaling by the TCR is required at multiple stages in the life of a T cell. In the thymus, TCR signaling is necessary for lineage commitment and repertoire selection, while in the periphery, TCR signaling is necessary for maintenance of the peripheral T cell pool and for activation and differentiation of mature T cells. Lck and Fyn, two members of the Src family of tyrosine kinases (SFKs), are involved in initiating the TCR-coupled signaling cascade [1], [2]. Following TCR engagement, Lck and/or Fyn phosphorylate the tyrosines within the ITAMs of the CD3 and TCRzeta chains. This proximal signaling event leads to the recruitment of other signaling molecules to the TCR signaling complex and to the subsequent activation of signaling pathways that ultimately lead to the nucleus and initiation of gene transcription.

It is generally accepted that both Lck and Fyn positively regulate signal transduction by the alphabetaTCR because, in the absence of either one of these SFKs, alphabetaTCR signaling responses are impaired following anti-CD3 mAb stimulation [3]-[9]. However, it has also been shown that Lck and Fyn localize to different subcellular compartments [10], [11] and have different substrates [11], [12], suggesting that they have discrete functions during alphabeta T cell activation. This idea is supported by the disparate phenotypes of Lck- and Fyn-deficient mice. In Lck-/- mice, thymus cellularity is severely reduced, thymocyte development is almost completely blocked at the CD4+CD8+ (double-positive; DP) stage, and very few mature alphabetaTCR+ cells are detected in peripheral lymphoid tissues [12]-[14]. In contrast, Fyn-/- mice exhibit a mild defect in alphabeta T cell development, as shown by the fact that Fyn-/- thymocytes, when in vitro stimulated, do not flux calcium or proliferate as well as wild-type (WT) thymocytes [3], [5]. Despite this signaling defect in the thymus, equivalent numbers of alphabeta T cells are found in the periphery of Fyn-/- and WT mice [3], [5].

While recent studies have confirmed that Lck functions primarily as a positive regulator of alphabetaTCR signaling [15]-[17], evidence is accumulating in support of Fyn acting as a negative regulator of alphabetaTCR signaling. First, it has been shown that Fyn is responsible for phosphorylating the adaptor protein, phosphoprotein associated with glycolipid-enriched membranes or PAG, in both resting thymocytes and T cells [11]. Once phosphorylated, PAG then recruits Csk, an inhibitor of SFKs [18]. Recruitment of Csk to phosphorylated PAG is required for optimal Csk kinase activity because, in the absence of Fyn, there is reduced phosphorylation of PAG and reduced Csk kinase activity [11]. Therefore, by indirectly controlling the activity of the inhibitor Csk, Fyn may negatively regulate the activation threshold of alphabeta T cells [11]. It has also been shown that CD8+ T cells from Fyn-/- F5 alphabetaTCR Tg mice are hyperresponsive in comparison to CD8+ T cells from WT F5 alphabetaTCR Tg mice following in vitro stimulation with peptide and APCs [19]. This hyperresponsiveness is manifested as enhanced proliferation, increased IL-2 production and more effective cytolytic activity [19]. CD4+ T cells from Fyn-/- DO11.10 alphabetaTCR Tg mice, however, do not display increased proliferation compared to CD4+ T cells from WT DO11.10 alphabetaTCR Tg mice when stimulated, either in vitro or in vivo, with peptide and APCs [20]. Nonetheless, when activated under the appropriate priming conditions, CD4+ T cells from Fyn-/- DO11.10 alphabetaTCR Tg mice produce significantly more IL-4 or IFNgamma than CD4+ T cells from WT DO11.10 alphabetaTCR Tg mice [20]. Taken together, these findings suggest that Fyn negatively regulates the alphabetaTCR signaling response.

Since studies investigating the functions of Lck and Fyn have focused primarily on alphabeta T cells, it is not known whether their functional dichotomy is observed in only alphabeta T lineage cells or in both alphabeta and gammadelta T lineage cells. Analyses of Lck-/- and Lck-/- Fyn-/- mice have in fact revealed differences in the requirements for these SFKs in alphabeta and gammadelta T cell development. In Lck-/- mice, the number of thymic and peripheral gammadelta T cells is only modestly reduced compared to their numbers in WT mice [13], [14], [21]. Moreover, in Lck-/- Fyn-/- mice, in which alphabeta T cell development is completely abrogated, a small number of gammadeltaTCR+ cells do develop and can be detected in secondary lymphoid tissues, the small intestine, and the epidermis [21], [22]. These differential requirements for Lck and Fyn in alphabeta and gammadelta T cell development suggest that these SFKs may have different functions in alphabeta- and gammadeltaTCR signal transduction. To investigate this, we evaluated the individual roles of Lck and Fyn in the development and function of gammadelta lineage cells. Here, we report that Lck and Fyn expression levels vary in gammadelta lineage cells depending on their stage in development, with thymic gammadelta T cells expressing relatively high levels of Lck and Fyn and peripheral gammadelta T cells expressing relatively low levels of Lck and Fyn. These differences in the cellular levels of Lck and Fyn play a role in regulating the strength of the gammadeltaTCR signaling response at the different developmental stages because, when we reduced Lck or Fyn expression levels by using Lck+/- and Fyn+/- mice, we observed significant effects on alphabeta/gammadelta lineage choice, gammadelta thymocyte maturation, and gammadelta T cell effector function. Moreover, because reducing the levels of Lck or Fyn altered the gammadeltaTCR signaling response, such that low Lck levels weakened gammadeltaTCR signal strength and low Fyn levels augmented gammadeltaTCR signal strength, we conclude that Lck and Fyn have similar functions in alphabeta- and gammadeltaTCR signal transduction, with Lck serving to amplify the TCR signal and Fyn serving to dampen the TCR signal.

Although it is generally accepted that gammadelta T cells express Lck and Fyn, this idea is based more on indirect evidence from studies investigating gammadelta T cell development in Lck- or Fyn-deficient mice [13], [14], [21], [23], [24] than on a direct demonstration of expression [25]. To resolve this, we developed an intracellular (i.c.) flow cytometric assay to measure and compare the relative levels of Lck and Fyn in alphabeta and gammadelta lineage cells from wild-type (WT) mice. Using Lck-/- and Fyn-/- cells as negative staining controls, we found that both Lck and Fyn are expressed in DN gammadelta thymocytes and peripheral DN gammadelta T cells (Fig. 1A). On average, DN gammadelta thymocytes expressed Lck and Fyn at higher levels than DP and mature CD4+ thymocytes, whereas peripheral DN gammadelta T cells expressed Lck at levels comparable to those in CD4+ T cells and Fyn at levels lower than those in CD4+ T cells (Fig. 1A). When Lck and Fyn expression levels were compared between thymic and peripheral gammadelta T cells, we found that both SFKs are expressed at significantly higher levels in DN gammadelta thymocytes than in DN gammadelta T cells (Fig. 1B-C). This finding suggested that the expression levels of both Lck and Fyn are down-regulated once gammadelta T cells emigrate from the thymus to the secondary lymphoid organs. This same phenomenon was also observed for alphabeta lineage cells; however, the degree of reduction in Lck and Fyn expression levels between the thymus and LN was greater for gammadelta lineage cells than alphabeta lineage cells (Fig. 1C and data not shown). Taken together, these data indicated that immature and mature gammadelta lineage cells express Lck and Fyn and that the expression of these SFKs is dynamic during gammadelta T cell development and maturation.

Because DN gammadelta thymocytes expressed higher levels of Lck and Fyn than mature DN gammadelta T cells, we sought to determine whether high levels of Lck or Fyn were required for gammadelta lineage commitment and/or development in the thymus. To investigate this, we reduced the expression levels of Lck or Fyn during T cell development by using Lck+/- and Fyn+/- mice. To verify that protein expression was reduced in the heterozygous mice, we compared the relative expression levels of Lck and Fyn in immature and mature CD4+ lineage cells from Lck+/- and Fyn+/- mice with those from WT mice. As expected, we observed a 50% reduction in Lck expression levels in Lck+/- mice and a 50% reduction in Fyn expression levels in Fyn+/- mice (Fig. 2A). In addition, there was no compensatory increase in the expression of one SFK when expression of the other SFK was reduced (Fig. 2A).

Because gammadelta T cell development appeared not to be affected in Lck+/- and Fyn+/- mice, we examined the levels of Lck and Fyn in gammadelta lineage cells of the heterozygous mice to determine whether they were reduced by 50% as they were in alphabeta lineage cells (Fig. 2A and C). Another reason why we may not have observed any defects in gammadelta T cell development in Lck+/- and Fyn+/- mice is because thymocyte development and selection are able to compensate for alterations in gammadeltaTCR signal transduction, which may result from reductions in Lck or Fyn expression. To address this, we mated a gammadeltaTCR transgene onto Lck+/- and Fyn+/- genetic backgrounds to determine whether fixing the specificity of the gammadeltaTCR revealed defects in alphabeta/gammadelta lineage choice and/or gammadelta T cell development. For these experiments, we used the Vgamma6/Jgamma1/Cgamma1 and Vdelta1/Ddelta1/Jdelta2/Cdelta transgenic (gammadeltaTCR Tg) mouse [28], which we have previously used to study alphabeta/gammadelta lineage choice, gammadelta T cell development and gammadeltaTCR signal transduction [29]-[31]. It is important to note that, although Vgamma6/Vdelta1+ gammadelta T cells are only generated in the fetal thymus of a WT (non-gammadeltaTCR Tg) mouse [28], the Vgamma6/Vdelta1+ gammadelta T cells generated in the gammadeltaTCR Tg mouse represent adult gammadelta T cells, as they express the panel of gammadelta-biased genes [31] typical of adult but not fetal gammadelta T cell populations [32]. Moreover, one of the advantages of using this gammadeltaTCR Tg mouse model to study alphabeta/gammadelta lineage choice is that the rearranged TCRgamma and -delta chains are expressed early during T cell development prior to TCRbeta expression and, as a consequence, the alphabeta/gammadelta lineage decision is mediated exclusively by the gammadeltaTCR [29].

By fixing the specificity of the gammadeltaTCR, we observed significant effects on the alphabeta/gammadelta lineage fate decision when the levels of Lck but not Fyn were reduced. In WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice, equivalent numbers of DN gammadeltaTCR+ thymocytes (gammadelta lineage) and DP thymocytes (alphabeta lineage cells) are generated (Fig. 3A-B). Reducing Lck expression resulted in a striking 4-fold increase in thymus cell number compared to WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice, most likely due to the significant increase in the percentage of DP thymocytes (Fig. 3A). Consequently, the number of alphabeta lineage cells in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice was significantly higher than that in WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice (Fig. 3B). These findings are consistent with those of a previous study, in which gammadeltaTCR-dependent generation of DP thymocytes but not of DN gammadeltaTCR+ thymocytes was observed in the absence of Lck expression [33]. In contrast, reducing Fyn levels had no effect on total thymus cell number. Nonetheless, we did observe a decrease in both the percentage and number of DP thymocytes in Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice, although these differences were not statistically significant from the percentage and number of DP thymocytes in WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice (Fig. 3A-B). Interestingly, despite the changes in the numbers of DP thymocytes in the heterozygous mice, the numbers of DN gammadeltaTCR+ thymocytes, or gammadelta lineage cells, in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice were comparable to their number in WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice (Fig. 3A-B).

It has previously been shown that genetic manipulation of gammadeltaTCR signal strength affects alphabeta/gammadelta lineage choice in a consistent manner. Namely, when the gammadeltaTCR signaling response is strengthened, gammadelta lineage fate is favored and, conversely, when the gammadeltaTCR signaling response is weakened, alphabeta lineage fate is favored [29], [33]. Our finding that the number of alphabeta lineage cells was significantly increased in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice compared to WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice suggested that reducing Lck levels weakened gammadeltaTCR signal strength. Surprisingly, this increase in the number of DP thymocytes was not accompanied by a corresponding decrease in the number of DN gammadelta thymocytes in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg (Fig. 3B). We reasoned that the number of DN gammadelta thymocytes in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice may not reflect the number of thymocytes that adopted the gammadelta fate but instead reflected an expansion of the thymocytes that already adopted the gammadelta fate. To investigate this, we compared the proliferative status of DN gammadelta thymocytes in WT gammadeltaTCR Tg, Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg, and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice by measuring their expression of the Ki-67 Ag, which is a marker of actively cycling cells [34], [35]. We found that the frequency of Ki67+ DN gammadelta thymocytes in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice was significantly higher than the frequency of Ki67+ DN gammadelta thymocytes in WT gammadeltaTCR Tg and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice (Table 1). These data suggested that the number of DN gammadelta thymocytes in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice does not reflect the number of thymocytes that adopted the gammadelta fate.

Next, we compared the expression levels of the gammadeltaTCR and CD5 on the surface of DN gammadelta thymocytes from WT gammadeltaTCR Tg, Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg, and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice to gauge the effects of reducing Lck or Fyn levels on the phenotype of the cells choosing the gammadelta lineage. To investigate the effects of reducing the levels of Lck or Fyn on the maturation of gammadelta T cells in the thymus and their subsequent ability to migrate to the periphery, we enumerated DN gammadelta T cells in the LNs of WT gammadeltaTCR Tg, Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg, and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice. Compared to WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice, we observed a significant decrease in the number of DN gammadelta T cells in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice but not in the number of DN gammadelta T cells in Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice (Fig. 3C). Consistent with the lower numbers of peripheral gammadelta T cells in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice was the finding that there were fewer DN gammadelta T cells in these mice that expressed CD24, a marker of recent thymic emigrant gammadelta T cells [36], and that there were more cells that expressed CD44, a marker of activated cells, memory cells, and/or cells undergoing homeostatic proliferation [36]-[38] (Fig. 4B). Moreover, we found that the gammadeltaTCR and CD5 surface levels that were noted among the DN gammadeltaTCR+ thymocytes from the three genotypes were maintained on their respective peripheral gammadelta T cells, with the exception that Lck+/- gammadelta T cells expressed significantly lower levels of CD5 than WT gammadelta T cells (Fig. 4C). Therefore, although DN gammadelta thymocytes were generated in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice in numbers comparable to WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice, these mice had reduced numbers of mature DN gammadelta T cells. These data indicated that a reduction in Lck levels but not Fyn levels affects the maturation and/or survival of thymic gammadelta T cells.

gammadelta T cell effector fate has been shown to segregate with expression of specific surface antigens, specifically CD122+ and/or CD27+ gammadelta T cells preferentially produce IFNgamma [39], [40], whereas IL-23R+ gammadelta T cells preferentially produce IL-17 [41]-[43]. Given these findings, we sought to determine whether reducing the levels of Lck or Fyn altered the ability of a gammadelta T cell to become an IL-17- and/or IFNgamma-producing effector cell. To accomplish this, we chose to use our gammadeltaTCR Tg mouse model as it generates, on the WT background, 30-fold more DN gammadelta T cells than non-gammadeltaTCR Tg mice [30]. This means that DN gammadelta T cells can be analyzed without the concern that purification by positive selection using an anti-TCRgammadelta mAb may crosslink the gammadeltaTCR and, in turn, pre-activate the gammadelta T cell. First, we determined whether there were any differences in the percentages of gammadelta T cells expressing CD122, CD27 or IL-23R among WT gammadeltaTCR Tg, Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg, and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice. We found that both Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice had similar percentages of CD27+ and CD27- gammadelta T cells as WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice, but the percentages of CD27+ cells co-expressing CD122+ in both Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice was reduced compared to WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice (Fig. 5A). As CD122 expression by gammadelta T cells is induced when the gammadeltaTCR interacts with its ligand in the thymus [39], these results suggested that the selection and/or survival of CD122+ gammadelta T cells is impaired when Lck or Fyn levels are reduced. To evaluate IL-23R expression among WT, Lck+/-, and Fyn+/- gammadelta T cells, we performed quantitative real-time RT-PCR analysis to detect transcription of IL12RB1 and IL23R, which encode the two subunits of the IL-23R [44]. No significant differences were noted in the relative amounts of IL12RB1 and IL23R transcripts among WT, Lck+/-, and Fyn+/- gammadelta T cells (Fig. 5B), indicating that the selection and/or survival of gammadelta T cells with the potential to produce IL-17 is not affected when Lck or Fyn levels are reduced.

Because Lck+/- and Fyn+/- gammadelta T cells do not efficiently produce IFNgamma, it was of interest to determine whether reducing Lck or Fyn levels also affected the ability of gammadelta T cells to produce other cytokines. To test this, we evaluated TNFalpha production by gammadelta T cells from Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice, since gammadelta T cells, including those that produce IFNgamma, have been shown to produce this cytokine [40]. As shown in Fig. 5D, we found that more gammadelta T cells from Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice than from WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice were producing TNFalpha. These data indicated that reducing Lck or Fyn levels does not impair the ability of gammadelta T cells to produce TNFalpha and suggested that the TCR signals required to activate the genetic program for IFNgamma production are different than those for TNFalpha production.

###begin title 38
Discussion
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 164 168 164 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Lowell1">[45]</xref>
Since SFKs have both positive and negative roles in receptor signaling, it has been postulated that these kinases function more like rheostats than on/off switches [45]. Our data support this idea, as changes in the cellular levels of Lck or Fyn at different stages in the life of a gammadelta lineage cell affected the strength of the gammadeltaTCR signaling response and, in turn, affected alphabeta/gammadelta lineage commitment, gammadelta T cell maturation and gammadelta T effector cell differentiation.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 210 214 195 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Petrie1">[26]</xref>
###xml 216 220 201 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Ciofani1">[27]</xref>
###xml 735 739 697 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Olszowy1">[46]</xref>
###xml 900 902 862 864 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hi</sup>
The expression levels of Lck and Fyn change during T cell development and maturation. Immature thymocytes (i.e., DN2 thymocytes), which have the potential to become either alphabeta or gammadelta lineage cells [26], [27], expressed relatively high levels of both Lck and Fyn. In thymic gammadelta lineage cells, these high levels of Lck and Fyn were maintained and, not until the gammadelta lineage cells were exported from the thymus, did their Lck and Fyn expression levels decrease. However, in immature alphabeta lineage cells, Lck and Fyn expression levels dramatically declined and, at the DP stage, their levels of Lck and Fyn were extremely low. The low SFK expression in DP thymocytes has also been reported by Olszowy et al. [46], who used quantitative Western blot analysis to measure Lck and Fyn protein levels in thymocyte subsets. Interestingly, Lck and Fyn levels were increased in TCRhi DP (data not shown) and SP thymocytes compared to DP thymocytes, suggesting that positive selection upregulated both Lck and Fyn expression. It is important to note that mature SP thymocytes, after leaving the thymus, downregulated Lck and Fyn expression levels, but not to the levels observed for DP thymocytes nor to the extent observed between thymic and peripheral gammadelta T cells.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 155 158 147 150 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 166 169 158 161 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 522 525 499 502 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 773 776 734 737 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 830 833 791 794 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1207 1211 1137 1141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Petrie1">[26]</xref>
###xml 1213 1217 1143 1147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Ciofani1">[27]</xref>
###xml 1319 1322 1237 1240 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1521 1524 1423 1426 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1585 1588 1487 1490 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 170 174 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 526 530 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 604 608 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 777 781 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1323 1327 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1525 1529 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To investigate the importance of the quantitative difference in Lck and Fyn expression levels between thymic and peripheral gammadelta T cells, we used Lck+/- and Fyn+/- mice to study the effect of reducing Lck or Fyn expression levels on gammadelta T cell development and function. We chose to reduce, as opposed to eliminate, Lck and Fyn expression levels to prevent any compensatory action that one SFK may exhibit in the absence of the other. Although Fyn levels were reduced by 50% in alphabeta lineage cells from Fyn+/- mice, they were only reduced by 40% in gammadelta lineage cells from the same mice. There are two possible explanations, which are not mutually exclusive, for why Fyn levels were not reduced to the expected 50% in gammadelta lineage cells from Fyn+/- mice. First, the relatively high levels of Fyn in Fyn+/- gammadelta thymocytes may be a result of selection, where only cells with high levels of Fyn survive and continue to mature. The second possibility is that the high Fyn levels in gammadelta lineage cells reflect high Fyn levels in a precursor population, such as DN2 thymocytes, which have the developmental potential to give rise to alphabeta and gammadelta lineage cells [26], [27]. Indeed, we found that Fyn expression was only reduced by approximately15% in DN2 thymocytes from Fyn+/- mice. Therefore, even though we cannot rule out selection of gammadelta thymocytes with high levels of Fyn, it is conceivable that the relatively high levels of Fyn in gammadelta thymocytes from Fyn+/- mice may be a direct result of the high levels of Fyn in Fyn+/- DN2 thymocytes.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 370 374 370 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Venkitaraman1">[47]</xref>
###xml 376 380 376 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Page2">[48]</xref>
###xml 505 509 505 509 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Peschon1">[49]</xref>
###xml 510 514 510 514 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Maraskovsky1">[53]</xref>
The high level of Fyn in thymic precursors highlights the importance of Fyn activity during an early stage of T cell development. As surface TCR complexes are not expressed at this stage, it is possible that Fyn is required for signaling through other receptors. One such receptor may be the IL-7 receptor (IL-7R), as Fyn has been shown to be recruited to this receptor [47], [48]. Given this association and that DN2 thymocytes require IL-7R expression and signaling for their survival and proliferation [49]-[53], it is possible that relatively high levels of Fyn are required for proper IL-7R signaling at this stage.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 404 408 351 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Hayes1">[29]</xref>
###xml 410 414 357 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Haks1">[33]</xref>
###xml 416 420 363 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Kreslavsky1">[54]</xref>
###xml 763 766 678 681 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 942 945 825 828 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1498 1501 1325 1328 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1695 1699 1491 1495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Hayes1">[29]</xref>
###xml 1923 1926 1696 1699 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 784 788 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 821 825 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 963 967 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1519 1523 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1944 1948 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The first stage in T cell development where we observed regulation of gammadeltaTCR signal strength by Lck and Fyn is during alphabeta/gammadelta lineage commitment. We and others have previously demonstrated that TCR signal strength influences the alphabeta/gammadelta lineage decision, with a strong signal favoring gammadelta lineage commitment and a weak signal favoring alphabeta lineage commitment [29], [33], [54]. By fixing the specificity of the gammadeltaTCR, we were able to detect changes in the gammadeltaTCR signal response that were not apparent with a polyclonal gammadeltaTCR repertoire. Reducing the expression of Lck weakened gammadeltaTCR signal strength and resulted in a striking increase in the percentage and number of DP thymocytes in Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice compared to WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice. Moreover, weakening of the gammadeltaTCR signal was confirmed by the finding that DN gammadelta thymocytes from Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice expressed higher levels of the gammadeltaTCR than WT DN gammadelta thymocytes. Together, these results indicated that relatively high levels of Lck are required to achieve the appropriate TCR signal response to support the gammadelta lineage choice. Conversely, reducing Fyn expression levels strengthened the gammadeltaTCR signaling response, as evidenced by the decrease, albeit not significant, in the number of DP thymocytes and the significant decrease in gammadeltaTCR surface expression on DN gammadelta thymocytes from Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice. In contrast to the results of our previous study, in which gammadelta T cell fate was favored over alphabeta T cell fate when gammadeltaTCR signal strength was augmented [29], reducing Fyn expression had modest effects on the generation of alphabeta and gammadelta lineage cells. This difference may be attributed to the fact that Fyn expression was reduced by 35% in gammadelta thymocytes from Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice (data not shown), suggesting that immature DN thymocytes expressing low levels of Fyn were unable to survive and develop into alphabeta or gammadelta lineage cells.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 481 482 441 442 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 499 500 451 452 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 531 534 483 486 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 604 607 548 551 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 790 791 718 719 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 808 809 728 729 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 552 556 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Our data also demonstrated that the alterations in gammadeltaTCR signal strength by reducing Lck or Fyn expression levels affected gammadelta T cell maturation in the thymus. When gammadeltaTCR signal strength was weakened by reducing Lck levels, there was a significant decrease in the number of DN gammadelta T cells in LNs despite normal numbers of gammadelta lineage cells present in the thymus. In support of a maturation defect, the frequency of recent thymic emigrants (CD24+ DN gammadeltaTCR+) was reduced in the LNs of Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice. Because of the reduction in thymic output, Lck+/- gammadelta T cells seemingly underwent homeostatic proliferation in the periphery, evidenced by the increased frequency of gammadelta T cells with an activated/memory phenotype (CD44+ DN gammadeltaTCR+). On the other hand, strengthening the gammadeltaTCR signal response by reducing Fyn levels had little effect on thymic maturation. These data not only demonstrate that signaling by the gammadeltaTCR is required following gammadelta lineage commitment for gammadelta thymocyte maturation, but also indicate that the cellular levels of Lck in gammadelta thymocytes regulate the gammadeltaTCR signaling response at this developmental stage.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 119 123 104 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Jensen1">[39]</xref>
###xml 125 129 110 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Schweighoffer1">[55]</xref>
###xml 438 442 395 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Jensen1">[39]</xref>
###xml 571 572 520 521 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 617 618 554 555 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 652 656 581 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Ribot1">[40]</xref>
###xml 793 794 714 715 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 799 800 720 721 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 870 871 783 784 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 876 877 789 790 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 960 961 865 866 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1034 1035 931 932 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1109 1112 990 993 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1138 1141 1011 1014 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1203 1204 1060 1061 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1267 1271 1116 1120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Sim1">[28]</xref>
###xml 1293 1294 1142 1143 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1714 1715 1515 1516 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 2231 2236 1984 1989 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IL23R</italic>
###xml 2241 2248 1994 2001 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IL12RB1</italic>
###xml 1159 1163 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1340 1344 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Unlike alphabeta thymocytes, gammadelta thymocytes do not need to encounter Ag to mature and emigrate to the periphery [39], [55]. However, a recent study demonstrates that Ag encounter in the thymus by gammadelta T cells controls their effector fate, with Ag-experienced gammadelta T cells expressing CD122 and preferentially producing IFNgamma and Ag-naive gammadelta T cells lacking CD122 expression and preferentially producing IL-17 [39]. In addition to CD122 expression, gammadelta T cell effector fate has been shown to segregate with CD27 expression, in that CD27+ gammadelta T cells produce IFNgamma and CD27- gammadelta T cells produce IL-17 [40]. We found that either weakening or augmenting the gammadeltaTCR signal response had an impact on the generation and/or survival of CD122+ CD27+ gammadelta T cells but not on the generation and/or survival of CD122- CD27+ gammadelta T cells. Unfortunately, we do not know whether the defect lies in CD122+ gammadelta T cell selection, survival or both, as very few (<0.5%) CD122+ gammadelta T cells are detected in the thymus of WT gammadeltaTCR Tg, Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg, and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice (data not shown). Since Vgamma6/Vdelta1+ gammadelta T cells are normally generated in the fetal thymus [28], the paucity of CD122+ gammadelta thymocytes in WT gammadeltaTCR Tg mice may be due to decreased or negligible expression of the ligand for the Vgamma6/Vdelta1 gammadeltaTCR in the adult thymus. Nonetheless, we propose that weakening the gammadeltaTCR signal results in the generation of fewer CD122+ gammadelta thymocytes following Ag encounter in the thymus. However, due to homeostatic proliferation in the periphery, the size of the CD122+ gammadelta T cell pool is consequently increased. When the gammadeltaTCR signal is augmented, on the other hand, Ag encounter in the thymus may lead to negative selection and, in turn, fewer gammadelta thymocytes and peripheral gammadelta T cells expressing CD122. Surprisingly, in regards to gammadelta T cells with the potential to produce IL-17, we found that neither weakening nor augmenting gammadeltaTCR signal strength had an impact on their generation and/or survival, based on the comparable expression of IL23R and IL12RB1 in gammadelta T cells from the three genotypes. These results indicate that gammadelta T cells destined to become IL-17 producers, unlike those destined to become IFNgamma producers, are not dependent on Lck- or Fyn-mediated signaling for their generation and/or survival, and suggest that the choice to become an IFNgamma effector versus an IL-17 effector is not a binary fate decision.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 322 323 294 295 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 355 356 319 320 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 541 544 469 472 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 570 573 490 493 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1018 1022 914 918 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Goldrath1">[38]</xref>
###xml 1158 1162 1042 1046 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008899-Jensen1">[39]</xref>
###xml 1414 1415 1290 1291 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1498 1499 1362 1363 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 591 595 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Consistent with their being multiple gammadelta effector fates is our finding that, following TCR stimulation, 10% of WT gammadelta T cells produce IFNgamma, 5% produce IL-17 and 30% produce TNFalpha [a third of these also produce IFNgamma, while none produce IL-17 (data not shown)]. Interestingly, the frequency of CD122+ gammadelta T cells but not CD27+ gammadelta T cells was found to be an indicator of IFNgamma production by gammadelta T cells, regardless of whether the stimulated gammadelta T cells were from WT gammadeltaTCR Tg, Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg, and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice. While our finding is consistent with the findings of the recent study demonstrating that CD122 expression correlates with IFNgamma production, it is not consistent with those of the other study demonstrating that CD27 expression correlates with IFNgamma production. An explanation for the difference in results may be how, in each study, gammadelta T cells were stimulated. In our study, as well as that of Jensen et al. [38], gammadelta T cells were stimulated by crosslinking the TCR with anti-CD3 or anti-TCRdelta mAbs. However, in the study of Ribot et al. [39], gammadelta T cells were first activated and expanded in the presence of anti-CD3 mAb and IL-2 for four days prior to re-stimulation with the phorbol ester, phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate, and ionomycin. These data, taken together, suggest that CD122+ gammadelta T cells rapidly produce IFNgamma following TCR activation, whereas CD27+ gammadelta T cells require multiple days post TCR activation to differentiate into IFNgamma-producing cells. Notably, both weakening and augmenting gammadeltaTCR signal strength affected the proficiency by which gammadelta T cells produced IFNgamma but not TNFalpha. This finding indicates that gammadeltaTCR signal strength regulates the quality of the cytokine effector response of stimulated gammadelta T cells. Moreover, having both weak and strong TCR signals leading to a similar outcome is not novel in T cell biology, as for example it well known that DP thymocytes that receive TCR signals that are too weak or too strong undergo apoptosis [reviewed in 56].
###end p 46
###begin p 47
In summary, we have demonstrated that the cellular levels of Lck or Fyn regulate the strength of the gammadeltaTCR signaling response. Specifically, we found that reducing Lck levels, thereby weakening gammadeltaTCR signal strength, had profound effects on alphabeta/gammadelta lineage commitment, gammadelta thymocyte maturation, and the generation of IFNgamma-producing effectors. In contrast, when Fyn levels were reduced, thus augmenting gammadeltaTCR signal strength, we observed defects in the generation of both IL-17- and IFNgamma-producing effectors. Thus, this study has revealed that a relatively strong gammadeltaTCR signaling response is required following gammadelta lineage commitment for gammadelta T cell maturation and gammadelta T effector cell differentiation.

###begin title 57
Flow Cytometry
###end title 57
###begin p 58
###xml 894 896 866 868 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2b</sub>
###xml 1009 1010 981 982 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 885 890 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Flow cytometric analysis for surface antigens was performed by pre-incubating cells with the anti-CD16/CD32 mAb for at least 10 min to block non-specific binding of immunoglobulins to Fc receptors followed by staining with fluorochrome-conjugated Abs against the various surface antigens. I.c. staining for Ki-67 was performed according to the manufacturer's instructions (BD Pharmingen). I.c. staining for Lck and Fyn in addition to IL-17A, TNFalpha and IFNgamma was performed by first fixing cells in a final concentration of 1.5% formaldehyde for 10 min at 37degreesC. Fixed cells were then stained for surface antigens, permeabilized with Perm/Wash Buffer (BD Pharmingen) for 20 min at 4degreesC, and then stained with mAbs against the appropriate proteins. The mAbs specific for IL-17A, TNFalpha and IFNgamma were directly conjugated to a fluorochrome, while FITC-conjugated anti-mouse IgG2b Ab was used as a secondary reagent to detect the anti-Lck mAb or the anti-Fyn mAb. For all experiments, 0.1-2x106 cells were collected on a LSR II using FACSDiva software (BD Immunocytometry Systems, San Jose, CA, USA) and analyzed using FlowJo software (Tree Star Inc., Ashland, OR, USA). Dead cells were excluded from analysis based on forward and side scatter profiles.
###end p 58

###begin p 60

DN gammadelta T cells were purified by negative selection from the LNs of WT gammadeltaTCR Tg, Lck+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg and Fyn+/- gammadeltaTCR Tg mice using the MACS magnetic bead separation system (Miltenyi Biotec, Auburn, CA, USA). Cells were washed, incubated with anti-FITC MACS beads for 15 min at 4degreesC, and then separated on an autoMACS cell separator, according to manufacturer's directions. The purity of the resulting DN gammadelta T cell populations were typically >/=99%.
