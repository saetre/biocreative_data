Expansion of a chromosomal repeat in Escherichia coli: roles of replication, repair, and recombination functions
###end article-title 0

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 42 59 42 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 363 367 363 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 42 58 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Previous studies of gene amplification in Escherichia coli have suggested that it occurs in two steps: duplication and expansion. Expansion is thought to result from homologous recombination between the repeated segments created by duplication. To explore the mechanism of expansion, a 7 kbp duplication in the chromosome containing a leaky mutant version of the lac operon was constructed, and its expansion into an amplified array was studied.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 20 24 20 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 81 85 81 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 247 252 247 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA </italic>
###xml 278 285 278 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recBCD </italic>
###xml 289 294 289 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ruvC </italic>
###xml 345 355 345 355 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ruvC recG </italic>
###xml 454 457 454 457 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dam</italic>
###xml 459 469 459 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">polA, rnhA</italic>
###xml 474 479 474 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">uvrD </italic>
###xml 618 622 618 622 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">red </italic>
###xml 679 686 679 686 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recBCD </italic>
###xml 717 720 717 720 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">red</italic>
###xml 784 789 784 789 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA </italic>
###xml 645 657 <span type="species:ncbi:10710">phage lambda</span>
Under selection for lac function, colonies bearing multiple copies of the mutant lac operon appeared at a constant rate of approximately 4 to 5 per million cells plated per day, on days two through seven after plating. Expansion was not seen in a recA strain; null mutations in recBCD and ruvC reduced the rate 100- and 10-fold, respectively; a ruvC recG double mutant reduced the rate 1000-fold. Expansion occurred at an increased rate in cells lacking dam, polA, rnhA, or uvrD functions. Null mutations of various other cellular recombination, repair, and stress response genes had little effect upon expansion. The red recombination genes of phage lambda could substitute for recBCD in mediating expansion. In the red-substituted cells, expansion was only partially dependent upon recA function.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
These observations are consistent with the idea that the expansion step of gene amplification is closely related, mechanistically, to interchromosomal homologous recombination events. They additionally provide support for recently described models of RecA-independent Red-mediated recombination at replication forks.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 36 53 36 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 220 224 220 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA</italic>
###xml 341 345 341 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA</italic>
###xml 446 447 446 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 448 449 448 449 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 36 52 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Expression of a chromosomal gene in Escherichia coli can be elevated by gene amplification. The mechanism of this amplification is thought to consist of two steps, duplication and expansion. Duplication is rare, largely recA-independent, and occurs between microhomologies in the chromosome as a replication accident. Expansion is frequent, recA-dependent, and thought to result from unequal crossing-over events between the duplicated segments [1-3].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 47 55 47 55 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 151 168 151 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#981;(lacIX13-lacZ)</italic>
###xml 187 189 185 187 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 248 252 246 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 260 261 258 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 267 269 265 267 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 285 289 283 287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 322 338 320 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#981;(lacI33-lacZ)</italic>
###xml 458 459 454 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 460 461 456 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 532 533 528 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 534 535 530 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 552 556 548 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 683 687 679 683 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 736 737 732 733 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 857 862 853 858 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ampC </italic>
###xml 863 864 859 860 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 905 912 901 908 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recBCD </italic>
###xml 916 922 912 918 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ruvABC</italic>
###xml 935 939 931 935 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA</italic>
###xml 1000 1002 996 998 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 47 54 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Recent investigations of gene amplification in E. coli have focused on amplification of plasmid-borne genes. A phenotypically leaky F'-borne mutation, phi(lacIX13-lacZ), gives rise to Lac+ revertants bearing amplified arrays of 40-80 copies of the lac region [4]. Lac+ revertants of F'lac bearing the +1 frameshift allele phi(lacI33-lacZ), extensively employed in studies of adaptive mutation, consist mainly of one-base deletions in runs of iterated bases [5,6], but clones bearing amplified arrays appear at a lower rate as well [7,8]. Properties of lac amplification have generally supported the duplication-expansion model. (i) An engineered duplication of the frameshift mutant lac locus amplifies at a greatly elevated frequency [9], as predicted by the idea that duplication is the rate-limiting step (and as had been seen in the case of chromosomal ampC [2]). (ii) Amplification is dependent upon recBCD and ruvABC, as well as recA, indicating an important role for homologous recombination [10].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 157 162 157 162 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tetA </italic>
###xml 403 408 403 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA </italic>
###xml 471 475 471 475 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dnaQ</italic>
###xml 477 481 477 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dnaE</italic>
###xml 483 487 483 487 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dnaB</italic>
###xml 492 497 492 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dnaN </italic>
###xml 498 500 498 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
Expansion of a pre-existing repeat has also been studied primarily on plasmids. In one study, a pBR322 derivative was constructed with two directly repeated tetA genes, each bearing an inactivating mutation, but arranged in such a way that a single unequal crossover would generate an array of three copies, one of which was a functioning gene. In this system, expansion was reduced only five-fold in a recA mutant; expansion was elevated in strains bearing mutations in dnaQ, dnaE, dnaB, or dnaN [11].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 101 118 101 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#981;(lacI33-lacZ) </italic>
###xml 136 138 134 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 182 187 180 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">polA </italic>
###xml 231 235 229 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">xonA</italic>
###xml 381 386 379 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">polA </italic>
###xml 400 405 398 403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">xonA </italic>
Expansion of a pre-existing duplication was compared with amplification of a single copy of F'-borne phi(lacI33-lacZ) in another study [10]. Expansion was found to be increased in a polA mutant, and unaffected by overexpression of xonA, while amplification from a single copy was inhibited by both of these conditions. It was concluded that the amplification defects caused by the polA mutant and by xonA overproduction were in duplication, not expansion.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 133 150 133 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#981;(lacI33-lacZ) </italic>
###xml 172 173 170 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
This study was undertaken to characterize expansion of a repeated sequence in the bacterial chromosome. A duplication of chromosomal phi(lacI33-lacZ) was constructed (Fig. 1). As expected, it expands at a high rate under selection for function. The effects of mutations in various recombination, replication, DNA repair, and stress response genes on expansion of the duplication were tested. The findings support the idea that expansion occurs via homologous recombination, and suggest as well that many of the recombination events leading to expansion take place at replication forks.
###end p 13

###begin title 15
Results
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 3 11 3 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 65 82 65 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#981;(lacI33-lacZ) </italic>
###xml 330 331 328 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 737 753 735 749 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#981;(lacI33-lacZ)</italic>
###xml 886 888 882 884 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 3 10 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
An E. coli strain bearing a chromosomal duplication of the leaky phi(lacI33-lacZ) allele, when plated on minimal medium containing lactose as the only available carbon source, gives rise to approximately 1000-fold more colonies, over the course of a week, than an otherwise isogenic strain bearing a single copy. As shown in Fig. 2, the colonies start appearing two days after plating, and accumulate at an average rate of approximately 4-5 per million viable duplication-bearing cells plated per day, two to seven days after plating. Daily colony counts vary widely between independent cultures, as well as day-to-day on the same plate. This variation is considerably greater than that observed in experiments with single copy F'-borne phi(lacI33-lacZ), in which the appearance of colonies after day two fits a Poisson distribution, implying that the mutations occurred after plating [12]. In contrast, the variation in colony counts seen in experiments with the chromosomal duplication strain indicate that most of the variation between cultures exists prior to plating (unpublished data). This observation is consistent with the hypothesis that each culture contains copy number variants which arise during growth, and that the probability of colony formation varies with copy number at the time of plating. Despite this variability, if 12 or more independent cultures are plated, and daily colony counts are averaged, the rate of accumulation is seen to be nearly constant, as reflected in the close fit of the data points to a straight line.
###end p 16

###begin p 18
###xml 7 9 7 9 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>

###xml 373 374 373 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 510 511 510 511 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 532 534 532 534 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 724 725 724 725 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 835 837 835 837 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 862 866 862 866 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 1132 1134 1126 1128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 1163 1167 1157 1161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 1189 1191 1183 1185 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 1504 1508 1495 1499 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 1545 1549 1536 1540 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 1676 1679 1667 1670 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac</italic>
###xml 1849 1851 1837 1839 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 2020 2021 2008 2009 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 2066 2083 2054 2069 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#981;(lacI33-lacZ) </italic>
###xml 2115 2119 2101 2105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
The Lac+ colonies appearing in these experiments can arise either by expansion or by mutation. A strain bearing even a single chromosomal copy of the un-frameshifted lacI-lacZ fusion grows well on lactose minimal medium. However, most or all of the excess Lac+ colonies produced by the duplication-bearing strain contain expanded arrays of the structure diagrammed in Fig. 1. The amplified sequences in these clones are readily visualized as specific bands in restriction enzyme digests of total cellular DNA [4]. In tests of 28 Lac+ revertants from 22 cultures bearing the duplication, including four colonies which appeared on day two, all had expanded; none of 10 single-copy revertants contained amplified arrays (Table 1). Quantitation of total DNA, and DNA in the amplified bands, as described in the methods section, from 10 Lac+ clones, indicated a mean lac copy number of 72, with a standard deviation of 17. Expansion of this magnitude would be expected to result in beta-galactosidase production comparable to a single-copy un-frameshifted gene, as the frameshift mutation reduces beta-galactosidase production 100-fold [12]. The modest variability of lac copy number in the Lac+ clones presumably reflects a sort of optimum or equilibrium, in which the benefit of more beta-galactosidase is balanced by the cost of extra DNA in the chromosome (70 copies of the repeat make the chromosome roughly 10% larger). Cultures of the expanded-array variants contain unknown numbers of point-revertant lac genes. Under continued selection for lac function, it is possible that the population would eventually be taken over by revertants which, bearing a single good copy of lac, have the benefit of sufficient beta-galactosidase without the cost of more DNA. However, it is likely that such a changeover would take many generations because the Lac+ revertant has, if anything, only a small selective advantage - it does not have a noticeably faster growth rate on lactose minimal medium, for example. Hastings et al. [8] tested this idea, and found that amplified phi(lacI33-lacZ) clones kept under selection for lac function do not form revertants readily.

###xml 652 654 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 688 689 686 687 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 728 745 726 741 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#981;(lacI33-lacZ) </italic>
###xml 1027 1031 1023 1027 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 1114 1116 1110 1112 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
The clones appearing as Lac+ colonies acquire their ability to grow on lactose while under selection. Colonies re-streaked on lactose minimal plates form colonies visible to the unaided eye in 24 hours or less, regardless of whether they were picked on day 2 or day 7. Reversion and amplification of F'-borne phi(lacI33-lacZ) are adaptive, in that they occur only in the presence of lactose, not when the bacteria are simply starved [8,13]. The leakiness of the mutant allele is critical for adaptive mutation: residual lactose metabolism is enough to power the replication/recombination/repair processes involved, though not enough for cell division [12]. The experiment graphed in Fig. 3 shows that expansion of a chromosomal phi(lacI33-lacZ) is similarly adaptive. Cultures were plated on minimal medium containing no available carbon source. Lactose was added after two or four days by injection under the agar slab. There was no sudden burst of colonies appearing two or three days later, as would be expected if expanded lac arrays had accumulated during starvation. Rather, the kinetics of appearance of Lac+ colonies resembled that seen in the cells initially plated on lactose, with a delay of either two or four days, and at declining rates, suggesting that the starving cells gradually lost their potential to expand.

###xml 716 718 714 716 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 719 721 717 719 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 843 845 841 843 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 875 892 873 888 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#981;(lacI33-lacZ) </italic>
Strains combining the duplication with mutations in DNA transaction genes were tested for Lac+ colony formation (Fig. 4). A null mutation in recA reduced the rate nearly 1000-fold, nearly down to that of a strain bearing a single copy of phi(lacI33-lacZ) (labeled "sc" near the bottom of Fig. 4). The recA mutation had no significant effect in the single copy background. The strong recA dependence of expansion in this experiment contrasts with the weak recA dependence seen in a previous study of expansion of a plasmid-borne duplication [11], but is not surprising, for two reasons: (i) The duplicated segment in this study was much larger, and recA dependence tends to increase with increasing homology lengths [14,15]. (ii) The assay employed in the previous study required only a single recombination event, whereas becoming strongly Lac+ by expansion of a chromosomal phi(lacI33-lacZ) duplication probably involves more than one recombination event.
###end p 24

###begin p 26

Null mutations in recBCD and ruvC reduced Lac+ colony formation 100-fold and 10-fold, respectively. Other mutations eliminating single recombination functions, recF, recG, recN, recQ, recR, and ruvAB, had little overall effect. A ruvC recG double mutant was also tested. Like the recA mutation, and as in other homologous recombination events [16], it generated Lac+ colonies approximately 1000-fold less efficiently than wild type.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 20 33 20 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli yfgL </italic>
###xml 113 115 113 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 135 136 135 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 149 154 149 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfgL </italic>
###xml 444 449 444 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfgL </italic>
###xml 463 465 463 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 20 27 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
A disruption of the E. coli yfgL gene was reported to confer a strong recombination/repair deficiency phenotype [17]. As shown in Fig. 4, however, a yfgL null mutation constructed for this study has little or no effect on expansion. It also confers no UV-sensitivity or transductional recombination phenotype, in either an MG1655 or an AB1157 strain background (not shown); others have found no recombination/repair phenotype associated with a yfgL null as well [18].
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 48 52 48 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA</italic>
###xml 54 60 54 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recBCD</italic>
###xml 62 66 62 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ruvC</italic>
###xml 72 82 72 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ruvC recG </italic>
###xml 96 98 96 98 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 236 238 236 238 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 302 304 302 304 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 332 334 332 334 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 436 440 436 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA</italic>
###xml 442 448 442 448 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recBCD</italic>
###xml 454 458 454 458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ruvC</italic>
###xml 474 476 474 476 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 620 630 620 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ruvC recG </italic>
###xml 669 671 669 671 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 769 779 769 779 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ruvC recG </italic>
###xml 1048 1050 1048 1050 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 1279 1280 1279 1280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
To test the hypothesis that the deficiencies of recA, recBCD, ruvC, and ruvC recG mutants in Lac+ colony formation are due to their inability to expand the duplication, two alternative explanations were considered and ruled out. (i) Lac+ revertants of these mutants could grow much more slowly than Lac+ revertants of wild type. Lac+ colonies were restreaked on minimal lactose plates on the days they arose. In the cases of wild type, recA, recBCD, and ruvC, all of the Lac+ clones formed colonies visible to the unaided eye by 24 hours after restreaking, independent of the day on which they arose. In the case of the ruvC recG double mutant, none of the 8 tested Lac+ revertants formed visible colonies by 24 hours, but all did so by 48 hours. However, counting the ruvC recG colonies after 8 days instead of 7 only increased the median from 0.029 to 0.056 colonies per million viable cells plated (data not shown). Thus, slow growth of revertants can account for only a small part of the deficiencies of the recombination mutants in forming Lac+ colonies. (ii) The mutants could be proficient at expansion, but deficient at survival on the selection plates. Survival of the mutants on lactose minimal medium was tested as described in the methods section. The results (Table 1) indicate that none of the mutants has a substantial survival defect relative to wild type.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 7 9 7 9 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 114 115 114 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>

###xml 288 293 288 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recG </italic>
###xml 315 325 315 325 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ruvC recG </italic>
The Lac+ revertants of the deficient mutants consist of varying populations of expanded and mutated clones (Table 1). In the case of recA, none of 10 tested clones had an expanded lac array. The frequency of expanded arrays among recBCD revertants was 9 out of 10; ruvC was 10 out of 10, recG was 18 out of 18, and ruvC recG was 6 out of 14.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 7 9 7 9 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 34 39 34 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recG </italic>
###xml 89 90 89 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 423 428 423 428 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recG </italic>
###xml 454 455 454 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 802 807 802 807 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recG </italic>
###xml 816 824 816 824 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 852 854 852 854 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 816 823 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The Lac+ reversion phenotype of a recG null mutant is more complex than the data in Fig. 4 suggest. The mutant strain's rate of colony formation tends to increase sharply late in the experiment, with the new colonies tending to appear as satellites of older colonies (not shown). Factors influencing the timing and extent of this satellite-based population explosion include plating density, but are otherwise unknown. The recG mutant data shown in Fig. 4 are from selected experiments, in which the plating density was low, and satellitism was not as strongly evident as in other experiments. Satellitism of this sort suggests that something produced by the older colonies on the plate stimulates recombination, perhaps via a genotoxic effect. It is consistent with the finding that overexpression of recG protects E. coli against weak organic acids [19].
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 91 96 91 96 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA </italic>
###xml 124 130 124 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lexA3 </italic>
###xml 239 241 239 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 263 265 263 265 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 328 340 328 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lexA71::Tn5 </italic>
###xml 355 360 355 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sulA </italic>
###xml 447 456 447 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recAo281 </italic>
The effects on expansion of varying RecA activity were tested by plating mutants affecting recA expression. The uninducible lexA3 mutation has been reported to reduce the frequency of a number of different homologous recombination events [20]; it also reduced Lac+ colony formation approximately five-fold. The SOS-constitutive lexA71::Tn5 mutation (in a sulA null background, to suppress its lethality) had no significant effect; neither did the recAo281 operator constitutive allele.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 85 90 85 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">polA </italic>
###xml 202 206 202 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">polB</italic>
###xml 208 212 208 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dinB</italic>
###xml 218 223 218 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">umuDC</italic>
###xml 291 296 291 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">priA </italic>
###xml 359 364 359 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rnhA </italic>
A number of replication genes were tested for roles in expansion. A null mutation in polA increased the rate of expansion 5-fold; null mutations in the other non-essential DNA polymerase-encoding genes polB, dinB, and umuDC, had little or no effect. Loss of the replication restart function priA caused a small decrease in expansion efficiency, while loss of rnhA caused a nearly 7-fold increase.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 17 21 17 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dam </italic>
###xml 70 72 70 72 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 225 229 225 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dam </italic>
###xml 238 240 238 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 241 243 241 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 263 264 263 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 268 277 268 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dam mutH </italic>
###xml 357 361 357 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dam </italic>
###xml 380 385 380 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutH </italic>
A strain lacking dam function exhibited a 35-fold elevated rate of Lac+ colony formation. Apparently, part of this elevated rate is due to the double-strand breaks which occur as the result of mis-directed mismatch repair in dam mutants [21,22]. As shown in Fig. 4, a dam mutH double mutant exhibited an intermediate rate between those of wild type and the dam single mutant. The mutH null allele by itself had little or no effect.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 75 80 75 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">uvrD </italic>
###xml 102 104 102 104 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 147 151 147 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutM</italic>
###xml 153 157 153 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutY</italic>
###xml 161 171 161 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutM mutY </italic>
###xml 186 190 186 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada </italic>
Other DNA repair functions were tested for effects on expansion as well. A uvrD null mutant formed Lac+ colonies at a 10-fold elevated rate, while mutM, mutY, a mutM mutY double, and an ada null mutation had no significant effects.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 152 154 152 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 199 204 199 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoS </italic>
###xml 208 212 208 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">relA</italic>
###xml 231 232 231 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 280 282 280 282 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
The question of whether expansion of a chromosomal repeat occurs as part of a stress response, like amplification starting from a single episomal copy [23], was explored by testing null mutations in rpoS and relA. As shown in Fig. 4, these mutations had little or no effect on Lac+ colony formation.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 121 125 116 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">red </italic>
###xml 143 162 138 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recC-ptr-recB-recD </italic>
###xml 182 190 177 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 228 229 223 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 290 292 285 287 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 411 414 406 409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">red</italic>
###xml 441 446 436 441 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA </italic>
###xml 471 473 466 468 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 514 519 509 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA </italic>
###xml 575 579 570 574 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 593 594 588 589 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 715 720 710 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recF </italic>
###xml 762 767 757 762 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recG </italic>
###xml 852 854 847 849 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 861 866 856 861 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rnhA </italic>
###xml 909 912 904 907 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">red</italic>
###xml 54 66 <span type="species:ncbi:10710">phage lambda</span>
###xml 182 189 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Expansion mediated by the Red recombination system of phage lambda was studied in a series of strains in which the phage red genes replace the recC-ptr-recB-recD gene cluster in the E. coli chromosome (designated "red+" in Fig. 4). Replacing RecBCD with Red has no effect on the rate of Lac+ colony formation, but it changes the extent to which expansion is dependent upon other recombination functions. In the red-substituted background, a recA null mutation reduces Lac+ colony formation only 35-fold. Among the recA revertants, 8 of 10 that were tested contained expanded lac arrays (Table 1), showing that Red, unlike RecBCD, can promote expansion in the absence of RecA. Red-mediated expansion is reduced by a recF null mutation, and elevated slightly by a recG null; these mutant effects are seen in Red-mediated gene replacement events as well [24]. The rnhA null mutation has a stronger effect in the red-substituted background than in wild type, elevating the rate of expansion 45-fold.

###begin title 37
Discussion and conclusion
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 56 64 56 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 179 184 179 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recA </italic>
###xml 188 194 188 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recBCD</italic>
###xml 320 327 320 327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">recBCD </italic>
###xml 337 339 337 339 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 378 395 378 393 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#981;(lacI33-lacZ) </italic>
###xml 477 481 475 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">polA</italic>
###xml 483 486 481 484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dam</italic>
###xml 488 492 486 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">uvrD</italic>
###xml 498 503 496 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rnhA </italic>
###xml 504 506 502 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 507 509 505 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 510 512 508 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 56 63 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The genetic requirements of homologous recombination in E. coli vary with the particular event examined, but some features of chromosomal events are nearly general: dependence on recA and recBCD, and mild or no dependence upon a variety of other recombination functions whose roles are revealed mainly in the absence of recBCD function [14]. Expansion by duplicated chromosomal phi(lacI33-lacZ) fits this general pattern. Similarly, mutations with known hyper-rec phenotypes - polA, dam, uvrD, and rnhA [20,25,26] - also cause an elevated rate of expansion. These observations support the idea that expansion is best understood as a homologous recombination event or series of events.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 226 228 221 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 229 231 224 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 273 276 268 271 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">red</italic>
###xml 422 424 417 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 425 427 420 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 32 44 <span type="species:ncbi:10710">phage lambda</span>
The Red recombination system of phage lambda promotes RecA-independent recombination between chromosomes if at least one of the chromosomes is replicating, and RecA-dependent recombination between non-replicating chromosomes [27-30]. The RecA-independent expansion seen in red-substituted bacteria suggests that at least some of the Red-mediated recombination events involved in expansion take place at replication forks [30,31].
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 145 150 145 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rnhA </italic>
###xml 154 158 154 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dam </italic>
###xml 238 243 238 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rnhA </italic>
###xml 329 331 329 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 339 343 339 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dam </italic>
###xml 377 382 377 382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">oriC </italic>
###xml 404 406 404 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 702 704 702 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 705 707 705 707 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 765 769 765 769 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dam </italic>
###xml 1007 1016 1007 1016 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dam mutH </italic>
The involvement of replication in recombination events leading to expansion is additionally suggested by the increased rates of expansion of the rnhA and dam mutants. Replication in both these mutants escapes cell cycle regulation. In an rnhA mutant, unsynchronized DNA replication initiates at multiple sites in the chromosome [32]. In a dam mutant, initiation is confined to oriC but is not regulated [33]. Elevated recombination frequencies in both of these mutants may be due, at least in part, to an increased occurrence of double strand breaks. Both exhibit greatly reduced viability in the absence of RecBCD function, possibly because RecBCD is needed to repair the excess double strand breaks [34,35]. Eliminating the mismatch repair endonuclease MutH in a dam mutant prevents the double strand breaks which result from mis-directed mismatch repair, but does not bring expansion down to the wild type level; unregulated replication itself is a possible cause of the residual excess expansion in the dam mutH double mutant. The mechanism by which improperly regulated replication forks provoke more recombination events than normally regulated replication forks is unknown, but there are a number of possible explanations. They might be more prone to breaking down or to running into each other, or just more numerous in the cell.

Strains to be tested for reversion to Lac+ were grown to saturation in M9 0.1% glycerol minimal medium at 37degreesC, and plated on M9 0.1% lactose plates at 37degreesC. M9 minimal media, supplemented with thiamine at 5 mug/ml, were as described [7]. Strains with poor viability in rich media (ruvC recG, polA, priA) were titered on M9 glucose plates; retention of the duplication in these cases was assessed by testing the chloramphenicol resistance of individual colonies from the titer plates. Lactose minimal plates were inoculated with 1-2 x 109 cells, either of the strain to be tested by itself, or, in most cases, of the strain to be tested plus a non-reverting, lac deletion-bearing scavenger strain [13]. ###begin title 54
Expansion test
###end title 54
###begin p 55
###xml 840 842 828 830 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 1069 1081 <span type="species:ncbi:10710">phage lambda</span>
Revertant colonies appearing on the M9 lactose plates were streaked on M9 lactose plates, which were incubated at 37degreesC until visible colonies formed. Heavy inocula constituting the bulk of the growth from the streaks were then scraped from the plate and grown to saturation in 5 ml M9 0.1% lactose minimal medium at 37degreesC. (Some of the revertant colonies tested, in the wild type background only, were inoculated directly from the selection plates into liquid lactose minimal medium; but all revertants in the wild type background tested positive for expanded arrays, regardless of the variation in culture methods). DNA was extracted by the use of a procedure involving freezing and thawing, lysozyme digestion, extraction with a phenol/chloroform/isoamyl alcohol mixture, extraction with ether, and precipitation with ethanol [30]. Portions were digested with EcoR1 and RNase, and subjected to electrophoresis in an agarose gel, followed by ethidium bromide staining. For quantitation of the repeat-specific bands, standards consisting of HindIII-digested phage lambda DNA of known concentration were included in the gel, in separate lanes. Total DNA in the sample was quantitated by spotting RNase-treated samples on an agarose slab containing ethidium bromide at 1 mug/ml, along with standards of known concentration. Band and spot intensities were measured by the use of digital photography and Kodak 1D software.

###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 132 136 132 136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lac </italic>
###xml 211 213 211 213 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
