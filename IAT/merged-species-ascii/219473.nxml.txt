Synergistic repression of anaerobic genes by Mot3 and Rox1 in Saccharomyces cerevisiae
###end article-title 0

###begin p 3
###xml 224 227 224 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN</italic>
###xml 228 231 228 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TIR</italic>
###xml 232 235 232 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ERG</italic>
###xml 637 641 637 641 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 646 650 646 650 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 800 804 800 804 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
###xml 813 817 809 813 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mot3</italic>
###xml 1253 1257 1245 1249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
Two groups of anaerobic genes (genes induced in anaerobic cells and repressed in aerobic cells) are negatively regulated by heme, a metabolite present only in aerobic cells. Members of both groups, the hypoxic genes and the DAN/TIR/ERG genes, are jointly repressed under aerobic conditions by two factors. One is Rox1, an HMG protein, and the second, originally designated Rox7, is shown here to be Mot3, a global C2H2 zinc finger regulator. Repression of anaerobic genes results from co-induction of Mot3 and Rox1 in aerobic cells. Repressor synthesis is triggered by heme, which de-represses a mechanism controlling expression of both MOT3 and ROX1 in anaerobic cells; it includes Hap1, Tup1, Ssn6 and a fourth unidentified factor. The constitutive expression of various anaerobic genes in aerobic rox1Delta or mot3Delta cells directly implies that neither factor can repress by itself at endogenous levels and that stringent aerobic repression results from the concerted action of both. Mot3 and Rox1 are not essential components of a single complex, since each can repress independently in the absence of the other, when artificially induced at high levels. Moreover, the two repression mechanisms appear to be distinct: as shown here repression of ANB1 by Rox1 alone requires Tup1-Ssn6, whereas repression by Mot3 does not. Though artificially high levels of either factor can repress well, the absolute efficiency observed in normal cells when both are present-at much lower levels-demonstrates a novel inhibitory synergy. Evidently, expression levels for the two mutually dependent repressors are calibrated to permit a range of variation in basal aerobic expression at different promoters with differing operator site combinations.

###begin title 4
INTRODUCTION
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 271 272 271 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c1">1</xref>
###xml 273 274 273 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c2">2</xref>
###xml 407 408 407 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c3">3</xref>
###xml 409 410 409 410 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c4">4</xref>
###xml 460 461 460 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c3">3</xref>
###xml 602 603 602 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c5">5</xref>
###xml 628 629 628 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c6">6</xref>
###xml 789 790 789 790 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c1">1</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
Yeast adapt to the absence of oxygen by expressing several groups of genes. Two of the anaerobic regulons are negatively regulated by heme, which is produced only in the presence of oxygen. The members of one heme-repressed gene group are referred to as 'hypoxic genes' (1,2) and encode intracellular proteins dedicated to more efficient utilization of oxygen. They are all regulated by the Rox1 repressor (3,4), which is induced during aerobic growth by heme(3), resulting in 'aerobic repression' of the regulon. In the absence of oxygen, expression of Rox1 ceases, partly through inhibition by Hap1 (5), and Rox1 is degraded (6), de-repressing transcription. The widely varying efficiency of Rox1 repression of various hypoxic genes depends on the number and fidelity of operator sites (1).

###xml 964 966 964 966 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c15">15</xref>
###xml 1016 1020 1016 1020 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 1022 1024 1022 1024 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c17">17</xref>
###xml 1135 1137 1135 1137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c12">12</xref>
###xml 1208 1210 1208 1210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c18">18</xref>
The second group of heme-repressed genes are the DAN/TIR/ERG genes, which are transcriptionally activated through a common response element (7) by Mox4/Upc2, a binucleate zinc cluster protein (8,9). Eight of these genes encode the Dan/Tir cell wall proteins (10-12), three of which are essential for anaerobic growth (12); others are involved in sterol synthesis (13) or transport (8). Regulators controlling expression of this group include the oxygen-inhibited Mox4/Upc2 activator, and two repressors bound at neighboring sites responsible for aerobic repression (13). One of these is Rox1 (9). The second, originally designated Rox7, was identified as a repressor of DAN1 and some of the other DAN/TIR genes, as well as of the hypoxic gene ANB1 (9). As we show here, Rox7 is Mot3. It was previously identified as a zinc finger regulator which functions either as a repressor or activator of a diverse group of genes (14-16), acting through a consensus site (14,15) shown to be important for aerobic repression of ANB1 (17). Through its versatility as an activator and repressor, Mot3 participates in a cell wall remodeling process (12) in which anaerobic and aerobic cell wall proteins [the CWP proteins (18)] are alternately expressed in response to oxygen.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 371 376 371 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HEM13</italic>
###xml 381 385 381 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OLE1</italic>
###xml 423 427 423 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 432 436 432 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 512 513 512 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c7">7</xref>
###xml 514 516 514 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c12">12</xref>
###xml 568 569 568 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c3">3</xref>
###xml 570 571 570 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c5">5</xref>
Anaerobic genes encode proteins serving several different purposes. Presumably, it is advantageous for the regulatory system to permit gene-to-gene variation in basal and induced expression as well as cross-talk by other regulatory pathways. Thus, repression in aerobic cells ranges from moderate, for some genes, e.g. those whose basal expression is indispensable, like HEM13 and OLE1, to highly stringent, for genes like DAN1 and ANB1. Aerobic repression depends on heme signaling through multiple mechanisms (7,12). We show here that expression of Mot3, like Rox1 (3,5), is induced to inhibitory levels by heme. It acts to de-repress a mechanism which includes Hap1, Tup1-Ssn6 and a fourth unidentified factor. Induction levels for Mot3 and Rox1 appear to be calibrated to establish a range of responses to oxygen levels for each target gene, as determined by the number of operator sites in each promoter.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
The requirement for both Mot3 and Rox1 for inhibition of aerobic expression might suggest each is a necessary component of a single complex. Arguing against this, we show that either repressor can almost fully compensate for the lack of the other when artificially expressed at high levels. However, at the low levels present in aerobic cells, Mot3 and Rox1 act synergistically to achieve stringent repression. Though functioning in concert, the two mechanisms are distinct, as shown by the observation that Rox1, but not Mot3, requires the Tup1-Ssn6 co-repressor for its function.
###end p 8

###begin title 18
RESULTS
###end title 18
###begin title 19
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX7</italic>
###xml 19 23 19 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
ROX7 identified as MOT3
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 4 8 4 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX7</italic>

###xml 133 137 133 137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 190 194 190 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox7</italic>
###xml 240 244 240 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 256 260 256 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX7</italic>
###xml 339 348 339 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1&#8211;URA3</italic>
###xml 473 477 473 477 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 568 570 568 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c14">14</xref>
###xml 571 573 571 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c15">15</xref>
The ROX7 locus was defined by recessive mutations in one of three complementation groups (9) selected for constitutive expression of DAN1, which is normally repressed during aerobic growth. rox7 mutations also de-repressed the hypoxic gene ANB1. We cloned ROX7 from a centromeric library by complementation in cells carrying an integrated DAN1-URA3 fusion (see Materials and Methods). Complementing plasmids carried the YMR070W open-reading frame, previously identified as MOT3, encoding a zinc finger protein that regulates expression of a number of different genes (14,15), but which had not been associated with oxygen regulation.
###end p 20
###begin title 21
###xml 34 38 34 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 43 47 43 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
Synergistic aerobic repression of ANB1 and DAN1 by Mot3 and Rox1
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 51 55 51 55 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox7</italic>
###xml 79 83 79 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 121 125 121 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 130 134 130 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 141 142 141 142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f1">1</xref>
###xml 280 284 280 284 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 321 325 321 325 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 401 402 401 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c7">7</xref>
###xml 403 404 403 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c9">9</xref>
###xml 468 472 468 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 523 527 523 527 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 552 556 552 556 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
###xml 624 625 620 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f1">1</xref>
###xml 660 664 656 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
###xml 707 711 699 703 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mot3</italic>
###xml 716 720 704 708 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
###xml 738 739 722 723 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f1">1</xref>
As expected from the constitutive phenotype of the rox7 alleles, disruption of MOT3 caused partial loss of repression of ANB1 and DAN1 (Fig. 1), indicating that Mot3 functions along with Rox1 as an aerobic repressor of both genes. We focused on the roles of the two repressors on ANB1 expression, since the regulation of DAN1 is complicated by the contributions of two other oxygen-sensitive factors (7,9) and shows a lesser dependence on Rox1 and Mot3. Disruption of ROX1 caused an apparent full loss of repression of the ANB1 gene: expression in the rox1Delta strain was equal under aerobic and anaerobic conditions (Fig. 1); moreover, expression in aerobic rox1Delta cells was equal to that in anaerobic mot3Deltarox1Delta cells (Fig. 1B), representing the fully de-repressed output of the promoter. Hence, Mot3 has no repression function on its own under these conditions, and even though Rox1 represses weakly by itself, the efficient repression observed in normal aerobic cells occurs only when the two proteins function in concert, i.e. 'synergistically'. Negative synergy is a less well defined concept than positive, but a reasonable index for synergisic inhibition is a comparison of the residual activities when one or both repressors are present. These were determined to be 100 or 20-40%, respectively, with Mot3 or Rox1 alone, versus <1% when both are functioning. Hence, the residual expression with both repressors present is much less than that with either one alone, i.e. the combined inhibitory effects are clearly more than multiplicative (see Discussion).
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 33 37 33 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 42 46 42 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 195 199 195 199 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 207 211 207 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
###xml 258 262 254 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mot3</italic>
###xml 404 405 396 397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c1">1</xref>
###xml 443 447 435 439 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 562 563 554 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c1">1</xref>
###xml 564 565 556 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c7">7</xref>
###xml 603 607 595 599 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 616 620 608 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
###xml 625 629 617 621 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mot3</italic>
###xml 654 658 646 650 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 685 689 677 681 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 776 777 768 769 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c7">7</xref>
###xml 778 779 770 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c9">9</xref>
###xml 916 920 908 912 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tup1</italic>
###xml 925 929 913 917 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ssn6</italic>
###xml 974 975 958 959 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f1">1</xref>
###xml 1011 1015 995 999 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 1018 1022 1002 1006 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 1069 1070 1053 1054 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c6">6</xref>
###xml 1155 1159 1139 1143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tup1</italic>
###xml 1164 1168 1144 1148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ssn6</italic>
###xml 1193 1197 1169 1173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
Differences in the expression of DAN1 and ANB1 in these mutants reveals a variable degree of participation in repression by the two factors at different promoters. The full loss of repression of ANB1 in the rox1Delta strain compared with partial loss in the mot3Delta strain implies that Rox1 plays a more important role than Mot3 at this promoter, presumably reflecting the preponderance of Rox1 sites (1). In contrast, aerobic repression of DAN1 was more affected by loss of Mot3 than of Rox1, also consistent with the relative numbers of Mot3 and Rox1 sites (1,7). The greater overall sensitivity of ANB1 to both rox1 and mot3 mutations compared with DAN1 may reflect the fact that DAN1 is also repressed by at least two other oxygen-sensitive mechanisms in aerobic cells (7,9). The results show constitutive expression of both genes in the absence of the Tup1-Ssn6 complex. The fact that expression in anaerobic tup1Deltassn6Delta cells is lower than in wild type (Fig. 1A) may result from de-repression of ROX1or MOT3, which are both regulated by the heterodimer (6, and see below); the same could hold for the relatively lower expression in aerobic tup1Deltassn6Deltacells compared with rox1Delta cells (see Discussion).
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Mot3 function is independent of oxygen
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 174 178 174 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 183 187 183 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 218 222 218 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 320 321 320 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f2">2</xref>
###xml 561 562 561 562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c4">4</xref>
The role of Mot3 in aerobic repression suggested that it might be converted to a repressing form in the presence of oxygen (or heme). To test this we monitored expression of DAN1 and ANB1 in anaerobic cells expressing MOT3 under galactose control. Both genes were repressed in cells expressing high levels of Mot3 (Fig. 2), implying that inhibition of transcription by the repressor is concentration dependent and not the result of a conformational change occurring as a result of the presence of oxygen and/or heme. The same was earlier demonstrated for Rox1 (4).
###end p 25
###begin title 26
###xml 13 17 13 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
Induction of MOT3 by heme and repression by Hap1 and Tup1-Ssn6
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 95 99 95 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 162 166 162 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 215 216 215 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f3">3</xref>
###xml 288 292 288 292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 294 295 294 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c3">3</xref>
Concentration-dependent repression by Mot3 suggested that heme inhibits expression by inducing MOT3 expression. This was confirmed by the observation first, that MOT3 mRNA is down-regulated in anaerobic cells (Fig. 3) and second, that expression is de-repressed by heme, in parallel with ROX1 (3).
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 83 87 83 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 146 147 146 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f4">4</xref>
###xml 191 195 191 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 294 298 294 298 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 307 311 307 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hap1</italic>
###xml 367 371 363 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 414 418 410 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 438 442 434 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hap1</italic>
###xml 607 611 599 603 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 730 734 722 726 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tup1</italic>
###xml 739 743 727 731 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ssn6</italic>
###xml 761 762 745 746 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f4">4</xref>
###xml 865 869 849 853 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 874 878 858 862 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
We tested factors known to mediate heme regulation for involvement in induction of MOT3. Neither Hap1 nor Hap2 were required for expression (Fig. 4A). However, rather than activating aerobic MOT3 expression, Hap1 functions by repressing in the absence of heme, as indicated by higher levels of MOT3 mRNA in hap1Delta cells during anaerobic growth. Heme regulation of MOT3 is not attributable solely to Hap1, since MOT3 mRNA levels in the hap1Delta strain were still higher in the presence of oxygen. This implicates another unknown oxygen-sensitive factor in the regulatory pathway. Anaerobic repression of MOT3 also depends on Tup1-Ssn6 more than on Hap1, as indicated by the presence of de-repressed levels of mRNA in anaerobic tup1Deltassn6Delta cells (Fig. 4B). Whether the co-repressor complex is recruited by Hap1 and/or another repressor is not clear. Thus, MOT3 and ROX1 expression are co-regulated in four respects: heme induction, repression of basal expression by Hap1, regulation by a second heme-responsive system, and repression by Tup1-Ssn6.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 33 37 33 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 69 73 69 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 75 76 75 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c6">6</xref>
###xml 120 129 120 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3-lacZ</italic>
###xml 146 150 146 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mot3</italic>
###xml 258 259 254 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f4">4</xref>
We tested for auto-repression of MOT3 analogous to that observed for ROX1 (6), but saw no increase in expression of the MOT3-lacZ reporter in the mot3Delta strain (data not shown). We also observed that neither factor regulates expression of the other (Fig. 4A and data not shown).

###begin title 30
Rox1 requires Tup1-Ssn6, but Mot3 functions through another mechanism
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 54 56 54 56 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c23">23</xref>
###xml 57 59 57 59 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c25">25</xref>
###xml 72 75 72 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN</italic>
###xml 76 79 76 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TIR</italic>
###xml 80 83 80 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ERG</italic>
###xml 91 92 91 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c9">9</xref>
###xml 104 105 104 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f1">1</xref>
###xml 197 199 197 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c26">26</xref>
###xml 200 202 200 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c27">27</xref>
###xml 441 445 441 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 457 461 457 461 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tup1</italic>
###xml 466 470 462 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ssn6</italic>
###xml 508 512 504 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
###xml 530 531 522 523 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f1">1</xref>
###xml 908 912 900 904 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 917 921 909 913 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 957 961 949 953 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tup1</italic>
###xml 966 970 954 958 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ssn6</italic>
###xml 1006 1010 994 998 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 1048 1052 1036 1040 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 1072 1076 1060 1064 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 1091 1092 1079 1080 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f5">5</xref>
Tup1 and Ssn6 are co-repressors of the hypoxic genes (23-25) and of the DAN/TIR/ERG genes (9) (see Fig. 1). It has been assumed that the heterodimer is recruited by Rox1, as with other repressors (26,27). If Tup1-Ssn6 is required for Rox1 function, loss of either the repressor or the co-repressor would be expected to cause similar levels of constitutive expression. However, it has been repeatedly observed that constitutive expression of ANB1 in aerobic tup1Deltassn6 cells is significantly lower than in rox1Delta cells (Fig. 1). This difference doesn't mean that Rox1 and Tup1-Ssn6 don't interact, since Rox1 might be able to repress inefficiently by itself. Another explanation might be that the weaker Mot3 repressor, rather than Rox1, recruits the heterodimer. To test for these interactions, we made use of the observation that Gal4-driven expression of either factor causes efficient repression of ANB1 and DAN1 in wild-type anaerobic cells. In a tup1Deltassn6 strain, Gal4-induced expression of MOT3 still caused efficient repression of ANB1, but expression of ROX1 did not (Fig. 5). Hence, Rox1 requires the Tup1-Ssn6 co-repressor in this context, while Mot3 evidently functions through a different mechanism.
###end p 31
###begin title 32
Mot3 and Rox1 are independent synergizing repressors
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 158 159 158 159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f1">1</xref>
###xml 487 491 487 491 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 495 499 495 499 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 560 564 560 564 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 569 573 569 573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 580 581 580 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f2">2</xref>
###xml 927 931 927 931 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mot3</italic>
###xml 941 945 937 941 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
###xml 989 993 981 985 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 1070 1071 1062 1063 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f6">6</xref>
###xml 1365 1366 1357 1358 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f1">1</xref>
###xml 1371 1372 1363 1364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f6">6</xref>
The failure of Rox1 and Mot3 to repress by themselves is in striking contrast to the highly stringent repression observed when both factors are present (Fig. 1). The mutual dependence of Mot3 and Rox1 could arise either because both are essential parts of a single mechanism (e.g. Tup1-Ssn6) or because they act independently in a synergistic fashion. We tested whether either factor is sufficient when over-expressed, starting from the observation that Gal4-driven expression of either MOT3 or ROX1 in anaerobic cells resulted in efficient repression of both DAN1 and ANB1 (Fig. 2). This observation had implied that each factor is mechanistically sufficient for repression, since under these conditions high levels of Mot3 were paired with very low (uninduced) levels of Rox1, or vice versa. To rule out the possibility that low levels of the non-induced factor assisted the over-expressed one, we repeated the experiment in mot3Delta and rox1Delta mutants. In both cases, expression of ANB1 was still almost fully inhibited, in both aerobic and anaerobic cells (Fig. 6), each repressor compensating for the absence of the other. Thus, either one of the two independent repression mechanisms is effective at high operator occupancy. It was clear, however, that repression is significantly more efficient in aerobic wild-type cells with both factors present (Figs 1 and 6B), even at their relatively low endogenous levels. Hence, even though both Mot3 and Rox1 can repress independently at artificially high levels, stringent regulation in aerobic cells depends on a synergistic interaction.
###end p 33
###begin title 34
DISCUSSION
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 284 285 284 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f7">7</xref>
###xml 323 326 323 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN</italic>
###xml 327 330 327 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TIR</italic>
###xml 353 357 353 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 657 658 657 658 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c8">8</xref>
###xml 659 660 659 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c9">9</xref>
###xml 798 799 798 799 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c7">7</xref>
###xml 1056 1060 1056 1060 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
We have identified Mot3 as a repressor that functions synergistically with Rox1 to suppress aerobic expression of genes in two different anaerobic regulons. Mot3 and Rox1 are part of a complex regulatory network which mediates heme regulation through several different pathways (Fig. 7). Remarkably, heme regulation of the DAN/TIR genes, exemplified by DAN1, depends on four independent mechanisms, functioning at four different promoter sites: the Tup1-Ssn6-dependent Rox1 mechanism; the Tup1-Ssn6-independent Mot3 mechanism; an inhibitory mechanism which operates at the 'AR1' site through a regulatory domain in the oxygen-regulated Mox4/Upc2 activator (8,9); and a fourth anaerobic induction mechanism involving an unidentified activator operating at a second anaerobic response element (AR2) (7). Regulation through the AR1 and AR2 sites by heme is not highly stringent, and the combined action of Mot3 and Rox1 bound to their respective operators serves to enhance aerobic repression, reducing basal expression to undetectable levels. In the simpler ANB1 promoter, Mot3 and Rox1 appear to be solely responsible for heme regulation, providing a useful model for their possible interaction.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 108 112 108 112 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 116 120 116 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
###xml 125 129 125 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mot3</italic>
###xml 283 287 283 287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 884 892 884 892 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 955 957 955 957 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c17">17</xref>
The mutual dependence of Mot3 and Rox1 was originally inferred from the strongly constitutive expression of ANB1 in rox1 and mot3 mutants, which implies that neither factor can repress on its own at endogenous levels. We have demonstrated that the highly stringent repression of the ANB1 gene results from true synergy between the two repressors, i.e. through concerted action by independent mechanisms, rather than through obligatory co-participation in a single mechanism. The independent function of Mot3 and Rox1 was shown by the fact that over-expression of either can compensate for lack of the other. Strikingly, however, even when titrated by high levels of either Mot3 or Rox1, single-factor repression is still less stringent than in cells containing low levels of both repressors. One possible explanation for synergistic co-dependence might be cooperative binding, though in vitro studies have indicated that this does not occur on naked DNA (17). Perhaps more likely is the possibility that the two factors act through different targets in the PolII initiation complex, to produce concerted inhibition analogous to numerous examples of synergistic activation. A two-target model is strengthened by the indication that Mot3 and Rox1 utilize different mechanisms, with only the latter requiring Tup1-Ssn6 for its function.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 456 457 456 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c1">1</xref>
This regulatory system has solved the problem of allowing some anaerobic genes to be expressed at low or moderate levels in aerobic cells while others are stringently repressed. Apparently, during evolution of the regulatory pathway, the levels of Mot3 and Rox1 have been calibrated in such a way that on some promoters they are able to fully repress, and at others, less so, depending on the number and quality of operator sites [which vary considerably (1)], and possibly on the type and strength of activation mechanism driving expression of each gene.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 65 69 65 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 86 90 86 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 92 93 92 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c3">3</xref>
###xml 94 95 94 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c5">5</xref>
###xml 96 97 96 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c6">6</xref>
###xml 395 396 395 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c5">5</xref>
###xml 397 398 397 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c6">6</xref>
###xml 463 467 463 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 472 476 472 476 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
The response to oxygen depends on the fact, established here for MOT3 and earlier for ROX1 (3,5,6), that expression of the repressors is de-repressed from an inhibited state by heme. This occurs, at least in part, through a regulatory system which controls both genes, with repression in anaerobic cells being fully dependent on Tup1-Ssn6, and partly dependent on the heme-unbound form of Hap1 (5,6). A second factor which appears to play a role in repression of ROX1 and MOT3 has not been identified, nor have factors responsible for activation under aerobic conditions.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 144 146 144 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c23">23</xref>
###xml 245 249 245 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 279 283 279 283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 316 320 316 320 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tup1</italic>
###xml 325 329 321 325 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ssn6</italic>
###xml 396 400 388 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT3</italic>
###xml 500 504 492 496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 597 598 589 590 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f1">1</xref>
###xml 687 691 679 683 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rox1</italic>
###xml 725 729 713 717 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ROX1</italic>
###xml 733 737 721 725 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tup1</italic>
###xml 742 746 726 730 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ssn6</italic>
###xml 790 794 770 774 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 876 877 856 857 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f5">5</xref>
###xml 915 919 895 899 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 993 994 973 974 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg792f4">4</xref>
###xml 1333 1337 1313 1317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ANB1</italic>
###xml 1348 1350 1328 1330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c28">28</xref>
We have concluded that the Rox1 repression mechanism requires the participation of the Tup1-Ssn6 co-repression complex as originally suggested (23), and that Mot3 functions in a different way. This is based on the observation that repression of ANB1 caused by over-expression of ROX1 in anaerobic cells is lost in a tup1Deltassn6Delta strain, whereas repression resulting from over-expression of MOT3 is still in effect. However, the role of Tup1-Ssn6 is not clear. For example, though expression of ANB1 under anaerobic and aerobic conditions is equal in the absence of the heterodimer (see Fig. 1A), the mRNA level is consistently lower than in anaerobic wild-type cells or in aerobic rox1Delta cells. The de-repression of ROX1 in tup1Deltassn6Delta cells may cause reduced expression of ANB1, though this seems unlikely in view of the dependence of Rox1 on Tup1-Ssn6 (Fig. 5). More likely, reduced expression of ANB1 results from de-repressed expression of Mot3 (in glucose cultures) (Fig. 4). In addition, the absence of Tup1-Ssn6 may lead to reduced expression through some other perturbation of the regulatory circuits in this system. The genetic interaction of Rox1 and Tup1-Ssn6 suggests that Rox1 recruits Tup1-Ssn6 to the promoter during aerobic growth. However, recent evidence of constitutive binding of Tup1-Ssn6 to the ANB1 promoter (28) suggests that the functional relationship between Rox1 and Tup1-Ssn6 may not be one of recruitment.

###begin p 47
###xml 250 251 250 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c3">3</xref>
###xml 252 253 252 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c5">5</xref>
###xml 254 255 254 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c9">9</xref>
###xml 256 258 256 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c12">12</xref>
###xml 729 733 729 733 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DAN1</italic>
###xml 744 745 744 745 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c7">7</xref>
###xml 809 813 809 813 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CYC1</italic>
###xml 818 822 818 822 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SOD1</italic>
###xml 959 961 959 961 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c14">14</xref>
###xml 1017 1021 1017 1021 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CWP2</italic>
###xml 1041 1043 1041 1043 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg792c12">12</xref>
 The heme regulatory network controlling expression of anaerobic and aerobic genes. The scheme is consistent with evidence on the interlocked regulatory pathways controlling expression of aerobic and anaerobic genes, as described here and elsewhere (3,5,9,12). Heme regulates the anaerobic genes via several mechanisms. It de-represses Hap1-inhibited expression of the Mot3 and Rox1 repressors, which in turn block expression of several anaerobic genes during aerobic growth. A subset of anaerobic genes are activated by Mox4/Upc2, whose activation function and expression are both inhibited by heme during aerobic growth. In addition, there is a second unidentified oxygen-inhibited factor operating through the AR2 site in the DAN1 promoter (7). Heme also induces expression of a large group of genes (e.g. CYC1 and SOD1), via Hap1 in its alternate role as a heme-dependent activator. Finally, Mot3, known to function as either an activator or a repressor (14), also activates expression of the aerobically induced CWP2 mannoprotein gene (12).
