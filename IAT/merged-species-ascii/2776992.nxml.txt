###begin article-title 0
Insulin Signaling Regulates Mitochondrial Function in Pancreatic beta-Cells
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: SL OS EDA RK. Performed the experiments: SL TO AA JS CWL HB EDA. Analyzed the data: SL OS EDA RK. Wrote the paper: SL RK.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
Current address: Nagareyama General Hospital, Chiba, Japan
###end p 2
###begin p 3
###xml 318 319 315 316 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 454 455 442 443 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 735 736 720 721 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 428 432 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 570 578 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Insulin/IGF-I signaling regulates the metabolism of most mammalian tissues including pancreatic islets. To dissect the mechanisms linking insulin signaling with mitochondrial function, we first identified a mitochondria-tethering complex in beta-cells that included glucokinase (GK), and the pro-apoptotic protein, BADS. Mitochondria isolated from beta-cells derived from beta-cell specific insulin receptor knockout (betaIRKO) mice exhibited reduced BADS, GK and protein kinase A in the complex, and attenuated function. Similar alterations were evident in islets from patients with type 2 diabetes. Decreased mitochondrial GK activity in betaIRKOs could be explained, in part, by reduced expression and altered phosphorylation of BADS. The elevated phosphorylation of p70S6K and JNK1 was likely due to compensatory increase in IGF-1 receptor expression. Re-expression of insulin receptors in betaIRKO cells partially restored the stoichiometry of the complex and mitochondrial function. These data indicate that insulin signaling regulates mitochondrial function and have implications for beta-cell dysfunction in type 2 diabetes.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Introduction
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 208 211 208 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kim1">[1]</xref>
###xml 457 460 457 460 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Datta1">[2]</xref>
###xml 749 752 746 749 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Assmann1">[3]</xref>
###xml 1002 1005 993 996 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Otani1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1007 1010 998 1001 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kulkarni1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1012 1015 1003 1006 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kulkarni2">[6]</xref>
###xml 1017 1020 1008 1011 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Assmann2">[7]</xref>
###xml 1283 1286 1268 1271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Okada1">[8]</xref>
###xml 566 571 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 800 804 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1031 1035 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Insulin and insulin like growth factor I (IGF-I) play critical roles in triggering pleiotropic actions including metabolism, proliferation and survival in most mammalian cells including the pancreatic islets [1]. The two hormones bind to their cognate receptors and activate Akt/protein kinase B via the phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3-K)-generated polyphospho-phosphatidylinositol pathway to phosphorylate the protein BAD in the regulation of apoptosis [2]. Indeed, we and several other laboratories have used cell line, primary islet and genetically engineered mouse models to demonstrate that insulin/IGF-I signaling plays a critical role in the regulation of hormone secretion, proliferation and survival of pancreatic beta-cells (see review [3]). Among the various models, it is notable that mice with beta-cell specific knockout of insulin receptors (betaIRKO) and/or IGF-1 receptors demonstrate reduced expression of the glucokinase (GK) gene and blunted glucose-stimulated insulin secretion [4], [5], [6], [7]. betaIRKO mice also display an age-dependent decrease in islet mass, poor compensatory islet response to high fat-diet induced islet growth and increased susceptibility to diabetes, suggesting a role for insulin signaling in the regulation of beta-cell survival [8].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 234 237 225 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Anello1">[9]</xref>
###xml 291 295 282 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Matschinsky1">[10]</xref>
###xml 297 301 288 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Matschinsky2">[11]</xref>
###xml 329 333 317 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Wiederkehr1">[12]</xref>
###xml 335 339 323 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Veluthakal1">[13]</xref>
###xml 419 423 407 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Danial1">[14]</xref>
###xml 592 595 580 583 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Anello1">[9]</xref>
###xml 445 453 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
While the precise defect in beta-cell growth and/or function that triggers overt diabetes is not fully understood, mitochondrial dysfunction in beta-cells has been implicated as one factor that impairs beta-cell survival and function [9]. Mitochondria play a major role in insulin secretion [10], [11] and in beta-cell apoptosis [12], [13] triggered by diverse internal or external cues ultimately promoting cell death [14]. Indeed, islets from patients with type 2 diabetes exhibit low ATP content and a blunted glucose-stimulated insulin secretion (GSIS) and increased mitochondrial volume [9], together implicating a role for mitochondria in the pathogenesis of beta-cell dysfunction.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 146 150 146 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kurmasheva1">[15]</xref>
###xml 366 370 363 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Danial2">[16]</xref>
###xml 522 526 516 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Lee1">[17]</xref>
###xml 528 532 522 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kim2">[18]</xref>
###xml 723 726 714 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kulkarni1">[5]</xref>
###xml 728 732 719 723 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kulkarni3">[19]</xref>
Growth factor-mediated pathways have been reported to modulate mitochondrial function and regulate the activity of the pro-apoptotic protein, BAD [15]. Interestingly, studies in hepatocytes and beta-cells have shown the presence of a BAD/glucokinase complex at the mitochondria suggesting a point of intersection for processes that regulate glycolysis and apoptosis [16]. Further, hyperglycemia has been reported to modulate the levels of GK and alter its interactions with mitochondria leading to apoptosis of beta-cells [17], [18]. Considering the critical role of glucokinase in cellular metabolism and survival, and our previous observations of reduced expression of GK in beta-cells lacking insulin or IGF-1 receptors [5], [19], we undertook the current study to explore whether absence of insulin signaling in beta-cells impacts mitochondrial function.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 214 215 208 209 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 300 301 294 295 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 81 86 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 91 96 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In this study, we report the presence of a BAD/GK complex at the mitochondria in mouse and human beta-cells. Furthermore, we provide evidence that lack of insulin receptors in beta-cells impacts localization of BADS, alters the stoichiometry of the BAD/GK complex and modulates phosphorylation of BADS that together contribute to defects in mitochondrial function.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Results
###end title 9
###begin title 10
Identification of BAD/glucokinase complex in beta-cell mitochondria
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 343 347 334 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Danial2">[16]</xref>
###xml 525 526 516 517 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 528 537 519 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g001">Fig. 1A&#8211;C</xref>
###xml 924 931 912 919 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g001">Fig. 1D</xref>
###xml 1095 1102 1083 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g001">Fig. 1D</xref>
###xml 1214 1221 1202 1209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g001">Fig. 1D</xref>
###xml 1247 1251 1235 1239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Lee1">[17]</xref>
###xml 1256 1257 1244 1245 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">L</sub>
###xml 1300 1301 1288 1289 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 1445 1452 1433 1440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g001">Fig. 1D</xref>
###xml 158 162 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 198 203 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 623 628 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 633 638 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
To explore the presence of a putative BAD/GK complex in beta cells, we isolated mitochondria from beta-cell lines, or islets derived from control or betaIRKO mice and from diabetic and non-diabetic human subjects. Mitochondria were subjected to microcystin pull down assays. Similar to descriptions of the mitochondrial complex in hepatocytes [16], we detected five proteins: WAVE1 (a protein kinase A scaffold protein), glucokinase (GK), protein kinase A (PKA), protein phosphatase 1 (PP1), and the pro-apoptotic protein BADS (Fig. 1A-C). All components of the BAD/GK complex were identified in cultured beta-cells and in mouse and human islets, indicating that this complex is conserved across species. Evaluation of the distribution of each component in cytosolic and mitochondrial fractions revealed that WAVE1 exists largely in the cytosol and mitochondrial WAVE1 comprises a small proportion of cellular WAVE protein (Fig. 1D). Interestingly, we observed robust expression of glucokinase in mitochondrial fractions when equal amounts of protein from mitochondria and cytososl were loaded (Fig. 1D). However, it is likely that the amount of GK that resides in the cytosol is greater than at the mitochondria (Fig. 1D). Among the BAD isoforms [17], BADL largely localized to the cytosol while BADS was identified in both cytosolic and mitochondrial fractions. On the other hand, PKA and PP1 were detectable in both cytosol and mitochondria (Fig. 1D).

###xml 299 308 293 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g001">Fig. 1A&#8211;C</xref>
###xml 313 320 307 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0007983.s004">Fig. S4</xref>
###xml 431 438 419 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g001">Fig. 1A</xref>
###xml 691 692 673 674 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 219 223 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 252 258 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 383 387 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 660 666 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Assessment of the effects of absence of insulin receptors or of insulin resistance on the complex, revealed a significant reduction in BADS in mitochondrial samples from beta-cell lines or islets isolated from betaIRKO mice and in islets isolated from humans with diabetes (75% of control, p<0.05) (Fig. 1A-C and Fig. S4). In mitochondria from beta-cell lines isolated from betaIRKO mice, PKA was reduced (12% of control, p<0.05) (Fig. 1A), and we also observed a trend towards a decrease in GK protein (29% of control, p = 0.08) in the mutants. Together, these data indicate that absence of insulin signaling in beta-cells (betaIRKO) or the diabetic state in humans is linked to reduced BADS in the mitochondrial complex.
###end p 14

###xml 3 4 3 4 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
BADS phosphorylation, p70S6K and JNK signaling
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 3 4 3 4 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 157 161 157 161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Datta2">[20]</xref>
###xml 284 288 284 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Datta2">[20]</xref>
###xml 354 355 354 355 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 547 554 541 548 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0007983.s001">Fig. S1</xref>
BADS, a pro-apoptotic protein belonging to the Bcl-2 family, can be phosphorylated in response to growth factors at three sites, Ser-112, Ser-136 or Ser-155 [20]. Kinases implicated in activating serine phosphorylation on BAD, include Akt/protein kinase B, p70S6K, PKA, Rsk, and PAK1 [20]. To investigate the role of insulin versus IGF-I in mediating BADS phosphorylation in beta-cells, we stimulated beta-cells with either hormone and examined mitochondrial or cytosolic fractions. For all experiments, the purity of the fractions was confirmed (Fig. S1).
###end p 16

Relative to total levels of BADS, basal levels of Ser-136 phosphorylation of BADS were also increased in betaIRKO mitochondria versus controls, and phosphorylation tended to increase further with insulin treatment (Fig. 2C). Compared to changes in Ser-112 phosphorylation, these changes were not as dramatic (Fig. S2A, B). Given the low abundance of BADS in the betaIRKO mitochondria we conclude that the majority of the remaining BADS was in the phosphorylated state. The phosphorylation of BADS is unlikely to involve Akt since its activation is extremely low in betaIRKO cells (see below). On the contrary, phosphorylation of BAD is likely due to enhanced IGF-I signaling in betaIRKO cells (see below) that potentially activates RSK1, which in turn, has been reported to phosphorylate BADS at both Ser-112 and 136 [22]. To evaluate relative changes in cytosol versus mitochondria, we next examined signaling events in cytosolic fractions. In betaIRKO cytosol, the pattern of BADS expression and phosphorylation were different than that observed in mitochondria. Thus, total BADS showed a trend towards an increase but Ser-112 phosphorylation was reduced in basal conditions (Fig. 2E,F,G). The mitochondrial and cytosolic data collectively suggest that BADS is redistributed away from the mitochondria in the cytosol in betaIRKO cells without a change in the total cellular content of BADS. This was confirmed following analysis of whole cell lysates showing no differences in total BADS between betaIRKO and control cells (Fig. 2H and Fig. S2A). Cytosolic BADS protein content tended to increase in insulin stimulated control cells, but did not change in betaIRKO cells. One possible explanation for these observations is that increased Akt activation, in control relative to betaIRKO cells results in increased phosphorylation of BADS at Ser-136 leading to sequestration of Ser-136 BADS with 14-3-3 proteins in the cytoplasm of controls (Fig. S2A) [23]. Therefore, relative to betaIRKO cells, there is more cytosolic Ser-136 BADs in control cells, which is consistent with increased Akt-mediated phosphorylation and cytosolic sequestration in control cells.

In the cytosolic fractions extracted from control cells, we observed a significant decrease in Ser-112 phosphorylation of BADS in response to either insulin or IGF-I stimulation (Fig. 2F,G). The mechanism for this attenuation is uncertain, but these observations raise the possibility that Ser-112 phosphorylation might be negatively regulated by insulin or IGF-I mediated activation of Akt. betaIRKO cytosol contains less Ser-112 BADS compared to control (Fig. 2F,G), which may suggest that phosphorylation of Ser-112 BADS, may preferentially occur at the mitochondria (Fig. 2B and Fig. S2A). Indeed, Ser-112 phosphorylation of BADS in betaIRKO cytosol did not increase following insulin treatment. In contrast, IGF-I treatment increased Ser-112 phosphorylation in betaIRKO cells (Fig. 2D) (p<0.05), suggesting that the IGF-I signaling module that activates BAD, might be hyperactivated in betaIRKO cells (Fig. 2F,G).

The JNK (c-Jun N-terminal kinase) pathway is activated by diverse stressors and can influence multiple cellular processes. Because mitochondrial dysfunction could activate stress signaling in beta-cells, we investigated JNK activity in mitochondria. JNK1 phosphorylation and total JNK1 protein was increased in mitochondria from betaIRKO cells (Fig. 3E), which is consistent with the report that JNK co-localizes with mitochondria through its interacting protein [26]. Increased mitochondrial membrane potential results from increased glycolysis and oxidative phosphorylation. We hypothesized that altered mitochondrial glucokinase activity contributes to the poor membrane potential response to glucose stimulation. ###xml 1302 1306 1260 1264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Oberkofler1">[32]</xref>
###xml 1308 1312 1266 1270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Oberkofler2">[33]</xref>
To further explore mechanisms that contribute to the lower mitochondrial membrane potential in the betaIRKOs, we measured oxygen consumption responses to treatment with glucose or DNP (2,4-dinitrophenol). Despite similar increases in oxygen consumption in glucose-stimulated control and betaIRKO cells, there was a dramatic reduction in ATP content in glucose-stimulated betaIRKO cells (Fig. 5C). The increase in oxygen consumption, accompanied by reduced ATP generation, and a blunted increase in membrane potential suggests that betaIRKO mitochondria are significantly uncoupled. Interestingly, expression levels of PGC-1alpha in the betaIRKO cells were markedly increased, which likely represents a compensatory response to dysfunctional mitochondria (Fig. 5D). Rescue of phenotype by re-expression of insulin receptors in betaIRKOs
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 382 389 355 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g006">Fig. 6A</xref>
###xml 491 500 464 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g006">Fig. 6A,B</xref>
###xml 582 583 555 556 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 695 702 665 672 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g006">Fig. 6C</xref>
###xml 842 849 812 819 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g006">Fig. 6D</xref>
###xml 127 132 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
To confirm that the defects in mitochondria in betaIRKO beta-cells are due to disrupted insulin signaling, we re-expressed the human insulin receptor-B isoform in betaIRKO beta-cells and revaluated the mitochondrial BAD/GK complex and mitochondrial function. In betaIRKO cells re-expressing insulin receptors, Akt phosphorylation was restored to approximately70% of control levels (Fig. 6A) and the enhancedexpression of IGF-1 receptors and phospho-p70S6K were normalized to control levels (Fig. 6A,B). In microcystin pull-down assays, we observed a near-complete restoration of BADS in the BAD/GK complex, while PKA was significantly higher in the re-expressors compared to betaIRKO fractions (Fig. 6C). Mitochondrial Thr-389 p70S6K and Ser-112 BAD phosphorylation were normalized in the absence of equivalent changes in phosphorylated JNK (Fig. 6D). However, the level of expression of GK in the complex was not completely restored suggesting that re-establishing insulin/Akt signaling only partially improves the stoichiometry of the BAD/GK complex in betaIRKO beta-cells.

###begin title 50
Discussion
###end title 50
###begin p 51
In this study we examined the significance of insulin signaling on mitochondrial function in pancreatic beta-cells. We report that disruption of insulin signaling in beta-cells alters the stoichiometry of a mitochondrial BAD/GK complex, reduces GK activity and promotes mitochondrial dysfunction. Re-expression of the insulin receptor in beta-cells that lack insulin receptors largely restores the complex and improves mitochondrial function, highlighting a significant role for insulin signaling in the maintenance of the BAD/GK complex and in the regulation of mitochondrial function.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 284 288 281 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Danial2">[16]</xref>
###xml 468 472 465 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Smith1">[34]</xref>
###xml 725 728 710 713 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Otani1">[4]</xref>
###xml 730 733 715 718 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kulkarni2">[6]</xref>
###xml 806 807 791 792 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 986 990 965 969 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Danial2">[16]</xref>
###xml 992 996 971 975 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Danial3">[35]</xref>
###xml 1181 1182 1160 1161 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 1354 1357 1333 1336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Anello1">[9]</xref>
###xml 47 52 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 57 62 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 720 724 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1051 1057 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 1139 1143 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1324 1332 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The identification of a BAD/GK complex in both mouse and human beta-cells in this study indicates conservation across species and supports the concept of a potential functional link between apoptosis and glycolysis at the mitochondria, which is similar to observations in hepatocytes [16]. Indeed, the WAVE protein in the complex has been suggested to anchor GK and BAD to the mitochondria to efficiently co-ordinate spatial and temporal aspects of glucose metabolism [34]. The alterations in the expression of different components in the complex in mitochondria from beta-cells from betaIRKO provide potential insights into the altered function of beta-cells and may explain some of the phenotypes observed in betaIRKO mice [4], [6]. Similar to effects observed in BAD-deficient hepatocytes, decreased BADS protein in the betaIRKO cells likely contributes to mitochondrial dysfunction and beta-cell dysfunction, which may contribute to the glucose intolerance in both of these mutants [16], [35]. The BAD/GK complex was also disrupted in islets from humans with type 2 diabetes and showed an interesting similarity with islets from bIRKO mice. Thus it is possible that reduced BADS in the mitochondria may be linked to the blunted glucose response and altered mitochondrial ultra structure observed in islets isolated from patients with type 2 diabetes [9].
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 167 171 164 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Boudina1">[36]</xref>
###xml 878 882 862 866 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Oberkofler1">[32]</xref>
###xml 884 888 868 872 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Oberkofler2">[33]</xref>
###xml 1052 1056 1033 1037 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Zhang1">[31]</xref>
###xml 1058 1062 1039 1043 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Pi1">[37]</xref>
###xml 1426 1430 1404 1408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Boudina1">[36]</xref>
###xml 1685 1693 1657 1665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0007983.s003">Fig. S3B</xref>
###xml 2145 2153 2111 2119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0007983.s003">Fig. S3A</xref>
Our study provides evidence that altered insulin signaling is associated with mitochondrial function in the beta-cell, as has recently been reported in cardiomyocytes [36]. The mechanism for mitochondrial dysfunction in betaIRKO cells is multifactorial and includes reduced glucose-mediated augmentation in mitochondrial membrane potential and reduced ATP levels that could be due either to decreased synthesis or to increased consumption in order to maintain the membrane potential. Of interest, mitochondrial dysfunction occurs despite increased oxygen consumption. These observations suggest that despite reduced glucokinase activity and potentially reduced glucose delivery to betaIRKO mitochondria they are also uncoupled. One potential mechanism for mitochondrial uncoupling is increased UCP2 expression, which may be driven by increased expression of PGC-1alpha and beta [32], [33]. Moreover, increased UCP2 expression has been implicated in limiting stimulus-secretion coupling in beta-cells in some animal models of diabetes but not in others [31], [37]. However, it is possible that this increased UCP2 expression may not contribute directly to mitochondrial dysfunction in betaIRKO cells and it could be the result of ROS overproduction in response to glucose stimulation since it was found that deletion of insulin receptors in heart increases their propensity to enhance ROS when exposed to fatty acid substrates [36]. Also, DNP experiments suggest that the capacity of the OXPHOS machinery may not be intrinsically impaired in betaIRKO cells; the improvement in oxygen consumption that we see in betaIRKO and the rescued cells might be due to altered mitochondrial mass (Fig. S3B); that might increase total mitochondrial respiration. Together, the mitochondrial defects in betaIRKO cells could be results of combined effects from reduced GK activity, ROS production and altered UCP2 expression. Finally, we also measured mitochondrial membrane potential response to glucose stimulation, and betaIRKO cells that re-expressed the insulin receptors showed a trend towards improved membrane potential response in response to glucose (Fig. S3A), which is also consistent with the increased respiration. Although our experiments have not definitively identified all mechanisms that could account for mitochondrial dysfunction, our study clearly shows that impaired insulin signaling in beta-cells fundamentally alters mitochondrial bioenergetics and function, which may ultimately impair insulin secretion.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 82 83 82 83 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 83 87 83 87 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Datta2">[20]</xref>
###xml 668 672 662 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Shimamura1">[38]</xref>
Consistent with the growth factor-induced phosphorylation and translocation of BADS[20] we observed significant alterations in mitochondria isolated from beta-cells lacking the insulin receptor. The high level of basal phosphorylation of Ser112-BAD and Ser136-BAD in the betaIRKOs likely reflects the effects of signaling via the IGF-1 receptors. It is possible that the relative levels of activation of insulin versus IGF-1 receptors determine the overall phosphorylation state of BAD at the mitochondria, with Ser112 phosphorylation being specifically mediated by Akt-independent pathways, and Ser136 phosphorylation being activated by both Akt and non-Akt pathways [38].
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 118 122 115 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Bonni1">[22]</xref>

###xml 297 304 294 301 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3SA/3SA</sup>
###xml 431 435 428 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Danial2">[16]</xref>
###xml 437 441 434 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Danial3">[35]</xref>
###xml 492 493 489 490 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 640 641 637 638 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 800 807 794 801 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g002">Fig. 2B</xref>
###xml 812 820 806 814 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0007983.s002">Fig. S2B</xref>
###xml 895 896 889 890 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 1033 1034 1027 1028 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 1073 1074 1067 1068 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 1256 1260 1250 1254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Danial2">[16]</xref>
###xml 1280 1281 1274 1275 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 1417 1418 1411 1412 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 1592 1593 1583 1584 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 1852 1855 1837 1840 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Otani1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1857 1860 1842 1845 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kulkarni1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1862 1865 1847 1850 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kulkarni2">[6]</xref>
###xml 1934 1935 1919 1920 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 305 310 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1847 1851 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Intact IGF-I signaling in betaIRKO cells could mediate the activation of RSK and MSK1 to phosphorylate BAD at Ser-112 [22]. The phosphorylation state of BADS has also been reported to impact its binding with other proteins such as Bcl-XL [39]. Further, the silencing of all serine sites in the BAD3SA/3SA mouse has been reported to reduce GK activity in hepatocytes while disruption of Ser-155 down-regulates GK activity in islets [16], [35]. These studies suggest that phosphorylation of BADS can modulate GK activity and impact apoptosis, likely by disruption of protein interactions in the complex. Our data suggest that the level of BADS phosphorylation correlates with function. This is reflected by the finding that betaIRKO cells exhibit extremely high Ser112 phosphorylation in mitochondria (Fig. 2B and Fig. S2B) and phosphorylation can potentially interrupt the interaction between BADS and other proteins, including GK. Furthermore, re-expression of insulin receptors reversed the increase in Ser112 phosphorylation of BADS and increased the content of total BADS in the complex. Together, these data suggest the potential relevance of interactions between Ser112 phosphorylation and function of the complex. Based on the report by Danial et al [16], restoration of BADS in the complex would augment glucokinase function and mitochondrial metabolism. Additional studies such as mutagenesis of Ser112 of BADS will be necessary to prove a direct role of this phosphorylation event. In betaIRKO mitochondria it is possible that phosphorylation at Ser-112 disrupts the residence of BADS in the mitochondrial complex and alters glucokinase activity and mitochondrial metabolism without affecting Ser-155-BAD (data not shown). These observations are consistent with the altered glucose sensing and altered apoptosis of beta-cells in betaIRKO mice [4], [5], [6]. The ability to reverse basal Ser-112 phoshorylation and restore BADS content to control levels by re-expressing the insulin receptor in betaIRKO cells further underscores the role of insulin signaling in regulating the mitochondrial complex.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 190 194 187 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Ueki1">[40]</xref>
###xml 196 200 193 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Fernandez2">[41]</xref>
###xml 423 427 420 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Smith1">[34]</xref>
###xml 429 433 426 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kim3">[42]</xref>
Another signaling pathway that could potentially influence the stoichiometry of the BAD/GK complex is the elevated expression of PTEN secondary to enhanced IGF-I signaling in betaIKRO cells [40], [41]. Increased PIP2 could decrease the binding of PKA in the VCA domain of the WAVE protein, which is known to anchor GK and BAD to the mitochondria to efficiently coordinate spatial and temporal aspects of glucose metabolism [34], [42] and may contribute to the partial normalization of PKA content upon re-expressing the insulin receptor in betaIRKO cells.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 173 177 167 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Harrington1">[43]</xref>
###xml 179 185 173 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g007">Fig. 7</xref>
###xml 497 501 491 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Dickson1">[25]</xref>
In the cytosol, the elevated p70S6K activation in betaIRKO cells could potentially serine-phosphorylate IRS1 and further dampen insulin-stimulation of Akt in betaIRKO cells [43] (Fig. 7), and potentiate IGF-I mediated pathways in the mutant cells. Indeed, activation of Akt/PKB has been reported to inhibit IGF-I activation of MAPK and mTOR pathways via Raf1 kinase, while adenoviral knockdown of Akt/PKB in INS-1 cells leads to enhanced ERK1/ERK2 signaling that is independent of IGF-I signaling [25].
###end p 57

###begin p 60
###xml 211 215 205 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Aoki1">[44]</xref>
###xml 500 504 491 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Che1">[45]</xref>
###xml 589 590 580 581 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
###xml 640 646 631 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007983-g007">Fig. 7</xref>
###xml 776 780 767 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-ONeillBT1">[46]</xref>
###xml 1143 1147 1127 1131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Patti1">[47]</xref>
###xml 1149 1153 1133 1137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Abel1">[48]</xref>
We observed high levels of JNK1 activation in mitochondria but not in cytosolic fractions in betaIRKO beta-cells, which is consistent with the report that JNK1 activation is linked to dysfunctional mitochondria [44]. The activation of JNK1 by IGF-I, but not by insulin, in both cytosolic and mitochondrial fractions in control beta-cells, implicates a role for IGF-I in the stress signaling pathway and is consistent with the potentiation of cytokine-mediated JNK following long-term IGF-I treatment [45]. We suggest that JNK1 could potentially contribute to Ser-112 phosphorylation on BADS to affect mitochondrial metabolism and function (Fig. 7). Our observations are consistent with the notion that down-regulation of Akt does not influence basal mitochondrial respiration [46]. It will be useful to dissect further, how insulin and IGF-I signaling pathways differentially modulate MAPK and mTOR target proteins, such as p70S6K and JNK1, to regulate mitochondrial function in beta-cells. Further work is also necessary to explore whether insulin/IGF-I signaling directly modulates PGC-1alpha to affect mitochondrial biogenesis and function [47], [48].
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 215 216 212 213 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</sub>
In summary, this study provides novel insights into the role of insulin signaling in the regulation of the BAD/GK complex, glycolytic enzyme activity and mitochondrial metabolism in pancreatic beta-cells. Ser112-BADS and its upstream kinases may be potential targets for the maintenance of the BAD/GK complex that is necessary for normal mitochondrial function and the regulation of beta-cell survival.
###end p 61

###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
###xml 10 15 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Mouse and human pancreatic islets

###begin p 66
###xml 89 92 89 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kulkarni2">[6]</xref>

###begin title 67
Mitochondrial isolation
###end title 67
###begin p 68
###xml 74 78 74 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Danial2">[16]</xref>
Mitochondria were isolated using conventional differential centrifugation [16]. Briefly, cell lines or islets were homogenized in mitochondrial lysis buffer (220 mM mannitol, 70 mM glucose, 10 mM HEPES, 1 mM EGTA, pH 7.4). Subsequently, the lysate was centrifuged at 700 g to separate nuclear and cytosolic fractions, the cytosolic fraction was further centrifuged at 7000 g at 4degreesC to pellet mitochondria. The isolated mitochondria were subjected either to microcystin capture assay (see below), Western blotting or mitochondrial glucokinase activity assay. For mitochondrial glucokinase activity assays, the isolated mitochondria were further purified through a 30%-70% percoll gradient by centrifugation at 20,000 rpm in a Beckman SW41 rotor for 40 min. The mitochondrial layer in the gradient was removed and washed twice and immediately used for activity assays.
###end p 68
###begin title 69
Microcystin capture assay
###end title 69
###begin p 70
Isolated mitochondria from each respective beta-cell line or from islets were solubilized in the lysis buffer containing 6 mM CHAPS, 150 mM NaCl, 0.01 M sodium phosphate (pH 7.2) and 2 mM EDTA. Based on the specific high affinity of microcystin for protein phosphatase 1 (PP1) (one of the components in the BAD/GK complex), the solubilized mitochondria were incubated with microcystin beads (Upstate, NY) overnight at 4degreesC to pull down the BAD/GK complex. After incubation, the microcystin beads were washed and boiled in protein sample buffer. The protein bound to the microcystin beads were resolved by SDS-PAGE gels and detected by Western blotting to identify the components precipitated on the beads.
###end p 70
###begin title 71
Western blotting
###end title 71
###begin p 72
###xml 317 321 311 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Kulkarni5">[51]</xref>
To evaluate the role of insulin or IGF-I signaling, cell lines were incubated with 100 nM of either insulin or IGF-I for 15 min at 37degreesC. The 15 min time point was chosen based on pilot experiments to induce signaling effects in mitochondria. Proteins were extracted from cells or islets as previously described [51]. The protein concentration of each sample was determined using a BCA kit (Pierce Biotechnology, Holmdel, NJ). The extracted proteins were resolved by SDS-PAGE, and the protein was transferred to PVDF membrane (Millipore, MA). Western blotting results were normalized to actin and quantitated by densitometry. Antibodies to the following proteins were used: Phospho-BAD, BAD, phospho-p70S6K, p70S6K, phospho-JNK1, JNK1 (Cell Signaling Technology, Boston, MA), glucokinase and protein phosphotase 1 (PP1) (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Santa Cruz, CA), PKA (Pharmingen, San Jose, CA) and evaluated in cytosol and mitochondrial fractions.
###end p 72
###begin title 73
Glucose-stimulated mitochondrial membrane potential response in beta-cell lines and dispersed islet beta-cells
###end title 73
###begin p 74
###xml 90 94 84 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Rosen1">[52]</xref>
###xml 61 65 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
beta-cells were dispersed from islets of control or betaIRKO mice as previously described [52]. Dispersed islet cells were cultured in RMPI medium with 10% FBS, antibiotics, 5.5 mM glucose on microscope coverslips and allowed to recover for 48 h. The dispersed islet cells were stained with insulin and glucagon antibodies to confirm purity of beta-cells. Cells were stained with 10 nM TMRE (Molecular probe, Carlsbad, CA) for 45 min prior to glucose stimulation. Fluorescence intensity of cells was initially recorded in the basal condition followed by stimulation with 18 mM glucose. The altered fluorescence of treated cells was recorded under a confocal microscope (Zeiss, Germany) within 10 min. The fluorescence intensity in basal and treated conditions was quantitated by NIH Image J software (n = 60 to 80 cells). The mitochondrial membrane potential change was calculated by averaging fluorescence intensities before and after stimulation.
###end p 74
###begin title 75
Mitochondrial glucokinase activity
###end title 75
###begin p 76
###xml 119 123 119 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Niswender1">[53]</xref>
###xml 343 344 331 332 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 384 390 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Mitochondrial glucokinase activity was measured using a glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PDH)-coupled enzyme assay [53]. The total NADPH generated reflects glucokinase activity in mitochondria. Briefly, approximately3 mg of isolated mitochondria was resuspended in reaction buffer containing 50 mM triethanolamine hydrochloride, 20 mM MgCl2, 100 mM KCl, 1 mM dithiothreitol, 0.1% bovine serum albumin, 10 mM ATP, 1 mM NADP and 4 microg/ml glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (Sigma, St. Louis, MO). Then, 90 mM glucose was added to initiate the reaction and monitored by absorbance at 340 nm for 20 min at RT. In order to decrease NADPH background generated from mitochondrial respiration, all reactions were performed in the presence of mitochondrial respiration inhibitors (4 microM Rotenone, 100 nM Antimycin A and 2 microg/ml Oligomycin).
###end p 76
###begin title 77
Electron microscopy and determination of mitochondrial number, size and volume density
###end title 77
###begin p 78
###xml 670 674 634 638 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Bugger1">[54]</xref>
###xml 306 311 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Islets were fixed with 2.5% glutaraldehyde at RT for 2 h. Ultra-thin sections (approximately60 nm) were cut and stained with uranyl acetate and lead citrate before being examined under JOEL Transmission Electron Microscope (Tokyo, Japan). At least five islets were chosen randomly from control or betaIRKO mouse islets and approximately4 randomly selected beta-cells from each islet were photographed. At least 15 islet beta-cells were examined from control or betaIRKO islets for number and morphology of the mitochondria. Mitochondrial volume density and number were analyzed by stereology in a blinded fashion using the point counting method, as described previously [54]. Five representative images were analyzed per genotype, and four grids per image were analyzed. Average size of the mitochondria was calculated by dividing mitochondrial volume density by mitochondrial number.
###end p 78
###begin title 79
Re-expression of insulin receptor in betaIRKO cells
###end title 79
###begin p 80
###xml 502 502 502 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"/>
###xml 738 738 734 734 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"/>
###xml 165 170 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 477 483 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Retroviral-packaging  (Phoenix cells carrying the retroviral vector was kindly provided by C. R. Kahn, MD (Joslin Diabetes Center, Boston, MA). The vector encoding human insulin receptor B was obtained from I. Leibiger, Ph.D (Karolinska Institute, Stockholm, Sweden). Briefly, empty vector or vector expressing the insulin receptor was transfected using lipofectamine (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) in O cells. The day after transfection, DMEM medium, supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 50microg/ml gentamicin (Gibco, Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) was replaced. Supernatant was collected 48 h post-transfection and subjected to ultracentrifugation using an SW-28 rotor at 20,000 rpm. Twenty four h after transfection, 400 microg/ml hygromycin (Gibco, Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) was added to select the cells in which the expression vector was successfully integrated.
###end p 80
###begin title 81
Measurement of Mitochondrial Respiration
###end title 81
###begin p 82
###xml 128 132 128 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007983-Amiott1">[55]</xref>
###xml 211 212 211 212 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 750 757 744 751 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in situ</italic>
Oxygen consumption rates (OCR) were measured in whole cells by using a fiber-optic oxygen sensing probe as previously described [55] or the Seahorse XF24. Briefly, cells were suspended at a concentration of 2x106 cells/ml by a magnetic stirrer at 37degreesC in a volume of 1 ml of buffer TD (137 mM NaCl, 5 mM KCl, 0.7 mM Na2HPO4, 25 mM Tris-Cl, pH 7.4) and transferred to the oxygen chamber. Endogenous (basal) respiration was measured for 1 min. Glucose (final concentration 25 mM) or the uncoupler 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP) was injected into the chamber (83 mM final), and stimulated rates of oxygen consumption were determined. The Seahorse machine (Seahorse Bioscience, Billerica, MA) was used to measure oxygen consumption rate in adherent cells in situ without trypsinization.
###end p 82
###begin title 83
ATP assay
###end title 83
###begin p 84
Cells were seeded in 12-well plates and incubated for 36-48 h before incubation for an additional 16 h with 2 mM glucose in DMEM with 0.1% BSA, followed by 2 mM glucose in KRB buffer for one h. The cells were then either incubated in KRB buffer containing 2 mM glucose (basal concentration) or KRB buffer containing 25 mM glucose (stimulatory concentration) for 30 min. ATP was then extracted with 0.5% TCA solution for 15 min at 4degreesC. An aliquot of the TCA extracted samples were diluted 20X with 0.1 M Tris-HCl, pH 7.75 buffer just before ATP measurement with the ENLITEN(R) ATP Assay System Bioluminescence Detection Kit (Promega, Madison, WI) according to the manufacturer's recommendation. Total protein concentration was measured using the BCA protein Assay (Pierce, Rockford, IL), and ATP generated was normalized to total protein.
###end p 84
