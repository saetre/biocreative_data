Pleural Tuberculosis in Patients with Early HIV Infection Is Associated with Increased TNF-Alpha Expression and Necrosis in Granulomas
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 571 578 563 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in situ</italic>
###xml 154 157 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 255 263 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 357 360 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 396 404 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 604 612 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 689 692 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 702 710 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 892 895 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 916 924 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Although granulomas may be an essential host response against persistent antigens, they are also associated with immunopathology. We investigated whether HIV co-infection affects histopathological appearance and cytokine profiles of pleural granulomas in patients with active pleural tuberculosis (TB). Granulomas were investigated in pleural biopsies from HIV positive and negative TB pleuritis patients. Granulomas were characterised as necrotic or non-necrotic, graded histologically and investigated for the mRNA expression of IL-12, IFN-gamma, TNF-alpha and IL-4 by in situ hybridisation. In all TB patients a mixed Th1/Th2 profile was noted. Necrotic granulomas were more evident in HIV positive patients with a clear association between TNF-alpha and necrosis. This study demonstrates immune dysregulation which may include TNF-alpha-mediated immunopathology at the site of disease in HIV infected pleural TB patients.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 187 190 187 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Algood1">[1]</xref>
###xml 192 195 192 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Thurlbeck1">[2]</xref>
###xml 453 456 453 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Hasleton1">[3]</xref>
###xml 577 580 577 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Ellner1">[4]</xref>
###xml 789 815 789 815 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 817 820 817 820 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 947 950 947 950 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Roper1">[5]</xref>
###xml 129 134 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 668 696 <span type="species:ncbi:12721">human immunodeficiency virus</span>
###xml 698 701 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 789 815 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 817 820 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
Granulomas represent a distinctive pattern of chronic inflammation that involves adaptive immunity, and are the hallmark of many human diseases of significance, such as tuberculosis (TB) [1], [2]. An important gap in current knowledge is an understanding of how host resistance to TB disease is mediated. The pleura is often involved in active TB disease and this can take on the form of effusions, tuberculous empyema or obliterative fibrous pleuritis [3]. The release of mycobacterial antigens into the pleural space, eliciting a delayed-type hypersensitivity (DTH) reaction [4], may play an important role in the pathogenesis of tuberculous pleurisy. Pleural TB in human immunodeficiency virus (HIV)-negative subjects has been taken to represent more of a protective immune response to Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb) than other forms of the disease due to the fact that a high proportion of individuals can recover without antibiotic therapy [5].
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 102 105 102 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 105 108 105 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Saunders1">[6]</xref>
###xml 102 105 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
Granuloma formation is considered to be an essential component of protective immune responses against Mtb[6]. The formation of the granuloma is firmly linked to the DTH reaction, which is a form of adaptive cell-mediated immunity (CMI) that is mediated by activated T lymphocytes and their cytokines. Epithelioid macrophages are present in the centre of the granuloma with some multi-nucleated giant cells and this central area is surrounded by lymphocytes. Dendritic cells may also be present.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 99 102 99 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Braun1">[7]</xref>
###xml 104 107 104 107 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Schluger1">[8]</xref>
###xml 187 190 187 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 540 543 540 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Clerici1">[9]</xref>
###xml 545 549 545 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Maggi1">[10]</xref>
###xml 551 555 551 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Vyakarnam1">[11]</xref>
###xml 616 619 616 619 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 682 686 682 686 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Imperiali1">[12]</xref>
###xml 688 692 688 692 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Lawn1">[13]</xref>
###xml 694 698 694 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Oyaizu1">[14]</xref>
###xml 700 704 700 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Polyak1">[15]</xref>
###xml 793 797 793 797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Mukadi1">[16]</xref>
###xml 924 927 924 927 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 1068 1072 1068 1072 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Garrait1">[17]</xref>
###xml 1074 1078 1074 1078 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Goletti1">[18]</xref>
###xml 0 3 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 13 19 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">people</span>
###xml 125 128 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 187 190 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
###xml 369 372 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 557 560 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 616 619 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
###xml 741 744 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 822 825 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 924 927 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
###xml 946 949 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
HIV infected people develop TB at a rate of 10% per year, rather than the norm of 10% per lifetime [7], [8], suggesting that HIV infection may affect the ability of granulomas to contain Mtb. It has been hypothesized that quantitative and qualitative T-helper responses may impact on AIDS pathogenesis. Although the supposed role of modulation of T helper responses in HIV pathogenesis remains controversial, a switch in the predominant response from Th1 to Th2 and the production of associated cytokines may facilitate disease progression [9], [10], [11]. HIV coinfection weakens the granulomatous host response to Mtb due to insufficient macrophage, T cell and cytokine responses [12], [13], [14], [15]. TB is often an early occurrence in HIV, even before any reduction in CD4+ T cell count [16], and the trafficking of HIV infected, and therefore dysfunctional, cells into the granuloma may lead to granuloma disruption. Mtb can also modulate HIV replication by generating a cytokine microenvironment favouring viral infection and spread in local mononuclear cells [17], [18].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 245 249 237 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Barnes1">[19]</xref>
###xml 315 319 307 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Flynn1">[20]</xref>
###xml 321 325 313 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Flynn2">[21]</xref>
###xml 386 390 374 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Rhodes1">[22]</xref>
###xml 402 406 386 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Bekker1">[23]</xref>
###xml 418 422 402 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Ehlers1">[24]</xref>
###xml 516 520 500 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Seah1">[25]</xref>
###xml 522 526 506 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-vanCrevel1">[26]</xref>
There is a delicate and complicated interplay between different cytokines and there are numerous reports of beneficial and detrimental effects of individual cytokines. High levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines, including IFN-gamma and TNF-alpha [19] are found in TB effusions but, in addition to protective effects [20], [21], there are also reports of detrimental effects of IFN-gamma [22], TNF-alpha [23], and IL-12 [24], especially if present at high concentrations. The role of IL-4 in the immunopathology of TB [25], [26] has been established and may be due to the suppression of the Th1 cytokines IL-12, IFN-gamma and TNF-alpha.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 339 346 331 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in situ</italic>
###xml 18 23 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 236 244 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 439 442 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 466 474 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 554 557 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
Understanding the human host response is important, as this would assist in the development of new vaccines and immunomodulatory adjuvants for the prevention or treatment of TB. We chose to investigate the Th1/Th2 profile in pleural TB patients by measuring the mRNA expression levels of the cytokines IL-12, IFN-gamma, TNF-alpha and IL-4 in situ. In addition we compared the cytokine profiles of necrotic and non-necrotic granulomas from HIV-positive and -negative patients in order to describe the association between cytokine production, necrosis and HIV positivity.
###end p 8

Materials and Methods
###end title 9
###begin title 10
###xml 14 26 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
Study design, participants and tissue specimens
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 722 726 722 726 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Lynch1">[27]</xref>
###xml 14 22 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 100 103 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 134 137 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 162 168 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">people</span>
###xml 170 173 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 199 207 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 218 221 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 231 239 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 331 339 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 440 448 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 795 803 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 860 868 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1016 1028 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
A total of 12 patients with pleural TB were recruited from Tygerberg Academic Hospital, including 6 HIV sero-negative (group 1) and 6 HIV sero-positive (group 2) people. HIV serology was done on all patients by ELISA. HIV positive patients were in the early stage of the disease and had no opportunistic infections besides TB. All patients presented with symptoms of pleuritis and had radiological confirmation of the effusion. None of the patients had had TB previously nor were any on treatment at the time of the study. Pleural biopsy tissue was obtained for diagnostic purposes. The diagnosis of TB was confirmed by the presence of granulomatous inflammation and Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN) positive bacilli in biopsy material [27]. Standard, fixed-dose anti-TB treatment was administered before the patients were referred to TB clinics. No follow-up data on these patients was available. Approval was granted by the Ethics Committee of the University of Stellenbosch. Written, informed consent was obtained from all the participants.

###begin title 12
Preparation of Riboprobes
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 55 59 55 59 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Fenhalls1">[28]</xref>
###xml 61 65 61 65 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Fenhalls2">[29]</xref>
###xml 131 135 131 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Hoefakker1">[30]</xref>
###xml 137 141 137 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Myatt1">[31]</xref>
###xml 564 571 564 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0004228-t001">Table 1</xref>
This was performed essentially as described previously [28], [29] using PCR conditions and primer sequences as published elsewhere [30], [31]. Briefly, peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were isolated from a healthy volunteer and stimulated for 18 hours with phytohemagglutinin (PHA). Total RNA was extracted and quantified and shown to be undegraded on a 1% agarose gel. cDNA was prepared using the Titan system (Boehringer Mannheim). The PCR products were cloned into the vector pGEM7Zf (Promega) and sequencing of the clones confirmed the DNA sequence (Table 1) and ascertained the orientation of the PCR product in order to synthesize sense and antisense riboprobes. After digestion the fragments were separated by electrophoresis on 1% agarose gels and extracted. RNA polymerases were used to transcribe digoxigenin-labelled RNA and labelling of the probes was confirmed by Northern blot analysis.

###begin title 15
###xml 8 15 8 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In Situ</italic>
RNA-RNA In Situ Hybridisation and Dual Labelling
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 55 59 55 59 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Fenhalls1">[28]</xref>
###xml 61 65 61 65 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Fenhalls2">[29]</xref>
This was performed essentially as described previously [28], [29] on paraffin-embedded lung tissue.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 844 849 <span type="species:ncbi:9940">sheep</span>
Briefly, consecutive 5 microm sections were applied to RNase-free slides previously coated with aminopropyltriethoxysilane (5 mug/ml, Sigma Aldrich), deparaffinized in xylene, rehydrated through graded ethanols and diethyl pyrocarbonate-treated water and incubated in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). The sections were treated with proteinase K in Tris-HCl-EDTA, washed with PBS, refixed in paraformaldehyde and acetylated in triethanolamine-acetic anhydride. The slides were then rinsed, dehydrated and air-dried before hybridization. Sections were incubated in a mixture of dextran sulphate, Tris-HCl, Denhardt's solution, EDTA, dithiothreitol, herring sperm DNA, tRNA and deionized formamide. Sections were hybridized for 18 hours in a humidified chamber at 50degreesC, washed in saline sodium citrate, incubated in Tris-HCl-NaCl containing sheep serum and then washed. Thereafter, sections were incubated with antidigoxigenin antibody conjugated to alkyline phosphatase (Boehringer Mannheim) and the signal detected with BCIP-NBT-INT (Dako). After the appearance of a brown colour slides were counterstained with Mayer's hematoxylin (Sigma Aldrich), rinsed in distilled water, mounted with Dako Faramount and viewed under a light microscope.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 163 167 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 223 227 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 233 238 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 259 263 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
For dual labelling, slides were rinsed and non-specific protein blocked with milk powder and Triton-X 100 in PBS. Sections were incubated with antibody diluted in goat serum, washed and labelled with secondary biotinylated goat anti-mouse antibody diluted in goat serum and Triton-X 100. Sections were washed again, incubated with streptavidin conjugated to alkyline phosphatase, washed in PBS and incubated with a solution of fast red (Vector Laboratories). Slides were counterstained with hematoxylin and mounted with Dako Faramount.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
Photography and Assessment of Slides
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 342 350 342 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004228-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 535 543 535 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004228-g002">Figure 2</xref>
Images were captured using a Zeiss light microscope with light parameters optimized for the actin stained slide. These settings were kept constant in order to maintain comparability between slides. Control slides were added parallel to each staining procedure to show that the positive colour was specific and that the mRNA was not degraded (Figure 1). The number of necrotic and non-necrotic granulomas were determined by H+E stain. A map of each section of the granuloma was created by photographing the slides at x25 magnification (Figure 2). Each granuloma was subsequently numbered. ###begin title 58
Discussion
###end title 58
###begin p 59
###xml 351 355 351 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Jalapathy1">[32]</xref>
###xml 560 564 556 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Lucas1">[33]</xref>
###xml 650 655 642 647 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 675 679 667 671 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-deNoronha1">[34]</xref>
###xml 985 989 977 981 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Lucas1">[33]</xref>
###xml 119 122 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 204 212 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Patients</span>
###xml 311 314 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 482 485 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 495 503 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 616 619 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 632 640 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
This work characterised cytokine expression at the site of disease during the granulomatous response in pleural TB, in HIV infected and uninfected individuals and in necrotic and non-necrotic granulomas. Patients with pleural TB presented with a mixed Th1/Th2 profile and this contradicts a characterisation of HIV negative pleural TB as Th1 dominant [32]. The main findings were a strong presence of TNF-alpha positive cells in necrotic granulomas, particularly in granulomas from HIV positive patients, where increased necrosis has previously been described [33]. Although we found high levels of TNF-alpha in our HIV positive TB patients, Noronha et al found the opposite [34]. This discrepancy may be due to different stages of immunosuppression in the two studies. In the present study granuloma morphology was intact, which suggests relative immune competence, whereas poorly formed or absence of granulomas, as seen in the Noronha study, may indicate advanced immunosuppression [33]. However, the presence of high expression levels of TNF-alpha mRNA as seen in our study does not necessarily equate to increased presence of TNF-alpha protein and may even be representative of a failed attempt to induce cytokine production.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 171 175 171 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Barnes1">[19]</xref>
###xml 177 181 177 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Barnes2">[35]</xref>
The investigation of immune responses at the site of disease is extremely valuable because cytokine responses do not necessarily mimic those found in the peripheral blood [19], [35]. This may be due to a redistribution of antigen specific cells to the site of disease (compartmentalization), where their effects on the eventual outcome of infection may be crucial.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 80 83 76 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 182 186 178 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Bekker1">[23]</xref>
###xml 188 192 184 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Roach1">[36]</xref>
###xml 324 328 320 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Fayyazi1">[37]</xref>
###xml 330 334 326 330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Keane1">[38]</xref>
###xml 365 369 361 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Fayyazi1">[37]</xref>
###xml 424 428 416 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Carswell1">[39]</xref>
###xml 430 434 422 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Rath1">[40]</xref>
###xml 519 523 511 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Viorritto1">[41]</xref>
###xml 1050 1053 1042 1045 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 1098 1102 1090 1094 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-RydellTormanen1">[42]</xref>
###xml 1144 1147 1136 1139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 1336 1340 1328 1332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Lee1">[43]</xref>
###xml 80 83 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
###xml 356 361 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1050 1053 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
###xml 1144 1147 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
Although the presence of TNF-alpha is beneficial in the immune response against Mtb, and is necessary for granuloma formation, it does cause immunopathology such as caseous necrosis [23], [36]. This is probably a consequence of apoptosis of infected macrophages, epithelioid cells and activated T cells within the granuloma [37], [38] as has been shown in human TB [37]. The link between the apoptotic activity of TNF-alpha [39], [40] and the formation of caseous necrosis may be due to secondary necrosis (reviewed in [41]). Whereas apoptosis is a strictly regulated process, and leads to the containment of cell debris, necrosis is unregulated and results in the spilling of intracellular contents. The processes may become linked when cell uptake is disrupted and the rate of cell death exceeds the rate at which macrophages are able to phagocytose apoptotic material. This leads to the impaired uptake of apoptotic cells and during this delay necrosis may develop. Thus, although primary necrosis may be very rare during lung inflammation due to Mtb infection, secondary necrosis may be common [42]. This mechanism may be compounded by the Mtb-mediated lysis of macrophages which triggers the macrophage cell death pathway. This not only allows dissemination of the bacilli but also contributes to the formation of necrotic lesions [43].
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 69 72 69 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 140 144 140 144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Fairbairn1">[44]</xref>
###xml 146 150 146 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Hirsch1">[45]</xref>
###xml 257 261 257 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Das1">[46]</xref>
###xml 263 267 263 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Fayyazi1">[37]</xref>
###xml 419 423 419 423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Uhal1">[47]</xref>
###xml 442 445 442 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 587 591 583 587 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Condos1">[48]</xref>
###xml 593 597 589 593 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Tan1">[49]</xref>
###xml 69 72 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
###xml 442 445 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
Although apoptosis is an important host defence mechanism to deprive Mtb of its intracellular sanctuary and inhibit its growth and survival [44], [45], it also facilitates mycobacterial dissemination and may therefore be orchestrated by the bacillus itself [46], [37]. It is unclear whether apoptosis favours the host or the pathogen as impaired apoptosis also maintains granulomas by preventing T cell clonal deletion [47]. The viability of Mtb after Fas ligand or TNF-alpha mediated apoptosis of immune cells is controversial with both reduced and unchanged bacterial numbers reported [48], [49].
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 105 109 105 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Fenhalls1">[28]</xref>
###xml 111 115 111 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Fenhalls2">[29]</xref>
###xml 830 834 810 814 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Carswell1">[39]</xref>
###xml 958 962 934 938 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Gately1">[50]</xref>
###xml 1088 1092 1060 1064 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Jouanguy1">[51]</xref>
###xml 1278 1282 1246 1250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Zhang1">[52]</xref>
###xml 210 218 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1251 1259 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Previous studies that investigated correlations between granuloma histology and cytokine mRNA expression [28], [29] suggested that the presence of IL-4 may not be an indicator of poor prognosis in pulmonary TB patients but may rather be an integral feature of tuberculous granuloma formation and may limit tissue damage. Their results showed that IL-4 positive granulomas tended to be non-necrotic whereas all necrotic granulomas were TNF-alpha positive and negative or weakly positive for IL-4 and IFN-gamma. Although we cannot extrapolate from these findings to a causative relationship between TNF-alpha and necrosis, it appears likely that TNF-alpha may promote necrosis. The caseous necrosis appears to be a consequence of apoptosis of infected macrophages and activated T cells and TNF-alpha is a known inducer of apoptosis [39]. An essential role for IL-12 and IFN-gamma in protective immunity to mycobacterial infection has been previously described [50]. Furthermore individuals with defective IL-12 or IFN-gamma receptors are susceptible to disseminated mycobacterial infection [51] and decreased IFN-gamma production by peripheral blood mononuclear leukocytes has been shown to correlate with decreased IL-12 receptor subunits B1 and B2 in patients with tuberculosis [52].
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 101 105 101 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Wigginton1">[53]</xref>
###xml 167 170 167 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 299 302 291 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 458 462 446 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Dorman1">[54]</xref>
###xml 464 468 452 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Roach1">[36]</xref>
###xml 470 474 458 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Wigginton1">[53]</xref>
###xml 794 798 774 778 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Barnes1">[19]</xref>
###xml 800 804 780 784 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Subramanyam1">[55]</xref>
###xml 806 810 786 790 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Wigginton1">[53]</xref>
###xml 33 41 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 146 151 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 167 170 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
###xml 299 302 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
###xml 546 549 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
The cytokine pattern observed in patients with pleural TB reflects a model by Wigginton and Kirscher [53] that was designed to predict CMI during human infection with Mtb. We found increased IFN-gamma expression in necrotic granulomas and, although, like TNF-alpha, it is crucial in defence against Mtb, high effector cytokine levels during chronic phases of disease may contribute to immunopathology, especially in the presence of high TNF-alpha expression [54], [36], [53]. The strong correlation between TNF-alpha and IFN-gamma expression and HIV positivity supports the concept that cytokine overproduction may represent an attempt to compensate for the failure of other CD4 effector T cell functions, including proper granuloma formation and the antigen-specific activation of macrophages [19], [55], [53].
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 292 296 288 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-HernandezPando1">[56]</xref>
###xml 298 302 294 298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Muller1">[57]</xref>
###xml 459 463 455 459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Wigginton1">[53]</xref>
###xml 595 599 587 591 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Dheda1">[58]</xref>
###xml 765 769 753 757 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Dheda2">[59]</xref>
###xml 771 775 759 763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Dheda3">[60]</xref>
###xml 1105 1110 1093 1098 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1110 1114 1098 1102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Dheda3">[60]</xref>
###xml 740 743 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 756 764 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1165 1168 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
By contrast, non-necrotic granulomas with a high number of IL-12 positive cells also expressed high levels of IL-4 and this was not associated with necrosis presumably because Th2 cytokines mediate local tissue inflammation and necrosis through their effect on TNF-alpha induced cytotoxicity [56], [57]. IL-4 may therefore provide a regulatory mechanism to antagonise necrosis-inducing factors but in doing so may also shift infection from latency to disease [53]. This is in contrast to other studies which have found that IL-4 works in synergy with TNF-alpha to increase necrosis (reviewed in [58]). We did not measure IL-4's natural antagonistic splice variant, IL-4delta2, which has been found to be expressed in the lungs of TB and TB-HIV co-infected patients [59], [60]. It is therefore possible that the upregulation of IL-4 seen in non-necrotic granulomas may be due, in part, to an upregulation of the splice variant. However, we still believe that IL-4 plays a predominant role because an upregulation of the splice variant would have facilitated an increase in inflammation and necrosis. Dheda et al[60] have also highlighted that, during infection with HIV, the switch from a Th1 to a Th2 response may not necessary be responsible for the progression to AIDS in the absence of opportunistic infections. However, when co-infected with TB, IL-4 expression is upregulated and this may allow an increase in viral replication.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 253 257 245 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Hart1">[61]</xref>
###xml 259 263 251 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Trinchieri1">[62]</xref>
Interestingly very few necrotic granulomas expressed a high level of IFN-gamma-positive cells in the presence of high IL-12 expression. This may suggest a breakdown in the regulatory function of IL-12 which usually regulates the expression of IFN-gamma [61], [62]. Non-necrotic granulomas tended to express low levels of all cytokines which suggests that a lower cytokine presence is less destructive.
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 476 480 476 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Dheda1">[58]</xref>
###xml 802 807 794 799 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 807 811 799 803 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004228-Dheda1">[58]</xref>
###xml 442 448 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">person</span>
###xml 452 458 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">person</span>
In summary, intracellular bacteria that can survive within macrophages lead to granuloma formation, which benefits the host by controlling local and systemic dissemination of pathogens but facilitates destructive pulmonary pathology. In TB, this manifests as caseous necrosis with tissue destruction and subsequent fibrosis. This facilitates disease transmission and therefore blocking any mechanism responsible for necrosis would also limit person-to-person disease transfer [58]. High levels of TNF-alpha suggests a poor prognosis in TB because it is associated with necrosis and dysregulated granuloma formation. TNF-alpha is required for granuloma formation in order to contain and/or remove the antigen but its continued presence results in the development of immunopathology as reviewed by Dheda et al[58]. IL-4 may present an integral feature of immune regulation as a pure Th1 response is not necessarily beneficial during granulomatous disease but instead the Th1/Th2 ratio should be finely balanced depending on the stage of disease. An early Th1 response is necessary to clear the antigen during the acute stage of infection whereas during chronic disease an abrogation of the Th1 response is necessary to minimise tissue damage caused by necrosis and fibrosis.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 504 507 500 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mtb</italic>
###xml 142 145 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
###xml 504 507 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mtb</span>
In conclusion, this study reports on the expression of Th1 and Th2 immune phenotypes at the site of disease in TB pleuritis. It is shown that HIV positivity results in increased necrosis associated with elevated TNF-alpha mRNA expression. Future studies should also investigate the expression of other cytokines, chemokines and their receptors to allow a more comprehensive understanding of factors that determine the fine balance between protective and immunopathological responses after infection with Mtb.
