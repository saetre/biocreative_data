###begin article-title 0
###xml 86 91 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Suppression of IkappaBalpha increases the expression of matrix metalloproteinase-2 in human ciliary muscle cells
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
The first two authors contributed equally to this work.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Purpose
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 273 278 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
An increase of matrix metalloproteinase-2 (MMP-2) has been found to improve outflow through the uveoscleral pathway. This experiment was designed to test whether reduction of inhibitor of nuclear factor kappa B alpha (IkappaBalpha) levels could enhance MMP-2 expression in human ciliary muscle (HCM) cells in vitro.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 81 93 81 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 377 389 357 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
The small interfering RNA (siRNA) targeting inhibitor of nuclear factor kappa B (IkappaBalpha) was transfected into HCM cells. The mRNA and protein levels of IkappaBalpha, nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-kappaB)p65, MMP-2, tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinase-2 (TIMP-2), and membrane-type 1 matrix metalloproteinase (MT1-MMP) in HCM cells were examined 24 h, 48 h, and 72 h after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection by real-time reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) and western blot. The activation of NF-kappaBp65 was determined through the translocation of NF-kappaBp65 by fluorescence microscope. Gelatin zymography was used to detect the secretion and activity of MMP-2.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 62 74 62 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 204 216 188 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 221 227 197 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TIMP-2</italic>
###xml 549 561 517 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 586 591 546 551 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">MMP-2</italic>
###xml 596 603 556 563 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">MT1-MMP</italic>
###xml 799 811 751 755 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 925 926 865 866 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">(</italic>
###xml 1088 1100 1024 1028 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
Real-time RT-PCR and western blot showed that transfection of IkappaBalpha siRNA led to gradual downregulation of IkappaBalpha and TIMP-2 both at the mRNA and protein level after 24 h, 48 h and 72 h. The IkappaBalpha and TIMP-2 mRNA levels decreased 92.7%+/-1.6% and 70.3%+/-13.1%, respectively, and the protein levels were reduced 87.3%+/-2.0% and 62.9%+/-0.8% (p<0.01), respectively, when compared to the control 72 h after siRNA transfection. Conversely, the MMP-2 and MT1-MMP mRNA and protein levels increased in the time-dependent manner after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection. The MMP-2 and MT1-MMP mRNA levels increased 178%+/-4.6% and 165%+/-8.2%, respectively, while protein levels were raised to 162%+/-3.7% and 157.6%+/-5.7% (p<0.01), respectively, when compared to the control 72 h after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection. Although no obvious changes were seen in either mRNA or protein levels of total NF-kappaBp65 (p>0.05), the protein level of NF-kappaBp65 increased dramatically in the nucleus as revealed by western blot and fluorescence staining 24 h, 48 h, and 72 h after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection. Moreover, gelatin zymography indicated that the secretion and activity of MMP-2 in treated cells were higher than those in the control cells. The maximum increases of pro-MMP-2 and active-MMP-2 were 172%+/-15% and 151%+/-14% (p<0.01), respectively, when compared to the control at the experiment's conclusion 72 h after siRNA transfection.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusions
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 53 65 53 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 171 183 159 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
Expression and activity of MMP-2 was enhanced by the IkappaBalpha siRNA in HCM cells through the activation of the NF-kappaB signaling pathway. Our results suggested that IkappaBalpha may therefore be a potential target for controlling the uveoscleral outflow pathway in glaucoma.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Introduction
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 26 27 26 27 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 41 42 41 42 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 432 433 428 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 434 435 430 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 564 565 560 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 566 567 562 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 33 39 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
In glaucomatous primates [1] and humans [2], prostaglandin (PG)F2alpha has been shown to lower the intraocular pressure (IOP), which results from increased uveoscleral outflow. Although the precise mechanism by which prostaglandins improve uveoscleral outflow is not fully understood, two possible mechanisms that have been studied are relaxation of the ciliary muscle and remodeling the extracellular matrix of the ciliary muscle [3,4]. Researchers have attributed the increase in uveoscleral outflow to a group of enzymes named matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) [5,6].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 232 233 232 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 436 437 436 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 438 439 438 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 784 786 784 786 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 942 944 942 944 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 1089 1091 1089 1091 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 1092 1094 1092 1094 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 1270 1272 1270 1272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
MMPs are zinc-dependent endopeptidases that play a key role in regulating the modulations of the extracellular matrix (ECM). These molecules are secreted much like proenzymes and become activated by proteolytic cleavage truncation [7]. MMP-2, a member of the family of MMPs, has been shown to have proteinase activity in the degradation of multiple ECM proteins such as type I collagen, laminin, fibronectin, and several proteoglycans [8,9]. MMP-2 is secreted as an inactive proenzyme, pro-MMP-2. The activation of pro-MMP-2 requires its cell surface localization and cleavage by cell membrane-type 1 matrix metalloproteinase (MT1-MMP), which controls a variety of physiologic and pathological processes through the proteolytic degradation of extracellular or transmembrane proteins [10]. Tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinase-2 (TIMP-2), the specific inhibitor of MMP-2, is also involved in this process by forming a receptor for pro-MMP-2 [11]. It has been confirmed that the extracellular spaces separating ciliary muscle fibers of the uveoscleral outflow pathway contain abundant ECM [12,13]. Thus, increased degradation of ciliary muscle ECM by MMP-2 could remodel the ECM among the ciliary muscle bundles and decrease the hydraulic resistance to uveoscleral flow [14].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 233 235 229 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 236 238 232 234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 450 452 434 436 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 1041 1043 993 995 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 1044 1046 996 998 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
Nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-kappaB) is one of the major transcription factors that plays a critical role in the gene regulation of multiple cellular processes such as inflammation, immune responses, cell proliferation, and apoptosis [15,16]. Five members were identified among the NF-kappaB family of transcription factors: v-rel reticuloendotheliosis viral oncogene homolog (Rel)A (p65), RelB, NF-kappaB1 (p50/p105), NF-kappaB2(p52/p100), and c-Rel [17]. In most cells, NF-kappaB forms various homodimers as well as heterodimers, mainly between the p65 and p50 proteins. The NF-kappaB dimers can form complexes in the cytoplasm with the inhibitor of nuclear factor kappa B alpha (IkappaBalpha), which masks the NF-kappaB nuclear localization sequence and results in latent inactivity. However, upon activation by extracellular stimuli or other transcriptional factors, IkappaBalpha is phosphorylated and degraded while NF-kappaB rapidly translocates to the nucleus where it activates the transcription expression of several important genes [18,19].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 228 230 216 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 251 253 239 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 279 281 267 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 317 319 305 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r23">23</xref>
###xml 181 186 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 233 238 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 256 262 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 408 413 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Several studies have documented that the suppression of IkappaBalpha could induce NF-kappaB nuclear translocation and consequently promote the increase of MMP-2 in several types of human cells including Ewing sarcoma EW7 cells [20], human skin cells [21], murine melanoma cells [22], and HT1080 (fibrosarcoma) cells [23]. However, the molecular mechanism of inducing the secretion and activation of MMP-2 in human ciliary muscle (HCM) cells still remains unclear. This question has led us to investigate whether the IkappaB/NF-kappaB signaling pathway influences MMP-2 expression in HCM cells in vitro.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Methods
###end title 15
###begin title 16
Primary HCM cells
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 111 113 111 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
###xml 376 378 376 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 577 583 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 630 636 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
Normal primary HCM cells were established and cultured as described previously by the Declaration of Helsinki [24]. HCM cells were prepared from eight donors with no history of eye diseases (five males, three females) around 18-24 h after death and were obtained from Zhongshan Ophthalmic Center (Sun Yat-sen University, Guangzhou, China) using a procedure described earlier [25]. The cells used in the present experiments were passaged four to six times to minimize the dedifferentiation that might occur in late passage. The HCM cells were further identified with monoclonal rabbit anti-smooth muscle alpha-actin and monoclonal rabbit anti-Desmin (Sigma, St. Louis, MO) by immunocytochemical staining.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Fluorescence immunostaining
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 814 826 806 810 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 275 279 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 285 291 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 645 651 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 721 725 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 731 737 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 910 916 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 941 945 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 951 957 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
HCM cells were grown on coverslips in six-well plates. After 24 h, the cells were washed three times with PBS, fixed with 4% formaldehyde, blocked in fresh 1% BSA/PBS for 1 h, and stained with monoclonal anti-smooth muscle alpha-actin (1:100; Sigma) and rhodamine-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG (1:50; Sigma). The nuclei of the cells were counterstained with 4',6'-diamidino-2-phenylindole dihydrochloride (DAPI, 1:1000; Sigma). Control cells were performed using PBS instead of the primary antibody. Stained cells were viewed with the Axioplan2 fluorescence microscope (Carl Zeiss, Oberkochen, Germany). HCM cells were stained with monoclonal rabbit anti-NF-kappaBp65 (1:50; BD, Franklin Lakes, NJ) and FITC-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG (1:500; BD) by the procedure described above 24 h, 48 h, and 72 h after IkappaBalpha small interfering RNA (siRNA) transfection. HCM cells were stained with monoclonal rabbit anti-Desmin (1:100) and goat anti-rabbit IgG (1:50) according to the instructions included with the SP-9001 Histostain-Plus Kit (Beijing Zhongshan Gold Bridge, Beijing, China).
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Preparation and transfection of siRNA
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 39 51 39 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 93 105 85 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 571 572 555 556 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sup>
###xml 650 662 634 638 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
The siRNA sequences used for silencing IkappaBalpha (GenBank ) were designed, and Cy3-labled IkappaBalpha siRNA was synthesized by Guangzhou Ribobio (Guangzhou, China).The sequences of siRNAs were as follows: sense, 5'-CUC CGA GAC UUU CGA GGA A dTdT-3'; antisense, 5'-UUC CUC GAA AGU CUC GG AG dTdT-3'. For nonsense control siRNA, we used irrelevant siRNA with random nucleotides and no known specificity (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). Its sequences were as follows: sense, 5'-UUC UCC GAA CGU GUC ACG UdTdT-3'; antisense, 5'-ACG UGA CAC GUU CGG AG A AdTdT-3'. HCM cells (3x104) were plated in a six-well plate, incubated for 24 h, and transfected with a IkappaBalpha-specific siRNA duplex (50 nM final concentration) or the nonsense control siRNA (50 nM) using the Lipofectamine 2000 reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) according to the manufacturer's instructions.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Transfection efficiency of siRNA
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 83 95 83 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>

The cells were washed twice with PBS 24 h, 48 h, and 72 h after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection and then viewed by the Axioplan2 fluorescence microscope (Carl Zeiss). Transfection efficiency was determined by counting the fraction of fluorescing cells in 10 fields of view.

Total RNA was extracted from HCM cells with an RNeasy kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) 24 h, 48 h, and 72 h after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection. The extracted RNA was pretreated with RNAase-free DNase, and 0.2 mug of RNA from each sample was used for cDNA synthesis primed with random hexamers. Real-time reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) was performed on six genes, NF-kappaBp65, IkappaBalpha, MMP-2, TIMP-2, MT1-MMP, and GAPDH (internal control). The real-time RT-PCR primers are summarized in Table 1. Real-time RT-PCR was performed in a total volume of 20 mul from a mixture containing 10 mul of SYBR1 Premix Ex Taq (Takara, Tokyo, Japan), 0.4 mul of forward primer (10 mM), 0.4 mul of reverse primer (10 mM), 0.4 mul of ROX Reference Dye (50x; Takara, Tokyo, Japan), 6.8 mul of dH2O, and 2 mul of template cDNA solution. Reactions were run in an ABI PRISM7000 Sequence Detector system (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA) under the following conditions: the PCR was run for 40 cycles of 95 degreesC for 5 s, 56 degreesC for 15 s and 72 degreesC for 31s after pre-incubation at 95degreesC for 10 s. All reactions were performed in duplicate. The specificity of the amplification reactions was confirmed by melting-curve analysis. Relative mRNA expression levels of the six genes in the siRNA-treated samples and controls were determined using the comparative threshold (Ct) method and analyzed with Sequence Detector software version 1.7 (Applied Biosystems). The fold changes in the mRNA expression level of IkappaBalpha, NF-kappaBp65, MMP-2, TIMP-2, and MT1-MMP in siRNA-treated and control samples were compared using the 2-DeltaDeltaCt method [26].

Western blot analysis
###end title 28
###begin p 29

Total subcellular and nuclear proteins in different groups were extracted by the Nuclear Extract Kit (Active Motif, Carlsbad, CA) 24 h, 48 h, and 72 h after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection, according to the manufacturer's instructions. Briefly, cells were scraped, washed in PBS, and centrifuged for 5 min at 700x g at 4 degreesC. The supernatant was discarded, and cells were resuspended in a hypotonic buffer and incubated for 15 min on ice. Detergent was added and the suspension vortexed for 10 s. Next, the suspension was centrifuged for 30 s at 14,000x g, and the nuclear pellet was resuspended in 50 mul of complete lysis buffer. The suspension was incubated for 30 min on ice on a rocking platform set at 140x g and centrifuged for 10 min at 14,000x g at 4 degreesC. The supernatant was used as nuclear extract. The protein concentration was measured by the Bio-Rad protein assay (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA). The total protein extracts (30 mug each) were separated from each sample by 10% sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and transferred onto poly vinylidene fluoride (PVDF) membranes. The membranes were blocked with 5% blocking reagent at room temperature for 1.5 h and probed with polyclonal rabbit anti-IkappaBalpha (1:1,000; Cell Signaling Technology, Danvers, MA), polyclonal rabbit anti-MT1-MMP (1:1,000; Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Santa Cruz, CA), and polyclonal rabbit anti-GAPDH (1:5,000; BD) overnight at 4 degreesC. The membranes were washed three times with the blocking buffer and incubated with goat anti-rabbit IgG conjugated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP; BD). The bands were visualized with an enhanced chemiluminescence kit (ECL kit; Cell Signaling Technology). The protein level of NF-kappaBp65 in both the total (30 mug) and nuclear (30 mug) extracts was detected by western blot analysis using polyclonal rabbit anti-NF-kappaBp65 (1:1,000; Cell Signaling Technology). MMP-2 and TIMP-2 expression was examined as follows: the medium was replaced with serum-free growth medium after transfection for 6 h. The conditioned medium was then collected and concentrated by centrifugal ultrafiltration (Amicon, Houston, TX) 24 h, 48 h, and 72 h after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection and stored at -80 degreesC. An equal amount (30 mug) of total proteins was concentrated from the medium to detect the expression of MMP-2 and TIMP-2 with polyclonal rabbit anti-MMP-2 (1:1,000; Cell Signaling Technology) and polyclonal rabbit anti-TIMP-2 (1:1,000; Santa Cruz Biotechnology) according to the procedures described above. The band intensities were quantified by Gel-pro Analyzer 4.0 (Media Cybernetics, Silver Spring, MD).

###begin title 30
Gelatin zymography analysis
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 99 111 99 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 678 679 668 669 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 695 696 685 686 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
The conditioned medium was collected by centrifugation and concentrated 24 h, 48 h, and 72 h after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection. Effect of IkappaBalpha siRNA on NF-kappaBp65 expression and nuclear translocation in HCM cells
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 397 408 373 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6A,C</xref>
###xml 513 525 485 489 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 590 599 554 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6B</xref>
###xml 756 765 708 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6D</xref>
###xml 848 860 796 800 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
To further investigate the molecular mechanism that explains how the knockdown of IkappaBalpha affects the secretion and activation of MMP-2, the NF-kappaB, which is in the downstream pathway of IkappaBalpha, was examined in HCM cells. Real-time RT-PCR and western blot analysis indicated that there were no significant differences between NF-kappaBp65 mRNA and protein levels at each time point (Figure 6A,C). Nevertheless, the expression of NF-kappaBp65 in the nucleus was markedly increased in HCM cells after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection when compared to those in the control cells (Figure 6B). Moreover, immunofluorescent staining demonstrated that silencing of IkappaBalpha could induce translocation of NF-kappaBp65 into the nucleus. As shown in Figure 6D, NF-kappaBp65 staining mainly localizes in the nucleus 24 h, 48 h, and 72 h after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection in HCM cells whereas little nuclear translocation of NF-kappaBp65 was observed in control HCM cells.

###begin title 53
Discussion
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 196 208 188 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 150 155 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In the current study, our results demonstrated that the IkappaB/NF-kappaB signaling pathway was involved in the expression and activation of MMP-2 in human ciliary muscle cells. Downregulation of IkappaBalpha by siRNA resulted in significant translocation of NF-kappaBp65 from the cytoplasm to the nucleus after 24 h to 72 h. The secretion of MMP-2 increased as well as the activation from pro-MMP-2 to active-MMP-2. In addition, the expression of MT1-MMP increased while the expression of TIMP-2 reduced both in mRNA and protein levels. It is implied from these results that NF-kappaB is an important transcriptional factor involved in the control of uveoscleral outflow through the promotion of MMP-2 expression.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 608 610 588 590 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
###xml 745 747 717 719 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 946 948 906 908 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 1196 1198 1144 1146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r23">23</xref>
It has been reported that NF-kappaB is involved in various activities and plays a key role in multiple biological processes including chemical stress, physical stress, physiologic stress, receptor ligands, proinflammatory cytokines, and apoptosis. At the same time, there are also multiple and complex signal transduction pathways leading to NF-kappaB activation. However, many of these activations are primarily performed by the so-called classical pathway, which involves the degradation of IkappaBalpha, leading to the release of the NF-kappaB complex, which in turn allows it to relocate to the nucleus [29]. Meanwhile, mounting evidence shows that the IkappaB/NF-kappaB classical pathway is involved in the expression of MMP-2. Han et al. [21] reported that TNF-alpha induced the breakdown of IkappaB in fibroblasts within the collagen lattice, which is a critical step leading to the activation of pro-MMP-2 by NF-kappaB. Philip and Kundu [22] showed that osteopontin could induce NF-kappaB activity through phosphorylation and degradation of IkappaBalpha by activating I-Kappa B Kinase (IKK), which ultimately triggers the activation of pro-MMP-2 in B16F10 cells. Park and his colleagues [23] indicated that propylene glycol monomethyl acetic ether (PMA) treatment resulted in IkappaBalpha phosphorylation in fibrosarcoma cells, which enhances MMP-2 production through the activation of NF-kappaB transcription factors.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 97 109 93 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 470 472 446 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r30">30</xref>
###xml 499 501 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 502 504 478 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 712 717 680 685 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">MMP-2</italic>
###xml 857 859 817 819 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r31">31</xref>
###xml 1015 1017 971 973 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r32">32</xref>
###xml 1081 1086 1033 1038 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">MMP-2</italic>
###xml 1209 1211 1157 1159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r30">30</xref>
###xml 822 827 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
To further investigate the potential role of NF-kappaB in uveoscleral outflow, the expression of IkappaBalpha was downregulated by specific siRNA. We found not only the translocation of NF-kappaBp65 from the cytoplasm into the nucleus but also the enhanced secretion and activity of MMP-2 after IkappaBalpha knockdown. Most MMPs are secreted in their inactive pro-form from cells and in the active-form outside the cells. Many studies have indicated that the secretion [30] and activation of MMP-2 [21,22] are associated with NF-kappaB . On the other hand, NF-kappaB signaling has also been demonstrated to participate in the regulation of MMP-2 at a transcriptional level as shown in our study, indicating that MMP-2 mRNA increases after NF-kappaB has been activated. It has been reported that PGF2alpha induces c-Fos in human ciliary smooth muscle cells [31] and that c-Fos is capable of physically interacting with NF-kappaBp65 through the Rel homology domain, enhancing its DNA binding and biological function [32]. Moreover, although there is no NF-kappaB-binding site on the MMP-2 promoter, it has been suggested that MMP-2 activation occurs in endothelial cells through an NF-kappaB-dependent pathway [30]. Apparently, the molecular mechanism driving the effect of NF-kappaB on MMP-2 needs to be further investigated.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 33 45 33 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 397 405 389 397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f4">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 493 505 485 489 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 835 847 819 823 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
In this study, downregulation of IkappaBalpha resulted in the decrease of TIMP-2 and the increase of MT1-MMP. TIMP-2 has been found to exhibit several biochemical and physiologic/biological functions including inhibition of active MMPs, pro-MMP activation, and so on. The balance between TIMP-2 and MMP-2 levels is critical in determining the activation status of MMP-2. Along with the results of Figure 4, we found that TIMP-2 expression was reduced while MMP-2 activation was enhanced after IkappaBalpha siRNA transfection in HCM cells. In addition, activation of MMP-2 also requires its cell surface localization and cleavage by cell membrane-bound MT1-MMP, the increased expression of MT1-MMP promoted the MMP-2 expression. The results showed that increased pro-MMP-2 activation reflected upregulation of MT1-MMP expression in the IkappaBalpha siRNA treated cells. In a word, the number of function attributed to being regulated by the transcription factor, NF-kappaBp65, is rapidly increasing. We observed that translocation of NF-kappaBp65 into the nucleus correlates with the decrease of TIMP-2 and the increase of MT1-MMP. Moreover, the activated NF-kappaB induced the secretion and activation of MMP-2. This evidence indicated that the expression of MMP-2 is mediated by the activation of NF-kappaB and that the IkappaB/NF-kappaB signaling pathway plays a significant role in this process.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 632 634 620 622 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
###xml 774 776 762 764 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r34">34</xref>
###xml 955 957 935 937 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r35">35</xref>
As generally recognized, Rel/NF-kappaB transcription factors appear to mediate survival signals that protect cells from apoptosis, which may result in the development of several tumors. It has also become clear that MMPs not only play a role in ECM modification but they also have effects on tumor cell adhesion and motility. In our study, the activated NF-kappaB and consequently, enhanced MMP-2 most likely exert an anti-apoptotic action, promoting the survival of defective cells, thereby causing tumor growth in HCM cells. However, not all reports support a survival role for Rel/NF-kappaB factors. Experiments by Grimm et al. [33] indicated that a dominant-negative RelA mutant prevented the cell death that was induced upon serum starvation of 293 cells. Jung et al. [34] reported that radiation-induced apoptosis of ataxia telangiectasia (AT) fibroblasts was found to be reduced by a transdominant-negative IkappaBalpha protein. Kasibhatla et al. [35] found that inhibition of NF-kappaB activity in a T cell hybridoma leads to decreased apoptosis in T cells. We have also discovered a few studies reporting the detrimental effects of MMP-2 on HCM cells. Therefore, we intend to shift our experimentation from a predominantly in vitro system to an animal model to further explore the role of the IkappaB/NF-kappaB signaling pathway.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
In summary, we demonstrated that the IkappaB/NF-kappaB signaling pathway plays an important role in the expression of MMP-2 in HCM cells. It is possible that the mechanism underlying the uveoscleral outflow pathway involves the inhibition of IkappaBalpha, leading to the activation of NF-kappaB p65, which in turn, results in the increase of secretion and activity of MMP-2. This molecular mechanism may provide a novel insight in the development of a new strategy for the treatment of glaucoma.
###end p 59
