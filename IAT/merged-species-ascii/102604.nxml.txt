Protein sequences conserved in prokaryotic aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases are important for the activity of the processivity factor of human mitochondrial DNA polymerase

Previous studies have shown that the small subunit of Xenopus DNA polymerase gamma (pol gammaB) acts as a processivity factor to stimulate the 140 kDa catalytic subunit of human DNA polymerase gamma. A putative human pol gammaB initially identified by analysis of DNA sequence had not been shown to be functional, and appeared to be an incomplete clone. In this paper, we report the cloning of full-length human and mouse pol gammaB. Both human and mouse pol gammaB proteins were expressed in their mature forms, without their apparent mitochondrial localization signals, and shown to stimulate processivity of the recombinant catalytic subunit of human pol gammaA. Deletion analysis of human pol gammaB indicated that blocks of sequence conserved with prokaryotic class II aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases are necessary for activity and interaction with human pol gammaA. Purification of DNA pol gamma from HeLa cells indicated that both proteins are associated in vivo.

The enzyme responsible for mitochondrial DNA replication and repair, DNA polymerase gamma (pol gamma) has been characterized in several higher eukaryotes as a heterodimeric enzyme (1-4). The larger catalytic subunit (pol gammaA) has been cloned in Drosophila (5), Xenopus (6) and humans (7,8). It has a size ranging from 125 to 140 kDa and a sequence relationship to the family A of DNA polymerases (6,9). The smaller subunits (pol gammaB) from Drosophila (10) and Xenopus (11) have been cloned and expressed in recombinant form. We recently reported that recombinant Xenopus pol gammaB can function as a processivity factor for human pol gammaA in in vitro replication assays (11).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 70 71 66 67 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c3">3</xref>
###xml 239 244 231 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>

Human pol gammaB has been difficult to study. Although Gray and Wong (3) reported co-purification of a smaller protein with the large subunit of human DNA pol gamma, this protein was not obviously present in the enzyme purified by Longley et al. (12). Putative cDNA clones for human and mouse pol gammaB have been identified in sequence databases (10). These clones lacked N-terminal mitochondrial localization signals and appeared to be truncated at their N-termini in comparison with the active Xenopus pol gammaB. Although the mouse clone (GenBank accession number AF006072) was clearly an incomplete cDNA, the reported cDNA sequence of human pol gammaB (GenBank accession number U94703) did contain sequences preceding the putative translation start site. Analysis of the human clone revealed sequences related to the N-terminus of Xenopus pol gammaB in a small open reading frame (ORF) separated from the remainder of the protein by two frame shift mutations. We have confirmed that this reflects an error in the original sequence. This was not evident upon comparison of the previously available human sequence with its Drosophila homolog, both of which were inactive in recombinant form, but became clear upon availability of the Xenopus homolog. We report the cloning of intact versions of both the human and mouse cDNAs encoding pol gammaB. The complete cDNA sequences extend the human and mouse ORFs further upstream to include sequences conserved in the N-terminal region of the Xenopus protein. The N-termini of all three vertebrate DNA pol gammaB proteins appear to encode mitochondrial localization signals. Both human and mouse pol gammaB were expressed and shown to stimulate the processivity of recombinant human pol gammaA in in vitro replication assays. The recombinant human pol gammaB protein has the same size as the protein purified from HeLa cell mitochondria. Purification of human pol gamma from HeLa cell mitochondria and western blot analyses indicated that pol gammaA and gammaB subunits are associated in vivo.
###end p 6

###xml 30 37 30 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus</italic>

###xml 30 37 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus</span>
###xml 185 190 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Alignment of human, mouse and Xenopus pol gammaB revealed a clear conservation of several sequence blocks also present in some class II aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases. Deletion analyses of human pol gammaB indicated that the conserved sequences are required for interaction with the catalytic subunit.

###xml 380 408 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">Escherichia coli strain BL21</span>
###xml 1090 1095 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Primers HGBNDE (5'-ACTTCGGAGACATATGCGCTCT-CGTGTAGCCG-3') and HGBNOT (5'-CAAATATAGCGG-CCGCTACATTCTTAGCTGATGAT-3') were used to amplify a 1.5 kb PCR product from a HeLa Marathon-ready cDNA (Clontech), using Pfu Turbo DNA polymerase (Stratagene). This product was cloned as an NdeI-NotI fragment in the vector pET22b(+) (Novagen) to generate clone pJAC39. Proteins were expressed in Escherichia coli strain BL21(DE3) after induction with 1 mM IPTG. Sequencing of this clone revealed the presence of several differences with the previously published sequence, including two frame shifts. Primer HGB1 (5'-TCATATGGATGCGGGGCAGCCGGAGC-3'), corresponding to sequence DAGQPE preceded by a methionine codon, was used in combination with the HGBNOT primer to clone by PCR a cDNA encoding the putative mature protein (clone pJAC44). For expression of proteins in insect cells, the NdeI-NotI insert was directly transferred into a pFastBacI baculovirus expression vector (Life Technologies) modified as previously described (11) to allow expression in insect cells of C-terminal his-tagged proteins. The human sequence has been deposited in GenBank under accession number AF177201.

###begin title 13
Purification of his-tagged proteins
###end title 13
###begin p 14
Bacteria expressing the recombinant protein were washed in a buffer containing 50 mM HEPES (pH 7.5), 0.2 M sucrose, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM PMSF, 1 microM pepstatin and 7 mM beta-mercaptoethanol, and sonicated in lysis buffer (50 mM sodium phosphate, pH 8, 300 mM NaCl, 20% glycerol, 1 mM beta-mercaptoethanol, 0.5 mM benzamidine-HCl, 1 microM pepstatin, 5 microg/ml leupeptin and 0.2 mM PMSF). Insoluble proteins were solubilized in the same buffer containing 8 M urea without glycerol. Insect cells expressing recombinant protein were lysed by sonication in binding buffer (50 mM sodium phosphate, pH 8, 300 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 3 mM beta-mercaptoethanol and 0.2 mM PMSF).
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 225 226 225 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g</italic>
###xml 1080 1086 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
For affinity purification on Ni-NTA columns, imidazole was added to 10 mM final concentration to the cell lysate containing soluble proteins. Both bacterial and insect cell lysates were precleared by centrifugation at 23 500 g for 10 min. The cleared lysates were incubated for 1 h with Ni-NTA beads (Qiagen) pre-washed in binding buffer containing 20 mM imidazole (W20). Proteins from insect cells were then washed three times with W20 and proteins from bacteria were washed in W60 (binding buffer containing 60 mM imidazole). Elution was carried out with EB250 (binding buffer containing 250 mM imidazole). Purified protein was dialyzed against a buffer containing 50 mM sodium phosphate, pH 8, 300 mM NaCl, 50% glycerol, 2 mM beta-mercaptoethanol and 0.2 mM PMSF, and stored at -80degreesC in aliquots after freezing in liquid nitrogen. To prepare protein to be used as antigen, bacterially expressed protein was purified from the urea-soluble fraction as described above but using buffers containing 8 M urea. Purified protein was dialyzed against PBS and used to inoculate a rabbit.

###begin title 16
Partial purification of DNA pol gamma from HeLa cells
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 65 66 65 66 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 299 300 299 300 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 592 593 592 593 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g</italic>
###xml 676 677 676 677 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g</italic>
HeLa cells were grown in 12 l of DMEM to a cell density of 7 x 105 cells/ml. Cells were harvested by centrifugation and washed in 240 ml TD buffer (135 mM NaCl, 5 mM KCl, 25 mM Tris-Cl, pH 7.6). After centrifugation as above, cells were resuspended in 210 ml of CaRSB buffer (10 mM NaCl, 1.5 mM CaCl2, 10 mM Tris, pH 7.5) and allowed to swell for 10 min on ice. Cells were then homogenized with a glass Dounce homogenizer and two-thirds volume of 2.5x MS buffer was added (1x MS is 210 mM mannitol, 70 mM sucrose, 5 mM EDTA, 5 mM Tris, pH 7.6). Nuclei were pelleted by centrifugation at 1500 g for 5 min, twice. A mitochondrial pellet was obtained by centrifugation at 27 000 g for 15 min, mitochondria were resuspended in MS buffer and then centrifuged again. Mitochondrial lysis was achieved by homogenization in MS buffer after addition of KCl to 250 mM and Triton X-100 to 0.5%. The mitochondrial lysate was clarified by centrifugation at 50 000 r.p.m. in a Beckman 70Ti rotor for 1 h and stored at -80degreesC.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 782 783 762 763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c2">2</xref>
Purification of pol gamma was carried out by chromatography of the lysate through DEAE-Sephacel at 250 mM KCl in buffer Z (20 mM Tris pH 8.4, 1 mM DTT, 1 mM EDTA, 10% glycerol, 0.2 mM PMSF, 10 mM benzamidine-HCl, 1 microM pepstatin, 1 microg/ml aprotinin, 1 microg/ml leupeptin). The flow through was dialyzed against buffer Z to a KCl concentration below 50 mM, loaded onto a SP-Sepharose column and proteins were eluted with a KCl gradient from 50 to 750 mM in the same buffer. The peak fractions were collected, pooled, diluted to a KCl concentration of 200 mM and loaded onto a Heparin Hi Trap column. Proteins were eluted with a KCl gradient from 200 mM to 1 M. Pol gamma was detected by assay for reverse transcriptase activity on poly(rA):oligo(dT), as described previously (2).

The polymerase domain of human pol gammaA was expressed in bacteria as a C-terminal his-tagged protein from an expression vector constructed as follows. An NdeI-containing primer was made in such a way that the ATG in the NdeI site matched the ATG in the sequence MDQEDL (between the exonuclease and the polymerase domains in the protein). PCR with these two primers on a HeLa Marathon-ready cDNA (Clontech) amplified a 2180 bp fragment that was then digested with NdeI and NotI and cloned into pET22B(+) (a three piece ligation was done due to the existence of an internal NdeI site in the amplified DNA). A putative sequence for human pol gammaB has been available in sequence databases for some time, originally identified based on the homology of expressed sequence tag clones to the Drosophila accessory subunit (10). It was surprising that this putative protein did not contain N-terminal sequences related to those of its functional Xenopus counterpart (11). This putative human pol gammaB also lacked an N-terminal mitochondrial localization signal. We therefore decided to search for human sequences in the expressed sequence tag (EST) database with homology to the N-terminus of the mature Xenopus protein, using BLAST (14). We found highly conserved sequences in the putative 5' UTR of the available human pol gamma accessory subunit cDNA sequence (GenBank accession number U94703), but in a different reading frame than that identified by Wang et al. (10). We realized that by introducing two nucleotides in the correct positions in the human sequence, the ORF could be extended further upstream to a putative translation start codon. With these insertions, this modified sequence would be slightly larger than the Xenopus one and the homology between the N-terminal regions of the proteins would be significantly increased. Furthermore, computer analysis of this sequence predicted a mitochondrial localization sequence comprising the first 25 amino acids (aa). These observations suggested that the original sequence might be in error and that the two reading frames might be continuous.

###xml 21 26 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Deletion analysis of human pol gammaB
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 785 786 737 738 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd219f4">4</xref>
###xml 1194 1195 1134 1135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd219f4">4</xref>
###xml 1800 1801 1732 1733 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd219f4">4</xref>
###xml 2067 2068 1979 1980 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd219f4">4</xref>
###xml 43 48 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We constructed several deletion mutants of human pol gammaB in order to characterize protein domains required for binding pol gammaA and stimulating polymerase activity. The N-terminal deletion clones, DeltaN1 and DeltaN2, lacked the first 56 and 81 residues, respectively. Clone DeltaN2 was designed to remove the sequence GPLGVEL conserved between all three vertebrate DNA pol gammaB proteins and GRS motif I. The C-terminal deletion clones, DeltaC1, DeltaC2 and DeltaC3, lacked the final 30, 115 and 161 residues, respectively. Clones DeltaC1 and DeltaC2 were designed to remove the sequences RSRDT and VLKLHPCLAPIKVA that are highly conserved with GRS sequences. Clone DeltaC3 was designed to remove sequences conserved with GRS motif 3. These constructs are illustrated in Figure 4A along with three internally deleted mutants DeltaI1, DeltaI2 and DeltaI3. All proteins were expressed and purified as described for the full-length protein. In every case a sufficient fraction of the bacterially expressed protein was soluble to permit affinity purification using Ni-NTA columns. The proteins eluted from the Ni-NTA columns were contaminated with some bacterial proteins as shown in Figure 4B. This contamination was more evident with the less soluble mutants, due to the lower yield of soluble protein. These contaminants did not affect the activity of the recombinant proteins when the mutant proteins were added to reactions containing the wild-type protein (data not shown). Note that some of these proteins displayed an aberrant electrophoretic mobility. For example, DeltaI1 (44.8 kDa) migrated more slowly than DeltaN2 (45.1 kDa). Activity assays on poly(dA):oligo(dT) followed by quantitation of acid-insoluble products indicated that all deleted proteins were essentially inactive (Fig. 4C) when used with pol gamma in equimolar amounts. DeltaN1 and DeltaI3 showed a low but detectable level of stimulation activity when used in a 10-fold molar excess over pol gammaA. These mutants were also able to interact with pol gammaA-DNA complexes in EMSA (Fig. 4C, lower panel). Mutant DeltaI2 was also capable of some degree of interaction in the binding assay, but did not significantly stimulate pol gammaA activity. Migration of the supershifted protein:DNA complexes was slower for mutants DeltaI2 and DeltaI3. This difference in the extent of the mobility shift cannot be explained simply by a difference in charge of the mutant proteins, since the pK values of these two mutant proteins (6.67 and 7.08, respectively) is similar to the pK of the wild-type protein (7.36). A more plausible explanation for the altered mobility of the protein-DNA complexes involving these two internal deletion mutants is that these proteins may adopt a different conformation than the wild-type protein.

Gray and Wong (3) identified two polypeptides co-purifying with DNA pol gamma activity from HeLa cell mitochondria, a catalytic subunit of 140 kDa and a small subunit of 54 kDa of unknown function. The 190 kDa native molecular mass of the enzyme suggested a heterodimeric structure containing one copy of each polypeptide. The size of the putative small subunit of human pol gamma, which we designate pol gammaB, was significantly larger than the 42.4 kDa human pol gammaB homolog of Drosophila pol gammaB identified in a database search by Wang et al. (10). When we cloned Xenopus pol gammaB (11), we noted that the Xenopus protein contained additional N-terminal sequences lacking in the putative human pol gammaB. We employed the strategy described above to clone a full-length version of the human pol gammaB that does exhibit homology to the N-terminus of the Xenopus protein, as shown in Figure 1. The cloned sequence contains a potential mitochondrial targeting signal and predicts a molecular mass of 52.2 kDa for the mature pol gammaB, which agrees well with the molecular mass of 54 kDa estimated by Gray and Wong (3). The recombinant human pol gammaB protein co-migrates with the pol gammaB purified from HeLa cells (Fig. 3, lower panel, compare lanes 5 and AB).

(11) first reported the homology between Xenopus pol gammaB and class II aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases (aaRSs), it was unclear how well this would apply to human pol gammaB since the sequence reported by Wang et al. (10) was significantly shorter than that of Xenopus pol gammaB. Our elucidation of the complete sequences of human and mouse pol gammaB has extended the homology between pol gammaB and aaRS proteins to include motif I of aaRSs. Since Xenopus and mouse pol gammaB stimulate the activity of human pol gammaA as well as human pol gammaB, it is virtually certain that all three proteins will share a common structure. The homology of all three proteins with aaRS sequences is so extensive that computer algorithms such as PredictProtein (15) predict that the common structure of pol gammaB will closely resemble that determined for T.thermophilus GRS. After the Xenopus pol gammaB sequence was published (11), a theoretical molecular modeling study appeared which supported this conclusion (16). Unfortunately, these authors did not employ the complete human pol gammaB in their analysis, so that the model lacks the N-terminal domain. The definitive analysis of the homology between DNA pol gammaB and tRNA synthetases will require experimental determination of the structure of the polymerase subunit.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 43 48 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 653 658 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 722 727 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 987 992 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We constructed several deletion mutants of human DNA pol gammaB to determine whether sequences related to aaRS proteins were essential for stimulation of pol gammaA activity and for binding of pol gammaB in complexes with pol gammaA and primer-template oligonucleotides. All of the mutants we generated were markedly impaired in both assays. The general lack of activity we observed for pol gammaB deletion mutants suggests that the protein requires a highly conserved structure in order to serve as an accessory factor for pol gammaA. Removal of 56 amino acid residues from the N-terminus is sufficient to essentially inactivate the protein. Since the mouse pol gammaB is truncated slightly (26 residues) with respect to human pol gammaB and is still fully active, this indicates that the remaining 30 residues in the N-terminal region are important for activity. Similarly, removal of 30 amino acid residues from the C-terminus, including the RSRDT motif conserved in GRS, inactivates human pol gammaB. Two mutants, DeltaN1 and DeltaI3, exhibited detectable activity when used in a molar excess over the catalytic subunit. These mutants are also able to supershift pol gammaA:DNA complexes when used in excess. This is consistent with a weaker or more unstable interaction as compared to the wild-type protein.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 597 599 581 583 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c17">17</xref>
###xml 600 602 584 586 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c18">18</xref>
It is interesting that deletions that completely inactivate the protein involve regions that are conserved in aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase sequences. The two deletion mutants that retain some activity, DeltaN1 and DeltaI3, are missing regions that are only weakly conserved between these two groups of proteins. DeltaN2 differs from DeltaN1 by the deletion of an additional 25 amino acids including a very well conserved sequence block, GPLGVEL, and is completely inactive. This block is part of motif 1 in class II aaRSs, which has been implicated in dimerization and orientation of motifs 2 and 3 (17,18). Mutant DeltaI2 protein is still capable of interaction with the pol gammaA:DNA complex but it is unable to activate pol gammaA. This mutant is missing a region that aligns with motif 2 of aaRSs which, together with motif 3, is believed to form the active site of the tRNA synthetase.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 657 663 645 651 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopu</italic>
###xml 780 785 768 773 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 788 790 776 778 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c19">19</xref>
###xml 657 664 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus</span>
###xml 774 779 <span type="species:ncbi:309978">Shiba</span>
The functional relationship, if any, between a subunit of an eukaryotic mitochondrial DNA polymerase and a prokaryotic aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase is still a matter of speculation. Although pol gammaB is more closely related to GRS than to other tRNA synthetases in genetic databanks, there are several reasons to think it is not an active mitochondrial GRS. First, preliminary experiments carried out in our laboratory to detect adenylation of amino acids as a subreaction of the tRNA aminoacylation reaction have not given positive results (data not shown). Second, we did not detect a free pool of pol gammaB not associated with pol gammaA in HeLa cell or Xenopus oocyte mitochondria. Finally, a likely candidate for the functional mitochondrial GRS has been identified by Shiba et al. (19).
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 407 423 403 419 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 484 486 480 482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c20">20</xref>
###xml 564 566 560 562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c21">21</xref>
###xml 633 635 629 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c22">22</xref>
###xml 668 670 664 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c23">23</xref>
###xml 671 673 667 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c24">24</xref>
###xml 710 724 706 720 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T.thermophilus</italic>
###xml 749 751 745 747 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c25">25</xref>
###xml 870 872 866 868 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c26">26</xref>
###xml 407 423 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 710 724 <span type="species:ncbi:274">T.thermophilus</span>
###xml 927 932 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1024 1029 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
It appears that the pol gammaB subunit was acquired by the catalytic subunit during evolution to enhance the processivity of the enzyme and possibly to serve other roles in replication. What other activities of an aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase may play a role in replication? There is growing evidence that aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases are involved in other cellular processes not related to tRNA aminoacylation. Escherichia coli GRS catalyzes the synthesis of dinucleoside polyphosphates (20), with suspected regulatory functions in replication and cell proliferation (21). Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases also play a role in intron splicing (22) and transcription termination (23,24). Phenylalanyl-tRNA synthetase from T.thermophilus binds DNA specifically (25), although the functional significance of this binding has not yet been identified. More recently, Magrath and Hyman (26) reported that GRS is involved in 3'-end formation in yeast, possibly by direct interaction with tRNA-like structures located at the 3' end of several yeast mRNAs.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 153 154 149 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd219f5">5</xref>
###xml 351 353 347 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c27">27</xref>
###xml 544 546 540 542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c28">28</xref>
###xml 974 975 966 967 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L</sub>
###xml 1062 1064 1054 1056 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c29">29</xref>
###xml 1075 1076 1067 1068 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd219f5">5</xref>
###xml 1152 1154 1144 1146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd219c30">30</xref>
###xml 1232 1233 1220 1221 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L</sub>
###xml 1445 1450 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Recent studies of the mechanism of priming of mtDNA replication suggest a hypothetical role for pol gammaB in replication initiation. As shown in Figure 5, DNA sequences in the control regions of several vertebrate mitochondrial DNAs that may be capable of adopting tRNA-like structures have been proposed to participate in initiation of replication (27). Priming of leading strand replication is thought to require synthesis of a highly folded RNA that can be recognized and cleaved by a mitochondrial RNA processing enzyme (MRP endonuclease; 28). The specificity of primer cleavage differs for free RNA or for RNA associated with mtDNA in an R-loop structure. It is possible that binding of DNA pol gamma in this region, possibly mediated by the small subunit, may influence the cleavage of the RNA by MRP endonuclease to generate primers or may facilitate primer utilization. A different priming mechanism has been proposed for the lagging strand origin of replication, OL, which is located within a cluster of tRNA genes and adopts a stem-loop conformation (29; see Fig. 5). The lagging strand primer is thought to be synthesized by a DNA primase (30). It is possible that binding of DNA pol gamma to tRNA-like structures near OL may influence the efficiency of primer utilization at this origin as well. These proposed functions for the small subunit are reminiscent of the function of aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase in mRNA 3' end formation in yeast and transcription termination in other systems noted above. The availability of an active heterodimeric pol gamma will permit direct experimental testing of such models in future experiments.
