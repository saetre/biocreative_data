Metalloprotease Meprinbeta in Rat Kidney: Glomerular Localization and Differential Expression in Glomerulonephritis
###end article-title 0

###end p 2
###begin p 3
###xml 205 208 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 362 365 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 827 832 <span type="species:ncbi:9940">sheep</span>
Meprin (EC 3.4.24.18) is an oligomeric metalloendopeptidase found in microvillar membranes of kidney proximal tubular epithelial cells. Here, we present the first report on the expression of meprinbeta in rat glomerular epithelial cells and suggest a potential involvement in experimental glomerular disease. We detected meprinbeta in glomeruli of immunostained rat kidney sections on the protein level and by quantitative RT-PCR of laser-capture microdissected glomeruli on the mRNA level. Using immuno-gold staining we identified the membrane of podocyte foot processes as the main site of meprinbeta expression. The glomerular meprinbeta expression pattern was altered in anti-Thy 1.1 and passive Heymann nephritis (PHN). In addition, the meprinbeta staining pattern in the latter was reminiscent of immunostaining with the sheep anti-Fx1A antiserum, commonly used in PHN induction. Using Western blot and immunoprecipitation assays we demonstrated that meprinbeta is recognized by Fx1A antiserum and may therefore represent an auto-antigen in PHN. In anti-Thy 1.1 glomerulonephritis we observed a striking redistribution of meprinbeta in tubular epithelial cells from the apical to the basolateral side and the cytosol. This might point to an involvement of meprinbeta in this form of glomerulonephritis.

###xml 2133 2137 2123 2127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Villa2">[28]</xref>
###xml 2139 2143 2129 2133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Hengst1">[29]</xref>
###xml 976 981 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Many forms of inflammatory kidney disease are characterized by excessive proliferation of mesangial cells (MC) and by increased deposition of extracellular matrix (ECM) in glomeruli and the tubulo-interstitium, with subsequent structural alteration of the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) and the mesangial matrix [1], [2]. Therefore, an analysis of the factors responsible for the regulation of MC proliferation and for the metabolism of ECM is of highest priority. The processing of ECM proteins in the kidney is influenced substantially by members of the metzincin superfamily of metalloendopeptidases, notably by the matrix metalloproteases (MMPs) [3]-[5] and by the ADAMs (a disintegrin and metalloprotease) [6], [7]. The role of MMPs in basement membrane remodelling in particular has recently received a lot of attention due to their ability to influence the progression of renal dysfunction [8]. Moreover, the pharmacological ablation of MMP enzymatic activity in a mouse model of Alport syndrome prior to the onset of proteinuria or structural defects of the GBM led to significant attenuation in disease progression and to prolonged survival [9]. Recently, the metalloendopeptidase meprin has been suggested to be involved in these processes, as well [10], [11]. Meprin (EC 3.4.24.18), an oligomeric zinc endopeptidase of the astacin family, was originally identified on the apical brush-border membrane of epithelial cells in the cortico-medullary portion of proximal kidney tubules and the intestine [12]-[15]. It is composed of alpha and beta subunits that are expressed separately or coordinately either forming homo- or hetero-oligomeric complexes [16], [17]. Meprins are highly conserved among different species and are capable of cleaving a wide range of protein substrates in vitro, such as ECM components (collagen type IV, laminin, fibronectin and nidogen [18]-[21]), cytokines (interleukin-1 beta), protein kinases, growth factors and peptide hormones [22]-[27]. Meprins act extracellularly in that they are either bound to the plasma membrane by transmembrane domains or secreted into the extracellular space [28], [29].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 345 349 332 336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Beynon1">[30]</xref>
###xml 578 582 565 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Trachtman1">[31]</xref>
###xml 865 869 852 856 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Walker1">[21]</xref>
###xml 1101 1105 1088 1092 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Herzog2">[32]</xref>
###xml 1370 1374 1357 1361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Carmago1">[10]</xref>
###xml 1376 1380 1363 1367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Kieran1">[33]</xref>
###xml 113 118 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 403 407 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 941 946 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1365 1369 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
The role of meprin in the pathogenesis of kidney disease has been studied previously in animal models. There are mouse strains (ICR or C57B1/6) that express both meprinalpha and beta in proximal tubular cells and others (C3H/He or CBA) that exclusively express meprinbeta, mostly in a latent form (pro-meprinbeta) with low proteolytic potential [30]. When exposed to different renal insults, the latter mice developed less severe forms of kidney disease compared to those with normal meprin levels, suggesting a potential role for meprin in the pathology of acute renal failure [31]. Moreover, in a model of ischemia and reperfusion injury, meprin was reported to be redistributed from the apical brush-border membrane to the basolateral side and the cytosol of tubular epithelial cells resulting in degradation of ECM and damage to adjacent peritubular structures [21]. Recently, it could be shown that this relocalization occurs also in a mouse model of acute kidney injury using cisplatin, suggesting that common mechanisms for meprin redistribution are involved in toxic and ischemic renal injury [32]. Furthermore, meprin was found to be directly cytotoxic to cultured tubular epithelial cells and its inhibition by actinonin and by the metal chelator 1,10-phenanthroline resulted in a protective effect on cell integrity during ischemia-reperfusion injury in rats [10], [33].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 328 332 328 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Ricardo1">[34]</xref>
###xml 508 512 505 509 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Kieran1">[33]</xref>
###xml 514 518 511 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Sadlier1">[35]</xref>
###xml 276 279 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 399 404 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Multiple studies have also revealed that meprin expression is down-regulated in models of renal injury leading to acute renal failure. For example, renal damage associated with hydronephrosis (unilateral urethral obstruction) occurred with an early and progressive decline in rat meprin subunits on both mRNA and protein levels [34]. In addition, microarray analysis demonstrated marked declines in mouse meprinbeta expression in acute renal ischemic reperfusion injury and in adriamycin-induced nephropathy [33], [35].
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 142 146 139 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Berthier1">[36]</xref>
###xml 252 256 249 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Martin1">[37]</xref>
###xml 257 261 254 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Asanuma1">[39]</xref>
###xml 660 663 651 654 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Lovett1">[3]</xref>
###xml 665 669 656 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Marti2">[40]</xref>
###xml 670 674 661 665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Salant1">[42]</xref>
###xml 28 31 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 475 478 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 507 510 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 566 569 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 579 584 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 836 839 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
In a previous study using a rat model of chronic renal allograft rejection, we observed a concomitant down-regulation of meprinbeta and MMP-9 [36]. MMP-9 has been shown to be secreted by both mesangial cells and glomerular epithelial cells (podocytes) [37]-[39]. We therefore decided to check for meprinbeta expression in glomeruli and then to further assess meprin regulation in models of glomerular disease. For this purpose, we focused on meprinbeta expression in healthy rat kidney and in two models of rat glomerular disease: passive Heymann nephritis (PHN), a rat model of human membranous nephropathy, and anti-Thy 1.1 mesangial proliferative nephritis [3], [40]-[42]. We demonstrate that a relocalization of the protease occurs following the induction of glomerular disease, an effect that may contribute to the pathogenesis of rat glomerulonephritis. Moreover, we demonstrate that meprin may represents a potential auto-antigen in PHN.

To assess meprinbeta expression in healthy kidney paraffin-embedded kidney sections from Wistar rats were analyzed by immunostaining (Fig. 1). As expected, we observed strong apical staining of proximal tubular cells (PT) using a specific anti-meprinbeta antibody (Fig. 1A, 1E). In addition, glomeruli (G) also displayed positive meprinbeta staining (Fig. 1B, 1E). No signal was observed when the primary meprinbeta antibody was replaced by normal rabbit serum (Fig. 1C, 1F), or following pre-incubation with a five-fold excess of recombinant human meprinbeta (Fig. 1D). No effect on signal intensity was seen when the antibody was pre-incubated with bovine serum albumin (data not shown). We confirmed these data using Fisher and Lewis rats (supplementary Figure S1).

###xml 690 693 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
To further investigate the localization of meprinbeta in glomeruli, we double-stained the Wistar kidney sections with anti-meprinbeta antibodies and anti-synaptopodin or anti-Thy 1.1 antibodies (Fig. 2). Synaptopodin is abundantly expressed in the foot processes of podocytes whereas Thy 1.1 is localized on the surface of glomerular mesangial cells. The staining pattern of meprinbeta revealed co-localization with synaptopodin (Fig. 2A-C), but not with Thy 1.1 (Fig. 2D-F). Both meprinbeta and synaptopodin displayed a linear staining pattern drawing the contour of the intra-glomerular capillaries. These findings clearly demonstrate the expression of meprinbeta protein in podocytes of rat glomeruli.

mRNA expression of meprinbeta in glomeruli
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 140 146 140 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g003">Fig. 3</xref>
###xml 251 262 251 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g003">Fig. 3A, 3B</xref>
###xml 566 573 560 567 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g003">Fig. 3C</xref>
###xml 127 138 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Wistar rats</span>
Next, we analyzed the mRNA expression by real-time quantitative RT-PCR (RQ-PCR) on laser-capture microdissected glomeruli from Wistar rats (Fig. 3). Importantly, the tissue surrounding the captured glomeruli remained intact following microdissection (Fig. 3A, 3B). RQ-PCR analyses using 18S RNA as a house-keeping gene confirmed high meprinbeta mRNA expression in glomeruli. The signal of meprinbeta mRNA derived from the 200 collected glomeruli was 467-fold higher when compared to the level of the respective total kidney homogenate (arbitrarily set as 1.0-fold) (Fig. 3C), explained by the fact that all glomeruli contribute to meprin expression, while tubular meprinbeta is confined to some proximal tubules of the cortico-medullary section and is therefore diluted in a whole kidney homogenate. The meprinbeta mRNA results support the immunostaining data described above.

###begin title 21
Meprinbeta in experimental PHN
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 185 189 185 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Kerjaschki1">[43]</xref>
###xml 191 195 191 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Kerjaschki2">[44]</xref>

###xml 871 878 865 872 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g004">Fig. 4I</xref>
###xml 9 12 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 22 27 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 238 242 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 269 274 <span type="species:ncbi:9940">sheep</span>
PHN is a rat model of human membranous nephropathy associated with altered podocyte slit diaphragm integrity, sub-epithelial immune deposits and expansion of glomerular basal membranes [43], [44]. Analysis of kidney sections from control rats after injection of normal sheep globulin showed a linear staining pattern for meprinbeta. This changed to a granular staining following injection of anti-Fx1A antiserum to induce PHN (Fig. 4A-C). At day 3, glomerular capillaries displayed a significant and well outlined meprinbeta and synaptopodin staining, whereas at day 6, the staining intensity became granular and clustered and the overall picture was less intense in signal strength (Fig. 4B-F and Fig. 4 compare J and K to L), with the granular pattern being more pronounced for synaptopodin (Fig. 4G, 4H), probably as a result of a deterioration of podocyte integrity (Fig. 4I).

###begin title 25
Detection of meprinbeta by anti-Fx1a antisera
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 76 80 76 80 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Heymann1">[45]</xref>
###xml 717 721 711 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Kerjaschki3">[46]</xref>
###xml 943 950 931 938 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g005">Fig. 5A</xref>
###xml 1125 1132 1110 1117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g005">Fig. 5A</xref>
###xml 1248 1255 1233 1240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g005">Fig. 5A</xref>
###xml 1456 1463 1435 1442 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g005">Fig. 5B</xref>
###xml 1582 1589 1561 1568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g005">Fig. 5B</xref>
###xml 2009 2016 1976 1983 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g005">Fig. 5B</xref>
###xml 347 352 <span type="species:ncbi:9940">sheep</span>
###xml 356 362 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 439 442 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 850 861 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Wistar rats</span>
###xml 1679 1684 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Since the introduction of PHN as a model for auto-immune glomerulonephritis [45], a substantial effort was made to identify responsible antigens. It has been recognized that biologically relevant antigens must be present in the brush borders of the proximal kidney tubules as well as in podocyte membranes. PHN is induced by the administration of sheep or rabbit anti-Fx1A antisera generated against the brush border membrane fractions of rat proximal kidney tubules. Meprinbeta is abundantly expressed in these membranes but, as shown above, also in glomeruli, the main target for the antibodies. Moreover, the staining pattern we observed for meprinbeta resembled the one obtained by immunostaining using anti-Fx1A [46]. Hence, we checked if meprinbeta was detected by the anti-Fx1A antiserum using Western blot analysis. In kidney homogenate from Wistar rats we detected a band of 110-kDa corresponding to the molecular size of meprinbeta (Fig. 5A, lane 1). The same band was observed when the blot was stripped and re-probed with a specific anti-meprinbeta antibody to show the specificity of the detection by anti-Fx1A (Fig. 5A, lane 3). In contrast, no band was detected when the membrane was blotted with normal serum as a negative control (Fig. 5A, lane 2). A 110-kDa band corresponding to meprinbeta was also obtained after immunoprecipitation using anti-Fx1A antiserum, followed by Western blot analysis with a specific anti-meprinbeta antibody (Fig. 5B, lane 1). No band appeared when the kidney homogenate was immunoprecipitated with normal serum as a negative control (Fig. 5B, lane 2). In addition, we stably transfected Madin-Darby canine kidney (MDCK) cells with human meprinbeta. Wild-type and meprinbeta transfected MDCK cells were then subjected to immunoprecipitation using anti-Fx1A antiserum, followed by Western blot analysis with anti-meprinbeta antibody. We obtained a 110-kDa band in lysates of meprinbeta-transfected MDCK cells whereas in wild-type MDCK cells this band was absent (Fig. 5B, line 3 and 5B, line 4). These findings clearly demonstrate that the anti-Fx1A antiserum, in addition to other brush border membrane proteins, detects meprinbeta. Meprinbeta could therefore be recognized at the podocyte membrane by the anti-Fx1A antiserum and thus contribute to the pathology of PHN as another auto-antigen.

###begin title 30
Meprinbeta in experimental anti-Thy 1.1 nephritis
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 248 252 248 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Floege2">[47]</xref>
###xml 345 349 345 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-SteinmannNiggli1">[48]</xref>
###xml 447 453 444 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g006">Fig. 6</xref>
###xml 577 584 571 578 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g006">Fig. 6B</xref>
###xml 680 687 671 678 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g006">Fig. 6A</xref>
###xml 1403 1413 1394 1404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002278-g006">Fig. 6D&#8211;6F</xref>
###xml 611 615 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 1219 1223 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 1323 1327 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
Anti-Thy 1.1 nephritis is characterized by an initial phase of complement-dependent mesangiolysis lasting for approximately 2 days, followed by a marked proliferative response of the residual mesangial cells associated with the accumulation of ECM [47]. We observed in preliminary experiments that the latter two features were reached at day 11 [48]. Therefore, this time point was chosen for nephrectomy and subsequent meprinbeta immunostaining (Fig. 6). We observed a dramatic decrease in meprinbeta expression in all glomeruli tested at day 11 after anti-Thy 1.1 injection (Fig. 6B). In contrast, in control rats injected with PBS we observed normal meprinbeta protein levels (Fig. 6A). This observation is in accordance with the alterations in glomerular structure featuring various glomerular diseases, particularly at the level of podocytes. Substantial damage to glomeruli was also reflected by a distorted synaptopodin staining, indicating loss of podocyte integrity (data not shown). These changes in protein levels occur along with the onset of proteinuria, having a maximum around day 2 and 3 after induction of disease. Moreover, we also noticed a different staining pattern in proximal tubules: in control rats, a strong signal was localized at the apical plasma membrane, whereas kidney sections of nephritic rats displayed a more diffuse staining throughout the tubular epithelial cells (Fig. 6D-6F). These findings point to a differential and rapid relocalization of meprinbeta towards the basolateral side and the cytoplasm of proximal tubular cells during mesangioproliferative glomerulonephritis.

###begin title 34
Discussion
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 115 119 115 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Bond3">[49]</xref>
###xml 254 258 254 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Walker1">[21]</xref>
Meprin comprises approximately 5% of the brush border membrane in rodent kidney, only second in abundance to actin [49]. Hence it is considered to be the major protein of the microvillar membrane with activity for degrading extracellular matrix proteins [21]. Numerous studies have demonstrated that renal meprin expression plays a key role in the severity and progression of renal injury, with meprin exacerbating the disease.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 410 414 407 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Le1">[50]</xref>
###xml 102 105 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 372 375 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
In our study we detected for the first time meprinbeta expression in the glomeruli of three different rat strains, a finding expanding our knowledge on meprin's previously described expression in the apical membrane of proximal tubular epithelial cells. Interestingly, a metalloendopeptidase with a molecular weight corresponding to that of meprin was previously found in rat glomeruli, but was not identified [50]. Using specific markers for glomerular cells and immuno-gold staining we identified the membrane of podocyte foot processes as the main site of glomerular expression of meprinbeta.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 856 860 847 851 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Kamata1">[51]</xref>
###xml 1180 1184 1171 1175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Farquhar1">[52]</xref>
###xml 1283 1287 1271 1275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Kerjaschki3">[46]</xref>
###xml 17 20 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 660 663 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 694 699 <span type="species:ncbi:9940">sheep</span>
###xml 703 709 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 1263 1266 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1664 1668 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
In two different rat models of glomerulonephritis we detected changes in the expression pattern of meprinbeta, both in the glomeruli as well as in the proximal tubules. In passive Heymann nephritis (PHN), the immunofluorescence signal for meprinbeta in glomeruli paralleled that of synaptopodin. An increasingly granular staining was observed indicative of the disruption in structural integrity of the podocyte foot processes. In addition, the intensity of meprinbeta staining was decreased both in the glomeruli and in the proximal tubules with disease progression. PHN is induced by the administration of antibodies generated against a membrane fraction of rat renal proximal tubular cells (sheep or rabbit anti-Fx1A antibodies), which results in the formation and deposition of sub-epithelial immune complexes at the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) [51]. This leads to the disruption of the functional integrity of the GBM and the filtration barrier, which in turn results in proteinuria. Megalin, a multiligand endocytic receptor, localized in the microvillus membrane of proximal tubular epithelial cells, was previously identified as an antigen in these immune deposits [52]. The observation of identical staining patterns with anti-meprinbeta and anti-rat Fx1A antibodies [46], the direct detection of meprinbeta by anti-Fx1A antibody in Western blots and in immunoprecipitation assays, and the subcellular localization of meprinbeta in the membrane of podocyte foot processes all suggest that meprinbeta may be involved in the pathogenesis of PHN by representing another auto-antigen. The decrease of meprinbeta immunostaining signal in glomeruli from rats with PHN may reflect the typical injury of podocytes in this disease or may reflect a reduced binding affinity of the staining antibody to the meprinbeta-immunocomplexes.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 186 190 186 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Harvey1">[53]</xref>
###xml 191 195 191 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Abbate1">[56]</xref>
###xml 402 410 402 410 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 466 470 466 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Kruse1">[19]</xref>
###xml 471 475 471 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Walker1">[21]</xref>
Physiologically, the integrity and the structure of the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) is maintained by the podocytes in a continuous replacement and recycling effected by proteases [53]-[56]. Meprin, expressed by podocytes, may act as such a processing entity in breaking down GBM components, prior to being substituted by new matrix proteins. A plethora of ECM components are processed by meprin in vitro, such as laminin, collagen IV, nidogen and fibronectin [19]-[21]. Damage to the podocytes, either directly in PHN or indirectly through mesangial injury in Thy 1.1 nephritis, may then interfere with the normal turnover function of meprin and may thus disturb the steady-state processing of GBM components and hence enhance progression of the disease.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 821 825 812 816 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Carmago1">[10]</xref>
###xml 827 831 818 822 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Walker1">[21]</xref>
###xml 833 837 824 828 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Trachtman1">[31]</xref>
###xml 901 905 892 896 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Herzog2">[32]</xref>
###xml 1352 1356 1340 1344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Trachtman1">[31]</xref>
###xml 1358 1362 1346 1350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002278-Craig1">[57]</xref>
###xml 126 129 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1216 1220 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1321 1325 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Thy 1.1 nephritis is a reversible glomerular disease that affects mesangial cells. We observed a loss of meprinbeta signal in rat podocytes at day 11 following induction of the disease. We postulate that the damage occurring in the mesangial compartment may also lead to changes in podocytes in Thy 1.1 nephritis. In addition, we observed a marked alteration in meprinbeta staining of proximal tubular cells despite the fact that Thy 1.1 nephritis is a glomerular disease: the immunostaining revealed a shift from a linear staining confined to the apical brush border membrane in the corticomedullary junction towards a diffuse cytoplasmatic staining involving the tubular basement membrane. These findings are reminiscent of meprinbeta relocalizations reported in tubular nephritis such as in hypoxia/reperfusion injury [10], [21], [31] or in an acute kidney injury model of cisplatin nephrotoxicity [32]. These data provide evidence that high levels of meprin are present in locations other than the apical membrane exposing the protease to a distinct set of substrates not normally available to meprinbeta. These include proteins of the basement membranes and cell-cell adhesion complexes. It has been shown that mice with low renal meprin expression levels had less severe forms of structural injury when compared to mice with higher meprin levels [31], [57]. Therefore, expression of meprin in epithelial cells of proximal tubules appears to be detrimental in an already stressed kidney and thus worsens the disease course.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 108 111 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 519 524 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
This report is to our best knowledge the first to show expression of the metalloendopeptidase meprinbeta in rat glomeruli. Moreover, our results suggest that localization, balance and regulation of meprinbeta may constitute features in kidney disease progression. In order to investigate whether meprinbeta also has a direct, causal role in the pathogenesis of glomerulonephritis such as PHN, further experiments using animal models are needed. Moreover, it will be interesting to correlate our results with studies in human glomerulonephritis to assess a role of meprinbeta as a possible auto-antigen in membranous nephropathy.
###end p 40

Sections were then incubated with fluorescence labelled secondary antibody for 45 min at room temperature in the dark, 4,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) stained for 2 min, mounted in Dako Cytomation Fluorescence-Mounting Medium and sealed with a cover slip. To prove specificity of the antisera, we either re-placed the primary antibodies with normal rabbit serum (DAKO Diagnostic) or we pre-incubated the primary antibody with recombinant meprinbeta (competitive inhibition) [60]. For double labelling, we applied a second primary antibody for 1 hour. We used the following antibodies: primary rabbit polyclonal anti-meprinbeta, targeting amino acids 468-612 of meprinbeta, as previously described [61], primary mouse monoclonal anti-synaptopodin (Progen, Heidelberg, Germany), primary monoclonal mouse anti-Thy 1.1 (Oxford Biotechology, UK), secondary goat anti-rabbit and goat anti-mouse (Dako Cytomation, Denmark), diluted 1:500, 1:4, 1:20, 1:1000 and 1:400 respectively.
