###xml 71 74 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Voluntary exercise-induced changes in beta2-adrenoceptor signalling in rat ventricular myocytes
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 188 190 188 190 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>

###xml 1399 1400 1375 1376 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>

###xml 385 389 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
Regular exercise is beneficial to cardiovascular health. We tested whether mild voluntary exercise training modifies key myocardial parameters [ventricular mass, intracellular calcium ([Ca2+]i) handling and the response to beta-adrenoceptor (beta-AR) stimulation] in a manner distinct from that reported for beneficial, intensive training and pathological hypertrophic stimuli. Female rats performed voluntary wheel-running exercise for 6-7 weeks. The mRNA expression of target proteins was measured in left ventricular tissue using real-time reverse transcriptase-polymerase chain reaction. Simultaneous measurement of cell shortening and [Ca2+]i transients were made in single left ventricular myocytes and the inotropic response to beta1- and beta2-AR stimulation was measured. Voluntary exercise training resulted in cardiac hypertrophy, the heart weight to body weight ratio being significantly greater in trained compared with sedentary animals. However, voluntary exercise caused no significant alteration in the size or time course of myocyte shortening and [Ca2+]i transients or in the mRNA levels of key proteins that regulate Ca2+ handling. The positive inotropic response to beta1-AR stimulation and the level of beta1-AR mRNA were unaltered by voluntary exercise but both mRNA levels and inotropic response to beta2-AR stimulation were significantly reduced in trained animals. The beta2-AR inotropic response was restored by exposure to pertussis toxin. We propose that in contrast to pathological stimuli and to beneficial, intense exercise training, modulation of Ca2+ handling is not a major adaptive mechanism in the response to mild voluntary exercise. In addition, and in a reversal of the situation seen in heart failure, voluntary exercise training maintains the beta1-AR response but reduces the beta2-AR response. Therefore, although voluntary exercise induces cardiac hypertrophy, there are distinct differences between its effects on key myocardial regulatory mechanisms and those of hypertrophic stimuli that eventually cause cardiac decompensation.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 272 278 272 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 263 283 263 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9">Fletcher <italic>et al.</italic> 1996</xref>
###xml 417 438 417 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b25">Moore &amp; Korzick, 1995</xref>
Regular exercise induces a range of morphological and functional cardiovascular adaptations to meet increased physical demand. These adaptations are thought to be beneficial, and increased activity in sedentary populations is recommended by health professionals (Fletcher et al. 1996). Animal models of exercise are useful in the investigation of the cellular mechanisms associated with exercise-induced adaptations (Moore & Korzick, 1995). We wish to achieve a better understanding of the beneficial effects upon the heart of the regular, mild exercise that is recommended to the general population and how these changes may contrast with changes reported for pathological hypertrophic stimuli, such as hypertension, and the beneficial changes seen in response to intensive training seen in athletes.
###end p 2

###xml 839 841 839 841 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 907 913 907 913 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 899 918 899 918 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b47">Wisloff <italic>et al.</italic> 2001</xref>
###xml 925 931 925 931 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 920 936 920 936 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b13">Kemi <italic>et al.</italic> 2007</xref>
###xml 938 942 938 942 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14">2008</xref>
###xml 1032 1038 1032 1038 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1027 1043 1027 1043 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b15">Kemi <italic>et al.</italic> 2005</xref>
###xml 228 232 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
One outcome of regular exercise is cardiac hypertrophy, and we have previously described concentric hypertrophy and increased force production in single myocytes in response to several weeks of voluntary wheel running in female rats (Natali et al. 2002). Initial compensated cardiac hypertrophy also occurs in response to pathological hypertrophic stimuli, such as hypertension, where an increase in foetal gene expression, the release of neurohormonal factors, including naturetic peptides, and an increase in inflammatory cytokines accompany the increase in myocardial mass. In addition, there is a modification of intracellular Ca2+ handling such that an increase in the amplitude of the intracellular Ca2+ transient and, as a consequence, an increase in myocyte shortening occur (Brooksby et al. 1993; Gomez et al. 1997). Changes in Ca2+ handling are also seen in response to exercise training (Wisloff et al. 2001; Kemi et al. 2007, 2008). Interestingly, these occur in a graded manner, dependent upon exercise intensity (Kemi et al. 2005).

###begin p 4
###xml 346 347 334 335 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 376 377 361 362 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>

###xml 421 437 403 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b51">Xiao <italic>et al.</italic> 2003</xref>
###xml 538 544 520 526 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 530 549 512 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2">Barbier <italic>et al.</italic> 2004</xref>
###xml 605 611 587 593 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 599 616 581 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b44">Werle <italic>et al.</italic> 1990</xref>
###xml 636 642 618 624 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 618 647 600 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b41">Sylvestre-Gervais <italic>et al.</italic> 1982</xref>
###xml 792 814 774 796 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b53">Yancey &amp; Overton, 1993</xref>
###xml 824 830 806 812 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 816 835 798 817 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b27">Moraska <italic>et al.</italic> 2000</xref>
###xml 843 849 825 831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 837 854 819 836 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b7">Brown <italic>et al.</italic> 2007</xref>
###xml 1031 1037 1010 1016 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1023 1042 1002 1021 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b27">Moraska <italic>et al.</italic> 2000</xref>
###xml 162 170 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 524 528 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
In heart failure, enhanced sympathetic tone and thus sustained beta-adrenoceptor (beta-AR) activation initially provide the required contractile compensation for patients with ventricular impairment. However, over time the contractile response to continued beta-AR stimulation becomes desensitized, resulting in a selective downregulation of beta1-AR density and a higher beta2:beta1 receptor ratio (Bristow et al. 1986; Xiao et al. 2003). Similar observations have been reported in cases of exercise-induced hypertrophy in rats (Barbier et al. 2004), although not all studies share these findings (Werle et al. 1990; Sylvestre-Gervais et al. 1982). However, these studies used enforced, intense exercise regimes that may cause stress adaptations in addition to exercise-induced adaptations (Yancey & Overton, 1993; Moraska et al. 2000; Brown et al. 2007). Importantly, with regard to beta-AR signalling, increased levels of circulating catecholamines and increased adrenal weight are well-established responses to stress (Moraska et al. 2000).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 204 206 204 206 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 54 57 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
In this study, we have used a voluntary wheel-running rat model to test the null hypothesis that (in contrast to pathological hypertrophic stimuli) changes in the expression of hypertrophic markers and Ca2+ handling do not occur in response to mild, voluntary exercise. Additionally, the effect of voluntary exercise training on the response to beta-AR stimulation has not previously been tested; thus, the second null hypothesis was that the response to beta-AR stimulation is unaltered by voluntary exercise training.
###end p 5

###begin title 9
Exercise training model
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 213 219 213 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 205 224 205 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b33">Rodnick <italic>et al.</italic> 1992</xref>
###xml 388 394 388 394 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 381 399 381 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b28">Natali <italic>et al.</italic> 2001</xref>
###xml 401 405 401 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b29">2002</xref>
###xml 563 573 557 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ad libitum</italic>
###xml 7 26 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Sprague-Dawley rats</span>
###xml 128 132 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 227 231 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Rats</span>
###xml 283 287 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 544 547 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Female Sprague-Dawley rats were weight- and age-matched and assigned to either a sedentary (SED) or trained group (TRN). Female rats have been shown to be more likely than males to run spontaneously (e.g. Rodnick et al. 1992). Rats were housed individually in plastic cages, and TRN rats had free access over a 6-7 week period to a vertical running wheels attached to their cages (Natali et al. 2001, 2002). Daily running distance was recorded. All animals were housed at 21degreesC with a 12 h-12 h light-dark cycle and had access to standard rat chow and water ad libitum.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
Cardiac myocyte isolation and contractility
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 333 334 333 334 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 364 365 364 365 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 374 375 374 375 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 377 378 377 378 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 389 390 389 390 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 680 686 674 680 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 671 691 665 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8">Calaghan <italic>et al.</italic> 1998</xref>
###xml 31 35 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
Following the training period, rats were weighed and killed humanely by cervical dislocation following stunning using Schedule 1 procedures sanctioned by the UK Home Office. The heart was rapidly removed and extraneous tissue dissected away. The heart was flushed with a modified Hepes-Tyrode solution of the following composition (mm): 130 NaCl, 5.4 KCl, 1.4 MgCl2, 0.4 NaH2PO4, 0.75 CaCl2, 5 Hepes, 10 glucose, 20 taurine and 10 creatine, pH 7.4 with NaOH at room temperature (20-22degreesC). The heart was then blotted and weighed before being mounted onto a Langendorff perfusion apparatus for isolation of myocytes using a collagenase-protease dispersion technique (Calaghan et al. 1998). At the end of the perfusion, the ventricles were separated and weighed. Single myocytes from the left ventricular free wall were isolated by mechanical dispersion. Cells were stored at 20-22degreesC in the modified Hepes-Tyrode solution and used within 8 h.

Following isolation, cells were added to an experimental chamber (volume 0.5 ml) on the stage of an inverted microscope (Nikon, Japan) and superfused at 37degreesC with solution of the following composition (mm): 135 Na+, 5 K+, 1 Mg2+, 102 Cl-, 20 HCO32-, 1 SO42-, 1 Ca2+, 20 acetate, 10 glucose and 5 units l-1 insulin. This solution was equilibrated with 5% CO2-95% O2 to give a pH of 7.35. Quiescent, rod-shaped cells were field stimulated to contract at a rate of 1 Hz with a pulse width of 5 ms. Simultaneous measurement of myocyte contractility and intracellular Ca2+ ([Ca2+]i) transients was carried out as previously described (Natali et al. 2002). Briefly, myocyte cell shortening was measured, using a video-edge detection system and expressed as a percentage of resting cell length. Cells were loaded with the fluorescent Ca2+ indicator fura 2 (acetoxymethyl ester form, 3 mum for 10 min) and illuminated alternately with light of 340 and 380 nm. The resultant fluorescence was collected at 510 +/- 20 nm and [Ca2+]i indexed as the 340:380 ratio. The time course of contraction and [Ca2+]i transient amplitudes was monitored as the time from stimulation to peak (in ms) and as the rate of decline in contraction (in mum s-1) and [Ca2+]i (in ratio units s-1) from peak to half-peak levels.

When basal contractile activity was established, cell shortening in response to either beta1-AR stimulation [10-7m ICI 118,551 in combination with 10-7m isoprenaline (ISO)] or beta2-AR stimulation (5 x 10-5m salbutamol in the presence of 10-7m atenolol) or both (10-9-10-6m isoprenaline) was recorded (Xiao & Lakatta, 1993). ###begin title 15
Isolation of RNA
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 404 410 398 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 396 415 390 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b48">Wittwer <italic>et al.</italic> 2002</xref>
###xml 714 716 704 706 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 740 742 718 720 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 1342 1344 1303 1305 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 95 113 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Sprague-Dawley rat</span>
Left ventricular free wall tissue samples were excised from 10 trained and 10 sedentary female Sprague-Dawley rat hearts, snap frozen in liquid nitrogen between the large flat ends of a pair of tongs and samples harvested on a cryostat. All samples were stored at -80degreesC until RNA extraction. Total RNA extraction was performed using a modified Qiagen mini-kit protocol for striated muscle (Wittwer et al. 2002). The cryostat sections were homogenized (rotorstrator homogenizer, Ultra Turrax T8, IKA, Staufen, Germany) in 666 mul RLT-beta-mercaptoethanol lysis buffer for 2 min. diethylpyrocarbonate (DEPC)-treated water and proteinase K was added to give a final volume of 2 ml (final concentration 24 mAU ml-1 approximately 0.8 mg ml-1; Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) and the mixture incubated at 45degreesC for 1.5-2 h. Samples were then centrifuged at 17949 g at 20-22degreesC for 5 min and the supernatant retained. Two millilitres of RLT-beta-mercaptoethanol was added prior to 2 ml ethanol before binding of the RNA to the Qiagen Mini-Column. DNAse was added to the column to prevent contamination from co-extracted DNA (RNAse free kit, Qiagen). Samples of RNA were separated by electrophoresis through 0.6% formaldehyde-1% agarose gels and visualized by ethidium bromide staining, and the concentration of RNA was adjusted to 1 mug mul-1. Cloned DNA was prepared from 4 mug of total RNA with random priming using Superscript III first-strand synthesis system (Invitrogen, Life Technologies, Rockville, MD, USA) and diluted 1:10 in Tris/EDTA buffer before use in real-time reverse transcriptase-polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR).
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Real-time RT-PCR
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 207 213 207 213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 200 218 200 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b40">Stones <italic>et al.</italic> 2007</xref>
###xml 1074 1075 1048 1049 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</italic>
###xml 1075 1076 1049 1050 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T</sub>
###xml 1254 1278 1228 1252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b20">Livak &amp; Schmittgen, 2001</xref>
Real-time RT-PCR was performed using TaqMan low-density arrays (Micro Fluidic Cards, Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA) using the ABI RISM 7900HT sequence detection system (Applied Biosystems; Stones et al. 2007). Each card consists of 384 wells, preloaded with predesigned fluorogenic TaqMan probes and primers, configured to allow detection of selected transcripts for eight samples. The probes were labelled with a fluorescent reporter dye, 6-carboxyfluorescein (FAM, Applera Corp., Norwalk, CT, USA), on the 5' end and with non-fluorescent quencher on the 3' end. cDNA containing the equivalent of 77 ng of RNA was mixed with 50 mul of TaqMan Universial PCR master mix (Applied Biosystems) to give a final volume of 100 mul and loaded into each well. The Microfluidic cards were thermal cycled at 50degreesC for 2 min and 94.5degreesC for 10 min, followed by 40 cycles at 97degreesC for 30 s and 59.7degreesC for 1 min. Data were collected with instrument spectral compensations by the Applied Biosystems SDS 2.2 software, and analysed using the threshold cycle (CT) relative quantification method, data being normalized to an aggregate of 36 transcripts rather than GAPDH or 18S alone to reduce data variability and increase reproducibility (Livak & Schmittgen, 2001).
###end p 18

###xml 425 443 425 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b30">Naylor <italic>et al.</italic> 2008</xref>
###xml 72 75 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 463 469 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Consistent with our previous findings using the voluntary wheel-running rat model (Natali et al. 2001, 2002), cardiac hypertrophy occurs in response to voluntary exercise. The type of exercise undertaken involves multiple short periods of running interspersed with short breaks under a low load associated with the resistance and inertia of the wheel. This results in concentric hypertrophy of the myocytes. Recent analysis (Naylor et al. 2008) indicates that in humans concentric hypertrophy can arise in response to both resistance and endurance training.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 117 123 117 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 112 128 112 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1">Arai <italic>et al.</italic> 1995</xref>
###xml 139 145 139 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 130 150 130 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b52">Yamazaki <italic>et al.</italic> 1996</xref>
###xml 160 166 160 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 152 171 152 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b37">Serneri <italic>et al.</italic> 1999</xref>
###xml 476 482 476 482 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 467 487 467 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b43">Thibault <italic>et al.</italic> 1995</xref>
###xml 447 450 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Endothelin 1 is implicated in the development of pathological hypertrophy resulting from haemodynamic overload (Arai et al. 1995; Yamazaki et al. 1996; Serneri et al. 1999). Whilst there was no change in the molecular correlates of ET-1 receptor type A in our model of physiological cardiac hypertrophy, there was a significant increase in the ET-1 B receptor. The latter constitute only 25% of the total population of endothelin receptors in the rat left ventricle (Thibault et al. 1995).
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 242 248 242 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 233 253 233 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b24">McMullen <italic>et al.</italic> 2003</xref>
###xml 255 259 255 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b23">2004</xref>
###xml 390 396 390 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 381 401 381 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b23">McMullen <italic>et al.</italic> 2004</xref>
In the heart, the insulin-like growth factor (IGF) and phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3-kinase) pathway has been linked with normal cardiac development and the generation of physiological hypertrophy in response to treadmill training (McMullen et al. 2003, 2004). When the IGF receptor 1 is overexpressed, compensated hypertrophy ensues that is similar to physiological hypertrophy (McMullen et al. 2004). However, the gene expression of the IGF-1 signalling pathway was not increased in our model of exercise-induced hypertrophy. Our experimental design imposed a single monitoring point for mRNA levels at 6-7 weeks; therefore, it is possible that an earlier sampling point when daily running is increasing rapidly may reveal changes in the expression of the genes responsible for the hypertrophic response.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 165 171 162 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 156 176 153 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b16">Kinnunen <italic>et al.</italic> 1993</xref>

###xml 700 725 693 718 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b11">Gwechenberger <italic>et al.</italic> 1999</xref>
###xml 980 986 965 971 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 973 991 958 976 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19">Kubota <italic>et al.</italic> 1997</xref>
###xml 1009 1015 994 1000 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 993 1020 978 1005 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b39">Sivasubramanian <italic>et al.</italic> 2001</xref>
###xml 590 593 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">SED</span>
###xml 837 852 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
Upregulation of the gene expression of atrial and brain natriuretic peptides and beta-myosin heavy chain are hallmark features of pathological hypertrophy (Kinnunen et al. 1993; Bhatia et al. 2003). The expression of these genes was unchanged in TRN hearts, demonstrating either distinct signalling pathways for physiological and pathological hypertrophy or, alternatively, that the severity of the physiological hypertrophic signals is less than that induced during pathological conditions. Furthermore, the inflammatory cytokine, TNF-alpha, was significantly reduced in TRN compared with SED. Cytokines are indicative of the inflammatory status of a tissue (Kapadia et al. 1995; Sheng et al. 1997; Gwechenberger et al. 1999). Tumour necrosis factor alpha can stimulate the hypertrophy and apoptosis of cultured cardiac myocytes, while transgenic mice that overexpress a secreted form of TNF-alpha develop concentric hypertrophy that progresses to dilated cardiomyopathy (Kubota et al. 1997; Sivasubramanian et al. 2001). Thus, voluntary moderate exercise improved the inflammatory status of the myocardium.
###end p 50

###xml 660 662 660 662 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 731 733 731 733 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 941 947 941 947 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 934 952 934 952 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b29">Natali <italic>et al.</italic> 2002</xref>
###xml 989 991 989 991 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 992 993 992 993 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 1161 1163 1161 1163 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 172 176 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 1119 1124 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Previous studies have reported changes in Ca2+ handling in hypertension-induced cardiac hypertrophy that result in increased contraction, e.g in spontaneously hypertensive rats (Brooksby et al. 1993; Gomez et al. 1997; McCrossan et al. 2004). Enforced, intense exercise training protocols have also been reported to alter myocyte Ca2+ transients and contraction (Wisloff et al. 2001; Kemi et al. 2007, 2008). These changes are graded, with smaller or no changes seen in some parameters with moderate enforced exercise regimes (Kemi et al. 2005). Our findings are consistent with this grading of response, since we find that mild exercise causes no change in Ca2+ handing or cell shortening or in the gene expression of the major Ca2+ handling proteins. These findings also support our earlier conclusion that increased absolute force development from such myocytes results principally from the increase in their cross-sectional area (Natali et al. 2002) rather than increased levels of [Ca2+]i. Thus, we predict that while all levels of exercise will result in increased myocardial mass, intense training undertaken by human athletes will additionally modify Ca2+ handling whilst mild exercise in the general population will not.
###end p 51

###xml 1173 1179 1140 1146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1165 1184 1132 1151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b27">Moraska <italic>et al.</italic> 2000</xref>
###xml 1192 1198 1159 1165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1186 1203 1153 1170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b7">Brown <italic>et al.</italic> 2007</xref>
###xml 83 89 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 125 129 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 298 302 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 742 746 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 812 816 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
In normal myocardium, there is a greater density of beta1-AR than beta2-AR in both humans (77%:23%; Bristow et al. 1986) and rats (56%:44%; Xiao et al. 2003). Several studies have reported a decrease in ventricular beta-AR density and the contractile response of myocytes to beta-AR stimulation in rats subjected to a variety of training regimes (Sylvestre-Gervais et al. 1982; Takeda et al. 1985; Plourde et al. 1991; Nieto et al. 1996). Werle et al. (1990) reported a swim-training induced decrease in beta1- and beta2-ARs with no affect on the ratio of beta1 to beta2. More recently, Barbier et al. (2004) reported a training-induced decrease in beta1-AR with no modification of beta2-AR following an 8 week treadmill training protocol in rats. In contrast, other studies found no change in beta-AR number in rats undertaking treadmill running regimes (Moore et al. 1982; Bohm et al. 1993; Scarpace et al. 1994; Roth et al. 1998) or swim training (Williams, 1980; Williams et al. 1984). Subtle differences in methodological approaches and the potential effect on circulating catecholamines that stressful, enforced exercise regimes can have (Yancey et al. 1993; Moraska et al. 2000, Brown et al. 2007) may explain the differences between observations in these studies.

###xml 374 375 362 363 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 416 417 401 402 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 480 481 462 463 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 516 517 495 496 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 559 560 535 536 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 615 616 588 589 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 670 671 640 641 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 723 724 689 690 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 776 777 739 740 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
In response to voluntary wheel-running exercise, we observed a training-induced decrease in response to mixed beta-AR stimulation in left ventricular myocytes without an effect upon the EC50 of the contractile and [Ca2+]i transient responses to isoprenaline. Investigation of beta-AR subtypes revealed that this probably results from an effect upon beta2-AR rather than beta1-AR, given that we saw a decrease in beta2-AR mRNA and decreased contractile function in response to beta2-AR stimulation with unchanged beta1-AR responses. Although the change in beta2-AR mRNA might suggest a decrease in the number of beta2-ARs, the modulation of the inotropic response to beta2-AR stimulation was reversed by inhibition of Galphai, suggesting that both the number of functional beta2-AR and postreceptor signalling are involved.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 309 310 303 304 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 319 320 310 311 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 333 339 324 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 325 344 316 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b5">Bristow <italic>et al.</italic> 1986</xref>
###xml 351 357 342 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 346 362 337 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b51">Xiao <italic>et al.</italic> 2003</xref>
Interestingly, our observations are the reverse of the situation seen in chronic heart failure and pathological cases of cardiac hypertrophy, where continued exposure of the myocardium to excessive levels of circulating catecholamines diminishes beta-AR function as a result of a decrease in the ratio of beta1- to beta2-AR (Bristow et al. 1986; Xiao et al. 2003).
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 225 231 225 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 216 236 216 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b36">Serikawa <italic>et al.</italic> 2003</xref>
###xml 245 251 245 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 238 256 238 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b22">MacKay <italic>et al.</italic> 2004</xref>
###xml 442 471 442 471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b17">Kornitzer &amp; Ciechanover, 2000</xref>
###xml 473 493 473 493 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b18">Kostova &amp; Wolf, 2003</xref>
###xml 596 598 596 598 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
When measuring mRNA levels, it must be acknowledged that this may not always indicate protein levels, because RT-PCR methods, used to quantify mRNA, measure total mRNA rather than the active, ribose-associated mRNA (Serikawa et al. 2003; MacKay et al. 2004). However, total protein levels are not always indicative of functional protein levels because of post-transcriptional variations in protein degradation or trafficking to active sites (Kornitzer & Ciechanover, 2000; Kostova & Wolf, 2003). Therefore, in addition to mRNA levels of selected targets, we measured the functional correlates (Ca2+ transients, inotropic response to beta-AR stimulation) of relevant channel/receptor activity.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 315 317 315 317 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 318 319 318 319 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 352 353 349 350 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 373 374 367 368 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
In summary, although mild voluntary exercise results in cardiac hypertrophy, there are distinguishing features between the response to voluntary exercise and to both pathological causes of hypertrophy and beneficial hypertrophy caused by intense exercise regimes. These include an absence in the modification of [Ca2+]i handling and a targeting of beta2-AR rather than beta1-AR signalling.
###end p 56
