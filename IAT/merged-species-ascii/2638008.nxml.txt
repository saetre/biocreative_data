Altered Hematopoiesis in Mice Lacking DNA Polymerase mu Is Due to Inefficient Double-Strand Break Repair
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1

###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 132 139 131 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vivo</italic>
###xml 277 280 266 269 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 281 285 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Polymerase mu (Polmu) is an error-prone, DNA-directed DNA polymerase that participates in non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ) repair. In vivo, Polmu deficiency results in impaired Vkappa-Jkappa recombination and altered somatic hypermutation and centroblast development. In Polmu-/- mice, hematopoietic development was defective in several peripheral and bone marrow (BM) cell populations, with about a 40% decrease in BM cell number that affected several hematopoietic lineages. Hematopoietic progenitors were reduced both in number and in expansion potential. The observed phenotype correlates with a reduced efficiency in DNA double-strand break (DSB) repair in hematopoietic tissue. Whole-body gamma-irradiation revealed that Polmu also plays a role in DSB repair in non-hematopoietic tissues. Our results show that Polmu function is required for physiological hematopoietic development with an important role in maintaining early progenitor cell homeostasis and genetic stability in hematopoietic and non-hematopoietic tissues.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Author Summary
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 886 890 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1078 1082 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1283 1288 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Double-strand breaks (DSB) in DNA are a highly deleterious type of genetic damage, potentially causing genomic rearrangements or cell death if unrepaired. DSB can be triggered by environmental factors (such as electromagnetic radiation or clastogenic chemicals) or normal cell metabolism. The main mechanism of DSB repair in mammals is thought to be the non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ) pathway. Our article describes how DNA polymerase mu (Polmu), a recently identified component of the NHEJ machinery, is required for hematopoiesis-the process that generates and maintains the correct balance of the millions of blood cells needed to sustain life and defend against infection. Hematopoietic stem cells (HSC) divide asymmetrically, yielding another HSC and a progenitor cell. These progenitors proliferate and differentiate, their progeny eventually generating mature blood cells. In mice in which Polmu is genetically eliminated, we found that hematopoietic progenitors proliferate slowly and are functionally impaired. The incidence of DSB in hematopoietic cells from these mice is increased, suggesting that reduced DNA repair may be the cause of the hematopoietic defects. DNA damage was also increased in tissues unrelated to hematopoiesis, including liver, kidney, lung, and mouse embryonic fibroblasts. Thus, these results demonstrate that Polmu plays an important role in general DSB repair in many cell lineages.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Introduction
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 118 121 118 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Wyman1">[1]</xref>
###xml 170 173 170 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Lieber1">[2]</xref>
###xml 174 177 174 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Weterings1">[4]</xref>
###xml 261 264 261 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Kruger1">[5]</xref>
###xml 265 268 265 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Takata1">[6]</xref>
###xml 310 313 310 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Allen1">[7]</xref>
###xml 314 317 314 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Lee1">[9]</xref>
In higher eukaryotes, DNA double strand breaks (DSB) are repaired through two main pathways: homologous recombination [1] and non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ) (reviewed [2]-[4]). Although NHEJ predominates during the G0/G1 and early S phases of the cell cycle [5],[6], both mechanisms can act in coordination [7]-[9].
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 139 143 138 142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Aoufouchi1">[10]</xref>
###xml 144 148 143 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Ruiz2">[13]</xref>
###xml 204 208 202 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Dominguez1">[11]</xref>
###xml 360 364 357 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Dominguez1">[11]</xref>
###xml 365 369 362 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Lucas1">[14]</xref>
###xml 528 532 520 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Mahajan1">[15]</xref>
###xml 681 685 667 671 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Mahajan1">[15]</xref>
###xml 687 695 673 681 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vitro</italic>
###xml 780 784 765 769 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Mahajan1">[15]</xref>
###xml 785 789 770 774 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-NickMcElhinny1">[17]</xref>
Polymerase mu (Polmu) is an error-prone DNA-directed DNA polymerase belonging to the PolX family, with a high amino acid similarity to TdT [10]-[13] and, like TdT, Polmu has terminal transferase activity [11]. Unlike TdT, Polmu is expressed in many tissues (liver, kidney, lung, brain, testis), although it is especially abundant in lymphohematopoietic organs [11],[14]. Polmu interacts with components of the NHEJ repair pathway (Ku 70/80) and is up-regulated after the induction of DSB by gamma-irradiation, a known clastogen [15]. Double immunostaining for Polmu and phosphorylated gamma-H2AX, a modified histone found at DSB sites, shows that Polmu is recruited to these sites [15]. In vitro, Polmu participates in specific NHEJ reactions that require DNA polymerase activity [15]-[17].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 122 126 121 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Bertocci1">[18]</xref>
###xml 416 420 402 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Bertocci1">[18]</xref>
###xml 510 514 496 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-SoulasSprauel1">[19]</xref>
###xml 629 636 614 621 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 684 692 668 676 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 692 696 676 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Capp1">[16]</xref>
###xml 29 34 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Analysis of a Polmu knockout mouse model has identified a specialized function for this enzyme during V(D)J recombination [18]. Specifically, Polmu is required for correct recombination of the immunoglobulin kappa light chain during B cell development, and its deficiency results in shorter, non-productive Vkappa-Jkappa junctions and hence lymphocyte cell death at the transition from PreB to Immature B cell stage [18]. Because V(D)J recombination is essentially a modified end-joining reaction (reviewed in [19]), the participation of Polmu in this process demonstrates its requirement for at least a subset of NHEJ reactions in vivo. This role of Polmu has also been demonstrated in vitro[16].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 32 36 31 35 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Dominguez1">[11]</xref>
###xml 37 41 36 40 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Lucas1">[14]</xref>
###xml 364 368 363 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Nijnik1">[20]</xref>
###xml 369 373 368 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Rossi1">[21]</xref>
###xml 526 530 525 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Prasher1">[22]</xref>
###xml 627 631 626 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Navarro1">[23]</xref>
###xml 741 745 740 744 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Bender1">[24]</xref>
###xml 762 765 761 764 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 895 899 894 898 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Masutani1">[25]</xref>
###xml 1141 1145 1140 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Rossi1">[21]</xref>
###xml 1226 1231 1225 1230 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y288C</sup>
###xml 1238 1242 1237 1241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Nijnik1">[20]</xref>
###xml 1456 1460 1455 1459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Nijnik1">[20]</xref>
###xml 1461 1465 1460 1464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Rossi1">[21]</xref>
###xml 407 411 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 535 539 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 658 662 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 766 770 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 901 905 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 1069 1073 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1190 1195 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1232 1236 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The expression pattern of Polmu [11],[14] suggests that it might participate in NHEJ in other tissues, especially in non-B cell hematopoietic populations. In recent years it has been demonstrated that several DNA repair pathways play a clear role in the maintenance of the hematopoietic system and of hematopoietic stem cell (HSC) number and function during aging [20],[21]. For example, in Ercc1-deficient mice hematopoiesis is impaired and bone marrow cellularity and hematopoietic progenitor cell (HPC) numbers are reduced [22]; in mice deficient in Brca2, the reconstitution capacity of HSC and progenitor cells is reduced [23]. Hypomorphic Rad50 mutant mice have growth defects and are predisposed to cancer and progressive HSC failure [24]. Irradiated Parp-/- mice are myelosuppressed, display extensive hemorrhaging and show altered extramedullary hematopoiesis during the recovery phase [25]. Mice deficient either in XPD (from the nucleotide excision repair pathway) or Ku80 (NHEJ) show reduced HPC numbers in bone marrow. Although the numbers of HSC in these mice is unaltered, HSC reconstitution potential during aging is reduced [21]. A similar phenotype is observed in another mouse model of NHEJ deficiency (Lig4Y288C mice; [20]), and together these reports strongly suggest that HSC accumulate mutations during aging that impair their function and highlight the role of the different repair pathways in maintaining hematopoietic homeostasis [20],[21].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 85 88 83 86 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 254 257 251 254 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 89 93 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We have investigated the role of Polmu in hematopoiesis and general DNA repair. Polmu-/- mice have reduced numbers of most peripheral blood populations and show impaired hematopoiesis as a result of the reduced numbers of hematopoietic progenitors. Polmu-/- hematopoietic and non-hematopoietic cells accumulate DSB, both spontaneously and after gamma-irradiation, indicating a role for Polmu in general DSB repair.
###end p 12

The generation of Polmu-/- mice has been described previously [14]. Mutant and wildtype (wt) mice were bred in our specific pathogen-free facilities and were routinely screened for pathogens. Most experiments were carried out with animals in the mixed original (129/Balb/c) background. Where indicated, experiments were carried out in the C57BL/6 background. B6.SJL mice, were purchased from The Jackson Laboratory (Bar Harbor, Maine), and housed at the CIEMAT animal facility. All experiments were performed according to Spanish and European regulations for the use and treatment of experimental animals, with the approval of the Centro Nacional de Biotecnologia (CNB) and the Fundacion Centro Nacional de Investigaciones Cardiovasculares (CNIC) animal ethics committees.

###begin title 16
Bone Marrow and Spleen Cell Suspensions
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 241 242 241 242 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 36 40 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Femurs and tibias were removed from mice, and bone marrow was extracted by complete flushing with PBS under sterile conditions. Spleens were retrieved and disaggregated in sterile PBS. In each case, tissue extracts were incubated in 0.85% NH4Cl to lyse erythrocytes. Cells were counted with a hemocytometer and used in subsequent assays.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Hematological, Histological, Flow Cytometry, and Cell Cycle Analyses
###end title 18
###begin p 19
Blood was collected in a capillary tube from the retro-orbital sinus and transferred to EDTA-coated tubes. For hematology, 25 microl were analyzed in an MS9 machine (Kemia, Madrid, Spain). For flow cytometry (FCM), whole blood was incubated with the corresponding antibodies, fixed, and erythrocytes lysed with OptiLyse C (Immunotech). Cells were analyzed in a Cytomics FC 500 cytometer (Beckman Coulter). Bone marrow cells were stained with antibodies in ice-cold PBS, 0.5% BSA, 2 mM EDTA. Antibodies used were anti-CD19-PE, -CD19-SPRD, -CD3-FITC, -CD4-SPRD, -CD8-PE, -CD11b (Mac1)-PE, -Gr1-FITC, -B220-FITC, -B220-PE, -IgM-PE, -Ter119-PE, -CD41-FITC, (BD-Pharmingen). Cell cycle status was monitored by estimating total cell DNA content in a flow cytometer after staining 70% methanol fixed cells with 10 microg/ml propidium iodide and 10 microg/ml RNase A.
###end p 19
###begin p 20
###xml 975 979 975 979 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Roederer1">[26]</xref>
For bone marrow progenitor analysis, bone marrow cells were extracted from femurs and tibias, counted, and stained in ice-cold PBS, 0.5% BSA plus 2 mM EDTA. Except for samples for detection of CMPs, GMPs and MEPs, samples were blocked with FcBlock (anti-CD16/CD32, BD Pharmingen). Samples were stained with the following antibodies: biotin-conjugated lineage antibody cocktail (anti-CD3e, -CD11b, -B220, -Ly6G/Ly6C and -TER-119), anti-CD117 (c-kit) (FITC or PE), anti-CD16/32-FITC, anti-CD135-PE, anti-Sca1-PE-Cy7 (all from BD Pharmingen); anti-CD127-Biotin, anti-CD127-PE (eBiosciences); anti-CD34-PE-Cy5 (Biolegend), and Streptavidin-Pacific Blue (Invitrogen). To exclude dead cells Hoechst 33582 was added to every sample. All samples were acquired with a BDFASCanto II cytometer (BDBiosciences). At least 1 million cells were acquired for each sample. Data were analyzed with FlowJo software (Tree Star, Inc.) and fluorescent-minus-one controls were used to define gates [26].
###end p 20
###begin p 21
For histology, femurs or tibias were formalin-fixed, decalcified and paraffin-embedded. Sections (5 microm) were hematoxylin-eosin stained. Photographs were acquired with a Leica DM RB microscope fitted with a DP 70 digital camera (Olympus) and linked to DP controller software (Olympus).
###end p 21

Splenocytes from wt or Polmu-/- animals were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium (Biowhitaker) supplemented with 10% FCS, non-essential amino-acids, 2 mM L-glutamine, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, 10 U/mL penicillin/estreptomycin, 10 microg/ml LPS (Sigma) and 10 ng/ml IL-4 (Preprotek Inc.). ###end title 24
###begin p 25

To evaluate proliferation during stimulation with SCF and IL-3, bone marrow cells (106) were seeded in Iscove's modified Dulbecco medium (IMDM) supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum (Gibco-BRL), 10% conditioned medium from IL-3-producing WEHI-3B cells, and 50 ng/ml human SCF (Stem Cell Technologies). For long-term bone marrow cultures (LTBMC), whole bone marrow suspensions were seeded in Methocult 5300 (Stem Cell Technologies) with 10-6 M hydrocortisone (Sigma), and incubated (33degreesC, 5% CO2) for 1-4 weeks as indicated, with a weekly culture medium change.
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 317 318 307 308 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>

Mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEF) were grown in Dulbecco's Modified Eagle's Medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% FCS, 2 mM L-glutamine, 10 mM HEPES, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, non-essential amino acids, and 50 microg/ml gentamycin (all from BioWhittaker). Cell cultures were maintained at 37degreesC, 95% humidity and 5% CO2.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 267 268 261 262 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
For MEF growth assays, embryos were minced with a razor blade and incubated in 0.25% trypsin, 0.1% EDTA for 30 min at 37degreesC. The cell suspension was plated in DMEM, 10% FCS containing antibiotics. Confluent cultures were split and passage cultures plated at 1x106 cells on 10-cm tissue culture plates (Falcon). Cells were trypsin passaged every 3-4 days. Cumulative cell growth was calculated with the formula PD = log(nf-ni)log2, where PD is population doubling and ni and nf are the initial and final number of cells.
###end p 27

###xml 1802 1805 1775 1778 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 431 435 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 574 578 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1058 1062 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1284 1288 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1431 1435 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1505 1509 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1806 1810 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Polmu is widely expressed, but is especially abundant in lympohematopoietic tissues and cells [11],[14]. Polmu is known to participate in DSB repair reactions in vitro[15]-[17], but the only in vivo role assigned so far is in Vkappa-Jkappa recombination during B cell development [18]. To investigate whether Polmu has a function in other hematopoietic lineages, we first studied peripheral blood (PB) cell populations in Polmu-/- mice. Concurring with the reduction in B cell numbers associated with abnormal Vkappa-Jkappa recombination, [18], the B cell count in Polmu-/- mice was 4.9-fold (p<0.001) lower than in wt controls (Figure 1A; CD19+ cells). Monocyte numbers were also reduced (2.2-fold, p<0.01, Figure 1A; Mac1+ Gr1- cells), as were neutrophil numbers, though here the difference was not statistically significant (p<0.09, Figure 1A; Mac1+Gr1+ cells). The only cell type analyzed that was apparently unaffected was the T cell lineage (CD3+ cells). Hematological analysis revealed moderate thrombocytopenia, with 403x109 platelets/ml in Polmu-/- mice, compared with 663x109 platelets/ml in wt counterparts (p<0.01, Figure 1B); this was associated with increased bleeding times (Figure S1) Thus peripheral blood populations other than B lymphocytes are altered in Polmu-/- mice. To assess whether the defective blood population profile originated in bone marrow (BM), we compared BM cell numbers between wt and Polmu-/- mice. Our results show that BM cellularity was 1.5-fold lower in Polmu-/- mice (p<0.001, Figure 1C). Immunohistochemistry confirmed that Polmu-/- BM contained fewer cells than wt BM, as shown by the dilated endothelial bone marrow sinusoids (Figure 1D). Therefore the most likely cause of the thrombocytopenia and reduced myeloid cell numbers in peripheral blood in Polmu-/- mice is a defect in BM hematopoiesis.

To investigate the function of Polmu during hematopoiesis, we analyzed wt and Polmu-/- (C57BL/6) BM cells by flow cytometry (FCM). The B lymphoid and myeloid compartments were significantly reduced in Polmu-/- BM (p<0.05), and there was a more moderate reduction in erythroid cell numbers (Figure 1E). To determine whether the reduction in B cell numbers was exclusively due to the previously described Vkappa-Jkappa defect [18], we used FCM to analyze B cell development in the bone marrow of Polmu-/- or wt C57BL/6 mice (see Figure 1F for representative plots and gating strategies). With the exception of PreB cells (B220+IgM-CD43-CD25+ cells), all BM B cell populations were significantly reduced in Polmu-/- BM. PreB cell frequency was increased in Polmu-/- BM (Figure 1H), indicating an accumulation at the PreB stage, probably due to a blockade at the PreB-to-immature (IgM+) cell transition. Similar results were obtained with Polmu-/- animals in the mixed background (not shown). These results suggest that low B cell numbers in Polmu-/- mice are not only the result of deficient Vkappa-Jkappa recombination but also reflect altered hematopoiesis prior the specific defect during Vkappa-Jkappa recombination described previously [18], suggesting that Polmu deficiency affects most hematopoietic lineages in bone marrow.

Hematopoiesis is a clonal process in which immature progenitors give rise to more committed progenitors. We therefore used colony forming unit (CFU) assays to determine total progenitor numbers in wt and Polmu-/- BM (Figure 2A-D). The numbers of PreB and myeloid progenitors (CFU-PreB and CFU-C) in Polmu-/-mice were 2.2- and 1.3-fold lower than in wt (p<0.001, Figure 2A,B). Megakaryocytic and erythroid progenitors (CFU-Mk and BFU-E) were also affected, though to a lesser extent (Figure 2C,D). Importantly, whereas granulomonocyte and monocyte progenitors (CFU-GM and CFU-M) were reduced in Polmu-/- BM (correlating with the peripheral blood monocyte deficiency), granulocyte progenitors (CFU-G) were not, thus explaining the absence of neutropenia (Figure 1A). These results show that Polmu deficient mice have lowered numbers of committed progenitors and suggest that lack of Polmu differentially affects lineage-specific progenitors.

To examine this in more depth, we next quantified hematopoietic lineage precursors by FCM. The populations measured were common lymphoid progenitors (CLP) [31], defined as Lin-IL7R-Sca1+c-kit+ cells, (Figure 2E); common myeloid progenitors (CMP), myelomonocytic progenitors (GMP) and megakaryocyte/erythroid progenitors (MEP) [32] (Figure 2F); hematopoietic stem cells were defined as Flt3- cells after gating on Lineage-c-kit+Sca1+[33] (Figure 2G). Although FCM analysis revealed that the frequency of these populations was little altered in Polmu-/- mice (Table S1), myeloid progenitors and stem cells were less abundant in the BM of Polmu-/- mice and some populations were more severely affected (Figure 2E-G); for example, HSC numbers were 39% lower in Polmu-/- BM (p<0.01). We then analyzed engraftment potential of Polmu-/- HSC in a competitive repopulation study [29]. In this experiment 2x106 BMNC (bone marrow nucleated cells) from male wt or Polmu-/- C57BL/6 mice were mixed with 2x106 competing cells from female B6.SJL mice (1:1), and the mixed population transplanted into lethally irradiated female B6.SJL recipients (see Figure 2H for experimental design). The percentage of donor and competitor cells was determined 16 weeks later and used to calculate the number of repopulating units (RU) in the donor BM. No differences were detected in either the frequency of (Figure 2I) or absolute (Figure 2J) RU numbers between wt and Polmu-/- bone marrow. These results indicate that although Polmu deficiency reduces the numbers of most hematopoietic progenitors and stem cell populations the defect can be rescued (at least partially), by transplantation into a healthy stroma (Figure 2I). Additionally, clonogenic analysis detected very few progenitors in Polmu-/- spleen (Figure S2), indicating that Polmu-/- mice do not compensate for the BM deficit with extramedullary hematopoiesis.

The low progenitor numbers in Polmu-/- mice could result either from impaired differentiation or from defective progenitor proliferation or self renewal capacity. To investigate this, we examined the proliferation of Polmu-/- hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells in vitro. In clonogenic differentiation assays, colonies of Polmu-/- progenitors were significantly smaller (50% smaller for CFU-C, p<0.05; 60% smaller for CFU-PreB, p<0.05) and contained fewer cells than wt colonies, indicating defective self-renewal or proliferative capacity (Figure 3A). This was confirmed by growing BMNC in conditions that promote myeloid progenitor expansion (see methods). After 4 days, wt BMNC cultures contained 2.3-fold more myeloid progenitors (p<0.001) and 2.8-fold more erythroid progenitors (p<0.01) than Polmu-/- cultures (Figure 3B). We then assayed BMNC in long-term bone marrow cell cultures (LTBMC, see methods). Two weeks after initiation, Polmu-/- cultures contained 35% fewer cells than wt (p<0.01, Figure 3C). Cell cycle analysis revealed an increased incidence of cell death in Polmu-/- cultures (Figure S3). Together with the bone marrow reconstitution experiments, these results suggest that Polmu-/- bone marrow stromal cells fail to efficiently support hematopoiesis and that Polmu-/- hematopoietic progenitor numbers do not expand in vitro as efficiently as WT progenitors.

One possible cause of reduced Polmu-/- hematopoietic precursors numbers is impaired DSB repair. To test this, we stained nucleated cells from bone marrow and spleen for phosphorylated gamma-H2AX, a marker of DNA double strand breaks [34]. Compared with wt, Polmu-/- bone marrow had a significantly higher number of DSB per nucleus (3.8 fold, p<0.05; Figure 4 A,B); in Polmu-/- spleen cells (mostly non-proliferating) a smaller increase was observed that did not reach significance (Figure 4A). This suggests that Polmu is required for DSB repair in cycling cells. We confirmed the higher DSB incidence in Polmu-/- bone marrow cells by comet assay, which revealed significantly longer comet tails in Polmu-/- BM cells (p<0.001; Figure 4C,D).

###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
###xml 47 51 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEF) derived from mice deficient in NHEJ proteins show a highly reduced proliferative capacity compared to wt controls [35]-[37]. To assess the roles of Polmu in DNA repair and proliferation in other tissues, we analyzed wt and Polmu-/- MEF. Consistent with the proposed role of Polmu in NHEJ, our results show that Polmu-/- MEF showed reduced growth after long-term culture and they enter senescence prematurely (Figure 4E). In addition, primary Polmu-/- MEF were genomically unstable compared with wt MEF. In particular, Polmu-/- MEF showed a 4-fold increase in chromosomal aberrations, with a striking 16-fold increase in the level of radial configurations and a 4.5-fold increase in chromosomal breaks, signatures of deficient DNA repair and increased radiosensitivity (Table 1 and Figure 4F; see Figure S5 for examples of chromosome aberrations). These results demonstrate that lack of Polmu results in DSB accumulation in bone marrow and probably in connective tissue. This suggests that proliferation deficiency by Polmu-/- cells is due to accumulation of unrepaired DSB or a general delay in DSB repair, which may lead to cell cycle arrest and cell death [38].

To test this in vivo, we irradiated Polmu-/- and wt mice (9 Gy) and analyzed survival over time. Fifteen days post-irradiation all Polmu-/- mice were dead, compared with only 40% of wt mice (p<0.001 Log rank test; Figure 5A). Most Polmu-/- mice died between days 9 and 11 (Figure 5A), and hematologic analysis revealed extreme neutropenia (not shown), indicating hematopoietic failure. The impact of Polmu deficiency on the response to irradiation damage is also illustrated by the higher radiosensitivity of Polmu-/- progenitors (Figure 5B; p<0.01). Bone marrow cells from wt and Polmu-/- mice showed G2 accumulation after irradiation (5Gy), suggesting that the G2/M cell cycle checkpoint is functional in Polmu-/- cells (Figure S4A). Western blot detected increased p21 accumulation in irradiated Polmu-/- splenocytes and even in non-irradiated cells (Figure S4B), suggesting activation of the G1 cell cycle checkpoint. Irradiation of Polmu-/- MEF (2-8 Gy) significantly reduced survival compared with similarly treated wt MEF (2 to 123-fold; p<0.05. Figure 5C). Histological analysis of irradiated mice showed vacuolar degeneration in liver (where we detect intense gamma-H2AX accumulation after irradiation; Figure S4C), hemorrhage and congestion in the lung, tubular degeneration in kidney, and tubular atrophy in testis (Figure 5D). An alternative explanation for increased tissue damage in irradiated Polmu-/- mice might be increased production of reactive oxygen species (ROS). We therefore measured intracellular ROS levels in irradiated bone marrow cells (4 and 8 Gy) by staining with DCFDA (Di-cloro-fluorescein diacetate: a marker of intracellular peroxides). ROS accumulation was unaffected by Polmu-/- deficiency (Figure 5E), indicating that ROS production does not contribute to the radiosensitivity of Polmu-/- animals or cells. These data thus show that the requirement for Polmu in DNA repair extends to tissues outside the hematopoietic compartment (Figure 5C,D).

To examine the effect of Polmu deficiency on DNA repair in the hematopoietic system more closely, we analyzed the frequency of phosphorylated gamma-H2AX foci in bone marrow and spleen after whole animal gamma-irradiation (5Gy). In both genotypes 91% of all BM cells were gamma-H2AX+, showing that these cells are more susceptible to gamma-irradiation. In contrast, the proportion of phospho-gamma-H2AX positive cells in Polmu-/- splenocytes (95%) was significantly higher than in wt cells (27%; p<0.001). Further, the number of gamma-H2AX foci per cell was significantly increased in Polmu-/- cells compared with wt (1.7-fold for BM cells and 4.8-fold for splenocytes, p<0.001; Figure 6A-B). Changes in gamma-H2AX phosphorylation were confirmed by western blot of gamma-irradiated (8 Gy) spleen B cells; as predicted, Polmu-/- cells show higher amounts of phospho-gamma-H2AX, and these levels are sustained for longer (Figure 6C). We confirmed reduced DSB repair in Polmu-/- BM cells by comet assay. Before cell extraction, irradiated mice (5Gy) were left to recover for 3 hours to allow in vivo DNA repair (see Methods). BM cells from these mice were separated by single cell electrophoresis under alkaline conditions, to measure the relative levels of DNA breaks; comet tail moment was 2-fold higher in Polmu-/- cells compared with wt cells (p<0.001; Figure 6D), indicating reduced DNA repair in Polmu-/- cells.

###xml 683 687 671 675 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Lucas1">[14]</xref>
###xml 688 692 676 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Bertocci2">[40]</xref>
###xml 191 196 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 448 452 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 607 611 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Polmu is a gap filling, error-prone DNA polymerase that, in association with the NHEJ core machinery, promotes microhomology use during DSB repair [17],[39]. Polmu deficiency does not affect mouse development or generate a severe phenotype in adults [14]. This is probably because its function can be replaced or compensated, at least in part, by other DNA polymerase activities [14],[17]. The most prominent phenotype reported to date in Polmu-/- mice is partial impairment of immunoglobulin Vkappa-Jkappa recombination [18], associated with alterations in the peripheral B cell compartment [18]. Polmu-/- mice are nonetheless able to mount an almost normal humoral immune response [14],[40].

###xml 1252 1260 1224 1232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1459 1462 1429 1432 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>

Here we show that Polmu is required for DSB repair in vivo and that this defect confers radiosensitivity, promotes genetic instability and leads to deficient hematopoiesis. Polmu deficiency alters many peripheral blood cell populations, affecting not only the B cell compartment [14], but also the myeloid lineages (Figure 1A). Polmu-/- bone marrow showed a severe reduction (approximately40%) in total bone marrow cell numbers (Figure 1C, D). This reduction affects all hematopoietic lineages as demonstrated by flow cytometry (Figure 1). Interestingly, B cell development is affected prior Vkappa-Jkappa recombination (Figure 1F-H). All committed hematopoietic progenitors were reduced in Polmu-/- BM; the hematopoietic defect was traced back to the hematopoietic stem cell, since FCM revealed a reduction in the number of HSC (Figure 2G). Interestingly, transplant of Polmu-/- bone marrow (together with competitor WT cells) into irradiated recipients rescued (at least partially) the hematopoietic defect, suggesting that Polmu-/- stroma does not completely support hematopoiesis, alternatively, the co-transplantation of hematopoietic wt cells might provide factors that support the development of Polmu-/- HSC. Polmu-/- progenitors do not expand in vitro as efficiently as WT controls, suggesting that lack of Polmu results in cell death or reduced proliferation. Taken together these results suggest that hematopoietic cells in the bone marrow of Polmu-/- mice are reduced due to a combination of extrinsic (defective bone marrow stroma) and intrinsic (reduced DNA repair and expansion potential) factors.

We demonstrate that hematopoietic Polmu-/- cells contain more DSB than their wt counterparts, as shown by the increase in the basal level of phospho-gamma-H2AX positive cells and comet assay results (Figure 4A-D) and increased basal chromosomal breakage (Table 1). The reduced DSB repair capacity of hematopoietic Polmu-/- cells is more evident after genotoxic insult, demonstrating increased genomic instability (Figure 6; Table 1). Our results also show that Polmu is required for DNA repair in other tissues; including connective tissue (MEF) (Table 1 and Figure 4E-F), liver and kidney (Figure 5B). Lack of Polmu results in radiation-induced cell death at doses that are non-lethal for wt mice (Figure 5A). However, immunohistochemistry failed to demonstrate any gross tissue or organ damage outside the hematopoietic system in non-irradiated Polmu-/- mice (not shown). This suggests that Polmu deficiency may be compensated for or tolerated in organs in which progenitor cell proliferation is slower than in the hematopoietic system or in MEF. Alternatively, it may indicate that - in some tissues - Polmu function is only required under stress conditions (for example, irradiation), which require faster repair. This is further supported by the fact that although most hematopoietic lineages are reduced in Polmu-/- BM, different subsets are affected to different extents. For example although CFU-GM and CFU-M progenitors are reduced in Polmu-/- BM, CFU-G progenitors are not. Another example is the reduction of the HSC population by 39% (p<0.001), while the CLP is unaffected. Whether the less-affected cell types have less DNA damage or use different DNA repair mechanisms remains to be investigated.

###xml 1507 1514 1495 1502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="sec" rid="s2">Methods</xref>
###xml 1574 1578 1562 1566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Bertocci1">[18]</xref>
###xml 94 98 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 611 616 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 758 763 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1064 1068 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1280 1285 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
To our knowledge this is the first report to show increased radiosensivity in Polmu deficient mice and cells. A previous study reported a relative lack of radiosensivity in Polmu-/- MEF [18]. This contrasts with our data, which strongly suggest that Polmu is necessary for the repair of double strand breaks. Polmu-deficient MEF showed reduced growth curves and increased basal and radiation-induced levels of chromosomal aberrations; phenotypes similar to those previously reported for Ku80-/- and LigIV-/- MEF [30], [35], [36], [37], [41]-[45]. Apart from the different genetic background of the two Polmu-/- mouse models, there are other possible explanations for this discrepancy. First, the gene targeting strategy is different between the two Polmu-/- mouse models. In our model, exons 2 to 4 are deleted, which eliminates the BRCT domain and completely abrogates protein expression in Western blot analysis [14]. In the knockout generated by Bertocci and coworkers, exons 6 to 11 are deleted, eliminating the core of the polymerase activity. Although these mice do not express full-length Polmu mRNA, no information is provided regarding protein expression [18],[40]. This is important, since a truncated Polmu BRCT domain with biological activity may be expressed in this mouse model. An alternative explanation is that the two studies use different experimental procedures to detect radiosensitivity. We gamma-irradiated MEF cultures plated at clonogenic dilutions (300 cells in 10 cm2 dishes, see Methods) and calculated survival by scoring colonies. In contrast, [18] performed cell survival assays at much higher cell densities and used X-rays as the agent of DNA damage; survival was assessed by trypan blue exclusion. Differences in the sensitivities of these approaches might account for the differences observed in MEF survival in the two models.
###end p 80
###begin p 81
###xml 98 102 98 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Nijnik1">[20]</xref>
###xml 103 107 103 107 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Navarro1">[23]</xref>
###xml 317 321 317 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Nijnik1">[20]</xref>
###xml 322 326 322 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Rossi1">[21]</xref>
###xml 370 374 370 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Rossi2">[46]</xref>
###xml 918 922 917 921 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Nijnik1">[20]</xref>
###xml 923 927 922 926 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Rossi1">[21]</xref>
###xml 674 678 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 779 783 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The role of DNA repair in hematopoietic homeostasis has become increasingly clear in recent years [20]-[23]. Knockout models of many DNA repair enzymes are characterized by either reduced stem cell function or alterations in specific hematopoietic populations. Moreover, since stem cells are protected from apoptosis [20],[21], probably because of their quiescent state [46], deficiency in DNA repair enzymes allows mutations to accumulate in these cells. Conversely, more committed progenitors, which cycle more rapidly, would be more sensitive to cell cycle arrest or apoptosis, thus explaining the observed reduction in the numbers of progenitor cells in Polmu-deficient mice. Thus the survival of HSC comes at the price of accumulated mutations; two recent reports show that mice deficient in DNA repair maintain expanded stem cell pools, but that these pools have a reduced differentiation potential during aging [20],[21].
###end p 81
###begin p 82
###xml 66 70 65 69 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Rossi1">[21]</xref>
###xml 85 89 84 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Nijnik1">[20]</xref>
###xml 236 239 234 237 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 558 562 555 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-Mahajan1">[15]</xref>
###xml 563 567 560 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000389-NickMcElhinny1">[17]</xref>
###xml 577 580 573 576 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1098 1101 1092 1095 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 222 226 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 240 244 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 301 305 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 581 586 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1102 1106 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Polmu deficiency, as reported for other NHEJ factors such as Ku80 [21] and Ligase IV [20], targets the hematopoietic system and HSC. There are, however, striking differences in the phenotypes observed in Ku80 and LigaseIV mice and Polmu-/- mice. In the first two cases, HSC were unaffected in younger mice but were progressively exhausted during aging. Polmu unlike Ku80 or Ligase IV only participates in a small number of NHEJ reactions that require an error-prone DNA polymerase to generate or promote microhomology pairing between the DNA ends of the DSB [15]-[17] and Polmu-/- mouse has reduced HSC numbers in the bone marrow. These suggest that different components of the NHEJ system will have different roles in hematopoiesis or that some of the DSB generated in hematopoietic cells require Polmu for their complete and efficient repair, and cannot be repaired by compensatory mechanisms with sufficient speed or efficiency to prevent cell death or premature senescence. Additionally, the whole body irradiation experiments presented here show that DSB-repair defects and cell death in Polmu-/- mice are not restricted to the hematopoietic system but are widespread, demonstrating a role for Polmu in DSB repair in other tissues. Our results thus show that Polmu is required for genomic stability and DNA repair, through its participation in DSB repair in hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells as well as in non-hematopoietic cells.
