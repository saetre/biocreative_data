###begin article-title 0
Poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP-1) is not involved in DNA double-strand break recovery
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Background
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 87 88 83 84 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 89 91 85 87 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 161 166 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The cytotoxicity and the rejoining of DNA double-strand breaks induced by gamma-rays, H2O2 and neocarzinostatin, were investigated in normal and PARP-1 knockout mouse 3T3 fibroblasts to determine the role of poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP-1) in DNA double-strand break repair.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Results
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 6 10 6 10 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 54 58 54 58 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 103 104 99 100 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 105 106 101 102 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 279 283 275 279 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 522 526 518 522 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 536 540 532 536 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 599 600 591 592 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 601 603 593 595 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 813 817 805 809 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 827 831 819 823 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 870 871 858 859 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 872 873 860 861 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
PARP-1-/- were considerably more sensitive than PARP-1+/+ 3T3s to induced cell kill by gamma-rays and H2O2. However, the two cell lines did not show any significant difference in the susceptibility to neocarzinostatin below 1.5 nM drug. Restoration of PARP-1 expression in PARP-1-/- 3T3s by retroviral transfection of the full PARP-1 cDNA did not induce any change in neocarzinostatin response. Moreover the incidence and the rejoining kinetics of neocarzinostatin-induced DNA double-strand breaks were identical in PARP-1+/+ and PARP-1-/- 3T3s. Poly(ADP-ribose) synthesis following gamma-rays and H2O2 was observed in PARP-1-proficient cells only. In contrast neocarzinostatin, even at supra-lethal concentration, was unable to initiate PARP-1 activation yet it induced H2AX histone phosphorylation in both PARP1+/+ and PARP-1-/- 3T3s as efficiently as gamma-rays and H2O2.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Conclusions
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 266 267 262 263 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 268 269 264 265 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
The results show that PARP-1 is not a major determinant of DNA double-strand break recovery with either strand break rejoining or cell survival as an endpoint. Even though both PARP-1 and ATM activation are major determinants of the cell response to gamma-rays and H2O2, data suggest that PARP-1-dependent poly(ADP-ribose) synthesis and ATM-dependent H2AX phosphorylation, are not inter-related in the repair pathway of neocarzinostatin-induced DNA double-strand breaks.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Background
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 504 505 504 505 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 506 507 506 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 793 794 789 790 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 795 796 791 792 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 1004 1005 1000 1001 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
Ionizing radiation induces multiple lesions in cell DNA including oxidative base damage, single-strand breaks (SSB) and double-strand breaks (DSB) in proportion to the radiation dose. Among the enzymes that have evolved for the repair of radiation damage, poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP-1), Ataxia mutated kinase (ATM) and the heterotrimeric DNA-dependent protein kinase (DNA-PK), play a leading role. ATM is rapidly activated by DSB to phosphorylate proteins in chromatin, most notably H2AX histone [1,2] and the catalytic subunit of the DNA-PK complex (DNA-PKcs). ATM is also essential to coupling of DNA damage detection to NF-kappaB activation and proper management of the oxidative stress inherent in radiation exposure. DNA-PKcs is recruited by the Ku70-Ku86 complex at sites of DSB [3,4]. Activated DNA-PKcs phosphorylates a range of protein substrates and, along with XRCC4 and Ligase 4 is essential to V(D)J recombination and DSB repair through the non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) pathway [5].
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 168 169 168 169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 194 195 194 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 196 197 196 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 269 270 269 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 524 526 524 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 677 682 677 682 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Waf1 </sup>
###xml 691 693 691 693 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 694 696 694 696 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 819 821 819 821 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 878 880 878 880 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 881 883 881 883 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 886 894 886 894 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In vitro</italic>
###xml 1016 1018 1016 1018 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
PARP-1, an ubiquitous 113 kDa enzyme, is also required for the detection and signalization of DNA strand interruptions. It is involved in early DNA damage recognition [6], base excision repair [7,8] and genome surveillance in a variety of situations (for a review see [9]). Activated PARP-1 carries out synthesis and transfer of long linear or branched ADP-ribose polymers (pADPr) to carboxyl groups in a limited number of nuclear protein acceptors, including PARP-1 itself in a reaction initiated by PARP-1 binding to SSB [10]. In addition, several DNA damage signaling or repair proteins possess high-affinity binding motifs for pADPr, among others XRCC1, DNA ligase III, p21Waf1 and p53 [11,12]. Two subunits of the DNA-PK heterotrimer, namely, Ku70 and DNA-PKcs also present high affinity motifs for pADPr binding [12], and PARP-1 co-immunoprecipitates with these proteins [13-15]. In vitro, the DNA-PKcs subunit can be ADP-ribosylated and stimulated by PARP-1; PARP-1 can in turn be phosphorylated by DNA-PKcs [16].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 144 146 144 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 147 149 147 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
Whether these protein complexes and post-translational modifications play a role in repair from radioinduced DSB, has been challenged recently [17,18]. In the prospect of unravelling this question, we reasoned that radiomimetic compounds acting to produce DSB with high selectivity and efficiency in the DNA of target cells should be used instead of ionizing radiation, since radiation generates oxidative stress response and elicits a large spectrum of lesions located at random in chromatin.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 166 171 166 171 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chrom</sub>
###xml 212 214 212 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 262 268 262 268 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chrom </sub>
###xml 416 422 416 422 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chrom </sub>
###xml 457 459 457 459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 549 551 549 551 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 639 641 639 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 642 644 642 644 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 699 701 699 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 788 790 788 790 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 791 793 791 793 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 796 804 796 804 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 805 807 805 807 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 808 810 808 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 820 822 820 822 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 844 846 844 846 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 847 849 847 849 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 796 803 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 813 818 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
We chose neocarzinostatin (NCS) for this purpose. NCS is the prototype of the "protein antibiotic" family. It is a complex consisting of a dodecadiyne antibiotic (NCSChrom) reversibly bound to a carrier protein [19]. NCS is active in the nanomolar range, and NCSChrom cleaves DNA in the course of a suicide reaction leaving no residual active drug after a few minutes incubation. The major DNA lesions induced by NCSChrom in DNA result from radical attack [20] and consist of a blunt end break bearing a thymidine-5'-aldehyde residue on one strand [21], with an atypical abasic site at two nucleotide interval on the complementary strand [22,23]. This abasic site is substrate for endonuclease III [24] in such a way that NCS-induced damage is rapidly converted into DSB in living cells [25,26]. E. coli [27,28], yeast [29] or mammalian cells [30-37] bearing a defect in DSB repair, are consistently hypersensitive to induced cell kill by NCS.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 25 28 25 28 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+</sup>
###xml 57 60 57 60 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/-</sup>
###xml 184 188 184 188 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 339 343 339 343 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 353 357 353 357 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 460 464 460 464 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 510 511 506 507 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 512 514 508 510 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 92 96 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
PARP-1 proficient (PARP-1+/+) and PARP-1 knockout (PARP-1-/-) 3T3 fibroblasts from syngenic mice, were used to investigate the role of PARP-1 in the recovery of NCS-induced DSB. PARP-1-/- 3T3s complemented with the full PARP-1 cDNA, were also used in this assay. The incidence and repair kinetics of DSB were measured in parallel in PARP-1+/+ and PARP-1-/- 3T3s, and the effect of 4-amino-1,8-naphthalimide, a potent PARP-1 inhibitor, was established in PARP-1+/+ 3T3s. The cytotoxic effect of gamma-rays and H2O2 was determined for comparison.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 148 149 144 145 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 150 151 146 147 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Immunofluorescence studies were also performed to assess pADPr synthesis and H2AX histone phosphorylation following exposure to NCS, gamma-rays or H2O2. The results show that PARP-1 is likely not to play a crucial role in the repair of DSB, at least in the absence of other types of DNA damage.
###end p 13

Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
###xml 37 38 33 34 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>

###end title 15

###xml 20 24 20 24 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 171 175 171 175 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 185 189 185 189 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>

###xml 351 355 351 355 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
PARP-1+/+ and PARP-1-/- 3T3s were exposed to increasing concentrations of NCS and cytotoxicity was determined through a growth assay (see Methods). Below 1.5 nM NCS PARP-1+/+ and PARP-1-/- 3T3s demonstrated exactly the same susceptibility to the lethal effect of NCS (Figure 1A). However, PARP-1+/+ were substantially more sensitive to NCS than PARP-1-/- 3T3s in the high range of NCS concentration.

###begin p 22
###xml 10 14 10 14 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 24 28 24 28 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 416 420 416 420 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 578 580 578 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 645 649 645 649 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 830 832 830 832 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
The PARP-1+/+ and PARP-1-/- 3T3 clones used in these experiments originated each from independent isolates immortalized at random. There was therefore a possibility that minor differences might exist between both cell lines with regard to cell death mechanism or efficiency at a high level of DSB, and contribute to altered NCS susceptibility in addition to the PARP-1 defect. To settle this question, we used PARP-1-/- 3T3s in which the full PARP-1 cDNA had been re-inserted by retroviral infection with a pBabe construct, allowing complete restoration of the PARP-1 activity [38]. The NCS response of these cells was compared to that of PARP-1-/- 3T3s from the same clonal isolate transfected with the void vector. Both cell lines showed exactly the same susceptibility to NCS, with a purely exponential dose-dependence (Figure 1B).

###begin title 23
Incidence of NCS-induced DSB
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 13 17 13 17 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>

Briefly, cells were exposed to increasing concentrations of NCS, up to 30 nM for 10-min at 37degreesC, then chilled in ice, harvested in PBS-EDTA buffer, and inserted into agarose plugs and lysed for PFGE analysis. The effect of NCS was compared to that of gamma-rays (up to 60 Gy) on cells irradiated in ice.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 112 114 108 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 273 277 265 269 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 287 291 279 283 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 431 433 419 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 683 684 667 668 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
The incidence of DSB was found to increase linearly with the gamma-ray dose, in agreement with earlier reports [39]. It also grew linearly with the NCS concentration, and incubation with 1 nM NCS yielded the same amount of DSB as 1.21 Gy gamma-rays (data not shown). PARP-1+/+ and PARP-1-/- 3T3s did not show any difference in this assay. Taking into account the fact that 1 Gy gamma-rays produces 41 DSB per diploid cell nucleus [40], the average incidence of DSB formed by 1 nM NCS in each 3T3 fibroblast was estimated at ca. 50 DSB. With consideration to the number of DSB induced by the treatment, the lethal efficiency of NCS was in the same range as that for gamma-rays (Table 1).

###begin title 34
Discussion
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 55 59 55 59 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 69 73 69 73 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 144 149 144 149 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Chrom</sub>
###xml 210 214 210 214 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 224 228 224 228 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 269 270 269 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
The incidence of NCS-induced DSB was the same in PARP-1+/+ and PARP-1-/- 3T3s, indicating that both cell lines incorporated equal amounts of NCSChrom. Moreover no difference, even minor was found between PARP-1+/+ and PARP-1-/- in the kinetics of DSB rejoining (Figure 3).
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 88 92 88 92 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 102 106 102 106 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 136 140 136 140 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 491 495 491 495 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 560 561 560 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
Consistent with this, the susceptibility to NCS-induced lethality was the same in PARP-1+/+ and PARP-1-/- 3T3s below 1.5 nM drug. PARP-1+/+ were comparatively more sensitive to NCS in the high range of NCS concentration. This is thought to result from minor differences in the susceptibility to DSB-induced cell death arising between different clonal isolates of 3T3 fibroblasts immortalized at random. As a matter of fact, retrovirus-mediated insertion of the PARP-1 cDNA in a single PARP-1-/- 3T3 clone, did not bear any change in NCS susceptibility (Figure 1).
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 55 56 55 56 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 57 59 57 59 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 67 68 67 68 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 121 125 121 125 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 138 139 138 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 318 320 314 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 321 323 317 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 378 379 374 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 380 382 376 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 383 385 379 381 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 393 397 389 393 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 446 450 442 446 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 497 499 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2C</xref>
###xml 508 512 504 508 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 553 557 549 553 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 594 595 590 591 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 596 597 592 593 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 681 683 677 679 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 781 783 777 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 911 912 907 908 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 913 915 909 911 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 916 918 912 914 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 1001 1004 997 1000 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+</sup>
###xml 1031 1034 1027 1030 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/-</sup>
###xml 1067 1069 1063 1065 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2C</xref>
###xml 1199 1200 1195 1196 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1201 1203 1197 1199 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 1225 1227 1221 1223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 1228 1230 1224 1226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 1332 1334 1328 1330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 1122 1137 <span type="species:ncbi:10029">Chinese hamster</span>
###xml 1169 1174 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
ANI totally repressed pADPr elongation in response to H2O2 (Figure 4) but did not alter the cytotoxicity of NCS in PARP-1+/+ 3T3s (Figure 2). These results corroborate those of other authors who showed that PARP-1 inhibitors did not affect cell kill by the topoisomerase IIalpha poison etoposide, another DSB inducer [41-43]. In contrast, and in agreement with earlier reports [6,44,45] PARP-1-/- 3T3s were considerably more sensitive than PARP-1+/+ 3T3s to the lethal effect of radiation (Figure 2C). PARP-1-/- 3T3s were also more sensitive than PARP-1+/+ to sub-millimolar concentrations of H2O2, a potent inducer of pADPr synthesis in response to SSB and oxidative base damage [46], highligting the toxic effect of a deficiency in base excision repair in PARP-1 knockout cells [47]. However, for both cell lines, and contrary to CHO cells that exhibited an exponentially concentration-dependent response to H2O2 [46], the survival curves presented a plateau indicating resistance of a major (PARP-1+/+) or minor fraction (PARP-1-/-) of the cell population (Figure 2C). A similar effect was reported by other authors in Chinese hamster V79 cells and in normal and AT human fibroblasts exposed to H2O2 or organic peroxides [48,49]. Differential expression of catalase, superoxide dismutase or glutathione peroxidase was ruled out [48] and at the moment there is no explanation to this observation.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 112 113 112 113 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 114 115 114 115 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 266 268 266 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 280 284 280 284 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 290 292 290 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 293 295 293 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 355 356 355 356 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 357 358 357 358 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 379 381 379 381 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 382 384 382 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 434 438 434 438 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 475 477 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 717 721 717 721 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 731 735 731 735 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 805 806 805 806 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 1108 1109 1108 1109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 1119 1126 1119 1126 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al. </italic>
###xml 1127 1129 1127 1129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 1363 1365 1359 1361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 1595 1597 1591 1593 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 1598 1600 1594 1596 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 1999 2001 1995 1997 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 53 58 <span type="species:ncbi:302767">comet</span>
Other authors using single-cell gel electrophoresis (comet assay) have shown that the rejoining of radiation-, H2O2- or methyl methane sulfonate-induced SSB, is dramatically hampered by PARP-1 knockout, and significantly delayed by PARP-1 inhibitors in L1210 cells [50] and PARP-1+/+ 3T3s [51,52] yet these inhibitors have poor incidence on the level of H2O2-induced cell death [46,53]. Ectopic expression of the PARP-1 cDNA in PARP-1-/- 3T3s restored a wild-type phenotype [51], demonstrating unequivocally that the repair capacity of PARP-1-deficient cells was drastically limited. We propose that this effect is relevant to the role of PARP-1 in base excision repair only, as there was no difference between PARP-1+/+ and PARP-1-/- 3T3s with regard to the rejoining kinetics of NCS-induced DSB (Figure 3). Moreover, immunofluorescence assays showed that, even though NCS at supra-lethal concentration did not induce detectable pADPr synthesis, the ATM-dependent phosphorylation of H2AX was activated in response to relatively low levels of NCS, irrespective of whether PARP-1 was defective or not (Figure 4). Bowman et al. [43] also showed that the topoisomerase IIalpha poison etoposide did not bring about pADPr synthesis in L1210 cells, even at concentrations in excess of those causing significant levels of apoptosis. Likewise, Dziegielewski and Beerman [54] recently showed that a defect in either ATM or DNA-PKcs may not confer cells enhanced susceptibility to the NCS homologue C-1027. On the other hand single-strand nicks in DNA are effective activators for PARP-1, not for DNA-PK [55,56]. Therefore, PARP-1-dependent pADPr elongation and ATM-dependent H2AX phosphorylation, two major post-translational modifications occurring in response to DNA single- and double-strand breaks, respectively, are likely not to be interrelated or redundant. This may elicit functional synergy between PARP-1 and ATM, consistent with the embryonic lethality observed in the double ATM/PARP-1 knockout [57].
###end p 38
###begin title 39
Conclusions
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 109 111 109 111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 381 383 381 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 455 456 455 456 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 457 459 457 459 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 467 469 467 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2C</xref>
Substantial differences in PARP-1 activation by ionizing radiation and alkylating agents have been reported [58] and our data suggest that PARP-1 is not a major determinant of cell survival to DNA double-strand breaks as long as the level of oxidative stress (as determined from pADPr synthesis) is limited. In consideration of studies with other DNA-damaging agents (reviewed in [59]) and of the comparatively high susceptibility of PARP-1 null 3T3s to H2O2 (Figure 2C), we propose that enhanced radiation sensitivity of PARP-1 null relatively to normal 3T3s, proceeds from defects in base excision repair in the former.
###end p 40

###begin title 49
Cytotoxicity determination
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 154 158 154 158 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+/+ </sup>
###xml 167 171 167 171 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 216 218 216 218 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sup>
###xml 233 235 233 235 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sup>
###xml 449 453 449 453 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
Due to a low cloning efficiency of 3T3 fibroblasts in a feeder layer technique, all cytotoxicity determinations were performed using growth assays. PARP-1+/+ or PARP-1-/- 3T3 fibroblasts were plated in triplicate (105 cells per 25 cm2 vented flasks) and incubated overnight before treatment. After treatment, the flasks were rinsed twice with HBSS and returned to fresh medium for exactly 5 doubling times, with one medium change at day 7 for PARP-1-/- 3T3s. Fibroblasts were harvested by 0.05% trypsin-0.02% EDTA, pelleted, resuspended in medium and counted under microscope in a Malassez cuvette.

###begin title 51
Radiation and drug treatment
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 88 90 88 90 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 116 119 116 119 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">137</sup>
Irradiation of cells was performed at room temperature in medium equilibrated with 5% CO2 in air, using an IBL-637 (137Cs) gamma-ray irradiator (Cis-Biointernational). The dose rate was 0.86 Gy/min or 8.0 Gy/min for survival or immunofluorescence studies, respectively.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 164 165 164 165 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
Aliquots of NCS were thawed just before use, adjusted to the suitable dilution in ice-cold PBS buffer, pH 6.0, and immediately introduced into culture flasks (25 cm2, 5 ml medium) with gentle agitation. The whole treatment was performed in dim light to avoid photo-induced degradation of the drug. The cytotoxic effect of NCS at fixed concentration was investigated as a function of the length of drug exposure. Cytotoxicity increased steeply with the length of contact and reached completion after 6-min only. More prolonged incubation did not result in increased cell death. Therefore, the length of contact with NCS was 10-min throughout.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 16 17 16 17 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 18 20 18 20 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>

###xml 178 179 178 179 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 180 182 180 182 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>

###xml 59 61 59 61 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B63">63</xref>

When present, ANI (30 muM) from a 3 mM stock solution in pure DMSO was introduced 1-h prior to irradiation or NCS, and removed 1-h later with two HBSS washes. ###begin title 56
DNA double-strand break determination
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 178 180 178 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>
###xml 181 183 181 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B65">65</xref>
###xml 189 191 189 191 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">14</sup>
###xml 224 226 224 226 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sup>
###xml 241 243 241 243 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sup>
###xml 423 425 417 419 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sup>
The determination of DSB formation and repair was carried out by pulsed field agarose gel electrophoresis (PFGE) through the clamped homogeneous electric field (CHEF) technique [64,65]. [2-14C]Thymidine-labeled cells (2-5 106 cells per 25 cm2 flask) were put in ice following treatment, harvested in ice-cold PBS supplemented with 2 mM EDTA by gentle scraping, and collected by sedimentation at 4degreesC. Pellets from 5 105 cells were resuspended in 150 mul, final volume, of PBS buffer at 37degreesC, and mixed under mild vortexing with an equal volume of 1.6% w/v low-melting-point agarose (BDH Laboratory) in PBS buffer at 37degreesC. The suspension (300 mul) was immediately pipetted into pre-chilled 4 x 10 mm moulds and allowed to form plugs on ice for 30-min. Embedded cells were subsequently lysed by immersion of the plugs in 2 ml of a solution containing 2% N-lauroyl sarkosine, 40 mM EDTA, 1 mg/ml proteinase K in PBS, pH 7.8 and incubated, firstly for 2 h in ice, secondly for 24 h at 50degreesC. The plugs were subsequently washed twice with PBS, and treated for 1-h with 200 mug/ml RNase A. The plugs were finally washed twice with PBS and stored at 4degreesC overnight prior electrophoresis.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 475 484 445 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe </italic>
###xml 488 502 458 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 475 483 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
###xml 488 501 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
The plugs were inserted into the wells of an 0.8% w/v agarose gel (BioRad, chromosomal grade) made in 0.75 x TAE buffer (40 mM tris-acetate, 2 mM EDTA, pH 8), and the gels submitted to PFGE at 14degreesC in a CHEF-DR III apparatus (Bio-Rad) with buffer recirculation at 14degreesC. Migration was for 72 h at 2 V/cm with three switch times, namely, 1200-s, 1500-s and 1800-s with angles of 96degrees, 100degrees and 106degrees, respectively. The molecular weight markers were S. pombe and S. cerevisiae chromosomes (Bio-Rad).
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 273 282 272 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vacuo </italic>
###xml 347 351 346 348 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174; </sup>
After electrophoresis, the gels were stained with 0.5 mug/ml ethidium bromide under mild agitation (30-min), followed by destaining for 1-h in fresh buffer. DNA fluorescence was visualized over an UV transilluminator with camera recording. The gels were subsequently dried in vacuo over a piece of Whatman paper and analyzed using a Phosphorimager(R) apparatus (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech), allowing a precise determination of the migration profile of DNA fragments and of the fractional radioactivity released from the plugs (FAR).
###end p 59
###begin title 60
Immunofluorescence of PARP-1, pADPr and gamma-H2AX
###end title 60
###begin p 61
###xml 123 124 115 116 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 125 127 117 119 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 486 487 466 467 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
3T3 fibroblasts were grown for 24-h on coverslips and exposed to either gamma-rays (5 Gy for gammaH2AX, 50 Gy for pADPR), H2O2 (1 mM, 10-min contact, 37degreesC), or graded concentrations of NCS (2 nM, 5 nM or 30 nM, 10-min contact, 37degreesC), and fixed 10-min after the beginning of treatment. For PARP-1 and pADPr immunofluorescence, fixation was by 4% formaldehyde (10-min, room temperature) in PBS followed by two PBS washes and neutralization of residual formaldehyde by 50 mM NH4Cl (10-min, room temperature). After a further PBS wash (5-min, 4degreesC), cells were permeabilized by 0.5% Triton X-100 in PBS (5-min, 4degreesC), and finally rinsed twice with PBS. For gammaH2AX determination, cells were fixed in a 1:1 v:v acetone:methanol mixe (-20degreesC, 10-min), dried in air and rehydrated in PBS (15-min, room temperature).
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 304 307 294 295 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174;</sup>
###xml 68 74 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Cell preparations on coverslips were subsequently incubated with 1% bovine serum albumin in PBS (10-min, room temperature) and exposed to anti-pADPr, anti-PARP-1 or anti-gammaH2AX primary antibody (1/200 dilution, 1-h, 37degreesC). The coverslips were rinsed thrice with PBS, and incubated with Alexa-488(R)-conjugated secondary antibody (1/500 dilution, 30-min, 37degreesC), rinsed thrice with PBS, counterstained with 4,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) and mounted. Immunofluorescence was visualized using a Zeiss Axiophot microscope equipped with a Micromax chilled camera (Princeton Applied Research).
