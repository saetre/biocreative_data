###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kluyvera</italic>
###xml 36 54 36 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enterobacteriaceae</italic>
###xml 176 197 176 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kluyvera cryocrescens</italic>
###xml 231 242 231 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Podoviridae</italic>
###xml 82 87 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 100 118 <span type="species:ncbi:47049">Bacteriophage Kvp1</span>
###xml 176 197 <span type="species:ncbi:580">Kluyvera cryocrescens</span>
Kluyvera, a genus within the family Enterobacteriaceae, is an infrequent cause of human infections. Bacteriophage Kvp1, the only bacteriophage isolated for one of its species, Kluyvera cryocrescens, is a member of the viral family Podoviridae.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 30 51 30 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kluyvera cryocrescens</italic>
###xml 30 51 <span type="species:ncbi:580">Kluyvera cryocrescens</span>
The genome of Kvp1, the first Kluyvera cryocrescens-specific bacteriophage, was sequenced using pyrosequencing (454 technology) at the McGill University and Genome Quebec Innovation Centre. The two contigs were closed using PCR and the sequence of the terminal repeats completed by primer walking off the phage DNA. The phage structural proteome was investigated by SDS-PAGE and mass spectrometry.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
At 39,472 bp, the annotated genome revealed a closer relationship to coliphage T3 than T7 with Kvp1 containing homologs to T3 early proteins S-adenosyl-L-methionine hydrolase (0.3) and protein kinase (0.7). The quantitative nature of the relationships between Kvp1 and the other members of the T7-like virus genus (T7, T3, phiA1122, phiYeO3-12, Berlin, K1F, VP4 and gh-1) was confirmed using CoreGenes.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 49 61 49 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Podoviridae </italic>
###xml 176 177 176 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 389 390 389 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 586 587 586 587 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 588 589 588 589 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 658 735 654 731 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Erwinia, Escherichia, Klebsiella, Morganella, Pseudomonas, Salmonella, Vibrio</italic>
###xml 741 749 737 745 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia</italic>
###xml 807 818 799 810 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caulobacter</italic>
###xml 824 833 816 825 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rhizobium</italic>
###xml 1092 1110 1084 1102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Autographivirinae </italic>
###xml 1193 1194 1183 1184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1213 1234 1203 1224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kluyvera cryocrescens</italic>
###xml 1351 1352 1341 1342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 1401 1412 1391 1402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Podoviridae</italic>
###xml 1441 1444 1431 1434 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Alu</italic>
###xml 1449 1452 1439 1442 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hae</italic>
###xml 1716 1725 1706 1715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 1213 1234 <span type="species:ncbi:580">Kluyvera cryocrescens</span>
###xml 1716 1737 <span type="species:ncbi:369257">Yersinia phage Berlin</span>
The T7-like bacterial viruses are members of the Podoviridae - phages with short tails - and are characterized by a simple but elegant temporal transcriptional control system [1]. The early genes are transcribed by the host RNA polymerase while the middle and late regions are transcribed by a single subunit phage-encoded RNA polymerase which recognizes unique 23 bp promoters sequences [2]. These viruses are one of the most common types of bacteriophages with 26-29 defined or tentative species according to the VIII report of the International Committee on the Taxonomy of Viruses [3,4]. Most of the host species are members of the gamma-Proteobacteria (Erwinia, Escherichia, Klebsiella, Morganella, Pseudomonas, Salmonella, Vibrio, and Yersinia) but viral isolates also infecting alpha-Proteobacteria (Caulobacter, and Rhizobium) have been isolated. Fifteen T7-like phages have been sequenced and deposited with GenBank. As a result of a reanalysis, at the protein level, of relationships within the "T7-like viruses" this group of bacteriophages have been classified into the subfamily Autographivirinae which currently possesses three genera: T7-like, Sp6-like and phiKMV-like viruses [5]. Kvp1, the first Kluyvera cryocrescens-specific bacteriophage, was isolated from the Matanza River in Buenos Aires (Argentina) by Gadaleta and Zorzopulos [6]. Morphologically this phage is a member of the Podoviridae. Eleven clones derived from AluI or HaeIII digestion of the viral DNA were sequenced, by these authors, revealing strong sequence similarity to coliphage T7. To further analyze the correct taxonomic position of this virus we have completed the sequence of its genome noting its very close similarity to Yersinia phage Berlin and coliphage T3.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Results and discussion
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 90 113 90 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacillus thuringiensis </italic>
###xml 132 133 130 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 155 156 153 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 791 803 789 801 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas </italic>
###xml 827 835 825 833 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Erwinia </italic>
###xml 896 905 894 903 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 933 956 929 952 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas aeruginosa </italic>
###xml 90 112 <span type="species:ncbi:1428">Bacillus thuringiensis</span>
###xml 791 813 <span type="species:ncbi:197783">Pseudomonas phage gh-1</span>
###xml 827 847 <span type="species:ncbi:418443">Erwinia phage Era103</span>
###xml 905 919 <span type="species:ncbi:227720">phage phiA1122</span>
###xml 933 955 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
Pyrosequencing (454 technology) has been used to determine the sequence of the genomes of Bacillus thuringiensis phage 0305phi8-36 [7] and coliphage JK98 [8], and, in this incidence, the genome of Kvp1. Sequencing resulted in 2 contigs with 53-fold coverage. While this type of sequencing can result in potential errors at oligonucleotide runs, none were observed in the data on Kvp1. The gap, representing 0 bp, was closed by PCR amplification and ABI sequencing; while the nature of the termini were verified by primer walking off phage DNA template. The total genome is 39,472 bp with 194 bp terminal direct repeats, and a base composition of 48.6 mol%G+C - characteristics remarkably consistent with other T7-like phages. By comparison, the genomes of T7-like phages range from 37.4 kb (Pseudomonas phage gh-1) to 45.4 kb (Erwinia phage Era103) while the reported terminal repeats range from Yersinia phage phiA1122 at 148 bp to Pseudomonas aeruginosa phage LKD16 at 428 bp.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 251 260 251 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 251 272 <span type="species:ncbi:369257">Yersinia phage Berlin</span>
No tRNA genes were discovered, which was not an unexpected observation since no T7-like phages have been found to harbour them; 46 ORFs were delineated encoding protein products which show the strongest sequence similarity to gene products (Gps) from Yersinia phage Berlin (). To investigate the relationships further we employed two homology tools, one of which function at the DNA sequence level (Mauve) and one, CoreGenes, which compares proteins.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 72 73 72 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
Several regions of dissimilarity (indicates by areas of white in Figure 1) centred at genes 1.05, 4.7-2.8, 5.3-5.5, 17-17.2, 18.2 and at the left end of the genome are noted. Several of these genes are not found in phage Berlin. The most interesting difference is in gene 17 which encodes the tail fibre. As with other Gp17 homologs sequence similarity is only found at the N-terminus, the part of the protein which is associated with the tail structure. The C-terminus is involved in ligand interactions and exhibits considerable differences.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 29 38 29 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>

###begin p 15
###xml 108 126 108 126 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Autographivirinae </italic>
###xml 681 682 679 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 683 685 681 683 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 747 749 745 747 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 799 801 797 799 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 906 908 904 906 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1190 1199 1188 1197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 1323 1324 1321 1322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 1190 1211 <span type="species:ncbi:369257">Yersinia phage Berlin</span>
Using CoreGenes Kvp1 shares 37 (61.7%), 12 (23.1%) and 9 (18.4%) homologs with the type phages of the three Autographivirinae genera - T7, Sp6 and phiKMV-like viruses. The results indicate that Kvp1 is a member of the T7-like virus genus. A comparison with the proteome of phage Berlin indicates 37 homologs - 82.2% common proteins. While the percentage of common proteins is less when compared with coliphage T3 (70.9% similarity) the early regions of T3 and Kvp1 are very similar in that Kvp1 encodes T3-like Gp0.3, 0.6 and 0.7 homologs. The product of early gene 0.3 (Ocr) is a small protein which mimics B-form DNA and binds to, and inhibits, type I restriction endonucleases [9,10]; and, possesses S-adenosyl-L-methionine hydrolase activity [11]. Gp0.7 produces functions in host gene shutoff [12] and as a protein kinase which phosphorylates host elongation factors G and P and ribosomal protein S6 [13]. A major dissimilarity between Kvp1 and T3/T7 is that while the latter phages possess multiple strong promoters recognized by the host RNA polymerase, only a single promoter showing homology to the consensus was found in the Kvp1 genome. In keeping with the protein similarity to Yersinia phage Berlin, the phage specific promoters are also most closely related in sequence to those of this bacterial virus (Fig. 2).
###end p 15

###begin p 17
###xml 84 85 84 85 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
Typical of this type of bacteriophage, Kvp1 displays a simple protein profile (Fig. 3) in which most of the protein bands can be assigned based upon the extensive knowledge of these phages, and the mass of the protein bands compared with the in silico analysis of the proteins based upon genomic analysis.
###end p 17

###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 391 393 391 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 746 748 746 748 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 749 751 749 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 821 823 821 823 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 1146 1147 1146 1147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
One band of interest, with a mass of 43.6 kDa, was noted migrating just above that of the major capsid protein (Gp10) which was not immediately linked, on the basis of its mass, to a product of one of the morphogenesis genes. One of the characteristics of some T7-like phages is that they display, on SDS-PAGE, two "versions" of the major capsid protein which are designated as 10A and 10B [14]. The sequences of the amino termini of these proteins are identical, but during translation a rare ribosomal slippage occurs permitting the elongation of the protein product. The features of this system are a protein slightly larger than the capsid, a slippery site in the DNA/RNA and a downstream stem-loop structure capable of forming a pseudoknot [15,16]. We obtained evidence for a potential pseudoknot using pknotsRG at [17] located 144 bp downstream from the end of the capsid gene, but no typical slippage site was observed. The nature of this protein was investigated by in-gel enzymatic digestion and high-resolution mass spectrometry. MALDI QqTOF MS analysis on a tryptic digest has yielded 70% sequence coverage of the protein Gp10 (Figure 4), and three unique peptides were present at m/z 2244.123, m/z 2372.219 and m/z 2692.310 which revealed the distinct C-terminal amino acid residues 327-372 from the protein sequences of Gp10. This indicates that Kvp1 produces a major capsid protein (10A) and a minor protein (10B) through programmed -1 frameshifting at TTTTCA. The Gp10B protein is predicted to have a calculated mass of 42.1 kDa, consistent with the estimated value of 43.6 kDa by SDS-PAGE gel.
###end p 19

###xml 0 92 0 92 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MALDI QqTOF MS and MS/MS analyses on the in-gel tryptic digest of the 43.6 kDa protein band.</bold>

###end p 20
###begin title 21
Conclusion
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 39 48 39 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Kluyvera </italic>
###xml 101 113 101 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Podoviridae </italic>
###xml 123 140 123 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Autographivirinae</italic>
###xml 329 338 327 336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 338 352 <span type="species:ncbi:227720">phage phiA1122</span>
Our data conclusively demonstrate that Kluyvera virus Kvp1 is a member of T7-like virus genus of the Podoviridae subfamily Autographivirinae. It differs from phages such as T3 and phiYeO3-12 which exhibit capsid frameshifting at lysyl residues, by ribosomal slippage at polyU residues (phenylalanine) - a property it shares with Yersinia phage phiA1122.
###end p 22

Materials and methods
###end title 23
###begin title 24
Purification of phage wV8
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 41 57 41 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K. cryocrescens </italic>
###xml 378 380 372 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 0 18 <span type="species:ncbi:47049">Bacteriophage Kvp1</span>
###xml 41 56 <span type="species:ncbi:580">K. cryocrescens</span>
Bacteriophage Kvp1 (HER400) and its host K. cryocrescens strain HER1400 were received from the Felix d'Herelle Reference Center for Bacterial Viruses at Universite Laval (Quebec, QC, Canada). The phage was propagated at 30degreesC using standard protocols, precipitated using polyethylene glycol 8000 and purified through two rounds of CsCl equilibrium gradient centrifugation [18].
###end p 25
###begin title 26
DNA sequencing
###end title 26
###begin p 27
The DNA was isolated using the SDS-proteinase K protocol of Sambrook and Russell (2001) and was submitted to the McGill University and Genome Quebec Innovation Centre (Montreal, QC, Canada) for DNA sequencing. This resulted in two contigs which were closed using PCR with custom primers and, standard dideoxy sequencing of the amplicon (University of Guelph, Laboratory Services, Guelph, ON, Canada). The termini were determined by primer walking.
###end p 27

###begin title 32
Proteomics
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 10 12 10 12 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 582 583 582 583 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 586 588 586 588 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sub>
SDS-PAGE [26] was carried out on CsCl-purified phage particles along with the PageRuler Unstained Protein Ladder (Fermentas, Burlington, ON, Canada) stained with Coomassie brilliant blue R250 and characterized using Bionumerics software (Applied Maths). Bands were further characterized by in situ trypsin digestion and mass spectrometry. Briefly, the excised gel bands were destained until colorless, and dried using a SpeedVac. Following reduction with DTT and alkylation with iodoacetamide, the protein was digested with 10 ng of sequencing grade trypsin (Calbiochem) in 25 mM NH4HCO3 (pH 7.6) at 37degreesC overnight. The proteolytic peptides were extracted, and cleaned up by a C18 Ziptip (Millipore). MALDI data were acquired using an Applied Biosystems/MDS Sciex QStar XL quadrupole time-of-flight (QqTOF) mass spectrometer under a nitrogen laser (337 nm), and 2,5-dihydroxybenzoic acid was used as the matrix. All peptide fingerprinting masses (m/z) on the MS spectrum were compared with the theoretical values generated in-silico by MS-Digest, a ProteinProspector program developed in the UCSF Mass Spectrometry Facility . The individual peptide sequence was identified by MALDI MS/MS measurements on the same instrument using argon as the collision gas.
