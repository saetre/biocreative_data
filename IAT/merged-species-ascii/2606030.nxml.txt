###begin article-title 0
Nematode Homologue of PQBP1, a Mental Retardation Causative Gene, Is Involved in Lipid Metabolism
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: HA SM HO. Performed the experiments: KT SY MM Wi TI SM. Analyzed the data: HS SM HO. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: HO. Wrote the paper: HA SM HO.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PQBP1</italic>
###xml 159 164 159 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PQBP1</italic>
###xml 166 174 166 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 69 77 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 105 115 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
PQBP1 is a causative gene for X-linked mental retardation (MR) whose patients frequently show lean body. C. elegans has a strictly conserved homologue gene of PQBP1, T21D12.3.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology and Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 13 18 13 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Venus</italic>
###xml 34 43 34 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3-</italic>
###xml 109 116 109 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
We generated Venus-transgenic and T21D12.3-mutant nematodes to analyze developmental expression patterns and in vivo functions of the nematode PQBP1 homologue protein (pqbp-1.1). During development, pqbp-1.1 is expressed from cell proliferation stage to larva stage. In larva, intestinal cells show the highest expression of pqbp-1.1, while it decreases in adult worms. The mutants of pqbp-1.1 show a decrease of the lipid content in intestinal cells. Especially, incorporation of fatty acid into triglyceride is impaired. ShRNA-mediated repression of PQBP1 also leads to reduction of lipid content in mammalian primary white adipocytes.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion/ Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 198 203 198 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PQBP1</italic>
###xml 214 222 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
These results suggest that pqbp-1.1 is involved in lipid metabolism of intestinal cells. Dysfunction of lipid metabolism might underlie lean body, one of the most frequent symptoms associating with PQBP1-linked MR patients.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 170 173 170 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Waragai1">[1]</xref>
###xml 302 305 302 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Okazawa1">[2]</xref>
###xml 307 310 307 310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Komuro1">[3]</xref>
###xml 398 401 398 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Waragai2">[4]</xref>
###xml 403 406 403 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Zhang1">[5]</xref>
###xml 649 652 649 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Kalscheuer1">[6]</xref>
###xml 653 656 653 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Kleefstra1">[8]</xref>
###xml 723 726 723 726 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-deBrouwer1">[9]</xref>
###xml 868 872 868 872 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Stevenson1">[10]</xref>
###xml 807 815 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 859 867 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
PQBP1 was identified as a binding protein to the polyglutamine (polyQ) tract of the causative gene products of a major group of neurodegeneration, polyglutamine diseases [1]. PQBP1 includes two protein interaction domains: WW domain (WWD) through which PQBP1 interacts with RNA polymerase II and NpwBP [2], [3] and C-terminal domain (CTD) through which it interacts with a splicing factor, U5-15kD [4], [5]. Subsequently, PQBP1 was identified as a causative gene for a group of mental retardation including Renpenning syndrome, Golabi-Ito-Hall and the Sutherland-Haan syndromes as well as non-symptomatic X-linked mental retardation with no anomaly [6]-[8]. The frequency of these PQBP1-linked disorders is relatively high [9]. Among various associating symptoms, one of the most frequent symptoms in these patients is lean body, which is found in 87% of the patients [10].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 138 146 138 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 254 262 254 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 473 478 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 659 664 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In this study, we investigated expression patterns of nematode homologue of PQBP1 (pqbp-1.1) during development, and generated mutants of T21D12.3. We found that pqbp-1.1 is expressed in intestinal cells storing lipid in nematodes and that the mutant of T21D12.3 shows reduction of lipid storage in intestinal cells although intestinal cells did not reduce in their number. Furthermore, we found shRNA-mediated knocking down of PQBP1 leads to reduction of lipid storage in mouse primary white adipocytes. These results suggest that PQBP1 is involved in lipid metabolism beyond species and that dysfunction of lipid metabolism might be a cause of lean body in human PQBP1 mutations.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
Results
###end title 11
###begin title 12
PQBP1 homologue is expressed in intestinal cells
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 48 56 48 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 167 171 167 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Okazawa2">[11]</xref>
###xml 320 328 320 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 555 559 555 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Nagai1">[12]</xref>
###xml 581 589 581 589 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 653 657 653 657 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-GengyoAndo1">[13]</xref>
###xml 673 681 673 681 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 686 694 686 694 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.4</italic>
###xml 696 701 696 701 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">pat-6</italic>
###xml 731 737 731 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g001">Fig. 1</xref>
###xml 858 863 858 863 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">pat-6</italic>
###xml 882 890 882 890 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 892 898 892 898 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g001">Fig. 1</xref>
###xml 953 961 953 961 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 0 10 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 203 208 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 353 363 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
C. elegans possesses a homologue gene of PQBP1, T21D12.3, which encodes a protein (pqbp-1.1) containing both of the essential protein interacting domains, WWD and CTD [11]. Amino acid identities between human and nematode PQBP1 are 35% in the whole molecule, 52% in WWD and 55% in CTD. To analyze expression patterns of T21D12.3, the PQBP1 homologue in C. elegans, we constructed a reporter plasmid expressing a fluorescent fusion protein of pqbp-1.1 and Venus, a variant of yellow fluorescent protein with fast and efficient maturation for visualization [12] under the control of T21D12.3 enhancer/promoter according to the method described previously [13]. Endogenously, T21D12.3 and T21D12.4 (pat-6) are regulated as an operon (Fig. 1). Therefore, we constructed a plasmid that contains 6.7 kb genome DNA from the 2.7 kb upstream of the neighboring gene (pat-6) to the 3' end of T21D12.3 (Fig. 1). Thereafter, Venus cDNA was subcloned into exon 4 of T21D12.3 to express a nematode PQBP1-Venus fusion protein (pqbp-1.1-Venus).
###end p 13
###begin title 14
###xml 49 54 49 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Venus</italic>
###xml 76 84 76 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 27 37 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
Construction of transgenic C. elegans expressing Venus under the control of T21D12.3 enhancer/promoter.
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 13 21 13 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.4</italic>
###xml 23 28 23 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">pat-6</italic>
###xml 173 178 173 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">pat-6</italic>
T21D12.3 and T21D12.4 (pat-6) are transcriptionally regulated as an operon. Therefore, 6.7 kb genome DNA from the transcriptional initiation site to the neighboring gene to pat-6 was subcloned into pGEMT-T plasmid. By injecting the plasmid into germ line of adult worm, we obtained transgenic nematodes in which the Venus cDNA was subcloned into exon 4 to express a PQBP1-Venus fusion protein under the control of endogenous enhancer/promoter.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 83 104 83 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Materials and Methods</italic>
###xml 83 104 83 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="sec" rid="s4"><italic>Materials and Methods</italic></xref>
###xml 249 255 249 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g002">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 257 258 257 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g003">3</xref>
###xml 342 348 342 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g002">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 426 433 426 433 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g003">Fig. 3A</xref>
###xml 725 732 725 732 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g003">Fig. 3C</xref>
###xml 888 891 888 891 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Okazawa1">[2]</xref>
###xml 893 897 893 897 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Okazawa2">[11]</xref>
###xml 899 910 899 910 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g003">Fig. 3B, 3C</xref>
By injecting the reporter plasmid, we obtained F2 transgenic worms as described in Materials and Methods. The fluorescence signals of the pqbp-1.1-Venus fusion protein indicated that pqbp-1.1 was expressed from 24-cells stage to Larva stage 4 (L4) (Fig. 2, 3). From 24 cells- to gastrulation-stage, pqbp-1.1-Venus was expressed ubiquitously (Fig. 2). At L1 stage, neural cells in the head ganglion express the fusion protein (Fig. 3A). From late L1 stage, intestinal cells expressed pqbp-1.1-Venus strongly and the high expression continued to L4 stage. The peak of expression was observed at L3 stage. Pharyngeal cells show an almost similar pattern of expression during development, while the peak seems to be at L4 stage (Fig. 3C). Higher magnification of the signals revealed that the fusion protein formed speckle-like intranuclear dots (or nuclear bodies) just like mammalian PQBP1 [2], [11] (Fig. 3B, 3C).

###begin title 27
Depletion of PQBP1 homologue leads to reduction of lipid storage
###end title 27
###begin p 28

###xml 72 76 72 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-GengyoAndo2">[15]</xref>
###xml 102 108 102 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">tm3004</italic>

###xml 312 318 312 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g003">Fig. 3</xref>
###xml 320 321 320 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g004">4</xref>

Screening of the T21D12.3 mutants was performed as described previously [15]. Consequently, a mutant (tm3004) was obtained from the screening, which lacks a part of the T21D12.3 genome including the first methionine sequence (Fig. 5A). Because the expression of T21D12.3 was rather confined to intestinal cells (Fig. 3, 4) and because intestinal cells play critical roles in nutritional state of nematode [16], we investigated intestinal lipid storage of these mutants in this study.

In addition to T21D12.3, the genome of C. elegans encodes another paralogue, T07A9.1 (pqbp-1.2), possessing a low homology to PQBP1 but lacking WWD. Thus, we similarly screened and obtained a mutant of T07A9.1 (tm3045) lacking a part of the gene (Fig. 5B). Therefore, pqbp-1.1 but not pqbp-1.2 seems to be a proper orthologue of PQBP1 at least from the aspects of molecular structure and lipid metabolism.

###begin title 39

###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 185 189 185 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Qi1">[14]</xref>
###xml 38 48 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 238 242 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 335 340 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We finally tested whether findings in C. elegans were applicable to mammalian adipocytes. Reviewing our previous in situ hybridization data confirmed expression of PQBP1 in fat tissues [14]. Primary white adipocytes prepared from newborn mice were transfected during growth phase with a plasmid PQBP1-shRNA-ZsGreen, which express both mouse PQBP1-shRNA and ZsGreen. We also used non-silencing shRNA-ZsGreen transfected and non-transfected cells as controls.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 88 95 88 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g008">Fig. 8A</xref>

Both the sizes and Oil Red staining intensities of lipid droplets were significantly reduced (Fig. 8B). Non-silencing shRNA / ZsGreen-expressing cells also did not show any significant change (Fig. 8B). These results collectively support that mammalian PQBP1 also decrease the lipid content.

###begin title 44
Discussion
###end title 44
###begin title 45
Nematode PQBP1 homologue is involved in lipid metabolism
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 212 222 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 532 543 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis</span>
The method used in this study, which detects a marker fusion protein (PQBP1-Venus) expression under the control of the endogenous enhancer/promoter, definitely shows the endogenous expression pattern of PQBP1 in C. elegans. The expression pattern of nematode PQBP1 was actually unexpected, since we had expected to observe a neuron-dominant expression pattern. However it is not rare that a molecule is differentially used in different organisms even if it is highly conserved beyond species. Moreover, PQBP1 is highly conserved in Arabidopsis although the plant does not have the brain.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 91 97 91 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g001">Fig. 1</xref>
###xml 98 98 98 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g002"/>
###xml 98 98 98 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g003"/>
###xml 98 99 98 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g004">4</xref>
###xml 177 183 177 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g005">Fig. 5</xref>
###xml 185 186 185 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g006">6</xref>
###xml 619 625 619 625 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g007">Fig. 7</xref>
###xml 743 748 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 749 757 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 965 970 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our results clearly showed that nematode pqbp-1.1 is expressed in cells for lipid-storage (Fig. 1-4) and that pqbp-1.1 depletion links to abnormal lipid metabolism in nematode (Fig. 5, 6). Mammals possess fat cells to store lipid in the body, while nematode keep lipid in intestinal cells. However, although the cell types for lipid storage are different, compositions of lipid (such as saturated or unsaturated fatty acid, triglyceride) and metabolic pathways are fundamentally conserved beyond species. In addition, the knock down experiment with shRNA showed reduction of lipid storage in mammalian white adipocyte (Fig. 7). Therefore, the mutant nematodes generated in this study could be a hypothetical model for the lean body symptom of human patients linked to PQBP1 mutations. This model will be available for further analysis of the detailed molecular mechanisms underlying the lean body, and such studies might develop PQBP1 to a novel target molecule in human lipid metabolism.
###end p 47
###begin title 48
Lipid metabolism in PQBP1 and polyQ pathologies
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 43 47 43 47 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Busch1">[18]</xref>
###xml 249 253 245 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Cui1">[19]</xref>
###xml 254 258 250 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Weydt1">[21]</xref>
###xml 455 474 444 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Puigserver1">[for review see 22]</xref>
###xml 666 670 651 655 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Puigserver1">[22]</xref>
###xml 781 784 762 765 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Okazawa1">[2]</xref>
###xml 786 789 767 770 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Waragai2">[4]</xref>
PQBP1 is known to interact with mutant Htt [18]. Meanwhile, mutant huntingtin (Htt), the causative gene product of Huntington's disease (HD), is known to downregulate peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1 alpha (PGC-1alpha) [19]-[21], a co-activator of peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor (PPAR-gamma) that is essential for transcription of a number of genes involved in glucose metabolism and beta-oxidation of fatty acid [for review see 22]. Interestingly, in addition to binding to the promoter, PGC-1alpha participates in RNA elongation and splicing through binding to factors anchored onto C-terminal domain of RNA polymerase II [22]. The binding property of PGC-1alpha to RNA polymerase II and splicing proteins is homologous to that of PQBP1 [2], [4], suggesting a possible relationship between PQBP1 and PGC-1alpha. Therefore, it might be worth analyzing whether PQBP1 is involved in the same transcription protein complex with PGC-1alpha and how PQBP1 affects lipid metabolism mediated by PGC-1alpha in normal physiology and in HD pathology.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 173 177 173 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Nishino1">[23]</xref>
###xml 211 215 207 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Matsusue1">[24]</xref>
###xml 322 326 318 322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Puri1">[25]</xref>
A hypothesis to account for the change of lipid granules would be that PQBP1 regulates expression of Fsp27/CIDE-C, which locates at lipid droplets and influences their size [23], like PPAR-gamma regulates Fsp27 [24]. CIDE-A, another lipid droplet-associated protein controlling triglyceride deposition in white adipocytes [25] might be another target of transcriptional control by PQBP1. Though we have obtained preliminary data to support the hypothesis (data not shown), these issues would be the next project regarding the lipid metabolism function of PQBP1.
###end p 50
###begin title 51
###xml 31 41 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
PQBP1 expression in neurons of C. elegans
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 395 402 395 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004104-g003">Fig. 3A</xref>
###xml 644 652 644 652 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">T21D12.3</italic>
###xml 1053 1057 1053 1057 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004104-Yoshimura1">[26]</xref>
###xml 306 314 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 698 708 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 802 812 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 854 864 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 1041 1046 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In this study, we focused on the lipid metabolism, and have not analyzed neuronal functions of the nematode homologue of PQBP1. However, in mammals, PQBP1 definitely functions in neurons and/or progenitor cells, and the neuronal functions are essential for understanding MR symptoms found in PQBP1-mutated patients. Here, we found that pqbp-1.1 was expressed in head ganglion cells at L1 stage (Fig. 3A), which may correspond to neural progenitor cells in mammals. Although our preliminary observation showed no remarkable decline of locomotive activity, further analysis is needed for definite evaluation and it is essential for understanding T21D12.3 function in learning and memory functions in C. elegans. Meanwhile, analysis of PQBP1 functions in neurons are now undergoing in our laboratory with Drosophila mutants and mammalian neural stem cells. Drosophila mutants show a unique type of learning impairment (unpublished observation), which is different from the long-term memory disturbance observed in transgenic fly overexpressing human PQBP1 [26]. These experiments using various animal models will promote understanding of the whole scheme of PQBP1 diseases and might lead to certain insights into basic biology in the future.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 6 14 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 560 564 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 594 598 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Human patients with mutations of the PQBP1 gene show mental retardation and microcephaly as major symptoms. However, PQBP1 is expressed widely in multiple tissues of mammals. Therefore, if different animal models have different advantages for analyzing multiple molecular functions of a gene, we should better take the merit. We should employ appropriate animal models for appropriate functional analyses. In this study, we took advantage of the nematode model for analyzing the phenotype in lipid metabolism. We have already constructed conditional knock out mice and RNAi-mediated knock down mice as well as fly models for analyzing neuronal functions of PQBP1, which will be reported elsewhere.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
The "lean body" is not a tiny symptom because the research on this symptom might link to cell metabolism and the related disorders. Although the phenotype we observed in this study might be a downstream consequence of essential molecular functions of PQBP1, still this work shows a novel finding to suggest a possible involvement of PQBP1 in the lipid metabolism.
###end p 54

###begin title 63
Nile Red staining of intestinal cells and morphological observation
###end title 63
###begin p 64
Nile Red (5H-benzo[a] phenoxazine-5-one, 9-doethylamino) was solved in acetone at 500 microg/ml and diluted to 2.5 microg/ml with phosphate-buffered saline. 3 ml of the solution was layered on a 35 mm NGM plate and dried at room temperature. Starved worms were cultured for 3 days before observation. Microscopic images were obtained by Olympus BX-50 and Olympus SenSys CCD camera. Fluorescence of Nile Red was observed with a Rhodamine filter (580 nm). Nile Red staining images were obtained at the same magnification (20x) and same exposure time (260 msec). Signal intensities in the images were quantified using Aquacosmos (HAMAMATSU). The arbitrary signals of intestinal cells were subtracted by those of body cavity to standardize the intensities. More than 100 cells were quantified in each type of nematode.
###end p 64

###begin title 69
Primary culture of white adipocytes and shRNA transfection
###end title 69
###begin p 70
###xml 391 392 365 366 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 493 494 467 468 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sup>
###xml 502 503 476 477 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
###xml 177 183 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 845 851 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 1250 1256 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
White adipocytic precursor cells were obtained from Primary Cell Co., Ltd (Hokkaido, Japan). The cells were grown in growth medium (Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium, 10% foetal bovine serum, 10unit/ml penicillin, 10 microg/ml streptomycin, 17 microM pantothenic acid, 33 microM (+)-biotin, 100 microM ascorbic acid, 1 microM octanoic acid, and 50 nM Triiodothyronine) at 37degreesC and 5% CO2 for 24 hours. The cells were transferred to 10 cm dish pre-coated with collagen at a density of 2x104cells/cm2 and incubated for 12 hours. Thereafter, the cells were transfected with PQBP1 shRNA-ZsGreen plasmid using Effecten (Qiagen) and incubated for 12 hours. Transfection with a non-sense shRNA plasmid and the mock treatment were performed in parallel. Subsequently, the medium was changed to Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum, 10unit/ml penicillin, 10 microg/ml streptomycin, 17 microM pantothenic acid, 33 microM (+)-biotin, 100 microM ascorbic acid, 1 microM octanoic acid, 50 nM Triiodothyronine, 10 microg/ml Insulin, and 2.5 microM Dexamethasone for 48 hours to differentiate the cells to white adipocyte. ###end title 71
