The genome and proteome of a virulent Escherichia coli O157:H7 bacteriophage closely resembling Salmonella phage Felix O1

###begin p 2
###xml 47 64 47 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 181 192 181 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella </italic>
###xml 211 229 211 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Erwinia amylovora </italic>
###xml 536 553 534 551 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 47 71 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">Escherichia coli O157:H7</span>
###xml 211 228 <span type="species:ncbi:552">Erwinia amylovora</span>
###xml 536 560 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">Escherichia coli O157:H7</span>
###xml 665 677 <span type="species:ncbi:10665">coliphage T4</span>
Based upon whole genome and proteome analysis, Escherichia coli O157:H7-specific bacteriophage (phage) wV8 belongs to the new myoviral genus, "the Felix O1-like viruses" along with Salmonella phage Felix O1 and Erwinia amylovora phage phiEa21-4. The genome characteristics of phage wV8 (size 88.49 kb, mol%G+C 38.9, 138 ORFs, 23 tRNAs) are very similar to those of phage Felix O1 (86.16 kb, 39.0 mol%G+C, 131 ORFs and 22 tRNAs) and, indeed most of the proteins have their closest homologs within Felix O1. Approximately one-half of the Escherichia coli O157:H7 mutants resistant to phage wV8 still serotype as O157:H7 indicating that this phage may recognize, like coliphage T4, two different surface receptors: lipopolysaccharide and, perhaps, an outer membrane protein.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Findings
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 189 206 189 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 308 316 308 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 361 362 361 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 363 364 363 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 410 411 410 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 640 648 640 648 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 753 764 753 764 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella </italic>
###xml 780 781 780 781 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 782 783 782 783 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 189 213 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">Escherichia coli O157:H7</span>
###xml 308 323 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">E. coli O157:H7</span>
###xml 353 359 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calves</span>
###xml 640 655 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">E. coli O157:H7</span>
Bacteriophages (phages) are promising potential alternatives to antibiotics as therapeutics to reduce carriage of pathogens by food animals, thus preventing the spread of organisms such as Escherichia coli O157:H7 along the food chain. Our research has shown that a cocktail of virulent phages can eliminate E. coli O157:H7 from experimentally infected calves [1,2]. Phage V8, isolated originally from sewage [3] was renamed wV8 in our laboratory to indicate that it was obtained from the National Microbiology Laboratory (Winnipeg), and was included in the phage cocktail due to its complementary host range on common phage types (PTs) of E. coli O157:H7. Here we report on the genome and proteome of phage wV8, noting its very close similarity to the Salmonella phage Felix O1 [4-7].
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 278 289 278 289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Myoviridae </italic>
###xml 440 441 440 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 708 709 708 709 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
Phage wV8, purified as described below, was negatively stained with 1% (w/v) uranyl acetate for 20 s and the particles were observed using a LEO912AB and a Philips EM 300 transmission electron microscope operating at 100 kV and 60 kV, respectively. Phage wV8 is a member of the Myoviridae and is morphologically identical to Felix O1 and related phages. Viral particles were morphologically intact and generally have extended tails (Figure 1). Measurement of 20 particles indicated phage wV8 has a head 70.4 nm in diameter and a tail 112.8 x 16.8 nm long. These closely resemble those reported for phage Felix O1, in which the head measured 73 nm in diameter and the noncontracted tail was 113 x 17 nm long [8]. Phage wV8 has a neck of 7 x 7 nm, a collar disk of 10 x 2 nm, and four fibres of 40 x 2 nm that are generally folded along the tail, but may become unfolded in some particles. Tails have transverse striations of 3 nm periodicity, but sometimes present a pattern of overlapping subunits.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 0 69 0 69 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Electron micrographs of phage wV8 showing typical myovirus morphology</bold>
Electron micrographs of phage wV8 showing typical myovirus morphology. Open arrowheads (Figure 1B) point to extended tail fibres while filled arrowheads (Figures 1A, 1B) point to the more commonly observed folded tail fibres. A collar and neck can be seen on several of the particles.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 98 106 98 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 147 148 147 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 157 177 157 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella enterica </italic>
###xml 219 227 219 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 254 265 254 265 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella </italic>
###xml 804 805 797 798 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sup>
###xml 808 810 801 803 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sup>
###xml 943 951 930 938 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1004 1012 991 999 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1046 1048 1033 1035 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 1095 1106 1082 1093 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella </italic>
###xml 1121 1122 1108 1109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 98 113 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">E. coli O157:H7</span>
###xml 157 176 <span type="species:ncbi:28901">Salmonella enterica</span>
###xml 219 234 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">E. coli O157:H7</span>
###xml 943 958 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">E. coli O157:H7</span>
###xml 1004 1019 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">E. coli O157:H7</span>
For host range studies, phage wV8 was tested for lytic activity on reference strains of 12 common E. coli O157:H7 PTs, the entire ECOR collection [9] and 12 Salmonella enterica serovars. Lytic activity on the reference E. coli O157:H7 PT strains and the Salmonella serovars was determined at multiplicities of infection (MOI) of between 0.001 and 10 in broth cultures in microplates incubated for 5 h at 37degreesC before inspection for complete lysis (no visible turbidity). Bacteria showing no visible lysis at any MOI were considered resistant to phage wV8, while those showing complete lysis at MOIs of 10 or less were considered sensitive to phage wV8. Strains of the ECOR collection were tested as freshly seeded bacterial lawns on agar plates spotted with 20 mul of diluted phage wV8 containing 104-106 pfu. After incubation for 18 h at 37degreesC, strains showing >50% lysis were considered sensitive. Phage wV8 is highly specific for E. coli O157:H7 strains, completely lyses the 12 most common E. coli O157:H7 (PTs) isolated in Canada [10] and has no lytic activity against any of the Salmonella strains (Table 1).

###begin p 11
###xml 28 36 28 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 221 223 221 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 436 441 436 441 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rIIA </italic>
###xml 634 636 634 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 651 653 651 653 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 28 35 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Phage wV8 was propagated on E. coli strain EC990779 (ECOR strain 6, O173:H), precipitated from clarified lysates using polyethylene glycol 8000 and purified through two rounds of CsCl equilibrium gradient centrifugation [11]. The DNA was isolated as described by these authors and subjected to pyrosequencing at the National Microbiology Laboratory (Winnipeg, MB). Prior to annotation, the genome was opened immediately upstream of the rIIA gene so that it could be directly compared with the sequence of Felix O1. The genome was annotated using Kodon (Applied Maths, Austin, TX) and a variety of online tools  including tRNAScan-SE [12] and ARAGORN [13] at their default setting. The GenBank accession number for this sequence is .
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 111 122 111 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella </italic>
###xml 420 421 420 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
The genome characteristics of wV8 (size: 88.49 kb, 38.9 mol%G+C, 138 ORFs, 23 tRNAs) closely resemble those of Salmonella phage Felix O1 (86.16 kb, 39.0 mol%G+C, 131 ORFs and 22 tRNAs) and, indeed many of the proteins have their closest homologs with those of Felix O1 (NC_005282). This is also substantiated by SDS-PAGE analysis of the structural proteins of wV8 and Felix O1 which show considerable similarity (Figure 2). The one notable exception lies in the largest proteins (wV8 Gp83, 91.5 kDa; and, Felix O1 orf184, 84.1 kDa), which bioinformatic analyses revealed to be the tail fibre proteins. Matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization quadrupole time-of-flight (MALDI QqTOF) MS analysis of the wV8 protein indicates that these two proteins are homologous.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 0 85 0 85 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Structural proteins of phages Felix O1 (lane B) and wV8 (lane C) revealed by SDS-PAGE</bold>
Structural proteins of phages Felix O1 (lane B) and wV8 (lane C) revealed by SDS-PAGE. Clear boxes are to the left side of the phage tail fibre protein bands.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
Tandem mass spectrometric (MS/MS) measurements were performed to sequence all the trypsin-digested peptides in order to obtain the high confidence protein identification in the databases. Initial MS/MS search using Mascot  against NCBI databases retrieved a putative tail fibre protein from phage Felix O1 (NCBI: GI:38707850, NP_944923), where five sequences out of the observed 28 peptides were matched (m/z 775.427, 1361.735, 1764.869, 2019.015, 2215.053). Using a custom wV8-specific protein database, a thorough examination of all peptide sequences confirmed the protein assignment, with these five peptides providing 44.3% sequence coverage. Sequence alignment of the Felix O1 and wV8 tail fibre orthologs using ALIGN  revealed 65.7% identity.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 437 438 437 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 441 443 441 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
Tail fibre proteins from related phages typically show strong sequence similarity at the N-termini, where the protein associates with the phage tail plate. The carboxy termini, associated with receptor interaction, vary considerably. With Felix O1 and wV8, we see a completely different type of relationship: four regions of similarity separated by regions of dissimilarity, with both the C- and N-termini conserved (see Additional file 1) [14].
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 32 34 32 34 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 74 82 74 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 124 132 124 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 267 275 267 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 74 89 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">E. coli O157:H7</span>
###xml 124 139 <span type="species:ncbi:83334">E. coli O157:H7</span>
###xml 267 274 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 486 498 <span type="species:ncbi:10665">coliphage T4</span>
Since Felix O1 is LPS-specific [15], we analyzed wV8-resistant mutants of E. coli O157:H7. An overnight broth culture of an E. coli O157:H7 strain was mixed with excess wV8 and incubated on plates for 24 h. Nine independent mutants were isolated and serotyped by the E. coli (VTEC) Reference Laboratory at the Laboratory for Foodborne Zoonoses. Approximately one-half of these still serotyped as O157:H7, while half were untypable (rough) indicating that this phage may recognize, like coliphage T4, two different surface receptors: lipopolysaccharide and, perhaps, an outer membrane protein.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 65 67 65 67 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 92 94 92 94 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 138 140 138 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 255 256 255 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 579 581 579 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
Whole genome comparisons were made at the DNA level using Mauve [16] and Advanced Pipmaker [17] and at the protein level using CoreGenes [18]. The latter program revealed that Felix O1 and wV8 share 92% of their proteins in common. Mauve analysis (Figure 3) reveals considerable sequence similarity between Felix O1 and wV8 with a few noticeable differences which centre at 11.9, 26.7 52.3, and 60 kb on the Felix O1 genome. The presence of heterologous sequences within these phage genomes is completely in accord with the evolution of the viruses via horizontal gene transfer [19].
###end p 17

Alignment, based upon BLASTN of the genome of phages wV8 (top) and Felix O1 (bottom). The contiguous black boxes under the phage names represent the position of the genes. Regions of nucleotide similarity are indicated by the height of the coloured bars while those regions which are dissimilar are in white. ###begin p 19
###xml 148 153 148 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 174 185 174 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Myoviridae </italic>
###xml 357 375 357 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Erwinia amylovora </italic>
###xml 357 374 <span type="species:ncbi:552">Erwinia amylovora</span>
Based upon an extensive analysis of relationships between prokaryotic viruses (Lavigne R, Summer EJ, Seto D, Mahadevan P, Nilsson AS, Ackermann H-W et al.: Classification of Myoviridae bacteriophages using BLASTP-tools: submitted) this level of similarity indicates that wV8 should be classified into the newly proposed genus,"Felix O1 viruses", along with Erwinia amylovora phage phiEa21-4.
###end p 19

Conclusion
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 54 65 54 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Myoviridae </italic>

E. coli O157:H7-specific phage wV8 is a member of the Myoviridae and is closely related to the Salmonella-specific phage, Felix O1. Their tail fibre proteins show a unique pattern of sequence relationship.
