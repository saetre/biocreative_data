###begin article-title 0
Activity of the beta-catenin phosphodestruction complex at cell-cell contacts is enhanced by cadherin-based adhesion
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 135 138 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 247 250 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 411 414 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 610 613 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 679 682 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 826 829 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
It is well established that cadherin protein levels impact canonical Wnt signaling through binding and sequestering beta-catenin (beta-cat) from T-cell factor family transcription factors. Whether changes in intercellular adhesion can affect beta-cat signaling and the mechanism through which this occurs has remained unresolved. We show that axin, APC2, GSK-3beta and N-terminally phosphorylated forms of beta-cat can localize to cell-cell contacts in a complex that is molecularly distinct from the cadherin-catenin adhesive complex. Nonetheless, cadherins can promote the N-terminal phosphorylation of beta-cat, and cell-cell adhesion increases the turnover of cytosolic beta-cat. Together, these data suggest that cadherin-based cell-cell adhesion limits Wnt signals by promoting the activity of a junction-localized beta-cat phosphodestruction complex, which may be relevant to tissue morphogenesis and cell fate decisions during development.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Introduction
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 521 544 505 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib32">Willert and Jones, 2006</xref>
###xml 895 915 870 890 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Heasman et al., 1994</xref>
###xml 917 933 892 908 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib3">Cox et al., 1996</xref>
###xml 935 955 910 930 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Fagotto et al., 1996</xref>
###xml 31 34 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 177 180 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 344 347 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 576 579 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 804 807 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 890 893 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
The protein beta-catenin (beta-cat) serves two functions that are fundamental to tissue structure and function. As a central component of intercellular adherens junctions, beta-cat links cadherin adhesion receptors to the actin cytoskeleton through the actin-binding protein alpha-catenin. As a key component of the Wnt signaling cascade, beta-cat partners with lymphocyte enhancer factor/T-cell factor (TCF) family DNA-binding proteins to recruit chromatin-modifying factors required for gene expression (for review see Willert and Jones, 2006). The fact that cells use beta-cat in both cell-cell adhesion and transcriptional activation has long suggested that these two processes may be coordinated. Indeed, gain- and loss-of-function studies support a model in which cadherins directly sequester beta-cat from the nucleus and serve as a sink for the Wnt-generated cytosolic pool of beta-cat (Heasman et al., 1994; Cox et al., 1996; Fagotto et al., 1996). However, whether changes in cell-cell adhesion, rather than changes in cadherin abundance, can affect Wnt signaling has remained unanswered.
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 394 410 371 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Liu et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 589 606 560 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Hart et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 746 761 714 729 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Ha et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 819 838 787 806 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib29">van Es et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 961 983 929 951 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">McCartney et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 985 1003 953 971 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib35">Yu and Bienz, 1999</xref>
###xml 1005 1020 973 988 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib36">Yu et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 1119 1141 1084 1106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">McCartney et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 1143 1162 1108 1127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib29">van Es et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 1164 1179 1129 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib36">Yu et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 1181 1194 1146 1159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib61">Polakis, 2000</xref>
###xml 1339 1359 1304 1324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Jarrett et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 28 31 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 162 165 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 307 310 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 584 587 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 702 705 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 1104 1107 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
In the absence of Wnt, beta-cat activity is largely regulated by a multiprotein complex that mediates the phosphorylation-dependent destruction of cytosolic beta-cat. Specifically, the scaffold protein axin recruits two Ser/Thr kinases, CK1-alpha and GSK-3beta, directing an ordered phosphorylation of beta-cat at Ser45 (by CK1-alpha) and subsequently at Thr41, Ser37, and Ser33 (by GSK-3beta; Liu et al., 2002). Phosphorylated Ser33 and Ser37 are recognized by the E3 ligase beta-TrCP (transducin repeat-containing protein), which leads to the ubiquitylation and degradation of beta-cat (Hart et al., 1999). The tumor suppressor adenomatous polyposis coli (APC) appears to facilitate the flux of beta-cat through the complex by displacing axin (Ha et al., 2004). APC2 is a second APC gene expressed in most epithelia (van Es et al., 1999). Unlike the APC mutated in colon cancers and found at the ends of microtubules, APC2 localizes to actin-rich structures (McCartney et al., 1999; Yu and Bienz, 1999; Yu et al., 1999). Although gain- and loss-of-function studies show that both APCs can inhibit beta-cat signaling (McCartney et al., 1999; van Es et al., 1999; Yu et al., 1999; Polakis, 2000), the different subcellular distributions of APC and APC2, along with evidence that APC2 protein expression is maintained in APC mutant tumors (Jarrett et al., 2001), suggest that they participate in different phosphodestruction complexes that are subject to distinct regulation.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 221 247 215 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">Klaus and Birchmeier, 2008</xref>
###xml 47 50 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 311 314 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 493 496 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
Wnts are the best-known inhibitors of the beta-cat phosphodestruction complex. These ligands engage a receptor complex and induce the local inactivation of GSK-3beta within and/or disruption of the axin scaffold complex (Klaus and Birchmeier, 2008). As a result, an N-terminally hypophosphorylated form of beta-cat accumulates, translocates to the nucleus, and activates TCF/lymphocyte enhancer factor family transcription factors. In this study, we show that N-terminal phospho forms of beta-cat and components of the phosphodestruction complex are localized to sites of cell contact, where the activity of this complex can be enhanced by cadherin-based cell adhesion.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results and discussion
###end title 6
###begin title 7
###xml 73 76 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
Epithelial cadherin (E-cad) reduces levels of the signaling form of beta-cat by promoting its N-terminal phosphorylation
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 281 302 272 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib11">Gottardi et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 57 60 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 227 230 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 276 279 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 413 416 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 502 505 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 589 592 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
The SW480 colon carcinoma cell line exhibits robust beta-cat/TCF signaling because of a loss-of-function mutation in APC and low levels of E-cad. Restoration of E-cad expression reduced the oncogenic signaling activity of beta-cat without markedly sequestering cytosolic beta-cat (Gottardi et al., 2001). This finding raised the possibility that Wnts might generate a minor, transcriptionally active form of beta-cat used for both signaling and adhesion. Alternatively, the cadherin might inhibit beta-cat signaling through a mechanism that catalytically inactivates the bulk pool of beta-cat.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 167 191 161 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Guger and Gumbiner, 2000</xref>
###xml 193 211 187 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib28">Staal et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 364 372 355 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Fig. 1 A</xref>
###xml 32 35 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 132 135 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 359 362 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 478 481 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
It is now appreciated that beta-cat unphosphorylated at Ser33, Ser37, and Thr41 is more transcriptionally active than forms of beta-cat phosphorylated at these sites (Guger and Gumbiner, 2000; Staal et al., 2002). Several recently developed reagents allow for detection of these specific phosphorylated and unphosphorylated epitopes in the N terminus of beta-cat (Fig. 1 A). Because SW480 cells are mutant for APC, the regulation and localization of these phospho forms of beta-cat, which are normally coupled with ubiquitylation and degradation, can be characterized.

###xml 288 310 282 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib30">van Noort et al., 2002</xref>

###xml 1138 1162 1114 1138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Guger and Gumbiner, 2000</xref>
###xml 1164 1182 1140 1158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib28">Staal et al., 2002</xref>

###xml 263 266 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 426 429 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 443 446 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 695 698 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 780 783 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 887 890 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 1008 1011 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 1243 1246 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 1323 1326 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
Using the mock (-E-cad) and E-cad-transfected cell lines previously described (Gottardi et al., 2001), we confirm that total levels of beta-cat are unchanged by cadherin expression (Fig. 1 B). However, using an antibody that recognizes the signaling form of beta-cat (Staal et al., 2002; van Noort et al., 2002), we observe an inverse correlation between E-cad levels and the abundance of this hypophosphorylated form of beta-cat (active beta-cat [ABC]; Fig. 1 B). Even mock-transfected SW480 cells, which normally express low levels of endogenous E-cad, up-regulate E-cad in densely plated cultures and correlate with reduced ABC (Fig. 1 B, third lane vs. first lane). The total amount of beta-cat appears unchanged, suggesting that E-cad may promote the phosphorylation of beta-cat and thereby loss of the unphosphorylated epitope. To test this, we used antibodies that recognize beta-cat phosphorylated on Thr41 and Ser45 (phospho-41/45 [P41/45]; Fig. 1 A) and observe an E-cad-dependent increase in beta-cat phosphorylated at these sites (Fig. 1 C). As phosphorylation at these residues is associated with reduced signaling activity (Guger and Gumbiner, 2000; Staal et al., 2002), these data suggest that E-cad may be able to inhibit beta-cat signaling catalytically by promoting the N-terminal phosphorylation of beta-cat.

To further characterize this mechanism, we sought to determine the subcellular localization and fractionation characteristics of these phospho forms of beta-cat. Of interest, beta-cat phosphorylated at either Thr41 and Ser45 (P41/45) or Ser33, Ser37, and Thr41 (P33/37/41) can localize to cadherin-containing cell contacts in cultured cells (Fig. 2, A, B, H, and I) as well as in situ at adherens junctions in a normal epithelial tissue that exhibits Wnt/beta-cat signaling (Fig. 2, J and K; and ; Woodhead et al., 2006). Because these phospho forms of beta-cat are only partially colocalized with cadherin and ABC (Fig. 2 and Fig. S1 A), we asked whether components of the beta-cat phosphodestruction complex also localize to cell contacts. Indeed, immunofluorescence analysis of E-cad-expressing SW480 cells reveals that axin, APC2, and GSK-3beta can be detected at cell contacts (Fig. 2, C-F). Although diffuse cytoplasmic and nuclear localizations of these proteins are also evident, as previously described (Henderson, 2000; Cong and Varmus, 2004), these data agree with earlier studies demonstrating APC2 and axin localization to cell contacts in Drosophila melanogaster and Xenopus laevis, respectively (Fagotto et al., 1999; McCartney et al., 1999; Yu et al., 1999; Jarrett et al., 2001).

Given that phospho-beta-cat and axin do not fully colocalize with E-cad at cell contacts (Fig. 2, A-C), we examined whether these proteins participate in different molecular complexes. A membrane fraction was isolated from E-cad-expressing SW480 cells (Fig. 3 A; Gottardi and Gumbiner, 2004a) and subjected to sucrose density gradient centrifugation (Fig. 3 B). Using this method, we observe that E-cad floats to the top of the gradient in a light sucrose fraction as expected for a transmembrane protein (Fig. 3 C, ii, lanes 6 and 7), whereas total beta-cat partitions equally between this E-cad fraction and more dense fractions (Fig. 3 C, iii, lanes 1-3). Consistent with evidence that phosphorylation of beta-cat occurs more efficiently in the presence of the axin complex (Dajani et al., 2003), axin, APC2, truncated APC, and GSK-3beta cosediment with phospho-beta-cat in the dense sucrose fractions (Fig. 3 C, vii-x, lanes 1-3). Together, these data suggest that the phospho-beta-cat observed at cell contacts may be localized to the membrane through an association with the axin-phosphodestruction complex rather than binding to E-cad.

It is formally possible that the sedimentation of phospho-beta-cat and components of the destruction complex to dense fractions could be caused by its large size rather than bona fide membrane association. Therefore, we sought to demonstrate that these components are membrane glycoprotein associated using the lectin Con A. As expected, we find that restoring cadherin expression in SW480 cells increases the amount of E-cad, total beta-cat and ABC that can be affinity precipitated with Con A beads (Fig. 3 F). Although axin, GSK-3beta, and APC2 (Fig. 3 F and not depicted) can be precipitated by Con A, restoring cadherin expression did not increase the abundance of these components in the glycoprotein fraction. The activity of GSK-3beta was also not altered, at least with regard to the inhibitory phosphorylation at Ser9. However, cadherin expression did increase the amount of the phospho-beta-cat recruited to a glycoprotein fraction (Fig. 3 F). Altogether, these data suggest that cadherins may be able to reduce the signaling form of beta-cat indirectly by promoting beta-cat recruitment to and/or flux through the phosphodestruction complex.

To explore this mechanism further, we assessed whether changes in cadherin-based adhesion could impact the signaling form of beta-cat in normal human epidermal keratinocytes (NHEKs), which are highly amenable to the Ca2+ switch approach to control the formation or dissolution of cadherin-based adhesions (Watt et al., 1984). To determine whether cell adhesion could impact the accumulation of cytosolic beta-cat during a Wnt-like signal, NHEKs were stimulated with the Wnt mimic LiCl in the presence of low or normal Ca2+, and beta-cat was affinity precipitated with GST-inhibitor of catenin and TCF (ICAT). ICAT is a small protein that binds to beta-cat in the same region as cadherins and can be used to isolate a cadherin-free cytosolic pool of beta-cat (Gottardi and Gumbiner, 2004b). We observe that loss of cadherin-based adhesion (low Ca2+) results in enhanced accumulation of cytosolic beta-cat (Fig. 4 A) as well as the hypophosphorylated form (Fig. 4 B, ABC). Importantly, this phenomenon occurs in the absence of changes in cadherin protein levels (Fig. 4, A and B), or the amount of beta-cat association with the cadherin (Fig. 4 C). The increase in beta-cat correlates with enhanced transcriptional activation of the beta-cat target gene Axin2 (Fig. 4 D). Cell adhesion can also limit a bona fide Wnt signal, as Wnt3a-activated alveolar epithelial cells show a density-dependent reduction in Axin2 expression (Fig. 4 F). These data indicate that cell-cell adhesion can attenuate beta-cat transcriptional activity by limiting accumulation of the signaling form of beta-cat.

Our observation that Ca2+-dependent adhesion limits the accumulation of cytosolic beta-cat independent of changes in the cadherin complex suggests that beta-cat turnover may be enhanced by adhesion. To test this directly, we measured the turnover of cytosolic beta-cat in the presence or absence of robust cell contacts in MDCK cells. The phospho forms of beta-cat localize to cell-cell contacts in these cells and exhibit fractionation characteristics similar to E-cad-expressing SW480 cells (). As with NHEKs, treating MDCK cells with LiCl generated cytosolic beta-cat, and this pool of beta-cat was followed by affinity precipitation with GST-ICAT () using the cycloheximide (CHX) chase method. Compared with low Ca2+ conditions, cytosolic beta-cat disappears more rapidly in the presence of normal Ca2+ (Fig. S3 B). This difference in turnover does not appear to be caused by the low concentrations of Ca2+ itself because cells plated at subconfluency possess more cytosolic beta-cat than confluent cells and exhibit slower turnover rates (Fig. 5 A). Critically, cadherin protein levels and the integrity of the cadherin-catenin complex are unchanged by density (Fig. 5, A and B) or low Ca2+ conditions (Fig. S3 C), as previously described (McCrea and Gumbiner, 1991). Ca2+-dependent adhesion also limits Wnt3a-dependent beta-cat accumulation (Fig. S3 D), indicating that the observed effect is not LiCl specific. Altogether, these data suggest that cadherin-based adhesion may limit the extent and/or duration of Wnt/beta-cat signals by enhancing the activity of a junction-localized phosphodestruction complex (Fig. 5 E). According to our model, when less adherent cells receive a Wnt signal such as during migrations that accompany wound healing, the ability of cadherins to activate the junction-localized degradation complex is somehow reduced, allowing the signaling pool of beta-cat to persist (Fig. 5 F). Indeed, an MDCK clone that stably expresses a TCF optimal promoter (TOP)-destabilized GFP (dGFP; Dorsky et al., 2002) shows enhanced reporter activation in cells along the colony edge (Fig. 5 C) or scratch wounds (Fig. 5 D).

An appeal of this model is that it speaks to the longstanding notion that reductions in cell-cell adhesion may release beta-cat for cell signaling. Although our data do not support a model in which beta-cat dissociates from the cadherin, we do show that there is a membrane-localized form of phospho-beta-cat that is not cadherin associated, raising the possibility that this beta-cat may be freed from the membrane as a result of changes in intercellular adhesion. ###xml 710 729 698 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Corbit et al., 2008</xref>
###xml 94 97 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 246 249 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 422 425 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 639 642 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>

It is becoming increasingly clear that a wide variety of molecular components can affect beta-cat signaling, although a simple framework for explaining these effects has not emerged. For example, a Drosophila genetic screen for enhancers of beta-cat signaling revealed that loss of proteins necessary for the establishment and maintenance of polarity such as the Fat cadherin, Stardust, and Discs large could enhance beta-cat signaling (Greaves et al., 1999). More recently, components required for primary cilium structure/function and machineries that control planar cell polarity and convergence/extension movements can antagonize beta-cat signaling at the level of Disheveled (Schwarz-Romond et al., 2002; Corbit et al., 2008). Although precise mechanisms are lacking, it is likely that many of these components ultimately affect the rate at which beta-cat is consumed by the phosphodestruction complex. Because cadherin-based adhesion is a necessary prerequisite for polarity establishment, cilium formation, and planar cell polarity signaling, it may be difficult to tease out whether cadherins promote the activity of the phosphodestruction complex directly or through these other various inputs.

###xml 337 359 337 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib34">Yamashita et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 612 620 606 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig5">Fig. 5 G</xref>
###xml 470 473 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
###xml 559 562 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">cat</span>
A model of adhesion-based modulation of Wnt signaling may also be relevant to asymmetric cell divisions that result in cells with distinct differentiative capacities, as seen in the Drosophila testes where a contact region between the germline stem cell and a cluster of somatic cells called the Hub is enriched with cadherins and APC2 (Yamashita et al., 2003). Specifically, we propose that cadherin-initiated inheritance and/or activation of a junction-localized beta-cat phosphodestruction complex may generate daughter cells with different levels of beta-cat signaling leading to distinct cellular outcomes (Fig. 5 G), suggesting an evolutionarily conserved mechanism relevant to development and stem cell biology.
