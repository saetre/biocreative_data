###begin article-title 0
The N-terminal half-domain of the long form of tRNase Z is required for the RNase 65 activity

###xml 552 557 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 562 586 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 607 610 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 660 665 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 692 697 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 865 870 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Transfer RNA (tRNA) 3' processing endoribonuclease (tRNase Z) is an enzyme responsible for the removal of a 3' trailer from pre-tRNA. There exists two types of tRNase Z: one is a short form (tRNase ZS) that consists of 300-400 amino acids, and the other is a long form (tRNase ZL) that contains 800-900 amino acids. Here we investigated whether the short and long forms have different preferences for various RNA substrates. We examined three recombinant tRNase ZSs from human, Escherichia coli and Thermotoga maritima, two recombinant tRNase ZLs from human and Saccharomyces cerevisiae, one tRNase ZL from pig liver, and the N- and C-terminal half regions of human tRNase ZL for cleavage of human micro-pre-tRNAArg and the RNase 65 activity. All tRNase ZLs cleaved the micro-pre-tRNA and showed the RNase 65 activity, while all tRNase ZSs and both half regions of human tRNase ZL failed to do so with the exception of the C-terminal half, which barely cleaved the micro-pre-tRNA. We also show that only the long forms of tRNase Z can specifically cleave a target RNA under the direction of a new type of small guide RNA, hook RNA. These results indicate that indeed tRNase ZL and tRNase ZS have different substrate specificities and that the differences are attributed to the N-terminal half-domain of tRNase ZL. Furthermore, the optimal concentrations of NaCl, MgCl2 and MnCl2 differed between tRNase ZSs and tRNase ZLs, and the Km values implied that tRNase ZLs interact with pre-tRNA substrates more strongly than tRNase ZSs.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
INTRODUCTION
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 49 54 49 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ELAC1</italic>
###xml 62 67 62 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ELAC2</italic>
###xml 172 173 172 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c1">1</xref>
###xml 174 175 174 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c6">6</xref>
###xml 419 427 419 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In vitro</italic>
###xml 629 636 629 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 725 726 725 726 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c2">2</xref>
###xml 727 728 727 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c4">4</xref>
###xml 729 730 729 730 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c6">6</xref>
###xml 731 733 731 733 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c13">13</xref>
###xml 805 824 805 824 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Thermotoga maritima</italic>
###xml 858 860 858 860 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">74</sup>
###xml 863 865 863 865 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">76</sup>
###xml 896 898 896 898 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">76</sup>
###xml 962 963 962 963 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c5">5</xref>
###xml 805 824 <span type="species:ncbi:2336">Thermotoga maritima</span>
The genome of almost every organism contains the ELAC1 and/or ELAC2 genes, the products of which have been shown to be transfer RNA (tRNA) 3' processing endoribonucleases (1-6). Although these enzymes have been called 3' tRNase or RNase Z, we renamed them tRNase Z (EC 3.1.26.11) to avoid confusion. tRNase Z is an enzyme responsible for the removal of a 3' trailer from pre-tRNA which is transcribed as a larger form. In vitro tRNA 3' processing experiments showed that in most cases the enzymes cleave pre-tRNAs immediately downstream of a discriminator nucleotide, onto which the CCA residues are added to produce mature tRNA in vivo, while in some cases additional cleavages occur 1 nt upstream, or 1 or 2 nt downstream (2-4,6-13). We have recently demonstrated that, exceptionally, the tRNase Z from Thermotoga maritima cleaves pre-tRNAs containing the 74CCA76 sequence precisely after the A76 residue to create the mature CCA 3' termini of tRNA molecules (5).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 4 9 4 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ELAC1</italic>
###xml 106 111 106 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ELAC2</italic>
###xml 202 203 202 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f1">1</xref>
###xml 371 372 371 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c1">1</xref>
###xml 431 432 431 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c1">1</xref>
###xml 500 510 500 510 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T.maritima</italic>
###xml 519 520 519 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c5">5</xref>
###xml 500 510 <span type="species:ncbi:2336">T.maritima</span>
The ELAC1 genes encode short forms of tRNase Z (tRNase ZS) that consist of 300-400 amino acids, while the ELAC2 genes produce long forms of tRNase Z (tRNase ZL) that contain 800-900 amino acids (Figure 1). The multiple protein alignment of tRNase Z orthologs has revealed that the C-terminal half region of tRNase ZL has high similarity to the whole region of tRNase ZS (1). These regions contain a well-conserved histidine motif (1), which has shown to be essential for the tRNase Z activity in the T.maritima enzyme (5).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 179 180 179 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f1">1</xref>
###xml 198 221 198 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 223 245 223 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caenorhabditis elegans</italic>
###xml 250 274 250 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 342 362 342 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana</italic>
###xml 198 221 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 223 245 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
###xml 250 274 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 332 337 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 342 362 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
Bacteria and archaea genomes contain a gene for the short form of tRNase Z only, while eukaryote genomes encode either only the long form or both the short and long forms (Figure 1). The genomes of Drosophila melanogaster, Caenorhabditis elegans and Saccharomyces cerevisiae encode only tRNase ZL. On the other hand, the genomes of human and Arabidopsis thaliana contain both tRNase ZS and tRNase ZL genes. This raises an interesting question about whether the short and long forms of tRNase Z play differential roles in the cells. It is possible that one is for nuclear tRNA processing, and that the other for the mitochondrial one. Alternatively, one of these enzymes might have an additional role for some RNA metabolism. The fact that tRNase ZL is approximately2-fold larger than tRNase ZS, and has the extra N-terminal region that is dispensable for the RNA cleaving catalytic reaction, implies that it is the long form that might have the additional role.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 107 116 107 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C.elegans</italic>
###xml 270 272 270 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c14">14</xref>
###xml 379 381 379 381 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c15">15</xref>
###xml 484 486 480 482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c15">15</xref>
###xml 107 116 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C.elegans</span>
###xml 313 318 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 401 406 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The long form of tRNase Z has the following interesting properties. RNAi knockdown experiments against the C.elegans tRNase ZL gene have shown that tRNase ZL plays a role in germline proliferation and that the down-regulation of the tRNase ZL gene results in sterility (14). On the other hand, over-expression of human tRNase ZL in HeLa cells causes a delay in G2-M progression (15). In addition, the human enzyme has been shown to interact physically with the gamma-tubulin complex (15).
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 148 149 148 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c1">1</xref>
###xml 316 317 316 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c1">1</xref>
###xml 542 543 542 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c1">1</xref>
###xml 544 546 544 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c16">16</xref>
###xml 547 549 547 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c17">17</xref>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 173 178 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The human tRNase ZL gene has been first identified as a candidate prostate cancer susceptibility gene by positional cloning and mutation screening (1). Two mutations in the human tRNase ZL gene, an insertion/frameshift (1641 ins G) and a missense change (Arg781His), segregate with prostate cancer in two pedigrees (1). The frameshift mutation results in premature termination after the miscoding of 67 residues. In addition, two common missense changes, Ser217Leu and Ala541Thr, seem to be associated with the occurrence of prostate cancer (1,16,17).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 162 164 162 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c18">18</xref>
###xml 165 167 165 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c20">20</xref>
###xml 539 546 539 546 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 558 566 558 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 568 570 568 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c21">21</xref>
###xml 571 573 571 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c24">24</xref>
Mammalian tRNase ZL can function as a four-base-recognizing RNA cutter (RNase 65) through a relatively stable complex between the enzyme and a 3'-truncated tRNA (18-20). Although little is known about the physiological role and substrate of RNase 65, it has been demonstrated that the 3'-truncated tRNA directs substrate specificity via four base pairings. In addition, the RNase 65 activity has led us to develop a technique to cleave any RNA specifically at any site by mammalian tRNase ZL under the direction of small guide RNA (sgRNA) in vivo as well as in vitro (21-24).
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 415 416 415 416 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 425 426 425 426 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 435 443 435 443 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
To elucidate possible differential roles and functions between tRNase ZL and tRNase ZS, we started by investigating whether the long and short forms have different preferences for RNA substrates. Here we demonstrate that indeed they have different substrate specificities and that the differences are attributed to the N-terminal half of tRNase ZL. Furthermore, we show that the optimal concentrations of NaCl, MgCl2 and MnCl2 for the in vitro pre-tRNA processing differ between tRNase ZSs and tRNase ZLs.
###end p 11

###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 230 246 230 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 303 304 303 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c3">3</xref>
###xml 339 349 339 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T.maritima</italic>

###xml 420 426 420 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 477 478 477 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c5">5</xref>
###xml 16 21 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 133 138 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 230 246 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 339 349 <span type="species:ncbi:2336">T.maritima</span>
###xml 354 360 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 420 426 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The recombinant human tRNase ZL and tRNase ZS, and the N-terminal (residues 1-480) and C-terminal (residues 482-826) half-domains of human tRNase ZL were over-expressed from the pTYB11 expression plasmids (New England BioLabs) in Escherichia coli and purified with chitin beads as described previously (3). The recombinant tRNase ZSs from T.maritima and E.coli were produced from the pQE expression plasmids (Qiagen) in E.coli and purified with nickel-agarose as described in (5).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 72 78 72 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 96 97 96 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c3">3</xref>
###xml 348 349 348 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g</italic>
###xml 651 652 644 645 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 666 667 659 660 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 16 21 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 72 78 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The recombinant yeast tRNase ZL was expressed from pGEX-4T-3/YKR079C in E.coli as described in (3). Harvested cell pellets were suspended in a 10 ml lysis buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.6, 500 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 1 mM dithiothreitol, 23 mM AEBSF, 100 mM EDTA, 2 mM bestatin, 0.3 mM E-64, 0.3 mM pepstatin A), sonicated and centrifuged at 1 00 000 g for 1 h. The cleared lysate was incubated with 50 mul glutathione-Sepharose beads at 4degreesC for 12 h. After exhaustive washing, the recombinant GST-tRNase ZL fusion protein bound to the beads was incubated with 1 U of thrombin in a buffer containing 50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 150 mM NaCl, 2.5 mM CaCl2 and 5 mM MgCl2 at 4degreesC for 12 h. To stop the thrombin reaction, EGTA was added to the solution, and the excised tRNase ZL in the supernatant was collected.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
RNA synthesis
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 18 21 18 21 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 31 33 31 33 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c25">25</xref>
###xml 60 63 60 63 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 72 74 72 74 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c26">26</xref>
###xml 118 121 118 121 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 123 125 123 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c20">20</xref>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 40 45 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The human pre-tRNAArg R-6TUUU (25), the human micro-pre-tRNAArg R-ATM5 (26), a 40 nt target RNA, the 3'-truncated tRNAArg (20) and a hook RNA were synthesized with T7 RNA polymerase (Takara Shuzo) from the corresponding synthetic DNA templates. The sequences of the target RNA and the hook RNA are 5'-GAAUACGCAUGCUAGCAGGUGCCCGGUGAAAGCUUGAUGU-3' and 5'-GGGCCAGCCAGGUUCGACUCCUGGCU-3', respectively. The transcription reactions were carried out under the conditions recommended by the manufacturer (Takara Shuzo), and the transcribed RNAs were purified by denaturing gel electrophoresis.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 42 45 42 45 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 71 74 71 74 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 28 33 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 51 56 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The RNA transcripts for the human pre-tRNAArg, the human micro-pre-tRNAArg, and the 40 nt target RNA were subsequently labeled with fluorescein according to the manufacturer's protocol (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech). Briefly, after the removal of the 5' phosphates of the transcribed RNAs with bacterial alkaline phosphatase (Takara Shuzo), the RNAs were phosphorylated with T4 polynucleotide kinase (Takara Shuzo) and ATPgammaS. Then a single fluorescein moiety was appended onto the 5' phosphorothioate site. The resulting fluorescein-labeled RNAs were gel purified before assays.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In vitro</italic>
In vitro RNA cleavage assays
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 72 75 72 75 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 93 96 93 96 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 250 251 249 250 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 282 290 275 283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 430 433 423 426 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 58 63 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The tRNA 3' processing assays for the fluorescein-labeled human pre-tRNAArg or micro-pre-tRNAArg (0.1 pmol) were performed with tRNase Zs of various origins in a mixture (6 mul) containing 10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5, 1.5 mM dithiothreitol and 3.2 mM MgCl2 at 50degreesC for 15 min. The in vitro cleavage assays for the fluorescein-labeled 40 nt target RNA (0.1 pmol) were carried out in the presence of the unlabeled 3'-truncated tRNAArg or hook RNA under the same conditions as above. In assays to determine optimal enzyme conditions, one of the parameters of the conditions was varied and the enzyme amounts used ranged from 10 to 80 ng. After resolution of the reaction products on a 10% polyacrylamide-8 M urea gel, the gel was analyzed with a Typhoon 9210 (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech).
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Kinetic analysis
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 36 46 35 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T.maritima</italic>
###xml 55 58 54 57 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 86 89 85 88 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 254 255 245 246 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 453 454 444 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 454 455 445 446 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</sub>
###xml 36 46 <span type="species:ncbi:2336">T.maritima</span>
###xml 72 77 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Various amounts (0.5-10 muM) of the T.maritima pre-tRNAArg(CCA) and the human pre-tRNAArg R-6TUUU were assayed at 37degreesC for 5 min using 0.1 muM of tRNase Z in a mixture (6 mul) containing 10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5, 1.5 mM dithiothreitol and 3.2 mM MgCl2. The reaction was in the linear phase during the 5 min. After resolution of the reaction products on a 10% polyacrylamide-8 M urea gel, cleavage percentages were quantitated with the Typhoon 9210. Km values were obtained from the best-fit line on a Lineweaver-Burk plot.

###begin title 45
A functional role of the N-terminal half of tRNase ZL
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 58 59 58 59 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 68 69 68 69 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 78 86 78 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 157 158 157 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f3">3</xref>
###xml 163 164 163 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f4">4</xref>
###xml 375 376 363 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c1">1</xref>
###xml 461 462 449 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f1">1</xref>
###xml 564 565 552 553 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f6">6</xref>
###xml 570 571 558 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f7">7</xref>
We found out that the optimal concentrations of NaCl, MgCl2 and MnCl2 for the in vitro pre-tRNA processing differ between tRNase ZSs and tRNase ZLs (Figures 3 and 4). With respect to the major structural difference between tRNase ZL and tRNase ZS, tRNase ZL has the extra N-terminal domain composed of approximately500 amino acids, which contains the pseudo-histidine motif (1) and has a similarity in some degree to the C-terminal domain and tRNase ZS (Figure 1). The present observation that only the long forms of tRNase Z can cleave the RNA complexes (Figures 6 and 7) implies that the N-terminal half has an additional important role in enzyme-substrate interactions.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 191 192 191 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f5">5</xref>
###xml 93 98 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 167 172 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
All of the tRNase ZSs did not cleave the micro-pre-tRNA at all, and the C-terminal domain of human tRNase ZL cleaved it inefficiently as compared with the full-length human tRNase ZL (Figure 5). This would be probably because the substrate-enzyme interaction is weakened due to the lack of the D-stem-loop and the anticodon-stem-loop in the substrate. The N-terminal half of tRNase ZL would be involved in the interaction with the substrate at least through the T-stem-loop and the acceptor stem, and would strengthen this interaction. This would allow tRNase ZL to recognize and to cleave the micro-pre-tRNA easily.
###end p 47

###xml 504 505 489 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkh774tb2">2</xref>
###xml 527 528 512 513 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 528 529 513 514 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</sub>
###xml 653 654 638 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 654 655 639 640 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</sub>
###xml 696 699 681 684 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 764 765 748 749 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 765 766 749 750 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</sub>
###xml 814 817 798 801 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 831 832 815 816 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkh774tb2">2</xref>
###xml 942 945 926 929 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arg</sup>
###xml 953 954 937 938 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkh774tb1">1</xref>
###xml 135 138 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 309 314 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 319 324 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 448 453 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 458 468 <span type="species:ncbi:2336">T.maritima</span>
###xml 710 713 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 880 883 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
This idea is supported by previous (26,27) and present kinetic experiments. The Km values for cleavages of full-length pre-tRNAsArg by pig tRNase ZL were quantitated to be approximately0.6 muM regardless of the presence of a 5' leader, and the Km values for cleavage of the full-length pre-tRNAArg R-6TUUU by human and yeast tRNase ZLs were 0.39 and 0.74 muM (Table 2). On the other hand, the Km values for cleavages of full-length pre-tRNAsArg by human and T.maritima tRNase ZSs were 1.3-2.4 muM (Table 2). About 3-fold lower Km values for tRNase ZL indicate that its N-terminal half is indeed important for stronger recognition of the substrates. The Km value for cleavage of the micro-pre-tRNAArg R-ATM5 by pig tRNase ZL was 0.57 muM, which was the same as the Km value for cleavage of the full-length pre-tRNAsArg R-UUU (Table 2). This is consistent with the observation that pig tRNase ZL has a strong activity against the micro-pre-tRNAArg (Table 1).
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 133 134 133 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f6">6</xref>
###xml 323 324 323 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f6">6</xref>
In the above context, we would also be able to explain why the RNase 65 activity was observed only with respect to tRNase ZL (Figure 6B). The discontinuous acceptor stem in the complex between the target RNA and the 3'-truncated tRNA, and the 5' extra nucleotides in the target before the 4 nt recognition sequence (Figure 6A) would disturb the enzyme-substrate interaction. These anomalies would make it difficult for the tRNase ZS to recognize the RNA complex even though the complex has the D-stem-loop and the anticodon-stem-loop. In contrast, tRNase ZL can recognize the substrate probably with the help of the N-terminal half-domain.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 245 246 245 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f7">7</xref>
This interpretation would also hold for the interaction of tRNase Z with the complex between the target RNA and the hook RNA. This complex has an additional anomaly in that it does not contain the D-stem-loop and the anticodon-stem-loop (Figure 7A). tRNase ZS would have more difficulty in substrate recognition, while, thanks to the N-terminal half, tRNase ZL would be able to endure even such a difficulty.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 105 115 105 115 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T.maritima</italic>
###xml 201 207 201 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 96 101 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 105 115 <span type="species:ncbi:2336">T.maritima</span>
###xml 201 207 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The N-terminal half-domain, when added to the reactions, did not cause stronger interactions of human or T.maritima tRNase ZS or the C-terminal half with the micro-pre-tRNA or the RNA complexes, while E.coli tRNase ZS became very weakly active against the micro-pre-tRNA in the presence of the N-terminal half. These observations suggest that the covalent connection between the N- and C-terminal half-domains is important but not necessarily prerequisite for the functional role of the N-terminal half.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 20 22 20 22 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c28">28</xref>
###xml 82 83 82 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh774f1">1</xref>
###xml 305 306 305 306 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkh774tb1">1</xref>
###xml 384 385 384 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 385 386 385 386 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</sub>
###xml 552 553 528 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkh774tb2">2</xref>
###xml 58 63 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 233 238 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 352 357 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 421 426 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 509 514 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 519 522 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
The Walker A motif (28) found in the N-terminal domain of human tRNase ZL (Figure 1A) might be involved in this N-terminal function possibly through binding to the 5' nucleotide triphosphate of pre-tRNA. The much lower activities of yeast tRNase ZL against the micro-pre-tRNA and the RNA complexes (Table 1) may be due to the lack of this motif in the yeast enzyme. Consistently, the Km value for the R-6TUUU cleavage by yeast tRNase ZL is approximately1.9- and approximately1.3-fold higher than those by the human and pig enzymes, respectively (Table 2).
###end p 52
###begin title 53
Other roles of tRNase ZL than the tRNA 3' processing
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 40 49 40 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C.elegans</italic>
###xml 150 152 150 152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c14">14</xref>
###xml 277 279 277 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c14">14</xref>
###xml 409 412 409 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ras</italic>
###xml 414 416 414 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c14">14</xref>
###xml 825 839 825 839 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus laevis</italic>
###xml 848 849 848 849 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c7">7</xref>
###xml 908 910 896 898 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">11</sup>
###xml 964 966 940 942 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sup>
###xml 40 49 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C.elegans</span>
###xml 825 839 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus laevis</span>
RNAi knockdown of the tRNase ZL gene in C.elegans causes slow growth and sterility, which results from a drastic reduction in germline proliferation (14). The nematode tRNase ZL is also needed for the germline over-proliferation phenotypes observed in three different alleles (14). In addition, a reduction of tRNase ZL suppresses the multivulva phenotype resulting from two different activating mutations in ras (14). These phenomena would be simply explained through the role of tRNase ZL in the tRNA 3' processing. Because a large number of tRNA molecules are required for early embryogenesis, if a sufficient amount of tRNase ZL that is essential for tRNA biosynthesis is not generated in the germline cells, their proliferations would be severely injured. Indeed, an enormous number of tRNase ZL molecules exist in each Xenopus laevis oocyte (7). This number has been estimated to be approximately5 x 1011 molecules, which is comparable to approximately5 x 1012 for the number of tRNA molecules.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 195 197 191 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c15">15</xref>
###xml 300 301 296 297 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 309 311 305 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c15">15</xref>
###xml 333 342 329 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C.elegans</italic>
###xml 565 573 557 565 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In vitro</italic>
###xml 333 342 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C.elegans</span>
It has been shown that in HeLa cells, tRNase ZL physically interacts with the gamma-tubulin complex, suggesting that tRNase ZL might play a role in mitosis as a modulator of centrosome assembly (15). Consistently, cells over-expressing tRNase ZL undergo a delay in cell cycle progression during the G2 phase (15). In contrast to the C.elegans case, the pre-tRNA cleaving activity of tRNase ZL would not be directly related to this phenomenon. Nothing is known about how tRNase ZL interacts with the gamma-tubulin complex and how this enzyme is involved in mitosis. In vitro binding assays for the interactions between gamma-tubulin and various recombinant tRNase ZL fragments would elucidate whether the modulator function is attributed to the N-terminal half region, which is not essential for the enzymatic activity.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 64 72 64 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 193 195 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c18">18</xref>
###xml 196 198 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c20">20</xref>
###xml 367 375 367 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 593 595 593 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c29">29</xref>
We have demonstrated that tRNase ZL shows the RNase 65 activity in vitro in the presence of 3'-truncated tRNAs, and that these RNAs exist ubiquitously in mammalian cell extracts so far tested (18-20). This suggests that tRNase ZL works as 4 nt RNA cutters in other cellular RNA metabolisms. The fact that tRNase ZL can also act as a different type of 4 nt RNA cutter in vitro under the direction of hook RNA implies that this cutter could work in the cells if there exists hook RNA. Recently, a huge number of miRNAs that are believed to work as translational regulators have been discovered (29). The length of miRNAs is approximately21-23 nt, which is similar to the length of hook RNA, suggesting that some of the miRNAs could work as hook RNAs that direct cellular RNA cleavage by endogenous tRNase ZL. Other unidentified small RNAs might also work as sgRNAs in the cells.
###end p 56
###begin title 57
tRNase ZL together with sgRNA can be used as a useful molecular biology tool
###end title 57
###begin p 58
###xml 78 86 78 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 163 165 163 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c21">21</xref>
###xml 166 168 166 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c24">24</xref>
###xml 331 332 331 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O</italic>
###xml 346 348 346 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh774c24">24</xref>
###xml 403 409 403 409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 610 611 610 611 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O</italic>
###xml 403 409 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 523 528 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 533 536 <span type="species:ncbi:9615">dog</span>
We have shown that mammalian tRNase ZL can cleave any RNA at any desired site in vitro under the direction of sgRNA, which includes 5' half tRNA and heptamer RNA (21-24). We have also examined the efficacy of this specific RNA targeting method in the living cells by introducing sgRNAs either as their expression plasmids or as 2'-O-methyl RNAs (24). The expressions of the exogenous reporter genes for E.coli chloramphenicol acetyltransferase and firefly luciferase were down-regulated by appropriately designed sgRNAs in human and dog culture cells, and the down-regulation continued for at least 48 h. A 2'-O-methyl heptamer for targeting the endogenous Bcl-2 mRNA has been also shown to work in Sarcoma 180 cells.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 360 367 360 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
Although these results have indicated that indeed appropriate sgRNAs can repress the targeted gene expressions, no direct evidence exists, yet, demonstrating that the down-regulation is due to specific mRNA cleavage by endogenous tRNase ZL and not due to simple antisense effects or other different mechanisms. We would be able to examine directly whether the in vivo down-regulation is really due to tRNase ZL cleavage by knocking-down either tRNase ZL or tRNase ZS gene expression by RNAi. From the present results which show that tRNase ZL and not tRNase ZS can cleave RNA complexes, we can predict that only when the tRNase ZL gene expression is repressed is the down-regulation by sgRNAs abolished.
###end p 59
