A Naturally Occurring Mutation in ropB Suppresses SpeB Expression and Reduces M1T1 Group A Streptococcal Systemic Virulence
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: AH VN MJW. Performed the experiments: AH RKA RK. Analyzed the data: AH RKA RK MK VN MJW. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: RKA RK. Wrote the paper: AH MK VN MJW.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 477 481 477 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 632 636 632 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 690 694 690 694 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 752 756 752 756 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 915 919 915 919 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 27 48 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">group A streptococcus</span>
###xml 50 53 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 949 955 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 1285 1288 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
Epidemiological studies of group A streptococcus (GAS) have noted an inverse relationship between SpeB expression and invasive disease. However, the role of SpeB in the course of infection is still unclear. In this study we utilize a SpeB-negative M1T1 clinical isolate, 5628, with a naturally occurring mutation in the gene encoding the regulator RopB, to elucidate the role of RopB and SpeB in systemic virulence. Allelic exchange mutagenesis was used to replace the mutated ropB allele in 5628 with the intact allele from the well characterized isolate 5448. The inverse allelic exchange was also performed to replace the intact ropB in 5448 with the mutated allele from 5628. An intact ropB was found to be essential for SpeB expression. While the ropB mutation was shown to have no effect on hemolysis of RBC's, extracellular DNase activity or survival in the presence of neutrophils, strains with the mutated ropB allele were less virulent in murine systemic models of infection. An isogenic SpeB knockout strain containing an intact RopB showed similarly reduced virulence. Microarray analysis found genes of the SpeB operon to be the primary target of RopB regulation. These data show that an intact RopB and efficient SpeB production are necessary for systemic infection with GAS.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 0 22 0 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Streptococcus pyogenes</italic>
###xml 142 145 142 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Carapetis1">[1]</xref>

###xml 461 464 461 464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Chatellier1">[3]</xref>
###xml 465 468 465 468 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Walker1">[5]</xref>
###xml 623 626 623 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Demers1">[6]</xref>
###xml 628 631 628 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Cleary1">[7]</xref>
###xml 0 22 <span type="species:ncbi:1314">Streptococcus pyogenes</span>
###xml 24 45 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">group A streptococcus</span>
###xml 47 50 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 72 77 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 163 166 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 425 428 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 485 488 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
Streptococcus pyogenes (group A streptococcus; GAS) is a Gram-positive, human-specific pathogen responsible for over 500,000 deaths each year [1]. Severe invasive GAS infections such as necrotizing fasciitis account for approximately 30% of these deaths, and the incidence of such acute conditions has been on the rise since the mid 1980's [2]. This resurgence has been paralleled by the emergence of a globally disseminated GAS cone belonging to serotype M1T1 [3]-[5]. While the M1T1 GAS has become the most common cause of streptococcal pharyngitis, this clone is also overrepresented in cases of severe invasive disease [6], [7].
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 185 188 185 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Kansal1">[8]</xref>
###xml 316 319 316 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Sumby1">[9]</xref>
###xml 474 478 474 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Musser1">[10]</xref>
###xml 641 645 641 645 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Cunningham1">[11]</xref>
###xml 646 650 646 650 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Nyberg1">[13]</xref>
###xml 812 816 812 816 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Raeder1">[14]</xref>
###xml 818 822 818 822 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Ringdahl1">[15]</xref>
###xml 846 850 846 850 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Kansal2">[16]</xref>
###xml 852 856 852 856 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Aziz1">[17]</xref>
###xml 907 911 907 911 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Rezcallah1">[18]</xref>
###xml 938 942 938 942 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Aziz1">[17]</xref>
###xml 1180 1187 1180 1187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1248 1252 1248 1252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Svensson1">[19]</xref>
###xml 1253 1257 1253 1257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Lukomski1">[21]</xref>
###xml 757 760 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
Studies of M1T1 clinical isolates from invasive disease cases have revealed an inverse relationship between expression of the extracellular cysteine protease SpeB and clinical severity [8]. The existence of a SpeB-negative invasive phenotype has been hypothesized that results from mutations in the regulator covR/S [9]. SpeB is a secreted cysteine protease initially expressed as 40 kDa zymogen which is then converted to the 28 kDa active form by autocatalytic processing [10]. SpeB is known to cleave numerous host proteins including components of the extracellular matrix, cytokine precursors, immunoglobulins and antimicrobial peptides [11]-[13], which could interfere with host immune functions. However, SpeB has also been shown to cleave a range of GAS proteins such as the fibrinogen-binding M1 protein [14], [15], various superantigens [16], [17], the secreted plasminogen activator streptokinase [18] as well as the DNase Sda1 [17], and thus possibly interfere with the proven virulence functions of these bacterial factors. The precise role(s) of SpeB throughout the course of infection are undoubtedly complex, and not surprisingly, different studies using different in vivo animal models have produced seemingly contradictory results [19]-[21].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 118 122 118 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Chaussee1">[22]</xref>
###xml 320 324 320 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">speB</italic>
###xml 324 328 324 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Chaussee2">[23]</xref>
###xml 330 334 330 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Neely1">[24]</xref>
###xml 565 569 565 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Chaussee3">[25]</xref>
###xml 571 575 571 575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Dmitriev1">[26]</xref>
###xml 618 622 618 622 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Chaussee3">[25]</xref>
###xml 624 628 624 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Pulliainen1">[27]</xref>
###xml 753 757 753 757 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Dmitriev1">[26]</xref>
###xml 759 763 759 763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Chaussee4">[28]</xref>
###xml 765 769 765 769 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Dmitriev2">[29]</xref>
###xml 1012 1016 1012 1016 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Neely2">[30]</xref>
###xml 1172 1176 1172 1176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Pulliainen1">[27]</xref>
###xml 154 157 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 186 189 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 370 373 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 467 470 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 886 895 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 943 946 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 1046 1052 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 1103 1106 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
In this study we examined the effect of a natural mutation in the gene encoding the regulator RopB (also known as Rgg [22]) identified in a SpeB-negative GAS clinical isolate. RopB is a GAS transcriptional regulator that has been shown to be essential for expression of SpeB and binds directly to the promoter region of speB[23], [24]. In studies performed in different GAS serotype strains, RopB has variably been suggested to be involved in the regulation of other GAS genes including those associated with metabolism of non-glucose carbohydrates and amino acids [25], [26], response to thermal and oxidative stress [25], [27] and the expression of virulence factors including DNases (MF-1 and MF-3) and hemolysins (streptolysin S and streptolysin O) [26], [28], [29]. Subsequent investigations into the effect of RopB on virulence have yielded differing results. A study utilizing a zebrafish intramuscular infection model with serotype M5 GAS showed that inactivation of RopB resulted in decreased virulence [30], whereas a study utilizing a murine intraperitoneal infection model with serotype M49 GAS showed that inactivation of RopB resulted in increased virulence [27].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 219 223 219 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 411 415 411 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Chaussee2">[23]</xref>
###xml 417 421 417 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Dmitriev1">[26]</xref>
###xml 498 504 498 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 504 508 504 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Dmitriev2">[29]</xref>
###xml 581 585 581 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 593 596 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
While such global differences in virulence effects could in part result from the differing animal models used, it may also reflect strain-specific variation in the RopB regulon. For example, separate studies have shown ropB mutation to have either no effect on hemolysis and DNase activity or, alternatively, to increase expression of hemolysin and DNase-encoding genes and the associated phenotypic activities [23], [26]. This strain-specific variation is highlighted in a recent work by Dmitriev et al.[29] that shows inter- and intra-serotypic variation in the transcriptome of ropB mutant GAS, with only members of the SpeB operon being commonly regulated in all strains tested.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 310 314 310 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 183 186 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 234 239 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
It is in the light of the current uncertainty surrounding RopB and its role in virulence that we sought to investigate the role of this transcriptional regulator in the serotype M1T1 GAS background that is the leading agent of severe human infection. This analysis begins with a naturally-occuring mutation in ropB identified in one such strain.
###end p 8

Materials and Methods
###end title 9
###begin title 10
Bacterial strains, media and growth conditions
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 97 101 97 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Kazmi1">[31]</xref>
###xml 126 130 126 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">speA</italic>
###xml 398 401 398 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Chatellier1">[3]</xref>
###xml 519 535 519 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 705 712 697 704 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 9 12 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 177 185 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 278 281 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 403 406 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 461 466 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 519 535 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 679 682 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 705 712 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The M1T1 GAS clinical isolates, 5448 and 5628, used in this study have been previously described [31]. Both 5448 and 5628 are speA-positive M1T1 strains that were isolated from patients with STSS that were recruited through an ongoing population-based surveillance for invasive GAS infections in Ontario, Canada. Both strains were determined to be derived from the same clone as detailed elsewhere [3]. GAS strains were grown in Todd-Hewitt broth containing 1% yeast extract (THY), or on Todd-Hewitt agar plates (THA). Escherichia coli were grown in Luria-Bertani broth (LB) or on Luria-Bertani agar (LA). For antibiotic selection, erythromycin (Erm) was used at 5 microg/ml for GAS and 500 microg/ml for E. coli.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 0 28 0 28 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Allelic exchange mutagenesis</bold>
###xml 79 83 79 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Jeng1">[32]</xref>
###xml 89 93 89 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 211 256 211 256 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="gene">5&#8242;-CAG GAT CCC TCA TTT CAG TTG ACA AGA AAC-3&#8242;</named-content>
###xml 275 320 275 320 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="gene">5&#8242;-CGC TCT AGA TAC CAA AAG GCT AGA CCT CTG-3&#8242;</named-content>
###xml 595 596 595 596 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">r</sup>
###xml 937 938 919 920 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">s</sup>
###xml 1003 1007 985 989 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 1130 1134 1112 1116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 1199 1203 1181 1185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 506 509 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 571 574 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
Allelic exchange mutagenesis was performed essentially as previously described [32]. The ropB allele plus upstream and downstream flanking regions was amplified from 5448 or 5628 using the primers RopB-F-BamHI (5'-CAG GAT CCC TCA TTT CAG TTG ACA AGA AAC-3') and RopB-R-XbaI (5'-CGC TCT AGA TAC CAA AAG GCT AGA CCT CTG-3'). The PCR products and temperature sensitive vector pHY304 were ligated using T4 ligase to create the plasmids pHY5448RopB and pHY5628RopB. The plasmid pHY5628RopB was transformed into GAS strain 5448 and the plasmid pHY5448RopB was transformed into GAS strain 5628, and Ermr transformants were grown at the permissive temperature for plasmid replication (30degreesC). Single-crossover chromosomal insertions were selected by shifting to the nonpermissive temperature (37degreesC) while maintaining Erm selection. Single crossover colonies were then grown in the absence of antibiotic selection at 30degreesC and Erms colonies were then screened for the presence of the appropriate ropB allele using DNA sequence analysis. Confirmed allelic exchange mutants were designated 5448R- (5448 containing the mutant ropB allele from 5628) and 5628R+ (5628 containing the wildtype (WT) ropB allele from 5448).
###end p 12
###begin title 13
SpeB activity assays
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 72 78 72 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 78 82 78 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Collin1">[33]</xref>
###xml 236 237 224 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 770 771 730 731 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 792 795 752 755 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">366</sub>
###xml 84 87 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
Cysteine protease activity assays were performed as described by Collin et al.[33]. GAS strains were grown overnight at 37degreesC. Cultures were then diluted 1:50 and grown for 17 h at 37degreesC. The cultures were centrifuged at 3200xg, and the supernatants sterile-filtered through 0.2 microm syringe-driven filters (Whatman). 200 microl of filtered supernatant was mixed with 200 microl of activation buffer (1 mM EDTA, 20 mM DTT in 0.1 M sodium acetate buffer, pH 5.0) and incubated for 30 min at 40degreesC. 400 microl of 2% (w/v) azocasein in activation buffer was then added and incubated for a further 1 h at 40degreesC. Trichloro-acetic acid was then added to a final concentration of 15% (w/v) and thoroughly mixed. The mixture was then centrifuged at 15,000xg for 5 min and the OD366 of the supernatant was then measured to indicate cleavage of the azocasein by SpeB.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
SpeB Western blot
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 129 130 129 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 586 592 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 704 708 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 714 720 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
For Western blot analysis, bacterial cultures were grown to late stationary phase (17 h) and pelleted by centrifugation at 3,200xg for 10 min. The supernatants were sterile-filtered through 0.22 microm syringe-driven filters (Millipore). Supernatants were diluted 1:8 and 10 microl of each sample was run on a 10% Bis-Tris Gel with MOPS running buffer (Invitrogen). Proteins were then transferred to a Nitrocellulose membrane (Invitrogen) by use of a Trans-Blot SD semi-dry transfer cell (BioRad) for 1 h at 20 V. The presence of SpeB in culture supernatants was detected using primary rabbit anti-SpeB diluted 1:1,000 for 1.5 h. Following subsequent washing, the membrane was incubated with a secondary goat anti-rabbit-HRP conjugate diluted 1:10,000 for 1 h. SuperSignal West Pico Chemiluminescent Substrate (Thermo Scientific) was used to expose autoradiography film as per the manufacturer's instructions.
###end p 16

###xml 200 203 200 203 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">600</sub>
###xml 248 249 248 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 538 539 514 515 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 19 24 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Fresh, heparinized human blood from healthy volunteers was washed twice with sterile PBS and resuspended to a final concentration of 2% (v/v) in PBS. Bacterial cultures were grown to mid-log phase (OD600 = 0.4), pelleted by centrifugation at 3,200xg and the supernatants sterile-filtered through 0.22 microm syringe-driven filters (Millipore). 100 microl of blood solution was mixed with 100 microl of serially diluted supernatant. The plates were incubated for 1 h at 37degreesC, then 1 h at 4degreesC. Following centrifugation at 1,500xg for 10 min, 100 microl was transferred into a fresh 96-well flat bottom plate and the absorbance at 405 nm was recorded.

###begin title 19
DNase activity assays
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 52 56 52 56 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Buchanan1">[34]</xref>
###xml 58 62 58 62 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Aziz2">[35]</xref>
###xml 116 119 116 119 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">600</sub>
###xml 185 186 185 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 321 322 305 306 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 333 334 317 318 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 73 76 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 251 255 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
DNase assays were performed as previously described [34], [35]. Briefly, GAS strains were grown to mid-log phase (OD600 = 0.4), and the supernatants collected by centrifugation at 3200xg. 2.5 microl of bacterial supernatant was added to 7.5 microl of calf thymus DNA (1 microg/ml) and 40 microl of DNase buffer (3 mM MgCl2, 3 mM CaCl2, 300 mM Tris; pH 7.4). The reaction was incubated for 5 min at 37degreesC and then stopped by the addition of 12.5 microl 0.33 M EDTA (pH 7.3). The relative DNA degradation was then compared by gel electrophoresis on a 1% agarose gel.

###begin title 21
Neutrophil killing assays
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 194 195 194 195 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5</sup>
###xml 216 217 216 217 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sup>
###xml 395 396 391 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 443 444 433 434 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 507 508 497 498 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 266 269 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
Human neutrophils were isolated from venous blood of healthy volunteers using the PolyMorphPrep kit as per the manufacturer's instructions (Axis-Shield, Norway). In 96-well plates, we mixed 2x105 neutrophils and 2x104 colony forming units (cfu) of logarithmic phase GAS in RPMI containing 2% heat-inactivated autologous plasma in a total volume of 200 microl. The plates were centrifuged at 500xg for 5 min and incubated at 37degreesC in 5% CO2 for 30 min. The neutrophils were then hypotonically lysed in H2O, serially diluted and plated on THA. The plates were incubated overnight at 37degreesC and cfu enumerated. Control wells containing bacteria but no neutrophils were used to determine survival. Percentage survival was calculated as [cfu/ml experimental well]/[cfu/ml control well]x100%.

###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 48 51 48 51 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">600</sub>

GAS strain 5628R+ was grown to mid-log phase (OD600 = 0.4), pelleted by centrifugation at 3,200xg and washed twice with sterile PBS. ###begin title 25
###xml 29 36 29 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
Tissue cage implantation and in vivo bacterial growth
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 10 17 10 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 128 132 128 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Kazmi1">[31]</xref>
###xml 150 151 150 151 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">8</sup>
###xml 91 97 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 287 291 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To obtain in vivo-derived RNA for microarray analysis, a previously described subcutaneous murine Teflon chamber model was used [31]. Approximately 108 cfu of bacteria were injected in sterile Teflon chambers that had been surgically inserted under the skin of age-matched female BALB/c mice. After 24 h, bacteria were collected, tested for purity and phenotypic homogeneity on blood agar plates and Columbia-casein agar plates, respectively. Only pure isolates with homogeneous protease activity (either positive or negative) were further selected for RNA extraction. Selected isolates recovered from Teflon chambers were centrifuged, and their pellets were washed twice in PBS, sheared with beads (QBiogene), then processed for RNA extraction according to the RNEasey protocol (Qiagen).
###end p 26
###begin title 27
Expression microarrays
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 251 254 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
For transcriptome analysis, we used oligomer-based microarrays printed in the Molecular Resource Center at the University of Tennessee Health Science Center. Each array consists of duplicates of 2,328 oligomers (70-mers) that represent all ORFs in M1 GAS, strain SF370 (GenBank accession # NC_002737) in addition to oligomers representing ORFs from prophages in strains MGAS8232 (GenBank accession# NC_003485) and MGAS315 (GenBank accession # NC_004070). The arrays also contained positive and negative controls of streptococcal ribosomal DNA and alien DNA (Stratagene), respectively.
###end p 28
###begin title 29
cDNA preparation and microarray hybridization
###end title 29
###begin p 30
Bacterial RNA was treated with DNase Turbo (Ambion) for 1 h to remove any genomic DNA contamination, then converted it to dendrimer-labeled cDNA using the 3DNA Array 900TM kits (Genisphere, ) following the manufacturer's protocol. Equal amounts of dendrimer-labeled cDNA from different pairs of isolates were mixed, applied to the glass microarrays, and incubated for 16 h. After this first hybridization, the arrays were washed, labeled with a mixture of Alexa Fluor 546 and Alexa Fluor 647 (Genisphere), incubated for 3 h, washed again, then scanned bv GenePix 4000B scanner (Axon Instruments, Inc.) We followed a cyclic design that allowed us to compare every condition to each other at least twice, and guaranteed dye swapping to eliminate effects of non-specific binding.
###end p 30
###begin title 31
Analysis of microarray data
###end title 31
###begin p 32
To analyze the microarray data, GenePixPro 4.0 software (Axon Instruments, Inc.) was used for image processing, fluorescent normalization and spot finding, then GeneSpring GX 7.3.1 (Agilent Technologies) was used for normalization, statistical analysis, clustering analysis and gene-list generation. All primary microarray data were submitted to the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) in accordance with MIAME standards (GEO accession # GSE13656).
###end p 32

###begin title 47
SpeB-positive bacteria revert to a SpeB-negative phenotype on subcutaneous infection
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 427 433 427 433 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">covR/S</italic>

###xml 814 819 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
C57BL6/J mice were subcutaneously infected with the SpeB-positive strain 5628R+. Three days post-infection, bacteria were recovered from the lesion and screened for SpeB status using the skim-milk agar method. While the inoculum was 100% SpeB-positive, bacterial populations recovered from the lesions of individual mice were found to be 2%, 4%, 6%, 8%, and 24% SpeB-negative. Five representative colonies were picked, and the covR/S locus was sequenced in each of them. All colonies sequenced were found to have substitution mutations in covR/S resulting in truncation of CovS. This finding reveals that when SpeB-negative colonies are selected for in vivo, this selection is predominantly a phenomenon associated with CovR/S inactivation. The ropB mutation of strain 5628 was not recapitulated on passage in the mouse subcutaneous infection model.

###xml 151 155 151 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 465 471 465 471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004102-g003">Fig. 3</xref>
###xml 594 598 594 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 85 88 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
Microarray analysis was performed on in vivo-derived RNA from the well characterized GAS strain 5448 and its derivative 5448R-, containing the mutated ropB allele from 5628. Fourty-seven genes were found to be down-regulated and 52 genes were found to be up-regulated in 5448R- greater than 2-fold (P<0.05). The most strongly down-regulated genes in 5448R- are members of the SpeB operon, as would be expected from the lack of SpeB expression and activity in 5628 (Fig. 3). In addition to the SpeB operon, genes of the streptolysin S operon were also found to be strongly down-regulated in the ropB mutant strain. Apart from the gene encoding the superantigen SmeZ, there was an absence of virulence-related genes found to be strongly up-regulated in the strain 5448R-, which expressed the truncated RopB.

###begin title 53
RopB is required for virulence in systemic infection
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 27 31 27 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 276 285 276 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004102-g004">Figure 4A</xref>
###xml 661 665 657 661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 982 989 978 985 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004102-g004">Fig. 4B</xref>
###xml 64 68 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 150 153 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 797 800 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
To determine the effect of ropB mutation on virulence, C57BL6/J mice were subjected to intraperitoneal infection with mid-logarithmic phase bacteria. GAS strain 5628R+ expressing a full length RopB showed increased virulence compared to the strain 5628 with a truncated RopB (Figure 4A). Strain 5628RDeltaSpeB containing a full-length RopB but lacking SpeB also showed reduced virulence, suggesting that the reduced virulence of 5628 may primarily result from the lack of SpeB expression in this strain. Virulence was also examined in an intravenous model of systemic infection. The well characterized strain 5448 and its isogenic mutant 5448R-, containing the ropB allele from 5628, were further included to examine the effect of this allelic variation in a well-characterized virulent strain of GAS. The two strains containing a truncated RopB (5628 and 5448R-) demonstrated similarly reduced virulence compared to the two strains containing a full-length RopB (5628R+ and 5448) (Fig. 4B), confirming that inactivation of RopB results in decreased virulence in systemic infection.

###begin title 57
Discussion
###end title 57
###begin p 58
###xml 221 225 221 225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Svensson1">[19]</xref>
###xml 226 230 226 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Lukomski1">[21]</xref>
###xml 422 425 422 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Kansal1">[8]</xref>
###xml 623 627 623 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Sumby2">[37]</xref>
###xml 629 633 629 633 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Walker2">[38]</xref>
###xml 805 811 805 811 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">covR/S</italic>
###xml 867 871 867 871 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 916 923 916 923 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 99 102 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 159 162 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 316 319 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 610 616 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 791 794 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
The cumulative contribution of the secreted cysteine protease SpeB to the pathogenesis of invasive GAS infection is at present unclear, and studies in various GAS serotypes and animal models have produced varying results [19]-[21]. In the globally-disseminated clonal M1T1 serotype associated with epidemic invasive GAS infection, an inverse relationship has been found between SpeB activity and clinical disease severity [8]. Recently, we and others have found a connection between inactivation of SpeB through mutation in the two-component regulatory system covR/S and development of invasive disease in the murine model [37], [38]. In this study, we have investigated a second mechanism of inactivation of SpeB, namely through truncation of the regulator RopB. We have shown that in M1T1 GAS, when the covR/S locus is intact and DNase Sda1 activity is unaffected, ropB point mutation results in reduced virulence in vivo, despite inactivation of SpeB activity.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 177 181 177 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Chaussee2">[23]</xref>
###xml 183 187 183 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Neely1">[24]</xref>
###xml 241 245 241 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Chaussee3">[25]</xref>
###xml 246 250 246 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Dmitriev2">[29]</xref>
###xml 306 310 306 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 583 587 583 587 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RopB</italic>
###xml 690 696 690 696 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">covR/S</italic>
###xml 810 813 810 813 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">sic</italic>
###xml 815 818 815 818 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ska</italic>
###xml 820 823 820 823 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">slo</italic>
###xml 825 829 825 829 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">speA</italic>
###xml 831 835 831 835 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">speJ</italic>
###xml 837 841 837 841 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">scpC</italic>
###xml 883 887 883 887 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Sumby2">[37]</xref>
###xml 921 925 921 925 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 219 222 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
The SpeB-negative, serotype M1T1 clinical isolate 5628 was used to investigate the role of RopB in virulence. RopB has previously been shown to be necessary for SpeB production [23], [24] as well as regulation of other GAS virulence factors [25]-[29]. The M1T1 strain 5628 contains a point mutation in the ropB gene that results in a truncation of the last 110 amino acids of the 280 amino acid protein. Restoration of full-length RopB in 5628R+ resulted in gaining SpeB expression, but did not result in changes in hemolysis, DNase activity or resistance to killing by neutrophils. RopB mutation in this particular strain does not result in the kind of global phenotypic change found with covR/S mutation where differential expression was observed of multiple genes encoding virulence determinants, including sic, ska, slo, speA, speJ, scpC and the hyaluronic acid synthesis operon [37] which were all unaffected in the ropB mutant examined in this work.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 151 155 151 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Aziz1">[17]</xref>
###xml 257 263 257 263 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">covR/S</italic>
###xml 263 267 263 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Walker2">[38]</xref>
###xml 563 567 563 567 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Sumby2">[37]</xref>
###xml 947 951 947 951 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Cole1">[42]</xref>
###xml 1194 1200 1194 1200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">covR/S</italic>
###xml 1228 1234 1228 1234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">covR/S</italic>
###xml 1312 1316 1312 1316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 1354 1361 1354 1361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1397 1401 1397 1401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 1461 1467 1461 1467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">covR/S</italic>
###xml 146 150 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 844 847 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
###xml 1299 1302 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
Recently, we have demonstrated that WT, SpeB-positive bacteria undergo a phase-shift to a SpeB-negative phenotype after subcutaneous infection of mice [17]. This phase switch was observed to be the result of mutations in the two-component regulatory system covR/S[38]. CovR/S is a global regulator, whose inactivation is linked to an invasive phenotype and involves up-regulation of many genes encoding virulence factors such as streptodornase, streptokinase, streptolysin O, streptococcal inhibitor of complement and the hyaluronic acid capsule synthesis operon [37]. Of the down-regulated genes in CovR/S mutant strains, SpeB would appear to be one of the most important due to its ability to degrade many host and self-proteins. It is the loss of SpeB expression in these mutant bacteria that is hypothesized to allow the invasive spread of GAS by sparing from SpeB degradation self-proteins involved in plasminogen accumulation and activation [42]. In this study we found that SpeB-positive bacteria with a restored RopB reverted to a SpeB-negative phenotype on subcutaneous infection. Furthermore, the switch to a SpeB-negative phenotype appeared to occur exclusively through mutations in covR/S. This result suggests that covR/S mutations are the predominant method of phenotypic switching in GAS and that ropB mutation is not readily selected for in vivo. This leads to the conclusion that ropB mutation in 5628 does not represent a similar mechanism to covR/S mutation and may (a) be an incidental occurrence or (b) arise secondary to different selection pressures than those associated with the shift to invasive infection.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 40 47 40 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 103 111 103 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 259 266 259 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 488 496 488 496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 501 508 501 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
Microarray analysis was conducted using in vivo-derived RNA, since the transcriptome of bacteria grown in vitro may differ greatly from the transcriptome found during the course of infection. Implantation of tissue cages allows for bacteria to be grown in an in vivo environment for 24 hours prior to recovery and RNA extraction. The microarray data showed members of the SpeB operon to be the most strongly down-regulated genes following inactivation of RopB. This finding supported the in vitro and in vivo data that suggested SpeB is the main target of RopB regulation in this strain.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 413 420 413 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 985 991 981 987 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">covR/S</italic>
###xml 1128 1134 1124 1130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">covR/S</italic>
###xml 1155 1159 1151 1155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Sumby2">[37]</xref>
###xml 1176 1180 1172 1176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ropB</italic>
###xml 1399 1402 1395 1398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Walker1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1404 1408 1400 1404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-Cole1">[42]</xref>
###xml 1410 1414 1406 1410 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004102-McArthur1">[43]</xref>
###xml 1323 1326 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
Virulence studies utilizing systemic models of infection showed that a full-length RopB was required for virulence. The clinical isolate 5628 expressing a truncated RopB, as well as the allelic exchange mutant 5448R- also expressing the truncated RopB from 5628, showed reduced virulence compared to strains containing a full-length RopB. These data illustrate the necessity for an intact RopB for full virulence in vivo. In addition, the strain 5628RDeltaSpeB expressing an intact RopB but lacking SpeB showed similarly reduced virulence to the strain with a truncated RopB. While the down-regulation of the streptolysin S operon may contribute to the reduced virulence, this finding implies that the loss of SpeB in this strain is the main cause of the lack of virulence exhibited. This finding leads to the conclusion that inactivation of SpeB alone is not sufficient to initiate invasive disease. Moreover, these data suggest that SpeB loss, only in the context of mutation of the covR/S regulatory circuit, promotes invasion. Of note, the additional virulence factors streptokinase and M1 protein, are both up-regulated in covR/S mutant M1T1 strains [37], but not in the ropB mutant under investigation in this study. Streptokinase and M1 protein are believed to play a role in the accumulation of host plasmin at the GAS cell surface, a process which is thought to accentuate invasive disease [5], [42], [43].
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 74 77 <span type="species:ncbi:36470">GAS</span>
Together these data provide evidence of RopB's role in virulence. In M1T1 GAS, an intact RopB and efficient SpeB production are necessary for systemic infection.
