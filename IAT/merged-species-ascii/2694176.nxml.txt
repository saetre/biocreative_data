###xml 69 77 65 69 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2 </sub>
Interplay between the heterotrimeric G-protein subunits Galphaq and Galphai2 sets the threshold for chemotaxis and TCR activation
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Background
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 269 276 269 272 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 281 289 277 281 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2 </sub>
###xml 393 400 385 388 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 518 525 506 509 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
TCR and CXCR4-mediated signaling appears to be reciprocally regulated pathways. TCR activation dampens the chemotactic response towards the CXCR4 ligand CXCL12, while T cells exposed to CXCL12 are less prone to subsequent TCR-activation. The heterotrimeric G proteins Galphaq and Galphai2 have been implicated in CXCR4-signaling and we have recently also reported the possible involvement of Galphaq in TCR-dependent activation of Lck (Ngai et al., Eur. J. Immunol., 2008, 38: 32083218). Here we examined the role of Galphaq in migration and TCR activation.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Results
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 179 186 179 182 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 294 302 290 294 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2 </sub>
Pre-treatment of T cells with CXCL12 led to significantly reduced Lck Y394 phosphorylation upon TCR triggering indicating heterologous desensitization. We show that knockdown of Galphaq significantly enhanced basal migration in T cells and reduced CXCL12-induced SHP-1 phosphorylation whereas Galphai2 knockdown inhibited CXCL12-induced migration.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Conclusion
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 23 31 23 27 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2 </sub>
###xml 92 99 88 91 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 231 238 223 226 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
Our data suggest that Galphai2 confers migration signals in the presence of CXCL12 whereas Galphaq exerts a tonic inhibition on both basal and stimulated migrational responses. This is compatible with the notion that the level of Galphaq activation contributes to determining the commitment of the T cell either to migration or activation through the TCR.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Background
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 100 101 100 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 331 332 323 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 333 334 325 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 450 451 442 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 452 453 444 445 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 337 341 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
Chemokines dictate the migration and positioning of leukocytes at steady-state and in inflammation [1]. The chemokine CXCL12, also known as stromal cell-derived factor-1alpha (SDF-1alpha), is a member of the CXC chemokine subfamily and serves as the only known ligand for the chemokine receptor CXCR4 expressed on most leukocytes [1-3]. Mice lacking CXCL12 or CXCR4 die perinatally of cardiovascular, neurologic, vascular, and hematopoietic defects [4,5]
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 112 119 112 115 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i </sub>
###xml 124 130 120 122 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q</sub>
###xml 141 142 133 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 143 144 135 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 163 169 155 157 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i</sub>
###xml 328 334 316 318 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i</sub>
###xml 424 430 408 410 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i</sub>
###xml 440 448 420 424 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2 </sub>
###xml 453 461 429 433 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i3 </sub>
###xml 500 501 472 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 505 512 477 480 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i3</sub>
###xml 512 516 480 484 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">-/- </sup>
###xml 544 546 512 514 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 557 564 525 528 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2</sub>
###xml 564 568 528 532 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">-/- </sup>
###xml 671 672 635 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 689 696 653 656 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 799 800 759 760 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 516 520 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 568 572 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The CXCR4 receptor is a G-protein coupled receptor (GPCR) and couples to heterotrimeric G-proteins of both the Galphai and Galphaq-families [6-8]. Members of the Galphai-family of G-proteins are pertussis toxin sensitive and pre-treatment of cells with pertussis toxin severely attenuates chemotaxis in response to chemokines. Galphai-proteins are therefore thought to be essential for chemokine-mediated migration. Of the Galphai-family, Galphai2 and Galphai3 are strongly expressed by lymphocytes [9]. Galphai3-/- mice are without phenotype [10] whereas Galphai2-/- mice display dysfunctional T cell functions as well as defective chemotaxis in response to chemokines [6]. In contrast, Galphaq has been shown to be required for chemotaxis of dendritic cells and granulocytes, but not for T cells [7].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 143 145 143 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 146 148 146 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 448 450 448 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 608 614 608 610 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q</sub>
###xml 770 771 766 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 859 861 855 857 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
Stimulation of the T cell receptor (TCR), the B cell receptor, or the Ly49D activating receptor all reduce lymphocyte migration toward CXCL12 [11-14] suggesting that cells dedicated to proliferation/differentiation are less sensitive towards chemokines. The opposite is also true, as cells exposed to CXCL12 show less phosphorylation of ZAP-70 and LAT following subsequent TCR activation indicating reciprocal regulation between CXCR4 and the TCR [13]. In addition, recruitment of chemokine receptors into the immunological synapse has been shown to stabilize T cell-APC interaction through activation of a Galphaq-dependent pathway. This renders the cell less responsive to chemotactic gradients and gives higher T cell proliferative responses and cytokine production [8]. Recently, the TCR and CXCR4 were shown to closely associate upon CXCL12-stimulation [15], and it was suggested that CXCR4 signaling occurs through the immunoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motif (ITAM)-domain present in the CD3zeta chain. Collectively, these results point to a direct crosstalk between the signaling machineries utilized by the TCR and CXCR4.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 29 36 29 32 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 111 113 107 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 131 138 127 130 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 292 299 284 287 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 610 617 598 601 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 737 743 721 723 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q</sub>
###xml 766 773 746 749 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
We have recently shown that Galphaq knockdown in T cells inhibits the activation of the Src family kinase Lck [16]. The effect of Galphaq on Lck activation might be conferred by a direct interaction and/or by affecting the threshold for TCR-triggering. We demonstrate here that knockdown of Galphaq significantly enhances basal T cell migration while inhibiting TCR-induced Lck activation. Pre-treating cells with CXCL12 prior to stimulation with anti-CD3 leads to a similar inhibition of Lck activation indicating that cells directed to migrate are less responsive to TCR triggering. The signals conveyed by Galphaq appear to be mediated through a SHP-1 pathway. Our data suggest that optimal TCR activation requires signaling through Galphaq, and that removal of Galphaq locks the cell in migration modus making the cell less responsive to TCR signaling
###end p 11

###begin title 17
Cell culture and transfections
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 244 246 244 246 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6 </sup>
###xml 51 55 <span type="species:ncbi:10633?0.9966199903428296">SV40</span>
The Jurkat TAg T cell line stably transfected with SV40 large T antigen was kept in logarithmic growth in RPMI-1640 (Invitrogen) supplemented with 10% FCS, 1% Penicillin-Streptomycin, 1% Non-essential amino acids and 1% Sodium Pyruvate. 20 x 106 cells were washed and resuspended in 400 mul OptiMEM (Invitrogen), mixed with cDNA or siRNA and electroporated in 0.4 cm electroporation cuvettes (Bio-Rad) at 250 V/cm and 975 muF. Cells were harvested 48 hours post transfection.

###begin title 19
Cell stimulation and lysis
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 46 47 46 47 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 352 353 345 346 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 355 356 348 349 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
Cells were resuspended in RPMI 1640 to 50 x 106/ml and pre-incubated at 37degreesC for 5 minutes. Cells were then stimulated with 100 ng/ml CXCL12 or 1.5 mug/ml OKT3 for the indicated time points and the reaction was subsequently stopped by lysis in ice cold lysis buffer (50 mM HEPES, 100 mM NaCl, 5 mM EDTA, 50 mM NaF, 10 mM Na-pyrophosphate, 1 mM Na3VO4, 1 mM PMSF, 1.0% Triton X-100). Where indicated, cells were pre-treated with 100 ng/ml CXCL12 or 10 muM PP2 for 30 minutes at 37degreesC prior to stimulation. Samples were spun down at 13 000 rpm for 10 min and the supernatant boiled with SDS-buffer, subjected to SDS-PAGE and transfered to PVDF filters. Filters were subjected to immunoblotting with the indicated antibody over night at 4degreesC, washed 3 x 10 minutes with TBS-T, incubated 45 minutes with secondary antibody at RT, washed 3 x 10 minutes with TBS-T before incubation with SuperSignal West Pico Chemiluminescent Substrate for 5 minutes.

###begin title 23
Migration assay
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 261 263 260 262 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6 </sup>
###xml 374 375 372 373 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5</sup>
###xml 472 473 464 465 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 124 127 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">Cat</span>
Migration assays were performed in 24-well plates with 5.0 mum pore-sized polycarbonate membrane inserts (Costar Transwell, Cat. no. CLS-3421). Cells were washed in RPMI-1640 supplemented with 2% FCS and 25 mM Hepes (chemotaxis medium) and resuspended to 1 x 106 cells/ml. Six hundred mul chemotaxis medium was added to wells with or without 10 ng/ml CXCL12. Cells (1.5 x 105) were loaded into the transwell inserts and allowed to migrate for 2 hrs at 37degreesC and 5% CO2. Cells that had passed through the filter into the lower chamber were collected and 10 mul of an internal bead control (Bangs Laboratories, Fishers, IN) was added. The cells were counted by flow cytometry (FACS Calibur, Becton Dickinson) and normalized with reference to the internal bead control. Migration index was calculated as the number of cells migrating towards CXCL12 in the test well relative to the number of unmanipulated cells migrating towards medium in the control wells.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
CXCR4 surface expression
###end title 25
###begin p 26
Forty-eight hours post-transfection, Jurkat T cells were stimulated with 100 ng/ml CXCL12 for different time points, washed in ice-cold PBS, fixed in 4% paraformaldahyde for 10 minutes at 37degreesC, washed in PBS, and resuspended in PBS w/1% BSA and anti-CXCR4-PE for 30 min at 4degreesC. Thereafter the cells were washed in PBS w/1% BSA and subjected to flow cytometric analysis for detection of surface CXCR4 expression.

###begin title 29
Results and Discussion
###end title 29
###begin title 30
###xml 77 83 77 79 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q</sub>
CXCL12-pretreatment attenuates TCR-induced Lck activation comparable to the Galphaq-kd-kd phenotype
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 88 95 88 91 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 145 147 141 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 271 278 267 270 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 283 290 275 278 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2</sub>
###xml 372 374 360 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 412 417 400 401 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</sub>
###xml 450 452 434 436 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 453 455 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>

We have previously shown that siRNA-mediated knockdown of the heterotrimeric G protein Galphaq in T cells attenuates TCR-induced Lck activation [16]. While the TCR is not thought to be directly associated with G proteins, the chemokine receptor CXCR4 can couple to both Galphaq and Galphai2. Furthermore, CXCR4 has been shown to associate with TCR upon CXCL12 activation [15]. Activation of tyrosine kinases by Galpha-subunits has been demonstrated [17-20] and could represent the mechanism by which CXCR4 activates Lck [21]. We show here that the Src family kinase inhibitor, PP2, abrogated CXCL12-induced tyrosine-phosphorylation of several proteins (Figure 1A, marked with *), a further indication that Src family kinases act downstream of CXCR4. However, the changes in tyrosine phosphorylation following CXCL12 treatment are modest compared to those of anti-CD3 treatment (data not shown). Furthermore, pre-treatment of Jurkat T cells with CXCL12 prior to stimulation with anti-CD3 attenuated TCR-induced Lck activation (Figure 1B). Similarly, we also observed reduced ZAP-70 phosphorylation in cells pre-stimulated with CXCL12 (Figure 1B). This is in line with a previous report showing reduced TCR-induced phosphorylation of ZAP-70 and LAT in CXCL12-pretreated cells [13] suggesting that post-activation heterologous desensitization is occurring possibly as a result of Galphaq sequestration. Consistent with this notion surface expression of CXCR4 was also down after 30 minutes incubation with CXCL12. To address the role of Galpha-subunits in controlling the switch between TCR and CXCR4-signaling events, we subjected Jurkat T cells to siRNAs against Galphaq or Galphai2. We achieved more than 90% knockdown with these siRNAs as analysed by Western blotting (Fig. 1C). As previously demonstrated [16] TCR-induced Lck Y394 autophosphorylation was reduced in Galphaq-knockdown but not Galphai2-knockdown T cells demonstrating that Galphaq may directly or indirectly activate Lck (Fig 1C).

The reciprocal regulation between the TCR and chemokine receptors [13,22,23] suggest that the response to a specific chemokine is determined both by the differentiation and the activation state of the responding T cell. This implies that migrating T cells are less responsive to activation through the TCR. The physiological relevance of this switch between migration modus and TCR activation modus may be to ensure that migrating T cells do not get distracted by activation cues until they reach their destination. Conversely, TCR triggered T cells are less responsive to chemotactic gradients to ensure optimal activation. We observed that knockdown of Galphaq enhanced basal migration 2-fold (Figure 2A), suggesting that Galphaq may set the threshold for directed migration. This supports the findings of Molon et al. [8] who speculated that CXCR4 signaling through Galphaq promoted adhesion instead of migration. Accordingly, we saw that knockdown of Galphai2 inhibited directed CXCL12-induced migration, while Galphaq knockdown had no effect (Figure 2A) confirming the notion that Galphai2 is the principal Galpha-subunit involved in chemotaxis. The inhibitory effect of Galphai2 knockdown could however be partly reversed with simultaneous knockdown of Galphaq (Figure 2A). We speculate that the presence of Galphaq and absence of Galphai2 may result in a combined strong inhibitory effect on chemotaxis. Removal of Galphaq, will then lead to partial release of the inhibitory effect of Galphai2 knockdown alone. Although T cells from Galphaq-/- mice show no migrational defects [7], data from knockout mice are difficult to interpret as compensatory mechanisms may have been established during development. Acute knockdown with siRNA provides an alternative method to examine protein functions. How CXCR4 switches its signaling through Galphai2 and Galphaq is unknown, but the level of TCR engagement has been implicated [8,13]. Surface expression of CXCR4 following CXCL12-stimulation was neither affected by knockdown of Galphaq nor Galphai2 during a 1-hour time course (Figure 2B, only 0 minutes and 30 minutes shown). The level of surface expression of CD3 on Jurkat Tags has recently been reported [24]. Furthermore, we suggest that neither Ca2+-elevation nor ERK1/2 phosphorylation are involved in chemotaxis as has been suggested in a previous report [7]. While elevation of intracellular Ca2+ was abrogated in the Galphaq-knockdown cells upon CXCR4-stimulation (Figure 2C), it was intact in Galphai2-knockdown cells. The level of ERK1/2 phosphorylation was similar in both Galphaq-knockdown and Galphai2-knockdown Jurkat cells. However, concomitant knockdown of both Galphaq and Galphai2 completely attenuated ERK1/2 phosphorylation (Figure 2D), indicating that this is a G-protein dependent event.

###xml 654 662 630 634 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2 </sub>
###xml 699 706 671 674 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 754 761 722 725 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2</sub>
###xml 1079 1085 1043 1045 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q</sub>
###xml 1128 1130 1088 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4C</xref>
###xml 1247 1253 1207 1209 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q</sub>
Src homology phosphatase-1 (SHP-1) is an Lck-activated tyrosine phosphatase reported to negatively regulate CXCL12-induced migration [25]. As we have recently reported that Galphaq may activate Lck we examined how Galphaq manipulation would affect SHP-1 regulation in migration. Knockdown of Galphaq resulted in decreased tyrosine phosphorylation of SHP-1 compared to control cells (Fig 4A and 4B). This may indicate a role for Galphaq in the activation of SHP-1. Interestingly, increased basal phosphorylation of SHP-1 was observed in Galphai2 knockdown cells. This may be due to skewing of the CXCR4 signaling pathway towards Galphaq as there is less Galphai2 available. Conversely, knockdown of Galphaq might skew the CXCR4 signaling pathway towards Galphai2. To assess the role of SHP-1 in chemotaxis we transfected Jurkat T cells with the phosphatase-deficient, mutant SHP-1 C453S (c/s) that competes with endogenous SHP-1. Cells transfected with the mutant showed significantly increased basal and CXCL12 induced migration compared to control cells indicating that an Lck-Galphaq-SHP-1 pathway may regulate migration (Fig 4C). Migration is probably not exclusively regulated by SHP-1 and other negative regulators of migration activated by Galphaq-kd might exist.

###begin title 42
Conclusion
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 44 51 44 47 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 114 116 110 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 126 134 122 126 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2 </sub>
###xml 178 185 170 173 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 293 295 281 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4D</xref>
###xml 398 400 386 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 401 403 389 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 404 406 392 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 480 487 468 471 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 509 516 493 496 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 565 567 545 547 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 584 591 564 567 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 776 783 752 755 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 784 785 756 757 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 818 826 790 794 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2 </sub>
###xml 830 837 798 801 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 950 957 914 917 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;q </sub>
###xml 1043 1051 1003 1007 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;i2 </sub>
We have previously shown that knockdown of Galphaq attenuates TCR-induced Lck activation by an unknown mechanism [16]. While Galphai2 has a positive role in inducing migration, Galphaq may have a negative regulatory role by promoting Lck activity resulting in increased SHP-1 activity (Figure 4D). Previous reports have demonstrated a reciprocal regulation between the TCR and chemokine-receptors [13,22,23]. Based on our recent report, an optimal TCR-triggering may require the Galphaq signaling pathway as Galphaq knockdown attenuates TCR-induced Lck activation [16]. In contrast, Galphaq knockdown appears to release migration from tonic inhibition. This is in line with data showing increased proliferation and cytokine-production when chemokine-receptors signal through Galphaq [8] suggesting that a switch from Galphai2 to Galphaq through the chemokine-receptor promotes T cell activation rather than migration. In conclusion, we suggest that Galphaq activation strengthens the commitment of T cells to activation through the TCR while Galphai2 activation commits the T cell to migration. This might contribute to ensure that T cells migrating towards a site of infection do not get distracted by unnecessary activation cues, while activated T cells are fully committed towards differentiation and proliferation.
