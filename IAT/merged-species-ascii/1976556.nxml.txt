Neuroadaptations in Human Chronic Alcoholics: Dysregulation of the NF-kappaB System
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: GB TY. Performed the experiments: AO SJ TY. Analyzed the data: AO SJ AK RM IP. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: DS TG CH. Wrote the paper: IB TE GB AO AK RM RH YH LT TY.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 366 371 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Alcohol dependence and associated cognitive impairments apparently result from neuroadaptations to chronic alcohol consumption involving changes in expression of multiple genes. Here we investigated whether transcription factors of Nuclear Factor-kappaB (NF-kappaB) family, controlling neuronal plasticity and neurodegeneration, are involved in these adaptations in human chronic alcoholics.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods and Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 512 516 496 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RELA</italic>
###xml 149 154 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Analysis of DNA-binding of NF-kappaB (p65/p50 heterodimer) and the p50 homodimer as well as NF-kappaB proteins and mRNAs was performed in postmortem human brain samples from 15 chronic alcoholics and 15 control subjects. The prefrontal cortex involved in alcohol dependence and cognition was analyzed and the motor cortex was studied for comparison. The p50 homodimer was identified as dominant kappaB binding factor in analyzed tissues. NF-kappaB and p50 homodimer DNA-binding was downregulated, levels of p65 (RELA) mRNA were attenuated, and the stoichiometry of p65/p50 proteins and respective mRNAs was altered in the prefrontal cortex of alcoholics. Comparison of a number of p50 homodimer/NF-kappaB target DNA sites, kappaB elements in 479 genes, down- or upregulated in alcoholics demonstrated that genes with kappaB elements were generally upregulated in alcoholics. No significant differences between alcoholics and controls were observed in the motor cortex.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 137 141 133 137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RELA</italic>
We suggest that cycles of alcohol intoxication/withdrawal, which may initially activate NF-kappaB, when repeated over years downregulate RELA expression and NF-kappaB and p50 homodimer DNA-binding. Downregulation of the dominant p50 homodimer, a potent inhibitor of gene transcription apparently resulted in derepression of kappaB regulated genes. Alterations in expression of p50 homodimer/NF-kappaB regulated genes may contribute to neuroplastic adaptation underlying alcoholism.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 360 363 360 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Fadda1">[1]</xref>
###xml 365 368 365 368 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Koob1">[2]</xref>
###xml 613 616 613 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Parsons1">[3]</xref>
###xml 618 621 618 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Schmidt1">[4]</xref>
###xml 709 712 709 712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Crews1">[5]</xref>
###xml 714 717 714 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Sullivan1">[6]</xref>
###xml 865 868 865 868 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Harper1">[7]</xref>
###xml 869 873 869 873 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Agartz1">[11]</xref>
Although mechanisms of alcoholism remain to be elucidated, the molecular hypothesis postulates that that alcohol dependence and toxicity result from neuroadaptations to chronic alcohol consumption based on alterations in gene expression. Molecular and cellular adaptations in the nucleus accumbens, ventral tegmental area, amygdala and prefrontal cortex (PFC) [1], [2] have been implicated in the behavioral changes such as craving and relapse induced by chronic alcohol consumption. Chronic alcohol abuse also causes deficits in perceptual-motor skills, visual-spatial functions, abstraction and problem solving [3], [4]. These impairments may be related to alcohol-induced damage to the PFC and hippocampus [5], [6]. White matter and cell loss in the PFC, loss of hippocampal volume and shrinkage of hippocampal neurons are characteristic of these maladaptations [7]-[11].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 125 129 125 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-FlatscherBader1">[12]</xref>
###xml 130 134 130 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Liu1">[14]</xref>
###xml 414 418 414 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-FlatscherBader1">[12]</xref>
###xml 419 423 419 423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Liu1">[14]</xref>
###xml 113 118 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Earlier work has found that alcohol abuse is associated with widespread changes in gene expression in the PFC of human brain [12]-[14]. Differentially expressed genes form functional groups implicated in immune response, cell survival, inflammation, signal transduction and energy production. Pronounced differences have been found in genes involved in myelination, protein trafficking, apoptosis and neurogenesis [12]-[14]. Re-programming of gene expression in chronic alcoholics apparently involves transcription factors that are responsive to the primary effects of ethanol, and that regulate multiple pathways leading to neuropathology and neuronal dysfunctions.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 163 167 159 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt1">[15]</xref>
###xml 168 172 164 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Li1">[17]</xref>
###xml 390 394 378 382 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Guan1">[18]</xref>
###xml 396 400 384 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Li2">[19]</xref>
###xml 1130 1134 1076 1080 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Ghosh1">[20]</xref>
###xml 1135 1139 1081 1085 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Moynagh1">[22]</xref>
###xml 1332 1336 1270 1274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt1">[15]</xref>
###xml 1338 1342 1276 1280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Mattson1">[16]</xref>
###xml 1344 1348 1282 1286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt2">[23]</xref>
###xml 1350 1354 1288 1292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Bakalkin1">[24]</xref>
###xml 1553 1557 1487 1491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt1">[15]</xref>
###xml 1559 1563 1493 1497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Mattson1">[16]</xref>
###xml 1565 1569 1499 1503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Meffert1">[25]</xref>
###xml 1570 1574 1504 1508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Mazzeo1">[31]</xref>
Transcription factors of the NF-kappaB family are inducible proteins that regulate expression of genes involved in inflammation, immune response and cell survival [15]-[17]. These factors are homo- or heterodimers of p65 (Rel A), p50 and other proteins of the NF-kappaB family. The p65/p50 heterodimer (NF-kappaB) generally activates gene transcription while the p50 homodimer represses it [18], [19]. In most cell types, NF-kappaB is sequestered in the cytoplasm in a complex with inhibitor IkappaB proteins. Nuclear translocation of NF-kappaB is induced by multiple extracellular stimuli that trigger activation of an IkappaB kinase (IKK) complex, which phosphorylates the IkappaBs leading to their ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation. The released NF-kappaB migrates to the nucleus to act as a transcription factor. The IKK complex contains the two kinases IKKalpha and IKKbeta and the regulatory subunit NEMO/IKKgamma, and functions as integrator of signals regulating NF-kappaB activity. Transactivating capacity is also regulated in the cell nuclei through phosphorylation of p65 and p50 by IKKbeta and other kinases [20]-[22]. In the brain, the p65 and p50 NF-kappaB subunits are abundantly expressed in neurons and glia, and a substantial fraction of NF-kappaB is located in the cell nuclei and constitutively active [15], [16], [23], [24]. The NF-kappaB-mediated activation of gene transcription contributes to long-term changes in synaptic signaling, cognitive behavior, developmental cell death and chronic neurodegenerative disorders [15], [16], [25]-[31].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 235 239 231 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt1">[15]</xref>
###xml 241 245 237 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Mattson1">[16]</xref>
###xml 247 251 243 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Moynagh1">[22]</xref>
###xml 253 257 249 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Meffert1">[25]</xref>
###xml 258 262 254 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt3">[29]</xref>
###xml 494 498 490 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-FlatscherBader1">[12]</xref>
###xml 499 503 495 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Liu1">[14]</xref>
###xml 527 531 519 523 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt1">[15]</xref>
###xml 532 536 524 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Li1">[17]</xref>
###xml 538 542 530 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Huang1">[32]</xref>
###xml 544 548 536 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Nickols1">[33]</xref>
###xml 996 999 984 987 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kril1">[8]</xref>
###xml 1001 1005 989 993 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kril2">[10]</xref>
###xml 1267 1271 1251 1255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Liu1">[14]</xref>
###xml 684 689 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 801 806 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1242 1247 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Several observations including NF-kappaB activation by glutamate, cytokines and oxidative stress, ability to transmit signals from the cytoplasm and synapses to the cell nuclei and regulation of synaptic plasticity and neuron survival [15], [16], [22], [25]-[29], suggest a role of this transcription factor in adaptation to chronic alcohol intake. Induction of oxidative stress by alcohol and alcoholism-associated alterations in the expression of inflammatory, cell survival and myelin genes [12]-[14] regulated by NF-kappaB [15]-[17], [32], [33] support this hypothesis. In the present study, we aimed to evaluate whether the NF-kappaB system is involved in neuroadaptation of the human brain to chronic alcohol abuse. The PFC involved in alcohol dependence and cognitive behavior, and impaired in human chronic alcoholics was chosen for analysis. The motor cortex (MC) that is not engaged in these processes to the same extent and is less affected by alcohol with respect to neuronal density [8], [10], was studied for comparison. The NF-kappaB system was characterized with biochemical and molecular techniques, and the alterations found were correlated with changes in the pattern of gene expression previously identified in the PFC of human chronic alcoholics [14].
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Materials and Methods
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Case Selection
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 268 275 268 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0000930-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 506 510 506 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Association1">[34]</xref>
###xml 1236 1241 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Brain samples (superior frontal cortex, Brodmann area 9 and motor cortex, Brodmann area 4) were obtained from the Tissue Resource Center, University of Sydney, Australia (). A total of 30 cases, all males, including 15 alcoholic and 15 control subjects were analyzed (Table 1). Alcoholic and control cases were matched for age at death, and postmortem interval (PMI). Alcohol subjects met criteria for Diagnostic and Statistical Manual for Mental Disorders, 4th edition, (American Psychiatric Association) [34] and also National Health and Medical Research Council (NHMRC)/World Health Organization criteria as individuals who consumed greater than 80 g of ethanol per day for the majority of their adult lives. Controls had either abstained from alcohol completely or were social drinkers who consumed less than 20 g of ethanol per day on average. Cases with a history of poly drug abuse (evidence that the individual abused other drugs such as cocaine or heroin) or with medical complications such as liver cirrhosis and the Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome or alcoholic cases with concomitant diseases were excluded. Samples were taken by qualified pathologists under full ethical clearance from the Sydney South West Area Health Service Human Ethics Committee (X03-0117) and informed written consent from the next of kin, kept at -80degreesC and shipped to Sweden on dry ice.

###begin title 18
mRNA quantification by TaqMan(R) low density array
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 1248 1252 1218 1222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Vandesompele1">[35]</xref>
###xml 1406 1410 1376 1380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Johansson1">[36]</xref>
RNA preparation was performed using RNeasy Lipid Tissue Mini Kit (QIAGEN, Maryland, USA). RNA was quantified by Nanodrop(R) and Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent, Palo Alto, CA) was used to control RNA quality. Only RNA with clear ribosomal RNA, 18S and 28S, was used for further analysis. cDNA was synthesized with the High Capacity cDNA Archive Kit (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA). mRNA levels were quantified by TaqMan(R) Low Density Arrays (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA). In a pre-prepared micro fluidic card containing probes and primers for each gene, cDNA and TaqMan(R) Universal PCR Master Mix (Applied Biosystems) was added in a final concentration of 65 pg cDNA per sample and gene. Every sample was run in duplicate on the same array for each gene. The PCR amplification was performed at 50degreesC for 2 min, 94. 5degreesC for 10 min and 40 cycles of 97degreesC for 30 seconds followed by 59.7degreesC for 1 min. To measure the quantity of a given RNA species, the threshold cycles (Ct) were monitored by the Applied Biosystems 7900HT Fast Real-Time PCR System. Each mRNA expression was calculated by relative quantification using a normalization factor (geometric mean of two reference genes selected by geNORM program, ) [35] and qBASE program for internal and external calibration and also for easy care of large RT-PCR datasets (). According to our analysis of reference genes [36] the beta-actin (ACTB) and ribosomal large P0 (RPLP0) genes for PFC, and the 18S and RPLP0 genes for the MC were chosen for normalization.
###end p 19

Powdered tissue samples were boiled in pre-warmed SDS extraction buffer (0.45 M Tris-HCl, pH 8.5, 2.5% glycerol, 4% SDS, 0.5 mM DTT and 5xprotease inhibitors for 5 min, sonicated and protein extracts were aliquoted and kept at -80degreesC until usage. Protein concentration was determined with the DC protein assay (Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA, USA). Aliquots of tissue extracts (50-120 microg protein) were heated for 5 min at 95degreesC in the presence of 5 mM beta-mercaptoethanol and resolved by SDS-PAGE on 10% Tricine gels as described earlier [37]. ###begin title 27
Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 59 63 59 63 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Bakalkin1">[24]</xref>
###xml 248 250 236 238 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">32</sup>
The EMSA was performed essentially as described previously [24]. Protein extracts (10 microg) were added to the binding mixture (20 microl of 10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5, 20 mM KCl, 1 mM EDTA, 7.5% glycerol, 1.5 mM DTT, 20 microg BSA and 40-60,000 cpm [32P] -labeled oligonucleotide) in duplicates and incubated for 20-30 minutes at room temperature. The reaction mixture was brought to 0.6% or 0.03% deoxycholate (DOC) after 30 min followed by the addition of 1.2% NP-40 and were loaded onto a 5% polyacrylamide gel in 0.5xTGE (25 mM Tris-HCl, 0.19 M glycine, 1 mM EDTA, pH 8.5) buffer. Reference samples consisting of pooled cerebellar extracts from control subjects were loaded onto two wells on each edge of the gel to ascertain reproducibility of the gel, and also for inter-gel comparison. In competition experiments, 10 ng of unlabeled wt-kappaB or m-kappaB oligonucleotide was added to the incubation mixture prior to the initiation of the binding reaction. After electrophoresis the gels were fixed in 40% methanol containing 3% acetic acid for 10 min, dried and exposed to intensifying screen. Detection was performed on phosphorimager BAS 1500 (Fuji Film, Kanagawa, Japan) and densities were analyzed using Fuji Film Image Gauge software.

NF-kappaB- and p50 homodimer DNA-binding sites in genes differentially expressed in the PFC of human alcoholics compared to control subjects [14] were analyzed with the Prometheus system [41]. Transcription Factor (TF) Binding Sites (TFBS) were identified in phylogenetically conserved (human versus rat) regions of the 10 kb-promoter region. The weight matrices for TFBS identification were taken from JASPAR [42]. Prometheus uses the GeneLynx Database [43] and the GeneLynx application programming interface to obtain the genomic coordinates and sequences for human and rat species with an upstream promoter length of 10 kb. LAGAN aligner algorithm was run using a max gap length of 5 bp, a conservation rate of 45 bp, conservation super rate of 65 bp and a score cutoff of 25 bp [44], [45]. The TFBS finding algorithms were run using a window size of 50 bp, a conservation cutoff of 70% and a TF score threshold of 80% for human-rat comparison. The system uses the Grid computing paradigm [46] for the execution of computationally intensive steps involved in the TFBS finding process: genomic sequence alignment and TFBS searching. The Grid computational resources are provided by NorduGrid Virtual Organization (). Significance of differences in a number of TFBS between gene sets was evaluated with the Chi-Square test. 479 out of 531 differentially expressed genes identified by Liu et al. [14] were analyzed (Table supplemental (S) 1); 52 out of the 531 genes were not included because of missing annotations/sequences. Brain autopsy material used in the present and Liu et al. [14] studies was partially obtained from the same source (Tissue Resource Center, University of Sydney); only two of all subjects were analyzed in both studies. Sets of downregulated and upregulated genes were compared with each other, and with a control set of 1164 genes (Table S1). Control set did not include genes differentially expressed in the PFC of alcoholics.

###begin title 31
Statistical Analysis
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 486 487 486 487 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
Results are expressed as mean and standard deviation (normally distributed variables) or median and range (non-normal distribution). Normality of data distribution was analyzed using Shapiro-Wilk's W-test. In data sets that were not normally distributed non-parametric analysis (Mann-Whitney and Spearman correlations) was used. A general stepwise linear regression model was used to evaluate group differences and identify covariates (age, brain pH and postmortem interval). Student's t-test was used to assess differences between groups when no covariates were found. Variables with a significant association with group were included in the final statistical model as covariates. Pearsons correlation analysis was performed to determine the association between different variables. Slopes in the correlation equation were compared with a multiple regression model. Differences in the number of up-, down-regulated and control genes containing TFBS were evaluated with Chi-Square test. Significance was set at P<0.05 and trends considered for P<0.10. Statistical analysis was carried out using the Statistica 6.0 package (StatSoft Scandinavia, Uppsala, Sweden).

NF-kappaB DNA-binding activity was compared in alcoholics and controls using EMSA. The assay identified three main complexes in both PFC and MC (Figure 3A). Two upper complexes (I, II) were specific since their formation was inhibited in the presence of unlabeled wt-kappaB oligonucleotide (Figure 3A, lane 2) but not m-kappaB oligonucleotide (Figure 3A, lane 3). Formation of the third, lower complex (III) was not blocked by wt- or m-kappaB oligonucleotide demonstrating low specificity and affinity of this factor for binding to DNA. We previously identified protein factors in the complexes I and II as NF-kappaB (p50/p65 heterodimer) and p50 homodimer, respectively, using the supershift/depletion assay with anti-p65- and p50-antibodies and also UV-crosslinking in extracts of human neuronal cell lines and rat brain [24], [47]. We also identified complex III as the Ku protein that binds to double-stranded DNA ends, does not recognize specific DNA sequences, and is present at high levels in the human brain [48], [49]. The levels of the constitutively active and total (active plus latent) NF-kappaB DNA-binding were measured in the presence of 0.03% and 0.6% DOC, respectively; at high concentration DOC dissociates complexes of NF-kappaB with the IkappaB inhibitory protein [24], [38], [47]. The NF-kappaB/complex I was induced by pre-incubation of extracts from the PFC of control subjects or alcoholics with 0.6% DOC only by 12% and 14%, respectively; thus NF-kappaB was virtually present in the activated form in this structure (Figure 3A, lane 4). 0.6% DOC slightly inhibited p50 homodimer binding to DNA. The p50 homodimer was a dominant kappaB binding factor in the human cortex; the levels of its DNA-binding activity exceeded those of NF-kappaB by 2.4-2.7 fold in the PFC and MC of controls and alcoholics (Student's t-test for dependent samples: PFC/controls, T1,10 = 8.56, P<0.0001, n = 11; PFC/alcoholics, T1,12 = 12.77, P<0.0001, n = 13 (see Figure 3B, lines 1, 2, 4-8); MC/controls, T1,10 = 10.83, P<0.0001, n = 11; MC/alcoholics, T1,12 = 8.48, P<0.0001, n = 13).

###xml 2082 2090 2018 2026 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0000930.s004">Table S4</xref>
###xml 712 717 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1828 1833 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1834 1837 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Downregulation of DNA-binding of the p50 homodimer and NF-kappaB in alcoholics may result in changes in expression of genes that are regulated through kappaB elements, binding sites for these transcription factors. To test this hypothesis we used a computational approach to compare the number of genes containing kappaB elements in two sets of genes that were previously identified by one of the co-author groups [14] as downregulated (n = 270) or upregulated (n = 209) in the PFC of alcoholics (Table S1). A group of 1164 genes that were randomly selected from the set, which did not include genes differentially expressed in alcoholics, was studied for comparison (control genes; Table S1). Previous analyses human chromosome 22 with chromatin immunoprecipitation and genomic microarrays [50] demonstrated that kappaB elements are enriched at the 5'-end of genes. Therefore, we focus on the identification of kappaB elements in 10 kb-promoter regions. p50 homodimer and NF-kappaB bind to virtually the same kappaB elements. Weight matrix for NF-kappaB in the JASPAR database [42] consists of sequences of the "NF-kappaB"-subtype that are also targets for the p50 homodimer (Figure 5A). p50 protein binds to kappaB sites as homodimer and therefore has slightly higher affinity for symmetric kappaB elements [51], [52]; this is reflected in the composition of the second kappaB matrix of the "p50 homodimer"-subtype present in the JASPAR database (Figure 5B). We used both matrices in the analysis (Figure 5). To increase prediction specificity we used a phylogenetic footprinting approach based on the observation that functional Transcription Factor Binding Sites (TFBS) are more often located in evolutionary conserved regions [53], [54]. Analysis of the 479 differentially expressed genes with phylogenetic footprinting of human-rat gene sequences demonstrated that 58 genes contain one or more putative kappaB binding sites for both p50 homodimer and NF-kappaB (the "NF-kappaB"-subtype), and 19 genes contain at least one putative "p50 homodimer"-subtype kappaB binding site (Table S4). Among 1164 control genes, 188 genes contain putative "NF-kappaB"-subtype whereas 77 genes putative "p50 homodimer"-subtype of kappaB binding sites.

Next, we evaluated differences in the proportion of genes that contain the "NF-kappaB"- or "p50 homodimer"-subtypes of kappaB elements in 10 kb promoter region between upregulated, downregulated and control genes (Figure 5C, D; Table S4). There was a significant difference in the frequency of occurrence of genes with each "NF-kappaB"- or "p50 homodimer"- subtype of kappaB elements between up- and down regulated genes and control genes (Chi-square test, df = 2: chi2 = 8.30, P<0.05; chi2 = 7.75, P<0.05). ###xml 243 247 239 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RELA</italic>
###xml 341 349 334 342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000930-g006">Figure 6</xref>
The main findings of the present study are that DNA-binding of NF-kappaB and p50 homodimer as well as RELA gene expression are downregulated in the PFC of chronic alcoholics. The downregulation of DNA-binding might result from the decrease in RELA gene expression, and from the alterations in stoichiometry in p65, p50 and IKKbeta proteins (Figure 6). p65 and p50 proteins constitute NF-kappaB and p50 homodimer while IKKbeta integrates signals regulating the NF-kappaB system through phosphorylation of IkappaBalpha and p65. No significant differences between control and alcoholic groups in the NF-kappaB system were found in the MC suggesting that dysregulation of NF-kappaB-mediated adaptive mechanisms is specific for the PFC.
###end p 64
###begin title 65
Model for the molecular adaptations in the NF-kappaB system in the PFC in alcoholics.
###end title 65
###begin p 66
###xml 36 40 36 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RELA</italic>
###xml 104 114 104 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RELA/NFKB1</italic>
(A) Chronic alcohol intake inhibits RELA gene transcription and induces alterations in stoichiometry of RELA/NFKB1 mRNAs and p65/p50 proteins, resulting in downregulation of NF-kappaB DNA-binding. In cells with dominant NF-kappaB this adaptation results in inhibition of NF-kappaB-dependent transcription. (B) Alcohol acts upstream of an unidentified protein kinase that modulates DNA-binding of the p50 homodimer. This results in hypophosphorylation of p50 and downregulation of p50 homodimer DNA-binding. In control individuals the p50 homodimer inhibits gene transcription acting through kappaB binding sites in cells in which it prevails over NF-kappaB. In alcoholics, downregulation of the p50 homodimer eliminates transcriptional repression leading to upregulation of kappaB-mediated transcription. Dysregulation of the NF-kappaB/p50 homodimer-dependent gene transcription contributes to cellular neuroplastic adaptations and neurodegeneration.
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 126 131 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 540 545 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 561 564 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 683 688 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Several factors may affect the quality of RNA and proteins, and DNA binding activities of transcription factors in postmortem human tissues. In the present study, the RNA quality was controlled and no samples with degraded RNA were used in the analysis. No degraded proteins seen as smearing or additional bands on western blots were evident in any sample. Using our protocol for fast extraction of nuclear proteins from frozen tissues, we observed similar levels of the NF-kappaB/p50 homodimer DNA binding activities in extracts of frozen human samples and of rat brain tissues prepared immediately after decapitation, thus demonstrating low if any inactivation of these factors in human samples. Six subjects demonstrated no NF-kappaB, p50 homodimer and Ku protein DNA binding activity were characterized by low brain pH values and were omitted from the analysis. Levels of RNA, proteins and NF-kappaB/p50 homodimer DNA-binding did not significantly correlate with postmortem interval and age at death of subjects analyzed suggesting that influences of these covariates on studied molecular parameters are negligible.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 86 90 78 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt1">[15]</xref>
###xml 91 95 83 87 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Li1">[17]</xref>
###xml 97 101 89 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Ghosh1">[20]</xref>
###xml 102 106 94 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Moynagh1">[22]</xref>
###xml 108 112 100 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Chen1">[51]</xref>
###xml 374 378 362 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Ghosh1">[20]</xref>
###xml 380 384 368 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Chen1">[51]</xref>
###xml 386 390 374 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Zhong1">[55]</xref>
###xml 576 580 560 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Guan1">[18]</xref>
###xml 582 586 566 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Li2">[19]</xref>
###xml 777 785 761 769 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000930-g006">Figure 6</xref>
NF-kappaB is classically activated through dissociation from IkappaB in the cytoplasm [15]-[17], [20]-[22], [51]. DNA-binding and transactivation functions are also regulated through posttranslational modifications of NF-kappaB proteins in the cell nucleus. Phosphorylation at several sites by the IKK complex increases the DNA binding and transcriptional activities of p65 [20], [51], [55]. p50 phosphorylation by protein kinase A and other kinases is critical for binding of the p50 homodimer to DNA, which results in transcriptional repression of NF-kappaB dependent genes [18], [19]. In the apparent absence of changes in the p50 content, the downregulation of p50 homodimer DNA-binding in chronic alcoholics may occur due to the deficient phosphorylation of this protein (Figure 6).
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 199 203 191 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Hoberg1">[56]</xref>
###xml 282 287 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The p50 homodimer is capable of suppressing the transactivation activities of NF-kappaB by occupying the same kappaB sites and recruiting corepressor complexes containing histone deacetylases (HDAC) [56]. We found that the p50 homodimer is the dominant kappaB binding factor in the human PFC. Bioinformatic analysis demonstrated that the proportion of genes with putative kappaB elements was significantly higher in the set of genes upregulated in the PFC of alcoholics compared to the set of downregulated genes. The most plausible explanation for these observations is that in alcoholics the p50 homodimer-induced repression of gene transcriptions weakens due to the p50 homodimer downregulation thus allowing the recruitment of NF-kappaB that activates transcription from kappaB elements.
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 209 213 209 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Liu1">[14]</xref>
###xml 283 291 279 287 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0000930.s005">Table S5</xref>
###xml 371 375 367 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">DAXX</italic>
###xml 509 513 505 509 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Salomoni1">[57]</xref>
###xml 619 623 611 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Michaelson1">[58]</xref>
Genes differentially expressed in the PFC in alcoholics were previously pooled into 16 groups according to their functions associated with apoptosis, immune/stress response, myelination and gene transcription [14]. Genes in 14 out of 16 groups have putative NF-kappaB binding sites (Table S5). Among the upregulated genes in alcoholics is the death-associated protein 6 (DAXX). It is involved in cell survival/cell death and spatial learning processes. Interestingly, DAXX acts as an anti-apoptotic regulator [57] through its ability to repress gene transcription activated by NF-kappaB and other transcription factors [58]. Overall, bioinformatic data support the notion of a critical role of adaptations in the NF-kappaB/p50 system in the differential regulation of gene expression in the PFC of chronic alcoholics.
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 134 138 134 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Huang2">[59]</xref>
###xml 140 144 140 144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Niehrs1">[60]</xref>
###xml 204 209 204 209 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">trans</italic>
###xml 261 265 261 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Huang2">[59]</xref>
###xml 267 271 267 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Niehrs1">[60]</xref>
###xml 311 331 311 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RELA, NFKB1, IKK&#946;</italic>
###xml 336 348 333 337 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
Tight spatio-temporal co-expression of genes functioning in the same cell-biological process is a widespread phenomenon in eukaryotes [59], [60]. Synexpression groups have been found to be coordinated by trans-acting factors regulating common promoter elements [59], [60]. Strong correlation between content of RELA, NFKB1, IKKbeta and IkappaBalpha mRNAs suggests common regulation of transcription for these four genes. This mechanism appears to operate in the PFC but not in the MC where no strong correlations between these four mRNAs were observed.
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 185 189 177 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Altura1">[61]</xref>
###xml 190 194 182 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Davis1">[64]</xref>
###xml 427 431 415 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Altura1">[61]</xref>
###xml 433 437 421 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Blanco1">[62]</xref>
###xml 594 598 578 582 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Crews2">[63]</xref>
###xml 890 894 866 870 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Crews2">[63]</xref>
Several animal studies suggest that chronic alcohol intake alters CNS immune and inflammatory responses through interference with NF-kappaB and expression of NF-kappaB-controlled genes [61]-[64]. Thus, chronic alcohol administration up-regulates inflammatory mediators in the animal brain and isolated astrocytes followed by activation of NF-kappaB and the upregulation of inducible NO synthase and cyclooxygenase-2 expression [61], [62]. Neuroinflammation may be also promoted by serum TNFalpha and other cytokines elevated due to systemic and hepatic inflammation induced by alcohol drinking [63]. Serum cytokines apparently enter the brain where they activate microglia. Serum and brain-produced TNFalpha acting through the NF-kappaB system may inhibit glutamate transport and, consequently, induce a hyperglutamatergic state that contributes to increased drinking and neurodegeneration [63].
###end p 72
###begin p 73
###xml 55 58 55 58 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Fadda1">[1]</xref>
###xml 60 64 60 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Grant1">[65]</xref>
###xml 66 70 66 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Grant2">[66]</xref>
###xml 165 169 161 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt1">[15]</xref>
###xml 171 175 167 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Mattson1">[16]</xref>
###xml 177 181 173 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Meffert1">[25]</xref>
###xml 182 186 178 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Guerrini1">[27]</xref>
###xml 509 513 493 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Rulten1">[67]</xref>
###xml 515 519 499 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Ward1">[68]</xref>
###xml 603 607 587 591 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Rulten1">[67]</xref>
###xml 1014 1018 990 994 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Grundstrom1">[69]</xref>
###xml 1020 1024 996 1000 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Hajishengallis1">[70]</xref>
###xml 739 744 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Withdrawal from alcohol results in neuronal excitation [1], [65], [66] through activation of glutamate receptors and calcium influx; both stimuli activate NF-kappaB [15], [16], [25]-[27]. Cycles of alcohol intoxication and withdrawal, which initially activate NF-kappaB, when repeated over years may lead to adaptations in NF-kappaB that tolerate excessive stimulation. This hypothesis is supported by animal studies demonstrating that acute ethanol administration activates the NF-kappaB system in the brain [67], [68], which is then downregulated when ethanol is administered for the next three weeks [67]. Another adaptation to chronic alcohol consumption is the downregulation of the p50 homodimer, a dominant kappaB-binding factor in human cortex. This apparently results in derepression of genes regulated through kappaB-elements in their promoters. These types of adaptations, ensuring a low responsiveness and/or compensatory response to chronic stimulation, differ from that observed in the immune system [69], [70], which is based on the over-expression of the inhibitory p50 subunit.
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 121 125 117 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt1">[15]</xref>
###xml 127 131 123 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Mattson1">[16]</xref>
###xml 133 137 129 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Meffert1">[25]</xref>
###xml 138 142 134 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt3">[29]</xref>
###xml 207 211 203 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-ORiordan1">[28]</xref>
###xml 231 235 227 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Yeh1">[71]</xref>
###xml 255 259 251 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Meffert2">[26]</xref>
###xml 261 265 257 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-OMahony1">[72]</xref>
###xml 470 474 462 466 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt3">[29]</xref>
###xml 626 629 614 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PKA</italic>
Several lines of evidence demonstrate that NF-kappaB plays an important role in synaptic plasticity and long-term memory [15], [16], [25]-[29]. This factor is regulated by glutamate and required for LTP/LTD [28], fear conditioning [71] and spatial memory [26], [72]. NF-kappaB regulates, through its effects on expression of PKA, phosphorylation of CREB that is essential for transduction of synaptic signals into the cell nucleus and eventually for learning and memory [29]. These studies imply that adaptations in NF-kappaB in chronic alcoholics may influence neurocognitive functions through dysregulation of expression of PKA and other plastic genes in neurons.
###end p 74
###begin p 75
###xml 63 67 59 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Fosb</italic>
###xml 254 258 250 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Hope1">[73]</xref>
###xml 260 264 256 260 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Ehrlich1">[74]</xref>
###xml 890 894 866 870 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kearns1">[75]</xref>
###xml 999 1003 971 975 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Michaelson1">[58]</xref>
###xml 1030 1034 1002 1006 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Rosenfeld1">[76]</xref>
###xml 1041 1045 1013 1017 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Teferedegne1">[77]</xref>
###xml 1055 1059 1027 1031 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt3">[29]</xref>
###xml 502 507 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In animal studies, upregulation of DeltaFosB, a product of the Fosb gene induced by chronic administration of virtually all drugs of abuse was found to persists for several weeks and may maintain stable expression of genes associated with drug addiction [73], [74]. Dysregulation of the NF-kappaB system observed in the present study is associated with chronic alcoholism, and as such represents the first example of sustained transcriptional adaptation to chronic intake of substances of abuse in the human brain. The sustained adaptations in the NF-kappaB system apparently represent a shift to new steady state or allostatic levels in the NF-kappaB/p50 activities that may underlie persistent alterations in expression of their target genes. Multiple mechanisms may ensure the stability of alterations that, hypothetically, may involve a) the feedback loop between NF-kappaB and IkappaB [75]; b) feed-back and feed-forward NF-kappaB/p50 interactions with other transcription factors such as DAXX [58], glucocorticoid receptors [76], SP-1 [77] and CREB [29].
###end p 75
###begin p 76
###xml 229 233 225 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RELA</italic>
###xml 373 381 369 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000930-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 482 486 474 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Kaltschmidt1">[15]</xref>
###xml 488 492 480 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Mattson1">[16]</xref>
###xml 494 498 486 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Meffert1">[25]</xref>
###xml 499 503 491 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-ORiordan1">[28]</xref>
###xml 1206 1210 1182 1186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Mulligan1">[78]</xref>
###xml 1491 1495 1463 1467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000930-Mulligan1">[78]</xref>
###xml 1606 1611 1578 1583 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NFKB1</italic>
###xml 118 123 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1219 1224 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1372 1377 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
In conclusion, we have found that chronic alcohol consumption downregulates NF-kappaB and p50 homodimer in the PFC of human brain. These adaptations may be induced through several mechanisms that a) regulate transcription of the RELA gene, b) maintain the stoichiometry of p65 and p50 proteins, and c) control DNA-binding of p50 homodimer possibly via its phosphorylation (Figure 6). NF-kappaB may be targeted through the glutamate receptors, calcium influx and oxidative processes [15], [16], [25]-[28], all affected by cycles of alcohol consumption and withdrawal. Allostasis in the NF-kappaB system found in the present study may ensure tolerance and/or compensatory response to excessive stimulation in chronic alcoholics. Downregulation of the inhibitory p50 homodimer apparently contributes to the enhanced expression of genes regulated through kappaB elements in the PFC in alcoholics. Adaptations in the NF-kappaB system may be essential for persistent neuroplastic changes and neurodegeneration underlying dependence to alcohol and associated impairment of cognitive functions. An important role of the NF-kappaB system in alcoholism is emphasized by the recent animal transcriptome meta-analysis [78] and the human genetics findings. The NF-kappaB signaling pathway was found to be overrepresented in genes divergent between alcohol-preferring and nonpreferring mouse genotypes suggesting the participation of this system in establishing a high level of voluntary alcohol drinking [78]. The Collaborative Study on the Genetics of Alcoholism reported evidence for association of variations in the NFKB1 (p50) gene with the risk for alcoholism (data presented at the International Narcotic Research Conference, 2005; with permission of Dr. Howard Edenberg).
