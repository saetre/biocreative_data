###begin article-title 0
NF-kappaB activity marks cells engaged in receptor editing
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
R.H. Amin's present address is Fred Hutchison Cancer Research Center, Seattle, WA 98109.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 496 508 492 496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 596 602 573 579 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/lacZ</sup>

###xml 761 779 731 741 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;<sup>+/lacZ</sup></italic>
###xml 1211 1215 1153 1157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IRF4</italic>
###xml 1257 1258 1196 1197 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1280 1284 1219 1223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IRF4</italic>
###xml 457 462 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 789 793 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 918 922 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Because of the extreme diversity in immunoglobulin genes, tolerance mechanisms are necessary to ensure that B cells do not respond to self-antigens. One such tolerance mechanism is called receptor editing. If the B cell receptor (BCR) on an immature B cell recognizes self-antigen, it is down-regulated from the cell surface, and light chain gene rearrangement continues in an attempt to edit the autoreactive specificity. Analysis of a heterozygous mutant mouse in which the NF-kappaB-dependent IkappaBalpha gene was replaced with a lacZ (beta-gal) reporter complementary DNA (cDNA; IkappaBalpha+/lacZ) suggests a potential role for NF-kappaB in receptor editing. Sorted beta-gal+ pre-B cells showed increased levels of various markers of receptor editing. In IkappaBalpha+/lacZ reporter mice expressing either innocuous or self-specific knocked in BCRs, beta-gal was preferentially expressed in pre-B cells from the mice with self-specific BCRs. Retroviral-mediated expression of a cDNA encoding an IkappaBalpha superrepressor in primary bone marrow cultures resulted in diminished germline kappa and rearranged lambda transcripts but similar levels of RAG expression as compared with controls. We found that IRF4 transcripts were up-regulated in beta-gal+ pre-B cells. Because IRF4 is a target of NF-kappaB and is required for receptor editing, we suggest that NF-kappaB could be acting through IRF4 to regulate receptor editing.
###end p 2
###begin p 3
###xml 49 50 49 50 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">8</sup>
###xml 61 78 61 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib11">Cobb et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 247 265 247 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib6">Brack et al., 1978</xref>
###xml 267 281 267 281 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib46">Tonegawa, 1983</xref>
###xml 481 485 481 485 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IgHC</italic>
###xml 523 527 523 527 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IgLC</italic>
###xml 897 916 897 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib40">Schatz et al., 1989</xref>
###xml 918 940 918 940 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib33">Oettinger et al., 1990</xref>
###xml 1135 1155 1135 1155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib3">Bassing et al., 2002</xref>
B lymphocytes gain the potential to recognize >108 antigens (Cobb et al., 2006) by using a novel genetic mechanism called V(D)J recombination to generate a large repertoire of Ig heavy chain (IgHC) and Ig light chain (IgLC) variable domain exons (Brack et al., 1978; Tonegawa, 1983). Variable domain exons are composed of V, D, and J gene segments (IgHC) or V and J gene segments (IgLC). Successive stages of B cell development are defined by the ordered assembly of Ig genes; the IgHC locus rearranges in pro-B cells, the IgLC locus rearranges in pre-B cells, and the newly synthesized B cell receptor (BCR) is first expressed on the cell surface in immature B cells. V(D)J recombination begins with recognition and cleavage of a pair of recombination signal sequences (RSSs) flanking rearranging gene segments by the V(D)J recombinase composed of the lymphoid-restricted RAG1 and RAG2 proteins (Schatz et al., 1989; Oettinger et al., 1990). After RAG-mediated cleavage, the nonhomologous end-joining machinery repairs the DNA breaks, forming coding joints between the gene segments and signal joints between the two broken RSS ends (Bassing et al., 2002).
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 121 137 121 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Alt et al., 1987</xref>
###xml 510 533 502 525 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib44">Sen and Baltimore, 1986</xref>
###xml 650 669 638 657 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib19">Hayden et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 813 829 801 817 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib50">Wan et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 1248 1260 1204 1208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 1334 1352 1278 1296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib10">Chiao et al., 1994</xref>
Transcription of rearranging gene segments correlates with their developmentally regulated activation for rearrangement (Alt et al., 1987). Mutations that disrupt this "germline" transcription interfere with V(D)J recombination. This has led various workers to examine specific transcription factors for their ability to influence gene rearrangement and B cell development. One such factor, NF-kappaB, was initially discovered as a result of its ability to bind to a sequence in the Igkappa intronic enhancer (Sen and Baltimore, 1986). NF-kappaB is composed of homo- or heterodimers of five rel family members: RelA (p65), RelB, c-Rel, p50, and p52 (Hayden et al., 2006). Recent evidence suggests that additional proteins may associate with the rel proteins and influence the affinity and specificity of binding (Wan et al., 2007). Inactive NF-kappaB is sequestered in the cytoplasm bound to an inhibitory protein of the IkappaB family. Various signaling pathways result in the activation of a kinase that phosphorylates IkappaBalpha leading to its degradation. Once released from IkappaBalpha, NF-kappaB can translocate to the nucleus, bind DNA sequences, and regulate transcription. Remarkably, one of the transcriptional targets of NF-kappaB is IkappaBalpha itself, leading to negative-feedback regulation of NF-kappaB activation (Chiao et al., 1994).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 258 278 246 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib41">Scherer et al., 1996</xref>
###xml 280 300 268 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib32">O'Brien et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 302 322 290 310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">Bendall et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 546 563 526 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Feng et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 565 582 545 562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Jimi et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 615 636 595 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Igarashi et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 708 725 688 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Feng et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 757 778 733 754 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Igarashi et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 971 990 939 958 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">Sasaki et al., 2006</xref>
Previous work attempting to elucidate the role of NF-kappaB in B cell development has lead to contradictory conclusions. Expression of a mutant IkappaBalpha "superrepressor" was reported to prevent light chain gene rearrangements in a transformed cell line (Scherer et al., 1996; O'Brien et al., 1997; Bendall et al., 2001). Retrovirus-mediated expression of a similar IkappaBalpha superrepressor in primary B cells, however, revealed a different phenotype: a block at the pro-B stage of development as defined by cell surface marker expression (Feng et al., 2004; Jimi et al., 2005) or a complete lack of B cells (Igarashi et al., 2006). This block could be overcome by expression of an antiapoptosis gene (Feng et al., 2004) or by neutralizing TNF-alpha (Igarashi et al., 2006). Adding to this confusion, targeted disruption NEMO, a protein required in some pathways leading to IkappaBalpha degradation, did not seem to alter B cell development until the mature stage (Sasaki et al., 2006).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 314 327 310 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib31">Nemazee, 2006</xref>
###xml 407 428 403 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Casellas et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 549 553 545 549 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IgLC</italic>
###xml 660 663 648 651 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAG</italic>
###xml 703 724 687 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib49">Verkoczy et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 906 924 878 896 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Inlay et al., 2004</xref>
A potential role for NF-kappaB in the regulation of IgLC gene rearrangement was reported by workers studying receptor editing, a process in which engagement of the BCR on an immature B cell with self-antigen provokes further recombination in an effort to replace the offending variable exon with an innocuous one (Nemazee, 2006). At least 25% of the primary B cell repertoire is thought to undergo editing (Casellas et al., 2001). These workers found that in vitro cross-linking of BCR on immature B cells leads to an increase in RAG expression and IgLC gene rearrangement that correlates with NF-kappaB activation and the binding of NF-kappaB to sites in the RAG locus and the kappa intronic enhancer (Verkoczy et al., 2005). Another group, however, showed that targeted mutation of the NF-kappaB binding site in the kappa intronic enhancer had little if any apparent effect on V-to-Jkappa rearrangement (Inlay et al., 2004).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 132 136 128 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">lacZ</italic>
###xml 211 223 201 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 231 247 213 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Beg et al., 1995</xref>
###xml 258 270 240 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 109 114 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
In an attempt to clarify the role of NF-kappaB in B cell development, we took advantage of a targeted mutant mouse that expresses a lacZ complementary DNA (cDNA; encoding beta-galactosidase [beta-gal]) from the IkappaBalpha locus (Beg et al., 1995). Because IkappaBalpha is directly regulated by active nuclear NF-kappaB, we were able to analyze NF-kappaB activity using a fluorescent beta-gal substrate and multiparameter flow cytometry without perturbing its endogenous activity. Our results point to a role for NF-kappaB in the regulation of receptor editing.

In an attempt to assess NF-kappaB activity at various stages of B cell development, we analyzed IkappaBalpha+/lacZ mice. Although these mice express less IkappaBalpha protein than wild-type mice, they display no obvious phenotype (Beg et al., 1995). To confirm that beta-gal activity correlates with IkappaBalpha expression in IkappaBalpha+/lacZ reporter mice, IkappaBalpha transcripts were analyzed in RNA purified from pre-B cells sorted for beta-gal activity as assayed using the fluorescent substrate fluorescein di-beta-d-galactopyranoside (FDG). Using real-time PCR, we determined that the beta-gal+ pre-B cells express 13-fold more IkappaBalpha messenger RNA (mRNA) as compared with beta-gal- cells (Fig. 1 A). The greater amounts of IkappaBalpha transcripts in beta-gal+ cells confirms that beta-gal detected using FDG is a suitable reporter for IkappaBalpha expression in IkappaBalpha+/lacZ mice.

Several groups have shown that IkappaBalpha transcription is regulated by NF-kappaB activity (Gugasyan et al., 2000; Ghosh and Karin, 2002; Hoffmann et al., 2002; Hayden et al., 2006; Hayden and Ghosh, 2008). Therefore, we hypothesized that beta-gal activity also reports NF-kappaB activity in IkappaBalpha+/lacZ mice. We confirmed this using an Ableson virus-transformed pro-B cell line made from IkappaBalpha+/lacZ mice. After LPS treatment, beta-gal was up-regulated (Fig. 1 B) and p65 was relocalized from the cytoplasm to the nucleus in this cell line (Fig. 1 C). beta-gal activity was observed in IkappaBalpha+/lacZ Ableson cells within 2 h of LPS treatment and the level of beta-gal activity increased with longer treatment, reaching a plateau after 8 h (Fig. 1 B). To determine the half-life of beta-gal in this system, we treated the IkappaBalpha+/lacZ Ableson cell line with LPS for 9 h, washed out the LPS, and recultured the cells in the presence of the protein synthesis inhibitor cycloheximide (Fig. 1 D). Using this approach, we found the half-life of beta-gal to be 7.55 h. These experiments demonstrate that known activators of NF-kappaB can also activate beta-gal in IkappaBalpha+/lacZ cells and that beta-gal is reporting recent NF-kappaB activity.

###begin title 13
Increased light chain gene rearrangements correlate with IkappaBalpha expression in pre-B cells
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 104 110 93 99 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/lacZ</sup>
###xml 92 110 89 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;<sup>+/lacZ</sup></italic>
###xml 151 163 140 144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 403 411 377 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Fig. 2 A</xref>
###xml 749 757 699 707 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Fig. 2 B</xref>
###xml 1032 1038 967 973 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 1169 1181 1100 1104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
We used multiparameter flow cytometry to analyze beta-gal expression in cells isolated from IkappaBalpha+/lacZ bone marrow to determine the pattern of IkappaBalpha transcription and, thus, NF-kappaB activity during B cell development. We observed stage-specific beta-gal expression; it was low in pro-B cells, peaked at the pre-B stage, remained high in immature B cells, and was low in mature B cells (Fig. 2 A). The IkappaBalpha-positive and -negative subsets of pre-B cells were chosen for further analysis. We used real-time RT-PCR to quantify various light chain locus transcripts. A 10-fold greater amount of Vlambda1,2-Clambda-rearranged transcript was observed in beta-gal-expressing pre-B cells compared with beta-gal-negative pre-B cells (Fig. 2 B). Germline kappa transcripts are transcripts through the unrearranged kappa locus and correlate with the accessibility of the locus to the recombinase. There was a twofold increase in both germline and rearranged kappa transcripts in the pre-B cells with beta-gal activity (Fig. 2). These data indicate that increased kappa accessibility and, presumably, increased light chain gene rearrangement correlate with IkappaBalpha expression and NF-kappaB activity in pre-B cells.

We analyzed RAG1 and RAG2 transcripts in the beta-gal-positive and -negative populations to assess whether differences in expression of these proteins might be responsible for the differences observed in light chain rearrangements. ###begin title 17
Receptor-editing markers correlate with IkappaBalpha expression
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 522 530 507 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig3">Fig. 3 A</xref>

###xml 578 595 563 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib48">Vela et al., 2008</xref>

###xml 707 708 682 683 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>

###xml 806 818 781 785 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
Considering what process might divide the pre-B cell compartment into nonequivalent subpopulations, we proceeded to test the idea that NF-kappaB might be specifically activated in cells undergoing receptor editing. To test this idea, we examined beta-gal-positive and -negative pre-B cells for various markers of receptor editing. Rearrangements involving the RS element, which lies 25 kb 3' of Ckappa, and either a Vkappa gene segment or the IRS sequence in the J-C interval are considered hallmarks of receptor editing (Fig. 3 A; Tiegs et al., 1993; Retter and Nemazee, 1998; Vela et al., 2008). Using a PCR assay, we detected more RS-IRS and RS-Vkappa rearrangements in the beta-gal+ than in the beta-gal- pre-B cell population (Fig. 3 B). Thus, RS rearrangements are enriched in pre-B cells expressing IkappaBalpha.

###xml 1086 1098 1044 1048 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
The relative amounts of primary and secondary kappa rearrangement reaction intermediates can also help identify cell populations undergoing receptor editing. Primary kappa rearrangements occur on germline alleles. After this initial Vkappa-to-Jkappa rearrangement, an upstream Vkappa can rearrange to a downstream Jkappa if the primary rearrangement is nonproductive, if it is unable to pair with the preexisting IgHC, or if it contributes to a self-specific BCR. These are termed secondary rearrangements and are increased in cells undergoing receptor editing. Ligation-mediated PCR (LM-PCR; Schlissel et al., 1993) was used to detect reaction intermediates associated with primary and secondary Igkappa rearrangements in DNA purified from beta-gal-positive and -negative pre-B cells. In both populations, similar amounts of primary Igkappa rearrangement intermediates were detected, but secondary rearrangement intermediates were highly enriched in the beta-gal+ subpopulation (Fig. 3 C). Thus, secondary Igkappa rearrangements, which are enhanced in receptor editing, correlate with IkappaBalpha expression.

The BCR is down-regulated from the cell surface during receptor editing (Tze et al., 2000). One feature that distinguishes cells undergoing receptor editing from proper pre-B cells is cytoplasmic kappa expression (Pelanda et al., 1997). To examine this feature of receptor editing, we cultured bone marrow from an innocuous BCR knockin mouse (B1-8 alpha-HEL-kappa, see subsequent section), in the presence or absence of anti-IgM F(ab')2 fragments, that mimic a signal inducing receptor editing (Hertz and Nemazee, 1997). As expected, the cultures where IgM was cross-linked down-regulated IgM off the cell surface, resulting in levels of cytoplasmic Igkappa greater than those seen in sIgM- cells from unperturbed cultures (Fig. 4 A). The level of cytoplasmic Ig kappa, however, is less than that found on sIgM+ cells cultured in the absence of F(ab')2 fragments (Fig. 4 A).

We proceeded to analyze wild-type mice for cytoplasmic kappa expression in three different B cell populations: CD19+IgM-, IgMlow, and IgMhigh cells (Fig. 4 B). We found that only a small percentage of CD19+IgM- cells express cytoplasmic kappa (13%; Fig. 4 B). We suggest that this cytoplasmic Igkappa+ subpopulation of sIgM- cells is undergoing receptor editing. We then analyzed IkappaBalpha+/lacZ bone marrow. In this same CD19+IgM- population, we detected cytoplasmic Igkappa in beta-gal+ cells but not in beta-gal- cells (Fig. 4 C). This result implies that, in the pro-/pre-B cell compartment, cells undergoing receptor editing are beta-gal+. In wild-type mice, the majority of IgMlow and IgMhigh cells express cytoplasmic Igkappa (74 and 87%, respectively; Fig. 4 B). IgMlow cells are either early immature cells (in the process of up-regulating IgM expression) or cells undergoing receptor editing (in the process of down-regulating surface IgM). Most IgMlow cells undergoing receptor editing will be cytoplasmic Igkappa positive because these cells once expressed this protein on the cell surface and are now in the process of down-regulating it or deleting the offending Igkappa variable exon. Newly generated innocuous immature B cells, which are also IgMlow, will not have synthesized high levels of Igkappa protein, so these cells do not necessarily express high levels of cytoplasmic Igkappa. High levels of cytoplasmic Igkappa were expressed in the majority of the beta-gal+IgMlow cells but were only expressed in approximately30% of beta-gal-IgMlow cells (Fig. 4 C). This suggests that the beta-gal+IgMlow cells are receptor-editing cells and beta-gal-IgMlow cells may be newly generated immature B cells. The majority of IgMhigh cells express cytoplasmic Igkappa, regardless of beta-gal expression levels (Fig. 4 C). This is not surprising, as cells with high IgM expression are continuously synthesizing immunoglobulin, accounting for the cytoplasmic Igkappa expression. Thus, expression of cytoplasmic Igkappa is associated with IkappaBalpha expression in populations of cells where receptor-editing signals are present.

To further test the idea that NF-kappaB is involved in receptor editing, IkappaBalpha+/lacZ mice were crossed to various BCR knockin mice. The B1-8 heavy chain, when paired with the anti-HEL-kappa light chain (alpha-HEL-kappa), produces an innocuous BCR (Casellas et al., 2001). Specific mutations in the B1-8 heavy chain result in autoreactivity when paired with alpha-HEL-kappa. Thus, B1-8high alpha-HEL-kappa and B1-8low alpha-HEL-kappa mice express mutated B1-8 heavy chains and self-specific BCRs (Casellas et al., 2001). There are few pro-/pre-B cells in B1-8 alpha-HEL-kappa because these mice express an innocuous BCR and rapidly generate immature and mature B cells (Fig. 5 A). B1-8low alpha-HEL-kappa and B1-8high alpha-HEL-kappa B cells also rush through pro- and pre-B cell development, but because the knocked-in BCR in these cells is self-specific, it is down-regulated at the immature stage during receptor editing. IkappaBalpha+/lacZ B1-8 alpha-HEL-kappa mice, with an innocuous BCR, express almost no beta-gal in pro-B, pre-B, and immature B cells (CD19+IgD- gate). Thus, when cells do not undergo receptor editing, the IkappaBalpha reporter is not expressed. However, in two different mice where a majority of B cells undergo receptor editing, the majority of B cells express the IkappaBalpha reporter.

RPS3, a component of the 40S ribosome subunit, was recently found to interact with p65, increase the activity and binding affinity of NF-kappaB, and influence the binding site preferences of the NF-kappaB complex (Wan et al., 2007). Various data implicate RPS3 in the regulation of IkappaBalpha and the kappa locus. Chromatin immunoprecipitation assays show that this ribosomal protein binds to the IkappaBalpha promoter. In addition, a small interfering RNA directed against RPS3 inhibits IkappaBalpha expression and a RPS3-specific short hairpin RNA reduces kappa expression. As with p65, RPS3 is localized in the cytoplasm but enters the nucleus when it is participating in transcriptional regulation (Wan et al., 2007). We used immunofluorescence microscopy to determine the localization of RPS3 and p65 in a mixture of pro-B, pre-B, and immature B cells (B220+IgD-) from B1-8 alpha-HEL-kappa and B1-8low alpha-HEL-kappa bone marrow. Interestingly, the B1-8 alpha-HEL-kappa cells contained mostly cytoplasmic RPS3 expression (only 17% of these cells scored positive for nuclear RPS3), whereas the majority (68% of the cells scored) of B1-8low alpha-HEL-kappa cells contained nuclear RPS3 expression (Fig. 5 C and ). Although there was more nuclear p65 in the B1-8low alpha-HEL-kappa cells compared with the B1-8 alpha-HEL-kappa cells, the difference is not as striking as RPS3 (Fig. S3). These data imply that RPS3 could be acting to regulate NF-kappaB during receptor editing.

To determine if the differences observed between light chain locus transcription and rearrangement in the IkappaBalpha-positive and -negative populations were dependent on NF-kappaB activity, we took advantage of a retrovirus expressing an IkappaBalpha superrepressor (IkappaBalphaDeltaN). IkappaBalphaDeltaN is missing amino acids 1-36 of the N terminus and cannot be phosphorylated or ubiquitinated. Therefore, it remains bound to NF-kappaB dimers and inhibits activation of the classical NF-kappaB pathway (Scherer et al., 1996). Pro-B cells in IL-7-dependent wild-type bone marrow cultures were infected with an IkappaBalphaDeltaN-expressing retrovirus or an empty vector control. We failed to detect a block in pro-/pre-B development in IkappaBalphaDeltaN-expressing cells using the surface CD19 expression and cytoplasmic mu (Fig. 6 A). There was, however, a twofold reduction in IgM+ cells among those expressing IkappaBalphaDeltaN (Fig. 6 A). RNA was purified and transcript levels in the IkappaBalphaDeltaN-infected pro-/pre-B cells were compared with empty vector-infected pro-/pre-B cells. RAG expression was comparable in these two populations (Fig. 6 B). In the IkappaBalphaDeltaN-infected pro-/pre-B cells, a large decrease in Vlambda1,2-Clambda-rearranged transcripts was observed (7-fold), as well as a decrease in germline kappa transcripts (10-fold; Fig. 6 B). These results indicate that B cells with diminished NF-kappaB activity have diminished lambda light chain gene rearrangements and kappa accessibility. This confirms our results from the IkappaBalpha+/lacZ reporter mice that NF-kappaB influences light chain status without changing RAG expression.

To investigate potential targets of regulation by NF-kappaB that might play a role in receptor editing, transcript levels of multiple NF-kappaB target genes were analyzed in RNA purified from sorted beta-gal-positive and -negative pre-B cells. Bcl-2 family members are antiapoptosis genes and targets of NF-kappaB (Catz and Johnson, 2001). Mice that contain self-specific BCR transgenes, as well as a Bcl-2 transgene, display enhanced receptor editing (Lang et al., 1997), suggesting that survival regulated by this gene may be involved in receptor editing. We used RT-PCR to compare levels of transcripts from the antiapoptosis genes Bcl-2, Bcl-X, and Mcl-1 in beta-gal-positive and -negative pre-B cell cDNA. There was no increase in these antiapoptosis genes in the beta-gal-positive cells (). These data indicate that increased survival, mediated by these antiapoptosis genes, is unlikely to be responsible for the increased receptor editing observed in the beta-gal-positive cells.

###begin title 35
DISCUSSION
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 205 222 201 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;-lacZ</italic>
Receptor editing is stimulated in immature B cells by BCR recognition of autoantigen, which results in down-modulation of BCR from the cell surface. BCR engagement is known to activate NF-kappaB. Using an IkappaBalpha-lacZ knockin reporter allele, we detected a subpopulation of pre-B cells that contains active nuclear NF-kappaB and found that this same population expresses markers of receptor editing. We propose that this correlation between NF-kappaB activity and receptor editing may indicate a functional role for this transcription factor in a key mechanism of self-tolerance.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 148 168 144 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib41">Scherer et al., 1996</xref>
###xml 170 190 166 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib32">O'Brien et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 192 212 188 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">Bendall et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 259 280 255 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Igarashi et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 282 301 278 297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">Sasaki et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 824 846 812 834 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib12">Derudder et al. (2009)</xref>
###xml 863 867 851 855 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NEMO</italic>
###xml 869 873 857 861 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IKK1</italic>
###xml 879 883 867 871 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IKK2</italic>
Previous results have suggested various roles for NF-kappaB during B cell development, ranging from involvement in light chain gene rearrangements (Scherer et al., 1996; O'Brien et al., 1997; Bendall et al., 2001) to being dispensable for B cell development (Igarashi et al., 2006; Sasaki et al., 2006). Because receptor editing involves a specific subset of light chain gene rearrangements, our data imply that NF-kappaB could be involved in light chain rearrangements without being required for B cell development, which is consistent with seemingly contradictory results. In addition, the role of NF-kappaB in receptor editing could be either direct or indirect, and its activation in pre-B cells might be via the classical or nonclassical pathway or, perhaps, even a novel pathway. Suggestive of the latter possibility, Derudder et al. (2009) have found that NEMO, IKK1, and IKK2 are all dispensable for receptor editing. Further experiments will be required to determine whether alternative pathways of NF-kappaB activation exist in developing B cells.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 244 252 237 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Ig&#955;</italic>
###xml 459 464 435 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IRF-4</italic>
###xml 601 623 568 590 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib36">Pongubala et al., 1992</xref>
###xml 625 647 592 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Eisenbeis et al., 1995</xref>
###xml 686 705 653 672 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib34">Pathak et al., 2008</xref>
Much of the data presented in this paper are correlative; nuclear NF-kappaB activity correlates with beta-gal expression, which in turn correlates with numerous markers of receptor editing in phenotypic pre-B cells including elevated levels of Iglambda rearrangement and transcription, increased cytoplasmic Igkappa expression, increased Jkappa locus RS rearrangements, and increased Jkappa RSS replacement breaks. We also observed a several-fold increase in IRF-4 mRNA levels, which is consistent with prior studies showing that IRF-4 plays a role in Igkappa and Iglambda locus activation enhancers (Pongubala et al., 1992; Eisenbeis et al., 1995) and is involved in receptor editing (Pathak et al., 2008). We provided a critical test of these correlations by examining NF-kappaB activity in two knockin models of receptor editing, in each case finding that nuclear NF-kappaB activity is elevated as compared with cells expressing a knocked-in innocuous receptor.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 123 124 111 112 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 176 183 164 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Ig&#954;</italic>
###xml 199 207 183 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Ig&#955;</italic>
In addition, we found that overexpression of a cDNA encoding an IkappaBalphaDeltaN mutant suppressed the generation of sIgM+ immature B cells and diminished levels of germline Igkappa and rearranged Iglambda transcripts in a short-term primary cell culture system. The results of this perturbation of NF-kappaB activity are consistent with a causal role for this factor in receptor editing.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 259 264 251 256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Bcl-2</italic>
###xml 266 272 258 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Bcl-xL</italic>
###xml 278 283 270 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mcl-1</italic>
###xml 583 584 568 569 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 597 598 579 580 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
It is possible that the activation of NF-kappaB in a population of cells undergoing receptor editing is serving a purpose other than the induction of editing. For example, NF-kappaB is known to regulate the expression of various antiapoptotic genes including Bcl-2, Bcl-xL, and Mcl-1. Thus, it is possible that NF-kappaB's role in editing may be to promote a sufficient period of cell survival to allow for ongoing V(D)J recombination activity to result in the generation of a self-tolerant BCR. To test this idea, we compared the levels of antiapoptosis gene mRNA in sorted beta-gal+ and beta-gal- pre-B cells. We found no significant differences in expression levels between these cell populations (Fig. S1).
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 174 193 174 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib43">Schram et al., 2008</xref>
###xml 326 346 322 342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib35">Pelanda et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 431 432 424 425 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 480 486 469 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Fig. 4</xref>
###xml 564 565 549 550 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 568 569 553 554 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 713 733 698 718 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib43">Schram et al. (2008)</xref>
###xml 850 862 835 839 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;</italic>
###xml 867 872 844 849 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Bcl-x</italic>
A series of elegant studies from the Behrens laboratory has led to the suggestion that receptor editing is associated with the loss of IgM from the surface of editing cells (Schram et al., 2008). Others have found that cytoplasmic Igkappa expression is elevated after BCR down-regulation in cells undergoing receptor editing (Pelanda et al., 1997). These results are consistent with our observation that a high fraction of beta-gal+ pre-B cells express cytoplasmic Igkappa chain (Fig. 4). We found that cytoplasmic Igkappa levels increase in the population of CD19+IgM- cells that results when immature B cells are treated with cross-linking anti-IgM antibodies, an in vitro mimic of the receptor-editing signal. Schram et al. (2008) showed that this loss of surface BCR in editing cells was associated with increased levels of transcription of both IkappaBalpha and Bcl-x, which are known NF-kappaB target genes.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 65 89 65 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib37">Retter and Nemazee, 1998</xref>
###xml 91 108 91 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib48">Vela et al., 2008</xref>
###xml 198 215 198 215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Chen et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 217 237 217 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib35">Pelanda et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 239 265 239 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib1">Ait-Azzouzene et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 383 400 378 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib48">Vela et al., 2008</xref>
###xml 674 679 659 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 184 189 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 268 272 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
RS rearrangements are considered a hallmark of receptor editing (Retter and Nemazee, 1998; Vela et al., 2008). Large increases in these rearrangements are observed in receptor-editing mouse models (Chen et al., 1997; Pelanda et al., 1997; Ait-Azzouzene et al., 2005). Mice lacking the RS sequence display decreased receptor editing and fewer B cells expressing a lambda light chain (Vela et al., 2008). An increase in RS rearrangement to both IRS and Vkappa gene segments correlates with beta-gal expression in our experiments. The increase in RS rearrangement indicates that a substantial number of cells in the beta-gal-positive pre-B cell population have inactivated the kappa locus, providing further evidence that this population is undergoing receptor editing.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 87 93 75 81 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/lacZ</sup>
###xml 75 93 71 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I&#954;B&#945;<sup>+/lacZ</sup></italic>
###xml 245 248 221 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAG</italic>
###xml 395 398 371 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAG</italic>
###xml 474 477 450 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAG</italic>
###xml 525 546 501 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib24">Jankovic et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 548 569 524 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib49">Verkoczy et al., 2005</xref>
In both experimental systems used to study NF-kappaB activity, analysis of IkappaBalpha+/lacZ pre-B cells, and retroviral infection of cultured B cells with IkappaBalphaDeltaN, the change in light chain status was not accompanied by a change in RAG expression. This data implies that an increase in RAGs is not responsible for receptor-editing rearrangements. There are two hypotheses regarding RAG expression during receptor editing: either an increase or a persistence of RAG expression upon recognition of an autoantigen (Jankovic et al., 2004; Verkoczy et al., 2005). We interpret our data as being consistent with the idea that RAG expression is maintained, not necessarily increased, during receptor editing.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 142 158 134 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib50">Wan et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 315 318 307 310 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 311 334 303 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">B1-8<sup>low</sup> &#945;-HEL-&#954;</italic>
###xml 410 430 394 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">B1-8 &#945;-HEL-&#954;</italic>
###xml 305 309 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 384 388 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
RPS3 interacts with p65, increases the activity and binding affinity of NF-kappaB, and influences the binding sites of the NF-kappaB complex (Wan et al., 2007). Using an immunofluorescence microscopy assay, we found increased nuclear RPS3 in pro-B, pre-B, and immature B cells from receptor-editing model mice (B1-8low alpha-HEL-kappa) compared with the same population of cells from mice with innocuous BCRs (B1-8 alpha-HEL-kappa). Preliminary data suggests that knocking down RPS3 causes a defect in receptor editing (unpublished data). We infer from this data that RPS3 is influencing binding sites of NF-kappaB during receptor editing and we are currently examining this issue in greater detail.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
Our results implicate NF-kappaB in the regulation of receptor editing. These data imply that NF-kappaB is not required for B cell development to take place but, instead, contributes to a key process that prevents the expression of self specific BCRs.
###end p 45
