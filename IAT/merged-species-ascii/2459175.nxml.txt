Adaptive evolution of SCML1 in primates, a gene involved in male reproduction
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
This is an Open Access article distributed under the terms of the Creative Commons Attribution License (), which permits unrestricted use, distribution, and reproduction in any medium, provided the original work is properly cited.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 94 100 94 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 195 201 195 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 288 294 288 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
Genes involved in male reproduction are often the targets of natural and/or sexual selection. SCML1 is a recently identified X-linked gene with preferential expression in testis. To test whether SCML1 is the target of selection in primates, we sequenced and compared the coding region of SCML1 in major primate lineages, and we observed the signature of positive selection in primates.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 50 56 50 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 231 246 231 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML2 and SCMH1</italic>
###xml 268 274 268 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 572 578 572 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 685 691 685 691 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 448 463 <span type="species:ncbi:9544">rhesus macaques</span>
We analyzed the molecular evolutionary pattern of SCML1 in diverse primate species, and we observed a strong signature of adaptive evolution which is caused by Darwinian positive selection. When compared with the paralogous genes (SCML2 and SCMH1) of the same family, SCML1 evolved rapidly in primates, which is consistent with the proposed adaptive evolution, suggesting functional modification after gene duplication. Gene expression analysis in rhesus macaques shows that during male sexual maturation, there is a significant expression change in testis, implying that SCML1 likely plays a role in testis development and spermatogenesis. The immunohistochemical data indicates that SCML1 is preferentially expressed in germ stem cells of testis, therefore likely involved in spermatogenesis.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 26 32 26 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
The adaptive evolution of SCML1 in primates provides a new case in understanding the evolutionary process of genes involved in primate male reproduction.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 89 90 89 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 91 92 91 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
Proteins involved in sexual reproduction often evolve rapidly due to positive selection [1-4]. Although the selective forces are unclear, a variety of hypotheses have been proposed including mate choice, intra-sexual competition and sexual conflict, which are different forms of sexual selection. The rapid evolution of these proteins may contribute to several important biological aspects such as reproduction and speciation. It has long been recognized that gene duplication is a major source of genomic novelties. Therefore, the newly duplicated genes involved in reproduction are likely the targets of natural and/or sexual selection.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 41 42 41 42 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 115 120 115 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1</italic>
###xml 194 200 194 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 274 280 274 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 358 359 358 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 71 76 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 180 185 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 320 325 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Using exon trapping, van de Vosse et al [5] identified a novel gene in human located on Xp22, named as SCM-like-1 (SCML1), which is similar with the Scm gene in Drosophila. In the human genome, SCML1 spans 18 kb and contains 8 exons. Northern blot analysis detected a major SCML1 transcript of approximately 3-kb in all human adult and fetal tissues tested [5].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 0 6 0 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 220 221 220 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 222 224 222 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 278 280 278 280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 281 283 281 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 527 529 527 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 530 532 530 532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 535 541 535 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 402 406 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 652 661 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 666 673 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
SCML1 gene is a polycomb group (PcG) gene. Most of the PcG genes are expressed throughout embryonic, larval and pupal development, and are required continuously to maintain restricted homeotic expression in Drosophila. [6-10]. Most mammalian PcG genes have Drosophila homologs [11,12]. Compared to Drosophila, the mammalian PcG genes have acquired novel functions during evolution because PcG knockout mice exhibit numerous phenotypes including hematopoietic defects, neural crest defects, cardiac anomalies, and sex reversal [12,13]. SCML1 is likely a recently duplicated gene during mammalian evolution due to the absence of orthologs in Drosophila, zebrafish and chicken.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 46 52 46 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML2 </italic>
###xml 56 61 56 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCMH1</italic>
###xml 146 148 146 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 171 173 171 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 189 195 189 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCMH1 </italic>
###xml 256 258 256 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 259 261 259 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 349 351 349 351 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 434 436 434 436 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 439 445 439 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML2 </italic>
###xml 483 485 483 485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 515 521 515 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML2 </italic>
###xml 632 634 632 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 640 646 640 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML2 </italic>
###xml 650 656 650 656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCMH1 </italic>
###xml 686 690 686 690 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCM </italic>
###xml 787 793 787 793 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
In the SCM family, there are other two genes, SCML2 and SCMH1, which have orthologs in all vertebrate species and are located on chromosome Xp22 [14] and chromosome 1p34 [15] respectively. SCMH1 is a core component of polycomb repressive complex 1 (PRC1) [16-18] which is involved in the maintenance of repression and can block chromatin remodeling[17], and it plays an important role in regulation of homeotic genes in embryogenesis[19]. SCML2 is also involved in PRC1's regulation[20]. A recent study showed that SCML2 is over-expressed in acute myeloid leukaemia, suggesting its role in differentiation and cell cycle regulation[21]. As SCML2 and SCMH1 are the ancient copies in the SCM family, they would serve as the ideal reference genes when dissecting the molecular evolution of SCML1 in primates.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 82 88 82 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 170 172 170 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 279 285 279 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 372 378 372 378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 148 153 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 158 168 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
Through a genome-wide comparison, we have identified 34 candidate genes including SCML1 that showed rapid nonsynonymous sequence divergence between human and chimpanzee [22], therefore an implication of adaptive evolution of these genes during primate evolution. To test whether SCML1 is the target of selection in primates, we sequenced and compared the coding region of SCML1 in major primate lineages, and we observed the signature of positive selection.
###end p 13

The major lineages of primates were sampled, including three great ape species (chimpanzee-Pan troglodytes, gorilla-Gorilla gorilla and orangutan-Pongo pygmaeus), two lesser ape species (white-browed gibbon-Bunopithecus hoolock and white-cheeked gibbon-Nomascus leucogenys), two Old World monkey species (rhesus macaque-Macaca mulatta and Yunnan snub-nosed monkey-Rhinopithecus bieti) and one New World monkey species (common marmoset-Callithrix jacchus). ###end title 17
###begin p 18

All the samples were sequenced for the full length coding region of SCML1. Primers for all the primates were designed by aligning the published sequences of human (Esembl ID: ENSG00000047634) and macaque (Ensembl ID: ENSMMUG00000012899, Ensemble genome browser [24]). The primer sequences are listed [see Additional file 1].
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 274 280 274 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
PCRs were performed with rTaq under conditions recommended by the manufacturer (TAKARA Company). Sequencing was performed in both directions with forward and reverse primers using the BigDye terminator sequencing kit on an ABI 3130 automated sequencer. There are 8 exons in SCML1 gene, and the first exon is non-translational, therefore, not sequenced in this study. Overlapping chromatogram files retrieved from the sequencer were analyzed and edited using the SeqMan program in the Lasergene software package (DNASTAR Inc).

###begin title 20
Sequence analysis
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 76 78 76 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 79 81 79 81 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 309 311 309 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 312 314 312 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 369 371 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 390 392 390 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ds</italic>
###xml 413 414 413 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</italic>
###xml 414 415 414 415 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">N</italic>
###xml 414 415 414 415 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>N</italic></sub>
###xml 541 543 541 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 189 204 <span type="species:ncbi:9483">common marmoset</span>
The DNA sequences were aligned with the CLUSTALW program implanted in Mega [25,26] and checked manually. There are several in-dels (do not change the reading frame) in the coding region of common marmoset, and those sites were removed in the sequence analysis. The known phylogeny of primate species was used[23,27]. The ancestral sequences were inferred by PAML 3.15 [28]. The synonymous (ds) and nonsynonymous (dN) substitution rates of each branch were calculated with the use of the maximum likelihood method under the free-ratio model [28].
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Test of selection
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 117 118 117 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</italic>
###xml 118 119 118 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">N</italic>
###xml 118 119 118 119 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>N</italic></sub>
###xml 120 121 120 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</italic>
###xml 121 123 121 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S </italic>
###xml 121 123 121 123 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>S </italic></sub>
###xml 436 438 432 434 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dN</italic>
###xml 439 441 435 437 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dS</italic>
###xml 471 473 467 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 474 476 470 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 558 564 554 560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 653 659 645 647 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#969; </italic>
###xml 715 716 703 704 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p</italic>
###xml 724 729 712 713 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#969;</italic>
###xml 775 776 759 760 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p</italic>
###xml 779 780 763 764 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p</italic>
###xml 786 787 770 771 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p</italic>
###xml 799 804 783 784 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#969;</italic>
###xml 1093 1095 1073 1075 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 1096 1098 1076 1078 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 1529 1531 1497 1499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 1717 1719 1685 1687 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
Positive selection can be inferred from a higher proportion of nonsynonymous than synonymous substitutions per site (dN/dS > 1). To detect specific amino acid sites under positive selection, we applied the site models in the codeml program of the PAML package. Using this set of models, we obtained the log likelihood estimates (L) of a tree topology under models that impose alternative assumptions in terms of rate variation (omega = dN/dS) over different codon sites [29,30]. The model M0 was used to evaluate the general sequence substitution pattern of SCML1 in primates assuming a constant omega ratio across codon sites. M0 estimates the overall omega for the data. The M1a model estimates single parameter, p0, with omega0 = 0, and the remaining sites with frequency p1 (p1 = 1-p0) assuming omega1 = 1. We first compared model M0 with M1a to determine which model is more realistic for the data and M1a tuned out to be the better one. Then we compared model M2a (selection) and M1a (nearly neutral) to test if invoking of positive selection in model M2a would better explain the data [31,32]. It was suggested that under certain scenarios, a beta distribution of omega is more realistic, therefore, we also conducted the selection test by comparing model M7 and M8, in which a beta distribution of omegawas assumed. We also conducted a more stringent test by comparing M8 with M8a. The LRTs between nested models were conducted by comparing twice the difference of the log-likelihood values (2DeltaL) between two models [32]. If the log likelihood test suggests the presence of sites under positive selection, we then identified these sites by using a Bayesian method to estimate posterior probabilities (P) [33].
###end p 23

###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 77 79 77 79 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 159 161 155 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 178 179 174 175 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</italic>
###xml 179 181 175 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">N </italic>
###xml 179 181 175 177 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>N </italic></sub>
###xml 185 186 181 182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</italic>
###xml 186 188 182 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S </italic>
###xml 186 188 182 184 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>S </italic></sub>
###xml 317 319 313 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>

The ratios of dN and dS were estimated by using PAML3.15, and the Z test(data not show) was used to evaluate the ratio difference between each branches [26]. Similar neutrality tests described above were used in comparing the evolutionary patterns among the three genes.
###end p 26
###begin p 27

The rapid protein sequence evolution can be explained either by Darwinian positive selection or by relaxation of negative (or purifying) selection. To test the hypothesis that they could be under Darwinian positive selection, we conducted the analysis for positive selection at individual amino acid sites using maximum likelihood models by estimating omega values[28,29,37]. The results are presented in Table 1. We first conducted the analysis using M0. In the M0 analysis, the log likelihood L is -2619.84, and the estimated omega = 1.169, implying that there are varied evolutionary forces acting on the amino acid sites of SCML1 (neutral, negative selection and/or positive selection). Model M1a (nearly neutral) assumes two site classes in the sequence (0 < omega < 1 and omega1 = 1 fixed), and is significantly better than M0 (2DeltaL = 13.26, P = 0.0002). Therefore, we use M1a as the null hypothesis in detecting selection.

We next compared M1a and M2a (selection model), and M2a fits the data significantly better than M1a (2DeltaL = 26.52 P < 0.0001), a strong signature of positive selection on SCML1 in primates. ###xml 692 693 692 693 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S3">3</xref>
###xml 878 884 878 884 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 969 975 969 975 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 454 459 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 664 670 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 822 836 <span type="species:ncbi:9544">rhesus macaque</span>
Having shown that the evolution of SCML1 in primates is consistent with positive selection, by dissecting its expression pattern, we attempt to understand the driving force and the functional consequence of selection. We first detect the expression pattern of SCML1 by testing 12 different tissue types in 1-2 year old macaques and the result is shown in Figure 4. Testis and pancreas have the highest expression when compared with the other tissues. In human, according to the micro-array expression data, SCML1 is also preferentially expressed in testis [39]. The abundant expression of SCML1 in testis confirms its involvement in male reproduction. However, in humans [see Additional file 3], liver, fetal liver, and pituitary all show higher expression than pancreas, which is different from the expression pattern of rhesus macaque, implicating functional modifications of SCML1 during primate evolution. This is consistent with the proposed positive selection on SCML1 in primates.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 34 40 34 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 0 61 0 61 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">The relative expression levels of <italic>SCML1 </italic>in different tissues.</bold>

###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 50 56 50 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 163 168 163 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">i.e. </italic>
###xml 324 329 324 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1</italic>
###xml 438 439 438 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 521 522 521 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 547 553 547 553 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 710 712 710 712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 713 715 713 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 1003 1009 1003 1009 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 1075 1089 <span type="species:ncbi:9544">rhesus macaque</span>
We then detect the potential expression change of SCML1 during male sexual maturation and a significant change was detected in testis. We compared two age groups, i.e. 1-2 year old monkeys (sexually immature) and 7-8 year old monkeys (sexually matured). The result indicates that both age groups have abundant expression of SCML1, and there is a significant reduction (about 60%) in the adult group (p = 0.015, two-tailed t test) (Figure 5). We next carried out immunohistochemical analysis of the two age groups (Figure 6). The result shows that SCML1 is preferentially expressed in the germ stem cells of testis (spermatogonial stem cells in 1-2 year old monkeys, and spermatogonia in 7-8 year old monkeys) [40-48], and again the 1-2 year old group has higher expression than the adult group. The higher expression in the 1-2 year old monkeys is probably due to the relative abundance of stem cells at the sexually immature stage. The preferential expression in the stem cells of testis suggests that SCML1 is likely involved in spermatogenesis during sexual maturation in rhesus macaque.
###end p 53

###begin title 56
Discussion
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 20 26 20 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 287 288 287 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 289 290 289 290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 291 293 291 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 294 296 294 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 337 343 337 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
We demonstrate that SCML1 evolves rapidly in primates, which was caused by Darwinian positive selection. Genes expressed exclusively or preferentially in testis are likely involved in male reproduction and have been shown to evolve rapidly under positive selection in previous studies ([1-3,49-55]. Our observation of rapid evolution in SCML1 provides another example of male reproductive gene under Darwinian positive selection in primates.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 147 148 147 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 189 194 189 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1</italic>
###xml 195 197 195 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 355 356 355 356 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 482 484 482 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 525 527 525 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 528 530 528 530 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B63">63</xref>
###xml 599 601 599 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>
###xml 602 604 602 604 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B65">65</xref>
###xml 678 684 678 684 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 843 844 843 844 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 1108 1114 1108 1114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 1220 1226 1220 1226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
Darwinian positive selection may lead to functional changes of the target genes during evolution. The SAM domain located in the C terminal (Figure 1) is the only known functional domain of SCML1[56]. Among primates, the amino acid sequences of the SAM domains are relatively conserved across species and there is one site under positive selection (Figure 1). The SAM domain is known to exhibit diverse protein-protein interaction modes, and is involved in developmental regulation [57]. Through functional domain prediction [56-63], besides of the SAM domain, we identified a total of six fragments[64,65] (amino acid position 1-9, 13-23, 72-79, 88-114, 125-157 and 224-239) in SCML1 containing potential functional domains. For example, the fragment 1-9 is a signal peptide. The positively selected sites using 95% cutoff are listed in Table 1, and most of them are also located in the potential functional domains other than the structural domains (4/1 and 5/2 for model M2a and model M8 respectively). This distribution bias of the positively selected sites indicates that Darwinian positive selection on SCML1 targets the putative functional domains, which is consistent with the proposed functional modification of SCML1 during primate evolution.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 108 110 108 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 111 113 111 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B66">66</xref>
###xml 170 176 170 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 322 328 322 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 374 375 374 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 525 530 525 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">e.g. </italic>
###xml 610 616 610 616 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 707 709 703 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 920 926 908 914 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 1060 1065 1048 1053 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">e.g. </italic>
###xml 1077 1079 1065 1067 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B67">67</xref>
###xml 1080 1082 1068 1070 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B70">70</xref>
###xml 530 540 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 545 550 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 627 637 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 642 647 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 721 727 <span type="species:ncbi:9581">gibbon</span>
###xml 804 817 <span type="species:ncbi:9544">rhesus monkey</span>
Sexual selection is the favored explanation for the observed adaptive evolution of male reproductive genes [35,66]. The immunohistochemical and RT-PCR data suggests that SCML1 is important for the development and normal function of testis in primates. Therefore, it is reasonable to propose that the adaptive evolution of SCML1 in primates is likely due to sexual selection[4]. Sperm competition, one of the major mechanisms for sexual selection has been used to define the driving force of selection in promiscuous species, e.g. chimpanzee and human, which seems to explain the observed adaptive evolution of SCML1 since both chimpanzee and human are among the rapidly evolving lineages (omega > 1, Figure 2A). However, gibbon is a monogamous species with a high omega value, and the highly promiscuous rhesus monkey does not show accelerated evolution (omega = 0.09). Therefore, the branch-specific rapid evolution of SCML1 in primates does not provide consistent support for the sexual selection hypothesis. Other evolutionary mechanisms need to be tested, e.g. speciation [67-70].
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 14 20 14 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 135 141 135 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 312 317 312 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML </italic>
###xml 361 367 361 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
###xml 458 460 458 460 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B71">71</xref>
###xml 461 463 461 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B73">73</xref>
###xml 178 183 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 188 191 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 200 203 <span type="species:ncbi:9615">dog</span>
###xml 205 208 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cow</span>
The origin of SCML1 probably occurred at the early stage of mammalian radiation about 100 million years ago because we do not identify SCML1 in non-mammalian species, neither in mouse and rat, but in dog, cow and primates. The comparison of evolutionary and expression patterns among the three genes of the same SCML family suggests that the rapid evolution of SCML1 likely led to function modification of testis development and spermatogenesis in primates [71-73].
###end p 60
###begin title 61
Conclusion
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 26 32 26 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCML1 </italic>
The adaptive evolution of SCML1 in primates provides a new case in understanding the evolutionary process of genes involved in primate male reproduction.
