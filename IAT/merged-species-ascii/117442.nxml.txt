Avian papillomaviruses: the parrot Psittacus erithacus papillomavirus (PePV) genome has a unique organization of the early protein region and is phylogenetically related to the chaffinch papillomavirus
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Background
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 113 132 113 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psittacus erithacus</italic>
###xml 214 233 214 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psittacus erithacus</italic>
###xml 3 23 <span type="species:ncbi:10577">avian papillomavirus</span>
###xml 100 111 <span type="species:ncbi:57247">grey parrot</span>
###xml 113 132 <span type="species:ncbi:57247">Psittacus erithacus</span>
###xml 214 248 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">Psittacus erithacus papillomavirus</span>
###xml 250 254 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 278 282 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 322 342 <span type="species:ncbi:10577">avian papillomavirus</span>
An avian papillomavirus genome has been cloned from a cutaneous exophytic papilloma from an African grey parrot (Psittacus erithacus). The nucleotide sequence, genome organization, and phylogenetic position of the Psittacus erithacus papillomavirus (PePV) were determined. This PePV sequence represents the first complete avian papillomavirus genome defined.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Results
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 280 297 280 297 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fringilla coelebs</italic>
###xml 4 8 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 215 219 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 269 278 <span type="species:ncbi:37598">chaffinch</span>
###xml 280 297 <span type="species:ncbi:37598">Fringilla coelebs</span>
###xml 315 318 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
The PePV genome (7304 basepairs) differs from other papillomaviruses, in that it has a unique organization of the early protein region lacking classical E6 and E7 open reading frames. Phylogenetic comparison of the PePV sequence with partial E1 and L1 sequences of the chaffinch (Fringilla coelebs) papillomavirus (FPV) reveals that these two avian papillomaviruses form a monophyletic cluster with a common branch that originates near the unresolved center of the papillomavirus evolutionary tree.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Conclusions
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 212 226 212 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psittaciformes</italic>
###xml 231 244 231 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Passeriformes</italic>
###xml 4 8 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 180 184 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 189 192 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
The PePV genome has a unique layout of the early protein region which represents a novel prototypic genomic organization for avian papillomaviruses. The close relationship between PePV and FPV, and between their Psittaciformes and Passeriformes hosts, supports the hypothesis that papillomaviruses have co-evolved and speciated together with their host species throughout evolution.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Background
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 200 201 200 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 202 203 202 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 406 407 406 407 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 408 409 408 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 151 156 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 641 661 <span type="species:ncbi:10577">avian papillomavirus</span>
###xml 694 703 <span type="species:ncbi:37598">chaffinch</span>
###xml 764 784 <span type="species:ncbi:10577">avian papillomavirus</span>
Papillomaviruses are a large group of pathogens that cause epithelial proliferations in a wide spectrum of vertebrate species. More than 100 different human papillomaviruses (HPV) have been isolated [1,2]. Such an extensive genotype variety has not yet been detected in nonhuman species, although papillomavirus genomes have been isolated from many species where careful investigational efforts were made [3,4]. Most papillomaviruses appear to be species-specific or at least restricted to infection of closely related animals within the same genus. Papillomavirus genomes have been cloned from 20 mammalian host species. Thus far, only two avian papillomavirus genomes were cloned, one from a chaffinch and the second from a parrot papilloma. To date no complete avian papillomavirus genome had been sequenced.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 50 67 50 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fringilla coelebs</italic>
###xml 191 192 191 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 208 217 208 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fringilla</italic>
###xml 348 349 348 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 350 351 350 351 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 446 463 446 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Carduelis chloris</italic>
###xml 466 467 466 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 487 503 487 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Serinus canarius</italic>
###xml 506 507 506 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 50 67 <span type="species:ncbi:37598">Fringilla coelebs</span>
###xml 222 225 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 296 299 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 446 463 <span type="species:ncbi:37601">Carduelis chloris</span>
###xml 477 485 <span type="species:ncbi:54072">canaries</span>
###xml 487 503 <span type="species:ncbi:9135">Serinus canarius</span>
In a large survey of 25,000 captured chaffinches (Fringilla coelebs) in the Netherlands, papillomas were found on the foot or tarsometatarsus (the bare part of the leg) of 1.3% of the birds [5]. The DNA of a Fringilla PV (FPV) was isolated from such skin papillomas, and two partial sequences of FPV, totaling about 900 basepairs, were determined [6,7]. Papillomavirus particles have also been observed using electron microscopy in greenfinches (Carduelis chloris) [8], and in canaries (Serinus canarius) [9].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 123 149 123 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psittacus erithacus timneh</italic>
###xml 152 154 152 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 161 180 161 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psittacus erithacus</italic>
###xml 215 217 215 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 319 320 319 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 467 487 467 487 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Amazona ochrocephala</italic>
###xml 546 552 546 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ara sp</italic>
###xml 565 577 565 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aratinga sp.</italic>
###xml 594 613 594 613 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cacatua moluccensis</italic>
###xml 616 618 616 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 619 621 619 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 110 121 <span type="species:ncbi:57247">grey parrot</span>
###xml 123 142 <span type="species:ncbi:57247">Psittacus erithacus</span>
###xml 161 195 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">Psittacus erithacus papillomavirus</span>
###xml 236 240 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 323 327 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 392 403 <span type="species:ncbi:57247">grey parrot</span>
###xml 467 487 <span type="species:ncbi:151761">Amazona ochrocephala</span>
###xml 594 613 <span type="species:ncbi:136504">Cacatua moluccensis</span>
Cutaneous papillomas were observed on the palpebrae, commisure of the beak, and the head of a captive African grey parrot (Psittacus erithacus timneh) [10]. The Psittacus erithacus papillomavirus genome was cloned [11], and abbreviated PePV in accordance with the nomenclature guidelines for nonhuman papillomaviruses [3]. PePV DNA was also detected in one other oral papilloma of an African grey parrot, and was not present in an oral papilloma of an Amazon parrot (Amazona ochrocephala), nor in 24 cloacal papillomas of Amazon parrots, macaws (Ara sp.), conures (Aratinga sp.) and cockatoos (Cacatua moluccensis) [12,13].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 67 87 <span type="species:ncbi:10577">avian papillomavirus</span>
###xml 126 137 <span type="species:ncbi:57247">grey parrot</span>
###xml 139 143 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
This report describes the first complete nucleotide sequence of an avian papillomavirus from a cutaneous lesion of an African grey parrot: PePV.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Results and discussion
###end title 12
###begin title 13
###xml 25 29 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
Complete sequence of the PePV genome
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 76 77 76 77 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 248 250 248 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 296 299 296 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sal</italic>
###xml 36 40 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 127 131 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 200 228 <span type="species:ncbi:10562">bovine papillomavirus type 4</span>
###xml 230 233 <span type="species:ncbi:10571?0.2991202346041056|species:ncbi:10784?0.08211143695014662">BPV</span>
###xml 269 273 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 465 469 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 521 525 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
The complete nucleotide sequence of PePV contains 7304 basepairs (bp) (Fig. 1), and has a GC-content of 49.3%. The size of the PePV genome is the second-smallest of the animal papillomaviruses, after bovine papillomavirus type 4 (BPV-4) (7265 bp) [14]. The part of the PePV E1 ORF containing the SalI cloning site is homologous and colinear with the corresponding region in other PVs. This indicates that no sequences have been lost during the establishment of the PePV clone. The position of the first nucleotide of the PePV genome corresponds to the start codon of the first major open reading frame in the early protein region.
###end p 14

###begin title 16
Open reading frame organization
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 464 465 464 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 1995 1997 1995 1997 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 1998 2000 1998 2000 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 377 381 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 468 472 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 980 984 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 1311 1315 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 1497 1501 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 1510 1514 <span type="species:ncbi:10633?0.9966199903428296">SV40</span>
###xml 1893 1897 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
All papillomaviruses have their open reading frames (ORFs) on the same coding (sense) strand of their circular double-stranded DNA genome. Usually, a papillomavirus genome contains seven major ORFs coding for five early (E) proteins E6, E7, E1, E2 and E4, followed by two late (L) capsid proteins L2 and L1, and a non-coding upstream regulatory region (URR). The layout of the PePV genome is different from the organization of other characterized PV genomes (Fig. 2). PePV does not contain classic E6 or E7 ORFs. Instead, it contains an E8 ORF in front of the E1 ORF, followed by an E9 ORF which overlaps with the aminoterminal part of the E1. The E8 ORF has the capacity to code for a 177 amino acid (aa) protein with a predicted molecular weight of 19.6 kilodaltons (kDa), and the E9 ORF encodes a 195 aa protein of 22.7 kDa. Both E8 and E9 do not show recognizable homology with any other known papillomavirus or non-papillomavirus proteins in GenBank. Manual alignment of the PePV E8 with a series of E7 proteins revealed two Cys-X-X-Cys motifs separated by 23 amino acids instead of the usual 28 or 29 residues. In the aminoterminal part of E8, a stretch of amino acids (DNLLCHESSMDD) is similar to the putative cellular division motif involved in retinoblastoma tumor suppressor protein (pRb) binding. In PePV, this pRb-binding motif is more related to the pRb-binding domain of the large T (LT) antigen of polyomaviruses than that of the E7 of other papillomaviruses. However, alignment of PePV E8 with SV40 LT did not reveal further similarities. The E8 is maybe part of a remnant of a very ancient common evolutionary origin of the early region proteins of avian papillomaviruses and the LT proteins of polyomaviruses. Computational motif searches (using SMART, InterPro and Prosite algorithms) failed to detect biologically significant sites, patterns or conserved protein motifs in PePV E8 and E9. Using the PSORT algorithm for the prediction of the subcellular location of proteins [15,16], E8 was predicted to be cytoplasmic, and E9 to be either nuclear or cytoplasmic. The cellular existence and function of E8 and E9 remains unknown.

###xml 205 207 205 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 344 346 344 346 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 347 349 347 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 465 467 465 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 88 107 <span type="species:ncbi:10564">deer papillomavirus</span>
###xml 113 136 <span type="species:ncbi:10569">reindeer papillomavirus</span>
###xml 225 231 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 250 253 <span type="species:ncbi:10571?0.2991202346041056|species:ncbi:10784?0.08211143695014662">BPV</span>
###xml 381 384 <span type="species:ncbi:10571?0.2991202346041056|species:ncbi:10784?0.08211143695014662">BPV</span>
###xml 502 505 <span type="species:ncbi:10571?0.2991202346041056|species:ncbi:10784?0.08211143695014662">BPV</span>
###xml 515 519 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 779 783 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
Unusual ORFs have also been described in the genomes of the Europen elk papillomavirus, deer papillomavirus, and reindeer papillomavirus, which contain a transforming gene (E9) between the E2 and L2 ORFs [17]. The subgroup B bovine papillomaviruses (BPV-3, -4, and -6) are another group of papillomaviruses that lack an E6 (but do have an E7) [14,18]. Instead of an E6, there is a BPV-4 E8 ORF that encodes a 42-residue polypeptide that can transform NIH3T3 cells [19]. No discernable homology between BPV-4 E8 and PePV E8 sequences was detected. It seems that E6 functions are either not required by some papillomaviruses, or that they are performed by another viral (or host) protein. It remains to be established if this unique organization of the early region is typical for PePV or common to other/all avian papillomaviruses.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Upstream regulatory region (URR)
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 254 255 254 255 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sub>
###xml 330 331 330 331 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 3 7 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
In PePV, the noncoding region or upstream regulatory region (URR) between the stop codon of L1 and the first ATG of E8 is only 460 bp long (nucleotides (nt) 6845-7304). Only one typical palindromic E2-binding site (E2BS) with the consensus sequence ACC-N6-GGT is found at nt 7214-7225. Two additional atypical putative E2BS (ACC-N4-GGT) are found at nt 7020-7029 and 7174-7183. The URR also contains a polyadenylation site (AATAAA; nt 7279-7284) located 16 nucleotides 5' of a CA dinucleotide, necessary for the processing of the L1 and L2 capsid mRNA transcripts.
###end p 21

###begin title 29
Phylogenetic analysis
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 249 250 249 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 251 253 251 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 24 28 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 55 64 <span type="species:ncbi:37598">chaffinch</span>
###xml 66 69 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 136 139 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 210 213 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 395 399 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 404 407 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
In order to compare the PePV sequence with that of the chaffinch (FPV), we retrieved two partially overlapping partial sequences in the FPV L1 ORF (GenBank accession numbers K02020 and U29669) and one piece of FPV E1 sequence (K02019) from GenBank [7,20]. A total of 312 amino acids (132 E1 and 180 L1 residues) could be compared for both viruses. The similarity at the amino acid level between PePV and FPV was 68% in the E1 region, and 47% in the L1 region.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 270 271 270 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 382 383 382 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 384 386 384 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 387 389 387 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 31 35 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 41 44 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 179 184 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
To define the relationships of PePV with FPV and with other papillomaviruses, we constructed a phylogenetic tree based on a compound E1/L1 312 amino acid sequence alignment of 50 human and animal papillomaviruses. The resulting neighbour-joining phylogenetic tree (fig. 4) clusters different papillomavirus groups, largely according to their tissue tropism and oncogenic potential [1,21,22]. The two avian papillomaviruses form a monophyletic cluster with a common branch that originates close to the unresolved center of the unrooted papillomavirus evolutionary tree, near to the origin of the branch that groups the cutaneous papillomaviruses associated with epidermodysplasia verruciformis (EV). The avian papillomaviruses occupy a unique position among the other known papillomaviruses, with whom they are only distanly related.

Divergence between PePV and FPV coincides with divergence of their Psittaciformes and Passeriformes host species
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 93 95 93 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 148 160 148 160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Paleognathae</italic>
###xml 238 249 238 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Galliformes</italic>

###xml 306 316 306 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Neognathae</italic>
###xml 494 496 494 496 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 608 610 608 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 617 636 617 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psittacus erithacus</italic>
###xml 666 677 666 677 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psittacidae</italic>
###xml 698 712 698 712 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psittaciformes</italic>
###xml 747 764 747 764 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fringilla coelebs</italic>
###xml 795 807 795 807 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fringillidae</italic>
###xml 828 841 828 841 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Passeriformes</italic>
###xml 195 202 <span type="species:ncbi:8801">ostrich</span>
###xml 222 225 <span type="species:ncbi:8790">emu</span>
###xml 251 257 <span type="species:ncbi:9103">turkey</span>
###xml 259 266 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 283 287 <span type="species:ncbi:8839">duck</span>
###xml 289 294 <span type="species:ncbi:8847">goose</span>
###xml 617 636 <span type="species:ncbi:57247">Psittacus erithacus</span>
###xml 736 745 <span type="species:ncbi:37598">chaffinch</span>
###xml 747 764 <span type="species:ncbi:37598">Fringilla coelebs</span>
Mammals and birds diverged around 310 million years (Myr) ago during the late Paleizoic Era [23]. The start of the first avian differentiation into Paleognathae (larger, flightless birds such as ostrich, rheas, cassowary, emu, and kiwi), Galliformes (turkey, chicken), Anseriformes (duck, goose) and other Neognathae (all other extant modern birds) is currently thought to have coincided with the Mesozoic breakup of the world-continent Pangaea at the Jurassic-Cretaceous boundary 146 Myr ago [24]. Most of the extant avian orders were establishing themselves at the Cretaceous-Tertiary boundary 65 Myr ago [25]. The Psittacus erithacus belongs to the family of the Psittacidae in the order of the Psittaciformes (parrots), whereas the chaffinch (Fringilla coelebs) belongs to the family of the Fringillidae in the order of the Passeriformes (perching birds).
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 378 394 378 394 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Felis domesticus</italic>
###xml 479 486 479 486 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Felidae</italic>
###xml 495 502 495 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Canidae</italic>
###xml 554 556 554 556 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-8</sup>
###xml 601 603 601 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 1338 1352 1338 1352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Psittaciformes</italic>
###xml 1357 1370 1357 1370 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Passeriformes</italic>
###xml 1729 1731 1729 1731 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 280 284 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 289 292 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 378 394 <span type="species:ncbi:9685">Felis domesticus</span>
###xml 415 441 <span type="species:ncbi:35258">canine oral papillomavirus</span>
###xml 657 661 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 666 669 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 737 740 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 814 818 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 871 875 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 880 883 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 1021 1025 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 1030 1033 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
###xml 1284 1288 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 1289 1292 <span type="species:ncbi:11313?0.3020408163265306|species:ncbi:10261?0.2571428571428571|species:ncbi:10786?0.17142857142857143|species:ncbi:10785?0.07755102040816327">FPV</span>
The rates of nucleotide substitutions between two homologous sequences can be used as a measure for the time elapsed since the two sequences diverged (i.e. the molecular clock concept). When we know the papillomavirus mutation rate, we can approximate the divergence time between PePV and FPV. We earlier calculated a papillomavirus mutation rate based on the divergence of the Felis domesticus PV (FdPV-1) and the canine oral papillomavirus (COPV) since the divergence of their Felidae and the Canidae host species 38-50 Myr ago, to be 0.73 to 0.96 x 10-8 nucleotide substitutions per site per year [26]. We constructed a 921 bp pairwise alignment between PePV and FPV for 441 bp in E1 and 480 bp in L1 where the nucleotide sequence of FPV was available in GenBank (corresponding to nt 1908-2348 and 6107-6586 in PePV). In this 921 bp alignment, 385 differences between PePV and FPV were observed. Using the estimations of the mutation rates derived from the feline/canine papillomavirus divergence, this corresponds to PePV and FPV diverging from each other 44 to 57 Myr ago. Since these calculations were based on alignments of the most conserved regions in the papillomaviral genome, this calculation is likely an underestimation of the true divergence time. This would place the PePV/FPV divergence at about the same time that their Psittaciformes and Passeriformes host species were diverging from each in the Late Cretaceous or Early Tertiary period. The high level of congruence between divergences in the papillomavirus phylogenetic tree and the divergence of their host species lineages supports co-speciation. Co-speciation was also hypothesized to be a prominent feature in mammalian and avian herpesvirus evolution [27].
###end p 35
###begin title 36
Papillomaviruses in inbred species: emerging infectious pathogens?
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 1014 1016 1014 1016 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 1321 1323 1321 1323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 1482 1484 1482 1484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 1554 1566 1554 1566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pan paniscus</italic>
###xml 1783 1785 1783 1785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 1786 1788 1786 1788 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 17 29 <span type="species:ncbi:57247">grey parrots</span>
###xml 292 303 <span type="species:ncbi:57247">Grey parrot</span>
###xml 431 436 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 1212 1224 <span type="species:ncbi:29064">snow leopard</span>
###xml 1335 1350 <span type="species:ncbi:9778">Florida manatee</span>
###xml 1535 1552 <span type="species:ncbi:9597">pygmy chimpanzees</span>
###xml 1554 1566 <span type="species:ncbi:9597">Pan paniscus</span>
Although African grey parrots are not yet officially listed as an endangered species, their survival is threatened by the same factors that most other exotic parrot species face, and they are monitored by the World Wildlife Fund for conservation concerns. In nature, the range of the African Grey parrot extends from Guinea-Bissau and Sierra Leone to southern Cameroon, Congo, Uganda, nortwestern Tanzania, and southwestern Kenya. Human-caused destruction and fragmentation of their habitats in the tropical rain forests cause an increase in inbreeding and reduced heterozygosity. The illegal parrot smuggling trade also causes the natural population size to decrease, and the ensuing import restrictions lead animal breeders to a higher degree of inbreeding in the captive population. Reduced diversity in the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) genes due to inbreeding and genetic bottlenecks may contribute to an increased sensitivity to emerging infectious pathogens, as has been observed in exotic felids [28]. Whenever a population goes through a demographic and genetic reduction, papillomaviruses seem to become more prevalent. This has been documented in endangered exotic felid species, such as the snow leopard, where papillomaviruses are causing an increasing number of cutaneous squamous cell carcinomas [29]. Also the Florida manatee, one of the most endangered marine mammals in American coastal waters, currently suffers from an epidemic of viral papillomatosis [30]. We have previously described this phenomenon in pygmy chimpanzees (Pan paniscus) and in Greenlandic Inuits and Navajo Indians, where species-specific papillomaviruses (PpPV and HPV-13, respectively) cause oral focal epithelial hyperplasia, a disease rarely encountered in non-inbred populations [31,32].
###end p 37
###begin title 38
Papillomaviruses are ancient viruses that infect amniotes
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 14 21 14 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Amniota</italic>
###xml 450 452 450 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 1106 1108 1106 1108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 1090 1103 <span type="species:ncbi:9853">European elks</span>
The amniotes (Amniota) are a clade that includes the dinosaurs and most of the extant land-dwelling vertebrates, namely mammals, birds and reptiles. They evolved 360 to 286 Myr ago during the Carboniferous Period in the late Paleozoic Era. Papillomaviruses have currently been characterized in more than 20 mammalian species, and in 2 avian species. Papillomavirus particles have also been described in a a Bolivian side-neck turtle reptile species [33]. Since papillomaviruses have been described in mammals, birds and reptiles, and were never found in amphibians or fish, it is tempting to speculate that the host-specificity of papillomaviruses would encompass the amniotes. This means that species-specific papillomaviruses could potentially infect more than 20,000 living species, living in virtually every habitat of the planet. We know that papillomaviruses have been detected throughout the world, even in non-gregarious hosts. This wide geographic distribution cannot be attributed to transmission as an airborne infection (with the possible exception of pulmonary fibromatosis in European elks) [34], since transmission of papillomaviruses requires close direct cutaneous or mucosal contact.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 319 325 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 435 441 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Together with the viral species-specificity and the genomic stability of their double-stranded DNA, this requirement for close physical contact makes it unlikely that interspecies transmission in recent history can account for the global presence of a spectrum of papillomaviruses in many amniotes. Assuming that, like humans, all of the more than 20,000 species in the amniotes clade have their own set of species-specific genotypes (humans have more than 100 HPV genotypes), papillomaviruses could be the oldest, largest, and most diverse viral family.
###end p 40

###end title 41
###begin title 42
DNA sequencing
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 34 37 34 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sal</italic>
###xml 74 76 74 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 148 151 148 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sau</italic>
###xml 160 163 160 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sau</italic>
###xml 224 227 224 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bam</italic>
###xml 289 296 285 292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 449 452 445 448 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sau</italic>
###xml 4 8 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>
###xml 131 135 <span type="species:ncbi:197772">PePV</span>

The PePV genome was cloned in the SalI restriction enzyme site of pBR322 [11]. Subclones were prepared by partial digestion of the PePV-insert with Sau3AI. The Sau3AI restriction fragments were ligated with dephosphorylated BamHI-cut pUC19. After transformation of MAX Efficiency DH5alpha E. coli (Life Technologies/Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA), the bacteria were incubated for blue-white colony screening on agar plates containing X-gal and IPTG. Ten Sau3AI-subclones with a PePV-insert ranging in size from 250 to 1900 basepairs were investigated. Plasmid DNA was extracted using the QIAGEN Midi Plasmid Purification Kit (QIAGEN, Hilden, Germany). Nucleotide sequencing was started using pBR322-specific primers or the universal primers in the multiple cloning site of pUC19. Primer walking sequencing was performed, using 59 sequencing primers to cover the complete genome on both strands. Sequencing was performed on an ABI Prism 310 Genetic Analyzer (Perkin-Elmer Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA) at the Leuven and Prague core DNA sequencing facilities. Chromatrogram sequencing files were inspected with Chromas 2.2 (Technelysium, Helensvale, Australia), and contigs were prepared using SeqMan II (DNASTAR, Madison, WI).
