Adding to Yersinia enterocolitica Gene Pool Diversity: Two Cryptic Plasmids from a Biotype 1A Isolate
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Recommended by Frederick D. Quinn
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 106 129 106 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yersinia enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 447 455 447 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yersinia</italic>
###xml 631 641 631 641 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>

###xml 1750 1751 1738 1739 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</sub>
###xml 1843 1860 1831 1848 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 106 129 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Yersinia enterocolitica</span>
###xml 1110 1127 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 1635 1640 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">swine</span>
###xml 1843 1860 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
We report the nucleotide sequence of two novel cryptic plasmids (4357 and 14 662 base pairs) carried by a Yersinia enterocolitica biotype 1A strain isolated from pork. As distinguished from most biotype 1A strains, this isolate, designated 07-04449, exhibited adherence to eukaryotic cells. The smaller plasmid pYe4449-1 carries five attributable open reading frames (ORFs) encoding the first CcdA/CcdB-like antitoxin/toxin system described for a Yersinia plasmid, a RepA-like replication initiation protein, and mobilizing factors MobA and MobC. The deduced amino acid sequences showed highest similarity to proteins described in Salmonella (CcdA/B), Klebsiella (RepA), and Plesiomonas (MobA/C) indicating genomic fluidity among members of the Enterobacteriaceae. One additional ORF with unknown function, termed ORF5, was identified with an ancestry distinct from the rest of the plasmid. While the C+G content of ORF5 is 38.3%, the rest of pYe4449-1 shows a C+G content of 55.7%. The C+G content of the larger plasmid pYe4449-2 (54.9%) was similar to that of pYe4449-1 (53.7%) and differed from that of the Y. enterocolitica genome (47.3%). Of the 14 ORFs identified on pYe4449-2, only six ORFs showed significant similarity to database entries. For three of these ORFs likely functions could be ascribed: a TnpR-like resolvase and a phage replication protein, localized each on a low C+G island, and DNA primase TraC. Two ORFs of pYe4449-2, ORF3 and ORF7, seem to encode secretable proteins. Epitope-tagging of ORF3 revealed protein expression at 4degreesC but not at or above 27degreesC suggesting adaptation to a habitat outside swine. The hypothetical protein encoded by ORF7 is the member of a novel repeat protein family sharing the DxxGN(x)nDxxGN motif. Our findings illustrate the exceptional gene pool diversity within the species Y. enterocolitica driven by horizontal gene transfer events.

###begin title 3
1. Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 31 39 31 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yersinia</italic>
###xml 145 146 145 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 178 201 178 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yersinia enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 399 400 399 400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 415 432 415 432 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 771 788 771 788 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 831 832 831 832 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 834 835 834 835 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 902 903 902 903 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1037 1054 1037 1054 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 1128 1129 1128 1129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 178 201 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Yersinia enterocolitica</span>
###xml 376 381 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 415 432 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 690 695 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 771 788 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 1037 1054 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 1058 1063 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">swine</span>
Pathogenicity within the genus Yersinia typically relies on the presence of the virulence plasmid pYV encoding a type 3 secretion system (T3SS) [1]. The enteropathogenic species Yersinia enterocolitica is subdivided into nonpathogenic (biotype 1A), high-pathogenic (biotype 1B) and low-pathogenic (biotypes 2-5) representatives, based on their virulence potential in the oral mouse infection model [2]. Isolates of Y. enterocolitica biotype 1A typically lack the virulence plasmid and other known chromosomally encoded virulence factors and are, therefore, considered avirulent in general. However, several lines of evidence indicate that at least some biotype 1A strains are pathogenic to human provoking gastrointestinal symptoms indistinguishable from those caused by Y. enterocolitica strains harbouring the virulence plasmid [3, 4]. Biotype 1A strains frequently harbour plasmids of various size [5] that could carry novel virulence determinants to explain the apparent pathogenicity of certain strains. A major animal reservoir of Y. enterocolitica is swine resulting in contaminated pork as important transmission route [6]. 
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 41 58 41 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 41 58 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 84 92 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Among 20 arbitrarily selected strains of Y. enterocolitica biotype 1A isolated from patients with diarrhoea, of animal source or from environmental sampling, a single isolate, designated 07-04449, exhibited significant adherence to several eukaryotic cell lines. This strain, isolated from pork, harbours two novel plasmids which we have sequenced in the search of novel virulence determinants.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
2. Materials and Methods
###end title 6
###begin title 7
2.1. Bacterial Strains and Culture Conditions
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 0 1 0 1 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y</italic>
###xml 1 17 1 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 86 111 86 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">National Reference Centre</italic>
###xml 116 126 116 126 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 497 499 491 492 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 524 526 517 518 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 0 17 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
Y. enterocolitica biotype 1A isolates were selected from the strain collection of the National Reference Centre for Salmonella and other enteric pathogens, Robert Koch Institute, Wernigerode, Germany. All strains were routinely cultured in Luria-Bertani (LB) broth. To avoid possible stimulation of virulence gene expression, all strains were cultured at 27degreesC for standard procedures except for infection studies. Where appropriate, antibiotics were supplemented as follows: kanamycin at 30 mug/mL, streptomycin at 30 mug/mL.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
2.2. Isolation of Plasmids
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 27 50 27 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yersinia enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 27 50 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Yersinia enterocolitica</span>
Plasmids carried by strain Yersinia enterocolitica 07-04449 were isolated by using a plasmid preparation kit (Qiagen). Plasmids were resolved on 0.7% agarose gels. Plasmids pYe4449-1 and pYe4449-2 were cut out from the agarose gel and were purified with the QIAquick gel extraction kit (Qiagen).
###end p 10
###begin title 11
2.3. Cell Adhesion Analyses
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 160 161 160 161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 717 718 711 712 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 333 337 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
Culturing of eukaryotic cell lines and analysis of adhesion of yersiniae to cell lines Int-407, HeLa, Hep-2, and IEC-6 were performed essentially as described [7]. Int-407, Hep-2, and HeLa cells were cultured in EMEM medium (Cell Concepts) supplemented with 1% nonessential amino acids, 2 mM glutamine, and 5% heat-inactivated fetal calf serum (FCS), and IEC-6 cells were cultured in DMEM (Cell Concepts) supplemented with 2 mM glutamine, 5% heat-inactivated FCS, and 0.1 IU/mL insulin. For infection experiments, eukaryotic cells were cultivated in SonicSeal slide wells (Nunc). After infection with yersiniae (multiplicity of infection of 50), cells were incubated for 3 hours at 37degreesC in the presence of 5% CO2, then washed five times with PBS, fixed in methanol for 5 minutes, stained with Accustain modified Giemsa stain (Sigma), and examined under the microscope.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
2.4. Sequencing and Assembly
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 131 138 131 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 608 612 608 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Blue</italic>
###xml 618 622 618 622 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Blue</italic>
###xml 703 710 703 710 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 847 851 847 851 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Blue</italic>
###xml 131 138 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 703 710 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Plasmid pYe4449-1 was incubated with the EZ-Tn5 <KAN-2> Tnp Transposome kit (Epicentre Biotechnologies) in vitro, transformed into E. coli Top10 (Invitrogen) and selected for kanamycin resistant clones. Plasmids from four colonies were isolated, and sequencing reactions were performed with the transposon-specific primers provided with the transposome kit. Gaps of pYe4449-1 were filled by primer walking sequencing. Plasmid pYe4449-2 was digested with restriction endonuclease BamHI. One restriction fragment of approximately 0.8 kbp was cut out from an agarose gel, purified, and inserted into vector pMOSBlue (pMOSBlue Blunt Ended Cloning Kit, Amersham Biosciences). The vector was transformed into E. coli Top10. Grown colonies were picked and plasmids were isolated as described earlier. The cloned pYe4449-2 fragment was sequenced with pMOSBlue-specific primers. Subsequently, the rest of the plasmid was sequenced by primer walking. Sequence coverage was approximately 4-fold for both plasmids. The sequences were assembled with DNASTAR Lasergene v7.2 SeqMan Pro. The nucleotide sequences of plasmids pYe4449-1 and pYe4449-2 have been deposited in the GenBank database (accession numbers FJ696405 and FJ696406, resp.).
###end p 14
###begin title 15
2.5. Sequence Analyses
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 499 500 499 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 592 593 592 593 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
Potential coding regions were determined by softberry () tools and confirmed applying Glimmer (). Comparison of nucleotide sequences of pYe4449-1 and pYe4449-2 and deduced protein sequences with sequences available in databases was accomplished using various NCBI () tools. However, C+G islands, direct and inverted repeats were identified with programs provided by the Mobyle bioinformatics portal (). Superhelically induced duplex destabilization (SIDD) sites were identified applying WebSIDD (; [8]). Search for putative protein secretion signals was performed with the SignalP server (; [9]). Possible phosphorylation sites were identified using the NetPhos 2.0 Server (). Protein secondary structure analyses were performed with different ExPASy tools ().

2.7. Plasmid Curation
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 55 72 55 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 131 133 131 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 134 136 134 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 185 188 185 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;g</italic>
###xml 215 217 214 215 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 245 247 243 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 55 72 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
Attempts to cure plasmids pYe4449-1 and pYe4449-2 from Y. enterocolitica 07-04449 were accomplished following standard procedures [12-14]. In brief, curing agents ethidium bromide (500 mug/mL), acridine orange (450 mug/mL), or promethazine (120 mug/mL) were added to overnight cultures diluted 100-fold in LB broth and subsequently incubated for 24 hours at 37degreesC. Dilution series were plated on LB agar and incubated overnight at 37degreesC. Loss of plasmids was checked by PCR and plasmid isolation.

2.8. Mating Experiments
###end title 21

###xml 374 381 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>

Plasmids pYe4449-1 and pYe4449-2 each were tagged with a kanamycin resistance cassette (KmR) by in vitro transposon mutagenesis applying the EZ-Tn5 <KAN-2> Tnp Transposome kit (Epicentre Biotechnologies). Donor strain Y. enterocolitica 07-04449 was electroporated with EZ-Tn5 <KAN-2> transposon-tagged plasmid pYe4449-1 and pYe4449-2, respectively, and mated with recipient E. coli TOP10 beforehand selected for spontaneous streptomycin resistance. Overnight cultures of donor and recipient strains grown at 27degreesC were diluted 1:40 in 2xYT medium supplemented with selecting antibiotics and incubated for 2 hours at 27degreesC. Also, 1 mL of donor and recipient cultures were washed twice and resuspended in 500 l muL 2xYT medium. Donor and the recipient were subsequently mixed and centrifuged at 3000 g for 2 minutes, resuspended in 1 mL 2xYT medium and were plated on a nutrient broth agar plate. After five hours of incubation at 27degreesC, the bacteria were washed from the plate, the suspension was centrifuged at 3000 x g for 2 minutes, resuspended in 200 l muL 2xYT medium, and plated on selective agar plates containing kanamycin and streptomycin. For comparison, occurrence of spontaneous streptomycin resistance of donor strain Y. enterocolitica 07-04449 was determined in parallel.

###begin title 28
###xml 31 54 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Yersinia enterocolitica</span>
3.1. The Adhesive Phenotype of Yersinia enterocolitica 07-04449
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 27 44 27 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 607 618 607 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1(a)</xref>
###xml 838 850 838 850 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1(a) </xref>
###xml 854 865 854 865 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1(b)</xref>
###xml 27 44 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 576 579 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
We screened 20 isolates of Y. enterocolitica biotype 1A from Germany for their potential to adhere to HeLa, Int-407, Hep-2, and IEC-6 eukaryotic cell lines as a first indicator of pathogenicity. Only one strain, termed 07-04449, which was obtained from pork, showed significant adherence to eukaryotic cells. On average, 4.2 +/- 0.5 bacteria of strain 07-04449 were found attached to each Int-407 cell, whereas only 0.2 +/- 0.2 bacteria of other strains were found attached to each Int-407 cell. Most pronounced adherence of the 07-04449 isolate was found in interaction with rat intestinal cell line IEC6 (Figure 1(a)). Interestingly, we observed that 0704449 yersiniae attaching to IEC-6 cells were markedly elongated compared to planktonic ones or compared to 07-04449 cells attached to HeLa, Int-407 or Hep-2 cells, respectively (cf. Figure 1(a) and Figure 1(b)). These findings stimulated us to search for possible plasmid-encoded adherence- and pathogenicity-related factors. We identified two plasmids, termed pYe4449-1 and pYe4449-2 that were completely sequenced.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
3.2. Characterisation of pYe4449-1 (4357 Base Pairs)
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 133 150 133 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 168 170 168 170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 255 272 255 272 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 302 304 302 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 415 427 415 427 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Figure 2(a) </xref>
###xml 431 438 431 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tab1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 569 588 569 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella enterica</italic>
###xml 735 743 735 743 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yersinia</italic>
###xml 824 841 824 841 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 133 150 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 255 272 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 569 604 <span type="species:ncbi:59201">Salmonella enterica subsp. enterica</span>
###xml 824 841 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
The smaller plasmid, pYe4449-1, consists of 4357 base pairs (bps) and a C+G content of 53.7%. For comparison, the C+G content of the Y. enterocolitica genome is 47.3% [11] and the C+G content of the recently sequenced 95.5 kbp plasmid pYE854 from another Y. enterocolitica biotype 1A strain was 42.3% [16]. Sequence analysis revealed six open reading frames (ORFs) with a minimum coding capacity of 50 amino acids (Figure 2(a) and Table 1). Plasmid pYe44491 encodes an antitoxin/toxin system, likely conducive to plasmid maintenance, with the highest similarity to the Salmonella enterica subsp. enterica CcdA/B system (70% and 68% identity, resp.). This is the first report of a CcdA/B family antitoxin/toxin system associated with a Yersinia plasmid. The chromosomally encoded CcdA/B-like system of the high-pathogenicity Y. enterocolitica strain 8081 is more divergent with 53% and 48% identity, respectively. Interestingly, several attempts to cure pYe44491 following different protocols failed, indicating that the CcdA/B system is functional. 
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 21 27 21 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ccdA/B</italic>
###xml 146 172 146 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"> Plesiomonas shigelloides </italic>
###xml 335 346 335 346 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Klebsiella </italic>
###xml 147 171 <span type="species:ncbi:703">Plesiomonas shigelloides</span>
###xml 335 355 <span type="species:ncbi:160057">Klebsiella sp. KCL-2</span>
Directly adjacent to ccdA/B but in the opposite orientation, the mobilizing factors MobA and MobC are encoded. They show the highest similarity to Plesiomonas shigelloides proteins (45% and 77% identity, resp.). Further, an ORF encoding a RepA-like replication initiation protein was identified on pYe4449-1, sharing 75% identity with Klebsiella sp. KCL-2 RepA. Finally, a hypothetical protein without significant similarity to proteins in the databases is encoded by ORF5 (no hits with e-values below 0.1). 
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 546 548 546 548 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
We analysed the C+G content of the plasmid in sections to identify possible horizontal gene transfer events and identified one low C+G island. Interestingly, this island colocalised with ORF5. While the C+G content of ORF5 is 38.3%, the rest of pYe4449-1 shows a C+G content of 55.7% suggesting a descent differing from its plasmid background. Downstream of ORF5, a palindromic sequence was identified capable of forming the 11-bp stem of a stem-loop hairpin likely involved in Rho-independent termination of the transcript associated with ORF5 [17].

###begin title 36
3.3. Characterisation of pYe4449-2 (14662 base pairs)
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 213 234 213 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Klebsiella pneumoniae</italic>
###xml 612 624 592 604 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Figure 2(b) </xref>
###xml 628 635 608 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tab2">Table 2</xref>
###xml 213 234 <span type="species:ncbi:573">Klebsiella pneumoniae</span>
The C+G content of the larger plasmid pYe4449-2 (54.9%) was similar to that of pYe4449-1 (53.7%). On the nucleotide level, the segment from bp position 12260 to 125 is 81% identical to bp segment 36316-34658 of a Klebsiella pneumoniae plasmid harbouring several antibiotic resistance genes (gbshortmid R:FJ223605.1shortmid R:). ###begin p 38

###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 52 63 52 63 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig3">Figure 3(b)</xref>

###xml 855 872 855 872 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 1061 1078 1061 1078 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 1104 1106 1104 1106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 1108 1110 1108 1110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 253 273 <span type="species:ncbi:138072">Hamiltonella defensa</span>
###xml 336 355 <span type="species:ncbi:7029">Acyrthosiphon pisum</span>
###xml 679 696 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 855 872 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 1061 1078 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
ORF10, which is situated in another low C+G region (Figure 3(b)) encodes a hypothetical protein 40% identical to a P-loop ATPase of APSE bacteriophages. APSE phages are lambda-like phages associated with the genomes of facultative endosymbionts such as Hamiltonella defensa that are closely related to Yersinia and found in aphids like Acyrthosiphon pisum and other sap-feeding insects [21, 22]. It is interesting to note that lysogenic APSE phages have been implicated in the carriage of virulence genes such as Shiga-like toxin, cytolethal distending toxin, and YD-repeat toxin genes [21]. This suggests that APSE-like phages might have contributed to pathogenicity of certain Y. enterocolitica biotype 1A strains by a carry-over of virulence determinants. Moreover, this finding lends support on the hypothesis that a regular part of the life cycle of Y. enterocolitica could be the colonization and infection of insects where genetic exchange with the facultative insect endosymbionts would be favoured. This hypothesis is substantiated by the endowment of Y. enterocolitica with insecticidal genes [23, 24].
###end p 39

While the role of ORF13 is unclear, ORF14 encodes DNA primase TraC suggesting that the plasmid is transferable. This was confirmed by tagging the plasmid with the EZ-Tn5 <Kan-2> transposon, retransformation into Y. enterocolitica 07-04449, and mating with E . coli Top10.
###end p 40

###xml 447 464 447 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 492 500 492 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E . coli</italic>
###xml 447 464 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
No sequences homologous to known origins of replication could be identified on pYe4449-2. Superhelically induced duplex destabilization (SIDD; [8]) of pYe4449-2 was analysed to search for regulatory sequences that might indicate the positioning of the origin of replication [25]. SIDD analysis of the plasmid revealed a single highly significant site (8333-8463) representing a possible origin. Copy number of pYe4449-2 was approximately 15-20 in Y. enterocolitica 07-04449 and half of it in E . coli.
###end p 42
###begin title 43
3.4. A Novel Secretable and Phosphorylatable Repeat Protein
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 368 376 368 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig3">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 677 695 677 695 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Bacteroides ovatus</italic>
###xml 710 731 710 731 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Nitrosomonas europaea</italic>
###xml 770 775 770 771 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#947;</italic>
###xml 992 993 988 989 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</sub>
###xml 1063 1071 1059 1067 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig3">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 1148 1149 1144 1145 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</sub>
###xml 1193 1200 1189 1196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tab3">Table 3</xref>
###xml 1261 1262 1257 1258 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 1282 1304 1278 1300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</italic>
###xml 677 695 <span type="species:ncbi:28116">Bacteroides ovatus</span>
###xml 710 731 <span type="species:ncbi:915">Nitrosomonas europaea</span>
###xml 770 791 <span type="species:ncbi:86473">gamma-proteobacterium</span>
###xml 1282 1304 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
Retrieval for the presence of repeats within the nucleotide sequences of both plasmids applying "etandem" of the Mobyle toolbox revealed a 108 nucleotide long triple tandem repeat sequence within ORF7 which is localized on a low C+G island of pYe4449-2. Subsequent analysis of the ORF7-deduced protein sequence identified three repeats, each 36 amino acids in length (Figure 3). Each repeat contains two nonameric repeat modules of the sequence RTDSLGNTY. The triple tandem organisation identified on the nucleotide and the translational level suggests that the likely functional unit contains two of these nonameric repeat modules. The closest homologues of the ORF7 product, Bacteroides ovatus BACOVA_04330, Nitrosomonas europaea NE2449, and MPG2080_02246 of a marine gamma-proteobacterium are all hypothetical proteins with unknown functions. Multiple sequence analyses revealed that all these proteins are repeat proteins. From these repeats, we condensed the common repeat motif DxxGN(x)nDxxGN with a variable number of residues between two DxxGN sequences (Figure 3). BLAST searches revealed the existence of repeated arrangements of DxxGN(x)nDxxGN motifs in several predicted proteins (Table 3). The highest number of repeats, twenty of the form DxxGN(x)7DxxGN, was found in Pseudomonas aeruginosa hypothetical protein PLES_34491. 
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 107 112 107 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 304 311 300 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tab3">Table 3</xref>
###xml 344 345 340 341 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</sub>
###xml 582 584 578 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
Secondary structure prediction analyses of the ORF7 hypothetical protein indicate that it completely lacks alpha-helical elements whereas for almost 80% of the sequence, a random coil is predicted. Similar results were obtained for the ORF7 homologues and repeat-containing regions of proteins listed in Table 3. This suggests that the DxxGN(x)nDxxGN repeat motif triggers a specific conformation of functional importance. Due to the lack of significant sequence similarity to structurally resolved proteins, no three-dimensional prediction was possible using the ESyPred3D server [21].
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 110 111 110 111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 265 267 265 267 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">19</sup>
###xml 286 288 273 275 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">23</sup>
The hypothetical protein encoded by ORF7 was further checked for a secretion signal using the SignalP server [9]. A highly significant signal sequence was identified (signal peptide probability: 0.998) and the most likely cleavage site predicted was within peptide 19VQAdownward arrowST23 (cleavage site probability: 0.979) immediately before the first repeat domain. The protein is thus likely a secreted protein.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 802 813 802 813 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig3">Figure 3(b)</xref>
Moreover, the protein sequence was found to be extremely rich in serine, threonine, and tyrosine residues. Among the 106 residues to be secreted, there are 16 serine, 18 threonine, and 8 tyrosine residues constituting almost 40% of the protein altogether. This prompted us to speculate about a possible virulence function interfering with eukaryotic cell signalling. Consequently, we searched for putative phosphorylation sites applying the NetPhos 2.0 Server. In support of our hypothesis, 10 serine and 2 threonine residues were predicted as probable phosphorylation sites with a score of 0.917 and higher while the threshold is 0.5. Of these sites, four were predicted with scores above 0.99. The ten most likely phosphorylation sites with highly significant scores of 0.968 and above are marked in Figure 3(b). Interestingly, the serine residues localised within the nonameric repeat modules are all significant targets of phosphorylation. Thus, it seems very likely that this repeat protein, if entering the host cell, would interfere with host cell signalling pathways, possibly by trapping serine/threonine kinases and/or phosphatases. 
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 210 211 210 211 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</sub>
###xml 260 268 260 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yersinia</italic>
###xml 436 437 436 437 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</sub>
###xml 599 601 599 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">26</xref>
Taken together, the hypothetical protein encoded by ORF7 is likely to be a secreted repeat protein, a member of a novel repeat protein family possibly sharing a conserved structural motif determined by DxxGN(x)nDxxGN repeats. We provisionally term it YsrA for Yersinia secretable repeat protein A. However, a possible role of YsrA as virulence factor is supported by its secretability, phosphorylatability, and the existence of DxxGN(x)nDxxGN repeats in eukaryotic proteins. Several bacterial toxins interfering with host cellular signalling pathways are suspected of being adopted from eukaryotes [26].
###end p 48
###begin title 49
3.5. Expression of ORF3 and ORF7 Products and Relation to the Adhesive Phenotype
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 132 133 132 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 157 168 157 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="sec" rid="sec3.4">Section 3.4</xref>
All hypothetical proteins encoded by pYe4449-1 and pYe4449-2 were checked for a putative secretion signal using the SignalP server [9]. Apart from ORF7 (see Section 3.4.), only ORF3 was identified to likely encode a secreted protein (signal peptide probability: 0.996). In an attempt to investigate a possible role of ORF3 and ORF7 in adhesion of strain 07-04449 to eukaryotic host cells, we screened for EZ-Tn5 <Kan-2> transposon insertions into these ORFs. We identified insertions into ORF3 and ORF7 after base pair positions 4303 and 6387, respectively. Plasmid profiles and PCR analyses confirmed that pYe4449-2 was completely substituted for the respective mutated plasmid in the host strain. The mutants were compared to the parental strain with respect to cellular adhesion as described earlier. However, both were indistinguishable from the wild-type strain suggesting that neither ORF3 nor ORF7 contributes to adhesion. 
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 160 161 160 161 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sub>
###xml 423 424 423 424 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sub>
###xml 449 450 449 450 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sub>
###xml 644 654 626 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig4"> Figure 4 </xref>
###xml 744 745 726 727 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sub>
###xml 812 813 788 789 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 1191 1192 1167 1168 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sub>
###xml 1431 1439 1395 1403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yersinia</italic>
###xml 1503 1507 1467 1471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">yltA</italic>
Next, we constructed plasmids to complement the ORF3 and ORF7 mutant strains using plasmid pACYC184 as backbone. In doing so, we introduced an Arg-Gly-Ser-(His)6 epitope-encoding sequence at the 3'-end of each ORF construct to allow detection of the respective protein by Western blotting. In each case, the likely promoter region according to Softberry analysis () was included. The resulting plasmids pACYC184/ORF3-RGS(H)6 and pACYC184/ORF7-RGS(H)6 were transformed into insertional mutant strains carrying pYe4449-2/ORF3::Tn5 and pYe4449-2/ORF7::Tn5, respectively. Cultures were analysed after growth at 37degreesC, 27degreesC and 4degreesC. Figure 4 shows a Western blot analysis of the expression of epitope-tagged ORF3 product ORF3-RGS(H)6 after incubation at 4degreesC over night. Detection with an RGS(H)4-specific antibody revealed a band of 20-22 kDa in the bacterial pellet fraction (P) of the complemented ORF3 mutant but not in the corresponding supernatant (S). Since the calculated molecular mass of the tagged ORF3 product is 22.1 kDa for the unprocessed protein and 19.7 kDa after processing of the predicted secretion signal, it remains to be determined whether ORF3-RGS(H)6 was cytosolic or associated with the bacterial envelope after sec-dependent translocation. Strikingly, no expression was detected after incubation at 27degreesC or 37degreesC (data not shown). The ORF3 gene product was, therefore, termed Yersinia low temperature protein A (YltA) and the gene name abbreviated yltA accordingly. By contrast, no epitope-tagged ORF7 product could be detected in an analogous experiment (data not shown). We attribute this to specific characteristics of the ORF7 product for the reason that we could successfully produce a recombinant glutathione S-transferase (GST)-ORF7 fusion protein lacking the putative secretion signal (data not shown). However, after cleavage of the GST fusion with thrombin, only released GST could be visualized by Coomassie or silver stain on denaturing gels, but not the ORF7 fusion part. We have ruled out intrinsic thrombin cleavage sites, both by in silico analysis and by kinetic analyses of the cleavage reaction. We thus conclude that the lack of evidence of ORF7 expression could be due to aberrant behaviour of the protein during preparation. 
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 48 50 48 50 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">27</xref>
###xml 52 54 52 54 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">28</xref>
###xml 188 205 188 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 188 205 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
It is interesting to note that Bresolin et al. [27, 28] recently demonstrated the enhanced expression of several genes, including insecticidal toxin genes, in a virulence plasmid-carrying Y. enterocolitica strain at 10degreesC suggesting that invertebrate hosts are part of their life cycle. Evidence presented on that ancestors of strain 07-04449 were in genetic exchange with endosymbionts of insects, and the finding of enhanced expression of YltA at low temperature fits well with this hypothesis.
###end p 52
###begin title 53
3.6. Detection of Virulence-Associated Heat-Stable Enterotoxin B Gene (ystB) and Prevalence of pYe4449 Plasmids
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 116 120 116 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ystB</italic>
###xml 161 163 161 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">29</xref>
###xml 202 204 202 204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">30</xref>
###xml 292 296 292 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ystB</italic>
###xml 589 593 589 593 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ystB</italic>
###xml 750 752 750 752 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">30</xref>
To indicate a possible virulence potential of isolate 07-04449, we checked for the presence of virulence-associated ystB encoding the heat-stable enterotoxin B [29]. A recent study by Bhagat and Virdi [30] described the correlation of virulence-associated genes of biotype 1A strains such as ystB with clonal groups rather than with source of isolation (that is clinical or nonclinical). Among the 20 isolates we had initially screened for adherence, four isolates were of nonclinical origin (two from pork including 07-04449, one from lettuce, one from cured trout). All 20 isolates were ystB-positive (data not shown) and, therefore, likely belong to clonal group A which is believed to be better equipped for virulence compared to clonal group B [30]. This analysis may further indicate that clonal group B is underrepresented among isolates from Germany. However, a more comprehensive study is required to substantiate this trend.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 139 156 139 156 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 190 207 190 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 514 518 514 518 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">repA</italic>
###xml 535 539 535 539 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">mobA</italic>
###xml 139 156 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 190 207 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
Further, data on the prevalence of plasmids pYe4449-1 and pYe4449-2 were collected. To this end, we have analysed plasmid profiles from 54 Y. enterocolitica biotype 1A isolates and from 348 Y. enterocolitica isolates belonging to pYV carrying biotypes 2-5. Of the 54 biotype 1A strains analysed, 18 strains harboured plasmids with a size similar to pYe4449-1 and none was found with a size similar to that of pYe4449-2. The 18 strains with small plasmids were analysed by PCR with a pair of primers binding to the repA gene and to the mobA gene, respectively, designed to amplify the ccdA/B region inbetween. Two strains gave positive results, with amplicons similar in length to those obtained from the pYe4449-1 control, indicating homologous backbones. However, none of these plasmids was positive in an ORF5-specific PCR. Among the 348 pYV-carrying isolates, 21 harboured additional plasmids but none was similar in size to either pYe4449-1 or pYe4449-2. Taken together, we found both of the plasmids described here to be unique among the isolates tested.
###end p 55
###begin title 56
3.7. Concluding Remarks
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 15 17 15 17 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">31</xref>
###xml 100 117 100 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 309 326 309 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 399 407 399 407 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yersinia</italic>
###xml 628 645 628 645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 100 117 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 309 326 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 628 645 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
Howard et al. [31] have recently determined the core gene pool of 94 representatives of the species Y. enterocolitica. Strikingly, only 20.8% of the genes were shared by all strains illustrating the exceptional heterogeneity of this species. Our description of two novel plasmids adds to the diversity of the Y. enterocolitica gene pool. Interestingly, not a single ORF showed highest similarity to Yersinia sequences suggesting that this species was or still is in intensive genetic exchange with bacteria including genera that are not in the focus of any research yet. Consequently, efforts in understanding the life cycle of Y. enterocolitica will help to understand these genetic fluxes and vice versa. Studying plasmid biology of yersiniae will be a main pillar of such endeavours.
