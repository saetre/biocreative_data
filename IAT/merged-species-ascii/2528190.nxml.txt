The proofreading exonuclease subunit epsilon of Escherichia coli DNA polymerase III is tethered to the polymerase subunit alpha via a flexible linker

###xml 0 16 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Escherichia coli DNA polymerase III holoenzyme is composed of 10 different subunits linked by noncovalent interactions. The polymerase activity resides in the alpha-subunit. The epsilon-subunit, which contains the proofreading exonuclease site within its N-terminal 185 residues, binds to alpha via a segment of 57 additional C-terminal residues, and also to theta, whose function is less well defined. The present study shows that theta greatly enhances the solubility of epsilon during cell-free synthesis. In addition, synthesis of epsilon in the presence of theta and alpha resulted in a soluble ternary complex that could readily be purified and analyzed by NMR spectroscopy. Cell-free synthesis of epsilon from PCR-amplified DNA coupled with site-directed mutagenesis and selective 15N-labeling provided site-specific assignments of NMR resonances of epsilon that were confirmed by lanthanide-induced pseudocontact shifts. The data show that the proofreading domain of epsilon is connected to alpha via a flexible linker peptide comprising over 20 residues. This distinguishes the alpha : epsilon complex from other proofreading polymerases, which have a more rigid multidomain structure.

INTRODUCTION

###xml 811 812 749 750 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 82 98 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
The DNA polymerase III (Pol III) holoenzyme is the major chromosomal replicase in Escherichia coli (1). This enzyme complex is composed of 10 different polypeptide subunits. The catalytic core contains one each of the alpha (130 kDa), epsilon (27 kDa) and theta (8.8 kDa) subunits encoded by the dnaE, dnaQ and holE genes, respectively. The alpha-subunit contains the 5'-3' DNA polymerase active site (2,3), the epsilon-subunit is responsible for 3'-5' proofreading exonuclease activity (4) and the theta-subunit has no identified enzymatic activity (5). The alpha:epsilon:theta core complex is active alone as a proofreading DNA polymerase, and copurification of these three subunits demonstrates their tight physical association (6,7). Direct interactions between epsilon and alpha (8) and epsilon and theta (5) have been demonstrated using purified subunits, but no interaction has been detected between alpha and theta.
###end p 4

###xml 689 691 657 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 426 442 <span type="species:ncbi:10678">bacteriophage P1</span>
###xml 496 503 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The theta-subunit is not essential, as a DeltaholE mutant is normally viable (9) and theta has only a modest stimulatory effect on the exonuclease activity of epsilon on a mispaired primer terminus (5). Genetic studies with the temperature-sensitive dnaQ49 mutant allele indicated that theta stabilizes the structure of epsilon (10), and this effect may also be achieved by the theta homolog HOT (homolog of theta) encoded by bacteriophage P1 (11). Remarkably, the phage genome (94 kb) relies on E. coli Pol III for its replication but, with the exception of the Ban (DnaB analog) DNA helicase and SSB (single-stranded DNA-binding protein), does not encode any other replication proteins (12).

Two crystal structures of alpha have been determined: of a C-terminally truncated version of E. coli alpha (13), and of full-length Thermus aquaticus alpha (14). Structures are also known of the N-terminal globular domain of epsilon (epsilon186), both alone (15) and in complex with HOT (16,17). The structure of the epsilon186:theta complex determined by nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy (18,19) is in agreement with the epsilon186:HOT structure. No structure has been determined of full-length epsilon but residues following epsilon186 within its C-terminal region (in the following referred to as C-terminal segment (CTS) of epsilon or epsilonCTS; Figure 1) are known to be responsible for binding of alpha (20,21). This 57-residue segment contains a Q-linker sequence proposed to provide a flexible tether between domains (22), followed by the C-terminal 40-residue segment that has been shown to interact tightly with alpha when fused to maltose-binding protein (21). ###begin p 8
The present study was carried out to obtain structural information about the alpha:epsilon:theta complex. By studying the epsilon:theta, alpha:epsilon and alpha:epsilon:theta complexes and probing potential weak interactions between alpha and epsilon186 by a novel two-pronged NMR assignment strategy, we show that residues in the epsilonCTS are flexible and that those in the Q-linker region remain so even when assembled in the Pol III core complex. This provides the first experimental evidence that epsilon is indeed tethered to alpha by a flexible peptide linker, suggesting a mechanism for transition between polymerization and proofreading modes in Pol III that is fundamentally different from those in other polymerases whose structures in both modes are known. In addition, experiments to produce epsilon by cell-free protein synthesis shed further light on the function of theta.

Samples of the epsilon:theta complex containing site-specific mutants of epsilon were produced by cell-free protein synthesis from linear PCR-amplified DNA with 5'-phosphorylated primers to promote ligation to circular DNA in the reaction mixture (35). The DNA was prepared in two steps, using Vent DNA polymerase (New England Biolabs, Ipswich, MA, USA) for all PCR reactions. First, the site-specific mutation was introduced using two separate PCR reactions (50 mul each) with 20-30 ng of pSH1017 template, where the first reaction used primer 1133 and one of the reverse primers of Table S1 (containing the desired mutation) and the second reaction used one of the forward primers of Table S1 and primer 1134. The PCR products were mixed in an equimolar ratio and the residual primers removed using the Qiaquick PCR purification kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). In the second step, the T7 promoter and terminator sequences were added in two separate PCR reactions (50 mul each) using the primer pairs 1131 and 1134, and 1132 and 1133, respectively (Table S1), with 20-30 ng of purified PCR product from the first step. The two PCR products were mixed in an approximately equimolar ratio and the residual primers removed as above, denatured at 95degreesC (5 min) and reannealed at room temperature (approximately5 min). Cell-free synthesis of full-length epsilon at 37degreesC produced similar yields in both the autoinduction system with coexpression of T7 RNA polymerase from plasmid pKO1166 (33) and in our standard system with purified T7 RNA polymerase (28,31). Yields were about 3-4 mg/ml at 37degreesC and 2 mg/ml at 30degreesC. At both temperatures, the protein was produced in insoluble form. Similarly, the protein was in inclusion bodies when overexpression was carried out in vivo at 30 or 37degreesC, using standard expression protocols in LB medium (25,37), or at room temperature using the autoinduction medium described by Studier (26).

Cell-free synthesized samples of the 36 kDa epsilon:theta complex, where epsilon was selectively labeled with 15N-Ala or 15N-Thr, were found to be adequate for the acquisition of 15N-HSQC NMR spectra without chromatographic purification (Figure 3). The cross-peaks of the N-terminal domain of epsilon were readily assigned by comparison with the reported assignments of epsilon186 (38,39). Conservation of their chemical shifts indicated that neither the structure of the N-terminal domain nor its interface with theta is significantly affected by the presence of the epsilonCTS, and provide no indication that the epsilonCTS interacts with portions of the folded core of epsilon186. The additional cross-peaks observed must arise from Ala and Thr residues in the epsilonCTS (Figure 1). Their chemical shifts are characteristic of a random-coil polypeptide chain. Many of them are also more intense than the signals from the globular N-terminal domain but their line widths are not sufficiently narrow to indicate that the segment is completely free in solution. In addition, the 15N-Ala labeled sample displays at least one more cross-peak than expected from the amino acid sequence and peak doubling is observed also in the 15N-Thr labeled sample (see below). This may be explained by a heterogeneous chemical environment due to nonspecific interaction with other components of the cell extract, considering that the cell-free reaction mixture inhibited the binding of the epsilon:theta complex to alpha (see below). Initially, we attempted to form the alpha:epsilon:theta complex by the addition of purified alpha to the NMR sample containing the unpurified epsilon:theta complex. 15N-HSQC spectra of samples containing 15N-Ala or 15N-Thr labeled epsilon were, however, indistinguishable from spectra in the absence of alpha even after addition of alpha in 2-fold excess. This seemed to be due to nonspecific association of epsilon with other proteins (or nucleic acids) in the cell-free reaction mixture since after partial purification via a DEAE column, the epsilon:theta complex readily bound to alpha (data not shown). Therefore, we prepared the alpha:epsilon:theta complex by cell-free synthesis of epsilon in the presence of purified alpha and theta. Subsequent purification of the complex was straightforward, requiring only two steps of chromatography (Figure 2C).

To verify that these 15N-alanine and 15N-threonine resonance assignments also apply to the alpha:epsilon:theta complex, we added paramagnetic lanthanides to Pol III core complexes containing appropriately labeled epsilon (Figure 5). The epsilon-subunit has a natural metal-binding site that can bind a single lanthanide ion, resulting in substantial pseudocontact shifts (PCS) (40), which displace each 15N-HSQC cross-peak by a chemical shift increment that is similar in the 1H and 15N dimensions. PCS could indeed be measured in the alpha:epsilon:theta complex (Figure 5). The sign and magnitudes of the PCS observed for Ala4, Thr6, Thr183 and Ala186 with Dy3+, Tb3+ and Er3+ were in agreement with those measured previously for epsilon186 (39). Along the sequence Ala186, Ala188, Thr193, Ala200 and Thr201, the PCS steadily decreased toward zero. This would be expected for a flexible polypeptide chain for which positive and negative PCS are averaged by extensive sampling of the space around the metal ion.

In summary, we have demonstrated that the Pol III alpha:epsilon:theta core complex is characterized by substantially increased mobility of all Ala and Thr residues in the polypeptide segment between Thr183 and Thr201 of epsilon, whereas residues farther toward the C-terminus are immobilized by binding to alpha.

The N-terminal domain of epsilon, epsilon186, has been shown previously to be incapable of forming an isolable complex with alpha (21,41). If the epsilon:theta complex is tethered to alpha via a long flexible linker involving the C-terminal residues of epsilon, weak binding of the globular N-terminal domain of epsilon to alpha may still be sufficient to put the exonuclease domain in a defined location with respect to the polymerase subunit. 15N-HSQC spectra of the epsilon186:theta complex with uniformly 15N-labeled epsilon186, however, were not affected by the presence of alpha (data not shown), indicating that any association between epsilon186 and alpha would involve a dissociation constant Kd > 0.1 mM. This lower limit for Kd is consistent with a reported estimate from gel filtration data (21).

###begin title 54
DISCUSSION
###end title 54
###begin p 55
###xml 111 115 99 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dnaQ</italic>
###xml 229 231 217 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 1038 1046 958 966 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">Figure 3</xref>
In contrast to the soluble N-terminal domain of the epsilon-subunit, epsilon186, overexpression of full-length dnaQ leads to insoluble protein which in the past could only be solubilized by a denaturation and refolding protocol (37). Cell-free synthesis circumvents this problem elegantly by allowing the formation of protein-protein complexes from nascently produced epsilon in the presence of its natural binding partners theta and alpha. In addition, nascently synthesized epsilon readily formed the ternary alpha:epsilon:theta complex, whereas the epsilon:theta complex was unable to bind to alpha in the reaction mixture of cell-free synthesis. This effect appears to be due to an engagement of the epsilonCTS in nonspecific interactions with other components of the cell-free reaction mixture, as partial purification of the epsilon:theta complex by anion exchange chromatography restored the binding capacity to alpha. The nonspecific interactions also explain the apparent heterogeneity observed for the NMR signals from the CTS (Figure 3).
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 182 184 160 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 293 295 261 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
Our experiments confirm the role of theta as an important factor for stabilizing the structure of epsilon. The epsilon186 domain is prone to irreversible denaturation at 25degreesC (42), whereas the complex with theta can be studied at 30degreesC without degradation of NMR signals over time (38). The temperature sensitivity of the N-terminal domain of epsilon explains why the presence of theta is required during cell-free synthesis of epsilon to obtain the full-length epsilon:theta complex in soluble form. In the absence of theta, cell-free synthesis of epsilon leads to precipitation even in the presence of alpha. Precipitation is more pronounced at higher temperatures, in agreement with the sensitivity of the N-terminal domain to denaturation. The fact that alpha coprecipitates with epsilon highlights the independent roles of the N- and C-terminal domains of epsilon, where binding to alpha via the epsilonCTS is still possible if the globular N-terminal domain unfolds and aggregates.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 46 53 36 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 131 133 121 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
Whether theta acts as a chaperone for epsilon in vivo is not clear, in view of the low cellular concentration of Pol III subunits (43). In any case, neither theta nor the N-terminal domain of epsilon seem to act as a specific receptor of the epsilonCTS in the absence of alpha, as NMR spectra of epsilon:theta samples containing selectively labeled epsilon and uniformly labeled theta did not reveal significant differences from corresponding spectra of the epsilon186:theta complex.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 188 190 182 184 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">15</sup>
###xml 206 215 200 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">Figures 3</xref>
###xml 221 222 215 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 614 621 568 575 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">dnaQ932</italic>
###xml 817 819 755 757 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
Among the C-terminal 57 residues of epsilon, the segment between Ala186 and Thr201 is much more mobile than the residues closer to the C-terminus. Judging by the signal intensities of the 15N-Ala residues (Figures 3A and 5A), this holds both for the epsilon:theta and alpha:epsilon:theta complexes. Therefore, the 'sticky' part of the epsilonCTS is limited to residues following Thr201. In the complex with alpha, the contacts must involve residues close to or even including the C-terminal alanine residue of epsilon, as no NMR signal could be observed for this residue. This is consistent with properties of the dnaQ932 mutant allele, which encodes a version of epsilon lacking only the last three residues. Its recessive phenotype suggests it is incapable of competing with wild-type epsilon for binding to alpha (44). Considering that epsilon217 can coprecipitate a C-terminally truncated form of alpha, alpha917 (), at least some of the residues between Thr201 and Thr218 seem to be involved in binding to alpha. This indicates a large binding epitope, which would be difficult to explore by site-directed mutagenesis.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
Residues 183-201 of epsilon thus form the core of a highly flexible tether in the alpha:epsilon:theta complex. Combined with the absence of detectable interactions between the epsilon186:theta complex and alpha, this means that the proofreading exonuclease domain enjoys an extraordinary degree of positional and orientational freedom with respect to the polymerase subunit to which it is tethered.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 98 100 88 90 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 141 143 131 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 144 146 134 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 371 373 351 353 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3+</sup>
###xml 407 409 387 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 527 535 501 509 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">Figure 5</xref>
Similar to epsilon, the polymerase active site of the alpha-subunit binds a divalent metal ion (Mg2+) in a similar coordination environment (13,14). It is likely that this ion can also be replaced by a lanthanide ion, but there was no evidence for PCS induced in epsilon by a paramagnetic lanthanide bound to alpha in any of our experiments, even though PCS induced by Dy3+ can extend over distances >40 A (45). As all PCS were readily explained as intramolecular effects from a lanthanide bound to the active site of epsilon (Figure 5), it is unlikely that the proofreading domain is positioned close to a lanthanide ion in the polymerase active site of alpha in the alpha:epsilon:theta complex. We do not know yet whether the presence of primer-template DNA or of any of the other subunits of the polymerase III holoenzyme complex would change this; there has certainly been no indication in published work of an interaction between epsilon and any Pol III subunit other than alpha and theta.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 23 25 23 25 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 121 122 111 112 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">K</italic>
###xml 122 123 112 113 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">d</sub>
###xml 219 221 201 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 222 224 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
Wieczorek and McHenry (46) reported that a 320-residue N-terminal segment of alpha binds epsilon with the same affinity (Kd about 5 nM) as the 1160-residue full-length alpha-subunit. In the crystal structures of alpha (13,14), the N-terminal 270 residues fold into a globular (php) domain of about 40 A diameter. Considering that a 20-residue peptide in extended conformation can span well over 60 A, the flexible tether by which epsilon is attached to alpha could readily bridge the distance between almost any attachment site on the php domain and the polymerase active site.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 309 311 295 297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 312 314 298 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 341 343 327 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
The alpha:epsilon:theta Pol III core complex is distinctly different from proofreading polymerases whose structures have been determined in that the polymerase and exonuclease active sites reside in different subunits. In examples of structures of single-chain proofreading polymerases like DNA polymerase I (47,48) and RB69 DNA polymerase (49), the proofreading domain shares a large and well-defined interface with the polymerase domain, suggesting that both domains are rigidly associated. Repair of a mismatched template-primer DNA end thus involves translocation of the DNA from the polymerase to the exonuclease active sites which are located about 30 A apart.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
Our observations that neither epsilon186 nor theta interact to any appreciable degree with the epsilonCTS or with alpha, and that the long linker between the catalytic domain of epsilon and the C-terminal region that is firmly bound to alpha is still flexible in the Pol III core complex, suggests a fundamentally different mechanism for transition of Pol III from the polymerization to the editing mode. The transition might involve conformational changes in alpha that expose a cryptic binding site for the epsilon186 domain, such that the two active sites are brought closer together, rather than protein-mediated transfer of the DNA substrate as occurs in other enzymes. Since we have shown the exonuclease domain to be relatively freely mobile in the alpha:epsilon:theta complex, it might thus be able to swing in closer to the polymerase active site to repair a mismatched terminus when this is required.
###end p 63
###begin title 64
CONCLUDING REMARKS
###end title 64
###begin p 65
###xml 304 306 304 306 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 612 620 606 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 841 843 835 837 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 881 883 875 877 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
In this work, we have exploited several powerful new techniques for structural biology in solution to study the structure of the Pol III core complex. The assignment of NMR resonances by site-directed mutagenesis has long been used in situations where resonance assignments by other means are difficult (50). As mutations can change the chemical shifts by perturbing the 3D protein structure, this strategy is not always successful. While this problem hardly occurs in random-coil polypeptide segments as in the C-terminal region of epsilon, chemical shift changes were indeed induced by the mutation of Ala186 (Figure 6D). In this situation, PCS induced by paramagnetic lanthanide ions provided a conclusive second line of evidence. We anticipate that the combination of mutation by PCR, cell-free protein synthesis from PCR-amplified DNA (35) and PCS induced by lanthanide tags (51) will become an attractive tool set for many hitherto difficult cases like those examined here.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 276 283 272 279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 382 384 374 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 544 546 524 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 671 673 643 645 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 674 676 646 648 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 276 283 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The present study also illustrates the power of cell-free protein synthesis in the presence of separately purified cofactors for the production of soluble protein-protein complexes. In a similar manner, we were able to produce the complex between the chi- and psi-subunits of E. coli Pol III in soluble form by cell-free synthesis of psi in the presence of separately purified chi (33). In the chi:psi complex, the N-terminal segment of 26 residues of psi is unstructured in the complex with chi, but required for binding to the gamma-subunit (33). Similarly, the Pol III tau-subunit binds to alpha via a C-terminal polypeptide segment which is unstructured in pure tau (52,53). The emerging picture of the polymerase III holoenzyme is that of a machine composed of globular building blocks that are linked by flexible tethers.
