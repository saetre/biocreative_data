Purification and kinetic studies of recombinant gibberellin dioxygenases
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
The 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases (2ODDs) of gibberellin (GA) biosynthesis have a key role in the metabolism of a major plant hormone. The activity of recombinant GA 2ODDs from many species has been characterised in detail, however little information relates to enzyme purification. Native GA 2ODDs displayed lability during purification.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 31 48 31 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 99 113 99 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pisum sativum </italic>
###xml 158 159 158 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</italic>
###xml 363 364 363 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 364 366 364 366 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m </italic>
###xml 364 366 364 366 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>m </italic></sub>
###xml 385 386 384 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 386 390 385 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max </italic>
###xml 386 390 385 389 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>max </italic></sub>
###xml 414 418 413 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 420 424 416 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 426 430 419 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 431 432 421 422 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 433 434 423 424 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 437 439 427 429 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20</sub>
###xml 463 483 453 473 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana</italic>
###xml 793 794 783 784 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 794 796 784 786 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m </italic>
###xml 794 796 784 786 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>m </italic></sub>
###xml 812 813 801 802 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 813 817 802 806 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max </italic>
###xml 813 817 802 806 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>max </italic></sub>
###xml 844 848 833 834 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 850 854 836 837 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 856 860 839 840 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 861 862 841 842 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 863 864 843 844 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 867 869 847 849 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20</sub>
###xml 31 47 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 99 112 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">Pisum sativum</span>
###xml 463 483 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
Two GA 2ODDs were expressed in Escherichia coli and purified to homogeneity. The GA 2-oxidase from Pisum sativum L., PsGA2OX1, was expressed as a glutathione s-transferase (GST) fusion. It was purified in the three steps of affinity chromatography, GST removal and gel filtration. Highly pure PsGA2OX1 was obtained at a yield of 0.3 mg/g of cells. It displayed a Km of 0.024 muM and a Vmax of 4.4 pkat/mg toward [1beta,2beta,3beta-3H3]GA20. The GA 3-oxidase from Arabidopsis thaliana, AtGA3OX4, was expressed as a poly(His)-tagged thioredoxin fusion. It was purified by Immobilised Metal Affinity Chromatography followed by gel filtration. Cleavage of the fusion took place between the two purification steps. Highly pure AtGA3OX4 was obtained at a yield of 0.01 mg/g of cells. It displayed a Km of 0.82 muM and Vmax of 52,500 pkat/mg toward [1beta,2beta,3beta-3H3]GA20.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 83 91 83 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 391 401 391 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in planta </italic>
###xml 449 453 449 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sln </italic>
###xml 83 90 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Fusion tags were required to stabilise and solubilise PsGA2OX1 and AtGA3OX4 during E. coli expression. The successful purification of milligram quantities of PsGA2OX1 enables mechanistic and structural studies not previously possible on GA 2ODDs. A moderate yield of pure AtGA3OX4 requires the further optimisation of the latter stages of the enzyme purification schedule. PsGA2OX1's action in planta as deduced from the effect of the null mutation sln on GA levels in seeds is in agreement with the kinetic parameters of the recombinant enzyme.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 256 257 256 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
The 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases (2ODDs) of gibberellin (GA) biosynthesis have a key role in plant hormone metabolism. Three principal classes of 2ODDs control the later stages of the pathway where bioactive GA is produced and inactivated (Figure 1). Activation and deactivation of the GA molecule are performed by GA 3-oxidase and GA 2-oxidase enzymes, respectively.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 157 158 157 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 258 259 258 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
The genes encoding GA 2ODDs have been described from many species. They belong to multigene families which show spatial and temporal patterns of expression [1]. Factors including hormone levels and light have been shown to influence GA 2ODD gene expression [2], suggesting that the enzymes are important in regulating the overall GA pathway. The genetic manipulation of GA levels for agricultural purposes has often involved 2ODD-catalysed steps.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 32 41 32 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 101 118 101 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 286 287 286 287 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 288 289 288 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 101 117 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
The GA 2ODDs are well suited to in vitro studies because they retain good activity when expressed in Escherichia coli and their natural substrates are mostly known and available in synthetic form. Thus, a reasonable amount of data relates to the activity of recombinant GA 2ODDs, e.g. [3-7]. Their catalysis generally appears to reflect that of the native enzymes.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 263 264 263 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 265 267 265 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 485 487 485 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
Unfortunately, the excellent literature on the activity of recombinant GA 2ODDs is accompanied by a lack of progress on their purification and structural characterisation. In native form the enzymes displayed a lability that hindered their complete purification [8-11]. The problems associated with this lability have ostensibly not been overcome through using recombinant expression methods. The sole report on a pure GA 2ODD relates to a native GA 20-oxidase from pumpkin endosperm [12].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 81 95 81 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pisum sativum </italic>
###xml 128 149 128 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana </italic>
###xml 81 94 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">Pisum sativum</span>
###xml 99 102 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">pea</span>
###xml 128 148 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
Herein, we report the successful purification of a recombinant GA 2-oxidase from Pisum sativum L. (pea) and a GA 3-oxidase from Arabidopsis thaliana (Arabidopsis), findings that represent a significant step for an important group of enzymes.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
Expression and purification of the GA 2-oxidase, PsGA2OX1
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 74 82 74 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 4 7 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">pea</span>
###xml 74 81 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The pea GA 2-oxidase, PsGA2OX1, accumulated intact and in soluble form in E. coli when expressed as a GST fusion in pGEX. The expression conditions employed were determined to be optimal with respect to incubation temperature and induction conditions. Trials with a GST fusion expression system driven by the stronger T7 promoter did not improve expression levels (results not shown).
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 114 116 114 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 211 213 211 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 373 375 373 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 470 472 470 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 480 481 480 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Affinity chromatography of GST-PsGA2OX1 from the soluble cell fraction gave a high degree of purification (Figure 2A). Subsequent removal of the GST tag yielded PsGA2OX1 with only two major contaminants (Figure 2A). Gel filtration removed these contaminants without significant loss of PsGA2OX1 and increased its purity to > 95% as estimated from SDS-PAGE analysis (Figure 2B). Pure PsGA2OX1 was obtained in milligram quantities and was concentrated to 10 mg/mL (Figure 2B, Table 1).
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 57 58 57 58 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 331 339 331 339 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 382 383 382 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 473 474 473 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 331 338 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
PsGA2OX1 was active at all stages of purification (Table 1). EDTA had an inhibitory effect on activity that was removed most effectively by dialysis rather than dilution. However, when crude samples were dialysed, activity diminished, presumably because of enzyme inactivation or degradation. In addition, endogenous components of E. coli lysates are suspected to inhibit GA 2ODDs [7]. Therefore, the activity measurements after the first two stages of purification (Table 1) gave apparent values.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
Concentrated highly pure PsGA2OX1 retained activity after storage at -70degreesC.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Kinetic analysis of PsGA2OX1
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 147 148 147 148 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 149 150 149 150 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 153 156 153 156 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 164 165 164 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 172 173 172 173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 173 175 173 175 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M </italic>
###xml 173 175 173 175 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>M </italic></sub>
###xml 214 215 213 214 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 215 219 214 218 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max </sub>
The activity of this pure PSGA2OX1 displayed a saturation curve of the Michaelis Menten type in response to increasing concentration of substrate [3H3]GA20 (Figure 3). The KM was calculated to be 0.024 muM and the Vmax 4.4 pkat/mg.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Expression and purification of the GA 3-oxidase, AtGA3OX4
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 226 228 226 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4A</xref>
The Arabidopsis GA 3-oxidase, AtGA3OX4, accumulated intact and in soluble form when expressed as a poly(His)-tagged TRX fusion in pET32 to a level that was discernible by SDS-PAGE analysis of the soluble cell fraction (Figure 4A).
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 200 204 200 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
During purification AtGA3OX4 tended to precipitate, although precipitation could be minimised during the first step by dilution of the soluble cell lysate and the use of a buffer of pH 6.3 with 10 mM beta-ME.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 96 98 96 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4A</xref>
###xml 106 107 106 107 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
TRX-AtGA3OX4 was purified to a high degree by Immobilised Metal Affinity Chromatography (Figure 4A, Table 2). Cleavage of the TRX tag from AtGA3OX4 was enabled by a thrombin site engineered into the expression construct - the Factor Xa site encoded by pET32 failed to cleave.
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 196 200 196 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 342 346 339 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 422 424 416 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4B</xref>
###xml 432 433 426 427 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
AtGA3OX4 was difficult to separate from TRX, remaining strongly associated with it subsequent to thrombin cleavage. The single factor identified that most obviously mitigated this association was beta-ME, with low pH further aiding separation. Resolution of the two proteins was achieved by gel filtration in a buffer of pH of 5.8 with 10 mM beta-ME. Highly pure AtGA3OX4 was thus obtained in microgram quantities (Figure 4B, Table 2).
###end p 26
###begin p 27
Once pure, AtGA3OX4 resisted concentration. The problem was partially overcome by the addition of Triton X-100. However, the maximum concentration of AtGA3OX4 achieved was 1.5 mg/mL and its activity did not tolerate freezing.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
Otherwise, AtGA3OX4 was active at all stages of purification, permitting a kinetic study on highly pure AtGA3OX4.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
Removal of the TRX tag had no obvious affect on AtGA3OX4 activity levels. Triton X-100 had an inhibitory effect but this seemed to disappear with dilution. The use of low pH buffers during purification did not obviously dampen AtGA3OX4's activity. Indeed, we found AtGA3OX4 displayed 80 % activity when assayed in the phosphate buffer of pH 6.3 used for the first purification step, providing the reducing agent DTT was present (results not shown). However, the buffer of pH 5.8 in the second step was not compatible with activity assays.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 248 250 248 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4B</xref>
In light of the difficulties associated with obtaining AtGA3OX4 in high yield, further purification of the fusion, TRX-AtGA3OX4, was conducted. Milligrams of highly pure TRX-AtGA3OX4 were obtained. The protein was amenable to concentration (Figure 4B) and could be frozen at -70degreesC without activity loss.
###end p 30
###begin title 31
Kinetic analysis of AtGA3OX4
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 139 140 139 140 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 141 142 141 142 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 145 148 145 148 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 156 157 156 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 164 165 164 165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 165 167 165 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M </italic>
###xml 165 167 165 167 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>M </italic></sub>
###xml 205 206 204 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 206 210 205 209 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max </sub>
The activity of highly pure AtGA3OX4 displayed a saturation curve of the Michaelis Menten type in response to increasing concentration of [3H3]GA20 (Figure 5). The KM was calculated to be 0.82 muM and the Vmax 52, 500 pkat/mg.
###end p 32
###begin title 33
Discussion
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 64 72 64 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 137 139 137 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 64 71 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 278 281 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">pea</span>
High levels of activity are frequently obtained for GA 2ODDs in E. coli expression systems in the absence of accumulated soluble enzyme [13]. The instability generally reported in native 2ODDs appears to be a feature of the recombinant enzymes. In early trials of GA 2ODDs, the pea GA 2-oxidase, PsGA2OX1, and the Arabidopsis GA 3-oxidase, AtGA3OX4, showed promise as candidates for purification (D.R. Lester, unpublished). In both cases, a N-terminal fusion tag was needed to obtain soluble intact protein.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 409 411 409 411 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">++</sup>
###xml 24 27 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">pea</span>
The purification of the pea GA 2-oxidase, PsGA2OX1, was straightforward. No problems were encountered during chromatography, tag removal or enzyme concentration. All steps of purification were performed in a buffer that preserved enzyme activity. The pH was within physiological range when the chelator EDTA and the reducing agent DTT were present. (The latter were considered desirable because PsGA2OX1 is Fe++-dependent and its active site is likely to be susceptible to metal-catalysed oxidation). The milligram amounts of PsGA2OX1 that we obtained are unprecedented for a highly pure GA 2ODD.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 61 64 61 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sln</italic>
###xml 101 104 101 104 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 130 132 130 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 133 135 133 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 176 179 176 179 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 327 329 327 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 330 332 330 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 339 343 339 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sln </italic>
###xml 419 422 419 422 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 427 430 427 430 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">29 </sub>
###xml 489 492 489 492 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 533 537 533 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sln </italic>
###xml 640 642 640 642 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20</sub>
###xml 702 704 702 704 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 769 771 769 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 790 792 790 792 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20</sub>
###xml 810 814 810 814 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SLN </italic>
###xml 818 822 818 822 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sln </italic>
###xml 870 872 870 872 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 941 950 941 950 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in planta</italic>
###xml 120 123 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">pea</span>
The gene encoding PsGA2OX1 is affected by the null mutation, sln, which causes the accumulation of GA20 within maturing pea seed [14,15]. The mutation has a lesser effect on GA20 levels in the stem, presumably because other GA 2-oxidases compensate for its loss in this tissue. Mature seeds contain at least two GA 2-oxidases [11,14]. The sln phenotype indicates that PsGA2OX1 is the main catalyst for the conversion GA20 to GA29 at latter stages of seed maturation, when 2-oxidation of GA20 takes place at high levels. Although the sln mutation is not deleterious to developing seeds, the mature seeds contain abnormally high amounts of GA20, which, after germination, is converted to the bioactive GA1 resulting in the slender (overgrowth) phenotype of the seedling [16]. The levels of GA20present in mature SLN and sln seeds are 11 ng/g and 4,118 ng/g, respectively [16]. The difference can be solely attributed to the action of PsGA2OX1 in planta.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 116 118 116 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 377 379 377 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 81 84 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">pea</span>
Multiple GA 2-oxidases have been previously partially purified in low yield from pea seeds of unspecified maturity [11]. The incomplete purification was attributed to a low abundance of the enzymes in the tissue, and instability during purification. It can be assumed that native PsGA2OX1 was among these GA 2-oxidases because its gene is expressed throughout seed maturation [15]. Recombinant expression methods have now enabled us to complete the successful purification of PsGA2OX1.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 18 19 17 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 19 21 18 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M </italic>
###xml 19 21 18 20 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>M </italic></sub>
###xml 54 57 53 56 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 141 143 139 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 164 165 162 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 165 167 163 165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M </italic>
###xml 165 167 163 165 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>M </italic></sub>
###xml 374 376 372 374 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20</sub>
###xml 106 109 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">pea</span>
At 0.024 muM, the KM of recombinant PsGA2OX1 toward GA20 was much lower than that obtained for the native pea seed GA 2-oxidases (1.55 muM) [11]. The comparison of KM results is complicated, however, because the native preparation contained multiple activities. Certainly, a high substrate affinity of PsGA2OX1 is consistent with its ability to clear the maturing seed of GA20.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 112 114 112 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 243 262 243 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Phaseolus vulgaris </italic>
###xml 263 264 263 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 323 324 323 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 324 328 324 328 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max </sub>
###xml 382 385 382 385 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 400 404 400 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SLN </italic>
###xml 408 412 408 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sln </italic>
###xml 91 94 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">pea</span>
###xml 243 261 <span type="species:ncbi:3885">Phaseolus vulgaris</span>
The gene encoding PsGA2OX1 is expressed relatively strongly throughout the latter weeks of pea seed maturation [15]. That the enzyme is abundant in mature seeds was supported by a study of GA 2-oxidases from this tissue in the related species Phaseolus vulgaris [8]. If present in abundance, it is plausible that PsGA2OX1 (Vmax of 4.4 pkat/mg) could account for the difference in GA20 levels between SLN and sln seeds.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 4 5 4 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 5 9 5 9 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max </sub>
###xml 61 64 61 64 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 101 103 101 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 274 275 274 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 275 279 275 279 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max </sub>
###xml 338 339 338 339 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 339 343 339 343 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max </sub>
###xml 364 367 364 367 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 375 376 375 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
The Vmax of the partially pure native GA 2-oxidases toward GA20 was 24.8 pmol/h mg (0.0068 pkat/mg) [11]. The higher figure of the recombinant enzyme suggests that its purity and/or retention of activity was enhanced compared with the native preparation. Interestingly, the Vmax of PsGA2OX1 was several orders of magnitude lower than the Vmax of AtGA3OX4 toward GA20 (Figure 5). However, this difference is difficult to interpret considering the two enzymes are from different species and are likely to be expressed differentially within the plant alongside isoenzymes.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 197 198 197 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
With their role in GA activation, the GA 3-oxidases are particularly interesting to study, but in native form the enzymes are difficult to purify. It required 6,661 immature seeds for Kwak et al. [9] to obtain 0.086 mg of enzyme described as partially pure. Recombinant methods enabled us to partially overcome the problems associated with purification of a recombinant GA 3-oxidase. Our experience provides valuable insight for future work.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 245 247 245 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 349 353 349 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
The first step we used in the purification of the Arabidopsis GA 3-oxidase, AtGA3OX4, was IMAC chromatography. This method does not permit the inclusion of DDT or EDTA in buffers and therefore has been considered unsuitable for purifying 2ODDs [17]. Nonetheless, AtGA3OX4 with high specific activity was obtained using this method. Evidently, 10 mM beta-ME was able to protect the enzyme active site from metal-catalysed oxidation during chromatography. The result suggests that this widely used and highly efficient purification strategy does not necessarily compromise the activity of a 2ODD.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 246 250 246 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 771 772 768 769 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
AtGA3OX4 showed a tendency to precipitate, one that became more pronounced when it was cleaved, but not physically separated, from TRX. This diminished the yield of the protein, particularly in the final step. A beneficial effect from low pH and beta-ME implied that an exposed cysteine was causing the precipitation. However, a combination of factors were probably to blame because the aforementioned conditions did not fully overcome the problem. Furthermore, replacement by site-directed mutagenesis of the cysteine predicted as most likely to be exposed (residue 9) brought no improvement. Certainly, AtGA3OX4 displayed hydrophobic behaviour when pure, sticking to tubes unless Triton X-100 was present. Hydrophobicity was also a feature of the GA 3-oxidase of bean [9].
###end p 43
###begin p 44
Good scope exists for further optimisation of the AtGA3OX4 purification method. Efficient separation of AtGA3OX4 and TRX will lead to a greatly increased yield. Hydrophobic interaction chromatography is a possible alternative to gel filtration in separating the two proteins.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
AtGA3OX4 was amenable to concentration and freezing in the presence of TRX, whether or not it was chemically attached to it. The identification of buffer ingredients that replace TRX's stabilising role, such as alternative detergents, salts or organic solvents may improve the behaviour of purified AtGA3OX4.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 276 278 276 278 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 398 401 398 401 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 459 460 459 460 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 460 464 460 464 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max </sub>
###xml 525 527 525 527 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20</sub>
###xml 576 577 576 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</italic>
###xml 577 581 577 581 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max </sub>
###xml 697 698 697 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 712 713 712 713 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 713 715 713 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M </italic>
###xml 713 715 713 715 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>M </italic></sub>
###xml 736 739 735 738 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 781 782 779 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 783 784 781 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 856 857 853 854 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
AtGA3OX4 is encoded by a member of a family of four GA 3-oxidase genes in Arabidopsis. Generally the non-13-hydroxylated GA pathway predominates in Arabidopsis, however recent studies found comparable levels of non-13-hydroxylated and 13-hydroxylated GAs in developing seeds [18]. This tissue is the main site of expression of the gene encoding AtGA3OX4 (AtGenExpress microarray data), therefore GA20 is likely to be a natural substrate of AtGA3OX4. The high Vmax gained here shows that AtGA3OX4 can efficiently metabolise GA20. The value of 52,500 pkat/mg is higher than the Vmax of 62 nmol/min mg (1,033 pkat/mg) reported for a purified GST fusion of another Arabidopsis GA 3-oxidase, AtGA3OX1 [7]. AtGA3OX4's KM of 0.82 muM toward GA20 was lower than that of AtGA3OX1 (10 muM) [6,7], yet higher than the value for the bean seed GA 3-oxidase (0.29 muM) [9]. Accumulated kinetic information with respect to both 13-hydroxylated and non-13-hydroxylated GAs on each of the GA 3-oxidases of Arabidopsis will give valuable insight into the control of active hormone levels in this model species.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
Conclusion
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 457 465 457 465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 457 464 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The previous lack of reports on the complete purification of GA 2-oxidases and GA 3-oxidases is probably due to a combination of their lability and their low abundance in most plant tissues. One might speculate that a crucial role in the control of active hormone levels requires a short enzyme half-life. This study on PsGA2OX1 and AtGA3OX4 shows that GA 2-oxidases and GA 3-oxidases are not necessarily inherently unstable and can be purified active from E. coli expression systems under standard conditions. Indeed, the GA 2-oxidase, PsGA2OX1, was exceptionally stable throughout purification. Problems were encountered with the Arabidopsis GA 3-oxidase, AtGA3OX4, due to its hydrophobicity and the presence of hydrostatic charges, rather than metal-catalysed autocleavage or proteolytic breakdown.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 96 98 96 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 99 101 99 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 217 219 217 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 220 222 220 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 347 349 347 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
Indeed, plant 2ODDs from secondary metabolic pathways are also problematic during purification [17,19]. Yet in some cases, they have been purified in high yield permitting detailed structural and mechanistic studies [20,21]. We note the recent elucidation of the structure of the labile 2ODD-related enzyme, ACC-oxidase, of ethylene biosynthesis [22]. Our work opens the door to similar studies on the GA 2ODDs.
###end p 49
###begin title 50
Methods
###end title 50
###begin title 51
Expression of PsGA2OX1
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 9 18 9 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PsGA2ox1 </italic>
###xml 50 53 50 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Eco</italic>
###xml 68 72 68 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tac </italic>
###xml 87 95 87 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 87 94 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The cDNA PsGA2ox1 (GenBank ) was ligated into the EcoRI site of the tac promoter-based E. coli expression vector pGEX4T2 (Amersham Biosciences).
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 76 84 75 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 101 108 100 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PsGA2ox</italic>
###xml 417 421 403 407 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>
###xml 76 88 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">E. coli BL21</span>
###xml 90 93 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">DE3</span>
A culture of 100 mL LB broth with 100 mug/mL ampicillin was inoculated with E. coli BL21 (DE3) pGEX4/PsGA2ox1 and grown overnight at 37degreesC with shaking. Thirty mL of starter culture was used to inoculate each of 3 flasks containing 1 L of LB broth with 100 mug/mL ampicillin. Flasks were shaken at 220 rev/min on a circular orbit of 2.54 cm at 37degreesC. Protein expression was induced when cells reached the OD600 of 1.0. After 2 h cells were harvested, washed with 1X PBS and frozen at -70degreesC.
###end p 53
###begin title 54
Purification of PsGA2OX1
###end title 54
###begin p 55
###xml 600 602 593 595 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g </italic>
###xml 794 800 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
The GST-PsGA2OX1 purification was performed with glutathione agarose beads (Amersham Biosciences) at 4degreesC according to the manufacturer's instructions with the following additional details. The buffer used for the lysis of cells and washing of beads was 50 mM Tris-HCl pH 8, 300 mM NaCl, 5 mM EDTA, 5 mM DTT and 0.4 mM PMSF. Cells (approximately 6 g) were resuspended in 120 mL of buffer and lysozyme was added to 0.1 mug/mL. After incubation at room temperature for 15 min, cells were lysed with a Vibra Cell sonicator at 65% amplitude for a total of 60 sec. The cell lysate was spun at 20,000 g and then GST-PsGA2OX1 was extracted from the supernatant using a bed volume of 2 mL of beads in batch mode. After a final wash, the beads were resuspended in 2 mL of buffer containing 10 U of bovine thrombin (Sigma) and incubated at 4degreesC overnight. Thrombin was extracted from the supernatant containing cleaved PsGA2OX1 using benzamidine agarose beads (Amersham Biosciences).
###end p 55
###begin p 56
The protein solution was concentrated to a volume of 300 muL using a Vivascience spin concentrator and applied to a Superdex 75 column (Amersham Biosciences). Gel filtration was performed at 6degreesC in the same buffer as was used for affinity chromatography. After separation, fractions containing PsGA2OX1 were pooled and dialysed versus 10 mM Tris-HCl pH 8.0, 1 mM DTT.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
Pure PsGA2OX1 protein was concentrated to 10 mg/mL using a Vivascience spin concentrator and frozen.
###end p 57
###begin title 58
Expression of AtGA3OX4
###end title 58
###begin p 59
###xml 9 18 9 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AtGA3ox4 </italic>
###xml 34 55 34 55 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana </italic>
###xml 34 54 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
The cDNA AtGA3ox4 (GenBank ) from Arabidopsis thaliana was amplified by PCR using Advantage cDNA polymerase mix (Clontech) with the forward primer 5'- GGTATTGAGGGTCGCCTGGTGCCACGCGGTTCTATGCCTTCACTAGCAGAAGAG-3' and reverse primer 5'-AGAGGAGAGTTAGAGCCTTAATTGGTGGGATTAACGAC-3'.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
The cDNA was inserted into the T7 promoter-based expression plasmid pET32 Xa/LIC (Novagen) using ligation independent cloning according to the manufacturer's instructions. The resulting construct expressed AtGA3OX4 with an N-terminal tag of both poly(His) and thioredoxin (TRX). A thrombin site was adjacent to the N-terminal of AtGA3OX4, encoded by the forward PCR primer.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 76 84 75 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 392 396 378 382 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>
###xml 76 88 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">E. coli BL21</span>
###xml 90 93 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">DE3</span>
A culture of 100 mL LB broth with 100 mug/mL ampicillin was inoculated with E. coli BL21 (DE3) pET32/AtGA3ox4 and grown overnight at 37degreesC with shaking. Thirty mL of starter culture was used to inoculate each of 3 flasks containing 1 L of LB broth with 100 mug/mL ampicillin. The flasks were incubated at 37degreesC with shaking at 220 rev/min on a circular orbit of 2.54 cm until the OD600 reached 1.0. IPTG was added to 1 mM and the cultures were grown for a further 3 h. Cells were harvested by centrifugation, washed with 1X PBS and frozen at -20degreesC.
###end p 61
###begin title 62
Purification of AtGA3OX4
###end title 62
###begin p 63
###xml 109 113 109 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 305 307 301 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g </italic>
###xml 565 569 555 556 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
Cells (approximately 7 g) were thawed and resuspended at room temperature in 120 mL of 5 mM imidazole, 10 mM beta-ME, 1X PBS pH 6.3 with 100 mug/mL lysozyme. Cell lysis was performed in a tube on ice using a Vibra Cell sonicator at 65% amplitude for 6 bursts of 10 s. The lysate was centrifuged at 20,000 g at 4degreesC and the soluble fraction was retained and kept on ice. Two more 10 s bursts of sonication were performed to remove high molecular weight DNA. The soluble fraction was diluted to a total volume of 300 mL with 1X PBS pH 6.3, 5 mM imidazole, 10 mM beta-ME.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
All column chromatography steps in the purification of AtGA3OX4 were performed at 6degreesC.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 111 114 111 114 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">++ </sup>
###xml 218 222 218 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 336 340 333 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 410 416 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
The crude protein solution was passed over a 5 mL HiTrap Chelating column (Amersham Biosciences) loaded with Ni++ using an Akta Purifier chromatography system. The column was washed with 50 mL of 5 mM imidazole, 10 mM beta-ME, 1X PBS pH 6.3 and the poly(His) TRX-AtGA3OX4 was eluted with a linear gradient of 0 -200 mM imidazole, 10 mM beta-ME, 1X PBS pH 6.3. Fractions containing TRX-AtGA3OX4 were pooled and bovine thrombin (Sigma, 20 U) was added.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 150 154 144 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
After incubation at 6degreesC overnight, cleaved protein was concentrated and loaded onto a Superdex 75 column equilibrated with 1X PBS pH 5.8, 10 mM beta-ME. Fractions containing AtGA3OX4 were pooled and concentrated to 1.5 mg/mL with 1% Triton X-100.
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 137 141 137 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 266 270 263 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
In parallel experiments, the intact fusion TRX-AtGA3OX4 was further purified by gel filtration on Superdex 200 with 1X PBS pH 5.8, 10 mM beta-ME. The purest fractions were pooled, protein was concentrated to 10 mg/mL and dialysed against 10 mM Tris-HCl pH 8.0, 2 mM beta-ME.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
Protein concentrations were measured with the Bradford method.

###begin title 69
Activity assays on the substrate GA20
###end title 69
###begin p 70
###xml 16 20 16 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 22 26 19 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 28 32 22 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 33 34 24 25 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 35 36 26 27 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 39 42 30 33 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
###xml 66 68 57 59 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 140 144 131 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">via </italic>
###xml 248 249 239 240 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 296 300 287 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946;</italic>
###xml 303 308 291 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946; </italic>
###xml 313 318 298 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#946; </italic>
###xml 329 331 311 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 351 354 333 336 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">29 </sub>
###xml 359 361 341 343 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">29</sub>
###xml 436 437 418 419 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 538 539 520 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 540 542 522 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 543 545 525 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 624 626 606 608 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sub>
###xml 627 628 609 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
The substrate [1beta,2beta,3beta-3H3]GA20 (approx. 0.4 TBq/mmole [23]), enabled the measurement of activity from both PsGA2OX1 and AtGA3OX4 via the formation of tritiated water. Quantitative estimates of product formation were possible because the 3H was incorporated at a ratio of 1:4:4 at the 1beta, 2beta and 3beta positions [23]. The conversion GA29 to GA29-catabolite was assumed to make a relatively insignificant contribution to 3H release by PsGA2OX1 under the conditions of assay. This assumption was based on previous findings [4,14,15]. Similarly, it was assumed that AtGA3OX4 produced insignificant amounts of GA5 [4]. It should be noted that the kinetic parameters may be influenced by the presence of the label in the substrate due to a tritium isotope effect.
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 95 97 95 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 225 226 224 225 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 636 639 624 627 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20 </sub>
The standard assays were conducted using cofactors concentrations of established methods e.g. [14]. Reactions were in a volume of 200 muL with 100 mM Tris-HCl pH 7.9, 4 mM ascorbate, 4 mM DTT, 4 mM 2-oxoglutarate, 0.5 mM FeSO4, 2 mg/mL BSA and 1 mg/mL catalase. Substrate (5 muL in methanol) was added to a concentration of 0.02 muM for PsGA2OX1 assays and 0.1 muM for AtGA3OX4 assays. The enzyme solution was added in volumes ranging from 1 to 50 muL. Highly active solutions were serially diluted using 1 mg/mL BSA before addition. The reactions took place at 30degreesC and were stopped with 25 muL acetic acid after 1 h. Labelled GA20 was precipitated by adding 780 muL of 25 mM EDTA containing 50 mg/mL activated charcoal and centrifuging at 5,000 rev/min for 5 min. The release of tritiated water was measured by radiocounting a 300 muL reaction aliquot in scintillation fluid with tritium standards alongside. The specific activity figures for each purification stage were derived from enzyme samples (or dilutions of samples) showing similar rates of product conversion.
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 16 17 16 17 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 18 19 18 19 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 22 24 22 24 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20</sub>
###xml 91 92 91 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 97 98 97 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
The substrate, [3H3]GA20, was varied in the assays for kinetic studies as shown in Figures 3 and 5. At higher substrate concentrations the maximum volume of methanol added was 10 muL (the extra methanol was shown not to be inhibitory). The amount of enzyme added was 1 mug for PsGA2OX1 and 0.1 mug for AtGA3OX4. Results were analysed using Origin by MicroCal.

###xml 654 657 <span type="species:ncbi:3888">pea</span>
The latter steps of biosynthesis of non-13-hydroxylated/13-hydroxylated GAs. The steps surrounding production of bioactive GAs in higher plants are catalysed by the 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases (2ODDs); 1 GA 20-oxidase, 2 GA 3-oxidase and 3 GA 2-oxidase. In pea the 13-hydroxylated pathway predominates; in Arabidopsis the non-13-hydroxylated pathway generally predominates, however developing seeds contain comparable levels of non-13-hydroxylated and 13-hydroxylated GAs [18]. The sln phenotype of pea arises from a null mutation affecting the predominant GA 2-oxidase (PsGA2OX1) of maturing seeds (steps 3). In this study the activities of a pea GA 2-oxidase (PsGA2OX1) and arabidopsis GA 3-oxidase (AtGA3OX4) are both assayed using the substrate [1beta,2beta,3beta-3H3]GA20. It should be noted that in addition both enzymes are likely to use GA9 as a natural substrate.
