Reduction of Dopamine Level Enhances the Attractiveness of Male Drosophila to Other Males
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 119 129 119 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 286 296 286 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 459 469 459 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 616 626 616 626 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1138 1148 1138 1148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
Dopamine is an important neuromodulator in animals and its roles in mammalian sexual behavior are extensively studied. Drosophila as a useful model system is widely used in many fields of biological studies. It has been reported that dopamine reduction can affect female receptivity in Drosophila and leave male-female courtship behavior unaffected. Here, we used genetic and pharmacological approaches to decrease the dopamine level in dopaminergic cells in Drosophila, and investigated the consequence of this manipulation on male homosexual courtship behavior. We find that reduction of dopamine level can induce Drosophila male-male courtship behavior, and that this behavior is mainly due to the increased male attractiveness or decreased aversiveness towards other males, but not to their enhanced propensity to court other males. Chemical signal input probably plays a crucial role in the male-male courtship induced by the courtees with reduction of dopamine. Our finding provides insight into the relationship between the dopamine reduction and male-male courtship behavior, and hints dopamine level is important for controlling Drosophila courtship behavior.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 229 232 229 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Bradbury1">[1]</xref>
###xml 234 237 234 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Wyatt1">[2]</xref>
###xml 257 267 257 267 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 528 531 528 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Hall1">[3]</xref>
###xml 533 536 533 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Yamamoto1">[4]</xref>
###xml 538 548 538 548 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 656 659 656 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Hall1">[3]</xref>
###xml 661 664 661 664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Yamamoto1">[4]</xref>
###xml 666 669 666 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Lasbleiz1">[5]</xref>
###xml 752 755 752 755 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Greenspan1">[6]</xref>
Courtship behavior is fundamental to the propagation of animal species and their adaptation to circumstances. Animals use multiple sensory cues to recognize conspecifics and to choose a potentially suitable mate for reproduction [1], [2]. Male courtship in Drosophila is a complex behavior which consists of a stereotypic set of behaviors, including orientation towards a female, taping with its forelegs, following the female, singing courtship song, licking the female's genital, attempting copulation, and finally copulation [3], [4]. Drosophila courtship behavior depends on visual, acoustic and chemosensory signals exchanged by the two partner flies [3], [4], [5], some of which can stimulate male or female courtship while others can inhibit it [6]. Thus, abnormal courtship behavior can be induced by either the change of courter's sensory perception or the change of courtee's attractiveness and receptivity.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 126 129 126 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Melis1">[7]</xref>
###xml 131 134 131 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Hull1">[8]</xref>
###xml 277 280 277 280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Monastirioti1">[9]</xref>
###xml 282 286 282 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Wright1">[10]</xref>
###xml 442 452 442 452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 452 456 452 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Liu1">[11]</xref>
###xml 617 621 617 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Neckameyer1">[12]</xref>
###xml 623 627 623 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Neckameyer2">[13]</xref>
###xml 679 683 679 683 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Neckameyer1">[12]</xref>
Dopamine (DA) is a very important neuromodulator in animals. Its roles in mammal sexual behavior has been extensively studied [7], [8]. In insects, DA is used as a neurotransmitter in the central nervous system and as a cuticle hardening and pigmenting factor in the epidermis [9], [10]. Tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) is the rate-limiting enzyme for DA synthesis. Studies demonstrate that DA elevation can enhance male-male courtship behavior in Drosophila[11] and that DA reduction affects specific aspects of courtship behavior: female sexual receptivity to males and male courtship conditioning towards immature males [12], [13], but did not change male courtship towards females [12]. Since the effect of DA reduction on male-male courtship has not been reported before, we used genetic and pharmacological tools to investigate the relationship between DA reduction and male-male courtship. We found that DA reduction could induce male-male courtship by enhancing the attractiveness of courtees.
###end p 5

Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7

###xml 821 824 781 784 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 818 824 778 784 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 914 921 874 881 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004574-g001">Fig. 1A</xref>
###xml 1144 1151 1092 1099 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004574-g001">Fig. 1A</xref>
###xml 1232 1235 1180 1183 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 1229 1235 1177 1183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
The flies we used carried a temperature-sensitive allele of the pale gene (plets1; 25degreesC is the permissive temperature, 31degreesC is the restrictive temperature) [14] which encodes TH that is expressed in both larval and adult nervous systems and also in the larval epidermis [15]. To test the efficiency of plets1 mutation, we measured the DA content in the plets1 males raised at 25degreesC (18.7+/-2 ng/mg mass, s.e.m. N = 5) and at 31degreesC (8.6+/-1 ng/mg mass, s.e.m. N = 6) respectively. The data showed that the plets1 males raised at 31degreesC indeed had significant lower level of DA as compared with sibling males raised at 25degreesC (p = 0.004, Mann-Whitney Rank Sum test), which indicates plets1 mutation can efficiently decrease DA level at the restrictive temperature. Males homozygous for the plets1 allele showed markedly different chaining behavior (measured by the chaining index, ChI; Fig. 1A) according to the temperature at which they were raised during adulthood. Males continuously raised at 25degreesC showed rare male-male interaction while sibling males raised at 31degreesC showed a markedly increased ChI (Fig. 1A). This behavioral variation is likely related to the DA reduction induced by plets1 mutation since this defect could be rescued by the addition of DA precursor (L-DOPA) in the food. Indeed, males raised at 31degreesC and fed during adulthood with L-DOPA showed a much lower ChI than sibling males raised at 31degreesC on unsupplemented food.

###xml 1272 1281 1224 1233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004574-g001">Fig. 1A,B</xref>
The chaining behavior observed between manipulated males could result from the reciprocal alteration of either the courter, the courtee, or both partners. To clearly assess the behavior of each partner, we measured the courtship index (CI) directed or received by individual mutant plets1 males when paired with single wild-type males. When mutant males were used as targets, the CI that they received from wild-type males varied according to their treatment: plets1 males raised at 31degreesC on standard food induced a high CI (Fig. 1B; Movie S1), but when raised at 25degreesC on standard food, they induced no courtship. Males raised at 31degreesC on L-DOPA-supplemented food received a low CI from wild-type males. The wild-type males raised at 31degreesC also showed an obvious attractiveness for the wild-type males (CI = 12.94+/-2.4 s.e.m., N = 68). The male-male courtship behavior induced by plets1 males raised on L-DOPA-supplemented food at 31degreesC was not significantly different as compared with the wild-type males raised on unsupplemented food at 31degreesC (CIplets1 = 9.78+/-2.9 s.e.m.; CIwild-type = 12.94+/-2.4 s.e.m.; p = 0.14, Mann-Whitney Rank Sum test), but was significantly higher than the sibling raised on unsupplemented food at 25degreesC (Fig. 1A,B), which indicates that temperature also has an effect on the male-male courtship behavior.

###xml 34 37 34 37 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 31 37 31 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>

###xml 796 799 790 793 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 793 799 787 793 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 927 934 915 922 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004574-g001">Fig. 1B</xref>
In the reciprocal interaction, plets1 males, raised at either temperature, directed a very low CI to wild-type target males (Fig. 1C). Since temperature showed an obvious effect on this kind of male-male courtship behavior, we used a complementary strategy to rule out the effect of temperature on the attractiveness [16] and to confirm the implication of DA reduction in male homosexual attractiveness (the male tendency to stimulate the courtship of other males, as opposed to the male propensity to court other males). Wild-type males were raised at 25degreesC and fed with AMPT, a drug that tends to reduce DA synthesis. Wild-type males fed with AMPT elicited a strong CI of control males whereas no reciprocal interaction was noted (Fig. 1B,C). This effect is similar to that observed in plets1 males raised at 31degreesC. Wild-type males simultaneously fed with AMPT and L-DOPA elicited no CI of untreated sibling males (Fig. 1B). Taken together, these genetic and pharmacological experiments indicate that DA reduction enhanced male attractiveness - but did not change their courtship - to other males.

###xml 1315 1322 1297 1304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004574-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>
###xml 1342 1345 1324 1327 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 1339 1345 1321 1327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 1428 1435 1410 1417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004574-g002">Fig. 2B</xref>
Drosophila male courtship and mate discrimination depend mostly on the perception of visual, pheromonal and acoustic signals exchanged by partner flies [6]. To assess the involvement of these signals in the attractiveness shown by males with reduced DA level, several experiments were designed to eliminate the effect of each type of signal. We measured the CI induced by males under different sensory deprived condition (Fig. 2A). To eliminate the visual signals, wild-type males (used as courters) were tested with intact plets1 mutant males raised at 31degreesC under red light where they are virtually blind [17]. They were also surgically ablated both their antennae and maxillary palps (NAM) to reduce their perception of pheromones [17] and of acoustic signals [18]. In another experiment, the plets1 mutant males raised at 31degreesC were washed with hexane to rinse out the pheromones to remove the effects of these olfactory and gustatory inputs (Fig. 2B). Wild-type males either olfactory/auditory- or visually-deprived showed significantly reduced CI to intact plets1 mutant males raised at 31degreesC (Fig. 2A). The deprivation of olfactory/auditory inputs had (i) a more dramatic effect on CI than the visual input deprivation, and (ii) the same effect as the two deprivations simultaneously applied (Fig. 2A). The fact that plets1 males washed with hexane completely lost their attractiveness to wild-type males (Fig. 2B) indicates that cuticular chemicals are the critical sensory signals used by male flies with decreased DA level to stimulate the courtship of wild-type males. These signals could either increase male attractiveness or reduce their aversiveness to other males.
###end p 12

###begin p 15
###xml 118 121 118 121 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 115 121 115 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 313 317 313 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Yamamoto2">[19]</xref>
###xml 457 460 457 460 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 454 460 454 460 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 538 545 526 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004574-g002">Fig. 2C</xref>
###xml 647 651 635 639 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Savarit2">[20]</xref>
###xml 892 902 868 872 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">25&#176;C</sub>
###xml 929 939 897 901 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">31&#176;C</sub>
###xml 1016 1019 976 979 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 1013 1019 973 979 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 1344 1347 1298 1301 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 1341 1347 1295 1301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 1584 1587 1532 1535 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 1581 1587 1529 1535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 1701 1704 1643 1646 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 1698 1704 1640 1646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 1801 1808 1737 1744 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004574-g002">Fig. 2C</xref>
###xml 2090 2097 2020 2027 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0004574-g002">Fig. 2C</xref>
To study the identity of these chemical signals, we measured the cuticular pheromones in the diversely manipulated plets1 and wild-type males. It has been known that 7-tricosene (7-T; inhibiting male-male courtship) and 7-pentacosene (7-P; stimulating male-male courtship) are the two main pheromones of male fly [19], and the reduced ratio of 7T/7P might enhance the attractiveness of males. A significantly decreased ratio of 7T/7P was observed in the plets1 males raised at 31degreesC as compared with the sibling males at 25degreesC (Fig. 2C). Since it has been reported that temperature change can affect the expression pattern of pheromones [20], the cuticular pheromones of wild-type male raised at different temperatures were measured and the data showed the ratio of 7T/7P in wild-type males raised at 31degreesC was also much lower than the sibling males raised at 25degreesC (7T/7P25degreesC = 1.70+/-0.3 s.e.m.; 7T/7P31degreesC = 0.65+/-0.06 s.e.m.; p = 0.01, Student's t-test). The ratio of 7T/7P in plets1 males raised at 31degreesC was significantly higher than that of wild-type males raised at the same temperature (p<0.001, Student's t-test), yet they showed much more attractiveness to other males as compared with wild-type males (p<0.001, Mann-Whitney Rank Sum test). This suggested that the decreased ratio of 7T/7P in plets1 males raised at 31degreesC was probably not the reason for their attractiveness. Further experiments proved that the lower ratio of 7T/7P was not responsible for the male attractiveness induced by DA reduction, because that: (i) the plets1 males raised on L-DOPA-supplemented food at 31degreesC showed significantly lower ratio of 7T/7P than that of plets1 males raised on unsupplemented food 31degreesC, but their attractiveness was obviously reduced (Fig. 2C; p = 0.01, Student's t-test); (ii) The ratio of 7T/7P in the wild-type males with low level of DA produced by pharmacological method was not statistically different as compared with the sibling males raised on unsupplemented food or on L-DOPA&AMPT-supplemented food at 25degreesC (Fig. 2C; Student's t-test), but these male flies still showed obvious attractiveness. Particularly, no correlation was found with the ratio of the two principal cuticular pheromones: 7-T and 7-P, nor with either the percentage or the absolute amount of any other minor secondary compounds between 23 carbons and 29 carbons detected on their cuticle with a GC-MS device (not shown). Therefore, our data showed no obvious causal relationship between the intensity of their homosexual attractiveness produced by DA reduction and the variation of their pheromones we tested.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Discussion
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 405 409 405 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Marican1">[21]</xref>
###xml 661 665 661 665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Kopp1">[22]</xref>
###xml 667 671 667 671 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Llopart1">[23]</xref>
###xml 718 721 718 721 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 715 721 715 721 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 859 863 847 851 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Wright1">[10]</xref>
###xml 865 869 853 857 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Pendleton1">[14]</xref>
###xml 960 963 948 951 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 957 963 945 951 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
Our study reveals that reduced DA level in males highly increased their attractiveness to wild-type males, but had no effect on their propensity to court other males, and the enhanced male attractiveness was mainly attributed to the change of chemical signals. Although we did not find any candidate compound responsible for the male attractiveness, which was consistent with the result of Marican et al. [21], we suspect that other yet unknown pheromonal compounds, whose level depends upon the DA level, may regulate male attractiveness to other males. The male pigmentation has been proposed to be a sex- and species specific signal for courtship and mating [22], [23]. The pigmentation of bodies was lighter in plets1 males raised at the restrictive temperature (31degreesC) than in males raised at the permissive temperature (25degreesC; data not shown) [10], [14], suggesting that visual cues might be also involved in the increased attractiveness of plets1 mutant males. Furthermore, the decrease of rejection efforts led by reduction of DA level is another possibility for the male-male courtship induced by males with low level of DA.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 242 261 242 255 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">wild-type31&#176;C</sub>
###xml 286 305 278 291 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">wild-type25&#176;C</sub>
Although the male attractiveness induced by DA reduction did not depend on the high temperature, which was proved by our pharmacological assay, the data showed that temperature itself could also enhance male attractiveness for other males (CIwild-type31degreesC = 12.94+/-2.4 s.e.m.; CIwild-type25degreesC = 3.80+/-2.2 s.e.m.; p<0.001, Mann-Whitney Rank Sum test). Then, is the male attractiveness induced by high temperature related to low level of DA? To test this question, we measured the DA content of wild-type males raised at 31degreesC (19.0+/-2 ng/mg mass, s.e.m. N = 6) and 25degreesC (16.8+/-3 ng/mg mass, s.e.m. N = 6) respectively. Our data showed that there was no significant difference between the DA level of wild-type males raised at 31degreesC and at 25degreesC, which indicates that the male attractiveness induced by high temperature is unrelated to the reduction of DA level.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 198 202 198 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Liu1">[11]</xref>
###xml 407 417 407 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 494 504 494 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
Interestingly, increased DA levels in males can also induce male-male courtship, which is due to the change of the sexual propensity of courters but not the change of the attractiveness of courtees [11]. Either high level or low level of DA can induce male-male courtship behavior, while the mechanism is different. Variations of DA level affect different yet complementary aspects of courtship behavior in Drosophila males, which indicates that DA system plays an important role in regulating Drosophila male courtship behavior.
###end p 19
###begin p 20
Our study provides new insight on the regulation of male attractiveness. The next goal will be to precisely identify the cuticular pheromones affected in the DA-depleted male flies. One challenge will be to understand the biological basis of the reciprocal effect induced by DA variation (increase/decrease) on the modulation of sensory communication and whether this novel finding can be generalized to other animals.
###end p 20

###begin title 22
Fly culture and surgery
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 30 38 30 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Canton S</italic>
###xml 72 75 72 75 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 68 75 68 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">pale<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 80 83 80 83 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 77 83 77 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 120 124 120 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Pendleton1">[14]</xref>
###xml 191 192 191 192 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 745 746 709 710 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</italic>
###xml 872 874 836 838 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ii</italic>
###xml 903 906 867 870 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 900 906 864 870 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
###xml 1174 1177 1126 1129 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ts1</sup>
###xml 1171 1177 1123 1129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ple<sup>ts1</sup></italic>
The wild-type strain used was Canton S. The mutant strains used was palets1 (plets1, a temperature-sensitive TH allele) [14]. Flies were collected within 4 hours after eclosion under light CO2 anesthesia and reared on unyeasted standard medium (Bloomington) eventually supplemented with different drugs. Flies were reared either at 25degreesC or 31degreesC, with 60% relative humidity, and a 12hr:12hr light-dark photoperiod. Male used in behavioral assays were aged for 6approximately8 days when raised at 25degreesC and 5 days when raised at 31degreesC after eclosion respectively. The males' antennae&maxillary palps were ablated (NAM) when the flies were one-day-old and the experiments were conducted seven days later. For drug treatment: (i) To reduce DA level in wild-type males: 5 mg/ml alpha-Methyl-DL-tyrosine methyl ester hydrochloride (AMPT, Sigma) was used; (ii) To increase DA level in plets1 males raised at 31degreesC and in wild-type males raised on AMPT-containing food at 25degreesC: 3 mg/ml L-DOPA (Sigma) was used. For all drug treatments, flies were collected less than 4 hours after eclosion and raised on drug-containing food, until the test. The plets1 males were raised at 25degreesC before adult eclosion. After eclosion, they were either kept at 25degreesC and tested when 8 day-old, or raised at 31degreesC and tested when 5 day-old.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Behavior
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 0 22 0 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Paired courtship assay</italic>
###xml 0 22 0 22 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>Paired courtship assay</italic></bold>
###xml 301 305 301 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Villella1">[24]</xref>
Paired courtship assay was performed in a courtship chamber (12 mmx18 mm) with a pair of flies. The courtship index (CI) is the percentage of time in a 5-min observation period that the tester male spent courting the target fly (this includes tapping, following, wing vibration, attempted copulation) [24]. Since both flies had the same appearance, they were distinguished from each other by a color dot painted on the thorax of the target flies, a few minutes before the experiment. Tester males were reared individually whereas target flies were reared in groups of 40approximately50.
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 0 14 0 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chaining assay</italic>
###xml 0 14 0 14 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>Chaining assay</italic></bold>
###xml 83 87 83 87 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Villella1">[24]</xref>
###xml 174 178 174 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0004574-Finley1">[25]</xref>
Chaining assay involved ten flies introduced in a 95 mmx23 mm tube with fresh food [24]. A chain was defined as a group of at least three males exhibiting courtship behavior [25]. Chaining index (ChI) is the percentage of time that groups of males spent courting during the 10-min observation period. Males were reared in groups of 60approximately70.

Quantitative determination of dopamine [26]
###end title 27
###begin p 28
Adult male flies were collected and quick-frozen in liquid nitrogen. The flies were homogenized in 0.1 M perchloric acid and then the homogenate was centrifuged at 12 000 g at 4degreesC for 30 min. Dopamine in the supernatant fluid was measured with an ELISA dopamine kit (LDN, Germany).
