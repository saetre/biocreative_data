Mutations in genes of Saccharomyces cerevisiae encoding pre-mRNA splicing factors cause cell cycle arrest through activation of the spindle checkpoint
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 95 116 95 116 <email xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">martin@post.tau.ac.il</email>

###begin p 2
###xml 379 383 379 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ISY1</italic>
###xml 385 389 385 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SYF2</italic>
###xml 394 399 394 399 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NTC20</italic>
###xml 470 474 470 474 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ISY1</italic>
###xml 544 548 544 548 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 554 558 550 554 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 687 691 675 679 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 696 700 684 688 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 779 783 763 767 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 789 793 769 773 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
Previous work has identified a group of genes whose products play important roles in two seemingly unrelated processes: cell cycle progression and splicing. The products of these genes show a network of physical and genetic interactions suggestive of the existence of a protein complex, the cell cycle and splicing complex (CSC). Here we analyze the genetic interactions between ISY1, SYF2 and NTC20, three non-essential components of the CSC. We show that mutations in ISY1 cause lethality in the absence of Ntc20p, and that the double mutant isy1Delta syf2Delta shows a temperature-dependent cell cycle arrest. This arrest is due to lower levels of alpha-tubulin, a protein encoded by TUB1 and TUB3, two intron-containing genes. We show that the low levels of alpha-tubulin in isy1Delta syf2Delta trigger activation of the spindle checkpoint, causing cell cycle arrest. Thus, our results have uncovered an unexpected role for pre-mRNA splicing in the maintenance of the fidelity of chromosome transmission during cell division.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 576 577 576 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c1">1</xref>
###xml 578 579 578 579 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c2">2</xref>
The eukaryotic cell cycle is a collection of ordered events in which the initiation of late processes depends upon the completion of early ones. Coordination of the timing and order of these events is critical for the high-fidelity transmission of genetic information. Work in several laboratories has demonstrated that this coordination is genetically controlled by surveillance mechanisms, termed checkpoints. Checkpoints are pathways that affect cell cycle progression in response to genotoxic agents or alteration of particular structures such as the spindle (reviewed in 1,2).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 156 161 156 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trans</italic>
###xml 221 222 221 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c3">3</xref>
###xml 223 224 223 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c4">4</xref>
###xml 625 626 625 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c3">3</xref>
###xml 709 733 709 733 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 735 736 735 736 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c5">5</xref>
###xml 703 708 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 709 733 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
Removal of introns from pre-mRNA is an important component of the regulation of gene expression in eukaryotes. Pre-mRNA splicing requires a large number of trans-acting factors, which together constitute the spliceosome (3,4). Biochemical studies have demonstrated that the spliceosome consists of a large number of specific proteins associated with functional small nuclear ribonucleoprotein particles (snRNPs) composed of small nuclear RNA molecules (U1, U2, U4, U5 and U6 snRNA). In addition, a large number of non-snRNP proteins are either transiently associated with, or constitute an integral part of, the spliceosome (3). Genetic screens have identified many proteins involved in splicing in the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae (5).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 4 15 4 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CDC40/PRP17</italic>
###xml 179 180 179 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c6">6</xref>
###xml 306 317 306 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc40/prp17</italic>
###xml 412 413 412 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c7">7</xref>
###xml 414 415 414 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c8">8</xref>
###xml 428 439 428 439 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CDC40/PRP17</italic>
###xml 542 543 542 543 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 617 622 617 622 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CDC40</italic>
The CDC40/PRP17 gene encodes a protein that plays a role during the second step of the splicing reaction. Mutations in this gene result in accumulation of splicing intermediates (6). This gene was also independently found to function in the mitotic and meiotic cell cycles. At the restrictive temperature, cdc40/prp17 strains arrest as large-budded cells with one undivided nucleus containing 2N content of DNA (7,8). Thus, the CDC40/PRP17 gene is involved in two seemingly unrelated processes: pre-mRNA splicing and progression through the G2/M phases of the cell cycle. For simplicity, we will refer to the gene as CDC40.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 117 118 117 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c9">9</xref>
###xml 119 121 119 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c10">10</xref>
###xml 162 165 162 165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SYF</italic>
###xml 214 217 214 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SYF</italic>
###xml 542 543 542 543 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 571 572 571 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c9">9</xref>
###xml 573 575 573 575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c10">10</xref>
###xml 750 775 750 775 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</italic>
###xml 865 867 865 867 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c11">11</xref>
###xml 868 870 868 870 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c12">12</xref>
###xml 750 775 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
Previously, we have implemented a screen to identify mutants that are synthetically lethal in the absence of Cdc40p (9,10). This screen identified several genes (SYF, synthetic lethal with forty). When some of the SYF genes were used as baits in two-hybrid screens, we uncovered a complex network of interactions between a group of splicing factors (including Prp8, Slu7, Prp22, Slt11, Syf1, Syf2, Syf3/Clf1, Cef1, Isy1 and Ntc20). Most of these splicing factors are involved in cell cycle progression and, when mutated, cells arrest at the G2/M phase of the cell cycle (9,10). Based on these results, we have suggested the existence of a protein complex [cell cycle and splicing complex (CSC)] that plays a role in both splicing andS.cerevisiae and Schizosaccharomyces pombe has confirmed the existence of protein complexes containing several of the CSC proteins (11,12).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 162 166 162 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 172 176 168 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 314 315 306 307 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 420 424 408 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 429 433 417 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
In this paper, we investigate the genetic interactions between three non-essential components of the CSC, and we report the characterization of the double mutant isy1Delta syf2Delta. Our results demonstrate that the double mutant is temperature sensitive and arrests in the cell cycle. Moreover, we show that the G2/M arrest phenotype observed in this mutant is due to the low levels of alpha-tubulin protein encoded by TUB1 and TUB3, two intron-containing genes. The low levels of alpha-tubulin activate the spindle checkpoint, leading to cell cycle arrest. Our results point at an unexpected role played by pre-mRNA splicing in guarding the stability of the genome during cell division.
###end p 8

###begin title 16
Microscopic and flow cytometry analysis
###end title 16
###begin p 17
To visualize nuclei, cells were fixed with 70% methanol overnight at 4degreesC, washed twice with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), and resuspended in 1 microg/ml 4',6'-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) in PBS. To determine nuclear position, the distance from the edge of the nucleus to the bud neck was measured for each budded cell, as well as the diameter of the mother cell. The ratio between these two distances was calculated. A ratio of 0.4 or larger was considered to be indicative of nuclear localization failure. Most wild-type large-budded cells exhibited a ratio of 0.25 or lower. At least 200 cells were analyzed for each strain.
###end p 17

###begin title 19

###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 13 17 13 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>

###xml 145 147 137 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c17">17</xref>
YOD13 cells (isy1Delta syf2Delta) were transformed with a cDNA expression library, regulated by the GAL1 promoter on the URA3-CEN vector pRS316 (17). The transformation mixture was plated on SGal-Ura plates at 25degreesC. After 12 h, cells were shifted to 35degreesC for 3-4 days until the appearance of colonies. Genetic interactions between ISY1, SYF2, NTC20 and CDC40
###end title 28

###xml 626 627 622 623 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 662 666 658 662 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SYF2</italic>
###xml 668 672 664 668 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ISY1</italic>
###xml 677 682 673 678 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NTC20</italic>
###xml 809 814 805 810 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CDC40</italic>
The SYF2 gene was previously identified in our screen for mutants unable to grow in the absence of Cdc40p. It encodes a small protein with no obvious structural motifs (9,10). ISY1 and NTC20 also encode two small proteins that lack any clear structural features. Neither gene was identified in our screen for mutations that were synthetically lethal with cdc40Delta, but both were found as two-hybrid interactors with Syf1p and Syf3/Clf1p (9,10,13). Syf2p, Isy1p and Ntc20p form part of a large protein complex, the CSC (9,10). Although mutations in most of the genes encoding the CSC proteins cause cell cycle arrest at the G2/M transition, single mutations in SYF2, ISY1 and NTC20 have no apparent viability problems or cell cycle phenotype. In order to study the relationship between these three genes and CDC40, we sought to create double mutant strains.

Null alleles of ISY1, SYF2 and NTC20, in which the ORFs were replaced with DNA fragments containing HIS3, TRP1 or KanMX, respectively, were created. These strains were individually crossed to cdc40Delta::LEU2 or cdc40Delta::URA3 strains. The double heterozygous strains were subjected to meiosis and tetrad analysis. As expected from our previous results (9,10), we were unable to obtain any syf2Delta cdc40Delta double mutant spores among 40 tetrads dissected, indicating a synthetic lethality between mutations in these genes. Similarly, no isy1Delta cdc40Delta spores were obtained in 38 tetrads analyzed. Thus, mutations in SYF2 and ISY1, but not NTC20, are lethal in the absence of Cdc40p (Fig. 1).

To investigate the genetic interactions between the three non-essential genes of the CSC, we carried out crosses in all possible combinations between the genes ISY1, SYF2 and NTC20. Tetrad analysis showed that isy1Delta is synthetically lethal in combination with ntc20Delta: no viable double mutant spores were observed among 25 tetrads dissected. In contrast, isy1Delta syf2Delta and syf2Delta ntc20Delta double mutants were obtained at the expected numbers. Whereas the latter strain is viable at all temperatures and shows no obvious synthetic phenotypes, isy1Delta syf2Delta is temperature sensitive, being unable to grow at 37degreesC (Figs 1 and 2A). Below we describe a detailed analysis of the phenotypes of the isy1Delta syf2Delta strain.

To study if the temperature sensitivity of the isy1Delta syf2Delta strain is related to defects in the cell cycle, we examined whether the cells exhibit the typical phenotype of cdc mutants at restrictive temperatures. To this end, isy1Delta syf2Delta cells were synchronized at the G1 phase of the cell cycle with alpha-factor and then released at either permissive (25degreesC) or restrictive (37degreesC) temperatures. At different time points, samples were analyzed by flow cytometry. Figure 2B shows that isy1Delta syf2Delta cells released from G1 at the restrictive temperature replicated their DNA and subsequently accumulated with a 2N DNA content. Light microscopy of samples taken 3 h after release to 37degreesC showed that almost all the population was composed of large-budded cells (Fig. 2C and D). Staining the cells with DAPI and alpha-tubulin antibodies demonstrated that the arrested cells had undivided nuclei and short spindles (see below). We thus conclude that isy1Delta syf2Delta cells are unable to proceed through the G2/M stage at the restrictive temperature.

###xml 1627 1631 1569 1573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MOT2</italic>

###xml 1186 1198 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
Since splicing constitutes a key cellular mechanism, genes encoding splicing factors are often essential. Many temperature-sensitive splicing mutants have been isolated; most of them arrest at the restrictive temperature without any defined cell cycle morphology (10). We reasoned that the cell cycle-specific arrest of the isy1Delta syf2Delta double mutant strain might be caused by inefficient processing of a small number of intron-containing genes particularly dependent on Isy1p and Syf2p for their pre-mRNA splicing. If this were the case, we expected that introduction of the relevant target gene(s) in the form of cDNA should be sufficient to bypass the need for Isy1p and Syf2p and therefore should allow growth at the restrictive temperature. To test this hypothesis, we conducted a screen to isolate cDNAs able to suppress the temperature sensitivity of isy1Delta syf2Delta cells. Strain YOD13 (isy1Delta syf2Delta) was transformed with a cDNA library and screened for cell growth at 35degreesC. Table 1 shows that in addition to the ISY1 and SYF2 genes, two intron-containing genes, TUB1 and TUB3, were isolated. TUB1 is the major gene encoding the alpha-tubulin subunit in S.cerevisiae. TUB3 is a second gene encoding the alpha-tubulin subunit. It is expressed at lower levels than TUB1. The protein products of TUB1 and TUB3 are very similar, showing 90% sequence identity (18,19). TUB1 and TUB3 cDNA showed better suppression than three other cDNAs isolated, supporting growth of isy1Delta syf2Delta cells at temperatures up to 35degreesC (although not at 37degreesC). The three additional cDNA clones (encoding MOT2, RPN5 and YOL162w) were isolated only once each and allowed only weak growth at 35degreesC. They encode, respectively, a general transcription factor, a proteasome subunit and an unknown ORF. Since their effect seemed relatively weak, these cDNAs were not further analyzed.

Increased sensitivity of cells to anti-tubulin drugs is a common phenotype of tubulin mutants (18,19). To establish whether the phenotypes of the isy1Delta syf2Delta mutant were related to tubulin, we checked the ability of the double mutant cells to grow in the presence of increasing nocodazole concentrations. As shown in Figure 3, at a semi-restrictive temperature (30degreesC), the isy1Delta syf2Delta double mutant displays increased sensitivity to nocodazole relative to single mutants or to the wild-type control. ###xml 1026 1030 986 990 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 1443 1444 1389 1390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f4">4</xref>
###xml 3 15 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
In S.cerevisiae, spindle pole body duplication and spindle assembly take place concomitantly with initiation of DNA replication (20). Thus, during the S, G2 and early M phases, a short spindle is observed, whereas spindle elongation occurs during anaphase. Since TUB1 and TUB3 encode the alpha-tubulin subunit, which is a major component of the mitotic spindle, we considered the possibility that lower expression of TUB1 and TUB3 in the isy1Delta syf2Delta double mutant strain incubated at the restrictive temperature may disrupt the mitotic spindle and therefore cause cell cycle arrest. To test this hypothesis, we carried out a careful analysis of the spindle length of isy1Delta syf2Delta cells at the permissive and non-permissive temperatures. The cells were grown at 25degreesC and then shifted to the restrictive temperature (37degreesC). At different time points samples were taken for tubulin staining by indirect immunofluorescence with anti-alpha-tubulin antibodies. At the permissive temperature, the isy1Delta syf2Delta cells contained spindles of all sizes, as did the wild-type control. However, after 2.5 h of incubation at the non-permissive temperature, mutant cells exhibited either a short spindle or a dot of alpha-tubulin material. Following a longer incubation at 37degreesC, the mitotic spindle eventually disappeared, and after 5 h incubation at the non-permissive temperature almost no staining was detected (Fig. 4). These results indicate that Isy1p and Syf2p are required for maintenance of the mitotic spindle.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
###xml 32 36 32 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 42 46 38 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
Nuclear migration phenotypes of isy1Delta syf2Delta mutant cells
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 269 271 269 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c21">21</xref>
###xml 397 399 397 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c22">22</xref>
###xml 527 531 527 531 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 537 541 533 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 601 602 593 594 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f2">2</xref>
###xml 616 620 608 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 626 630 614 618 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 969 970 953 954 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f5">5</xref>
###xml 1306 1310 1290 1294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 1316 1320 1296 1300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 1528 1529 1504 1505 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f5">5</xref>
###xml 1560 1564 1536 1540 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 1570 1574 1542 1546 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
In wild-type cells, nuclear migration takes place early in the cell cycle. Therefore, in large-budded cells the nucleus is almost always seen near, or extended through, the bud neck. This process of nuclear migration to the bud neck requires extranuclear microtubules (21). In tubulin mutants, the nucleus does not divide; moreover, some tubulin mutants also exhibit defects in nuclear migration (22). To test the effect of Isy1 and Syf2 on these tubulin-dependent events, we followed nuclear migration and nuclear division in isy1Delta syf2Delta mutant and in wild-type cells. As we have shown (Fig. 2C and D), the isy1Delta syf2Delta strain accumulated large-budded cells when incubated at the non-permissive temperature. DAPI staining showed that more than 95% of these cells contained one undivided nucleus. Under these conditions, a significant nuclear migration defect is observed: in 50% of the arrested cells the nucleus is located away from the bud neck (Fig. 5). This number may be an underestimate, as a very conservative classification limit was used: only cells in which the distance between the edge of the nucleus to the neck was at least 40% of the diameter of the mother cells were classified as having a nucleus away from the neck. Similar defects, although less profound, can be seen in isy1Delta syf2Delta cells incubated at the permissive temperature. This is in stark contrast to the wild-type control cells, in which more than 95% of the large-budded cells had the nucleus localized to the bud neck (Fig. 5B). We therefore conclude that isy1Delta syf2Delta cells are defective in nuclear migration.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
###xml 22 26 22 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 31 35 31 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 45 49 45 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ISY1</italic>
###xml 54 58 54 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SYF2</italic>
Efficient splicing of TUB1 and TUB3 requires ISY1 and SYF2
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 55 59 55 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 64 68 64 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 154 158 154 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 163 167 163 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 212 216 212 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 222 226 218 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 412 416 404 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 418 422 410 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>

###xml 508 509 500 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f6">6</xref>
###xml 534 538 526 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ISY1</italic>
###xml 543 547 535 539 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SYF2</italic>
###xml 627 631 619 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 636 640 628 632 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 863 868 855 860 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RPL25</italic>
###xml 931 935 923 927 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 940 944 932 936 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
To test the possibility that the efficient splicing of TUB1 and TUB3 pre-mRNA is dependent on Isy1p and Syf2p, we examined the splicing efficiency of the TUB1 and TUB3 pre-mRNA by RT-PCR analysis. Total RNA from isy1Delta syf2Delta and wild-type strains was isolated before and after the cells were shifted to the non-permissive temperature. RNA was subjected to RT-PCR analysis using primers specific to either TUB1, TUB3 or, as a control, to the intron-containing ribosomal gene, RPL25. As shown in Figure 6, when cells deleted for ISY1 and SYF2 were incubated at the non-permissive temperature, an accumulation of unspliced TUB1 and TUB3 was observed. Such an accumulation was not seen in cells incubated at the permissive temperature, or in wild-type cells at either temperature. No pre-mRNA accumulation was seen using primers specific to the ribosomal gene RPL25 (data not shown). These results indicate that the splicing of TUB1 and TUB3 pre-mRNA is highly dependent on Isy1 and Syf2 proteins.
###end p 43
###begin title 44
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 10 14 6 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
isy1Delta syf2Delta cells have less alpha-tubulin protein at the restrictive temperature
###end title 44
###begin p 45
###xml 67 71 67 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 77 81 73 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 199 203 187 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 208 212 196 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 471 472 455 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f7">7</xref>
###xml 526 530 506 510 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 536 540 512 516 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 666 670 634 638 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 676 680 640 644 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 850 854 806 810 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 860 864 812 816 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 1005 1006 953 954 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f7">7</xref>
The disappearance of the mitotic spindle observed in double mutant isy1Delta syf2Delta cells at elevated temperatures might be due to a reduction in alpha-tubulin protein levels caused by defects in TUB1 and TUB3 pre-mRNA splicing. We therefore measured directly the levels of alpha-tubulin by immunoblotting. As a control we measured the amount of Vma5p, the c subunit of the vacuolar H (+) ATPase, which is also encoded by an intron-containing gene. As shown in Figure 7, alpha-tubulin protein levels decreased rapidly when isy1Delta syf2Delta cells were incubated at the non-permissive temperature. In contrast, constant amounts of alpha-tubulin were detected in isy1Delta syf2Delta cells incubated at the permissive temperature or in wild-type cells. The reduction in alpha-tubulin levels is not due to a general decrease in protein stability in isy1Delta syf2Delta cells at the non-permissive temperature, since the level of other proteins (such as Vma5) remained constant under all conditions (Fig. 7).
###end p 45
###begin title 46
###xml 25 29 25 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 35 39 31 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
The cell cycle arrest of isy1Delta syf2Delta strains is mediated by the spindle checkpoint
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 200 201 200 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c2">2</xref>
###xml 276 279 276 279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mad</italic>
###xml 287 289 287 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c23">23</xref>
###xml 299 302 299 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bub</italic>
###xml 310 312 310 312 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c24">24</xref>
###xml 493 495 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c25">25</xref>
###xml 663 665 663 665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c26">26</xref>
###xml 852 854 852 854 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c27">27</xref>
###xml 932 934 932 934 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c28">28</xref>
###xml 983 987 983 987 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 992 996 992 996 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 1279 1283 1279 1283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 1289 1293 1285 1289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 1320 1324 1312 1316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MAD1</italic>
###xml 1329 1333 1321 1325 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">BUB2</italic>
When the mitotic spindles are defective (due to the action of drugs or to mutated spindle components) cells delay the onset of chromosome segregation by activating the spindle checkpoint (reviewed in 2). This mechanism is dependent on the activity of two groups of genes, the mad genes (23) and the bub genes (24). Cell cycle arrest is achieved by inhibiting the anaphase-promoting complex-dependent degradation of specific proteins. Sister chromatid separation requires proteolysis of Pds1p (25), whereas assembly of replication-competent complexes on origins (to ensure DNA replication in the next S-phase) requires degradation of B-type cyclins, such as Clb2 (26). Recent work has shown that prevention of Pds1 degradation depends on the Mad1, Mad2, Mad3 and Bub1 checkpoint proteins, whereas the prevention of Clb2 proteolysis is mediated by Bub2 (27). Since mutations in genes encoding tubulin activate the spindle checkpoint (28), we tested whether the inefficient splicing of TUB1 and TUB3 transcripts causes cell cycle arrest by activating this control mechanism. We reasoned that if that were the case, mutations that inactivate the spindle checkpoint would prevent cell cycle arrest and would lead to catastrophic cell divisions and to cell death. We therefore created isy1Delta syf2Delta strains in which the MAD1 and BUB2 genes were deleted.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 4 8 4 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 14 18 10 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 63 67 55 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 73 77 61 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 83 87 67 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mad1</italic>
###xml 93 97 73 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bub2</italic>
###xml 325 326 301 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f8">8</xref>
###xml 545 546 521 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f8">8</xref>
###xml 572 576 548 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 582 586 554 558 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 592 596 560 564 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mad1</italic>
###xml 602 606 566 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bub2</italic>
###xml 824 825 784 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f8">8</xref>
###xml 986 990 946 950 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mad1</italic>
###xml 996 1000 952 956 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bub2</italic>
###xml 1079 1080 1031 1032 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f8">8</xref>
The isy1Delta syf2Delta double mutant strain and the quadruple isy1Delta syf2Delta mad1Delta bub2Delta mutant strain were grown to mid-logarithmic phase, and were then transferred to the non-permissive temperature. Individual cells were monitored (under the microscope) for their ability to undergo cell cycle arrest. Figure 8 shows that whereas the double mutant arrested as large-budded cells in response to the restrictive temperature, the quadruple mutant continued to divide and created microcolonies that did not continue to develop (Fig. 8). Although the quadruple isy1Delta syf2Delta mad1Delta bub2Delta mutant strain was able to undergo several divisions in non-permissive conditions, the cells rapidly lost viability, as can be seen by their reduced ability to recover when returned to permissive conditions (Fig. 8C). Similar results were observed when tubulin was depolarized by nocodazole: whereas the wild-type strain arrested for several hours as large-budded cells, the mad1Delta bub2Delta mutant strain failed to arrest and produced inviable microcolonies (Fig. 8B).
###end p 48
###begin p 49
These results indicate that the absence of the Isy1 and Syf2 proteins leads to reduced tubulin levels, which trigger cell cycle arrest by activating the spindle checkpoint.

###begin title 50
DISCUSSION
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 222 233 222 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CDC40/PRP17</italic>
###xml 286 287 286 287 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 302 303 302 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c8">8</xref>
###xml 343 345 343 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c29">29</xref>
###xml 346 348 346 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c31">31</xref>
###xml 588 589 588 589 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c9">9</xref>
###xml 590 592 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c10">10</xref>
###xml 593 595 593 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c13">13</xref>
###xml 754 758 754 758 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ISY1</italic>
###xml 760 764 760 764 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SYF2</italic>
###xml 769 774 769 774 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NTC20</italic>
###xml 918 922 918 922 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ISY1</italic>
###xml 951 956 951 956 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NTC20</italic>
###xml 1020 1024 1020 1024 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SYF2</italic>
###xml 1030 1034 1030 1034 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 1040 1045 1036 1041 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ntc20</italic>
###xml 1107 1108 1099 1100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f1">1</xref>
Cell cycle progression and pre-mRNA splicing are two apparently independent processes in eukaryotic cells. However, a growing body of evidence has recently accumulated indicating that these processes may be connected. The CDC40/PRP17 gene, for example, encodes a protein essential for G2/M transition (8), which also plays a role in splicing (29-31). Using synthetic lethal and two-hybrid screens we have previously identified a putative protein complex that genetically interacts with Cdc40p (i.e. CSC). The CSC contains proteins involved in both cell cycle progression and in splicing (9,10,13). Most of the genes encoding proteins of this complex are essential; however, the CSC also contains (at least) three proteins encoded by non-essential genes: ISY1, SYF2 and NTC20. In this paper we have dissected the genetic relationships between these genes, which exhibit a complex set of interactions. While deletion of ISY1 is lethal in the absence of NTC20 and is temperature sensitive in combination with a deletion of SYF2, the syf2Delta ntc20Delta double mutant shows no viability or growth defect (Fig. 1).
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 652 656 652 656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 662 666 658 662 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 809 813 801 805 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 819 824 807 812 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ntc20</italic>
The nature of the interactions observed between the CSC genes is still under investigation; currently the results could be interpreted in two alternative ways. (i) The three proteins could be part of one large complex with a role in both cell cycle progression and pre-mRNA splicing. The different gene interactions observed among the non-essential members of this complex could be due to different effects on the stability or function of the complex. For example, it is possible that for the protein complex to be fully stable/active, only the Isy1 protein is needed. In its absence, however, Ntc20p can provide some degree of stability, such that an isy1Delta syf2Delta strain is only temperature sensitive, and not completely lethal. In contrast, Syf2p alone is insufficient for CSC stability and thus the isy1Delta ntc20Delta double mutant is inviable. (ii) An alternative view posits that the elaborate interactions observed are due to the existence of several alternative protein complexes with a similar core, each containing different non-essential proteins. These complexes may act redundantly or together as mediators that link splicing to cell cycle progression. For example, functional complexes may exist in the cell, some carrying Isy1p, some carrying Ntc20p. In the absence of both proteins, no active complex can be formed, and the cells die.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 37 42 37 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CDC40</italic>
###xml 301 313 301 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 315 317 315 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c11">11</xref>
###xml 318 320 318 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c32">32</xref>
###xml 326 333 326 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 335 337 335 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c12">12</xref>
###xml 640 642 640 642 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c33">33</xref>
###xml 643 645 643 645 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c34">34</xref>
###xml 301 313 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 326 333 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 489 494 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The nature of the connection between CDC40 and the group of interacting proteins is still unclear. We were unable to detect physical or two-hybrid interactions between Cdc40p and any of the CSC proteins. Furthermore, protein complexes similar to the CSC have been recently purified biochemically from S.cerevisiae (11,32) and S.pombe (12). In neither case was Cdc40p found among the proteins isolated. On the other hand, in recent studies of a large-scale analysis of protein complexes of yeast, Cdc40p was identified in association with some (but not all) of the complexes precipitated with antibodies directed against members of the CSC (33,34). This may indicate that Cdc40p is loosely associated with some of the CSC components. Thus, Cdc40p may act alone, or in conjunction with other proteins, to affect the same cellular activity as the CSC. Alternatively, Cdc40 may associate transiently with the CSC, to facilitate the access or activity of other proteins. This process could also take place in the absence of Cdc40, provided Syf2 or Isy1 are functional.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 49 53 49 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 59 63 55 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 152 156 144 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 161 165 153 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 215 219 207 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 225 229 213 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 264 268 248 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 274 278 254 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 342 346 318 322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 351 355 327 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 478 482 454 458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 487 491 463 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 517 521 493 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 526 530 502 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 634 639 610 615 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 642 644 618 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c13">13</xref>
###xml 691 698 667 674 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1071 1073 1047 1049 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c35">35</xref>
###xml 1090 1094 1066 1070 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 1099 1103 1075 1079 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 1127 1131 1103 1107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 1137 1141 1109 1113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 1368 1372 1324 1328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 1377 1381 1333 1337 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
When shifted to the restrictive temperature, the isy1Delta syf2Delta double mutant cells arrest as large-budded cells with a short spindle. We isolated TUB1 and TUB3 cDNAs as suppressors of the cell cycle arrest of isy1Delta syf2Delta. RT-PCR analysis showed that isy1Delta syf2Delta cells incubated at the restrictive temperature accumulate TUB1 and TUB3 pre-mRNA at a higher level than other pre-mRNAs, indicating that the dependence on Isy1p and Syf2p for the removal of the TUB1 and TUB3 introns is specific. The TUB1 and TUB3 introns may contain unique features that make their pre-mRNA splicing highly dependent on the CSC. Dix et al. (13) have previously shown that Isy1p is required in vivo for the splicing of introns carrying suboptimal consensus splicing sequences. The need for specific splicing factors for the efficient splicing of a subset of genes is a well known phenomenon. For example, the splicing factor Slu7 is required for the efficient splicing of introns in which the distance between the 3'-splice site and the branch point is larger than 7 bp (35). The fact that TUB1 and TUB3 cDNA support growth of isy1Delta syf2Delta cells up to 35degreesC, but not at 37degreesC, indicates that under these conditions another transcript(s) becomes rate limiting. This putative transcript may contain introns that share some characteristics with those of TUB1 and TUB3.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 104 106 97 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c36">36</xref>
###xml 112 124 105 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 408 410 401 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c21">21</xref>
###xml 411 413 404 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c37">37</xref>
###xml 421 425 414 418 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tub1</italic>
###xml 430 434 423 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tub3</italic>
###xml 625 627 618 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c18">18</xref>
###xml 628 630 621 623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c19">19</xref>
###xml 631 633 624 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c22">22</xref>
###xml 634 636 627 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c38">38</xref>
###xml 676 680 669 673 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 686 690 675 679 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 1039 1043 1016 1020 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 1049 1054 1022 1027 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ntc20</italic>
###xml 1230 1234 1199 1203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 1240 1244 1205 1209 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 112 124 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 248 253 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Microtubules are ubiquitous cytoskeletal structures, consisting of alpha- and beta-tubulin hetrodimers (36). In S.cerevisiae, as in all eukaryotic cells, microtubules are necessary for chromosome movement. Unlike most other eukaryotes, however, in yeast the only other functions known to depend on microtubules are the movement of nuclei to the bud neck before mitosis and the fusion of nuclei after mating (21,37). Most tub1 and tub3 mutants show several common phenotypes such as supersensitivity to spindle disrupting agents, accumulation of large-budded cells, and defects in nuclear division, nuclear migration or both (18,19,22,38). We have shown that the double mutant isy1Delta syf2Delta cells exhibit similar phenotypes when incubated at non-permissive or semi-permissive conditions. The sensitivity to nocodazole, as well as the cell cycle arrest, could be fully suppressed by the presence of intronless alpha-tubulin cDNA, indicating that the phenotypes observed are truly due to lack of alpha-tubulin protein. Furthermore, the syf2Delta ntc20Delta strain, which grows well at all temperatures, was not sensitive to nocodazole (data not shown). These results strongly imply that the nocodazole sensitivity phenotype of isy1Delta syf2Delta cells is connected to the cell cycle arrest phenotype.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 204 208 192 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 213 217 201 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 231 235 219 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 241 245 225 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 294 295 274 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f6">6</xref>
###xml 323 327 303 307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 332 336 312 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 1268 1270 1223 1225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c39">39</xref>
###xml 1271 1273 1226 1228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c40">40</xref>
###xml 947 952 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The mechanism that links cell cycle progression and splicing exhibits an exquisite sensitivity to the level of pre-mRNA splicing. Our RT-PCR results show a approximately3-fold increase in accumulation of TUB1 and TUB3 pre-mRNAs in isy1Delta syf2Delta cells at the restrictive temperature (Fig. 6). Although splicing of the TUB1 and TUB3 transcripts is only reduced and not eliminated, a strong decrease in the levels of alpha-tubulin is seen. It is not yet clear why accumulation of unspliced transcripts has such a strong effect on the level of expression. Unspliced intermediates may exert their negative effect by affecting RNA stability or the efficiency of translation. Likewise, alpha-tubulin levels, as detected by immunoblot experiments, are strongly reduced; the protein, however, is not totally eliminated. It has been well established that the ratio between alpha- and beta-tubulin is of crucial importance for microtubule stability in yeast. Microtubule function is sensitive to very small changes in tubulin stoichiometry. Several reports have demonstrated that gene dosages of beta-tubulin in excess of alpha-tubulin, or overexpression of beta-tubulin, are not tolerated and result in rapid depolymerization of normal microtubules and loss of viability (39,40). Thus, the small effect in splicing of the alpha-tubulin-encoding genes is amplified, resulting in a strong destabilization of the microtubules.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 26 38 26 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 370 371 370 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f8">8</xref>
###xml 375 377 375 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c23">23</xref>
###xml 378 380 378 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c24">24</xref>
###xml 381 383 381 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c41">41</xref>
###xml 430 434 430 434 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 440 444 436 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 488 492 480 484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 497 501 489 493 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 831 835 823 827 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 841 845 829 833 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 873 877 857 861 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">isy1</italic>
###xml 883 887 863 867 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syf2</italic>
###xml 893 897 869 873 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mad1</italic>
###xml 903 907 875 879 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bub2</italic>
###xml 1104 1105 1072 1073 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf563f8">8</xref>
###xml 1296 1301 1264 1269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 1304 1306 1272 1274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c42">42</xref>
###xml 1322 1326 1290 1294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 1388 1392 1356 1360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cef1</italic>
###xml 1437 1441 1405 1409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 1633 1637 1601 1605 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEF1</italic>
###xml 26 38 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
The spindle checkpoint of S.cerevisiae is able to sense a multitude of spindle defects and halt cell cycle progression before the exit from mitosis. The genes in this pathway are necessary for the cell cycle arrest phenotype observed in the presence of spindle-depolarizing drugs: spindle checkpoint mutants fail to arrest in the presence of benomyl or nocodazole (Fig. 8B) (23,24,41). We have shown that the cell cycle arrest of isy1Delta syf2Delta cells as a consequence of inefficient TUB1 and TUB3 splicing is not direct (i.e. the cells do not arrest with a short spindle simply because they are unable to elongate it in order to proceed in the cell cycle). Instead, the defect in microtubule structure is detected by the spindle checkpoint, which initiates a cascade of events leading to cell cycle arrest. In contrast to the isy1Delta syf2Delta strain, the quadruple isy1Delta syf2Delta mad1Delta bub2Delta mutant (defective in the two branches of the spindle checkpoint) continues to divide at the non-permissive conditions in the presence of damaged spindles resulting in loss of viability (Fig. 8). Our results thus show that pre-mRNA splicing plays an important role in promoting the fidelity of chromosome segregation and cell cycle progression. While this work was in progress, Burns et al. (42) reported that TUB1 pre-mRNA splicing is also defective in temperature-sensitive cef1 mutants. Similar to results presented here, TUB1 cDNA could also suppress the cell cycle arrest of these mutants. These results further support our findings, since Cef1 is one of the components of the CSC. It is very likely that defects in CEF1 lead to cell cycle arrest by activating the spindle checkpoint, as we have shown here for Isy1 and Syf2.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 335 337 335 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c41">41</xref>
###xml 338 340 338 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c43">43</xref>
Maintaining genome stability is essential for all eukaryotes. The survival of cells and organisms requires accurate chromosomal segregation. The cell has developed several regulatory networks to maintain genome stability. Recent studies have identified some CSC components as important for sister chromatid separation and segregation (41,43). Thus, one of the possible roles of the CSC could be to ensure proper chromosome segregation by linking pre-mRNA splicing to spindle integrity.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 174 181 174 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c44">44</xref>
###xml 210 212 210 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c45">45</xref>
###xml 247 249 247 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c45">45</xref>
###xml 319 326 319 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 589 601 585 597 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 606 613 602 609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 174 181 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 319 326 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 589 601 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 606 613 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
The mechanism unveiled here is probably evolutionarily conserved. Orthologs for most of the CSC proteins exist in other eukaryotes, and mutations in many splicing mutants in S.pombe cause cell cycle arrest (44,45). Interestingly, a recent report (45) has shown that mutations in some genes encoding splicing factors in S.pombe can lead to a reduction in alpha-tubulin levels and to defects in microtubule integrity. The splicing defects result in unequal chromosome separation and in polyploidy. These findings suggest that the link between pre-mRNA splicing and spindle integrity in both S.cerevisiae and S.pombe may represent a common mechanism in eukaryotes.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 250 251 250 251 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>

###xml 436 437 428 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c8">8</xref>

###xml 512 516 496 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 579 584 563 568 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc40</italic>
###xml 627 631 607 611 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB1</italic>
###xml 636 640 616 620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TUB3</italic>
###xml 675 680 655 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc40</italic>
###xml 711 713 687 689 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf563c46">46</xref>
###xml 755 760 731 736 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CDC40</italic>
Are all splicing factors required for maintaining the stability of the genome? As mentioned above, mutants defective in several components of the CSC cause cell cycle arrest by a similar mechanism. Cells lacking the Cdc40 protein also arrest at the G2/M transition upon the shift to the restrictive temperature. As in the case of isy1Delta syf2Delta, prolonged incubation at the restrictive conditions induces spindle depolymerization (8). However, unlike the isy1Delta syf2Delta strain, introduction of TUB1 or TUB3 cDNA does not suppress the temperature-sensitive phenotype of cdc40Delta cells (data not shown). Furthermore, TUB1 and TUB3 pre-mRNA do not accumulate in the cdc40Delta strain (data not shown) (46). These results suggest that the CSC and CDC40 may be differentially required for the efficient splicing of two subsets of transcripts involved in cell cycle progression. Control of cell cycle progression via pre-mRNA splicing appears to be a complex process involving several regulatory pathways.
