###begin article-title 0
Differential expression of MUC genes in endometrial and cervical tissues and tumors
###end article-title 0

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 190 194 190 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 201 205 201 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1</italic>
###xml 207 211 207 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2</italic>
###xml 213 218 213 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B</italic>
###xml 220 235 220 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC and MUC8</italic>
###xml 318 322 318 322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 184 189 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 240 245 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Mucin glycoprotein's are major components of mucus and are considered an important class of tumor associated antigens. The objective of this study was to investigate the expression of human MUC genes (MUC1, MUC2, MUC5B, MUC5AC and MUC8) in human endometrium and cervix, and to compare and quantitate the expression of MUC genes in normal and cancerous tissues.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 44 48 44 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
Slot blot techniques were used to study the MUC gene expression and quantitation.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 33 37 33 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1</italic>
###xml 39 45 39 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 49 54 49 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 146 151 146 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2 </italic>
###xml 155 162 155 162 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC </italic>
###xml 232 236 232 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1</italic>
###xml 238 244 238 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 248 253 248 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 344 349 344 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 411 417 411 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 421 426 421 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
Of the five-mucin genes studied, MUC1, MUC5B and MUC8 showed high expression levels in the normal and cancerous endometrial and cervical tissues, MUC2 and MUC5AC showed considerably lower expression. Statistically, higher levels of MUC1, MUC5B and MUC8 were observed in endometrial adenocarcinomas compared to normal tissues. In contrast, only MUC1 levels increased with no significant changes in expression of MUC5B and MUC8 in cervical tumors over normal cervical tissues.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusion
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 50 54 50 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1</italic>
###xml 56 62 56 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 66 71 66 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 97 102 97 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 211 216 211 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 248 253 248 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2 </italic>
###xml 257 264 257 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC </italic>
Endometrial tumors showed increased expression of MUC1, MUC5B and MUC8 over normal tissues. Only MUC1 appears to be increase, in cervical tumors. All the studied tissues showed high and consistent expression of MUC8 mRNA. Low to neglible levels of MUC2 and MUC5AC were observed in all studied endometrial and cervical tissues.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Background
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 205 206 205 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 389 390 389 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 391 392 391 392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 547 552 547 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 553 554 553 554 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 604 609 604 609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 610 611 610 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
Mucins are high molecular weight glycoprotein components of mucus (> 250 kDa), which protect and lubricate the epithelial surfaces of the respiratory, gastrointestinal and reproductive tracts in the body [1]. Mucins are heavily glycosylated (40-80%) and the oligosaccharides are attached through O-glycosidic linkages to the hydroxyl group of serine and threonine in the protein backbone [2,3]. The striking feature of nearly all mucin genes isolated thus far is the presence of repeat sequences that are either tandem in nature as in the case of MUC1 [4] or slightly imperfect repeats as in the case of MUC8 [5]. In general, the repeats are found in the central portion of the protein backbone, which are flanked by unique regions. Mucins have been classified as either membrane-bound or secretory depending on the presence of a putative trans-membrane region.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 32 36 32 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 143 144 143 144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 145 147 145 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 173 175 173 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 176 178 176 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 197 199 197 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 200 202 200 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 227 229 227 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 251 253 251 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 254 256 254 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 284 286 284 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 287 289 287 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 369 373 369 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 622 627 622 627 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 668 670 668 670 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 722 727 722 727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 780 782 780 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 837 841 837 841 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 847 848 847 848 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</italic>
###xml 850 851 850 851 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</italic>
###xml 853 856 853 856 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5AC</italic>
###xml 858 860 858 860 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5B</italic>
###xml 866 867 866 867 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</italic>
###xml 904 908 904 908 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2</italic>
###xml 963 967 963 967 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 973 975 973 975 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </italic>
###xml 979 981 979 981 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4 </italic>
###xml 1018 1023 1018 1023 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 1027 1032 1027 1032 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC6 </italic>
###xml 1033 1035 1033 1035 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 1036 1038 1036 1038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 1193 1195 1193 1195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 1336 1341 1336 1341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 1346 1348 1346 1348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 1427 1431 1427 1431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 382 387 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1262 1267 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Altered mucin secretions and/or MUC gene expression patterns have been implicated in several cancerous conditions, such as gastric carcinomas [6-10], colorectal carcinomas [11-13], breast cancers [14-16] esophageal carcinomas [17], pancreatic tumors [18-20] and lung adenocarcinomas [21-23]. Accordingly, studies have also been conducted to determine the expression of MUC genes in human reproductive tissues and to investigate their possible altered quantitative and/or qualitative expression in cancerous conditions. Serial analysis of gene expression of ovarian cell lines and tissues indicated that among other genes, MUC1 is up-regulated in cancerous conditions [24]. Another study also showed that the expression of MUC1 correlated with poor prognosis in ovarian carcinoma [25]. Normal endocervical epithelium was found to express MUC genes 1, 4, 5AC, 5B, and 6, with relatively weak expression of MUC2. Normal endocervical and vaginal epithelium expresses MUC genes 1 and 4 and endometrial epithelium expresses MUC1 and MUC6 [26,27]. In an earlier report, we demonstrated the antigenic similarities between respiratory and reproductive tract mucins using a variety of mucin antibodies [28]. In another study, we reported the antigenic cross reactivity of human tracheal mucin with male and female reproductive tissues expressing MUC8 mRNA[29]. While these reports, in general, indicate the manifestation of a variety of MUC genes in reproductive tissues, information regarding mucin gene expression in corresponding tumor tissues is still lacking.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 293 297 293 297 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 488 492 488 492 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1</italic>
###xml 494 498 494 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2</italic>
###xml 500 506 500 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC</italic>
###xml 508 514 508 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 518 522 518 522 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8</italic>
According to The American Cancer Society's Cancer Facts and Figures, it is estimated that there will be 12,200 new cases of invasive cervical cancer (uterine cervix) and 40,100 new cases of endometrial cancer (uterine corpus) that will be diagnosed this year. Hence, a better understanding of MUC gene expression patterns in female reproductive malignancies would help enhance prognosis and therapy. To contribute towards this purpose, we investigated the expression of five mucin genes (MUC1, MUC2, MUC5AC, MUC5B and MUC8) in normal reproductive and cancerous tissues.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Methods
###end title 14
###begin title 15
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Human tissues
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 666 667 660 661 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 86 91 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Normal and malignant endometrium and cervical tissues were obtained from Co-operative Human Tissue Network (CHTN, Birmingham, AL) and National Disease Research Interchange (NDRI, Philadelphia, PA). Malignant tissues with over 95 percent tumor content were selected for the study. The nomenclature adopted for the tissues was as follows: endometrial adenocarcinomas (EA), normal endometrium (EN), cervical carcinomas (CA), normal cervix (CX). These tissues were acquired on dry ice and kept frozen at -80degreesC until use. The classification of tumors was performed according to the International Federation of Gynecology and Obstetrics (FIGO) as indicated in Table 2.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 206 214 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The normal tissues EN (mean age: 41, median age: 43) and CX (mean age: 43, median age: 44) used in the study were obtained by hysterectomy for benign gynecological disorders. The menstrual cycle of all the patients with normal tissues were in early to late proliferative phases, except for EN3, EN4, EN10, CX3, CX4 and CX13 were in late secretory phase. The provided pathology reports of both normal and tumor tissues indicated no inflammation or infections.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Total RNA isolation
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 537 539 537 539 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g </italic>
Total RNA was extracted from frozen tissues using the TRIzol reagent (Life Technologies) according to manufacturer's protocol. Briefly, endometrial and cervical tissues were ground using a mortar and pestle under liquid nitrogen. The ground tissue was transferred to tubes containing appropriate amounts of TRIzol reagent and homogenized using a Brinkmann homogenizer. The mixture was allowed to set for 5-10 min followed by the addition of appropriate amount of chloroform. This mixture was shaken vigorously and centrifuged at 8,000 x g for 30 min. The aqueous layer was collected and the RNA precipitated using isopropanol. The RNA pellet was washed using 70% alcohol and dissolved in RNase free water. To determine the quality of extracted RNA, the dissolved samples were elecrophoresed on 1% formaldehyde-agarose gels to check the integrity of 18s and 28s bands. Samples with OD 260/280 greater than 1.50 were used in the study.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Slot blot analyses
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 334 338 326 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1</italic>
###xml 340 344 332 336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2</italic>
###xml 346 351 338 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B</italic>
###xml 353 359 345 351 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC</italic>
###xml 361 366 353 358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 375 380 364 369 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">actin</italic>
###xml 489 491 478 480 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>
Total RNA (10 mug) extracted from tissues was blotted directly onto nylon membranes using a Hoefer PR 648 slot blot manifold (Amersham Biosciences, San Francisco, CA). The membranes were pre-hybridized in 5x SSPE, 5x Denhardt's solution, 0.5% SDS, 50% formamide and 40 mug/ml salmon sperm DNA for 12 h at 42degreesC. The cDNA probes (MUC1, MUC2, MUC5B, MUC5AC, MUC8 and beta-actin) were labeled by the random priming technique using DECAprime II kit (Ambion, Austin, TX). Hybridization of 32P-labeled cDNA probes was carried out at 42degreesC for 14-16 h. After hybridization, membranes were washed twice in 2x SSC and 0.1% SDS for 10 min. at room temperature, followed by two additional washes for 20 min at 65degreesC in 1x SSC and 0.1% SDS solution. The membranes were subsequently exposed to Kodak X-Omat AR films at -70degreesC. The films were scanned using a Personal SI Densitometer (Molecular Dynamics, Sunnyvale, CA). Following exposure, the membranes were stripped with 0.5% SDS and re-probed with beta-actin. Densitrometric units were calculated for each sample after normalization of the readings with corresponding densitrometric readings obtained using beta-actin cDNA probe. The hybridization experiments on total RNA from the tissues was performed in triplicate for statistical analyses.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Source of mucin cDNA probes
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 91 95 91 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 272 277 272 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 330 332 330 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 335 340 335 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2 </italic>
###xml 386 388 386 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 391 398 391 398 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC </italic>
###xml 412 418 412 418 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 521 526 521 526 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 594 595 594 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 666 671 666 671 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
The cDNA probes used in the study were designed to exclude the VNTR regions of the studied MUC genes. This step was deemed essential to avoid the differences in the numbers of tandem repeats commonly associated with mucins among different individuals. The cDNA probes for MUC1 (~500 bp) was kindly provided by Dr. Sandra Gendler [30], MUC2 (~450 bp) was a kind gift from Dr. James Gum [31], MUC5AC (~800 bp) and MUC5B (~340 bp) were generated in the laboratory using specific primers to the published sequences. Earlier, MUC8 cDNA probe (1.4 kb) sequence has been reported from our laboratory [5]. This sequence was used in this study to develop a non-repeat 195 bp MUC8 cDNA probe. A 1.1 kb cDNA probe specific for beta-actin was used for the analyses of housekeeping gene.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Reverse Transcription and Polymerase Chain Reaction (RT-PCR)
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 388 389 376 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Five micrograms of total RNA was reverse transcribed to cDNA by 200 units Superscript II reverse transcriptase (Life Technologies). The reaction mixture was then treated with 2U RNaseH at 37degreesC for 20 min and stored at -20degreesC. Twenty percent of the first strand cDNA was used for the PCR amplification. Primers and annealing temperatures used for RT-PCR are summarized in Table 1.
###end p 25
###begin title 26
Statistical analyses
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 45 49 45 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 220 222 220 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">t </italic>
The data obtained from slot blot analyses of MUC genes in both normal and tumor tissues were subjected to parametric statistical analysis using SAS software (SAS Institute, Cary, NC). Two tailed unequal variance student t test was used to establish statistical significance. A p value of less than 0.05 was considered significant.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
Results
###end title 28
###begin title 29
Quantitation of mucin gene expression in endometrial tissues
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 17 18 17 18 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 18 23 18 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 158 164 158 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 298 299 298 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 321 327 321 327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 456 461 456 461 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 622 623 622 623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 681 685 681 685 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 762 763 762 763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>
###xml 794 799 794 799 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2 </italic>
###xml 803 810 803 810 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC </italic>
As shown in Fig. 1MUC1 expression was significantly lower in the normal endometrium as compared to the endometrial adenocarcinomas (p = 0.001). Expression of MUC5B followed a similar pattern with higher expression observed in 8 out of 13 endometrial tumor tissues studied over normal tissues (Fig. 2). The differences in MUC5B levels between the cancerous and non-cancerous endometrial samples was significant (p = 0.006). On the other hand, expression of MUC8 was high in all endometrial tissues, with the expression in endometrial adenocarcinomas being significantly higher than the normal endometrium (p = 0.003) (Fig. 3). The box plot analyses showing the relationship between MUC gene expression in endometrial tumor and normal tissues are depicted in Fig. 7. No appreciable expression of MUC2 and MUC5AC in normal and tumor tissues was detected by slot blot analyses.
###end p 30

###xml 128 129 128 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 156 161 156 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B</italic>

###xml 256 262 256 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>

While the expression of MUC5B, was higher than MUC1 in normal cervical tissues, no statistical significance was observed in MUC5B levels between normal and cancerous cervical tissues (p = 0.14) (Fig. 5). The expression MUC8 mRNA, were high in all cervical tissues with no statistical difference observed in expression between cancerous and non-cancerous tissues (p = 0.5). Also, box plots depicting MUC gene expression in cervical tumor and normal tissues are illustrated in Fig. 8.

###xml 391 396 391 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 433 438 433 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2 </italic>
###xml 442 449 442 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC </italic>
###xml 563 566 563 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC</italic>
###xml 568 569 568 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</italic>
###xml 571 572 571 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</italic>
###xml 574 577 574 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5AC</italic>
###xml 579 582 579 582 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5B </italic>
###xml 586 588 586 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </italic>
###xml 646 649 646 649 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC</italic>
###xml 651 652 651 652 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</italic>
###xml 654 656 654 656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </italic>
###xml 660 662 660 662 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">7 </italic>
###xml 663 665 663 665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 744 748 744 748 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 802 807 802 807 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC4 </italic>
###xml 811 817 811 817 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 861 866 861 866 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC6 </italic>
###xml 870 877 870 877 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC </italic>
###xml 878 880 878 880 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 987 992 987 992 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2 </italic>
###xml 996 1003 996 1003 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC </italic>
###xml 141 146 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
This study is primarily focused on the quantitating the expression of five mucin genes, namely, MUC1, MUC2, MUC5AC, MUC5B and MUC8 in normal human endometrial and cervical tissues and respective tumors. Studies in tumors have suggested that mucins are aberrantly expressed in cancerous conditions. In the present investigation we observed that of the five MUC genes studied, MUC1, MUC5B and MUC8 were expressed at higher levels than MUC2 and MUC5AC in endometrial and cervical tissues. Similar studies by other investigators on endocervical tissues have revealed MUCs 1, 4, 5AC, 5B and 6 were expressed at high levels with very low expression of MUCs 2, 3 and 7 [26]. However, a follow-up study to this work quantifying the expression of these MUC genes in normal endocervical epithelium revealed that MUC4 and MUC5B were predominantly expressed as compared to MUC6 and MUC5AC [32]. While acknowledging the variations in the analyzed tissue types, it appears that expression patterns of MUC2 and MUC5AC genes in this study are broadly confirmatory with earlier reports.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 102 104 102 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 105 107 105 107 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 295 297 295 297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 334 338 334 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 439 441 439 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 476 481 476 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 567 569 567 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 610 614 610 614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 892 894 892 894 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 922 924 922 924 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 712 720 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 992 997 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Human endometrial epithelium undergoes progesterone-modulated differentiation during menstrual cycle [33,34]. The qualitative and quantitative changes in the secretion of the endometrium are associated with the proliferation of glandular epithelium with increased golgi and secretory apparatus [35]. Accordingly, mucin secretions and MUC gene expression of the female reproductive tissues are dependant on the stage of the menstrual cycle[36]. Earlier studies have shown that MUC1 expression in endometrial tissues is at the highest in early to mid secretory phases [37]. To minimize the ambiguity of elevated MUC gene expression due to menstrual cycle, the tissues studied in the present investigation are from patients in either proliferative or late secretory phases. Mucin gene regulation and expression has been associated with the effects of immune cell derived inflammatory mediators [38] and bacterial endotoxins [39] on the secretory epithelium in various chronic diseases states of human body. However, in this study the pathology reports of the obtained tissues indicate no inflammation or infection thus minimizing the effect of these mediators on overall results of this study.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 210 212 210 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 241 246 241 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 417 422 417 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 493 495 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 505 507 505 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 518 520 518 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 537 539 537 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 551 553 551 553 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
MUC1, a trans-membrane mucin, has been studied extensively in the female reproductive tract and is expressed in the endometrium. It plays an important role during implantation and maintenance of the embryo [40,41]. Our studies revealed high MUC1 levels in endometrial adenocarcinomas and cervical carcinomas when compared to the normal endometrial and cervical tissues. These results are in accord with studies where MUC1 up-regulation was reported in variety of carcinomas including pancreas[42], breast[43], stomach[44], colon rectum [45] and lung [46].
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 339 341 339 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 402 406 402 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1</italic>
###xml 460 462 460 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 481 486 481 486 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1 </italic>
###xml 924 926 924 926 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 760 768 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
In addition, to altered gene regulation patterns in cancerous conditions, it is reported that alterations may exist in the carbohydrate structures attached to the protein backbone or in the protein backbone itself. In MUC1, alteration in glycosylation patterns leads to tumor-specific peptide epitopes that are exposed in cancerous cells [47]. In breast cancer tissue, an alternatively spliced form of MUC1, which is completely devoid of repeats was observed [14]. This variant of MUC1 was not expressed in adjacent normal breast tissue. Such studies and others indicate the importance of mucins as tumor markers for diagnostic as well as for therapeutic purposes. For example, in ovarian cancers the CA125 antigen is routinely used to monitor the progress of patients. Recently, investigators found that the CA125 antigen does indeed belong to the mucin family of genes and its core was identified and designated as MUC16 [48].
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 58 63 58 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 98 99 98 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 144 149 144 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 258 263 258 263 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 319 321 319 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 368 373 368 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 444 449 444 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 575 580 575 580 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 725 729 725 729 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC </italic>
###xml 807 812 807 812 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 816 822 816 822 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 68 73 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 152 157 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our laboratory has previously reported a novel mucin gene MUC8 from human normal tracheal tissue [5] and here we have studied the expression of MUC8 in human normal as well as cancerous endometrial and cervical tissues. Our earlier studies demonstrated that MUC8 is expressed in the male and female reproductive tract [29]. The present work has led us to believe that MUC8 is a major mucin expressed in the female reproductive tract. Levels of MUC8 are significantly higher in the endometrial adenocarcinomas as compared to the normal endometrium. Although the expression of MUC8 was very high in the cervical tissues, no difference in expression was observed among the cancerous and non-cancerous tissues. In the context of MUC gene expression, this is the first study reporting differential expression of MUC8 and MUC5B in cervical and endometrial tissues.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 82 86 82 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC1</italic>
###xml 88 94 88 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5B </italic>
###xml 98 103 98 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC8 </italic>
###xml 169 174 169 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC2 </italic>
###xml 178 185 178 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MUC5AC </italic>
One significant conclusion that can be drawn from this study is that mucin genes, MUC1, MUC5B and MUC8 are all up-regulated in endometrial adenocarcinomas. Furthermore, MUC2 and MUC5AC were found to be expressed at extremely low levels in the endometrial and cervical tissues studied. In conclusion, this study provides significant information on mucin genes in the female reproductive tract and attempts to understand if there are disease-related changes that mucin genes may undergo in endometrial and cervical carcinomas.
