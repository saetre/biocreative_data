Neuropathic pain develops normally in mice lacking both Nav1.7 and Nav1.8

###begin p 2
###xml 51 52 47 48 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 62 63 58 59 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 345 346 341 342 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 356 357 352 353 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 404 405 400 401 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 490 491 486 487 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 501 502 497 498 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 738 739 734 735 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 866 867 862 863 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 876 877 872 873 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
Two voltage gated sodium channel alpha-subunits, Nav1.7 and Nav1.8, are expressed at high levels in nociceptor terminals and have been implicated in the development of inflammatory pain. Mis-expression of voltage-gated sodium channels by damaged sensory neurons has also been implicated in the development of neuropathic pain, but the role of Nav1.7 and Nav1.8 is uncertain. Here we show that deleting Nav1.7 has no effect on the development of neuropathic pain. Double knockouts of both Nav1.7 and Nav1.8 also develop normal levels of neuropathic pain, despite a lack of inflammatory pain symptoms and altered mechanical and thermal acute pain thresholds. These studies demonstrate that, in contrast to the highly significant role for Nav1.7 in determining inflammatory pain thresholds, the development of neuropathic pain does not require the presence of either Nav1.7 or Nav1.8 alone or in combination.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Background
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 327 328 306 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 423 424 398 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 552 553 523 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 851 852 818 819 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 647 652 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 785 791 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Voltage gated sodium channels (VGSC) underlie the electrical excitability of nerve and muscle. VGSCs consist of pore forming alpha-subunits and auxiliary beta-subunits. There are ten cloned alpha-subunits and 4 beta-subunits. The beta-subunits modulate the localisation, expression and functional properties of alpha-subunits [1]. Different alpha-subunits have distinct electrophysiological and pharmacological properties [2]. The complex pattern of expression of alpha-subunits may imply special roles for particular subunits in different cell types [3]. Many loss- as well as gain-of-function mutations of alpha-subunits have been identified in human conditions characterised with epilepsy, seizures, ataxia and increased sensitivity to pain. This suggests that mutations of VGSC in humans are significant factors in aetiology of neuronal diseases [4].
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 167 168 163 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 430 431 422 423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 432 433 424 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 438 439 430 431 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 449 450 441 442 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 527 528 515 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 529 530 517 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 531 532 519 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 537 538 525 526 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 615 616 603 604 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 652 653 640 641 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 719 720 707 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 725 726 713 714 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 820 822 808 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 823 825 811 813 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 975 976 959 960 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 997 998 981 982 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 1008 1010 992 994 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 1054 1055 1038 1039 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 1060 1062 1044 1046 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1324 1325 1304 1305 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 1339 1341 1319 1321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 1342 1344 1322 1324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 889 893 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Nociceptors are a subset of sensory neurons that respond to noxious thermal, mechanical and chemical stimuli. Nociceptors express multiple subtypes of alpha-subunits [3]. Tissue and nerve damage leads to changes in expression and function of alpha-subunits that in turn can lead to change in the excitability of sensory neurons. Changes in the excitability of sensory neurons are thought to underlie some chronic pain conditions [5,6]. Nav1.8 and Nav1.7 are two alpha-subunits that are abundant in nociceptive sensory neurons [3,7,8]. Nav1.8 is expressed exclusively in sensory neurons and is not found in the CNS [9]. Functional characterization of Nav1.8 positive neurons revealed that more than 85% are nociceptors [8]. Nav1.7 is expressed principally in peripheral neurons with very weak expression detected in CNS [10,11]. As there are no subunit specific blockers, gene knockouts in mice have provided insights into the role of individual alpha-subunits genes in pain [5]. Deletion of the Nav1.8 gene [12] and nociceptor-specific knockout of the Nav1.7 [13] gene have identified a role for these two alpha-subunits in setting mechanical and, to a lesser extent, thermal pain thresholds. In addition, behavioral studies have revealed deficits in inflammatory pain models, most dramatically in the nociceptor-specific Nav1.7 knockout [12,13]. These findings suggest that these alpha-subunits could be targets for new anti-inflammatory drugs.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 178 179 178 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 180 181 180 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 292 293 288 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 294 295 290 291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 584 586 576 578 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 587 589 579 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 640 642 632 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 665 666 657 658 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 734 735 726 727 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 855 857 847 849 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 902 908 894 900 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al </italic>
###xml 972 973 964 965 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 1050 1052 1042 1044 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1135 1136 1127 1128 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 1162 1163 1154 1155 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 1183 1185 1175 1177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 1238 1239 1230 1231 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 748 753 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1176 1181 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Peripheral nerve injury leads to lowered pain thresholds, enhanced responsiveness and/or ectopic activity in sensory neurons that ultimately leads to hyperalgesia and allodynia [5,6]. Changes in expression of alpha-subunits of VGSCs have been documented in models of peripheral nerve injury [5,6]. This has lead to the hypothesis that modulation of alpha-subunits in sensory neurons may underlie the increased neuronal excitability of sensory neurons following peripheral nerve injury. Pharmacological blockade of sodium channel activity has been shown to attenuate ectopic activity [14,15] and reverse hyperalgesia following nerve injury [16]. While the role of Nav1.7 in neuropathic pain remains to be investigated, analysis of a Nav1.8 knockout mouse indicated that it is not involved in alteration of pain threshold following peripheral nerve injury [17]. This is in contrast to the finding of Lai et al who reported that antisense oligonucleotides directed against Nav1.8 administered intrathecally completely reverse neuropathic pain behavior [18]. It is possible that this discrepancy could be due to the up-regulation of the Nav1.7 channel seen in the Nav1.8 knockout mouse [12] which might mask an otherwise important role for Nav1.8 in neuropathic pain.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 55 56 55 56 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 128 129 128 129 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 185 186 185 186 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 247 248 247 248 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 258 259 258 259 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 302 303 302 303 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 312 313 312 313 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 375 376 375 376 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 136 141 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
In the present study we investigated the role of the Nav1.7 channel in neuropathic pain using nociceptor-specific deletion of Nav1.7 in mouse. In addition, we readdressed the role of Nav1.8 in neuropathic pain by generating a double knockout of Nav1.8 and Nav1.7. We reasoned that the co-deletion of Nav1.7 in Nav1.8-expressing neurons should reveal any potential role for Nav1.8 in neuropathic pain.
###end p 7

Nociceptor-specific Nav1.7 knockout mice and their littermate controls were generated as described previously [13]. It is not possible to generate global knockouts of both Nav1.8 and Nav1.7 since global deletion of Nav1.7 is lethal at P0 [13]. Therefore, we generated Nav1.7 and Nav1.8 double-knockout (DKO) mice by exploiting the fact that the Nav1.8Cre allele has Cre sequence inserted in exon 1 followed by transcriptional stop signals [19]. Consequently homozygous Nav1.8Cre mice are Nav1.8 global-knockouts (Nav18 KO) and show no Nav1.8 currents in sensory neurons [19].

Acute pain thresholds
###end title 17
###begin p 18

We measured thermal pain thresholds using the hotplate and Hargreave's tests. In the hotplate test, which involves supraspinal activity, the response latency was not different between the three mouse groups (WT 36.1 +/- 6.5 sec, n = 7; Nav1.8 KO 29.0 +/- 5.6, n = 7 and DKO 32.7 +/- 6.35, n = 7), figure 3a. However, the response latency in the Hargreave's test was doubled in DKO group (15.30 +/- 0.91 sec, n = 7) compared to both WT (6.88 +/- 0.28, n = 11) and Nav1.8 KO (8.47 +/- 0.78, n = 7) groups, figure 3b. The latency in the Nav1.8 KO was higher that that of the WT group which is in agreement with the finding of Akopian et al using the conventional Nav1.8 global knockout [12]. The nociceptor-specific Nav1.7 knockout alone shows a 40% increase in latency in the Hargreave's test [13]

###begin title 29
Discussion
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 66 67 62 63 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 77 78 73 74 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 455 456 451 452 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 560 562 556 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 567 568 563 564 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 706 708 702 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 756 757 752 753 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 806 808 802 804 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 915 916 911 912 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 921 923 917 919 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
This study confirms that the two sodium channel alpha- subunits Nav1.7 and Nav1.8, expressed selectively in nociceptive sensory neurons, have important roles in nociception and in pain. In the absence of specific pharmacological blockers, the use of genetic approaches, a combination of global and nociceptor-specific knockouts, has enabled us to carry out studies exploring the contribution of these two isoforms of VGSCs in different pain conditions. Nav1.8 knockouts are already known to have deficits in inflammatory and visceral but not neuropathic pain [12]. Nav1.7 nociceptor-specific knockouts are almost totally refractory to changes in peripheral pain thresholds evoked by inflammatory mediator [13]. This mirrors the inflammatory phenotype of Nav1.7 gain-of-function mutations in erythermalgia [21]. This heritable disorder is caused by point mutations that lead to altered thresholds of activation in Nav1.7 [22]
###end p 30
###begin title 31
Acute pain thresholds
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 110 111 110 111 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 120 121 120 121 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 287 289 287 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 290 292 290 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 338 339 338 339 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 369 370 369 370 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 449 451 449 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 452 454 452 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 569 570 569 570 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 606 608 606 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3b</xref>
###xml 713 714 713 714 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 724 725 724 725 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 885 886 885 886 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 944 946 944 946 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 959 960 959 960 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 1005 1007 1005 1007 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 47 51 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 137 141 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
analysis of acute mechanical thresholds in DKO mice confirmed previous findings that the knockout of either Nav1.8 or Nav1.7 renders the mice resistant in the Randel-Selitto test of noxious mechanical pressure while the responses to von Frey hairs remain unchanged compared to controls [12,13]. Furthermore, it has been shown that both Nav1.8 and nociceptor-specific Nav1.7 knockouts have small increases of about 20-40% in their thermal threshold [12,13]. Interestingly, we found that the thermal threshold in the Hargreave's test was doubled in the DKO compared to Nav1.8 knockout or WT controls, figure 3b. The increase is more than the sum of the phenotype in the individual knockouts and may indicate that Nav1.8 and Nav1.7 are the major VGSC isoforms present in nociceptive terminals and their deletion dramatically increases pain threshold. This is supported by the fact that Nav1.7 is transported to nerve terminals of sensory neurons [10] and that Nav1.8 currents are localized in the terminals [23].
###end p 32
###begin title 33
Inflammatory pain
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 90 91 90 91 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 163 165 163 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 266 267 266 267 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 367 368 367 368 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 446 448 446 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 520 522 520 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4b</xref>
###xml 633 635 633 635 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4b</xref>
###xml 838 839 838 839 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 881 882 881 882 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 976 978 976 978 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 554 558 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We have not studied pain behavior in inflammatory models where the nociceptive-specific Nav1.7 knockout has shown complete deficits in inflammatory pain behavior [13], because it would not be possible to observe further changes brought about by the co-deletion of Nav1.8. We only investigated the pain response in the formalin model. While the nociceptive-specific Nav1.7 knockout showed a reduction in the second phase to about 50% of controls [13] the DKO showed a slightly bigger reduction to 30% of controls, figure 4b. This is in contrast to Nav1.8 mice that showed no reduction in the second phase compared to controls, figure 4b. Therefore, the reduction in the second phase in the DKO is a new phenotype rather than a mere summation of the phenotype in the individual knockouts. This highlights further the predominant role for Nav1.7 in inflammatory pain. Expression of Nav1.7 has been reported to increase in sensory neurons subsequent to induction of inflammation [24].
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 29 31 29 31 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 66 67 66 67 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 76 77 76 77 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 135 136 135 136 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 248 249 248 249 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 297 298 297 298 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 323 325 323 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 449 450 449 450 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 464 465 464 465 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 495 496 495 496 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 527 528 527 528 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
This study and our previous [13] work have explored the role of Nav1.7 in Nav1.8-expressing neurons, since our strategy exploits the Nav1.8 promoter to drive Cre expression. We have shown previously using non-quantitative RT-PCR that not all the Nav1.7 mRNA in DRGs from the nociceptor-specific Nav1.7 knockout is deleted [13]. This indicates that there is a population of sensory neurons, the size of which has yet to be determined, that express Nav1.7 but not Nav1.8. Therefore, the role of Nav1.7 in sensory neurons where Nav1.8 is not expressed (and which do not express Cre) remains to be studied.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 48 49 48 49 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 59 60 59 60 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
From all the above, our results indicate that Nav1.8 and Nav1.7 subunits contribute to the excitability of peripheral nerve terminals and their modulation is critical for peripheral sensitisation in inflammatory pain
###end p 36
###begin title 37
Neuropathic pain
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 30 31 30 31 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 139 140 139 140 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 294 295 294 295 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 308 309 308 309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 382 384 382 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 389 390 389 390 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 268 272 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We investigated the role of Nav1.7 expressed in nociceptors in neuropathic pain. Analysis of neuropathic pain in the nociceptor-specific Nav1.7 knockout using the Chung model showed that they developed mechanical allodynia to the same extent as the littermate control mice (homozygous floxed Nav1.7), figure 5. This clearly shows that unlike its critical role in inflammatory pain [13], Nav1.7 does not contribute to neuropathic pain.
###end p 38

###xml 441 443 441 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 453 459 453 459 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al </italic>
###xml 460 462 460 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 489 491 489 491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 565 566 565 566 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>

###xml 59 63 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In addition, the Nav1.8 knockout and WT as well as the DKO mice developed a profound mechanical allodynia the level of which was indistinguishable between the three groups. This provides further evidence that Nav1.8 does not contribute to mechanical allodynia as reported by Kerr et al [17]. In addition our results rule out Nav1.7 up-regulation in the Nav1.8 knockout as the reason behind the discrepancy between the finding of Kerr et al [17] and Lai et al [18]. Surprisingly Lai et al [18] failed to detect the changes in acute pain thresholds observed in the Nav1.8 KO both in this study and that of Akopian et al [12], which would be expected to occur if Nav1.8 had been down-regulated.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 95 96 95 96 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>

###xml 1404 1406 1400 1402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 619 624 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The lack of changes in mechanical allodynia in any individual or even in double knockouts of Nav1.8 and Nav1.7 subunits is consistent with recent data by Flake et al. They reported that changes in sodium currents shortly after nerve injury do not correlate with an increase in neuronal excitability and that the sum of changes to ionic currents, and not a single class of voltage-gated ion channel, underlie increased neuronal excitability [25]. This does not, however, exclude a potential role for both Nav1.7 and Nav1.8 in spontaneous neuropathic pain as suggested by the finding that neuromas in the Nav1.8 knockout mouse display less ectopic discharges than wildtype littermates [26]. These studies confirm the importance of Nav1.7 and Nav1.8 in the physiological processes that underlie altered peripheral thresholds in inflammatory pain. Neuropathic pain that arises as a consequence of nerve damage and neuronal dysfunction is, however, not dependent on the presence of these two sodium channels. In terms of the contribution of other alpha-subunits to neuropathic pain, a strong correlation has been found between the expression of Nav1.3 and the appearance of neuropathic pain. GDNF that reverses neuropathic pain behaviour also normalises Nav1.3 expression [27], and recent antisense studies have supported the view that increased Nav1.3 expression contributes to neuropathic pain development [28]
###end p 40
###begin title 41
Conclusion
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 55 56 55 56 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 66 67 66 67 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
In summary, our results indicate a critical role for Nav1.7 and Nav1.8 in setting pain thresholds of nociceptive nerve terminals and in their sensitisation following tissue damage and inflammation. However, the presence of these channels, or changes to their expression and function are not required for the establishment of mechanical allodynia arising from nerve injury.
###end p 42

Genotyping of mice strains
###end title 44
###begin p 45
###xml 2 3 2 3 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 23 24 23 24 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 62 64 62 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
Nav1.8Cre and floxed Nav1.7 lines were produced as described [13]. Both strains were back-crossed at last 5 times onto a C57/BL6 background.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 83 84 83 84 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 227 228 227 228 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
###xml 328 329 328 329 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v</sub>
Genomic DNA was prepared from tail biopsies and genotyped using PCR. Primers for Nav1.7 are (CAGAGATTTCTGCATTAGAATTTGTTC) and (AGTCTTTGTGGCACACGTTACCTC) which give a WT band of 317 bp and a floxed band of 461 bp. Primers for Nav1.8 are (TGTAGATGGACTGCAGAGGATGGA) and (ttacccggtgtgtgctgtagaaag) which give a WT band of 460 bp. Nav1.8Cre was detected by primers (aaatttgcctgcattaccggtcga) and (aaatgttgctggatagtttttactgcc) located inside Cre sequence which give a band of 249 bp.
