Our interest in the Schizosaccharomyces pombe RecQ helicase, rqh1+, led us to investigate the function of a related putative DNA helicase, srs2+. We identified the srs2+ homolog in S.pombe, and found that srs2+ is not essential for cell viability. A Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 double mutant grows extremely slowly with aberrant shaped cells and low viability. This slow growth does not appear to be related to stalled replication, as Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells showed higher survival rates, compared with Deltarqh1, when stalled forks were increased by UV irradiation or hydroxy urea treatment. Consistent with this result, we found that Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells progress through S-phase with a slight delay, but undergo a checkpoint-dependent arrest presumably at G2/M. Further, we found that Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 slow growth is related to recombination, as loss of either the rhp51+ or rhp57+ recombination genes improves cell growth in the double mutant. Deltasrs2 is also synthetic lethal with Deltarhp54, another homologous recombination gene. This lethality is suppressed in a Deltarhp51 background. Together, these results demonstrate a clear genetic interaction between rqh1+, srs2+ and the genes of the homologous recombination pathway.

The faithful replication of DNA is essential for maintaining the integrity of the cell's genetic information. Many challenges exist for the replication machinery to perform its task. One problem arises when the growing replication fork is halted. Replication fork arrest occurs when the replication complex encounters a site of DNA damage, some secondary DNA structures or nucleotide depletion, as in the case of treatment with hydroxy urea (HU) (1,2). Stalled replication forks have been shown to create sites for recombination, as demonstrated in both bacterial and eukaryotic systems (2-5). The loss of the recQ homolog in various systems leads to hyper-recombination when the cells undergo replication arrest induced by HU or ultraviolet (UV) DNA damage (6-13). These data suggest that the RecQ helicases act to suppress recombination at arrested replication forks, although the RecQ helicases have also been shown to promote certain types of recombination (10,14-16). Different models have been proposed to explain how RecQ helicases might suppress recombination. In Escherichia coli, arrested replication forks are believed to be stabilized by RecQ through the formation of a triple helix (10,17). Studies on the human and Schizo saccharomyces pombe RecQ helicases (Blm and Rqh1, respectively) suggest that they act to remove Holliday junctions formed at stalled replication forks, by reverse branch migration (18,19). In one of these studies, expression of the bacterial Holliday junction resolvase was shown to significantly suppress UV and HU sensitivity in S.pombe Deltarqh1 cells (18). These results suggest that cells lacking Rqh1 accumulate Holliday junctions at stalled replication forks.

Recent findings in Saccharomyces cerevisiae demonstrating a synthetic interaction between srs2 and the recQ homolog sgs1 suggest the possibility that Srs2 might also function at stalled replication forks (14,20-22). The S.cerevisiae Srs2 DNA helicase, which is structurally related to the bacterial helicase II (UvrD) (23), was first discovered in a screen for suppressors of trimethoprim sensitivity of rad6 and rad18 mutants (24,25). It was independently characterized as a suppressor of homologous recombination (named HPR5), as mutants showed a hyper-recombination phenotype (24,26,27). SRS2 has since been shown to be associated with the RAD6 post replication repair (PRR) pathway. Specifically, Srs2 functions with the RAD5-dependent error-free branch of PRR, where it has been shown to suppress the UV sensitivities of rad5, ubc13 and mms2 mutants (28). This suppression is lost in cells lacking a functional recombination system, consistent with a role of Srs2 blocking damage from being chanelled into the RAD52-dependent homologous recombination pathway (28,29). The synthetic interaction between srs2 and sgs1 was shown to be suppressed by mutations in the homologous recombination genes rad51, rad55 and rad57 (14). It has also been demonstrated that S.cerevisiaesrs2 shows either a lethal phenotype or poor growth when combined with a number of other mutants (rdh54, rad50, mre11, xrs2, top3 and rad54) (21,30,31). The synthetic interactions of these double mutants are furthermore suppressed by mutating the recombination genes rad51, rad52, rad55 and rad57 (14,30-32). In addition, it was shown that srs2 rad54 and srs2 rdh54 lethality can be suppressed by loss of checkpoint function (31). These data are complemented by evidence of genetic interaction between SRS2 and RAD52, based on suppression of rad52 mutations by deletion of SRS2 (33,34). srs2 mutants have also been shown to suppress hyporecombination of some rad51 and rad52 alleles (24,32). Together, these results demonstrate that Srs2 function in S.cerevisiae is linked to homologous recombination.

Both Srs2 and the RecQ helicases are associated with DNA damage response and/or genomic instability. In S.cerevisiae, Sgs1 and Srs2 have been shown to participate in activation of the intra-S checkpoint following exposure to MMS (35,36). In S.pombe, rqh1 mutants show sensitivity to UV and gamma radiation and treatment with HU (7,8,37). rqh1+ was originally identified as rad12+ in a screen for UV sensitive mutants (38). One phenotype associated with rqh1 mutants is that although cells undergo a normal G1-S arrest following DNA damage, they recover poorly. Fluorescence microscopy has revealed that the problem is in mitosis as cells divide with torn chromosomes and uneven distribution of nuclear material (7,8). It has been proposed in S.cerevisiae that Srs2 acts in recombination repair of double strand breaks to stabilize the invading single strand with its homolog, thereby acting against reverse branch migration (39). Srs2 is phosphorylated in response to intra-S DNA damage, and this phosphorylation is dependent on both checkpoint activation and Cdk1 (cyclin-dependent kinase) (36). Following DNA damage in S-phase, srs2 mutants do not fully arrest and Rad53 phosphorylation is reduced, suggesting Srs2 functions in an intra-S checkpoint. In addition, srs2 mutants suppress the lethality of a rad17 checkpoint mutant following intra-S DNA damage (36).
###end p 6

###xml 1116 1120 1084 1088 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>

###xml 1319 1323 1275 1279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 1325 1327 1281 1283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf581c41">41</xref>
###xml 1496 1500 1448 1452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>
###xml 1506 1510 1454 1458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 1569 1573 1513 1517 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 1643 1647 1583 1587 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>
###xml 1653 1657 1589 1593 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 1810 1814 1742 1746 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>
###xml 1820 1824 1748 1752 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 1844 1848 1768 1772 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>
###xml 1883 1888 1803 1808 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rhp54</italic>
###xml 1933 1938 1849 1854 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rhp51</italic>
###xml 241 248 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 372 379 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
Our interest in rqh1+ has led us to study other genes related to its activity such as top3+ (40). In this context we have sought to identify other potentially interacting proteins. Therefore, we identified a homolog of SRS2 by searching the S.pombe genomic database and created a null mutant, Deltasrs2. As reported previously (41), we found that Srs2 is not essential in S.pombe and that the single mutant does not exhibit significant sensitivity to either UV damage or treatment with HU. Furthermore, the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 double mutant shows a severe slow growth defect compared with the single mutants. Our studies found that the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 double mutant progresses through S-phase with a slight delay but arrests in G2/M with many of these cells ultimately undergoing an aberrant mitosis. The G2/M arrest is likely due to a checkpoint dependent DNA damage response, as deletion of the rad9+ checkpoint gene suppresses the accumulation of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells in G2/M. This arrest appears to be related to recombination, as deletion of recombination genes also suppresses the slow growth of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 and improves their viability. This result is in contrast to a recently published paper where it was reported that loss of recombination actually worsened the slow growth of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 (41). We will point out why we believe we find these differences. Interestingly, we found that when replication fork stalling is increased by treatment with UV or HU, Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells show improved survival compared with that of a Deltarqh1 single mutant. These data imply that the synthetic phenotype of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 is not a direct result of the double mutant inability to cope with stalled replication. Lastly, in studying the effect of loss of recombination on Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 we found that Deltasrs2 is synthetically lethal with Deltarhp54, and this lethality is suppressed in a Deltarhp51 mutant background.

###begin title 9

Yeast strains, molecular techniques and media

###begin p 10
###xml 72 73 72 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkf581tb1">1</xref>
###xml 130 135 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>

###begin p 12
###xml 14 18 10 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 28 32 20 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>

###begin title 13
Growth kinetics
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 427 428 421 422 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 438 439 432 433 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sup>
Analysis of the relative growth rates of strains was carried out by one of two approaches. In the first case, a single colony was picked from a freshly streaked plate and the cells streaked to single colonies on YEA plates. The plates were incubated at 30degreesC for 3-4 days and photographed. To get a more quantitative value of growth, overnight liquid cultures were started from single colonies and grown to mid log (2 x 105 to 1 x 106 cells/ml) at 30degreesC overnight. The cells were counted beginning at time 0 and then at every hour for 9 h. The data were plotted as fold increase against time in hours.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
UV and HU sensitivity
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 88 89 88 89 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sup>
###xml 99 100 99 100 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sup>
###xml 271 272 265 266 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
Overnight liquid cultures were started from single colonies and grown to mid log (1 x 106 to 5 x 106 cells/ml) at 30degreesC overnight. The cells were counted and an appropriate number of cells for each dose was plated onto YEA plates. Plates were irradiated with 100 J/m2 of 254 nm UV light, incubated for 3-5 days and counted. For HU sensitivity studies, the appropriate dilution of cells was spotted onto YEA plates containing the indicated concentration of HU. Plates were incubated for 4-10 days, counted and photographed. Alternatively, 12 mM HU was added to mid log cultures of cells that were then incubated with shaking at 30degreesC. Cells were plated onto YEA plates at time 0 and every 3 h after for 9 h. The plates were incubated at 30degreesC for 3-5 days and counted.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
###xml 41 42 41 42 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
Synchronization and release of cells in G1
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 25 26 25 26 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 370 371 352 353 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
###xml 531 532 497 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g</italic>
###xml 733 734 699 700 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
To synchronize cells in G1, we used a combination of nitrogen starvation and size fractionation on lactose gradients. Cells were grown to saturation in YEA and then switched to EMM for 24 h. These cells were washed twice with water and then resuspended in 100 ml of EMM without nitrogen and cultured at 30degreesC for 20 h. The cells were counted and approximately5 x 108 cells in 700 microl were layered onto 13 ml 7-30% lactose gradients made in EMM without nitrogen. The gradients were centrifuged for 4 min at approximately300 g. The tubes were punctured from the bottom and 0.5 ml fractions collected. Fractions were viewed microscopically and fractions containing the smallest cells pooled. These cells were released from the G1 block by switching them to YEA media. At 30 min intervals 1.5 ml aliquots were taken, collected by centrifugation, fixed in 70% ethanol and stored at 4degreesC.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
FACS analysis
###end title 19
###begin p 20
Fixed cells were collected by centrifugation and washed five times in 50 mM sodium citrate. These cells were resuspended in 0.5 ml of 50 mM sodium citrate, 100 microg/ml RNase and incubated at 37degreesC for at least 2 h. The cells were then stained by adding 0.5 ml 50 mM sodium citrate, 1 microM Sytox Green (Molecular Probes) and analyzed within 2 h. The cells were assayed using a Becton Dickson Facs Calibur with Cell Quest 4.0 software.
###end p 20
###begin title 21
Cell viability
###end title 21
###begin p 22
Fresh plates were struck for each strain, and day cultures started from single colonies, grown in YEA, with shaking at 30degreesC for 11 h. The cultures were counted and the cells plated on YEA plates. The plates were incubated at 30degreesC for 4 days and colonies counted.
###end p 22

###begin p 24

Fluorescence microscopy was carried out as previously described (41). Cells were fixed in 70% ethanol. Following fixation, cells were collected by centrifugation, washed in 50 mM sodium citrate and stained with DAPI.

The srs2+ gene of S.pombe was identified by screening the S.pombe database using the S.cerevisiae SRS2/HPR5 gene sequence as the query sequence with a BLAST search program. A haploid strain of S.pombe, sp30, was used to create Deltasrs2 by replacing srs2+ with the KanMX4 disruption cassette which codes for G418/kanamycin resistance (42). To investigate the potential role of Srs2 in DNA damage repair, we measured sensitivity to UV radiation and HU treatment in our S.pombe Deltasrs2 mutant. We conclude that the Srs2 protein plays no significant role in survival following UV irradiation (Fig. 1A and B) or treatment with HU (Fig. 1A and C).

We crossed Deltasrs2 with Deltarqh1 to create a Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 double mutant by random sporulation. Colonies arising from this cross were either normal sized or small. A large screen of over 100 colonies revealed that all normal sized colonies were either single mutants or wild type, while all small colonies were double mutant Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1. We initially suspected that the slow growth seen in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells might be due to a delay in S-phase based on the proposed role of both of these proteins functioning during DNA replication. To investigate this, we carried out FACS analysis on synchronized cells. Overnight cultures of wild type, Deltasrs2, Deltarqh1 and Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 were synchronized in G1 by growth in media lacking nitrogen. Cells were released from the block and samples collected for analysis at time intervals over several hours. In our initial studies, FACS profiles of the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 strain were difficult to interpret, presumably due to the abundance of abnormally large cells present in the population (data not shown). In subsequent experiments, following nitrogen starvation, large cells were eliminated by size selection using 7-30% lactose gradients. These size-selected cells were released from the block by switching to YEA media. FACS analysis of these cells gave much improved results. The results in Figure 3 show that Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells do exhibit a slightly elongated S-phase. The Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells exit the G1 block and progress through S-phase somewhat slower than wild-type cells or cells containing either single mutation. This is seen in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 as a greater number of cells in S-phase between 1 and 2 h after release from G1. This slight delay in S-phase would not seem to account for all of the slow growth seen in the double mutant.

Since Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells did not show a significant S-phase delay, we went on to determine if there was a more substantial delay in another part of the cell cycle. To address this, we used cells from the same synchronization experiments described above. Cells, fixed at various times after G1 release, were stained with DAPI and examined microscopically. At each time point, random fields of cells were scored as being either small/normal, elongated, having two nuclei or having torn or abnormal nuclei. On average about 250 cells were counted for each time point. Elongated cells were cells judged to be longer than a typical dividing cell. Figure 4 summarizes the results of wild-type and Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells. Pictures of DAPI stained cells representative of each classification of cells is shown at the top of each column. The percentage of each cell type seen at each time point are listed below each picture. At 0 h, essentially all wild-type and mutant cells were small, consistent with a good G1 arrest. By 2 h we still saw mainly small or normal sized cells in both strains. By 3.5 h post release we began to see cells from both wild-type and Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 that contained two nuclei, consistent with cells having completed mitosis. At 4 h, 28% of wild-type cells and 13% of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells were found to contain two nuclei. These results demonstrate that a sub-population of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells cycle normally. At 4 h many Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 were not cycling normally, as indicated by the presence of elongated cells with single nuclei (12% of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 compared with 3% of wild-type cells). This phenotype is indicative of cells delayed in G2/M, suggesting a checkpoint-dependent arrest. These elongated cells appear to accumulate, as their numbers increased to 25% by 5.5 h after release. Only a few percent of elongated cells were ever seen in wild-type cells.

In addition to the elongated cells, at the later time points (5-6 h), there was also an accumulation of cells with torn and abnormally shaped nuclei, indicative of cells that have undergone mitotic catastrophe. This morphology is reminiscent of the phenotype seen in Deltarqh1 cells following DNA damage or HU arrest. We also examined synchronized Deltasrs2 and Deltarqh1 single mutants through this same block and release time course. The Deltasrs2 cells were indistinguishable from wild-type cells, while a very small number (<5%) of Deltarqh1 cells did elongate with a single nucleus or had torn chromosomes (<2%, data not shown). From these results we conclude that the events that lead to cell growth arrest in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 are stochastic, as some cells fail to progress through the cell cycle while others proceed at what appears to be a normal rate of growth. Also, when cells do arrest it appears to be at G2/M although our FACS data do not rule out the possibility that the arrest is in very late S-phase.

One explanation for the G2/M arrest of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells is that their DNA is altered or damaged in such a way that a checkpoint response is elicited. To test this, we created a triple mutant strain in which the rad9+ checkpoint gene was deleted in a Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 background. Figure A shows that loss of the rad9+ gene suppresses the slow growth of the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 mutation. Furthermore, when these cells (Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 Deltarad9) were stained with DAPI and visualized by fluorescence microscopy, fewer elongated cells were detected (Fig. 5B). This result suggests that by deleting rad9+, the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells escape a checkpoint-dependent arrest, reducing their elongated cell cycle time and, therefore, partially suppressing their slow growth. We also reasoned that an apparent suppression of slow growth would occur if deletion of rad9+ improved cell survival in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells. We suggest this in light of our finding that the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 double mutant shows a high rate of spontaneous cell death, contributing to an appearance of slow growth. Therefore, we measured the plating efficiency of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 and compared it with that of the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 Deltarad9 triple mutant. The data shown in Table 3 reveal that the plating efficiency of the triple mutant was actually worse than that of the double mutant, 7.5% for Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 Deltarad9 versus 17.6% for Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1. Therefore, the increased growth rate in the triple mutant cannot be explained by improved cell viability. These data support the conclusion that loss of rad9+ in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 improves the growth rate because these cells can no longer induce a checkpoint-dependent arrest.

Thus, the phenotype associated with the simultaneous loss of both srs2+ and rqh1+ might be explained by increased rates of recombination. It follows that eliminating recombination genes might suppress the slow growth seen in this double mutant. To study this, we created two different recombination deficient strains, Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 Deltarhp51 and Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 Deltarhp57. One complicating factor with these studies is that in S.pombe recombination mutants themselves are slow growing, although not nearly as severe as Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1. Despite this, we were able to see clear suppression of the slow growth in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 when either recombination gene was deleted (Fig. 6A and B). We created the triple mutant by random spores and characterized over 100 colonies for each strain. Our Deltarhp51 and Deltarhp57 strains were disrupted with ura4+ while both Deltasrs2 and Deltarqh1 were disrupted with kanMX. This allowed us to rapidly screen our colonies. We found that every slow growing colony that we examined was Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 while faster growing colonies were always either Deltarhp51 or Deltarhp57. The figure shows that loss of rhp51+ partially suppresses the slow growth of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 double mutants, to a level similar to that of the Deltarhp51 single mutant (Fig. 6A). Deltarhp57 mutant was also found to suppress the slow growth of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 (Fig. 6B).

To test this we measured the plating efficiency of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 Deltarhp51 and compared it with the double mutant as well as the single mutants. In Table 3, we see that the triple mutant showed a plating efficiency of 40% as compared with Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1, which was only 17.4%. In light of the discrepancy between our data and that of Wang et al. (41) concerning suppression of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 slow growth by loss of recombination genes, we carried out one other experiment to eliminate the possibility that our suppression was the result of some spontaneous suppressor. To determine if a spontaneous suppressor did arise in our strains we crossed Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 to Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 Deltarhp51. The progeny were analyzed by random sporulation. Spores were found to be either large or small at about 50:50. A screen of over 100 colonies from the cross found that all of the large colonies that were tested contained Deltarhp51 (data not shown). This demonstrates that the suppression is due to the loss of recombination and not due to a spontaneous suppressor. From these results we show conclusively that the synthetic slow growth of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 is suppressed in a recombination deficient background. These results are consistent with findings reported in S.cerevisiae where it was demonstrated that the synthetic lethality or slow growth of srs2sgs1 is suppressed by mutations in genes coding for proteins of the recombination machinery (14,30,32).

Our FACS analysis of synchronized Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells indicates that these cells progress through S-phase slightly slower than wild type, which seems to argue against the slow growth being primarily a function of stalled replication. However, it is possible that the impairment seen in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells is related to an inability to properly respond to stalled replication forks, even though the major cell cycle arrest occurs outside of S-phase. If stalled forks are the problem, then increasing the number of stalled replication forks in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells by DNA damage with UV radiation or depleting the nucleotide pool with HU should have a profound negative effect on cell survival. In Figure 7A, it is seen that rather than being more sensitive to UV the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 double mutant was actually less sensitive to UV than that of the Deltarqh1 single mutant. Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 double mutants showed an equivalent sensitivity to 12 mM HU in liquid culture when compared with the Deltarqh1 single mutant (Fig. 7B). To consider the possibility that the lack of increased sensitivity to HU in the double mutant was a function of its slow growth, we examined the HU sensitivity of the double mutant with continuous HU exposure by plating cells directly onto HU-containing plates. Figure 7C shows plates containing 2.4 mM HU after 8 days of incubation. The Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 plate shows not only increased survival relative to the Deltarqh1 single mutant but also the presence of larger colonies. Together, these data support the conclusion that loss of srs2+ partially suppresses the HU and UV sensitivity of Deltarqh1. More importantly, this result argues against the idea that the synthetic phenotype seen in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 is solely dependent on stalled replication.

A summary of our findings on srs2+ is as follows: (i) Deltasrs2 single mutants grow normally and have a normal morphology; (ii) Deltasrs2 mutants are not sensitive to UV irradiation and treatment with HU; (iii) simultaneous loss of srs2+ and rqh1+ results in a synthetic phenotype in which the cells show extreme slow growth, aberrant cellular and nuclear morphologies and high spontaneous cell death; (iv) a large portion of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells undergo a checkpoint-dependent cell cycle arrest, most likely at G2/M, which is a stochastic event; (v) Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 double mutants show high levels of spontaneous mitotic catastrophe; (vi) the slow growth and poor viability of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 are partially suppressed by loss of the rhp51+ and rhp57+ homologous recombination genes; (vii) loss of srs2+ suppresses the UV and HU sensitivity of Deltarqh1 mutants; and (viii) Deltasrs2 is synthetic lethal with Deltarhp54, and this lethality is suppressed by loss of rhp51+. Deletion of the S.pombesrs2+ gene shares many properties in common with deletion of SRS2 in S.cerevisiae although the severity of phenotypes sometimes differs. For example, S.cerevisiae srs2 mutants are more sensitive to UV damage and its synthetic interaction with sgs1 appears more severe than that of Deltasrs2 and Deltarqh1. In addition we have gone on to show that loss of srs2+ partially suppresses the UV and HU sensitivity of Deltarqh1. We also find that Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells do not show a significant arrest in S-phase but rather arrest at what appears to be G2/M. The spontaneous cell death associated with Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 appears to be due to mitotic catastrophe.

###xml 0 25 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
Schizosaccharomyces pombe Deltasrs2 has a synthetic interaction with Deltarqh1, manifested as extreme slow growth, poor plating efficiency, high rates of spontaneous cell death and abnormal cellular and nuclear morphologies. One interpretation of a synthetic interaction is that the two genes function in parallel pathways in an essential or very important process. The role of Rqh1 and its homologs is not known, but most evidence points to it playing some role in suppressing recombination arising by stalled replication. The synthetic phenotype of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 would suggest that Srs2 plays a similar or alternative role at stalled forks. However, our data do not support this interpretation. First, if the phenotype of the double mutant results from a major defect in the cells' ability to cope with spontaneously arising stalled replication forks, then predictably these cells should arrest or slow in S-phase. Our FACS data from synchronized cells show that Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 show only a slight delay in S-phase (Fig. 3). Second, if the phenotype of the double mutant results from a defect in its ability to cope with replication fork stalling, then these cells should be extremely sensitive to any increase in the number of stalled forks. Again we find this not to be the case, as the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 double mutant actually shows increased survival when exposed to UV or treated with HU when compared with Deltarqh1 (Fig. 7A, B and C). These results argue against the phenotype of the double mutant resulting from stalled replication.

If Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells are not arresting in S-phase at a significant level, where do they arrest? In an effort to determine this, Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1, wild-type and single mutant cells were DAPI stained and examined microscopically at times after release from G1 synchronization. Cells from each time point were scored as being either small or normal in size, elongated (assumed to be in G2 arrest), bi-nucleated (cells having completed mitosis) or abnormal (cells with torn or fragmented nuclei). We draw two conclusions from these studies. Our first conclusion, based on the data in Figure 4, is that some double mutant cells appear to cycle normally. Initially both wild-type and Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells are small with single nuclei. At 3.5 h, which is the earliest time that double nucleated wild-type cells are seen, an equal number of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 are double nucleated. This shows that cells have progressed through mitosis. At later time points we see continued evidence of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells cycling normally, as double nucleated cells continue to be present. Our second conclusion is that many Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells undergo an arrest, most likely at G2/M. This conclusion is based on our observation that large numbers of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells accumulate as single nucleated (pre-mitotic) elongated cells. In combination with our FACS data showing no major accumulation of cells in S-phase, these elongated cells with a single nucleus must be in G2, unable to enter M. We cannot rule out the possibility that these cells are in very late S-phase. By 6 h there are essentially no elongated cells found in wild-type, yet 25% of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells are elongated. In addition to elongated cells, at 6 h 7% of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells have aberrant or torn nuclei. This occurs in elongated cells, suggesting that some arrested cells ultimately undergo mitotic catastrophe. While we did not look at time points beyond 6 h, we assume that the number of cells showing mitotic catastrophe will continue to accumulate with time. We speculate that most, if not all, elongated cells will fail to progress through mitosis properly. This cannot be determined by these experiments, as elongated cells will continue to accumulate. However, if this assumption is correct, then the 25% of elongated cells together with the 7% of cells having already undergone mitotic catastrophe represent one-third of the cell population dying during each round of the cell cycle. This number is in good agreement with a doubling time of approximately6 h for Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1. A recent study in S.cerevisiae demonstrated that 15% of srs2 sgs1 double mutants arrest in mitosis as large budded cells and that this arrest was checkpoint dependent (20). Cells arrested in mitosis were also found in 7% of srs2 and 2% of sgs1 single mutants, demonstrating that this is not strictly a phenotype of the double mutant. Interestingly, when we examined Deltasrs2 in a similar experiment, there was no evidence of G2/M arrested cells or cells undergoing a mitotic catastrophe. We saw evidence for a few elongated Deltarqh1 cells suggesting a mild arrest. These data demonstrate that the apparent slow growth of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells is not due to a homogeneous slowing of the entire cell population, but that this is a mixed population of normal cycling and arrested cells. This indicates that the cellular events leading to cell cycle arrest are stochastic.

The G2/M arrest seen in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells is likely due to the accumulation of some type of DNA damage or structure that induces a checkpoint response. This is further corroborated by our finding that deletion of the rad9+ checkpoint gene suppresses the accumulation of the elongated, G2/M arrested cells in the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 mutant (Fig. 5B). While we cannot be certain what DNA structure is recognized as damage, it is tempting to speculate that it is recombination intermediates. Previous studies demonstrated that Deltarqh1 mutants have no increase or only slightly elevated recombination rates (8,18). However, loss of Srs2 is associated with a 12-fold increase in spontaneous recombination rates (41,45). The elevated recombination rates in Deltasrs2 do not appear to have any obvious deleterious effect on cells, as they grow normally and have normal morphologies. We wondered if the severe phenotype seen in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 was due to the inability of these cells to deal effectively with recombination. This would appear to be the case as we found that deletion of either rhp51+ or rhp57+ markedly suppresses the slow growth and low viability of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells. Loss of recombination also returns Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells to a more normal cellular phenotype. This normalization suggests that recombination is at least a partial cause of the aberrant phenotype seen in the double mutant. One interpretation of these results is that potential problems caused by hyper-recombination in Deltasrs2 cells are suppressed by Rqh1 function, and so the problems are only seen upon simultaneous loss of both genes.

Our findings of slow growth suppression in Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 cells by loss of recombination are in contrast to the results of Wang et al. (41). They reported that Deltarhp51 failed to suppress the slow growth of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 and, in fact, the triple mutant grew worse. We found clear suppression by not only loss of rhp51+, but also by loss of rhp57+. Additionally, they reported that their Deltasrs2 Deltarhp51 double mutant looked like a Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 mutant and were more sensitive to UV damage than the Deltarhp51 single mutant. Our experiments showed neither a synthetic interaction between Deltasrs2 and Deltarhp51 nor increased UV sensitivity in the double mutant compared with the Deltarhp51 single mutant (data not shown). Other results with Deltasrs2 were the same between our laboratories. This suggests that the discrepancies may arise from the use of different Deltarhp51 mutants which could contain uncharacterized strain differences. We used the Deltarhp51 reported previously (46), while Wang et al. used a Deltarhp51 created for their studies. However, we are confident in our conclusion that loss of recombination suppresses the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 phenotype based on the fact that we observed suppression with two different recombination mutants and that comparable results have been established for the budding yeast S.cerevisiae. We also addressed the possibility that our suppression was due to a spontaneous suppressor. Our results argue against the presence of a spontaneous suppressor of the Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1 phenotype.

###xml 1056 1058 1032 1034 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf581c41">41</xref>
###xml 1112 1116 1088 1092 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>
###xml 1280 1284 1252 1256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>
###xml 577 589 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 871 878 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 952 964 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 1006 1013 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
While searching for recombination mutants that would suppress the slow growth of Deltasrs2 Deltarqh1, we found synthetic lethality between Deltasrs2 and Deltarhp54. This lethality was suppressed by deletion of rhp51+. Two possible explanations could account for this result. One is that Srs2 functions in recombination in a process in parallel with Rhp54 and that one of these two proteins is necessary to complete recombination initiated by Rhp51. This would explain why deletion of rhp51+ suppresses this synthetic lethality. An alternative explanation comes from studies in S.cerevisiae where Srs2 is known to function in the PRR pathway (24,29,47). Loss of srs2 in a rad6 mutant suppresses the UV sensitivity of these strains presumably by allowing repair of DNA damage to occur by homologous recombination. While we do not know if Srs2 plays the same role in PRR in S.pombe, the same synthetic lethality between Deltasrs2 and Deltarad54 exists in S.cerevisiae (31). We also know that loss of srs2+ in S.pombe leads to a hyper-recombination phenotype (41). From this, we can speculate that in the absence of srs2, damaged or incompletely replicated DNA is funneled into the Rhp51-dependent homologous recombination pathway. Presumably the process initiated by Rhp51 in a Deltasrs2 background requires Rhp54 to be completed, and the initiation of this process in the absence of Rhp54 leads to lethality. In this scenario, Srs2 plays an indirect role in recombination.
###end p 59

###xml 17 21 17 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>
###xml 21 22 21 22 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 32 36 32 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
Why does loss of srs2+ suppress rqh1 sensitivity to UV and HU?
###end title 60
###begin p 61
###xml 43 47 43 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>
###xml 47 48 47 48 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 59 63 55 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 314 318 306 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">srs2</italic>
###xml 328 332 316 320 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 673 674 661 662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf581c8">8</xref>
###xml 675 677 663 665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf581c18">18</xref>
###xml 921 925 905 909 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 1008 1013 992 997 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rhp51</italic>
###xml 1013 1014 997 998 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1018 1023 1002 1007 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rhp57</italic>
###xml 1023 1024 1007 1008 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1101 1105 1081 1085 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 1132 1136 1112 1116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SRS2</italic>
###xml 1140 1152 1120 1132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 1177 1182 1157 1162 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RAD51</italic>
###xml 1225 1227 1205 1207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf581c39">39</xref>
###xml 1312 1316 1288 1292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 1412 1416 1384 1388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rqh1</italic>
###xml 1140 1152 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
It is difficult to explain how deletion of srs2+ in a Deltarqh1 background can have a negative effect on cell growth and viability, yet have a protective effect on the ability of these cells to cope with UV and HU. We have already given a reasonable possible explanation for the synthetic interaction between Deltasrs2 and Deltarqh1 as it relates to normal growth. It is not necessary to link these two phenotypes since one happens in damaged or replication arrested cells and the other is a spontaneous response. One possibility is as follows: upon replication arrest, caused by DNA damage or HU treatment, recombination takes place at a high rate in the absence of Rqh1 (8,18). It is the process of recombination that leads to the decrease in viability seen in these cells. We base this latter statement on the finding that expression of a recombination resolvase partially suppresses the HU and UV sensitivity of Deltarqh1 cells. In addition, unpublished data from our laboratory show that loss of either rhp51+ or rhp57+ completely or nearly completely suppresses the HU or UV sensitivity of Deltarqh1. One proposed function of SRS2 in S.cerevisiae is to act downstream of RAD51 to stabilize recombination intermediates (39). By enhancing recombination, Srs2 might increase the HU and UV sensitivity of Deltarqh1 cells. This proposed function might explain why the loss of Srs2 improves the survival of Deltarqh1 cells when challenged with UV or HU. Obviously, further investigation will be necessary to answer this question with certainty.
