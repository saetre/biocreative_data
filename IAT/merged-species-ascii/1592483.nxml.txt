###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 385 398 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
DNA structure checkpoints are conserved eukaryotic signal transduction pathways that help preserve genomic integrity. Upon detecting checkpoint signals such as stalled replication forks or double-stranded DNA breaks, these pathways coordinate appropriate stress responses. Members of the PI-3 kinase related kinase (PIKK) family are essential elements of DNA structure checkpoints. In fission yeast, the Rad3 PIKK and its regulatory subunit Rad26 coordinate the detection of checkpoint signals with pathway outputs.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 24 35 24 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
We found that untreated rad26Delta cells were defective for two microtubule-dependent processes: chromosome segregation and morphogenesis. Interestingly, cytoplasmic accumulation of Rad26-GFP occurred following treatment with microtubule destabilizing drugs, but not during treatment with the genotoxic agent Phleomycin. Cytoplasmic accumulation of Rad26-GFP depended on Rad24, a 14-3-3 protein also required for DNA structure checkpoints and morphogenesis. Results of over expression and epistasis experiments confirm that Rad26 and Rad24 define a response to microtubule destabilizing conditions.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Two DNA structure checkpoint proteins with roles in morphogenesis define a response to microtubule destabilizing conditions.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 416 417 416 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 418 419 418 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
The fidelity of cell division and development require genomic stability. Conserved signal transduction pathways called DNA structure dependent checkpoints help ensure genomic stability by detecting unreplicated or damaged DNA. Once detected, the pathways initiate responses that coordinate cell cycle progression with DNA repair processes, maintain telomere structure, induce cellular senescence or cause apoptosis [1,2].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 145 146 145 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
Members of the PI-3 kinase related kinase (PIKK) family are central to DNA structure dependent checkpoints and other stress-responsive pathways [3]. PIKKs are large (>200 kD) proteins that harbor protein kinase activity in a conserved C-terminal catalytic domain that resembles the lipid kinase domain of PI-3 kinases. N-terminal to this kinase domain are protein-interaction and intramolecular folding domains. Following detection of a stress signal, changes in PIKK-protein interactions, folding and subcellular localization allow PIKKs to target downstream effector proteins and coordinate stress responses.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 88 89 88 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 197 198 197 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 199 200 199 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 327 336 327 336 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DDC2/PIE1</sup>
###xml 405 406 405 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 407 409 407 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 3 16 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 283 289 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 311 316 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 339 346 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus</span>
In fission yeast, a PIKK called Rad3 is central to DNA structure dependent checkpoints [4]. Rad3 physically binds to Rad26, a regulatory subunit required for normal levels of Rad3-kinase activity [5,6]. This Rad3/26 checkpoint complex is conserved throughout evolution and exists in humans (ATR/ATRIP), budding yeast (MECl/LCDlDDC2/PIE1), Xenopus (xATR/xATRIP) and possibly filamentous fungi (UvsB/UvsD) [7-12].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 135 137 135 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 226 228 226 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 229 231 229 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 616 618 616 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 619 621 619 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 622 624 622 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 905 907 905 907 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 908 910 908 910 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 1101 1103 1101 1103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 1104 1106 1104 1106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 428 433 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 443 448 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
These Rad3/26 complexes are sensors that detect and respond to DNA structure checkpoint signals such as double-stranded breaks (DSBs) [13]. Other conserved sensor complexes include the 9-1-1 (Rad9-Radl-Husl) complex and Crb2 [14-20]. The 9-1-1 complex appears to form a PCNA-like clamp that requires Radl7, a dynamic subunit of Replication Factor C, for loading onto DNA. Crb2 contains tandem BRCT-domains and resembles budding yeast Rad9 and human p53BPl. Following DNA damage, these three sensors relocalize independently of each other, suggesting that they detect aberrant DNA structures using parallel pathways [14,21-23]. Exactly how the 9-1-1 and Rad3/26-like complexes initially detect damage is not well understood. They may recognize many different signals, including single-stranded DNA overhangs bound by single-stranded binding protein, and DNA damaged-induced changes in chromatin structure [24,25]. Recent data suggest that the checkpoint signal for Crb2 localization is formed when DSBs alter the structure of nearby histones, and results obtained with p53BPl corroborate this finding [15,26]. Following the production of checkpoint signals and their detection, the events leading to Rad3/26 kinase activation and downstream signal transduction require all three sensor complexes.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 166 168 166 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 321 323 321 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 324 326 324 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 456 458 456 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 459 461 459 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 572 574 572 574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 675 677 675 677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 975 977 975 977 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 388 401 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
Depending on the checkpoint signal, the checkpoint-activated Rad3/26 kinase phosphorylates effector kinases Chkl or Cdsl, which in turn phosphorylate Mikl and Cdc25 [27]. This leads to increased levels of Mikl, a negative Cdc2 regulator, and possibly reduces the phosphatase activity of Cdc25, a positive Cdc2 regulator [28-32]. Checkpoint regulation of Cdc25 may also be mediated by the fission yeast 14-3-3 proteins Rad24 and, to a lesser extent, Rad25 [32,33]. These interactions compartmentalize Cdc25 in the cytoplasm, although the outcome of this is not understood [30]. Recently, it was shown that Rad24 promotes checkpoint-dependent retention of Chkl in the nucleus [34]. Therefore, 14-3-3 proteins may mediate the checkpoint response by affecting the localization of signaling proteins and checkpoint-targets. Interestingly, Rad24 is also required for proper cell morphogenesis, suggesting that this 14-3-3 protein is a component of pathways controlling cell shape [35].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 39 44 39 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 44 46 44 46 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 124 126 124 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 157 167 157 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916;</italic>
###xml 169 178 165 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad3&#916;</italic>
###xml 180 190 172 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad1&#916; </italic>
###xml 194 204 182 188 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad9&#916; </italic>
###xml 239 249 223 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hus1&#916; </italic>
###xml 253 264 233 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad17&#916; </italic>
We have been investigating why loss of rad26+ sensitizes cells to the microtubule depolymerizing agent thiabendazole (TBZ) [23]. Specifically, we found that rad26Delta, rad3Delta, rad1Delta and rad9Delta cells were sensitive to TBZ, while hus1Delta and rad17Delta cells shared wild type TBZ-sensitivity. Therefore, TBZ sensitivity does not result from a defective DNA structure checkpoint.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 413 415 413 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 416 418 416 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 301 306 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 656 669 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
The Mad2-dependent spindle assembly checkpoint restrains metaphase-to-anaphase progression when microtubules are compromised [36]. Experiments have shown that overlap between the spindle assembly and DNA structure checkpoints exist. For example, the spindle assembly checkpoint of fission and budding yeast delays mitotic progression when DNA structure checkpoint mutants are treated with replication inhibitors [37-39]. Thus, the two checkpoint systems cooperate to enhance survival following genotoxic stress. Elements of these pathways may also cooperate to promote mitotic arrest following microtubule stress, which would explain why mutations in some fission yeast DNA structure checkpoint genes cause TBZ sensitivity.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 70 81 70 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 107 112 103 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 112 114 108 110 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 291 302 287 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 486 497 478 485 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 523 528 511 516 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 528 530 516 518 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
Here, we initiated experiments to characterize the TBZ-sensitivity of rad26Delta cells. Our data show that rad26+ is required for the efficiency of two microtubule-dependent processes, chromosome segregation and cell polarity, and we suspect that defects in both processes may contribute to rad26Delta TBZ-sensitivity. Our data strongly suggest that Rad26 operates independently of the spindle assembly checkpoint to preserve both processes. With regard to the cell polarity defects of rad26Delta cells, our data show that rad26+ is required for proper growth patterns and the polar distribution of actin patches.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
We also observed that microtubule-destabilizing conditions caused Rad26-GFP to accumulate in the cytoplasm by a Rad24-dependent manner. Possible outcomes of this response are discussed.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Results
###end title 18
###begin title 19
###xml 4 15 4 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
Are rad26Delta cells specifically sensitive to TBZ or generally sensitive to microtubule-destabilizing conditions?
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 8 13 8 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 13 15 13 15 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 39 41 39 41 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 64 75 64 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 181 183 176 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 193 195 188 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 219 230 214 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 262 275 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
Loss of rad26+ caused TBZ sensitivity [23]. Here, we found that rad26Delta cell growth was also inhibited by 8 mug/ml Carbendazim (MBC), another microtubule-destabilizing compound [45] (Figure 1A). We conclude that the rad26Delta allele sensitizes the growth of fission yeast to different treatments that destabilize microtubules.
###end p 20

###begin title 22
TBZ does not produce DNA structure checkpoint signals
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 172 183 172 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 337 339 333 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 340 342 336 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 343 345 339 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 602 604 598 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 712 723 708 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 783 794 775 782 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad17&#916; </italic>
###xml 798 808 786 792 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hus1&#916; </italic>
###xml 838 840 822 824 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
By disrupting the mitotic spindle and interfering with chromosome metabolism, microtubule-destabilizing agents could conceivably affect the integrity of DNA and compromise rad26Delta cell growth. Rad22 is a homologous recombination protein that localizes to discrete foci when genotoxins cause double strand breaks or stall replication [22,46,47]. If microtubule-destabilizing conditions produce these effects, then Rad22-GFP foci will form following TBZ treatment. We found that Rad22-GFP foci formed following treatment with the DNA damaging agent Phleomycin, but not following TBZ treatment (Figure 1B). Therefore, double strand breaks and stalled replication forks are not responsible for the sensitivity of rad26Delta cells to TBZ, consistent with the previous observation that rad17Delta and hus1Delta cells were not TBZ-sensitive [23].
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Rad26-GFP accumulates in the cytoplasm during MBC treatment
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 246 248 246 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1C</xref>
###xml 268 273 268 273 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 274 278 274 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gfp </italic>
###xml 455 457 455 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 914 915 914 915 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
The data above suggest that Rad26 may participate in a cellular response to microtubule destabilizing conditions. To investigate this idea, we tested if Rad26-GFP localization changed during treatment with microtubule-destabilizing drugs (Figure 1C). Importantly, our rad26-gfp strain retained normal sensitivity to TBZ and MBC (data not shown). In untreated cells, dots of Rad26-GFP were observed in the nuclear region, consistent with previous results [23]. We also noticed that these cells contained a fluorescent cytoplasmic signal that was absent in the untagged control strain. At the present time, we cannot say for certain if this signal represents Rad26-GFP as opposed to background noise. Following 3 hours of MBC treatment, Rad26-GFP accumulated in the cytoplasm; earlier time-points revealed that cytoplasmic accumulation of Rad26-GFP could be detected within 20 minutes of MBC addition (below, Figure 8). TBZ-treatment also caused this redistribution of Rad26-GFP (data not shown). We did not detect redistribution of Rad26-GFP to the cytoplasm following treatment with Phleomycin. These data demonstrate that Rad26 localization changes in response to drugs that disrupt microtubules.
###end p 25
###begin title 26
###xml 35 46 35 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
The spindle assembly checkpoint of rad26Delta cells appears to operate normally during TBZ treatment
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 82 84 82 84 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 286 297 286 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
The spindle assembly checkpoint prevents mitosis when the spindle is compromised [48]. Defects in this pathway lead to (1) undelayed progression through mitosis, (2) premature sister chromatid separation and (3) chromosome loss during microtubule destabilizing conditions. We tested if rad26Delta cells displayed these phenotypes during TBZ treatment to investigate if Rad26 is a component of the spindle assembly checkpoint.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 20 25 20 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 25 27 25 27 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 112 121 112 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc25.22 </italic>
###xml 240 249 240 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc25.22 </italic>
###xml 285 287 285 287 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 288 290 288 290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 354 365 354 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 401 406 397 402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 406 408 402 404 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 422 424 418 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 449 460 445 452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 507 512 499 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 512 514 504 506 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 542 553 534 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 557 562 545 550 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 562 564 550 552 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 635 640 623 628 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 640 642 628 630 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
First, we tested if rad26+ was required to delay septation during TBZ treatment. The temperature sensitive (ts) cdc25.22 allele was used to synchronize cells in G2, and it is known that the spindle assembly checkpoint delays septation when cdc25.22 cells are released into TBZ-medium [49,50]. Following release from the G2-block, we found that untreated rad26Delta cells septated slightly faster than rad26+ cells (Figure 2A). During TBZ treatment, rad26Delta cells once again septated slightly faster than rad26+ cells. However, TBZ-treated rad26Delta and rad26+ cells delayed the onset of septation with similar kinetics. Therefore, rad26+ is not required to delay septation during TBZ treatment.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 35 46 35 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 0 70 0 66 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">The spindle assembly checkpoint of <italic>rad26&#916; </italic>cells appears functional</bold>
###xml 75 86 71 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 150 164 142 156 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc25.22 rad26</italic>
###xml 164 165 156 157 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 179 198 171 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc25.22 rad26&#916;</italic>
###xml 523 534 498 505 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 585 594 556 561 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad3&#916;</italic>
###xml 628 638 595 601 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916;</italic>
###xml 764 775 727 734 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 1130 1141 1077 1084 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 1188 1193 1131 1136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 1193 1195 1136 1138 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>

Second, we tested if rad26+ prevents chromosome loss during TBZ treatment (Figure 2B). Cells containing an adenine-marked minichromosome were cultured in rich liquid medium for 40 hours [51]. Cultures were then split in half; one half was left untreated for 8 hours, and the other half was treated with 20 mug/ml TBZ for 8 hours. We observed that 0% of rad26+, 5% of rad26Delta and 2% of rad3Delta cells experienced chromosome loss during the unperturbed growth period. The 5% difference between rad26Delta and rad26+ cells was statistically significant (p < 0.05; chi-squared), demonstrating that loss of rad26+ causes chromosome loss during normal cell growth. Following TBZ treatment, 29% of rad26+, 34% of rad26Delta and 21% of rad3Delta cells lost the minichromosome. As the difference between rad26+ and rad26Delta cells was still 5%, loss of rad26+ did not exacerbate chromosome loss during TBZ treatment. This result suggests that rad26+ is not required to prevent chromosome loss under microtubule-destabilizing conditions.

Third, we tested if rad26+ was required to prevent sister chromatid separation during TBZ treatment. We followed chromatid separation using a strain marked with a GFP-labeled chromosome 1 (Cenl-GFP) [52]. One GFP focus is visible during interphase and early mitosis when the sister chromatids are too close together to resolve individual Cenl-GFP signals using conventional fluorescence microscopy. Two foci become visible when sister chromatid separation occurs. Cenl-GFP cells were synchronized in G2 using the cdc25.22 allele before release into mitosis. We observed that sister chromatid separation was accelerated in untreated rad26Delta cells relative to untreated rad26+ cells (Figure 2C). Taking this result into consideration, both rad26Delta and rad26+ cells delayed sister chromatid separation with similar kinetics following release into media containing TBZ (Figure 2C). Therefore, rad26+ is not required to delay chromosome separation during TBZ treatment.

In addition to their critical role during chromosome segregation, microtubules are also important for generating and maintaining cellular morphology [53]. Fission yeast are cylindrically shaped cells that grow bipolarly from each end, and cytoplasmic microtubules mediate the transport of growth axis determinants to these ends. TBZ may affect the growth of rad26Delta cells if rad26+ is involved in the establishment or maintenance of morphology.

We tested if rad26Delta-dependent morphology defects would exacerbate those of a morphology mutant. Kin1 is a conserved serine-threonine kinase that localizes to new cell ends and is required for the proper distribution of actin patches and overall cell symmetry; its loss results in abnormally shaped cells [54-56]. Under normal growth conditions, we found that 19.1 + 4.3% of kin1Delta cells were round and had thus completely lost polarity, while 45 +/- 6.1% of rad26Delta kin1Delta cells were round (Figures 4E,F). Again, the rad26Delta allele caused a nearly 2-fold difference in morphological characteristics (p < 0.05). Therefore, loss of rad26+ exacerbates the polarity defects of kin1Delta cells.

If rad26+ influences cell polarity, then over expression of rad26+ may disrupt it. We used the nmt thiamine-repressible promoter to drive expression of exogenous rad26+ cDNA [41,57]. While rad26+ cells with empty vector maintained wild type morphology, 22% of cells over expressing rad26+ lost polarity and became abnormally shaped and spherical (Figure 4G,H). To test if rad26+ is required for the structure or arrangement of microtubules, we examined microtubule architecture in untreated and TBZ-treated rad26Delta cells. Actin is also required for fission yeast polarity [58]. Actin cables are typically oriented along the fission yeast growth axis and patches typically localize to sites of polarized growth at cell ends [59,60]. This bipolar localization of actin patches depends on microtubules and the growth axis determinants that they deliver to cell ends [61]. We used FITC-conjugated phalloidin to test if actin architecture was affected by loss of rad26+ (Figure 5B-D). In untreated rad26+ cells, 15% of rad26+ cells contained actin patches that were delocalized from the cell ends. Following TBZ treatment, 24% of rad26+ cells contained delocalized actin patches. In untreated rad26Delta cells, 38% of cells contained delocalized patches. TBZ treatment increased the percentage of cells with delocalized patches to 53%. Because we did not detect a difference between the number of patches in untreated and TBZ-treated rad26+ and rad26Delta cells (data not shown), we conclude that loss of rad26+ affects the establishment or maintenance of actin patches at polar growth sites.

###begin title 56

###xml 15 26 11 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
rad24Delta and rad26Delta alleles confer TBZ sensitivity by a mechanism independent of the cytokinesis checkpoint
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 151 153 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>
###xml 154 156 154 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B65">65</xref>
###xml 324 335 324 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24&#916; </italic>
###xml 488 493 484 489 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 493 495 489 491 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 572 583 568 575 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 587 598 579 586 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24&#916; </italic>
Rad24 is a component of the cytokinesis checkpoint that delays entry into the next mitotic cycle when the actinmyosin cytokinetic ring is compromised [64,65]. For example, when construction of the ring is jeopardized by Latrunculin A (LatA) treatment, wild type cells delay cell cycle progression as binucleate cells, while rad24Delta cells pass through the next round of mitosis and become multinucleate. Perhaps TBZ affects the structure or function of the actomyosin ring, and perhaps rad26+ is a component of this cytokinesis checkpoint. If so, that would explain why rad26Delta and rad24Delta cells are TBZ-sensitive.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 11 16 11 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 16 18 16 18 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 97 108 97 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24&#916; </italic>
###xml 135 146 131 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 169 171 161 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7B</xref>
###xml 193 204 185 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 208 219 196 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24&#916; </italic>
###xml 310 312 294 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7C</xref>
###xml 327 338 311 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24&#916; </italic>
###xml 386 397 366 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 429 434 405 410 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 434 436 410 412 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 518 523 494 499 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 523 525 499 501 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 529 534 505 510 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24</italic>
###xml 534 536 510 512 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
To test if rad26+ is a component of the cytokinesis checkpoint, cells were plated to LatA. While rad24Delta cells were LatA sensitive, rad26Delta cells were not (Figure 7B). Next, we tested if rad26Delta and rad24Delta cells became multinucleate (3 or more nuclei) following LatA treatment. As shown in Figure 7C, LatA treated rad24Delta cells became multinucleate after 5 hours, while rad26Delta cells did not. We conclude that rad26+ is not a component of the cytokinesis checkpoint. These data suggest that loss of rad26+ and rad24+ cause sensitivity to mictrotuble-destabilizers by a mechanism independent of the cytokinesis checkpoint.
###end p 58
###begin title 59
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24</italic>
###xml 5 7 5 7 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
rad24+ is required for cytoplasmic accumulation of Rad26-GFP during MBC treatment
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 97 99 97 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B66">66</xref>
###xml 115 120 115 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24</italic>
###xml 120 122 120 122 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 129 134 129 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad25</italic>
###xml 134 136 134 136 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 231 232 231 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
###xml 439 450 439 446 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad25&#916; </italic>
###xml 562 573 558 565 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24&#916; </italic>
###xml 716 727 708 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24&#916; </italic>
###xml 936 941 924 929 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24</italic>
###xml 941 943 929 931 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 973 978 961 966 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad25</italic>
###xml 978 979 966 967 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
14-3-3 proteins can affect signaling pathways by altering the cellular localization of proteins [66]. We tested if rad24+ and/or rad25+ were required for efficient cytoplasmic accumulation of Rad26-GFP during MBC treatment (Figure 8). A small percentage (3.7%) of untreated control cells contained cytoplasmic Rad26-GFP signal, while 97.6% of MBC-treated cells accumulated cytoplasmic Rad26-GFP signal within 20 minutes of treatment. In a rad25Delta background, 9.1% of untreated cells and 86.1% of MBC-treated cells contained cytoplasmic Rad26-GFP signal. In a rad24Delta background, 1.7% of untreated and 12.7% of MBC-treated cells contained cytoplasmic Rad26-GFP signal. Importantly, viability assays showed that rad24Delta cells retained greater than 95% viability following 1 hour of MBC or TBZ treatment (data not shown). Therefore, we conclude that efficient cytoplasmic accumulation of Rad26-GFP during MBC treatment depends on rad24+ and, to a much lesser extent, rad25+.
###end p 60
###begin title 61
Discussion
###end title 61
###begin title 62
Rad26 and Rad24 participate in a signaling pathway that responds to microtubule destabilizing conditions
###end title 62
###begin p 63
###xml 148 153 148 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 153 155 153 155 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 158 163 158 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24</italic>
###xml 163 165 163 165 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 247 252 247 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24</italic>
###xml 252 254 252 254 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 285 295 285 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916;</italic>
###xml 305 315 301 307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mad2&#916; </italic>
###xml 318 322 310 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nda2</italic>
###xml 323 327 315 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">KM52</italic>
###xml 347 358 339 346 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24&#916; </italic>
###xml 380 402 368 382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; rad24&#916; </italic>
###xml 460 465 440 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24</italic>
###xml 465 467 445 447 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
The evidence presented demonstrates that Rad26 and Rad24 operate in a pathway that responds to microtubule destabilizing conditions. First, loss of rad26+ or rad24+ caused sensitivity to microtubule destabilizing drugs. Second, over expression of rad24+ rescued the TBZ-sensitivity of rad26Delta, but not mad2Delta or nda2-KM52, cells. Third, the rad24Delta single mutant and the rad26Delta rad24Delta double mutant shared similar TBZ-sensitivity. And fourth, rad24+ was required for efficient cytoplasmic accumulation of Rad26-GFP that occurred following MBC treatment.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 8 13 8 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 13 15 13 15 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 19 24 19 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24</italic>
###xml 24 26 24 26 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 259 270 259 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 274 279 270 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 279 281 275 277 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 445 450 441 446 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24</italic>
###xml 450 452 446 448 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 492 502 488 494 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mad2&#916; </italic>
Is this rad26+ and rad24+ dependent pathway responding to spindle damage, morphological defects, or problems in other microtubule-dependent structures and/or processes? Our data suggest that this pathway does not respond to spindle damage because TBZ-treated rad26Delta and rad26+ cells delayed septation and chromosome separation with similar kinetics and experienced similar frequencies of minichromosome loss. Furthermore, over expression of rad24+ failed to rescue the TBZ-sensitivity of mad2Delta cells. To date, however, we have only detected Rad26 in the nucleus of untreated cells, consistent with a role for Rad26 in surveying nuclear defects associated with microtubule stress. Therefore, our data do not rule out the possibility that this pathway responds to spindle damage.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 146 157 146 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 243 254 239 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 295 305 287 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kin1&#916; </italic>
###xml 424 435 412 419 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 471 482 455 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24&#916; </italic>
###xml 559 564 539 544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 564 566 544 546 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 570 575 550 555 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24</italic>
###xml 575 577 555 557 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
Another possibility is that this pathway responds to loss of microtubule-dependent polarity structures. In this regard, TBZ treatment exacerbated rad26Delta defects associated with bipolar growth and the distribution of actin patches. Loss of rad26Delta also intensified the polarity defects of kin1Delta cells, again suggesting that Rad26 is required for polarity maintenance. Whereas the polarity defects ascribed here to rad26Delta cells are somewhat subtle, those of rad24Delta cells are obvious, as the cells have a more spherical appearance. Therefore, rad26+ and rad24+ may define a pathway that responds to defects in microtubule-dependent polarity structures. Part of the pathway's response may occur in the cytoplasm where Rad26-GFP accumulates. A clearer picture of this pathway will develop when we can define the purpose that cytoplasmic accumulation of Rad26 serves.
###end p 65
###begin title 66
###xml 46 51 46 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 51 53 51 53 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
Do errors in DNA metabolism caused by loss of rad26+ lead to morphological defects?
###end title 66
###begin p 67
###xml 24 35 24 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 94 149 90 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916;, rad3&#916;, rad1&#916;, rad9&#916;, hus1&#916; </italic>
###xml 153 164 129 136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad17&#916; </italic>
###xml 285 287 257 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 293 306 265 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 321 325 293 297 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MEC1</italic>
###xml 325 330 297 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad3+</italic>
###xml 325 330 297 302 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>rad3+</italic></sup>
###xml 332 336 304 308 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DDC2</italic>
###xml 336 343 308 315 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26+ </italic>
###xml 336 343 308 315 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>rad26+ </italic></sup>
###xml 347 351 319 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MEC3</italic>
###xml 351 357 323 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hus1+ </italic>
###xml 351 357 323 329 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>hus1+ </italic></sup>
###xml 448 453 420 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HUS1 </italic>
###xml 500 502 472 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B67">67</xref>
###xml 503 505 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B68">68</xref>
###xml 293 306 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 442 447 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We have shown here that rad26Delta cells lose a minichromosome at an elevated rate. Untreated rad26Delta, rad3Delta, rad1Delta, rad9Delta, hus1Delta and rad17Delta cells also have an increased number of Rad22 foci, suggesting that they accumulate spontaneous errors in DNA metabolism [22]. In S. cerevisiae, mutations in MEC1rad3+, DDC2rad26+ and MEC3hus1+ cause upto 200-fold increases in gross chromosomal rearrangements, while ablation of mouse HUS1 causes an array of chromosomal rearrangements [67,68]. Errors in DNA metabolism are therefore a common consequence of checkpoint loss.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 80 85 80 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 85 87 85 87 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 113 124 113 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 361 372 357 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 400 410 392 398 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kin1&#916; </italic>
###xml 428 437 416 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc25.22 </italic>
However, our data do not support the idea that genomic errors caused by loss of rad26+ affect morphology. First, rad26Delta cells displayed specific defects in actin, and not microtubule, patterns. If gross errors in DNA metabolism affect morphology, then we would expect this effect to be broad and inclusive of both cytoskeletal elements. And second, loss of rad26Delta compromised the polarity of kin1Delta and G2/M arrested cdc25.22 cells, neither of which is known to accumulate genomic errors in DNA.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 291 293 291 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B69">69</xref>
###xml 294 296 294 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B71">71</xref>
###xml 512 514 512 514 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B72">72</xref>
###xml 515 517 515 517 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B75">75</xref>
###xml 622 624 622 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B76">76</xref>
###xml 41 46 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 129 134 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 174 180 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 283 289 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
It is important to point out that ATM, a human PIKK involved in DNA structure checkpoint pathways, localizes to the cytoplasm of mouse Purkinje cells and in the endosomes of murine cerebellocortical neurons, and ATM mutations lead to loss of Purkinjie cells and neurodegeneration in humans [69-71]. In these contexts, cytoplasmic ATM is thought to influence the metabolism of reactive oxygen species, and loss of this activity may cause accumulation of oxidative stress and genomic lesions that lead to disease [72-75]. In addition, ATM was recently shown to translocate to the cytoplasm following the production of DSBs [76]. Again, cytoplasmic ATM is thought to protect cells, or influence their recovery, from genomic stress. In this report we found that cytoplasmic Rad26-GFP specifically accumulated following microtubule, not genomic, stress. Therefore, we predict that the outcome will influence mechanisms that protect against loss of microtubule dependent processes such as polarity.
###end p 69
###begin title 70
An evolutionarily conserved role for DNA structure checkpoint elements in polarity maintenance?
###end title 70
###begin p 71
###xml 106 117 106 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad24&#916; </italic>
###xml 165 166 161 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
###xml 169 171 165 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 317 319 313 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B77">77</xref>
###xml 447 449 443 445 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B78">78</xref>
###xml 471 475 467 471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ArtA</italic>
###xml 480 492 476 488 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. nidulans </italic>
###xml 594 596 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B79">79</xref>
###xml 18 31 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 325 331 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 480 491 <span type="species:ncbi:162425">A. nidulans</span>
Rad24 is the only fission yeast DNA structure checkpoint component with a documented role in polarity, as rad24Delta cells are more spherical than wild type (Figure 8) [35]. It is a member of the 14-3-3 family associated with the dynamic nucleoplasmic shuttling of proteins with phospho-serine and -threonine motifs [77]. In humans, >200 proteins bind a 14-3-3 phosphopeptide binding site, including some implicated in controlling actin dynamics [78]. Over expression of ArtA, an A. nidulans 14-3-3 gene, inhibits polarization and is therefore linked to the morphogenesis of filamentous fungi [79]. While little is known about how 14-3-3 proteins like Rad24 affect polarity, the evidence presented here suggest that Rad26 may be involved.
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 66 79 66 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. nidulan's </italic>
###xml 158 160 158 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B80">80</xref>
###xml 167 177 167 173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#916;atmA </italic>
###xml 463 473 459 465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#916;atmA </italic>
###xml 669 671 661 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B81">81</xref>
###xml 900 902 892 894 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B82">82</xref>
###xml 903 905 895 897 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B83">83</xref>
Roles in morphology have also been attributed to ATM and AtmA, an A. nidulan's PIKK that is homolgous to ATM and also required for DNA structure checkpoints [80]. The DeltaatmA cells displayed defects in establishing a normal growth axis at hyphal tips and incorporated cell wall material at subapical regions. The hyphal tips of these cells also curled backwards as opposed to radiating outward in a straight line like wild-type. Strikingly, the microtubules of DeltaatmA cells failed to converge at hyphal tips. In addition, altered morphology and altered actin filament patterns have been observed in ataxia-telangiectasia fibroblasts that harbor a mutation in ATM [81]. Interestingly, the microtubule arrays of these cells appeared normal. ATM has also been shown to physically interact with CKIP-1, a regulator of the actin cytoskeleton, and affect RhoA activity during the DNA damage response [82,83]. Together, these observations suggest that DNA structure checkpoint elements share an evolutionarily conserved role in regulating cell morphology.
###end p 72
###begin title 73
###xml 17 28 17 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
Why do untreated rad26Delta cells have a 5% elevated-rate of minichromosome loss?
###end title 73
###begin p 74
###xml 43 54 43 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 203 214 199 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 227 232 219 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 232 234 224 226 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
In addition to polarity defects, untreated rad26Delta cells experienced minichromosome loss. We present four of many possible explanations to account for this. First, Cdc2 activity may be deregulated in rad26Delta cells, since rad26+ and other elements of the DNA structure checkpoints are negative regulators of Cdc2. Deregulation of Cdc2 could conceivably lead to premature activation of Cdc2 and premature entry into mitosis.
###end p 74
###begin p 75
###xml 8 19 8 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 67 72 63 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 72 74 68 70 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 138 142 134 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad3</italic>
###xml 142 144 138 140 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 148 153 144 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 153 155 149 151 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 196 200 192 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad3</italic>
###xml 200 202 196 198 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 294 305 290 297 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916; </italic>
###xml 347 349 339 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 352 354 344 346 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B84">84</xref>
###xml 355 357 347 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B85">85</xref>
###xml 376 381 368 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 381 383 373 375 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 469 471 461 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B86">86</xref>
Second, rad26Delta cells may have abnormal cohesion. In this case, rad26+ may be required for proper heterochromatin structure, since (1) rad3+ and rad26+ are required for telomere structure, (2) rad3+ influences telomeric silencing, (3) overproduced Rad3 associates with telomeric DNA and (4) rad26Delta cells exhibit minichromosome loss (Figure 2B) [84,85]. Perhaps loss of rad26+ affects the formation of heterochromatin that is known to nucleate cohesion assembly [86]. In turn, compromised cohesion could accelerate chromosome separation.
###end p 75
###begin p 76
###xml 220 222 220 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B87">87</xref>
###xml 253 258 253 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 258 260 258 260 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 76 81 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Third, Rad26 may regulate spindle behavior. In this regard, Mecl of budding yeast prevents precocious chromosome segregation during a block to DNA replication by affecting spindle elongation as opposed to mitotic entry [87]. It is possible that loss of rad26+ affects the dynamics of spindle elongation and leads to chromosome loss by a similar mechanism.
###end p 76
###begin p 77
###xml 162 164 162 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B88">88</xref>
###xml 165 167 165 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B90">90</xref>
###xml 174 184 174 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26&#916;</italic>
###xml 12 17 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
And fourth, yeast spindle alignment is dependent on interactions between microtubules and cell polarity cues, including those of the cortical actin cytoskeleton [88-90]. The rad26Delta-polarity problems may affect these interactions and lead to chromosome segregation errors. Our speculative model follows, whereby Rad26 and Rad24 may define a pathway required for polarity maintenance. Like DNA structure checkpoint pathways, this pathway may ultimately function to preserve genomic integrity.
###end p 77
###begin title 78
Conclusion
###end title 78
###begin title 79
A novel role for DNA structure checkpoint elements: responding to microtubule destabilizing conditions
###end title 79
###begin p 80
###xml 50 63 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
The data presented here show that two elements of fission yeast DNA structure checkpoints (Rad26 and Rad24) define a pathway that responds to microtubule destabilizing conditions. We predict that the outcome may influence mechanisms that protect against loss of microtubule-dependent processes like polarity.
###end p 80
