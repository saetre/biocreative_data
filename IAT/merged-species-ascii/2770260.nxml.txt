###begin article-title 0
The Rts1 Regulatory Subunit of Protein Phosphatase 2A Is Required for Control of G1 Cyclin Transcription and Nutrient Modulation of Cell Size
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: KA DM DRK. Performed the experiments: KA SA. Analyzed the data: KA DRK. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: KA SA DM. Wrote the paper: KA DRK.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 893 897 893 897 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CLN2</italic>
###xml 959 963 959 963 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 146 151 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The key molecular event that marks entry into the cell cycle is transcription of G1 cyclins, which bind and activate cyclin-dependent kinases. In yeast cells, initiation of G1 cyclin transcription is linked to achievement of a critical cell size, which contributes to cell-size homeostasis. The critical cell size is modulated by nutrients, such that cells growing in poor nutrients are smaller than cells growing in rich nutrients. Nutrient modulation of cell size does not work through known critical regulators of G1 cyclin transcription and is therefore thought to work through a distinct pathway. Here, we report that Rts1, a highly conserved regulatory subunit of protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A), is required for normal control of G1 cyclin transcription. Loss of Rts1 caused delayed initiation of bud growth and delayed and reduced accumulation of G1 cyclins. Expression of the G1 cyclin CLN2 from an inducible promoter rescued the delayed bud growth in rts1Delta cells, indicating that Rts1 acts at the level of transcription. Moreover, loss of Rts1 caused altered regulation of Swi6, a key component of the SBF transcription factor that controls G1 cyclin transcription. Epistasis analysis revealed that Rts1 does not work solely through several known critical upstream regulators of G1 cyclin transcription. Cells lacking Rts1 failed to undergo nutrient modulation of cell size. Together, these observations demonstrate that Rts1 is a key player in pathways that link nutrient availability, cell size, and G1 cyclin transcription. Since Rts1 is highly conserved, it may function in similar pathways in vertebrates.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Author Summary
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 919 923 919 923 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Rts1</sup>
###xml 1035 1039 1035 1039 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Rts1</sup>
A critical point in the cell cycle occurs in G1 phase, when cells must decide whether to enter a new round of cell division. At this time, cells assess nutrient availability to ensure that they have sufficient resources to complete cell growth and division. Vertebrate cells also assess growth factors that control cell growth and determine when and where cell division occurs in the context of a multi-cellular organism. A cell-size checkpoint acts during G1 to delay entry into the cell cycle if the cell is below a critical size. When the appropriate signals have been received, cells commit to entry into the cell cycle by initiating transcription of G1 cyclins. The mechanisms that integrate external signals, cell growth, cell size, and entry into the cell cycle are poorly understood and represent a fundamental unsolved problem in cell biology. We discovered that a specific form of protein phosphatase 2A (PP2ARts1) functions in the pathways that integrate nutrient availability, cell size, and entry into the cell cycle. PP2ARts1 is highly conserved and may therefore carry out similar functions in all eukaryotic cells.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Introduction
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 103 106 103 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Andrews1">[1]</xref>
###xml 265 268 265 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Andrews1">[1]</xref>
###xml 590 593 590 593 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Richardson1">[2]</xref>
###xml 594 597 594 597 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Cross1">[3]</xref>
###xml 706 709 706 709 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Moffat1">[4]</xref>
###xml 710 713 710 713 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Measday1">[5]</xref>
###xml 923 926 923 926 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Measday1">[5]</xref>
###xml 927 930 927 930 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Tyers1">[9]</xref>
###xml 1060 1064 1060 1064 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Futcher1">[10]</xref>
###xml 1065 1069 1065 1069 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen1">[11]</xref>
###xml 1188 1191 1188 1191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Moffat1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1192 1196 1192 1196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-McCusker1">[12]</xref>
###xml 158 163 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Entry into the cell cycle is initiated by G1 cyclins, which bind and activate cyclin-dependent kinases [1]. There are two cyclin-dependent kinases in budding yeast that function during G1, called Cdk1 and Pho85, which are activated by numerous different G1 cyclins [1]. Cdk1 is activated by the cyclins Cln1, Cln2, and Cln3, while Pho85 is activated by Pcl1 and Pcl2, as well as by additional cyclins that do not appear to directly regulate G1 events. The G1 cyclins are redundant: cells lacking any two of the cyclins Cln1, Cln2 or Cln3 are viable, but loss of all three cyclins is lethal [2],[3]. Similarly, cells lacking Cln1 and Cln2 or Pcl1 and Pcl2 are viable, but loss of all four cyclins is lethal [4],[5]. The cyclin Cln3 plays a role in triggering transcription of a suite of genes required for initiation of G1 events, including the genes for Cln1, Cln2, and Pcl1, which are often referred to as late G1 cyclins [5]-[9]. Transcription of the late G1 cyclins is generally considered to be the key molecular event that marks entry into the cell cycle [10],[11]. The late G1 cyclins initiate growth of a new daughter bud and are also required for polar growth after bud emergence [4],[12].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 176 180 176 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Deshaies1">[13]</xref>
###xml 181 185 181 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Schneider1">[15]</xref>
###xml 524 528 524 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Costanzo1">[16]</xref>
###xml 529 533 529 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-deBruin1">[17]</xref>
###xml 931 935 931 935 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-DiComo1">[18]</xref>
###xml 936 940 936 940 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Manukyan1">[21]</xref>
###xml 1114 1117 1114 1117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Cross2">[6]</xref>
###xml 1118 1121 1118 1121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Dirick1">[7]</xref>
###xml 1122 1126 1122 1126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Skotheim1">[22]</xref>
Production of late G1 cyclins is tightly regulated. Cyclin mRNA and protein undergo rapid turnover, so mechanisms that act at the level of transcription play an important role [13]-[15]. Transcription of G1-specific genes, including the late G1 cyclin genes, is dependent upon the SBF and MBF transcription factors. SBF and MBF each include a distinct DNA binding subunit, called Swi4 and Mbp1, respectively, and a shared subunit called Swi6. SBF and MBF are kept inactive early in the cell cycle by a repressor called Whi5 [16],[17]. Loss of Whi5 causes transcription of late G1 cyclins to occur before the mother cell has completed growth, leading to premature bud emergence and a reduced cell size. Cdk1/Cln3 triggers transcription of late G1 cyclins by phosphorylating and inactivating Whi5. Transcription of late G1 cyclins can also be triggered by a redundant Cln3-independent pathway that is dependent upon the Bck2 protein [18]-[21]. The late G1 cyclin Cln2 promotes its own transcription via a positive feedback loop, which ensures that initiation of G1 events occurs in a coordinated, switch-like manner [6],[7],[22].
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 554 558 554 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Johnston1">[23]</xref>
###xml 559 563 559 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Fantes1">[24]</xref>
###xml 698 702 698 702 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen1">[11]</xref>
###xml 376 381 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Mechanisms that control G1 cyclin transcription play an important role in control of cell size. A cell size checkpoint links initiation of G1 cyclin transcription to cell size. Thus, transcription of late G1 cyclins is only initiated when the mother cell has reached a critical size, which contributes to cell size homeostasis. An interesting property of cell size control in yeast is that the critical cell size is modulated by external nutrients, such that cells growing in poor nutrients are significantly smaller than cells growing in rich nutrients [23],[24]. It is thought that nutrients modulate cell size by rapidly changing the critical cell size for initiation of G1 cyclin transcription [11].
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 130 134 130 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cln3</italic>
###xml 140 144 136 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bck2</italic>
###xml 150 154 142 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">whi5</italic>
###xml 334 338 322 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen2">[25]</xref>
The mechanisms that link initiation of G1 cyclin transcription to cell size and nutrient availability are unknown. Interestingly, cln3Delta bck2Delta whi5Delta triple mutants, which lack all upstream regulators known to play an important role in the control of G1 cyclin transcription, undergo normal nutrient modulation of cell size [25]. Thus, the signals that control cell size by linking G1 cyclin transcription to nutrient availability must act by a different mechanism. The mechanisms that link G1 cyclin transcription to cell size and nutrient availability are likely to be a key to understanding cell size control.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 284 288 284 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Janssens1">[26]</xref>
###xml 657 662 657 662 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Cdc55</sup>
###xml 671 675 671 675 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Rts1</sup>
###xml 675 679 675 679 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Zhao1">[27]</xref>
###xml 680 684 680 684 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Shu1">[28]</xref>
###xml 527 532 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Here, we report that a specific form of protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A) is required for control of G1 cyclin transcription and nutrient modulation of cell size. PP2A is a trimeric complex that consists of a catalytic "C" subunit, a scaffolding "A" subunit, and a regulatory "B" subunit [26]. Binding of different B-type regulatory subunits is thought to direct PP2A activity toward different substrates. Thus, the key to understanding PP2A is to understand the function and regulation of specific regulatory subunits. In budding yeast, two B subunits called Cdc55 and Rts1 bind to PP2A in a mutually exclusive manner, forming two distinct PP2A complexes: PP2ACdc55 and PP2ARts1[27],[28].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 113 117 113 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RTS1</italic>
###xml 133 138 133 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CDC12</italic>
###xml 296 300 296 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Spiliotis1">[29]</xref>
###xml 702 706 702 706 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Hartwell1">[30]</xref>
###xml 844 848 844 848 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Longtine1">[31]</xref>
###xml 916 920 916 920 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">swe1</italic>
###xml 1060 1064 1056 1060 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Longtine1">[31]</xref>
###xml 1065 1069 1061 1065 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Mortensen1">[36]</xref>
###xml 757 762 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
We discovered a role for Rts1 in controlling G1 cyclin levels while characterizing a genetic interaction between RTS1 and the septin CDC12. The septins are a conserved family of proteins that localize to the site of bud emergence in early G1 and to the bud neck during bud growth and cytokinesis [29]. The septins have been proposed to form a diffusion barrier between the mother and daughter cell, to serve as a signaling scaffold for activation of kinases, or to carry out functions in the secretory pathway. Temperature sensitive alleles of the septins cause cells to undergo a prolonged delay at G2/M while undergoing continuous polarized growth, leading to the formation of highly elongated cells [30]. The G2/M arrest is mediated by Swe1, the budding yeast Wee1 homolog, which phosphorylates and inhibits Cdk1 to delay entry into mitosis [31]. The G2/M delay and the elongated cell phenotype are eliminated by swe1Delta. A number of kinases have been identified that regulate septin function and localization, and may in turn be regulated by the septins [31]-[36]. These kinases include Elm1, Gin4, Cla4, and Hsl1. Loss of these kinases can cause a Swe1-dependent G2/M delay and an elongated cell phenotype similar to septin mutants.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 25 29 25 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 80 87 76 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cdc12-6</italic>
###xml 95 99 91 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Dobbelaere1">[37]</xref>
###xml 109 113 105 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RTS1</italic>
###xml 283 290 279 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cdc12-6</italic>
Previous work found that rts1Delta increased the restrictive temperature of the cdc12-6 allele [37]. Loss of RTS1 also caused altered septin ring dynamics; however, it remained unclear whether the observed changes in septin ring dynamics were sufficient to explain the rescue of the cdc12-6 temperature sensitive phenotype. For example, it was possible that in addition to regulating septin ring dynamics, Rts1 played additional roles in pathways that promote polar growth or Swe1-dependent G2/M delays. We therefore further investigated the role of Rts1 in polar cell growth and cell cycle progression.

We next considered the possibility that rts1Delta rescued the elongated cell phenotype of these mutants by eliminating the Swe1-dependent G2/M delay. To test this, levels of the mitotic cyclin Clb2 were assayed by Western blotting during a synchronized cell cycle in wild type, elm1Delta, and elm1Delta rts1Delta cells (Figure 1C). To test this, we utilized cells that over express SWE1 from the GAL1 promoter, which arrest at G2/M with high levels of G1 cyclins and undergo constitutive polar growth [12],[38]. In summary, rts1Delta showed genetic interactions with multiple G1 cyclins and cyclin-dependent kinases. Because the late G1 cyclins show extensive redundancy, mutations that cause reduced function of G1 cyclins should show synthetic interactions with mutations that cause a further reduction in cyclin levels. The fact that rts1Delta showed lethality when combined with cln1Delta cln2Delta, and reduced viability when combined with pcl1Delta pcl2Delta, suggests that Rts1 is required for the normal function of both pairs of cyclins, rather than mediating the functions of specific cyclins. The fact that rts1Delta also showed genetic interactions with cln3Delta, which is upstream of the late G1 cyclins, indicates that Rts1 does not act solely in a Cln3-dependent pathway that promotes transcription of the late G1 cyclins.

Since the cells used in these experiments were synchronized with mating pheromone, it was possible that the delayed accumulation of Cln2 was due to delayed release from mating pheromone arrest. To determine whether rts1Delta cells underwent normal release from mating pheromone arrest, we assayed the phosphorylation state of Cdc24, which is the guanine nucleotide exchange factor for Cdc42 [42]. In previous work, it was found that Cdc24 becomes hyperphosphorylated during mating pheromone arrest and undergoes dephosphorylation upon release from the arrest [12],[43]. Cdc24 then undergoes hyperphosphorylation during G1 that is dependent upon the Cla4 kinase and Cdk1 [12],[43],[44]. We found that Cdc24 underwent normal dephosphorylation in rts1Delta cells after release from mating pheromone arrest, which suggested that rts1Delta does not cause delayed release from mating pheromone arrest (Figure S1A). Cdc24 showed delayed phosphorylation in rts1Delta cells, consistent with delayed initiation of G1 events.

To further rule out the possibility that the G1 delay was due to mating pheromone-induced arrest, we used an alternative method for cell synchronization. Cells can be arrested in mitosis by depletion of Cdc20, which is required for proteolytic destruction of the mitotic cyclins [45]-[47]. Synchronization in metaphase was achieved by shifting cells to media lacking galactose for 4 hours, followed by releasing cells into galactose-containing media to initiate synchronous exit from mitosis. Cells lacking Rts1 showed a 30-40 minute delay in Cln2 accumulation and reduced Cln2 levels under these conditions (Figure 5B).

###begin title 46
Rts1 does not regulate the turnover of Cln2 protein
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 96 100 96 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Skotheim1">[22]</xref>

###xml 475 479 475 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CLN2</italic>
Recent work found that Cln2 acts in a positive feedback loop to stimulate its own transcription [22]. Thus, the delay in accumulation of CLN2 mRNA could be due to a failure in mechanisms required for normal accumulation of the Cln2 protein, which would disrupt the feedback loop. Overexpression of CLN2 from the GAL1 promoter might be expected to rescue this kind of defect. Therefore, we tested whether Rts1 functions in several mechanisms known to regulate accumulation of CLN2 protein.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 280 284 280 284 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Deshaies1">[13]</xref>

###xml 954 963 940 949 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pgen-1000727-g007">Figure 7A</xref>
###xml 1109 1113 1095 1099 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Deshaies1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1122 1134 1108 1120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cdc34-2 rts1</italic>
###xml 1240 1247 1222 1229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cdc34-2</italic>
###xml 1287 1291 1269 1273 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
Cln2 is a highly unstable protein and proteolysis plays an important role in regulation of Cln2 protein levels. Proteolysis of Cln2 is controlled by the SCF ubiquitin ligase complex, which recognizes phosphorylated sites at the C-terminus of Cln2 and targets Cln2 for destruction [13],[49]. Cdc34 is the E2 ubiquitin conjugating enzyme component of the SCF complex. We hypothesized that the reduced Cln2 protein levels observed in rts1Delta cells could be caused by increased SCF-dependent proteolysis of Cln2 protein. Reduced protein levels, in turn, would disrupt the positive feedback loop that promotes CLN2 transcription, thereby causing reduced and delayed transcription of CLN2 mRNA. To test this possibility, we created an rts1Delta strain that also contained a temperature sensitive allele of CDC34 (cdc34-2). Cells were released from a synchronized G1 arrest into 37degreesC media, and Cln2 protein expression was followed by Western blotting (Figure 7A). As previously reported, inactivation of Cdc34 in the control cells caused stabilization of Cln2 and a dramatic increase in Cln2 protein levels [13]. In the cdc34-2 rts1Delta cells, Cln2 protein levels were still reduced and did not accumulate to the high levels observed in cdc34-2 cells. This showed that the failure of rts1Delta cells to accumulate normal levels of Cln2 is due to a failure to produce Cln2, rather than to increased SCF-dependent destruction of Cln2.

###end title 52

###xml 355 359 355 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-deBruin1">[17]</xref>

###xml 642 646 638 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>

###xml 806 815 786 795 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pgen-1000727-g008">Figure 8A</xref>
###xml 851 855 831 835 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">whi5</italic>
###xml 907 911 883 887 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 924 933 896 905 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pgen-1000727-g008">Figure 8B</xref>
###xml 992 996 964 968 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 1053 1057 1021 1025 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">whi5</italic>
###xml 1153 1157 1117 1121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 1207 1211 1167 1171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
To further define the function of Rts1, we tested whether it acts in pathways known to control G1 cyclin transcription. The Whi5 transcriptional repressor delays G1 progression by inhibiting transcription of G1 cyclins. Whi5 acts by binding and inhibiting the SBF and MBF transcription factors, which are required for transcription of the G1 cyclins [16],[17]. The Cdk1/Cln3 complex relieves this inhibition by phosphorylating Whi5, which triggers export of Whi5 from the nucleus. Thus, it was possible that Rts1 played a role in the inactivation of Whi5. If this were true, whi5Delta should rescue the delayed expression of Cln2 observed in rts1Delta cells. However, we found that bud emergence and accumulation of Cln2 protein were still delayed in rts1Delta whi5Delta cells compared to whi5Delta cells (Figure 8A, and data not shown). In addition, whi5Delta did not rescue the temperature sensitivity of rts1Delta cells (Figure 8B). Loss of Whi5 advanced the production of Cln2 protein in rts1Delta cells, although not to the same extent observed in whi5Delta cells, which indicated that Whi5-dependent regulation of transcription occurs normally in rts1Delta cells. Thus, the delayed Cln2 expression in rts1Delta cells is not due to a failure to inactivate Whi5.

###begin title 54

###end title 54
###begin p 55

###xml 1395 1399 1371 1375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Russell1">[55]</xref>
###xml 1405 1409 1381 1385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">mih1</italic>
###xml 1477 1487 1449 1459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pgen-1000727-g011">Figure 11B</xref>
###xml 1526 1530 1498 1502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cln3</italic>
###xml 1624 1628 1592 1596 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen2">[25]</xref>
Our analysis of the role of Rts1 in control of G1 cyclins suggested that Rts1 does not function solely in the known critical pathways for control of G1 cyclin transcription that work through Cln3, Whi5, or Bck2. This was an intriguing discovery, because previous work found that the mechanisms responsible for nutrient modulation of cell size do not control G1 cyclin transcription via Cln3, Whi5 or Bck2 [11],[25]. We therefore hypothesized that Rts1 controls G1 cyclin transcription in a distinct pathway that mediates nutrient modulation of cell size. To test this, we determined whether Rts1 is required for nutrient modulation of cell size. We grew wild type and rts1Delta cells in rich or poor carbon sources and measured cell size with a Coulter counter. We found that Rts1 is required for nutrient modulation of size (Figure 11A). The slight shift in size observed for rts1Delta cells shifted to poor carbon sources is similar to the slight shift observed for sch9Delta and sfp1Delta, which have also been found to be required for nutrient modulation of cell size [25]. Since rts1Delta cells are abnormally large, we were concerned that they may already be above the critical size for initiation of G1 cyclin transcription, and therefore not subject to G1 size control. To test this, we used mih1Delta cells, which are abnormally large because they undergo extra growth during G2/M [54],[55]. The mih1Delta cells showed normal nutrient modulation of cell size control (Figure 11B). Furthermore, others have found that cln3Delta cells, which are also abnormally large, undergo normal nutrient modulation of cell size [25].

###begin title 70
Discussion
###end title 70
###begin title 71
Rts1 is required for normal control of G1 cyclin transcription
###end title 71
###begin p 72
###xml 137 141 137 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CLN2</italic>
###xml 216 220 216 220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
Loss of Rts1 caused reduced and delayed expression of multiple G1 cyclins, and a corresponding delay in bud emergence. Overexpression of CLN2 from a heterologous promoter largely rescued the delayed bud emergence in rts1Delta cells. We found no evidence that Rts1 regulates Cln2 protein stability. Together, these observations demonstrate that Rts1 functions in a pathway that regulates G1 cyclins at the level of transcription.
###end p 72
###begin p 73
###xml 172 176 172 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 200 207 196 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pgen-1000727-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 221 225 217 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 314 319 306 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">pho85</italic>
###xml 431 435 419 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
The results of genetic analysis further supported the conclusion that Rts1 works in a pathway that controls G1 cyclin levels. The genetic interactions that we observed for rts1Delta are summarized in Table 1. In general, rts1Delta caused slow growth or lethality when combined with deletions of G1 cyclin genes or pho85Delta. Due to the redundancy of the G1 cyclins, a general reduction in levels of G1 cyclin expression caused by rts1Delta would be expected to cause additive effects when combined with gene deletions that further reduce levels of G1 cyclins.

###xml 279 283 275 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-McCusker1">[12]</xref>

###xml 483 487 475 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gin4</italic>
The finding that Rts1 is required for normal levels of Cln2 protein provides an explanation for why rts1Delta caused reduced polar growth in mutants that fail to properly inactivate Swe1. Failure to inactivate Swe1 causes cells to arrest at G2/M with high levels of Cln2 protein [12]. Since Cln2 promotes polar growth, a reduction in Cln2 levels would be expected to cause reduced polar growth. Accordingly, we found that rts1Delta caused reduced and delayed accumulation of Cln2 in gin4Delta cells, which fail to inactivate Swe1.

###begin p 77
###xml 212 216 212 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 222 226 218 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-FernandezSarabia1">[56]</xref>
###xml 314 318 310 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">sit4</italic>
###xml 328 332 320 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 361 365 349 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 407 411 391 395 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">sit4</italic>
###xml 450 454 430 434 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CLN2</italic>
###xml 606 610 586 590 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-FernandezSarabia1">[56]</xref>
###xml 637 641 617 621 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">sit4</italic>
###xml 681 688 657 664 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ssd1-d2</italic>
###xml 722 726 698 702 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 765 769 737 741 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SSD1</italic>
###xml 769 773 741 745 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-FernandezSarabia1">[56]</xref>
###xml 784 788 756 760 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">sit4</italic>
###xml 794 798 762 766 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cln3</italic>
###xml 845 849 809 813 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RTS1</italic>
###xml 884 888 848 852 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cln3</italic>
###xml 900 904 860 864 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-FernandezSarabia1">[56]</xref>
Previous work found that G1 cyclin transcription is regulated by another PP2A-like phosphatase called Sit4. Loss of Sit4 caused decreased transcription of late G1 cyclins and defects in bud emergence, similar to rts1Delta [56]. However, there are a number of significant differences in the G1 phenotypes caused by sit4Delta and rts1Delta. First, in contrast to rts1Delta, defects in bud emergence caused by sit4Delta are not rescued by expression of CLN2 from a heterologous promoter, which demonstrates that Sit4 carries out functions required for bud emergence beyond controlling G1 cyclin transcription [56]. Second, the phenotype of sit4Delta cells is strongly enhanced by the ssd1-d2 allele, whereas the phenotype of rts1Delta is not affected by the status of SSD1[56]. Finally, sit4Delta cln3Delta cells are barely viable, whereas loss of RTS1 caused relatively mild effects in cln3Delta cells [56]. These phenotypic differences suggest that Rts1 and Sit4 may function in independent pathways that regulate late G1 cyclin levels in response to different signals, but do not rule out the possibility that they have overlapping functions.
###end p 77
###begin title 78
Rts1 does not function solely in one of the pathways known to play a critical role in transcription of G1 cyclins
###end title 78
###begin p 79
###xml 450 456 450 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CLN3-1</italic>
###xml 463 467 463 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">whi5</italic>
###xml 575 579 571 575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Costanzo1">[16]</xref>
###xml 620 624 616 620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cln3</italic>
###xml 630 634 622 626 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
We used epistasis analysis to determine whether Rts1 regulates G1 cyclin transcription via known mechanisms. This analysis demonstrated that Rts1 does not function solely in a Bck2-dependent pathway, and ruled out a role for Rts1 in a pathway known to regulate G1 cyclin transcription via Cln3-dependent inactivation of the Whi5 transcriptional repressor. Cln3 may also promote G1 cyclin transcription in a Whi5-independent manner. Overexpression of CLN3-1 makes whi5Delta cells smaller, which suggests that Cln3 can drive G1 cyclin transcription by an alternative mechanism [16]. In genetic crosses, we found that most cln3Delta rts1Delta spores failed to germinate, and cells lacking both Rts1 and Cln3 showed slow growth when compared to either single deletion. Thus, Rts1 does not appear to function solely in a Cln3-dependent pathway that promotes G1 cyclin transcription. Together, these observations suggest that Rts1 does not function solely in one of the known pathways that play an important role in promoting G1 cyclin transcription. Our results do not rule out the possibility that Rts1 contributes to multiple pathways.
###end p 79

###xml 670 674 642 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cln1</italic>

###xml 729 733 693 697 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bck2</italic>
###xml 745 749 705 709 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-DiComo2">[57]</xref>
###xml 750 754 710 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Nasmyth1">[58]</xref>
###xml 777 781 737 741 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 791 795 747 751 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">swi6</italic>
###xml 848 852 800 804 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cln3</italic>
###xml 864 868 812 816 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Nasmyth1">[58]</xref>
###xml 891 895 839 843 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SWI4</italic>
###xml 926 930 874 878 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cln3</italic>
###xml 936 940 880 884 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Nasmyth1">[58]</xref>
###xml 998 1002 942 946 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 1108 1112 1048 1052 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Rts1</sup>
We found that rts1Delta caused reduced and delayed expression of both SBF and MBF-dependent transcripts, which demonstrated that Rts1 acts in a pathway upstream of both transcription factors. We further found that rts1Delta caused a reduction in Swi6 phosphorylation at the time that cells initiate G1 cyclin transcription. Since Swi6 is the one shared component of SBF and MBF, this suggests that Rts1 regulates both transcription factors via Swi6. In support of this, the pattern of genetic interactions observed for rts1Delta is similar to the pattern previously reported for swi6Delta. Both swi6Delta and rts1Delta cause slow growth in cln2Delta cells, lethality in cln1Delta cln2Delta cells, and slow growth or lethality in bck2Delta cells [57],[58]. In addition, neither rts1Delta nor swi6Delta caused synthetic lethality in combination with cln3Delta cells [58]. In contrast, loss of SWI4 is lethal in combination with cln3Delta [58]. The fact that Swi6 undergoes reduced phosphorylation in rts1Delta cells, rather than hyperphosphorylation, indicates that it is unlikely to be a direct target of PP2ARts1.

###xml 191 195 191 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Zarzov1">[59]</xref>

###xml 377 381 377 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">slt2</italic>
###xml 437 441 433 437 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CLN1</italic>
###xml 445 449 441 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CLN2</italic>
###xml 502 506 498 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PCL1</italic>
###xml 511 515 507 511 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">PCL2</italic>
###xml 648 652 638 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">slt2</italic>

###xml 680 684 666 670 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CLN2</italic>
###xml 711 715 697 701 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 780 784 762 766 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CLN1</italic>
###xml 789 793 771 775 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CLN2</italic>

Phosphorylation of Swi6 that can be detected by an electrophoretic mobility shift is dependent upon the MAP kinase Slt2, and activation of Slt2 coincides with initiation of polar growth [51],[59]. The Slt2 pathway is activated by Pkc1, and overexpression of Pkc1 suppresses swi4 mutants, which demonstrates a role in controlling G1 cyclin transcription [60]. In previous work, slt2Delta was not found to cause reduction in the levels of CLN1 or CLN2 transcripts, but did cause a reduction in levels of PCL1 and PCL2 transcripts in cells grown at 37degreesC. Further work will be necessary to test for possible roles of Rts1 in Slt2-dependent regulation of G1 cyclin transcription.

###begin title 83
Rts1 is required for nutrient modulation of cell size
###end title 83
###begin p 84
Previous work found that nutrient modulation of cell size does not require Cln3, Whi5, or Bck2, which suggests that it works via a distinct mechanism. The discovery that Rts1 does not function solely in one of the pathways known to play a critical role in controlling G1 cyclin transcription therefore prompted us to test whether Rts1 is required for nutrient modulation of cell size. We found that Rts1 is required for nutrient modulated control of cell size, which identifies Rts1 as a new component of the pathways that integrate nutrient availability, cell size, and entry into the cell cycle.
###end p 84
###begin p 85
###xml 213 217 213 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Zaman1">[61]</xref>
###xml 514 518 514 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Zaman1">[61]</xref>
###xml 694 698 694 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Baroni1">[62]</xref>
###xml 699 703 699 703 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Tokiwa1">[64]</xref>
Little is known about the pathways responsible for nutrient modulation of cell size. Two conserved pathways play broad roles in controlling nutrient sensing, nutrient utilization, cell growth and cell cycle entry [61]. In one pathway, the TOR kinases respond to the availability of nitrogen and trigger activation of pathways that control cell growth and nitrogen utilization. A second pathway responds to carbon source availability and regulates growth via activation of the Ras GTPase and protein kinase A (PKA) [61]. The Ras/PKA pathway is required for control of cell size: increased activity of the pathway leads to increased cell size, while decreased activity leads to reduced cell size [62]-[64]. However, the mechanisms by which Ras/PKA control cell size are unknown.
###end p 85
###begin p 86
###xml 294 298 294 298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Urban1">[65]</xref>
###xml 417 421 417 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen2">[25]</xref>
###xml 422 426 422 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Fingerman1">[66]</xref>
###xml 427 431 427 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen3">[67]</xref>
###xml 657 661 657 661 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Urban1">[65]</xref>
###xml 662 666 662 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Zaman2">[68]</xref>
A key target of both pathways is ribosome biogenesis. Two key regulators of ribosome biogenesis that are thought to regulate cell growth are Sch9 and Sfp1. Sch9 is a member of the AGC kinase family and is closely related to vertebrate Akt kinase, while Sfp1 is related to transcription factors [65]. Sch9 and Sfp1 carry out overlapping functions in controlling transcription of genes required for ribosome biogenesis [25],[66],[67]. Loss of either protein causes reduced ribosome biogenesis, while loss of both is lethal. The TOR pathway appears to work through Sch9, but the mechanisms by which the Ras/PKA pathway controls ribosome biogenesis are unclear [65],[68].
###end p 86
###begin p 87
###xml 150 154 150 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen1">[11]</xref>
###xml 274 278 274 278 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen2">[25]</xref>
###xml 279 283 279 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Tokiwa1">[64]</xref>
###xml 519 523 519 523 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen3">[67]</xref>
###xml 639 643 639 643 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen1">[11]</xref>
###xml 644 648 644 648 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Jorgensen3">[67]</xref>
It is thought that nutrient modulation of cell size is achieved by changing the critical cell size required for initiation of G1 cyclin transcription [11]. Thus far, only three proteins have been found to be required for nutrient modulation of cell size: Sch9, Sfp1 and PKA [25],[64]. Notably, all three converge on control of ribosome biogenesis. Moreover, mutants that cause reduced rates of ribosome biogenesis cause cells to enter the cell cycle at a reduced cell size, leading to an overall reduction in cell size [67]. Together, these observations suggest a model in which the rate of ribosome biogenesis sets the critical cell size [11],[67]. According to this model, cells growing in rich nutrients have a high rate of ribosome biogenesis, which sends a signal that inhibits G1 cyclin transcription to allow the cell to reach a larger critical size.
###end p 87
###begin p 88
###xml 497 501 497 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 653 657 649 653 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 731 735 723 727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rts1</italic>
###xml 851 855 839 843 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Shu1">[28]</xref>
The mechanisms that link initiation of G1 cyclin transcription to ribosome biogenesis and nutrient availability are unknown. The identification of Rts1 as a new upstream regulator of G1 cyclin transcription that is also required for nutrient modulation of cell size is therefore a significant step towards understanding these pathways. Rts1 could function between ribosome biogenesis and G1 cyclin transcription. Alternatively, Rts1 could promote ribosome biogenesis, in which case the effects of rts1Delta could be due to decreased rates of ribosome biogenesis. Inactivation of factors that promote ribosome biogenesis cause reduced cell size, whereas rts1Delta causes increased cell size. However, we do not yet know what causes rts1Delta cells to have an increased cell size, and it could be due to a G2/M delay that occurs later in the cell cycle [28].
###end p 88
###begin p 89
###xml 332 336 332 336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pgen.1000727-Weinberg1">[69]</xref>
Since Rts1 is highly conserved, it may also play a role in mechanisms that integrate external signals, cell size, and G1 cyclin transcription in vertebrate cells. The signaling pathways that control G1 cyclin transcription in vertebrate cells are of considerable interest, since deregulation of these pathways contributes to cancer [69].
###end p 89

###begin title 96
Cell cycle time courses
###end title 96
###begin p 97
###xml 84 87 84 87 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">600</sub>
###xml 490 500 466 476 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GAL1-CDC20</italic>
###xml 1056 1060 1018 1019 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
Cells were grown overnight at room temperature on a shaking platform. Cells at an OD600 of 0.6 were arrested in G1 by the addition of 0.5 microg/ml alpha factor for 3.5 hours. Cells were released into a synchronous cell cycle by washing 3x with fresh YEPD pre-warmed to 30degreesC, and time courses were carried out at 30degreesC unless otherwise noted. To prevent cells from entering a second cell cycle, alpha factor was added back at 65 minutes. For metaphase arrest, strains containing GAL1-CDC20 were first grown overnight in YEP +2% raffinose +2% galactose and then washed into media containing 2% raffinose and allowed to arrest for four hours. Cells were released from metaphase arrest by adding 2% galactose and were then shifted to 30degreesC. At each time interval, 1.6 ml samples were collected in screw-top tubes. The cells were pelleted, the supernatant was removed, and 250 microl of glass beads were added before flash freezing. To lyse cells, 100 microl of 1x sample buffer (65 mM Tris-HCl (pH 6.8), 3% SDS, 10% glycerol, 50 mM NaF, 50 mM beta-glycerolphosphate, 5% 2-mercaptoethanol, bromophenol blue) was added. 2 mM PMSF was added to the sample buffer immediately before use from a 100 mM stock made in 100% isopropanol. Cells were lysed by shaking in a Biospec Multibeater-8 at top speed for 2 minutes. The tubes were immediately removed, centrifuged for 1 minute in a microfuge and placed in a boiling water bath for 5 minutes. After boiling, the tubes were again centrifuged for 1 minute and 10 microl was loaded on a gel (5 microl when probing for Nap1). For microscopy, 180 microl samples were collected and fixed by adding 20 microl of 37% formaldehyde for 1 hour. Cells were washed twice in 1x PBS, 0.05% Tween-20, 0.02% sodium azide and imaged by differential interference contrast microscopy. Bud emergence was quantified by counting the number of buds per 200 cells for each sample.
