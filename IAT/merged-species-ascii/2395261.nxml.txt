Characterisation of the tryptophan synthase alpha subunit in maize
###end article-title 0

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 21 43 21 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella typhimurium</italic>
###xml 434 442 413 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zea mays</italic>
###xml 21 43 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">Salmonella typhimurium</span>
###xml 427 432 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
###xml 434 442 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">Zea mays</span>
In bacteria, such as Salmonella typhimurium, tryptophan is synthesized from indole-3-glycerole phosphate (IGP) by a tryptophan synthase alphabetabetaalpha heterotetramer. Plants have evolved multiple alpha (TSA) and beta (TSB) homologs, which have probably diverged in biological function and their ability of subunit interaction. There is some evidence for a tryptophan synthase (TS) complex in Arabidopsis. On the other hand maize (Zea mays) expresses the TSA-homologs BX1 and IGL that efficiently cleave IGP, independent of interaction with TSB.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 104 106 104 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 114 116 114 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 153 155 153 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 354 356 350 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 361 369 357 365 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 545 547 537 539 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 628 630 616 618 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 652 661 640 649 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 713 715 701 703 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 54 59 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
###xml 528 533 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
In order to clarify, how tryptophan is synthesized in maize, two TSA homologs, hitherto uncharacterized ZmTSA and ZmTSAlike, were functionally analyzed. ZmTSA is localized in plastids, the major site of tryptophan biosynthesis in plants. It catalyzes the tryptophan synthase alpha-reaction (cleavage of IGP), and forms a tryptophan synthase complex with ZmTSB1 in vitro. The catalytic efficiency of the alpha-reaction is strongly enhanced upon complex formation. A 160 kD tryptophan synthase complex was partially purified from maize leaves and ZmTSA was identified as native alpha-subunit of this complex by mass spectrometry. ZmTSAlike, for which no in vitro activity was detected, is localized in the cytosol. ZmTSAlike, BX1, and IGL were not detectable in the native tryptophan synthase complex in leaves.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 20 28 20 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 32 41 32 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 92 94 92 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 46 51 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
It was demonstrated in vivo and in vitro that maize forms a tryptophan synthase complex and ZmTSA functions as alpha-subunit in this complex.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 94 102 94 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zea mays</italic>
###xml 250 251 250 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 252 253 252 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 485 486 485 486 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 487 488 487 488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 685 686 685 686 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 687 688 687 688 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 42 47 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 87 92 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
###xml 94 102 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">Zea mays</span>
###xml 204 209 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
Tryptophan is an essential amino acid for human nutrition. In kernels of cereals, e.g. maize (Zea mays), the tryptophan content is low, limiting the nutritional value. Significant effort is made to breed maize lines with enhanced tryptophan content [1,2]. In addition to its function as protein component, plants utilize tryptophan as precursor of a large variety of secondary metabolites like terpenoid indole alkaloids, indole glucosinolates, and indolic phytoalexins (reviewed in: [3,4]). Of special importance is the tryptophan-derived plant hormone indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), which is involved in numerous processes, including embryo development, apical dominance, and tropisms [4,5]. These essential functions of tryptophan emphasize the need to understand its synthesis in plants in more detail.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 21 38 21 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 42 64 42 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella typhimurium</italic>
###xml 170 171 170 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 476 477 462 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 496 497 482 483 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 498 499 484 485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 964 965 929 930 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1071 1072 1036 1037 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1073 1075 1038 1040 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 21 37 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 42 64 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">Salmonella typhimurium</span>
In bacteria, such as Escherichia coli and Salmonella typhimurium, tryptophan is synthesized from indole-3-glycerol phosphate (IGP) by a tryptophan synthase (TS) complex [6]. IGP is cleaved by the TS alpha-subunits (TSA) to indole and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (alpha-reaction). Then indole is transported via a 30 A intermolecular tunnel to the tryptophan synthase beta-subunits (TSB) that catalyze the condensation of indole and serine (beta-reaction) to tryptophan (Figure 1; for review, see [7,8]). This substrate channelling ensures that indole does not escape from the enzyme complex. The reaction mechanism of this bacterial alphabetabetaalpha complex has been studied in great detail. The alpha- and beta-subunits interact in a highly cooperative manner and regulate each other reciprocally by allosteric interactions. In addition, alternative TSBs that are highly active, independent of interaction with the unique TSA, are expressed in some prokaryotes [9]. In fungi interaction of TSA and TSB is obligate as both functions are present on a single polypeptide [9,10].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 0 51 0 51 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">General scheme of the tryptophan synthase reaction.</bold>
General scheme of the tryptophan synthase reaction. Indole, which is formed from IGP by the alpha-subunits is channelled to beta-subunits, which synthesize tryptophan from indole and serine.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 76 80 76 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TSA </italic>
###xml 84 88 84 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TSB </italic>
###xml 129 150 129 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana </italic>
###xml 179 183 179 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TSA </italic>
###xml 201 205 201 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TSB </italic>
###xml 308 310 308 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">At</italic>
###xml 331 333 331 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">At</italic>
###xml 415 420 415 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trp3 </italic>
###xml 424 428 424 428 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trp2</italic>
###xml 444 446 444 446 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 447 449 447 449 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 452 454 452 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">At</italic>
###xml 463 465 463 465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">At</italic>
###xml 505 507 505 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 508 510 508 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 511 513 511 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 637 639 630 632 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 129 149 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
The picture is more complex in higher plants. The homologs of the bacterial TSA and TSB genes are generally duplicated, e.g. the Arabidopsis thaliana genome contains two putative TSA and four putative TSB genes. Currently the role of these different isoforms is not fully understood. Functional relevance of AtTSA1 (At3g54640) and AtTSB1 (At5g54810) was demonstrated by the facultative tryptophan auxotroph mutants trp3 and trp2, respectively [11,12]. AtTSA1 and AtTSB1 are both localized in the plastid [11,13,14]. Based on immunoaffinity chromatography it was strongly suggested that the two proteins form an active alphabeta complex [14]. However, it is not known whether formation of such complexes is a general phenomenon in plants.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 102 106 102 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TSB </italic>
###xml 113 120 113 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZmTSB1 </italic>
###xml 124 130 124 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZmTSB2</italic>
###xml 204 206 204 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 215 217 215 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 361 377 361 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">orange pericarp </italic>
###xml 378 383 378 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">orp1 </italic>
###xml 385 391 385 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zmtsb1</italic>
###xml 393 398 393 398 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">orp2 </italic>
###xml 400 406 400 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zmtsb2</italic>
###xml 433 435 433 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 436 438 436 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 482 484 482 484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 493 495 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 502 504 502 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 659 661 659 661 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 791 793 791 793 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 796 800 796 800 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Igl </italic>
###xml 911 913 911 913 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 914 916 914 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 1068 1070 1065 1067 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 1071 1073 1068 1070 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1181 1185 1178 1182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bx1 </italic>
###xml 1189 1193 1186 1190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">igl </italic>
###xml 76 81 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
Among the cereals most information on TSA and TSB homologs is available for maize: Two highly similar TSB genes (ZmTSB1 and ZmTSB2) have been identified, sharing 96% identity on the mature protein level. ZmTSB1 and ZmTSB2 are functionally redundant active TSB enzymes. While single mutations in either gene do not affect tryptophan synthesis, the double mutant orange pericarp (orp1 = Zmtsb1, orp2 = Zmtsb2) is tryptophan auxotroph [15,16]. Four TSA homologs are present: BX1, IGL, ZmTSAlike [17], and ZmTSA (this work). BX1 is essential for providing indole as precursor of the natural pesticide 2,4-dihydroxy-7-methoxy-2H-1,4-benzoxazin-3(4H)-one (DIMBOA) [18]. The 3-dimensional BX1 structure has been determined and shown to be very similar to the active conformation of bacterial TSAs [19]. Igl transcription is triggered by insect feeding and IGL provides indole as a volatile signal for parasitic wasps [17,20]. BX1 and IGL efficiently cleave IGP to form indole, while the activity of bacterial TSA subunits is dependent on the interaction with a beta-subunit [17,18]. These enzymatic properties, their specific transcriptional regulation, and the lack of growth defects of bx1 and igl mutant plants suggested that additional TSA-homologs are involved in tryptophan biosynthesis.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 107 109 107 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 156 158 156 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 56 61 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
In this study we aimed to identify the TSA homolog from maize that is involved in tryptophan biosynthesis. ZmTSA is catalytically active and interaction of ZmTSA with a beta-subunit strongly enhances the catalytic efficiency of the alpha-reaction. A protein purification strategy was applied to obtain direct evidence that angiosperms, similarly to bacteria, form a tryptophan synthase complex.
###end p 14

###begin title 43
###xml 13 15 13 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
Isolation of ZmTSA
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 56 59 56 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bx1</italic>
###xml 61 64 61 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Igl</italic>

###xml 297 300 297 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Igl</italic>
###xml 305 314 305 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZmTSAlike</italic>
###xml 387 388 387 388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Z</italic>
###xml 448 461 448 461 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZmTSA. ZmTSA </italic>
###xml 531 537 531 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ZmTSA </italic>
###xml 768 770 768 770 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 822 824 822 824 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 902 903 902 903 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 3 8 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
###xml 149 154 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
In maize, four genes encoding TSA homologs are present. Bx1, Igl, and ZmTSAlike have been described previously [17,18]. A search of the GeneBank and maize genomic database (see Availability and requirements section for URL) revealed putative TSA sequences, which do not constitute alleles of Bx1, Igl, or ZmTSAlike. These sequences, partly represented by the Tentative Unique Gene (TUG) Zmtuc03-08-11.4557, correspond to a new gene, now designated ZmTSA. ZmTSA is located on chromosome 7 (contig AC191027, GeneBank). A full-length ZmTSA cDNA clone was isolated [GenBank:]. Most plant TSAs have divergent N-terminal sequences that have no counterpart in bacteria and represent transit peptides for plastid import. When this variable part is excluded from the analysis, ZmTSA is 63% identical to BX1, 67% to IGL, and 72% to ZmTSAlike on protein level, respectively [for an alignment, see Additional file 1].

For a further characterization of the closely related genes ZmTSA and ZmTSAlike their transcription levels were determined by RT-PCR in different tissues and developmental stages. ###begin title 70
Discussion
###end title 70
###begin p 71
###xml 154 156 147 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 286 293 279 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 577 579 570 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 199 204 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
###xml 249 254 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
Evidence for physical interaction of tryptophan synthase alpha and beta subunits in plants was provided by immunoaffinity chromatography for Arabidopsis [14] and by size exclusion chromatography for maize (this study). For the identification of the maize tryptophan synthase components in vivo, specific sequence qualifying peptides were assigned by LC-MS. This approach allows the analysis of enzyme complexes that are not sufficiently stable for application of a larger variety of chromatographic separations, necessary for purification to homogeneity. Applying this method, ZmTSA was identified as alpha-subunit of a tryptophan synthase complex.
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 159 161 145 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 162 164 148 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 176 178 162 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 253 255 235 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 334 335 313 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 436 444 408 416 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 451 452 423 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 458 466 430 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 558 559 523 524 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 563 565 525 527 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 577 579 539 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 650 652 609 611 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 676 677 635 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 57 62 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
###xml 170 175 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
###xml 436 443 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 458 465 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 628 633 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
The apparent molecular weight strongly indicates that in maize tryptophan synthase is functional as alphabetabetaalpha heterotetramer, similar as in bacteria [30,31]. In maize ZmTSA is the principal alpha-subunit of the complex. Catalytic efficiency of ZmTSA was enhanced more than 30-fold by interaction with the beta-subunit (Table 1). Such activating interaction between alpha- and beta-subunit is also well known from bacteria like E. coli (Table 1). In E. coli this activation was mutual, i.e. beta-activity strongly increased upon formation of an alpha2beta2 TS complex [32]. In contrast, no significant activation of the maize beta-subunit by ZmTSA was observed (Table 1). The reason for this difference between the bacterial and plant enzyme is not known.
###end p 72
###begin p 73
###xml 190 192 190 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 193 195 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 204 205 204 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 270 271 270 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 347 348 347 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 354 363 354 363 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 371 372 371 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 604 608 604 608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bx1 </italic>
###xml 623 631 623 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bx1 igl </italic>
###xml 3 8 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
In maize, ancestral TSAs have been recruited for secondary metabolism. The TSA homologs BX1 and IGL catalyze the formation of indole, which functions as DIMBOA precursor or volatile signal [17-19] (Table 1) and BX1 monomer activity was observed in leaf extracts (Figure 4). In addition, TSB dimers that have been observed in leaf extracts (Figure 4) and in vitro (Figure 3), which possibly function in salvage of indole by its conversion to tryptophan. It remains open, whether a mechanism involving BX1 monomer and TSB dimer contributes significantly to the total metabolic flux towards tryptophan. The bx1 mutant and the bx1 igl double mutant (M. Frey, unpublished data) are fully viable; therefore this process is not essential at any stage of development.
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 183 185 183 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 193 195 193 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 203 205 203 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 422 424 422 424 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 557 559 557 559 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mu</italic>
###xml 590 592 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 597 599 597 599 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 676 678 676 678 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 88 93 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
We propose that, despite the availability of the highly active monomers BX1 and IGL, in maize tryptophan is predominantly synthesized through a tryptophan synthase complex containing ZmTSA and ZmTSB1 or ZmTSB2, respectively. Probably this complex has been retained during evolution for tryptophan synthesis, as it enables substrate channelling and allosteric regulation. Knockout mutants could serve as ultimate proof for ZmTSA being essential. Therefore we have extensively screened public databases as well as the large Pioneer HiBred TUSC collection for Mu-transposon insertion mutants [33] in ZmTSA. However, no insertion alleles were identified. It remains open, whether Zmtsa knockout mutants are lethal.
###end p 74
###begin p 75
###xml 213 215 213 215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 246 248 246 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 298 300 298 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 362 363 362 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 397 399 397 399 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 440 442 440 442 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 507 508 507 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 703 705 703 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 723 725 723 725 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 747 754 747 754 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 816 818 812 814 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 862 864 858 860 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 747 754 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Based on import studies, subcellular fractionation, and target sequence prediction, it is suggested that in plants the biosynthesis of aromatic amino acids is predominantly localized in plastids (for review, see [29]). Consistent with these data ZmTSA contains a chloroplast targeting sequence and ZmTSA::GFP fusion proteins were targeted to the plastid (Figure 2). Interestingly, the TSA homolog ZmTSAlike lacks such a transit peptide and ZmTSAlike::GFP fusion proteins were located in the cytosol (Figure 2). It has been debated whether the biosynthesis of aromatic amino acids is also partially active in the cytoplasm, as e.g. a cytoplasmic isoform of chorismate mutase is expressed in Arabidopsis [34]. As recombinant ZmTSAlike, expressed in E. coli, did not show any alpha-activity it remains unclear, whether ZmTSAlike functions as cytosolic TSA isoform. ZmTSAlike might either require specific conditions and modifications or it has an unknown function in plant metabolism.
###end p 75
###begin title 76
Conclusion
###end title 76
###begin p 77
###xml 62 64 62 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Zm</italic>
###xml 175 196 175 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana </italic>
###xml 27 32 <span type="species:ncbi:4577">maize</span>
###xml 175 195 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
Four TSA homologs exist in maize. Only one of these isoforms, ZmTSA, is involved in the formation of a tryptophan synthase complex. Based on our data and previous results for Arabidopsis thaliana we propose that a ubiquitous tryptophan synthase complex is responsible for tryptophan formation in angiosperms, like in fungi and bacteria.
###end p 77
