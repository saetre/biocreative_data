Isocitrate Dehydrogenase of Helicobacter pylori Potentially Induces Humoral Immune Response in Subjects with Peptic Ulcer Disease and Gastritis
###end article-title 0

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 0 9 0 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 401 410 401 410 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 401 410 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
H. pylori causes gastritis and peptic ulcers and is a risk factor for the development of gastric carcinoma. Many of the proteins such as urease, porins, flagellins and toxins such as lipo-polysaccharides have been identified as potential virulence factors which induce proinflammatory reaction. We report immunogenic potentials of isocitrate dehydrogenase (ICD), an important house keeping protein of H. pylori.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 24 33 24 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 56 65 56 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico</italic>
###xml 165 174 165 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 200 207 200 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 332 335 332 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">icd</italic>
###xml 438 447 438 447 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 570 579 570 579 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 660 669 660 669 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 24 33 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 165 174 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 200 207 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 438 447 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 524 529 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 556 564 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 570 579 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 660 669 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
Amino acid sequences of H. pylori ICD were subjected to in silico analysis for regions with predictably high antigenic indexes. Also, computational modelling of the H. pylori ICD as juxtaposed to the E. coli ICD was carried out to determine levels of structure similarity and the availability of surface exposed motifs, if any. The icd gene was cloned, expressed and purified to a very high homogeneity. Humoral response directed against H. pylori ICD was detected through an enzyme linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) in 82 human subjects comprising of 58 patients with H. pylori associated gastritis or ulcer disease and 24 asymptomatic healthy controls. The H. pylori ICD elicited potentially high humoral immune response and revealed high antibody titers in sera corresponding to endoscopically-confirmed gastritis and ulcer disease subjects. However, urea-breath-test negative healthy control samples and asymptomatic control samples did not reveal any detectable immune responses. The ELISA for proinflammatory cytokine IL-8 did not exhibit any significant proinflammatory activity of ICD.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 7 16 7 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 201 210 201 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 7 16 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 201 210 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
ICD of H. pylori is an immunogen which interacts with the host immune system subsequent to a possible autolytic-release and thereby significantly elicits humoral responses in individuals with invasive H. pylori infection. However, ICD could not significantly stimulate IL8 induction in a cultured macrophage cell line (THP1) and therefore, may not be a notable proinflammatory agent.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 0 19 0 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Helicobacter pylori</italic>
###xml 186 189 186 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Linz1">[1]</xref>
###xml 191 194 191 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Akhter1">[2]</xref>
###xml 334 337 334 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Suerbaum1">[3]</xref>
###xml 339 348 339 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 657 666 657 666 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 687 690 687 690 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Akhter1">[2]</xref>
###xml 708 717 708 717 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 854 857 854 857 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Akhter1">[2]</xref>
###xml 0 19 <span type="species:ncbi:210">Helicobacter pylori</span>
###xml 96 101 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 179 185 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 339 348 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 639 645 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">people</span>
###xml 657 666 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 708 717 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
Helicobacter pylori is a Gram negative, curved bacterium that establishes chronic infections in human stomach. It is an important pathogen that is thought to have co-evolved with humans [1], [2]. It inhabits about half of the world population and is considered as a potential risk factor for the development of gastric adenocarcinoma [3]. H. pylori is a recognized cause of chronic gastritis, peptic ulcer disease, gastric carcinoma, and mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) lymphoma. Several mechanisms have been proposed to explain its role in pathogenesis of the gut. Despite high colonization rates only a small subset of infected people experience H. pylori-associated diseases [2]. Associations of H. pylori with disease-specific factors have remained more or less enigmatic even though the genome sequences were deciphered almost a decade ago [2].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 40 49 40 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 145 148 145 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Suerbaum1">[3]</xref>
###xml 261 264 261 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Suerbaum1">[3]</xref>
###xml 40 49 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 125 132 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patient</span>
One of the most distinctive features of H. pylori is the genetic diversity between clinical isolates obtained from different patient populations [3]. Recasting of the genome is the norm with this bacterium, creating thereby a lot of allelic and phase variation [3], posing difficulties in the development of diagnostics and vaccines.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 109 112 109 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Mgraud1">[4]</xref>
###xml 114 117 114 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Mitchell1">[5]</xref>
###xml 238 241 238 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Dunn1">[6]</xref>
###xml 336 345 336 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 582 585 582 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Goossens1">[7]</xref>
###xml 606 615 606 615 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 913 916 913 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Glupczynski1">[8]</xref>
###xml 101 108 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">persons</span>
###xml 336 345 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 606 615 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
Once acquired, the infection persists for years and, elicits detectable immune responses in infected persons [4], [5] characterized by increased levels of specific IgG and IgA in serum and augmentation of secretory IgA and IgM in stomach [6]. Therefore, noninvasive serological tests were recommended and developed for the diagnosis of H. pylori infection. Among these, the enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) is one of the most extensively used method as it is relatively affordable, fast, simple to perform, and could be easily adopted for screening large number of samples [7]. Exploring for anti H. pylori antibodies is often prescribed before endoscopy or before initiating treatment. Although due to immunological memory, serodiagnosis may not be preferred to check the success or outcome of eradication treatment, it remains a choice as a sensitive method probably because of its non-invasive nature [8].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 208 217 208 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 217 220 217 220 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Carroll1">[9]</xref>
###xml 221 225 221 225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-ProuzetMauleon1">[13]</xref>
###xml 278 282 278 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Kauser2">[12]</xref>
###xml 284 288 284 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-ProuzetMauleon1">[13]</xref>
###xml 326 329 326 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">gro</italic>
###xml 469 489 469 489 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Campylobacter jejuni</italic>
###xml 489 493 489 493 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Johansen1">[14]</xref>
###xml 495 499 495 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Newell1">[15]</xref>
###xml 627 636 627 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 733 737 733 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Cover1">[16]</xref>
###xml 738 742 738 742 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Yamaoka1">[18]</xref>
###xml 850 854 850 854 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Cover2">[19]</xref>
###xml 208 217 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 469 489 <span type="species:ncbi:197">Campylobacter jejuni</span>
###xml 627 636 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
Currently, there has not been any single 'gold standard' antigen, which can unambiguously be used across all populations. One of the important reasons for this could be the extraordinary diversity present in H. pylori[9]-[13] and also its specific adaptation in different hosts [12], [13]. Although proteins such as catalase, groEL, and flagellin are strongly immunogenic, they give nonspecific cross-reactions with other Gram-negative bacteria and, more particularly, Campylobacter jejuni[14], [15]. In contrast, virulence linked antigens such as CagA or VacA, show quite a high degree of sequence variability, as there exist H. pylori strains, which are genetically diverse and phenotypically variable for one or both the antigens [16]-[18]. Despite this, satisfactory associations between serum antibodies and invasive disease have been described [19].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 22 31 22 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 146 150 146 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Falush1">[20]</xref>
###xml 427 431 427 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Kimmel1">[21]</xref>
###xml 519 528 519 528 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 22 31 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 519 528 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
Housekeeping genes of H. pylori have been used recently to track population migrations and were found to be stable in the genome across centuries [20]. Housekeeping proteins however, have not been tested by and large for their use in diagnostics or vaccines. The metabolic enzymes seem to be a good candidate to be included into an immuno-diagnostic test kit since they often do not cross-react with sera from healthy controls [21]. Also, owing to their housekeeping function, they are highly conserved among different H. pylori strains irrespective of their genotypes and thus can serve as ideal immuno-diagnostic candidates.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 179 183 179 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Andersen1">[22]</xref>
###xml 184 188 184 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Banerjee1">[24]</xref>
###xml 280 295 280 295 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 408 412 408 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Banerjee1">[24]</xref>
###xml 473 482 473 482 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 601 610 601 610 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 670 674 670 674 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Kimmel1">[21]</xref>
###xml 280 295 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 377 385 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 473 482 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 552 559 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patient</span>
###xml 601 610 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
Proteins that are released from bacteria during late logarithmic growth phase, such as superoxide dismutase and isocitrate dehydrogenase (ICD), are described as autolysis markers [22]-[24] and some of them have been proved to be excellent diagnostic antigens. For example, ICD of M. tuberculosis has been described as a potential antigen to discriminate pulmonary tuberculosis patients from healthy controls [24]. However, previous comparisons of the antigenic patterns of H. pylori proteins (including some of the housekeeping proteins) recognized by patient sera revealed no association of specific H. pylori antigens with cases of particular gastroduodenal pathology [21].
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 67 76 67 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 173 182 173 182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 67 76 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 173 182 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 238 246 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
This study demonstrates interaction of the host immune system with H. pylori ICD in the form of significant humoral responses seen in disease subjects. Further, we describe H. pylori ICD antigen to distinguish ulcer disease and gastritis patients from uninfected healthy individuals.

###end title 16
###begin title 17

In silico modelling, expression and purification of H. pylori ICD protein
###end title 17
###begin p 18

###xml 407 416 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>

###xml 876 885 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 1003 1012 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>

###xml 1170 1177 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Protein sequence of ICD of H. pylori was analyzed to predict its immunogenicity based on antigenic index, hydrophilicity and surface probability. Figure 1A shows in silico antigenic profiles of ICD generated using Protean software (DNAStar Inc. USA). Briefly, several traces corresponding to immunoreactive amino acid motifs with predictably higher antigenic indices (approximately3.4) were identified. The H. pylori ICD thus displayed major antigenic stretches (Figure 1A) prompting us to look at it as a putatively immunogenic protein. The antigenicity indices of ICD were comparable to those previously reported for a PPE antigen Rv2430c of mycobacterial origin, which was further proved to be an immunodominant antigen [25]. The E. coli ICD was also analyzed in a similar manner and appeared to be putatively immunogenic; there was a clear difference found as compared to H. pylori ICD, with respect to the abundance and position of antigenic residues. Later, we constructed a homology model of the H. pylori ICD based on the crystal structure of E. coli ICD (PDB code 1AI2) and were able to portray backbone organization of H. Pylori (26695) ICD along with that of E. coli. The two ICDs were found to be significantly similar (RMS Deviation 0.45) yet differed with respect to 3 important surface exposed loop regions which are likely to constitute the areas of structural differences corresponding to the putative antigenic epitopes, possibly imparting differential antibody responses (Figure 1B).

Humoral immune responses directed against the H. pylori ICD were compared among patients with H. pylori infection (gastritis, NUD, DU and GC) and healthy controls (Figure 3). The immunoreactivity data were statistically analyzed and compared with connection to both infected and healthy sera. These data demonstrated that sera of all of the infected patients belonging to gastritis and duodenal ulcer (DU) categories carried statistically significant (P<0.0001) anti ICD antibody levels as compared to those of the healthy controls. The ICD protein, which has an apparently important metabolic role, was thus found to be able to elicit a strong humoral response in subjects with gastritis and DU. However, in cases of non-ulcer dyspepsia (NUD) and gastric cancer (GC), reduced humoral response was seen. Given these observations, it is likely that the release of ICD protein is associated with active, H. pylori infection. In contrast to observed humoral responses, the recombinant, H. pylori ICD did not induce any significant cellular immune responses as evidenced from lack of secretion of IL-8 from human macrophages induced with recombinant, H. pylori ICD in vitro (Figure 4). We therefore, suggest that ICD excites only the humoral compartment and may not contribute to gastric pathology or cytokine induced changes in gastric physiology.

###begin title 30
Discussion
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 58 67 58 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 267 276 267 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 346 355 346 355 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 433 442 433 442 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 561 565 561 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Kimmel1">[21]</xref>
###xml 771 775 771 775 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Banerjee1">[24]</xref>
###xml 58 67 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 267 276 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 346 355 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 433 442 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
Identification and characterization of highly immunogenic H. pylori proteins is a compulsory requirement for the development of serological tests based on recombinant purified antigens. Combined with the urea breath test, serology is a noninvasive approach to detect H. pylori infection, and several serological test kits based on many different H. pylori antigens are commercially available. Although the humoral immune response to H. pylori seems to be quite variable, combinations of frequently recognized antigens could prove useful for diagnostic purposes [21]. The metabolic enzymes such as ICD seem to be good candidates to be harnessed for serological test kit development mainly because they do not cross-react with sera from non-infected or healthy individuals [24].
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 49 58 49 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 179 183 179 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Kimmel1">[21]</xref>
###xml 251 260 251 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 322 331 322 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 489 498 489 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 49 58 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 251 260 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 322 331 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 361 369 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 489 498 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 508 516 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The significance of many immunogenic proteins of H. pylori including some metabolic enzymes has not been appreciated much in clinical samples in a previous proteomics based study [21]. Our study was however directed to evaluate purified, recombinant, H. pylori ICD for its immunological properties in different classes of H. pylori infected acid peptic disease patients. We attempted to systematically examine the presence and usefulness of immune responses elicited by the ICD protein in H. pylori infected patients.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 38 47 38 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 185 193 185 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001481-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 38 47 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 133 141 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 175 183 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 540 548 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Our analysis using recombinant ICD of H. pylori revealed significantly high antibody titers among peptic ulcer disease and gastritis patients, as compared with NUD and cancer patients (Figure 3). We speculate that the immune response seen in peptic ulcer cases and gastritis could perhaps be due to high loads of bacteria associated with invasive pathology and thereby a higher number of autolysed pathogens or pathogens presenting ICD on the surface. This may possibly explain the comparatively high-antibody response in invasive category patients.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 0 9 0 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 184 188 184 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Cao1">[23]</xref>
###xml 219 228 219 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>

###xml 382 386 382 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Andersen1">[22]</xref>
###xml 388 392 388 392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Banerjee1">[24]</xref>
###xml 394 398 394 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Pitson1">[27]</xref>
###xml 524 532 524 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 543 552 543 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 563 570 563 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 735 744 735 744 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 1025 1029 1025 1029 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Phadnis1">[26]</xref>
###xml 1150 1154 1150 1154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Cao1">[23]</xref>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 219 228 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 543 552 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 735 744 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
H. pylori antigens are described to be released into the extracellular space via multiple mechanisms such as specific secretion pathways, autolysis, and formation of membrane vesicles [23] and the surface properties of H. pylori have been suggested to be unique in allowing adsorption/surface localization of such proteins [26]. ICDs are traditionally known as markers of autolysis [22], [24], [27], released during the late logarithmic phase. The extracellular release of proteins could potentially be occuring only during in vitro growth of H. pylori. However, in vivo existence of such mechanisms, if demonstrated, might be able to explain the role of various secreted proteins in molecular pathology of ulcer disease. The entry of H. pylori proteins into the gastric submucosal space may have important functional consequences such as promotion of proinflammatory and chemotactic responses through release of cytokines from activated macrophages. Also, the extracellular release and or adsorption or surface localization [26] of potentially immunogenic proteins might support a bacterial strategy for diverting an effective local immune response [23]. Keeping these multiple mechanisms in mind, we checked proinflammatory potentials of ICD with the help of an IL-8 ELISA assay. In our observation, ICD did not significantly induce IL-8 secretion in differentiated, cultured macrophages. However, since we did not analyze role of ICD as regards to induction of other proinflammatory cytokines such as TNF-alpha and IL1-beta, it will be premature to justly rule out its involvement in gastric inflammation.
###end p 34

Having shown that H. pylori ICD elicits a humoral response, we investigated the basis of immune specificity of this protein as reflected by structure based modelling. Our sequence analyses and molecular modelling of H. pylori and E. coli ICDs did not reveal complete structural overlaps between the two (Figure 1); several regions of structural dissimilarities were discerned which possibly correspond to the putative antigenic epitopes in H. pylori ICD. Apart from the issues linked to structural conservation or diversity, we hypothesize that higher reactivity of H. pylori ICD as compared to ICD of (commensal) E. coli with the host immune system in vivo could be associated with 1) different invasiveness of H. pylori and E. coli 2) different magnitudes of bacterial load, turnover and autolysis 3) different repertoire of antigen presenting cells (macrophages versus dendritic cells) involved and their relative abundance 4) availability of surface exposed epitopes in H. pylori ICD (as those highlighted in Figure 1B), and 5) presence of active lesions of chronic inflammation, mucosal damage and chemotactic effects of the other H. pylori proteins in the milieu.

###xml 292 300 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 451 460 <span type="species:ncbi:197">C. jejuni</span>
###xml 461 469 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 500 509 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 563 572 <span type="species:ncbi:197">C. jejuni</span>
###xml 634 643 <span type="species:ncbi:197">C. jejuni</span>
###xml 644 652 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 692 701 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
While considering possible cross-reactivity from other invasive bacteria such as C. jejuni, we tested H. pylori ICD against 8 samples of C. jejuni enteritis patient sera and found that it did not cross-react significantly (data not shown). The H. pylori free status of our C. jejuni positive patients was determined based on the absence of symptoms of indigestion, stomach pain and heartburn etc. However, since we used only limited sera samples from C. jejuni patients, we are not inclined to brand H. pylori ICD as a standalone diagnostic antigen. Nonetheless, C. jejuni may not always comprise a much competitive background unless C. jejuni patients themselves are clinically colonized by H. pylori or vice-versa.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 88 97 88 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 299 308 299 308 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 1041 1050 1041 1050 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 1050 1054 1050 1054 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Pitson1">[27]</xref>
###xml 88 97 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 299 308 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 1041 1050 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
Finally, the promising results we obtained in terms of significant humoral responses to H. pylori ICD may not be construed as immediately applicable for field level diagnosis. Nonetheless, it is possible that our findings provide a solid foundation for the development of a sero-diagnostic test for H. pylori associated gastroduodenal pathologies. We suggest the potential of this antigen and its epitope specific peptides may further be analyzed by using a large battery of sera samples representing different disease subjects. Similarly, the status and significance of cross-reacting antibodies in sera corresponding to illnesses caused by bacteria such as Salmonella spp., Shigella spp. and other invasive enteropathogens might also be determined. It will be worthwhile to explore in-depth the involvement of ICD in signaling activities in the submucosal space, specially its interaction with the activated macrophages and lymphocytes. Also, given its important role in the tricarboxylic acid cycle and the absence of glyoxylate shunt in H. pylori[27], the evaluation of ICD as a possible interventional target poses an exciting proposal.
###end p 37
###begin title 38
Materials and Methods
###end title 38
###begin title 39
###xml 36 45 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
In silico antigenicity of predicted H. pylori ICD (HP0023)
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 23 36 23 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori icd</italic>
###xml 23 32 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
Nucleotide sequence of H. pylori icd (ORF HP0023) and the corresponding amino acid sequence was obtained from Pylorigene database () and was subjected to analysis by DNAStar package (DNAStar Inc. Madison, USA). Amino acid sequence of ICD was subjected to scrutiny for any antigenic determinants using Protean program within the DNAStar package. Parameters such as hydrophilicity, surface probability and antigenic index were calculated for the entire protein using this software. Antigenic indices showing more than 2 peaks at >3.0 scales were considered as significantly more antigenic.

###xml 26 35 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
Production of recombinant H. pylori ICD
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 25 38 25 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori icd</italic>

###xml 291 296 291 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 296 300 296 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001481-Banerjee1">[24]</xref>
###xml 430 437 430 437 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 25 34 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 105 114 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 115 122 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patient</span>
###xml 186 193 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 430 437 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The ORF corresponding to H. pylori icd (HP0023, 1.275 kb) was amplified by PCR from the genomic DNA of a H. pylori patient isolate MS8. The recombinant ICD protein was over expressed in E. coli and purified to homogeneity (Figure 2) according to the methods previously described by Banerjee et al[24]. The harvest of purified protein was standardized up to 2.0 mg of purified protein per 500 ml of starting culture of recombinant E. coli. The purified recombinant protein was dialyzed against 20 mM Tris HCl, pH 7.5, with 100 mM NaCl and 3% glycerol. Further, the purified protein was treated with polymixin B to rule out effects of any LPS contamination. The purified recombinant ICD was lyophilized and immediately stored frozen for downstream use in clinical diagnosis.

The study population (n = 82) comprised 58 H. pylori infected patient samples (for details of number of cases analyzed in each category, please see Figure 3) obtained from cases reporting mainly to the Deccan College of Medical Sciences and Allied Hospitals, (DCMS), Hyderabad, and 24 clinically healthy donors. ###begin title 47
ELISA for humoral responses
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 522 531 494 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>
###xml 1059 1060 1015 1016 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">o</italic>
###xml 1147 1148 1103 1104 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1149 1150 1105 1106 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1210 1211 1162 1163 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1213 1214 1165 1166 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 1378 1379 1330 1331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 1412 1413 1364 1365 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 1573 1574 1525 1526 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
###xml 66 72 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 522 531 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
###xml 541 546 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 918 923 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 928 939 <span type="species:ncbi:3704">horseradish</span>
###xml 983 994 <span type="species:ncbi:3704">Horseradish</span>
ELISA assay was performed to check the humoral immune response in humans against recombinant ICD. Briefly, the 96-well microtiter plates were coated with approximately500 ng of recombinant ICD protein. The coated plates were incubated overnight at 4degreesC, washed three times with PBS-T (0.05% Tween 20 in 1x PBS) and twice with PBS. Plates were then blocked with 100 microl of blocking buffer (2% BSA in 1x PBS) at 37degreesC for 2 hrs. The plates were washed three times with wash buffer PBS-T and twice with PBS. The H. pylori-infected human sera belonging to different clinical groups were serially diluted (1:50, 1:100, 1:200 and 1:400) in blocking buffer (1% BSA in PBS), to obtain optimum titers. Diluted sera [50 microl of 1:200 diluted sera] were added to antigen-coated wells and incubated for 1 hr at 37degreesC. The plates were rinsed thrice with PBS-T and twice with PBS and further incubated with anti-human IgG-horseradish peroxidase (Sigma) at 37degreesC for 1 hr. Horseradish peroxidase activity was detected using a chromogenic substance, o-phenylenediamine tetrahydrochloride (Sigma) in citrate-phosphate buffer (pH 5.4) and H2O2 as 1 microl/ml. The reactions were stopped by using 0.5 M H2SO4, and the absorbance values were measured at 492 nm in an ELISA reader (Bio-Rad). Each ELISA was repeated at least twice with all clinical sera isolates. Student's t test was performed to calculate p values based on the means of the serum antibody titers corresponding to healthy and infected classes by using the online scientific calculator of GraphPad (). P values less than 0.05 were considered statistically significant.
###end p 48

###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 254 256 254 256 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;6</sup>
###xml 500 509 496 505 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">H. pylori</italic>

###xml 13 18 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 123 129 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 500 509 <span type="species:ncbi:210">H. pylori</span>
THP-1 cells (human Monocyte, ATCC TIB 202) were grown and maintained in RPMI 1640 (Invitrogen) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, 2 mM Glutamine and 1% anti-bacterial and anti-mycotic solution (Gibco). For macrophage like stage, approximately 1x10-6 cells/well were differentiated using 5 ng/ml Phorbol-12 myristate 13c acetate (PMA, Sigma). Cells were incubated for 24 hours in a humidified atmosphere at 37degreesC. Cells without protein treatment (unstimulated cells) served as negative control. Assay was performed according to the instruction of manufacturer and the cytokine levels were calculated using the recombinant standard provided in the kit. Sensitivity of IL-8 was 3.1 pg/ml. Values were expressed as mean+/- SD.
