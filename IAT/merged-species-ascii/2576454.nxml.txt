Key Role of Splenic Myeloid DCs in the IFN-alphabeta Response to Adenoviruses In Vivo
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###begin p 2
###xml 511 518 490 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1088 1095 1046 1053 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1280 1283 1220 1223 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1634 1641 1553 1560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 290 295 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 318 322 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 409 413 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 625 629 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1284 1288 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The early systemic production of interferon (IFN)-alphabeta is an essential component of the antiviral host defense mechanisms, but is also thought to contribute to the toxic side effects accompanying gene therapy with adenoviral vectors. Here we investigated the IFN-alphabeta response to human adenoviruses (Ads) in mice. By comparing the responses of normal, myeloid (m)DC- and plasmacytoid (p)DC-depleted mice and by measuring IFN-alphabeta mRNA expression in different organs and cells types, we show that in vivo, Ads elicit strong and rapid IFN-alphabeta production, almost exclusively in splenic mDCs. Using knockout mice, various strains of Ads (wild type, mutant and UV-inactivated) and MAP kinase inhibitors, we demonstrate that the Ad-induced IFN-alphabeta response does not require Toll-like receptors (TLR), known cytosolic sensors of RNA (RIG-I/MDA-5) and DNA (DAI) recognition and interferon regulatory factor (IRF)-3, but is dependent on viral endosomal escape, signaling via the MAP kinase SAPK/JNK and IRF-7. Furthermore, we show that Ads induce IFN-alphabeta and IL-6 in vivo by distinct pathways and confirm that IFN-alphabeta positively regulates the IL-6 response. Finally, by measuring TNF-alpha responses to LPS in Ad-infected wild type and IFN-alphabetaR-/- mice, we show that IFN-alphabeta is the key mediator of Ad-induced hypersensitivity to LPS. These findings indicate that, like endosomal TLR signaling in pDCs, TLR-independent virus recognition in splenic mDCs can also produce a robust early IFN-alphabeta response, which is responsible for the bulk of IFN-alphabeta production induced by adenovirus in vivo. The signaling requirements are different from known TLR-dependent or cytosolic IFN-alphabeta induction mechanisms and suggest a novel cytosolic viral induction pathway. The hypersensitivity to components of the microbial flora and invading pathogens may in part explain the toxic side effects of adenoviral gene therapy and contribute to the pathogenesis of adenoviral disease.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Author Summary
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 564 569 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Adenoviruses (Ads) are important pathogens and promising vectors for gene therapy applications. In the course of adenoviral infections innate immune responses are activated, which can be beneficial for the antiviral host defense but also detrimental if activated in a deregulated manner. Type I IFNs are crucial for the innate immune control of various viral infections in the mammalian host. So far, the early, systemic release of IFN-alphabeta during viral infections has been attributed to specialized immune cells, the plasmacytoid dendritic cells. Here, in a mouse infection model, we show that wild type Ads, as well as adenoviral vectors, elicit rapid IFN-alphabeta production almost exclusively in another cell population, the splenic myeloid dendritic cells. This IFN-alphabeta storm depends on viral escape from endosomes to the cytosol and the requirements of the response are suggestive of a novel viral induction pathway. Furthermore, we show that virus induced IFN-alphabeta is the key mediator of Ad-induced hypersensitivity to the cytokine-inducing and toxic activity of lipopolysaccharide, a common constituent of Gram-negative bacteria. Since these bacteria comprise several commensals and pathogens, enhanced susceptibility to lipopolysaccharide may contribute to toxic reactions observed during adenoviral gene therapy and to the clinical symptoms of adenoviral diseases.
###end p 4

Introduction
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 111 114 111 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Kojaoghlanian1">[1]</xref>

###xml 446 449 446 449 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Shenk1">[3]</xref>
###xml 450 453 450 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Ginsberg1">[4]</xref>
###xml 620 623 620 623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Shenk1">[3]</xref>
###xml 624 627 624 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Greber1">[5]</xref>
###xml 41 47 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 116 121 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 259 264 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 275 280 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 415 420 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Adenoviruses (Ads) cause mild disease in humans, but are hazardous pathogens in immuno-compromised individuals [1]. Human Ads are dsDNA viruses grouped into six species. Species A, C, D, E, and F and species B Ads use different infectious entry pathways [2]. Human Ads enter mouse cells and express their early genes; however, the virus genome is not replicated and no viral progeny is made during the infection of mouse cells in vitro or in vivo[3],[4]. Furthermore, early viral gene expression can be abolished by UV-inactivation and well-defined mutants with defects of viral early genes or viral entry are available [3],[5]. Thus, the effects elicited by different components of the virus-host interaction preceding viral replication can be accurately evaluated.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 60 68 60 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 102 109 102 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 256 259 256 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Muruve1">[6]</xref>
###xml 260 263 260 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Raper1">[7]</xref>
###xml 569 572 565 568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Muruve1">[6]</xref>
###xml 573 576 569 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Raper1">[7]</xref>
###xml 577 580 573 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Mistchenko1">[8]</xref>
###xml 581 584 577 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Shayakhmetov1">[9]</xref>
###xml 585 589 581 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Zhu1">[10]</xref>
Ads transduce many different cell types and can be produced in vitro in sufficiently high amounts for in vivo administration. While these properties make them attractive for gene therapy applications, they can also trigger a severe systemic toxic reaction [6],[7]. Upregulation of inflammatory mediators, including cytokines and chemokines such as IL-1, IL-6, IL-8, IL-12, macrophage inhibitory protein-1/2, tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF) and recently also type I IFN has been observed in experimental and clinical infections with wt as well as with recombinant Ads [6],[7],[8],[9],[10].
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 208 212 201 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Pestka1">[11]</xref>
###xml 469 473 448 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-GarciaSastre1">[12]</xref>
###xml 474 478 453 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Honda1">[13]</xref>
###xml 479 483 458 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Stetson1">[14]</xref>
Type I IFNs represent one of the host's most important antiviral defense mechanisms. The type I IFN family comprises different IFN-alpha subtypes, a single IFN-beta and other less well characterized proteins [11]. All IFN-alpha species and IFN-beta interact with the same IFN-alphabeta cellular receptor, the activation of which mediates a wide range of direct and indirect innate antiviral or antimicrobial effects and modulates the antiviral adaptive immune response [12],[13],[14].
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 161 165 161 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-GarciaSastre1">[12]</xref>
###xml 166 170 166 170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Honda1">[13]</xref>
###xml 171 175 171 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Stetson1">[14]</xref>
###xml 176 180 176 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Ishii1">[15]</xref>
###xml 355 359 355 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-KurtJones1">[16]</xref>
###xml 360 364 360 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Jude1">[17]</xref>
###xml 365 369 365 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Jiang1">[18]</xref>
###xml 485 488 485 488 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">via</italic>
###xml 1151 1155 1134 1138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Honda1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1156 1160 1139 1143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Ishii1">[15]</xref>
###xml 1344 1348 1320 1324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Liu1">[19]</xref>
###xml 1349 1353 1325 1329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-AsselinPaturel1">[20]</xref>
###xml 1354 1358 1330 1334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Colonna1">[21]</xref>
At present, two main mechanisms of type I IFN induction by viruses resulting from the extracytoplasmic or cytoplasmic virus recognition, respectively, are known [12],[13],[14],[15]. The extracytoplasmic induction is initiated by triggering the surface-expressed transmembrane protein toll-like receptor (TLR) 4 with certain non-nucleic viral constituents [16],[17],[18] or upon recognition of viral nucleic acids in the endosomes of specialized cells (dendritic cells and macrophages) via different members of the TLR family. These include TLR3, TLR7/TLR8 and TLR9, sensing dsRNA, ssRNA and CpG DNA, respectively. For IFN-alphabeta induction TLR3 and TLR4 signal through the adaptor molecule TIR domain-containing adaptor protein inducing interferon beta (TRIF). This results in the activation of interferon regulatory factor (IRF)-3. TLR7, 8 and 9 signal through the adaptor molecule Myeloid differentiation factor 88 (MyD88). An important result of the MyD88-mediated pathway is the activation of IRF-7 (but not of IRF-3), which together with the transcription factors NF-kB and AP-1 initiates the induction of both the IFN-alpha and IFN-beta genes [13],[15]. This induction pathway is responsible for the strong, early IFN-alphabeta response to several replicating and inactivated viruses in pDCs, which express preferentially TLR7 and TLR9 [19],[20],[21].
###end p 9

###xml 106 110 99 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Sun1">[22]</xref>
###xml 111 115 104 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Kato1">[23]</xref>
###xml 372 376 365 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Kato1">[23]</xref>
###xml 377 381 370 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Yoneyama1">[24]</xref>
###xml 382 386 375 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Gitlin1">[25]</xref>
###xml 387 391 380 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Pichlmair1">[26]</xref>
###xml 392 396 385 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Hornung1">[27]</xref>
###xml 397 401 390 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Hornung2">[28]</xref>
###xml 1055 1059 1028 1032 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Honda2">[29]</xref>
###xml 1060 1064 1033 1037 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Sato1">[30]</xref>
###xml 1065 1069 1038 1042 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Erlandsson1">[31]</xref>
###xml 1238 1246 1211 1219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1246 1250 1219 1223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Diebold1">[32]</xref>
###xml 1251 1255 1224 1228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Hochrein1">[33]</xref>
###xml 1266 1273 1239 1246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
The "classical" cytosolic pathway is the major IFN-alphabeta producing mechanism in cells other than pDCs [22],[23]. Signal transduction leading to type I IFN gene induction is initiated by the recognition of intracellular virus-associated molecular patterns. dsRNA and 5'-triphosphate RNA produced during viral replication are sensed by the RNA helicases RIG-I and MDA-5 [23],[24],[25],[26],[27],[28]. This pathway has been extensively studied, mainly in virus-infected fibroblasts. Triggering of the aforementioned RNA helicases leads to the activation of the transcription factors NF-kB, IRF-3 and IRF-7 that are important for the induction of IFN-alphabeta and proinflammatory cytokines, including IL-6. In the cytosolic pathway, type I IFN gene induction is a sequential event and both IRF-3 and IRF-7 were shown to be important in the early phase when mostly IFN-beta is produced. The late phase of the IFN-alphabeta response is regulated by positive feedback via the increased levels of IRF-7 elicited by IFN-beta production during the early phase [29],[30],[31]. In addition to fibroblasts, the potential of mDCs and macrophages to produce significant amounts of type I IFNs in response to viral replication has been demonstrated in vitro[32],[33]; however, in vivo the specific contribution of these cells to systemic levels of IFN-alphabeta is not well documented.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 60 76 60 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">L. monocytogenes</italic>
###xml 207 211 204 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Ishii1">[15]</xref>
###xml 212 216 209 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Stockinger1">[34]</xref>
###xml 217 221 214 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-OConnell1">[35]</xref>
###xml 222 226 219 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Stetson2">[36]</xref>
###xml 227 231 224 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Ishii2">[37]</xref>
###xml 232 236 229 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Leber1">[38]</xref>
###xml 347 351 344 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Stetson1">[14]</xref>
###xml 352 356 349 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Ishii1">[15]</xref>
###xml 524 528 521 525 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Takaoka1">[39]</xref>
###xml 674 678 671 675 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Wang1">[40]</xref>
###xml 843 850 840 847 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 896 904 893 901 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 904 908 901 905 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Ishii3">[41]</xref>
###xml 60 76 <span type="species:ncbi:1639">L. monocytogenes</span>
###xml 639 644 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 735 739 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Recently, detection of bacterial DNA in cells infected with L. monocytogenes and recognition of transfected B-DNA has been shown to trigger IFN-beta production. This type of response strictly requires IRF-3 [15],[34],[35],[36],[37],[38]. Such a sensing system has been suggested to represent a further mechanism of cytosolic DNA virus recognition [14],[15] and the Z-DNA binding protein 1 (Zbp1, also referred as DNA-dependent activator of IFN regulatory factors, DAI) was shown to be a candidate DNA sensor in this pathway [39]. Notably however, in a follow-up study the same group found a critical role for DAI in L-929 cells but not in mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs) [40]. Furthermore, recent experiments with Zbp1/DAI knockout mice did not show the essential role of Zbp1/DAI in the induction of innate and adaptive responses to B-DNA in vivo and in macrophages, dendritic cells and MEFs in vitro[41].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 74 78 74 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Zhu1">[10]</xref>
###xml 79 83 79 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Huarte1">[42]</xref>
###xml 556 563 535 542 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1127 1130 1092 1095 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">via</italic>
###xml 43 47 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 413 417 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 519 523 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1585 1589 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The induction of type I IFN in Ad-infected mice has been recently studied [10],[42] and associated with both the extracytoplasmic and intracytoplasmic pathways. It was claimed that a part of the IFN-alphabeta response is initiated by TLR9 and MyD88 signaling in pDCs and another part by cytosolic DNA recognition in non-pDCs. However, the identification of IFN-alphabeta producing cell types directly in infected mice was not carried out. In the present study we investigated the IFN-alphabeta responses of Ad-infected mice and showed that the bulk of the in vivo induced IFN-alphabeta is produced by splenic mDCs. Furthermore, we found that TLRs, including TLR9 play no major role. The Ad-elicited IFN-alphabeta response required viral endosomal escape, suggesting a cytosolic induction pathway. Surprisingly however, the induction was independent of IRF-3 and dependent on stress-activated protein kinase/c-Jun NH2-terminal kinase (SAPK/JNK) activity, which is in contrast to the known induction mechanism initiated through cytosolic DNA recognition. Instead, the induction required IRF-7, and a positive feedback regulation via the type I IFN receptor. Although this does not exclude a role for cytosolic nucleic acid sensors, our data do not support the involvement of MDA-5, RIG-I and Zbp1/DAI in the induction of the IFN-alphabeta response to Ad. Furthermore, our results reveal distinct mechanisms in the induction of IFN-alphabeta and IL-6 by Ad. Finally, we show that Ad-induced IFN-alphabeta is a key mediator of hypersensitivity to bacterial lipopolysaccharides in infected mice. Enhanced susceptibility to LPS and to other microbial inducers of inflammation may contribute to toxic reactions observed during adenoviral gene therapy and to the clinical symptoms of adenoviral diseases.

In order to characterize the induction of type I IFNs by Ad in vivo, we first examined how two types of human Ad, Ad3 (species B) and Ad R700 (species C) [2] known to use distinct infectious entry routes, elicit an IFN-alphabeta response in vivo. According to published data, the CD11c+ cell population contains different types of DCs, as well as other cells such as lymphocytes, macrophages, granulocytes and NK cells [51],[52],[53]. Here, we studied the possible involvement of IFN feedback signaling in the IFN-alphabeta and IL-6 response to Ad3 by using wt and IFN-alphabetaR-deficient mice. The difference between the protein or mRNA levels of IFN-alpha and IFN-beta in wt versus mutant animals was approximately 100 and 20-fold, respectively. Furthermore, in contrast to wt mice, the characteristic rise in IFN-alphabeta levels between 4 and 8 h after infection was absent in IFN-alphabetaR-/- mice. Thus, in Ad-infected mice, production of both IFN-alpha and IFN-beta is strictly dependent on positive IFN-alphabeta feedback. The determination of IL-6 protein and mRNA levels in the same plasma and splenic tissue samples revealed that the induction of IL-6 is also positively regulated by IFN-alphabeta signaling (Fig. 5C), which is in agreement with a previous study [10].

We also tested whether IFN-alphabetaR-dependent signaling is involved in the IFN-alphabeta response of Ad-infected BMDCs in vitro. The transcription factors IRF-3 and IRF-7 have distinct and important roles in IFN-alphabeta production induced by viruses or other pathogens and their involvement can be characteristic for the induction mechanisms involved [13]. Specifically, IRF-3 has been shown to be critically involved in cytoplasmic DNA sensing and in the Ad-induced IFN-alphabeta production in BMMs in vitro[63]. In order to analyze the individual contribution of IRF-3 and IRF-7 to the Ad-induced IFN-alphabeta response in vivo, we infected mice deficient for these transcription factors with Ad3. We also compared these responses to those triggered by poly I:C, a known activator of the cytosolic IFN-alphabeta producing pathway in vivo. As shown in Fig. 7B, the lack of IRF-3 did not significantly influence the plasma levels of IFNalphabeta 4 or 8 h after infection in response to Ad. Thus, IRF-7, but not IRF-3, is essential for the induction of the IFN-alphabeta response during Ad infection. Compared to wt mice, poly I:C injected IRF-7 deficient mice produced significantly less, but still well detectable amounts of IFN-alphabeta.

During the course of our adenovirus preparations, we regularly found "empty capsids" which we separated and purified in addition to the mature virions. We tested the IFN-alphabeta stimulating activity of these preparations in vivo and observed that they were not active (Fig. S12A). Since empty capsids lack viral DNA and exhibit an altered protein composition [69], the absence of an inducing viral constituent(s) from these capsids could explain their inability to provoke an IFN-alphabeta response. Another possible explanation could be that endosomal escape is required for IFN-alphabeta induction, since empty capsids cannot escape from the endosome [69]. To test the latter possibility, we infected mice with 3.6x1010 viral particles of wt Ad2 and Ad2Ts1, a viral mutant deficient in endosomal escape [5]. As shown in Fig. 10A, in contrast to the wt virus, Ad2Ts1 did not induce detectable levels of type I IFN at 4 and 8 h after infection. Furthermore, the IL-6 response was also severely reduced in these animals (Fig. S12B). In a control experiment, mice infected with Ad2Ts1 grown at permissive temperature (32degreesC) and thus capable of endosomal escape, exhibited normal IFN-alphabeta responses (Fig. 10B). The inability of Ad2Ts1 to escape from endosomes of mDCs was confirmed by electron microscopy (Fig. 10E-H) and in vivo by the lack of Ad early E1A gene expression in the spleen of mice infected with Ad2Ts1 (Fig. 10I). Thus, escape from the endosome is critical for the induction of IFN-alphabeta and IL-6 by adenoviruses.

It should be noted that a further increase in the Ad2Ts1 dose (2.16x1011 particles) resulted in detectable, albeit very low levels of plasma IFN-alphabeta that were released with different kinetics (Fig. 10C). In this case, IFN-alphabeta was detectable as early as 2 h after infection and, in contrast to the results we obtained with wt viruses (Figures 1A, 10A and S1A), the levels of IFN-alphabeta did not increase significantly at the later time-points. This already suggests a mechanism for type I IFN induction by Ad2Ts1 that is fundamentally different from the IFN-alphabeta induction seen with wt Ad2. Since Ads are DNA viruses, they can possibly be detected by TLR9. In fact, the innate immune recognition of Ad in pDCs is TLR9 dependent. We therefore repeated the experiment with a high dose of Ad2Ts1 using TLR9-/- mice. As shown in Fig. 10C, TLR9-/- mice did not produce IFN-alphabeta in response to the mutant Ad2Ts1, quite in contrast to the results obtained with the wt virus (see Fig. 4A) Likewise, there was no detectable IFN-alphabeta release in Ad2Ts1-infected mice deficient in MyD88, an essential component of TLR9 signaling (not shown). Furthermore, the corresponding amount of empty particles (DNA-free) of Ad2Ts1 elicited no IFN-alphabeta response in wt mice (Fig. 10C). These data illustrate the critical role of TLR9 in the induction of IFN-alphabeta by means of high doses of Ad2Ts1. To exclude the possibility that contaminating DNA on the surface of the virions was responsible for the TLR9-dependent IFN-alphabeta induction, we treated the mutant virions with bensonase, which destroys all kinds of free nucleic acids. Bensonase-treated Ad2Ts1 still induced an IFN-alphabeta response in mice (not shown) supporting the view that endogenous viral and not contaminating DNA is responsible for IFN-alphabeta induction by the Ad trapped in the endosomes.

We also tested the role of endosomal escape of Ad in the in vitro induction in BMDCs of IFN-alphabeta and IL-6. Fig. 10D shows a dose dependent production of IFN-alphabeta by Ad2Ts1 grown at permissive temperature and Figures S12C and D the production of IFN-alphabeta and IL-6 by wt Ad2, but no production of either of the cytokines by Ad2Ts1 grown at the restrictive temperature. Interestingly, liposomal transfection of whole Ad2 Ts1 virions (but not of empty virus particles) in BMDCs resulted in the significant production of IFN-alphabeta that however, was critically dependent on IRF-3 (Fig. 7A). Similarly, Ad2 Ts1 did not induce IFN-alphabeta production in human monocyte derived DCs (Fig. S12E). The requirement of low pH for Ad3 infection was also tested using bafilomycin A1, a drug known to inhibit the acidification of endosomes. Experiments shown in Fig. S13A, B revealed that cells treated with this drug produced significantly reduced levels of IFN-alphabeta and IL-6 respectively, in response to Ad3. Similar results were obtained using treatment with ammonium chloride, another acidification inhibitor known to block Ad escape from endosomes (not shown). Collectively, the data in vitro and in vivo provide evidence that the late phase of the Ad infectious entry, in which the virus escapes from the endosome, triggers an innate response characterized by the production of type I IFNs and IL-6.

Induction of type I IFNs was shown to be critical for some innate immune responses to Ads [10].We therefore investigated whether a characteristic consequence of infection with Ad, the induction of LPS hypersensitivity [72], might be mediated by type I IFNs. For this purpose, we infected wt and IFNalphabetaR-/- mice with Ads, challenged them 16 h later with LPS and measured the TNF-alpha response. Non-infected LPS-treated mice served as a control. Unlike the infected wt mice, the Ad3-infected IFNalphabetaR-/- mice did not exhibit enhanced responses to LPS (Fig. 11A). An increased expression of the LPS receptor complex on target cells, may contribute to the enhanced reactivity to LPS [73],[74]. We therefore investigated whether macrophages of Ad infected mice overexpress the receptor components mCD14 and TLR4/MD-2. We found that splenic macrophages from Ad infected mice overexpress mCD14 in an IFN-alphabeta dependent manner (Fig. 11D) but the expression of TLR4/MD-2 was only minimally affected (Fig. S14D). Furthermore, we found increased acetylation levels of histone H4 at the TNF-alpha promoter in Ad-infected wt, but not in IFNalphabetaR-deficient macrophages (Fig. 11E). This suggests that Ad-induced IFN-alphabeta increases LPS-induced TNF-alpha production, at least in part by epigenetic changes at the TNF-alpha promoter.

###begin title 67
Discussion
###end title 67
###begin p 68
###xml 67 71 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 86 91 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 799 803 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In the present study we investigated the IFN-alphabeta response of mice infected with human Ads. We showed that the response is characterized by high levels of IFN-alpha and IFN-beta, which are produced simultaneously and almost exclusively by splenic mDCs. Furthermore, the response is entirely independent of viral replication, TLR-, MyD88-, TRIF- and IRF-3-signaling, but dependent on viral escape from the endosomes, activation of the SAPK/JNK pathway and IRF-7, and subsequent IFN-alphabeta feedback signaling. These data suggest an IFN-alphabeta induction pathway that is different from the extracytoplasmic and cytoplasmic pathways so far described. Finally, we show that IFN-alphabeta is a key mediator of the hypersensitivity to bacterial lipopolysaccharide, which develops, in Ad-infected mice.

###xml 1592 1596 1532 1536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Dalod2">[79]</xref>
###xml 1597 1601 1537 1541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Prakash1">[80]</xref>
###xml 220 224 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 301 305 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 339 343 <span type="species:ncbi:10366?0.7619047619047619">MCMV</span>
###xml 355 358 <span type="species:ncbi:11138?0.8024691358024691">MHV</span>
###xml 643 647 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 784 788 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 946 950 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1027 1031 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1235 1239 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
As shown previously and in this study, a wide spectrum of cells including pDCs, mDCs and macrophages [10],[45],[46],[63] produce IFN-alphabeta in response to Ad in vitro. The present finding that in vivo, in Ad-infected mice, splenic mDCs are the major source of IFN-alphabeta is surprising, since in mice infected with different viruses (MCMV, HSV, VSV, MHV, influenza, vaccinia and Sendai viruses) [27],[57],[58],[59],[75],[76],[77],[78] pDCs activated by the TLRs constitute the major contributors to the systemic levels of type I IFNs. In the present study the expression levels of IFN-alphabeta mRNAs in organs and cells from Ad-infected mice suggested a dominant role for splenic mDCs in the IFN-alphabeta response. Furthermore, the IFN response to Ad was practically absent in mice depleted of CD11chigh MHC II+ myeloid DCs. Also, there was a striking similarity between the IFN-alphabeta subtypes induced by Ads in the spleen of infected mice and those induced in mDC cultures in vitro. It should be emphasized that in mice infected with VSV, in which splenic pDCs are the main IFN producers, the spectrum of in vivo induced IFN-alpha subtypes was markedly different [76]. Finally, the finding that the response of Ad-infected mice was completely independent of TLR signaling and strongly dependent on IFN-alphabeta feedback provide further arguments against a major role for pDCs. In a number of viral infection models, IFN-alpha and -beta production by pDCs was mediated by TLR7/9 [13],[19],[23] and was at least partially independent of a positive IFN-alphabeta feedback [76],[77],[79],[80].

###xml 1421 1425 1396 1400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Blasius2">[81]</xref>
###xml 157 161 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 251 255 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 795 799 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 945 949 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1416 1420 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In variance to our data, pDCs and various types of non-pDCs [10],[42] were suggested to be responsible for the type I IFN responses to adenoviral vectors in mice. In [10] the loss of TLR9 signaling resulted in a reduction of the IFN-alpha response in mice infected with the recombinant species C Ad-lacZ [10]. This finding suggested a significant contribution of TLR9, and therefore of pDCs, to the Ad-induced IFN-alphabeta response in vivo. The ratio of pDCs to mDCs (approximately 1:1) in the spleen of animals used in the above study was quite different from that we and others [50] have found (approximately 1:10). This may explain the conflicting results on the role of TLR9 in vivo, between this previous study and ours. In another study [42] the induction of IFN-alphabeta was studied in mice with an artificially enlarged pool of DCs (due to prior pretreatment with sFLT-3L), 24 h after recombinant Ad administration. In our study naive mice were used and 24 h after Ad infection the levels of IFN-alphabeta were already below the detection limit. Of note also, the bone marrow stromal antigen 2 (BST2) (that is recognized by the PDCA-1 antibody used for isolation of pDCs in [42]), was shown to be up-regulated on numerous cell types following stimulation that triggers an IFN response [81]. Thus, the use of the PDCA-1 antibody for the isolation of pDCs seems to be more reliable in the case of uninfected mice [81].

###xml 1665 1669 1598 1602 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Zhu1">[10]</xref>
###xml 253 257 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 932 936 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1036 1040 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1574 1578 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Recently, an absolute IRF-3 dependency of the in vitro IFN-alphabeta response of bone marrow derived macrophages to Ad has been reported [63]. In our study IRF-3 deficiency had no significant effect on the levels of IFN-alphabeta induced in Ad-infected mice. In addition, in Ad-infected macrophages, the relatively low level of IFN-alpha4 mRNA and its negative regulation by the autocrine feedback was different from our in vivo findings (strong induction and positive regulation by feedback). This is in agreement with the concept that in vivo macrophages make no significant contribution to the IFN-alphabeta response to Ad. A likely explanation is that the induction of IFN-alphabeta in macrophages requires the high numbers of virions used in vitro, and that such multiplicities were never reached in vivo. Interestingly however, in contrast to IFN-alphabeta induction, the induction of IL-6 proceeded in the spleen of infected mice in both DC and non-DC populations. In accordance, the depletion of IFN-alphabeta-producing mDCs in mice prior to infection lowered, but did not entirely prevent the IL-6 response. It is possible that the pathways leading to the induction of IFN-alphabeta and IL-6 by Ad are different, at least in different cell types. Alternatively, in vivo, part of the IL-6 formed is induced indirectly via secondary mechanisms. In this context it is interesting that blockade of the p38 MAPK in mDCs in vitro had no effect on Ad-induced IFN-alphabeta production, but partially inhibited the production of IL-6. Our data showing that IFNalphabetaR-/- mice exhibit strongly impaired IL-6 responses to Ad is in agreement with a previous report [10] and shows that IFN-alphabeta is a positive regulator of IL-6 production. Moreover, our data indicate that this effect could be explained at least in part by the positive regulatory effect of IFN-alphabeta on Ad-induced IL-6 transcription and is achieved by the alteration of the chromatin structure at the IL-6 promoter.

###begin title 73
Requirements for IFN-alpha/beta induction by adenoviruses and its relation to known induction pathways
###end title 73
###begin p 74
###xml 346 349 332 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Greber1">[5]</xref>
###xml 879 883 851 855 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Honda3">[82]</xref>
###xml 996 1003 961 968 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 93 97 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The experiments carried out in this study in MyD88-, TRIF-, Unc93B and various TLR-deficient mice excluded a participation of TLRs in the IFN-alphabeta responses to Ads, including the recombinant Ad5-GFP. The only exception was the TLR9- and MyD88-dependent IFN-alphabeta response elicited by Ad2Ts1, a mutant virus deficient in endosomal escape [5]. However, compared to all other Ads used in this study, Ad2Ts1 induced very low levels of IFN-alphabeta, with faster kinetics and only when used in very high amounts. This is consistent with the finding that the mechanisms of type I IFN induction by Ad2Ts1 and the other adenoviruses are not the same. We suggest that the fast escape of Ads from the endosome circumvents activation of endosomal TLRs. The requirement for a longer endosomal retention time in TLR9-dependent IFN-alphabeta production has been recently demonstrated [82]. On the whole, our experiments indicate that Ad endosomal escape is required for the induction of IFN-alphabeta in vivo and suggest a cytosolic pathway. The same requirement was ascertained in the present study for the IL-6 response.

###xml 654 658 633 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Takaoka1">[39]</xref>
###xml 768 776 740 748 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 776 780 748 752 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Nociari1">[63]</xref>
###xml 1482 1486 1433 1437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Mangel1">[83]</xref>
###xml 1612 1623 1563 1574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-1000208-g010">Fig. 10F, H</xref>
###xml 1887 1890 1831 1834 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Greber1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1067 1071 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Sensors of nucleic acids are powerful initiators of the TLR-independent cytosolic IFN-alphabeta induction [12],[14],[15]. We excluded in this study a major involvement of the cytoplasmic RNA sensors RIG-I and MDA-5 in the induction of IFN-alphabeta by Ads. Likewise, our study does not support a major participation of the cytosolic DNA sensor DAI/Zbp1 in this induction either. However, our study did not formally exclude the existence of all potentially redundant recognition pathways. So far reported, the pathway(s) of IFN-alphabeta induction activated by cytosolic DNA is (are) strictly dependent on IRF-3 and minimally on IRF-7 [34],[35],[36],[37],[39]. IRF-3 was also reported to be essential for the adenoviral DNA-dependent induction of IFN-alphabeta in BMMs in vitro[63]. In agreement, in this study we show that transfection of BMDCs with naked adenoviral DNA or with whole virions of the endosomal escape deficient Ad2Ts1 results in a strictly IRF-3 dependent IFN-alphabeta response. Evidently however, the IFN-alphabeta response of Ad-infected BMDCs and mice is independent of IRF-3. These findings do not exclude a requirement for dsDNA recognition in the induction of IFN-alphabeta, but suggest a different induction mechanism and show the importance of cellular compartmentalization during normal Ad entry. A possible important factor involved in cytosolic Ad sensing might be the adenoviral cysteine protease L3/23, whose activation requires the presence of Ad DNA [83]. This assumption is supported by our finding that a small fraction of the protease-lacking Ad2Ts1 still reaches the cytosol (Fig. 10F, H), but is devoid of any IFN-alphabeta-inducing activity. This enzyme, apart from its involvement in maturation of viral proteins and endosomal escape, is essential for the stepwise disassembly of Ads in the cytosol and the release of viral DNA at the nuclear pore [5].
###end p 75
###begin p 76
###xml 306 310 292 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Honda2">[29]</xref>
###xml 74 78 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The absolute requirement of IRF-7 for type I IFN induction in Ad-infected mice shows that this transcription factor participates not only in the positive IFN-alphabeta feedback, but also in the initial IFN-alphabeta production and indeed plays a master role in the regulation of type I interferon response [29].
###end p 76
###begin p 77
###xml 121 129 114 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 134 141 127 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 262 266 248 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Stetson1">[14]</xref>
###xml 267 271 253 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Ishii1">[15]</xref>
###xml 272 276 258 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Stetson2">[36]</xref>
###xml 456 460 439 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Cheng1">[84]</xref>
###xml 513 517 496 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Sun1">[22]</xref>
###xml 518 522 501 505 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Ishii2">[37]</xref>
###xml 523 527 506 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Cheng1">[84]</xref>
###xml 528 532 511 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Kumar1">[85]</xref>
###xml 435 440 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 498 504 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 619 623 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
A further finding of the present study is the essential role for the MAPK SAPK/JNK in the IFN-alphabeta induction by Ads in vitro and in vivo, although the DNA-mediated cytosolic induction of IFN-alphabeta has been reported to be independent of MAPKs activation [14],[15],[36]. A cytosolic dsDNA-signaling pathway, mediated by the RNA helicase RIG-I and MAVS, and leading to the induction of IFN-beta has been recently demonstrated in human hepatoma cells [84]. This induction pathway is absent in murine systems [22],[37],[84],[85] and is therefore unlikely to participate in the IFN-alphabeta response of Ad-infected mice.
###end p 77
###begin p 78
###xml 376 380 362 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Honda1">[13]</xref>
###xml 381 385 367 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Stetson2">[36]</xref>
###xml 386 390 372 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Ishii2">[37]</xref>
###xml 155 160 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 221 226 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
On the whole, our findings do not support the participation of any known nucleic acid-mediated mechanisms in the elicitation of IFN-alphabeta responses to human Ads. In this context it is interesting that, as shown here, mouse embryonal fibroblasts do not produce IFN-alphabeta upon Ad infection, although they posses efficient cytosolic induction pathways for dsRNA or dsDNA [13],[36],[37], the latter shown also in this study. Rather, our data support the possibility that the IFN-alphabeta response to Ads occurs via a novel, not yet characterized cytosolic DNA or protein recognition pathway. Further studies are required to identify the viral components and the host receptors involved. We also emphasize that the known extra- and intra-cytosolic induction pathways may contribute to the IFN-alphabeta response in a host in which Ads replicate and free viral dsRNA and dsDNA are generated.
###end p 78
###begin p 79
###xml 504 508 469 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Look1">[86]</xref>
###xml 67 71 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 273 277 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
As mentioned above, the production of IFN-alphabeta in Ad-infected mice is strongly dependent on IFN-alphabeta feedback signaling. Inhibition of positive IFN-alphabeta feedback is a likely explanation for the negative regulation of the IFN-alphabeta response, observed, in mice infected with intact Ads (expressing early genes) in this study. Likely candidates for negative regulators of the IFN-alphabeta production are the E1A proteins. Previously, they were shown to inhibit Stat1 signal transduction [86], which plays a role in positive feedback signaling by means of the IFN-alphabeta receptor.
###end p 79
###begin title 80
Adenoviruses, toxic effects and therapeutic applications
###end title 80
###begin p 81
###xml 306 309 306 309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Muruve1">[6]</xref>
###xml 310 313 310 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Raper1">[7]</xref>
###xml 314 317 314 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Shayakhmetov1">[9]</xref>
###xml 443 447 443 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Fejer2">[72]</xref>
###xml 558 566 558 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 938 942 920 924 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Thomas1">[87]</xref>
###xml 943 947 925 929 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Freudenberg1">[88]</xref>
###xml 131 135 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 533 540 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patient</span>
###xml 644 649 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 719 727 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Because the adenoviral vectors used in this study correspond to some of those used in gene therapy trials, the present findings in mice may have important implications for Ad gene therapy applications. The adverse effects observed in therapeutic trials, such as systemic inflammatory response and toxicity [6],[7],[9] can be at least partly explained by the enhanced susceptibility of the Ad-infected host to microbial components, such as LPS [72] and lipopeptides (unpublished results) from incoming secondary pathogens or from the patient's own flora. Our in vitro finding of a strongly enhanced TNF-alpha response to LPS in Ad5-GFP-infected human DCs suggests that enhanced susceptibility to LPS may develop also in patients treated with adenoviral vectors. As shown here, this hypersensitivity is mediated by viral-induced IFN-alphabeta, which is in accordance with the role of IFN-alphabeta as a key mediator of sensitization to LPS [87],[88]. The increased mCD14 expression on LPS target cells and epigenetic changes on promoters of relevant genes (both shown here), may at least in part explain the role of IFN-alphabeta in the development of Ad induced LPS hypersensitivity.
###end p 81
###begin p 82
###xml 105 109 105 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-IacobelliMartinez1">[46]</xref>
###xml 110 114 110 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Lore1">[89]</xref>
###xml 268 272 268 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Huarte1">[42]</xref>
###xml 273 277 273 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Urosevic1">[90]</xref>
###xml 476 483 465 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 646 649 628 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000208-Kojaoghlanian1">[1]</xref>
###xml 73 78 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 210 218 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 374 379 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 469 475 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 607 615 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Type I interferon induction was recently found in recombinant Ad-treated human cells, especially in pDCs [46],[89] and in monocyte-derived DCs in the present study. Moreover, this response was observed also in patients administered with recombinant adenoviral vectors [42],[90]. Since as shown here, IFN-alpha pre-treatment is capable of increasing susceptibility to LPS of human DCs in vitro, we assume that Ad-induced IFN-alphabeta can induce LPS hypersensitivity in humans in vivo. Undesirable complications mediated by IFN-alphabeta can occur not only during gene therapy, but also in immunocompromised patients where Ads are major pathogens [1]. Our finding that blocking SAPK/JNK signaling inhibits the IFN-alphabeta response to Ads, is of potential interest for prevention or treatment of the direct and indirect adverse effects of IFN-alphabeta in Ad gene therapy.
###end p 82
