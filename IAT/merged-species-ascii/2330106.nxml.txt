Preliminary X-ray diffraction analysis of YcdB from Escherichia coli: a novel haem-containing and Tat--secreted periplasmic protein with a potential role in iron transport
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Current address: Institute of Molecular Medicine, University Department of Paediatrics, University of Oxford, John Radcliffe Hospital, Headington OX3 9DU, England.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
This is an open-access article distributed under the terms described at http://journals.iucr.org/services/termsofuse.html.
###end p 2
###begin p 3
###xml 116 123 116 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 116 123 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The crystallization and structure determination of the apo form of a novel haem-containing Tat substrate, YcdB from E. coli, has been solved to 2.0 A resolution. The preliminary structure shows similarity to other haem-dependent peroxidases, despite low sequence homology.
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 51 67 51 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 51 67 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
YcdB is a periplasmic haem-containing protein from Escherichia coli that has a potential role in iron transport. It is currently the only reported haem-containing Tat-secreted substrate. Here, the overexpression, purification, crystallization and structure determination at 2.0 A resolution are reported for the apo form of the protein. The apo-YcdB structure resembles those of members of the haem-dependent peroxidase family and thus confirms that YcdB is also a member of this family. Haem-soaking experiments with preformed apo-YcdB crystals have been optimized to successfully generate haem-containing YcdB crystals that diffract to 2.9 A. Completion of model building and structure refinement are under way.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Introduction
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 207 213 207 213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 219 221 219 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb1"> &#9654;</xref>

###xml 870 873 870 873 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">via</italic>
###xml 1335 1338 1335 1338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">via</italic>
###xml 1383 1387 1383 1387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">e.g.</italic>
###xml 1434 1438 1434 1438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">e.g.</italic>
###xml 1457 1463 1457 1463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1469 1471 1469 1471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb1"> &#9654;</xref>
Although the vast majority of organisms are absolutely dependent on iron, acquisition of sufficient quantities of this metal is often challenging owing to its poor solubility and potential toxicity (Andrews et al., 2003 ▶). Competition for iron resources is particularly acute for pathogenic bacteria, which must overcome the iron-withdrawal strategies of their hosts in order to satisfy their iron requirements (Litwin & Calderwood, 1993 ▶). Bacteria generally overcome iron restriction by utilizing a number of alternative iron transporters that enable them to scavenge iron from various sources. The most common type of iron-uptake strategy employed by bacteria involves the secretion and/or utilization of high-affinity iron chelators, known as siderophores, which bind and solubilize ferric iron. In Gram-negative bacteria, ferri-siderophore complexes are taken up via an outer-membrane (OM) receptor and binding-protein-dependent ABC inner-membrane system requiring the energy-transducing TonB-ExbBD complex for translocation across the OM. Related systems are used by pathogens to directly obtain iron from host iron-containing proteins. Often, bacteria contain multiple ferric complex-uptake systems enabling them to piratize siderophores produced by other organisms. Bacteria can also acquire the soluble ferrous form of iron via the FeoAB pathway, NRAMP-like transporters (e.g. MntH) or through metal-type ABC transporters (e.g. SitABCD) (Andrews et al., 2003 ▶).

###xml 141 146 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 167 172 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 825 841 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 977 994 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">Bacillus subtilis</span>
###xml 1345 1356 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B. subtilis</span>
###xml 1498 1505 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1650 1657 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Recent evidence suggests that bacteria possess another major route for iron uptake that resembles the high-affinity iron-transport system of yeast and other fungi. In yeast, this pathway consists of three plasma-membrane components: Frt1p, a polytopic ferrous permease, Fet3p, an Frtp1-associated multicopper oxidase that oxidizes ferrous iron to the ferric form during the Ftr1p-mediated uptake process, and Fre1p, one of several related flavin- and haem-containing NADPH-dependent ferric reductases that reduces extracellular ferric iron to the ferrous form prior to transport via Ftr1p (Kosman, 2003 ▶). In bacteria, genes encoding homologues of Ftr1p are common, being found in proteobacteria, actinobacteria and firmicutes, and are often adjacent to other iron-related genes (Woodhall, 2005 ▶; Debut et al., 2006 ▶). In Escherichia coli, the Ftr1p homologue (YcdN) is part of an iron and Fur (ferrous uptake regulator) repressed operon, ycdNOB (McHugh et al., 2003 ▶). In Bacillus subtilis and Neisseria meningitides, the ycdNOB-like operons, ywbLMN and NMB0034-36, respectively, are likewise iron-repressed (Baichoo et al., 2002 ▶; Grifantini et al., 2003 ▶). This iron control, together with the Ftr1p homology, is highly suggestive of an iron-transport role for these YcdNOB-like systems. This supposition has been recently confirmed in B. subtilis by the finding that the YwbLMN system is involved in the uptake of free ferric iron (Ollinger et al., 2006 ▶). Recent studies on YcdNOB from E. coli strain Nissle 1917 suggest a role in ferrous iron transport (Grosse et al., 2006 ▶). Interestingly, there is evidence that the ycdNOB operon of E. coli is induced by low pH (Stancik et al., 2002 ▶), conditions which favour ferric iron solubility and ferrous iron stability against oxidation.

###xml 619 630 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B. subtilis</span>
The specific roles of the ycdNOB-encoded YcdO and YcdB proteins in YcdN-mediated iron uptake are unclear. YcdO is unlike any other characterized protein in the sequence databases, but the corresponding gene is found associated with ycdN and ycdB homologues in all cases to date. YcdO possesses an N-terminal signal sequence, suggesting that it is exported to the periplasm in a Sec-dependent manner. YcdB is also periplasmic, but unlike YcdO it contains b-type haem and is secreted via the twin-arginine transport (Tat) pathway. Currently, it is the only known haem-containing Tat substrate (Sturm et al., 2006 ▶). The B. subtilis YcdB homologue (YwbN) has also been shown to be exported via the Tat system (Jongbloed et al., 2004 ▶).
###end p 8

Significantly, YcdB exhibits homology to the dye-decolorizing peroxidase, DyP, of Thanatephorus cucumeris (previously called Geotrichum candidum) Dec 1 (Kim & Shoda, 1999 ▶). The crystal structure of DyP has recently been solved, revealing the presence of a single haem b moiety within a cytochrome c peroxidase fold (Sato et al., 2004 ▶). A low-pH-enhanced peroxidase activity has also been reported for YcdB and, consistent with this, the ycdN homologue of N. meningitides is induced by H2O2-mediated redox stress (Grifantini et al., 2003 ▶, 2004 ▶; Sturm et al., 2006 ▶). However, it is unclear how these findings relate to the proposed iron-transport function of YcdNOB.

###xml 197 204 197 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E.&#160;coli</italic>
###xml 361 368 361 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 197 204 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 361 373 <span type="species:ncbi:83333">E. coli K-12</span>
In an effort to understand the role of these proteins in iron transport and elucidate their mechanism of action, we have embarked on X-ray structural studies of this novel iron-transport system in E. coli. This work reports the overexpression, purification, crystallization and preliminary X-ray crystallographic analysis of the apo form (haem-free) of YcdB of E. coli K-12 and recent progress towards solving the structure of the haem-containing form.

###xml 1367 1369 1355 1357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 1369 1370 1357 1358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">b</italic>
###xml 1469 1470 1457 1458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</italic>
###xml 37 49 <span type="species:ncbi:83333">E. coli K-12</span>
The ycdB gene was PCR-amplified from E. coli K-12 genomic DNA using Accuzyme (Bioline) and cloned into the overexpression vector pET-46 Ek/LIC (Novagen) generating pET46-His6ycdB. The cloning was performed such that the N-terminal Tat signal sequence (residues 1-35) of YcdB was replaced by the His6 tag and entero-kinase cleavage sequence (MAH6VD4KI) encoded by the overexpression vector. Overexpression of '(His)6YcdB' (43 kDa subunit weight) was achieved using BL21/lambdaDE3(pET46-His6ycdB) cultured in L-broth with ampicillin (100 microg ml-1) at 250 rev min-1 and 310 K and induced with 1 mM isopropyl beta-d-thio-galactopyranoside (IPTG) when the culture optical density at 650 nm reached 0.5. The IPTG-induced cells were cultured for a further 16 h, harvested by centrifugation for 20 min at 3000g and 277 K and resuspended in 3 ml (per gram cell weight) buffer A (25 mM HEPES pH 7.4, 50 mM imidazole, 0.2 M NaCl) before being lysed at 137 MPa using a French press. (His)6YcdB was then purified from the soluble supernatant by Ni2+-affinity chromatography (HiTrap Affinity resin, Pharmacia) using buffer A as the binding buffer and buffer B (buffer A plus 0.5 M imidazole) for elution (Fig. 1 ▶a). Two major (His)6YcdB elution peaks were obtained: the first corresponded to cofactor-containing (His)6YcdB, with the second corresponding to the apo form (Fig. 1 ▶b). The resulting protein was >95% pure according to SDS-PAGE analysis and was dialysed against 25 mM HEPES pH 7.4 prior to storage at 193 K.

###begin title 14
Crystallization
###end title 14
###begin p 15

Purified apo-(His)6YcdB was concentrated to 10 mg ml-1 in 25 mM HEPES buffer pH 7.4 using centrifugal membrane concentrators (Vivaspin). The final concentration was determined using a Bradford assay. The drop size was 2 microl plus 2 microl in all cases.

The protein for which a structure had been reported that shared the highest amino-acid sequence homology (25% over 183 residues, determined using WU-Blast2) was found to be DyP, a dye-colourizing peroxidase from T. cucumeris. No coordinates were available for DyP (Sato et al., 2004 ▶); however, two distant fungal homologues to DyP were present in the Protein Data Bank: lignin peroxidase (LiP) and manganese peroxidase (MnP). The two fungal peroxidases shared less than 10% overall sequence identity with YcdB. The program CHAINSAW (Schwarzenbacher et al., 2004 ▶) was used to align the target sequence (YcdB) and the two model sequences from LiP (PDB code ) and MnP (PDB code ) such that all the atoms common to the three models were retained. The resulting model, consisting of residues 34-291 (of a total of 423 residues) of the (His)6YcdB sequence, was used as the search model for molecular replacement against all data between 50 and 3.0 A using MOLREP (Vagin & Teplyakov, 1997 ▶). The program DM (Cowtan & Main, 1996 ▶) was used for noncrystallographic symmetry (NCS) averaging and solvent flattening of the electron-density map, which clearly showed distinguishable secondary-structural features (Fig. 4 ▶). Model building and refinement are in progress using the programs Coot (Emsley & Cowtan, 2004 ▶) and REFMAC (Murshudov et al., 1997 ▶), respectively.

The gene encoding YcdB from E. coli was cloned from genomic DNA and overexpressed using a T7 promoter-based vector and the apo form of (His)6YcdB was purified by Ni2+-affinity chromatography. Crystals of apo-(His)6YcdB were produced and conditions were found for the generation of a potential haem-containing derivative using preformed apo-(His)6YcdB crystals. Diffraction-quality crystals were obtained in each case through careful optimization of pH and cryoprotecting conditions, which gave reproducible crystals that diffracted to 2.0 A for apo-(His)6YcdB and more typically to 2.9 A for the haem-soaked derivative. Much of the success in obtaining suitable diffraction from haem-soaked apo-(His)6YcdB was achieved by careful optimization of the cryoprotectant (0.75 M ammonium sulfate, 5 mM NaCl, 0.5 M sodium formate, 6.4% PEG 400 and 2.5% glycerol in 0.1 M HEPES pH 7.0) in order to eliminate serious problems arising from phase separation and rapid crystal decay. However, numerous haem-soaked apo-(His)6YcdB crystals were tested and many exhibited high mosaicity (>1degrees) and showed signs of anisotropy and crystal splitting, making integration and scaling difficult. Efforts are under way to crystallize holo-(His)6YcdB, since a crucial aspect of this work is to determine the role of this protein in iron transport and as such the location and the surrounding environment of the haem are critically important.
###end p 26

###xml 225 229 225 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i.e.</italic>
###xml 433 439 430 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 445 447 442 444 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb19"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 627 634 624 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 627 634 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
With regard to the haem environment, it has been shown that although the proximal histidine exhibits structural similarity, there are noted differences in the haem environment between the related peroxidases currently known, i.e. between DyP and both LiP and MnP. DyP has been reported to contain a four-stranded antiparallel beta-sheet distal to the haem ligand, a structural feature which is not present in either LiP or MnP (Sato et al., 2004 ▶). The nature of this structural difference is presently unknown. Completion of model building and refinement will reveal the extent of the structural similarity between YcdB from E. coli and other structurally defined members of the superfamily of haem-dependent peroxidases.
