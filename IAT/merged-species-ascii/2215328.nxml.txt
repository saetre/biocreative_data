The Extracytoplasmic Stress Factor, sigmaE, Is Required to Maintain Cell Envelope Integrity in Escherichia coli
###end article-title 0

Extracytoplasmic function or ECF sigma factors are the most abundant class of alternative sigma factors in bacteria. Members of the rpoE subclass of ECF sigma factors are implicated in sensing stress in the cell envelope of Gram-negative bacteria and are required for virulence in many pathogens. The best-studied member of this family is rpoE from Escherichia coli, encoding the sigmaE protein. sigmaE has been well studied for its role in combating extracytoplasmic stress, and the members of its regulon have been largely defined. sigmaE is required for viability of E. coli, yet none of the studies to date explain why sigmaE is essential in seemingly unstressed cells. In this work we investigate the essential role of sigmaE in E. coli by analyzing the phenotypes associated with loss of sigmaE activity and isolating suppressors that allow cells to live in the absence of sigmaE. We demonstrate that when sigmaE is inhibited, cell envelope stress increases and envelope integrity is lost. Many cells lyse and some develop blebs containing cytoplasmic material along their sides. To better understand the connection between transcription by sigmaE and cell envelope integrity, we identified two multicopy suppressors of the essentiality of sigmaE, ptsN and yhbW. yhbW is a gene of unknown function, while ptsN is a member of the sigmaE regulon. Overexpression of ptsN lowers the basal level of multiple envelope stress responses, but not that of a cytoplasmic stress response. Our results are consistent with a model in which overexpression of ptsN reduces stress in the cell envelope, thereby promoting survival in the absence of sigmaE.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 523 526 523 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Kellenberger1">[1]</xref>
All organisms have stress responses that allow them to sense and respond to damaging conditions by altering gene expression. An additional level of complexity is introduced when the inducing signal is sensed on one side of a membrane and that information must be communicated across the membrane for a response to be generated. In Gram-negative bacteria this intercompartmental signaling is required to maintain the cell envelope, which consists of the inner and outer membranes, periplasmic space, and peptidoglycan layer [1].
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 440 443 440 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Nikaido1">[2]</xref>
###xml 694 695 690 691 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 732 735 728 731 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Majdalani1">[3]</xref>
###xml 736 739 732 735 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Ruiz1">[6]</xref>
The cell envelope is a complex, dynamic compartment that is crucial for the survival of the cell. It is not a static structure and can be remodeled in response to environmental conditions. The chemical environment of the cell envelope is distinct from that of the cytoplasm. The envelope lacks ATP, is oxidizing, and can be subject to fluctuations in ionic strength due to passive diffusion of small molecules through outer membrane porins [2]. As such, Gram-negative bacteria possess stress responses that are uniquely targeted to the cell envelope. These stress responses include the CpxAR (Cpx), BaeRS (Bae), and Rcs phosphorelays, the response governed by the alternative sigma factor sigmaE, and the phage shock (PSP) response [3]-[6]. Each of these responses is activated following perturbation of particular components of the envelope.
###end p 5

Although stress responses are important for reacting to damaging conditions, many stress proteins also play important roles in basic cellular physiology. This is particularly true for the sigmaE-dependent response in E. coli, as the rpoE gene, which encodes sigmaE, is essential for viability [7]. Despite the wealth of information about the role of sigmaE in response to cell envelope stress and the identification of the sigmaE regulon, the essential role of sigmaE is still unclear [8], [9]. One suppressor of DeltarpoE lethality, a deletion of the ydcQ gene, has been identified [10]. However, the function of this gene is not well understood and it is not clear how it suppresses the essentiality of rpoE.

###xml 1120 1124 1092 1096 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Grigorova1">[23]</xref>
###xml 1131 1132 1099 1100 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1308 1312 1276 1280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Costanzo1">[24]</xref>
sigmaE is activated by stresses that interfere with the folding of outer membrane proteins (OMPs) such as heat shock, overexpression of OMP genes, and mutations in genes encoding chaperones required for OMP folding [11]-[14]. In unstressed cells, sigmaE activity is low because sigmaE is sequestered at the inner membrane by the antisigma factor RseA (Fig. 1) [15], [16]. RseA is a single-pass inner membrane protein that binds to sigmaE and prevents sigmaE from interacting with RNA polymerase [15]-[17]. During envelope stress, RseA is degraded in response to unfolded porins by the sequential action of two inner membrane proteases, DegS and RseP, followed by the cytoplasmic protease ClpXP (Fig. 1) [18]-[22]. A periplasmic protein, RseB, binds to the periplasmic domain of RseA and enhances inhibition by RseA, protecting it from proteolysis (Fig. 1) [15], [16], [18], [23]. Continual degradation of RseA is required to provide the cell with sufficient free sigmaE to support viability, and deletion of either degS or rseP is toxic due to the stabilization of RseA and consequent sequestration of sigmaE[19], [20], [23]. sigmaE can also be activated independently of the RseA-dependent stress-signaling pathway by the cytoplasmic alarmone ppGpp, whose levels change in response to nutrient availability [24].

The sigmaE-dependent extracytoplasmic stress response.
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 5 6 1 2 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 369 370 361 362 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>

###xml 643 644 623 624 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 679 680 655 656 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
sigmaE is held at the membrane by the antisigma factor RseA. RseB binds to the periplasmic domain of RseA and protects RseA from proteolysis. Unfolded OMPs activate the protease DegS, which cleaves the periplasmic domain of RseA. The partially degraded RseA is now a substrate for RseP. RseP cleaves RseA further, releasing the cytoplasmic domain of RseA bound to sigmaE. This remaining domain of RseA is degraded by ClpXP thereby freeing sigmaE to interact with RNA polymerase and direct transcription of its regulon. The major classes of known genes in the sigmaE regulon are indicated. The alarmone ppGpp and protein DksA can activate sigmaE-dependent transcription once sigmaE is released from RseA, but are not shown for clarity. (IM inner membrane, OM outer membrane).

###xml 1133 1137 1113 1117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Dartigalongue1">[25]</xref>
###xml 1139 1143 1119 1123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Rezuchova1">[26]</xref>
As expected from its role in the stress response, the sigmaE regulon includes genes encoding periplasmic foldases, proteases, and chaperones that aid in OMP folding. In addition, sigmaE transcribes an array of biosynthetic enzymes that are involved in phospholipid, fatty acid, LPS, and membrane-derived oligosaccharide synthesis and transport, and a number of other cell envelope proteins including lipoproteins, inner membrane proteins, and envelope proteins of unknown function [9], [25], [26]. The sigmaE regulon also includes several genes that encode major components of complexes responsible for ushering LPS and OMPs across the periplasm and properly assembling them in the outer membrane [27], [28]. Recently sigmaE has been shown to transcribe several small RNAs that decrease the expression of OMPs, providing a mechanism to decrease the flux of OMPs to the envelope during times of stress [29]-[31]. In addition to the envelope-associated proteins, sigmaE-dependent promoters are found upstream of genes involved in cytoplasmic processes such as transcription, translation, DNA replication, and DNA/RNA modification [9], [25], [26].

The sigmaE regulon contains a number of essential genes, and the simplest explanation for why sigmaE is essential is that it is required to transcribe one or more of these genes. Alternatively, loss of expression of multiple non-essential genes dependent on sigmaE for expression could be lethal. However, many genes in the sigmaE regulon are also transcribed by sigma70. Chromatin immoprecipitation followed by genomic microarray (ChIP-chip) experiments examining the location of sigma70 on the E. coli chromosome suggest that approximately 40% of sigmaE-regulated promoters overlap with sigma70 promoters [32]. This number is likely to be an underestimation of the extent of dual regulation, since it does not include genes that also have sigma70 promoters farther away from the sigmaE promoter [32]. As such, sigmaE may be essential because genes in its regulon that are also transcribed by sigma70 are misregulated with lethal effect in the absence of sigmaE.

In this work we took two approaches to better understand the essential role of sigmaE. The first approach was to characterize the phenotypes of cells when sigmaE activity was inhibited to gain insights into what cellular functions were impacted. We demonstrate that sigmaE is required to maintain the integrity of the cell envelope; in the absence of sigmaE the cells developed blebs along their sides and lysed. The second approach was to isolate multicopy suppressors that allow cells to live in the absence of sigmaE activity. Two multicopy suppressors, ptsN and yhbW, were isolated that suppress both the lethality due to inhibition of sigmaE activity and the essentiality of the rpoE gene. ptsN is a member of the sigmaE regulon, while yhbW is not, and neither gene has previously been associated with cell envelope functions. Overexpression of ptsN lowered the basal activity of several envelope-sensing pathways, implying that it may suppress DeltarpoE lethality by reducing stress in the cell envelope.

To gain a better understanding of the role of a particular gene in cellular physiology, it is often informative to examine the phenotypes of a deletion mutant lacking the gene of interest. Because sigmaE is encoded by an essential gene, it is not possible to examine the phenotype of a DeltarpoE strain. Therefore we examined the effects of loss of sigmaE activity by inducing the overexpression of its inhibitors, rseA and rseB, encoded under the IPTG-inducible trc promoter on the pTrc99a plasmid, pRseAB. This method efficiently inhibits sigmaE (Fig. 2A) by preventing its association with RNA polymerase [15]-[17]. Overexpression of rseA and rseB and the consequent sequestration of sigmaE were lethal for the bacterium. In liquid culture, the cfu/ml began to decrease after 2 hours (approximately2.5 generations) following the addition of IPTG and the optical density stopped increasing and began to decrease slightly within 3 hours (approximately3.5 generation) (Fig. 2B and [15]). On solid media, the plating efficiency in the presence of IPTG was reduced by three orders of magnitude compared with non-inducing conditions (Table 1). To ensure that the observed phenotypes were due to inhibition of sigmaE and not overproduction of RseA and RseB, we introduced a point mutation into the rseA gene on the plasmid encoding rseA and rseB, changing the aspartate residue at position 11 of RseA to histidine. This mutation abrogates the antisigma factor activity of RseA (Fig. 2A and [16]). When overproduced along with RseB, the RseAD11H protein reached similar steady-state levels as the wild-type protein, was properly localized to the inner membrane, did not inhibit sigmaE activity, did not induce lysis, and did not reduce the plating efficiency (Fig. 2, Table 1, and data not shown).

Because sigmaE is known to respond to envelope stress and direct the synthesis of many components of the cell envelope, it is likely that this compartment will be negatively impacted and envelope stress will increase when sigmaE activity is blocked. We measured activation of reporter genes for the Cpx, Bae, and Rcs envelope stress responses (cpxP-lacZ activated by CpxR, spy-lacZ activated by BaeR and CpxR, rprA-lacZ activated by RcsB, Fig. 3). beta-galactosidase activity from the envelope stress reporters increased by 2.5 hours following inhibition of sigmaE (Fig. 4). Since activation of these stress responses could be due to a general increase in stress associated with cell death, we also monitored activation of the cytoplasmic stress response mediated by sigma32 using the sigma32-regulated htpG-lacZ reporter fusion. sigma32 activity increased relatively little (approximately2-fold) following inhibition of sigmaE compared with the larger increases seen for the envelope stress responses (3.5-fold for Cpx, 8-fold for Rcs, and 12-fold for the Bae/Cpx-dependent reporters). These results suggest that loss of sigmaE activity does increase stress in the cell envelope, but does not increase cytoplasmic stress to the same extent.

Since inhibition of sigmaE increases envelope stress and sigmaE can transcribe genes that are localized to the inner and outer membranes, we next asked if the envelope membranes were altered. Samples were taken from cultures 2.5 hours following overexpression of rseA and rseB. This time point was chosen for analysis because the cell envelope stress responses were activated, the cfu/ml had started to decrease, and the morphological defects described below were evident. Cell lysates were fractionated by centrifugation through discontinuous sucrose density gradients and several assays were used to detect fractions containing inner and outer membrane components. In each of these assays the results from cultures in which sigmaE activity was inhibited were similar to those from control cultures. We next examined the morphology of cells following inhibition of sigmaE. By 2 hours after addition of IPTG to overexpress rseA and rseB (the time that the cfu/ml began to drop), two phenotypes were evident by phase contrast microscopy. Ghosted cells and cells with blebs began to appear in the culture. The blebs formed primarily along the lateral wall of the cells and were found less frequently at either the poles or the septum. Usually only one bleb formed per cell. By 2.5 hours after addition of IPTG, approximately 20% of cells in the culture had blebs (Fig. 6) and the number of ghosted cells increased along with the amount of cellular debris, indicative of lysis. In a typical experiment, approximately 100 cells were viewed at each time point. The location of the blebs and the timing of their appearance with respect to loss of viability were very reproducible, suggesting that the phenotypes were related. By 3-4 hours after addition of IPTG some cells had additional blebs, the number of ghosted cells and the amount of cell debris increased, and many cells clumped together. Similar phenotypes were observed when sigmaE was inhibited by proteolytic stabilization of RseA through depletion of the DegS or RseP proteases (data not shown) and were not found following overexpression of rseAD11H and rseB from pRseAD11HB, providing further evidence that the phenotypes were due to inhibition of sigmaE and not overexpression of rseA and rseB. In addition, the same phenotypes were seen when the cells were grown in glucose or glycerol minimal media supplemented with amino acids indicating that the phenotypes were not a function of growth rate or medium composition.

###xml 1475 1482 1463 1470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0001573-g006">Fig. 6D</xref>
To further probe the morphology of cells following inhibition of sigmaE, fluorescent probes were used to detect cytoplasm, nucleic acids, and membranes in live cells. We visualized the cytoplasm by expressing the soluble fluorescent protein, YFP, from an expression construct integrated in the chromosome. Membranes and nucleic acids were visualized respectively with the FM4-64 and DAPI fluorescent dyes, which were added directly to the cultures. Neither the dyes nor cytoplasmic expression of YFP altered the phenotypes associated with inhibition of sigmaE. All of the cells and blebs were outlined with FM4-64, and both the cells and the blebs contained YFP within the FM4-64 boundary, with the exception of the ghosted cells (Fig. 6A-D). The ghosted cells were outlined with FM4-64, but no longer contained YFP (Fig. 6D, indicated by the arrowhead). YFP is not proteolytically labile, therefore the lack of a YFP signal in the ghosted cells is likely to be due to release of cytoplasmic contents upon lysis. All of the cells had DAPI staining, and both the compaction of the nucleoids and the number of nucleoids per cell did not differ significantly from control cells, suggesting that loss of sigmaE activity does not cause major defects in chromosome replication or segregation (Fig. 6A-D). Approximately 50% of the cells with blebs contained DAPI staining within the bleb, in addition to YFP (Fig. 6B,C). We hypothesize that some blebs do not contain DAPI staining (Fig. 6D, indicated by the arrow), either because the nucleoid was simply not drawn into the blebs, or there were not enough nucleic acids present to be visualized with DAPI.

The phenotypic studies demonstrate that when sigmaE activity is depleted, cell envelope integrity is compromised, resulting in lysis and bleb formation. However, these phenotypes are not readily explained by loss of expression of any gene(s) in the sigmaE regulon (see Discussion). To gain additional information about why sigmaE is essential, we turned to genetics and isolated multicopy suppressors that allowed E. coli to live in the absence of sigmaE activity. Cells containing pRseAB (strain SEA007) were transformed with pools of plasmids from the ASKA ORF library, in which each E. coli ORF was cloned under the control of an IPTG-inducible promoter [35]. Transformants were plated in the presence of IPTG to induce overexpression of rseA and rseB and the gene encoded on the ASKA plasmid. Of approximately 10,000 transformants, 44 putative suppressors were recovered.

Two classes of genes can be isolated with this selection, those encoding genes that restore sigmaE activity and those that no longer require sigmaE for growth. We distinguished between these classes using the chromosomally encoded sigmaE-dependent lacZ reporter gene (rpoHP3-lacZ[36]) in strain SEA007 and detected sigmaE activity by plating in the presence of Xgal and IPTG. Of the 44 clones isolated in the selection, 26 formed blue colonies, indicating that overexpression of the gene on the plasmid restored sigmaE activity, and 18 formed white colonies, indicating that sigmaE activity was not restored and therefore was no longer required for growth. This latter class contained plasmids that were potential suppressors of the requirement of sigmaE for viability. For each of these 18 plasmids, we verified that suppression was linked to the plasmid, the plating efficiencies when rseA and rseB were overexpressed were no longer reduced, and the morphological defects were significantly reduced.

The selection identified plasmids that permit growth in the presence of the overexpression of rseA and rseB. However, these strains could have low levels of sigmaE activity that contribute to viability. We therefore determined whether the rpoE gene could be deleted when the potential suppressor genes were overexpressed using a cotransduction assay. A nadB::Tn10 allele immediately upstream of a rpoE::kan deletion was introduced by P1 transduction into wild-type strains containing each of the 18 plasmids, and nadB::Tn10 transductants were selected. PtsN has been shown to bind to and inhibit potassium uptake by the potassium transporter TrkA [40]. K+ is the major cation in the E. coli cytoplasm and participates in a variety of processes related to known functions of sigmaE including adaptation to osmotic stress and maintenance of turgor pressure [42]-[44]. In addition, K+ binds and regulates a number of intracellular enzymes including RNA polymerase (as potassium glutamate) [45]. Therefore, if overexpression of ptsN suppresses the requirement of sigmaE for viability by inhibiting TrkA, rpoE should not be essential in a DeltatrkA strain. However, when a DeltatrkA strain was used as the recipient in the cotransduction assay, only nadB::Tn10 transductants were obtained indicating that DeltatrkA is not a suppressor (Table 2). Furthermore, the trkA gene was not required for suppression by ptsN, as cotransductants were readily obtained in a DeltatrkA strain when ptsN was overexpressed (Table 2).

If ptsN overexpression does in fact lower the basal level of envelope stress, then the decrease in reporter gene expression should be dependent on the transcriptional activators associated with the envelope stress pathways. Interestingly, in the DeltaydcQ strain the decrease in reporter gene expression of the Cpx-regulated cpxR-lacZ reporter was not dependent on the response regulator CpxR [10]. We measured the effects of overexpression of ptsN on the Cpx-dependent cpxP-lacZ and Rcs-dependent rprA-lacZ reporter fusions in strains lacking their respective transcriptional regulators, CpxR and RcsB. In a WT strain, overproduction of ptsN lowered expression of the rprA-lacZ reporter 3.2-fold compared with 1.8-fold in the DeltarcsB strain (Fig. 8B) and lowered expression of the cpxP-lacZ reporter 4.3-fold compared with 2.2-fold in the cpxR::Omegaspec strain (Fig. 8C). It has been reported previously that the cpxP-lacZ reporter is not expressed in a DeltacpxR variant of the E. coli strain MC4100 [10], [47]. However, we were able to reproducibly measure beta-galactosidase activity from this reporter in a cpxR::Omegaspec variant of strain MG1655, although expression was quite low (approximately250-fold lower than in a WT background). These results indicate that decreased activity of the reporters due to ptsN overexpression is at least partially dependent on cpxR and rcsB, suggesting that EIIANtr lowers envelope stress sensed by both pathways.
###end p 54
###begin title 55
Discussion
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 76 77 72 73 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 155 156 147 148 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 197 198 185 186 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 271 272 255 256 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 339 340 319 320 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 729 733 709 713 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ptsN</italic>
###xml 738 742 718 722 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">yhbW</italic>
###xml 793 797 773 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rpoE</italic>
###xml 804 808 784 788 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ptsN</italic>
###xml 833 834 809 810 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 843 846 819 822 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Rhodius1">[9]</xref>
Despite extensive knowledge about the regulatory pathways that control sigmaE activity, the stresses that activate these pathways, and the genes that sigmaE transcribes, the essential role of sigmaE in the cell has remained elusive. In this work we demonstrate that sigmaE is required for maintenance of cell envelope integrity. When sigmaE activity was inhibited, envelope stress sensed by several stress responses increased, indicating that the cell envelope was impacted. Many cells lysed and some developed blebs containing cytoplasmic material along their sides. Interestingly, these phenotypes were not accompanied by major alterations in the composition of the envelope membranes. We identified two multicopy suppressors, ptsN and yhbW, that allowed cells to live in the absence of the rpoE gene. ptsN is a member of the sigmaE regulon [9] and lowers the basal level of activity of several envelope stress responses by an unknown mechanism when overexpressed.
###end p 56
###begin title 57
###xml 22 23 18 19 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
How does loss of sigmaE activity lead to bleb formation and lysis?
###end title 57
###begin p 58
###xml 49 50 45 46 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 79 86 75 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 251 255 247 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Meisel1">[48]</xref>
###xml 257 261 253 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Spratt1">[49]</xref>
###xml 446 450 442 446 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Meisel1">[48]</xref>
###xml 452 456 448 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Spratt1">[49]</xref>
###xml 524 525 516 517 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1109 1110 1097 1098 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1330 1334 1318 1322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Denome1">[50]</xref>
###xml 1415 1416 1399 1400 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1678 1679 1658 1659 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1704 1705 1680 1681 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 79 86 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The blebs that form following inhibition of sigmaE resemble bulges formed when E. coli are treated with penicillin-like antibiotics that inhibit peptidoglycan synthesis, or when an inactive variant of the peptidoglycan synthase PBP 1B is overproduced [48], [49]. These treatments are also lytic, and lysis is thought to be triggered by holes formed in the peptidoglycan layer by murein hydrolases acting in the absence of peptidoglycan synthesis [48], [49]. The similarity in the phenotypes suggests that inhibition of sigmaE leads to weakening of and/or defects in the peptidoglycan layer. These defects potentially have two major outcomes. Either a hole forms in the peptidoglycan that is large enough to accommodate extrusion of the inner membrane and cytoplasm, evidenced by YFP signal in all of the blebs, or the cell lyses, evidenced by loss of YFP signal in the ghosted cells. The observation that blebs form in only 20% of the cells is likely to be due to the inherent stochastic nature of the process. Many cells may lyse before a bleb can form. Further support for the model that inhibition of sigmaE leads to defects in the peptidoglycan layer comes from preliminary results with a strain lacking four enzymes involved in peptidoglycan synthesis, PBPs 4, 5, 6, and 7, which results in irregular peptidoglycan synthesis [50]. This strain develops blebs and lyses more quickly following inhibition of sigmaE than the isogenic WT strain (data not shown). If the peptidoglycan is affected, we should be able to reduce cell death by growing the cells in high osmolarity medium. Unfortunately, this experiment was not technically feasible. High osmolarity induced the sigmaE response such that sigmaE was no longer inhibited by RseA, even when RseA was overexpressed (data not shown).
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 77 78 73 74 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 456 457 448 449 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
Even though these phenotypes are consistent with the model that loss of sigmaE activity leads to alterations in the peptidoglycan layer resulting in bleb formation and lysis, we cannot eliminate the possibility that bleb formation is caused by a different event, such as aberrant cell division. Future experiments examining the integrity of the peptidoglycan layer in more detail and the localization of cell division proteins following inhibition of sigmaE should help distinguish between these possibilities.
###end p 59
###begin title 60
###xml 68 69 64 65 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
Relationship between the phenotypes and the known functions of sigmaE regulon members
###end title 60
###begin p 61
###xml 43 44 39 40 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 132 133 124 125 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 215 216 203 204 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 281 282 265 266 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 499 500 479 480 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 518 522 498 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">yaeT</italic>
###xml 524 528 504 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">yfiO</italic>
###xml 530 533 510 513 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">imp</italic>
###xml 535 539 515 519 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">lptA</italic>
###xml 544 548 524 528 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">lptB</italic>
###xml 636 640 616 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Sperandeo1">[28]</xref>
###xml 642 646 622 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Braun1">[51]</xref>
###xml 647 651 627 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Malinverni1">[54]</xref>
###xml 776 777 752 753 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 777 781 753 757 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Sperandeo1">[28]</xref>
###xml 783 787 759 763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Braun1">[51]</xref>
###xml 788 792 764 768 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Wu1">[55]</xref>
Because the only reported function of sigmaE is to direct transcription, altered regulation of one or more of the genes in the sigmaE regulon should ultimately lead to bleb formation and loss of viability when sigmaE activity is blocked. However, given the known functions of sigmaE regulon members, we cannot point to one specific gene or set of genes in the regulon that is obviously associated with these phenotypes. Depletion phenotypes have been characterized in detail for five essential sigmaE-regulated genes (yaeT, yfiO, imp, lptA and lptB), which encode components of complexes that assemble LPS or OMPs in the outer membrane [28], [51]-[54]. Depletion of these genes causes striking defects in the cell envelope, but not those observed following inhibition of sigmaE[28], [51]-[55].
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 96 97 92 93 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 113 114 105 106 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 252 256 244 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bacA</italic>
###xml 314 317 306 309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Rhodius1">[9]</xref>
###xml 319 323 311 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-ElGhachi1">[56]</xref>
###xml 522 526 514 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-ElGhachi1">[56]</xref>
###xml 537 544 529 536 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 646 650 638 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bacA</italic>
###xml 698 702 690 694 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-ElGhachi1">[56]</xref>
###xml 711 712 699 700 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 789 792 777 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Rhodius1">[9]</xref>
###xml 886 890 874 878 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ftsZ</italic>
###xml 902 906 890 894 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Flardh1">[57]</xref>
###xml 945 946 929 930 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 537 544 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The blebbing phenotype suggests that peptidoglycan or cell division is affected by loss of sigmaE. Although sigmaE does not transcribe any known murein hydrolases or biosynthetic enzymes directly involved in peptidoglycan synthesis, it does transcribe bacA, which encodes an undecaprenyl pyrophosphate phosphatase [9], [56]. BacA hydrolyzes a phosphate moiety from undecaprenyl pyrophosphate to generate undecaprenyl phosphate, which is a lipid carrier required for synthesis of peptidoglycan and other cell wall polymers [56]. However, E. coli possesses at least three additional undecaprenyl pyrophosphate phosphatase enzymes and cells lacking bacA alone have no obvious morphological phenotypes [56]. A sigmaE-dependent promoter is also found upstream of the cell division protein FtsZ [9], but this promoter is one of multiple promoters that contribute to the complex regulation of ftsZ expression [57], making it unlikely that loss of sigmaE activity would have a large effect under non-stressed growth conditions.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 69 70 65 66 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 239 240 231 232 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 276 277 264 265 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 352 356 340 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">lpxA</italic>
###xml 358 362 346 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">lpxB</italic>
###xml 368 372 356 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">lpxD</italic>
###xml 372 375 360 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Rhodius1">[9]</xref>
###xml 461 462 449 450 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">N</italic>
###xml 554 558 542 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Anderson1">[58]</xref>
###xml 571 575 559 563 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">lpxA</italic>
###xml 580 584 568 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">lpxD</italic>
###xml 619 620 603 604 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
Since no direct connection can yet be made convincingly between sigmaE regulon members and the observed phenotypes, we think it likely that the phenotypes are caused by indirect effects resulting from loss of or altered regulation of sigmaE-dependent genes. For example, sigmaE transcribes genes required for synthesis of the lipid A component of LPS, lpxA, lpxB, and lpxD[9]. LpxA catalyzes the first step in lipid A synthesis and uses the same substrate, UDP-N-acetyl-D-glucosamine, as is used in the first dedicated step of peptidoglycan biosynthesis [58]. Changes in lpxA and lpxD expression in the absence of sigmaE could alter the flux of substrates through these pathways, affecting peptidoglycan synthesis and the integrity of the peptidoglycan layer.

###xml 997 1001 973 977 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Gralla1">[45]</xref>
###xml 1033 1037 1009 1013 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">trkA</italic>
###xml 1073 1077 1049 1053 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ptsN</italic>
###xml 1091 1095 1063 1067 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">trkA</italic>
###xml 1130 1133 1102 1105 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Ntr</sup>
###xml 1227 1231 1195 1199 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rpoE</italic>
In this work two genes were identified whose overexpression suppresses both the loss of viability following inhibition of sigmaE and the requirement of the rpoE gene for viability. Of the three known suppressors of DeltarpoE lethality (DeltaydcQ[10] and overexpression of ptsN and yhbW), the only gene with any known roles in the cell is ptsN. ptsN is a member of the sigmaE regulon, which suggests that it plays a role in cell envelope processes [9]. Several functions have been associated with the nitrogen PTS system, of which ptsN is a member, including balancing nitrogen and carbon metabolism, suppression of a temperature-sensitive allele of the essential GTPase Era by a DeltaptsN mutant, and inhibition of the potassium transporter TrkA [37], [38], [40]. This latter role of ptsN held the most promise for providing insights into the mechanism of suppression because K+ is important for osmotic regulation and can regulate RNA polymerase, all activities associated with sigmaE[42], [44], [45]. However, we demonstrated that trkA is not required for suppression by ptsN, nor is DeltatrkA a suppressor, indicating that EIIANtr has additional functions, yet to be discovered, that are responsible for suppression of DeltarpoE lethality. As discussed below, our results indicate that this novel activity likely involves regulation of cell envelope stress responses.

###xml 1599 1603 1563 1567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ptsN</italic>
###xml 1727 1731 1687 1691 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ydcQ</italic>
###xml 1760 1764 1720 1724 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cpxR</italic>
###xml 1764 1768 1724 1728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Button1">[10]</xref>
###xml 1948 1952 1904 1908 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ydcQ</italic>
###xml 2027 2031 1983 1987 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Button1">[10]</xref>
The basal level of activity of multiple envelope stress responses is reduced in the presence of all sigmaE suppressors for which data is available, overexpression of ptsN, DeltaydcQ[10] and an uncharacterized suppressor [46], suggesting that lower activity of envelope stress responses is an important part of the suppression mechanism. Preliminary results indicate that the basal levels of cell envelope stress responses are also lower following overexpression of yhbW, the other multicopy suppressor that we isolated. In light of these results, one model for suppression is that the suppressors strengthen the cell envelope such that loss of sigmaE activity and the resultant impact on the cell envelope are no longer lethal. If this model is correct, then the suppressors should lower envelope stress sensed by the signal transduction pathways that monitor the cell envelope. We found that the effects of ptsN overexpression on reporter gene expression for the Rcs and Cpx pathways were at least partially dependent on their respective stress regulators. In addition, sigmaE activity was not altered by ptsN overexpression in a strain lacking rseA, the only known envelope sensor for the pathway. Although it is possible that EIIANtr was unable to overcome the high level of activation of sigmaE in a DeltarseA strain background, we think it unlikely based on observations with another regulator of sigmaE, which does not act through RseA. sigmaE activity can be both activated and reduced in a DeltarseA strain in response to the cytoplasmic regulator ppGpp [24], suggesting that the effects of ptsN overexpression should be discernible. In contrast, the reduction in activity of the Cpx reporter gene examined in the DeltaydcQ strain was not dependent on cpxR[10]. However, epistasis experiments were only reported for the one reporter, and it is not known whether the activity of other cell envelope stress responses were reduced in the DeltaydcQ background independently of their respective signal transduction pathways [10].

###xml 996 997 972 973 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1045 1046 1017 1018 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1125 1132 1097 1104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 1166 1170 1138 1142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0001573-Liu1">[60]</xref>
###xml 1350 1351 1318 1319 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1404 1405 1368 1369 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1508 1509 1468 1469 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1696 1697 1652 1653 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 1717 1718 1669 1670 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</sup>
###xml 205 227 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">Salmonella typhimurium</span>
###xml 1125 1132 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The functions of core members of the sigmaE regulon are conserved in a number of bacterial species [9]. However, sigmaE is not essential in many of these bacteria including closely related species such as Salmonella typhimurium[59]. This observation led, in part, to a model proposing that sigmaE is not essential because it is required to transcribe a particular gene or set of genes [10]. Instead, sigmaE is essential because the bacteria overreact to its absence and, through an unknown mechanism that requires the ydcQ gene product, activate an unknown cell death pathway that kills the cells [10]. Our data are consistent with this model. However, we suggest that even though the functions of core regulon members are conserved in bacterial species in which sigmaE is not essential [9], significant differences can exist in the properties of the cell envelopes of different species and even strains within a species that may determine whether the bacteria can survive in the absence of sigmaE. For example, the majority of the work on sigmaE, including that presented in this paper, has been performed in K12 strains of E. coli, which lack the O-antigen of LPS [60]. This variation could significantly alter the properties of the bacterial cell envelope, making the bacteria more sensitive to disruptions in cell envelope integrity so that sigmaE would be essential. Therefore, we propose that sigmaE is indeed essential because it is required to transcribe genes in its regulon. In the absence of sigmaE these genes are not properly regulated, leading to disruption of cell envelope integrity and lysis. Future experiments delving in greater depth into the phenotypes of cells lacking sigmaE, the roles of sigmaE regulon members of unknown function, and the mechanism of suppression should help to distinguish between these models and provide new insights into the role of this important cell envelope stress response.
