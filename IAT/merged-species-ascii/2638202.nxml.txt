###xml 1437 1438 1437 1438 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
CLC-ec1, a bacterial homologue of the CLC family's transporter subclass, catalyzes transmembrane exchange of Cl- and H+. Mutational analysis based on the known structure reveals several key residues required for coupling H+ to the stoichiometric countermovement of Cl-. E148 (Gluex) transfers protons between extracellular water and the protein interior, and E203 (Gluin) is thought to function analogously on the intracellular face of the protein. Mutation of either residue eliminates H+ transport while preserving Cl- transport. We tested the role of Gluin by examining structural and functional properties of mutants at this position. Certain dissociable side chains (E, D, H, K, R, but not C and Y) retain H+/Cl- exchanger activity to varying degrees, while other mutations (V, I, or C) abolish H+ coupling and severely inhibit Cl- flux. Transporters substituted with other nonprotonatable side chains (Q, S, and A) show highly impaired H+ transport with substantial Cl- transport. Influence on H+ transport of side chain length and acidity was assessed using a single-cysteine mutant to introduce non-natural side chains. Crystal structures of both coupled (E203H) and uncoupled (E203V) mutants are similar to wild type. The results support the idea that Gluin is the internal proton-transfer residue that delivers protons from intracellular solution to the protein interior, where they couple to Cl- movements to bring about Cl-/H+ exchange.

###xml 1211 1239 1211 1239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Miller and Nguitragool, 2009</xref>
###xml 935 951 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Membrane proteins of the ubiquitous CLC family operate in varied physiological contexts, calling for transmembrane movement of inorganic anions (Jentsch, 2008). These proteins segregate into two distinct mechanistic subtypes: Cl--specific channels and proton-coupled Cl- (or, in plants, NO3-) exchangers (Zifarelli and Pusch, 2007). The channels have been studied electrophysiologically for many years, but the exchange transporters, recently recognized, are understood only in fuzzy mechanistic outline. Built as homodimers in which each subunit operates as a full-fledged exchanger (Nguitragool and Miller, 2007; Zdebik et al., 2008), these proteins swap substrate ions across the membrane in opposite directions with a stoichiometry of two Cl- ions per H+ (Accardi and Miller, 2004; DeAngeli et al., 2006; Nguitragool and Miller, 2006; Graves et al., 2008; Miller and Nguitragool, 2009). The x-ray crystal structure of CLC-ec1 from Escherichia coli, the only structurally known CLC, provides a view of the physical pathways by which Cl- and H+ move through proteins of this family (Dutzler et al., 2003; Miller, 2006). A mechanistic scheme for how these movements are coordinated has recently been proposed (Miller and Nguitragool, 2009).

Coupled Cl-/H+ transport uses two strictly conserved glutamates near the protein's extracellular or intracellular surfaces to transfer protons between the proximate aqueous solutions and the protein interior (E148 and E203 in CLC-ec1, denoted Gluex and Gluin, respectively). Substituting either of these residues with nonprotonatable side chains eliminates H+ but retains Cl- movement (Accardi and Miller, 2004; Accardi et al., 2005; Picollo and Pusch, 2005; Scheel et al., 2005; Jayaram et al., 2008; Zdebik et al., 2008). The proton-transfer function of Gluex is well established experimentally (Dutzler et al., 2003; Accardi and Miller, 2004): the side chain acts as a "gate" that blocks off the extracellular end of the Cl- pathway when deprotonated and swings away upon protonation to open this pathway to the outside. But the role of Gluin on the intracellular side is not so clear. It is known that this is the only internally facing carboxyl group that when mutated away abolishes H+ transport (Accardi et al., 2005), and a recent molecular dynamics simulation suggests that a side chain rotation of Gluin is required for transferring H+ into the proton pathway (Voth, G., personal communication); but no such structural rearrangement of Gluin has been observed crystallographically. Further experimental scrutiny is therefore needed to test the idea that this residue is an intracellular H+ transfer catalyst. Now, by examining an expanded panel of mutations at Gluin, we show that Cl-/H+ exchange is supported only by protonatable side chains, both acidic and basic, and that non-dissociable side chains produce uncoupled Cl- transporters with a wide range of absolute rates. These results agree in outline, but not in all details, with similar experiments on mammalian CLC exchangers (Zdebik et al., 2008), and they corroborate the proposal (Accardi et al., 2005) that Gluin shuttles protons between the intracellular solution and the still unknown internal workings of the exchanger.

Chemicals were of the highest grade available. Fluoro-iodoacetic acid (FIAA) was obtained from American Custom Chemicals, methanethiosulfonate compounds were from Toronto Research Chemicals, detergents were from Anatrace, and phospholipids were from Avanti Polar Lipids, Inc. Protein purification and reconstitution procedures were, with minor modifications, as described previously (Accardi et al., 2004; Walden et al., 2007). In brief, mutants of CLC-ec1 (GenBank accession no. ), His6 tagged at the C terminus, were constructed by PCR mutagenesis, and the full coding sequence was confirmed. These were expressed in E. coli, extracted in decylmaltoside (DM), and partially purified on Co-affinity beads (BD) by sequentially washing with 10-20 column volumes of wash buffer (WB; 100 mM NaCl, 20 mM tris-Cl, and 5 mM DM, pH 7.5), 10 vol of WB containing 20 mM imidazole, 10 vol of high salt WB (1 M NaCl, 20 mM tris-Cl, and 5 mM DM, pH 7.5), and 10 vol of WB. Protein was eluted with WB containing 400 mM imidazole, and the His tag was removed by a 1-h digestion with lysine endoproteinase C (0.05 U/mg protein; Roche). Protein was further purified by anion-exchange chromatography (Poros HQ; Applied Biosystems) for planar lipid bilayer experiments or gel filtration (Superdex 200) for liposome flux assays and for structure determination. Purified protein was reconstituted at various protein densities (0.2-50 microg/mg lipid) into liposomes formed by detergent dialysis from E. coli phospholipids and stored at 20 mg lipid/ml at -80degreesC in aliquots. All mutants expressed well (1-3 mg/L culture) and ran as homodimers on gel filtration chromatography.

For introducing non-natural side chains into the Gluin position, a single-cysteine mutant, E203C, on a cysteine-free background (C84A/C302A/C347S) was used (Nguitragool and Miller, 2007). Various electrophilic reagents were reacted at pH 8.0 with this protein to form the desired side chain adducts: iodoacetic acid (IAA), FIAA, 2-carboxylethyl methanethiosulfonate, and 2-sulfonatoethyl methanethiosulfonate. Reactions were monitored quantitatively with the Ellman reagent, as described for this protein (Nguitragool and Miller, 2007). Protein samples were passed through Superdex 200 equilibrated with 50 mM NaCl, 50 mM NaPi, and 5 mM DM, pH 8.0, and four shots of 1 mM MTS reagent was added at 15-min intervals. Reactions with 100 mM IAA or FIAA were run for 20 or 32 h, respectively. Reactions were terminated by gel filtration in 100 mM tris-Cl, 3 mM EDTA, and 5 mM DM, pH 8.0. Table S1, available at , reports details of these cysteine-modification reactions.

Cl- gradient-driven H+ pumping and passive Cl- efflux assays were performed as described previously (Nguitragool and Miller, 2007; Walden et al., 2007; Jayaram et al., 2008), at pH 4-5, where CLC-ec1 is maximally active. Proton pumping used liposomes with high protein density (5.0 microg protein/mg lipid) loaded with 300 mM KCl and 40 mM citrate, pH 5.0. After thawing, liposomes were extruded with a 0.4-microm filter; just before each run, a 100-microl liposome sample was centrifuged through a 1.5-ml G-50 column equilibrated in pump buffer (290 mM K-isethionate, 10 mM KCl, and 2 mM glutamate, pH 5.2) to establish a Cl- gradient. The liposomes were added to 1.8 ml of pump buffer in a stirred cell with pH continuously monitored with a glass electrode, and H+ pumping was triggered by the addition of 0.5 microg/ml valinomycin (Vln). After approximately30 s, the pH gradient was collapsed with 0.5 microg/ml p-trifluoromethoxyphenyl hydrazone (FCCP).

###xml 980 987 980 987 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table I</xref>
Our purpose here is to challenge the Gluin position with a variety of mutations and to gauge their effects on Cl-/H+ exchange. The proposal that Gluin acts as an essential H+ shuttle makes a strong prediction: any nonprotonatable residue substituted here should prevent H+ from participating in the transport cycle; such substitutions might affect the absolute rate of Cl- transport, but any Cl- flux remaining should be "uncoupled," i.e., purely passive and unaccompanied by H+ movement (Walden et al., 2007). Protonatable side chains other than Glu might-or might not-support coupled Cl-/H+ exchange, according to this proposal. We therefore assessed the behavior of CLC-ec1 transporters mutated at Gluin via three methods performed with purified protein reconstituted into phospholipid membranes: (1) H+ pumping driven by Cl- gradients in liposomes, (2) I-V relations in planar lipid bilayers in the presence of Cl- or pH gradients, and (3) passive downhill Cl- turnover rate. Table I reports empirical metrics for these three activities for all mutants tested.

To investigate effects of the mutations on Cl- transport, we measured turnover rates under standard "Cl- dump" conditions at zero voltage: 300 mM Cl-, pH 4.5, at 25degreesC, as described in detail (Walden et al., 2007; Jayaram et al., 2008). Liposomes loaded with high KCl were suspended in low Cl- (but equal K+), and efflux of KCl was initiated by adding Vln, a K+-specific ionophore that renders Cl- movement rate limiting and sets the liposome voltage to zero; a proton ionophore was also present to prevent the buildup of a pH gradient during the Cl- efflux, and at the end of the time course the total trapped volume was determined by disrupting the liposomes with detergent. Representative traces of Cl- appearing in the liposome suspension are shown (Fig. 3 A) for representative CLC variants, and unitary turnover rates calculated from these data are reported for all mutants in Fig. 3 B. Cl- flux correlates with H+ pumping above; the fastest transporters are the most coupled ones, and all the uncoupled transporters are slower than WT.

In light of the strict conservation of Gluin among the CLC exchangers and of the proposed influence of local electrostatics on anion binding in the Cl- pathway (Faraldo-Gomez and Roux, 2004), we were surprised that the positively charged substitutions (H, K, and R) all support H+/Cl- exchange. Because these residues are so much more basic than Glu, we expected altered pH rate profiles. But the facts contradict this expectation: in the His and Lys mutants, the pH dependence of Cl- turnover rate is strikingly similar to WT (Fig. 4), despite the huge difference in pKa values of the amino acid side chains, which span a range of 4 to 12.

The Cys mutant above offers opportunities to plant non-natural side chains at Gluin and thereby examine the influence of side chain length and acidity on transporter function. For these experiments, we used a fully functional "Cys-less" transporter with the three natural cysteines in CLC-ec1 replaced by inoffensive residues; Gluin was then substituted on this background with Cys to create a single-Cys mutant that behaves identically to the same substitution on the WT background: slow Cl- turnover rate and complete loss of H+ pumping. Reaction of this mutant with iodoacetate or fluoro-iodoacetate introduces carboxyl-bearing side chains one atom unit longer than Glu and of substantially different acidities (Fig. 5 and Table I); the IAA adduct (Cys-1, pKa approximately4.0) pumps protons at 25% of WT activity, whereas activity of the more acidic fluorinated isostere (Cys-2, pKa approximately2.0) is further lowered by another factor of 5.6 Lengthening the side chain by two additional atom units while maintaining the WT acidity (Cys-3, pKa approximately4.5) reduces pumping activity to 15% of WT; however, a sulfonate-bearing side chain of similar length but far greater acidity (Cys-4, pKa approximately0) is fully inhibited, as though this side chain is effectively nonprotonatable at the pH of the assay.

###xml 1198 1199 1194 1195 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>

X-ray crystal structures were determined at 2.9-3.2 A resolution for two of the mutants described above (Table S2): one fully coupled and fast (His), the other completely uncoupled and slow (Val). Crystals were grown in Br-, a functional Cl- substitute (Nguitragool and Miller, 2006), to allow unequivocal visualization of the bound anion by anomalous diffraction (Lobet and Dutzler, 2005; Accardi et al., 2006). The backbone trajectories of the two mutants are virtually identical to WT (Calpha rmsd 0.35, 0.41 A), as shown in Fig. 6 A, and the structures are also alike in the mechanistic heart of the protein-the ion-coupling region between Gluex and Gluin (Fig. 6 B). In both mutant structures, as with WT, the dehydrated central Br- ion is occluded, blocked toward the extracellular side by the deprotonated Gluex carboxylate, and toward the intracellular by an inner gate formed by S107 and Y445 (Jayaram et al., 2008). Moreover, anion occupancy of the Cl- pathway is similar to that seen in both WT and the uncoupled Gln substitution (Accardi et al., 2006), with Br- ions producing prominent anomalous difference peaks in the inner and central sites. We therefore cannot attribute the low Cl- transport rate in the Val mutant to physical disruption of the Cl- pathway; instead, we imagine that inhibition of Cl- flux arises from the unavailability of transportable protons, a circumstance that stalls the transport cycle. The His mutant, being well coupled, does not suffer this disability.

###xml 825 845 825 845 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Accardi et al., 2005</xref>

Despite its overall structural similarity, the Val mutant differs from WT in one striking way. In the mutant, electron density is missing, through disorder, for the R28 side chain (Fig. 6 B), which in WT forms a cross-subunit salt bridge with Gluin; disordering of R28 also occurs in the uncoupled Gln mutant (Accardi et al., 2005). In contrast, in the well-coupled His mutant, R28 side chain density appears in precisely the same position as in WT, now with a guanidinium nitrogen within respectable H-bonding distance (3.1 A) of a His imidazole nitrogen, presumably deprotonated. It is therefore tempting to suppose that an intimate interaction between the proton-transfer group and R28-a salt bridge in WT or a hydrogen bond in the His mutant-is required for an efficient transport cycle; but that idea swiftly collapses (Accardi et al., 2005) with the "negative result" that R28L displays WT-like coupling and Cl- turnover rate, as we have also confirmed with R28Q and R28E (not depicted).

###xml 470 471 470 471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>a</sub></italic>
###xml 782 783 782 783 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
These experiments are simply summarized. Cl-/H+ exchange transport in CLC-ec1 occurs only if the Gluin position carries a protonatable group. The chemical nature of the proton-competent side chains is remarkably wide-ranging: WT coupling by carboxyls on Glu, Asp, and by imidazole nitrogen on His, partial coupling by carboxyls of long-chain Cys adducts, by the primary amine of Lys, and even by guanidinium of Arg. Such variety in side chain size, shape, charge, and pKa is unanticipated for a key mechanistic residue, but it highlights the one feature common to all competent side chains: an ability to bind and release protons, a fundamental requirement for proton transfer. This capability is necessary but not sufficient, as no side chain lacking a dissociable group supports H+ movement, but several protonatable moieties (thiol, phenol, and sulfonate) fail in this task.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 61 62 61 62 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 140 141 140 141 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 206 208 206 208 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 248 249 248 249 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 385 386 385 386 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 535 536 535 536 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 621 640 621 640 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">Walden et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 642 662 642 662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib12">Jayaram et al., 2008</xref>
###xml 741 742 741 742 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 853 873 853 873 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Accardi et al., 2005</xref>
Another pattern emerging here is that the absolute rate of Cl- transport correlates impressively, although imperfectly, with the degree of H+ coupling. The fully coupled transporters are fast, 1,000-2,000 s-1, whereas the uncoupled variants move Cl- at 2-30% of the WT rate. Inhibited turnover in uncoupled exchangers is unsurprising for mechanisms in which protonation is linked to Cl- binding and conformational change; mutants inept at moving protons through the protein interior could jam the transport cycle and thereby provide Cl- ions with rare opportunities to slip passively through the protein's anion pathway (Walden et al., 2007; Jayaram et al., 2008). It is particularly intriguing that the Val substitution shows the slowest Cl- flux of all because this residue appears at the equivalent position in all known channel-type CLC homologues (Accardi et al., 2005). The segregation of CLC channels carrying Val and exchangers carrying Glu is mirrored in the approximately600 CLC sequences in the current Uniprot database, which fall into "Glu" or "Val" subtypes, with infrequent exceptions (Table S3).
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 156 175 156 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Zdebik et al., 2008</xref>
###xml 195 196 195 196 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 199 200 199 200 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 248 250 248 250 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in</sub>
###xml 326 327 326 327 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 355 356 355 356 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 404 405 404 405 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 424 425 424 425 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 621 628 621 628 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Xenopus</italic>
###xml 673 674 673 674 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 775 776 775 776 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 121 126 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 621 628 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus</span>
###xml 826 831 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
These results on a bacterial exchanger are largely harmonious with similar experiments on the equivalent position in two human homologues, CLC-4 and CLC-5 (Zdebik et al., 2008). In that study, Cl- /H+ antiport occurred with E, D, H, or Y at the Gluin position, but not with A, R, V, C, M, and N; moreover, mutants failing in H+ transport also failed in Cl- transport. With CLC-ec1, however, we observe Cl- transport in all H+-uncoupled mutants, albeit at lowered rates. This apparent discrepancy between the bacterial and eukaryotic CLCs is not serious because the CLC-4/CLC-5 experiments used voltage clamp recording in Xenopus oocytes, which might miss slow, uncoupled Cl- transport buried in the background "leak" currents intrinsic to the recording system. However, the H+ transport activity seen in the Tyr mutant of the human CLCs appears to be a real difference between the homologues, as the liposome assay used here would have picked up comparable proton-pumping activity.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 91 111 91 111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Accardi et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 113 132 113 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Zdebik et al., 2008</xref>
###xml 188 190 188 190 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in</sub>
###xml 213 214 213 214 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 308 309 308 309 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 455 457 455 457 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in</sub>
###xml 475 477 475 477 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ex</sub>
###xml 524 544 512 532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Accardi et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 565 566 553 554 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 913 915 889 891 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in</sub>
###xml 945 946 921 922 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1019 1020 995 996 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
This survey of proton transfer on the intracellular side of CLC-ec1 expands previous work (Accardi et al., 2005; Zdebik et al., 2008) and buttresses the idea that the carboxyl group of Gluin does indeed act as a H+ shuttle essential for coupling ion movements in CLC exchangers. However, the details of the H+ transfer step itself remain vague. A central issue, conspicuous by our ignorance about it, is the pathway taken by the proton after it leaves Gluin on its way to Gluex approximately15 A away. We previously argued (Accardi et al., 2006) that the central Cl- ion itself, located halfway between these two glutamates, acts as a critical way station in the proton pathway. But this still leaves approximately8 A of nominally nonpolar distance that the proton must traverse to reach that site. A recent molecular dynamics study (Voth, G., personal communication) observes that upon protonation in silico, Gluin rotates toward the central Cl- as several intracellular water molecules enter to mediate the required H+ transfer. Although experimental work is currently silent on whether such a side chain rearrangement actually occurs in proteo, a mechanism like this would neatly account for proton transfer on the intracellular side of CLC exchangers.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 60 61 60 61 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 115 116 115 116 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">a</sub>
###xml 115 116 115 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>a</sub></italic>
###xml 139 140 139 140 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 187 188 187 188 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 277 297 277 297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib1">Accardi et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 299 303 299 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">2005</xref>
###xml 382 383 382 383 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 453 455 453 455 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ex</sub>
###xml 482 483 482 483 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 549 550 549 550 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 686 687 686 687 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
It is also worthwhile to puzzle over the pH dependence of Cl- turnover, which is unexpectedly insensitive to the pKa of the intracellular H+ transfer residue. Previous work showed that Cl- flux varies with pH in response to titration of the extracellular proton-transfer gate (Accardi et al., 2004, 2005). We infer from this that ion transport is rate limited by the extracellular H+ transfer step (which is linked to conformational rearrangement of Gluex), and that intracellular H+ transfer must be much faster than this. Only when intracellular H+ transfer is severely compromised, as by the nonprotonatable or highly acidic/basic side chains described here, does its influence on Cl- transport become apparent.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 103 105 103 105 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in</sub>
###xml 414 416 414 416 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in</sub>
###xml 738 767 738 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">&#196;delroth and Brzezinski, 2004</xref>
###xml 769 789 769 789 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib6">Br&#228;nden et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 917 918 917 918 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 966 968 954 956 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 1179 1186 1167 1174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 1188 1205 1176 1193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib11">Iyer et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 1179 1186 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The results set the stage for attacking future questions about how protons move between solvent and Gluin. This is not a trivial process. In crystal structures, the side chain at this position, although physically close to solvent, is not directly exposed to it. Rather, it is covered by the N-terminal helix from the twin subunit in the homodimer. If this helix were sufficiently mobile, direct protonation of Gluin from solvent could occur; alternatively, solvent protons might first bind to carboxylates located nearby on the water-exposed surface, as in the "proton antennas" that accelerate proton-transfer steps above the formal diffusion limit in membrane proteins, such as photosynthetic reaction centers or cytochrome c oxidase (Adelroth and Brzezinski, 2004; Branden et al., 2006). Experimental evidence on CLC exchangers is still too primitive to address possibilities like this. In any case, because the H+ transport rate of CLC-ec1, approximately1,000 s-1 at pH 4.5, is approximately three orders of magnitude below the diffusion limit, no special mechanisms need be invoked for the protein to achieve its biological purpose: facilitating extreme acid resistance in E. coli (Iyer et al., 2002).
