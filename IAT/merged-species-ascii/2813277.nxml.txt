###begin article-title 0
The Type III Secretion Effector NleE Inhibits NF-kappaB Activation
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: CN KB SB IR. Performed the experiments: CN KB SK EM GH MF IA SB. Analyzed the data: CN KB SK SB TFM YBN IR. Wrote the paper: CN KB SB IR.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 378 394 374 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 378 394 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 396 400 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 503 507 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
The complex host-pathogen interplay involves the recognition of the pathogen by the host's innate immune system and countermeasures taken by the pathogen. Detection of invading bacteria by the host leads to rapid activation of the transcription factor NF-kappaB, followed by inflammation and eradication of the intruders. In response, some pathogens, including enteropathogenic Escherichia coli (EPEC), acquired means of blocking NF-kappaB activation. We show that inhibition of NF-kappaB activation by EPEC involves the injection of NleE into the host cell. Importantly, we show that NleE inhibits NF-kappaB activation by preventing activation of IKKbeta and consequently the degradation of the NF-kappaB inhibitor, IkappaB. This NleE activity is enhanced by, but is not dependent on, a second injected effector, NleB. In conclusion, this study describes two effectors, NleB and NleE, with no similarity to other known proteins, used by pathogens to manipulate NF-kappaB signaling pathways.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Author Summary
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 261 277 257 273 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 345 352 341 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 456 463 452 459 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 261 277 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 345 352 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 456 463 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 465 469 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 689 693 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
The innate immune system senses intruding pathogens and in response, mounts an inflammatory reaction. Essential for this response is the activation of the transcription factor NF-kappaB, which mediates reprogramming of gene expression in the host. The bacteria Escherichia coli is usually a non-pathogenic resident of our intestinal track. Some E. coli strains, however, cause disease or food poisoning; one of these pathogenic strains is enteropathogenic E. coli (EPEC). This pathogen employs a syringe-like organelle, termed type three secretion system (TTSS), to inject into the intestinal host cell a battery of toxic proteins termed effectors. We found that two of the effectors that EPEC injects into the host cell upon infection block the activation of NF-kappaB and thus interfere with the host immune response. These findings elucidate the intricate cross-talk between the host immune system and the pathogen.

###begin title 5
Introduction
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 17 33 17 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 413 416 413 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Dean1">[1]</xref>
###xml 694 697 694 697 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Iguchi1">[2]</xref>
###xml 17 33 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 35 39 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 492 496 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
Enteropathogenic Escherichia coli (EPEC) belong to a group of pathogens defined by their ability to form "attaching and effacing" (AE) histopathology on intestinal epithelia. These pathogens employ their type III protein secretion system (TTSS) to inject (translocate) toxic proteins (effectors) into the host cell. The injected effectors subvert normal host cell functions to benefit the bacteria (summarized in [1]). To date, 21 effectors or putative effector genes have been described for EPEC. Six of them are encoded in the LEE region that also encodes the TTSS structural genes, whereas the other 15 effector genes are distributed within three prophages and three insertion elements (IE) [2].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 33 34 33 34 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</underline>
###xml 42 43 42 43 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">a</underline>
###xml 53 54 53 54 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</underline>
###xml 63 64 63 64 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</underline>
###xml 288 291 284 287 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Doyle1">[3]</xref>
###xml 292 295 288 291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Kawai1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1122 1125 1074 1077 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Chen1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1126 1129 1078 1081 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Karin1">[6]</xref>
Upon infection, bacterial PAMPs (pathogen-associated molecular patterns) including LPS, flagellin, lipoproteins, and CpG DNA stimulate host cell Toll-like receptors (TLRs) in the host cells, leading to a formidable immune response via the activation of the transcription factor NF-kappaB [3],[4]. NF-kappaB comprises a family of closely related transcription factors that play a key role in the expression of genes involved in inflammation, immune, and stress responses. NF-kappaB is a collective term used for homo- and heterodimeric complexes formed by the Rel/NF-kappaB proteins. In mammals, five of such proteins are known: RelA (p65), RelB, c-Rel, p50 (NF-kappaB1), and p52 (NF-kappaB2). Under nonstimulating conditions, NF-kappaB is retained in the cytoplasm through its association with inhibitory proteins (IkappaBs). A variety of signaling pathways activate IkappaB kinases (IKK) to phosphorylate IkappaB, leading to its ubiquitination and degradation by the proteasome. This allows translocation of NF-kappaB to the nucleus, activation of NF-kappaB-regulated genes, and establishment of an inflammatory response [5],[6].
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 187 190 179 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Savkovic1">[7]</xref>
###xml 191 194 183 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Hecht1">[8]</xref>
###xml 353 356 341 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Berin1">[9]</xref>
###xml 357 361 345 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Malladi1">[10]</xref>
###xml 362 366 350 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Miyamoto1">[11]</xref>
###xml 367 371 355 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Zhou1">[12]</xref>
###xml 481 485 465 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Hauf1">[13]</xref>
###xml 486 490 470 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Maresca1">[14]</xref>
###xml 55 59 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 133 137 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 262 266 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 408 412 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 535 539 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 884 888 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
Previous reports have suggested that during infection, EPEC manipulate NF-kappaB-mediated inflammation. Initially, it was shown that EPEC activate NF-kappaB by a TTSS-dependent mechanism [7],[8], but later, it was reported that the TTSS is not required and that EPEC activate NF-kappaB via a TTSS-independent mechanism, presumably by activation of TLRs [9],[10],[11],[12]. Moreover, some reports showed that EPEC actually repress NF-kappaB activation by a TTSS-dependent mechanism [13],[14]. Taken together, these reports suggest that EPEC first elicit NF-kappaB activation by a TTSS-independent mechanism and subsequently, it utilize the TTSS mechanism to mediate TTSS-dependent NF-kappaB-repression. However, the major gap in the above hypothesis is that the putative effector that presumably represses NF-kappaB activation has never been identified. In this report we confirm that EPEC block NF-kappaB activation via a TTSS-dependent mechanism and show that the NleE effector is necessary and sufficient to block NF-kappaB activation via inhibition of IkappaB phosphorylation and thus induces its stabilization. In addition, we show that a second effector, NleB, is required for better repression of NF-kappaB activation, suggesting that the function of NleB is related to that of NleE.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Results
###end title 9
###begin title 10
###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
EPEC inhibit IkappaB degradation and NF-kappaB activation by a TTSS-dependent mechanism
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 969 978 921 930 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">escN::kan</italic>
###xml 1057 1064 1005 1012 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-1000743-g001">Fig. 1A</xref>
###xml 15 19 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 435 439 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 538 542 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 941 945 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 1035 1039 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
The ability of EPEC to either inhibit or induce NF-kappaB activation is controversial. Therefore, we re-examined this point using HeLa cells as host cells and IkappaB stability as a read-out for NF-kappaB activation. Importantly, TNFalpha treatment strongly stimulates IkappaB degradation in these cells, but under the used experimental conditions they exhibited minimal IkappaB degradation upon exposure to the PAMPs of the infecting EPEC. This allowed the uncoupling of infection and NF-kappaB activation. HeLa cells were infected with EPEC culture for 3 h, during which the bacteria injected the TTSS effectors into host cells. The infected cells were then treated with 10 ng/ml TNFalpha to activate NF-kappaB and at different time points post TNFalpha-induction, cellular lysates were subjected to western analysis. The results show that TNFalpha treatment induced rapid degradation of IkappaB in uninfected cells or cells infected with EPEC TTSS-deficient mutant (escN::kan). In contrast, IkappaB in cells infected with wild-type EPEC remained stable (Fig. 1A).

###xml 55 59 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>

We next tested whether the stabilization of IkappaB by EPEC was associated with inhibition of NF-kappaB translocation to the nucleus. To monitor NF-kappaB activation, we used a reporter cell line (AGS SIB02) stably expressing the NF-kappaB subunit p65 fused to GFP. Cells were infected with wild-type EPEC or, as a negative control, with EPEC TTSS mutant (escV::kan). After 3 h of infection, cells were washed, induced with TNFalpha, and at 15, 45, 60, and 75 min post TNFalpha-induction, the cells were fixed, stained with Hoechst 33342, and analyzed by automated microscopy. Importantly, whereas wild-type EPEC repressed p65-GFP translocation to the nucleus, the TTSS escV mutant was strongly attenuated in this activity (Fig. 1B). To validate the above microscopic analysis we carried out identical experiment, but instead of using microscopy to analyze the cells we fractionated the cells into cytoplasmic and nucleus fractions and determined the amount of p65 in the different fractions by immunoblot using anti-p65 antibody. The results were in agreement with the microscopic analysis: wild type EPEC, but not the escV mutant, blocked translocation of p65 to the nucleus (Fig. 1C). Thus supporting the notion that EPEC inhibit NF-kappaB activation by a TTSS-dependent mechanism [13],[14], and suggesting that EPEC deliver into infected cells one or more effectors that inhibits NF-kappaB activation.

###xml 186 190 182 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nleH</italic>
###xml 279 286 271 278 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-1000743-g001">Fig. 1D</xref>
###xml 159 163 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>

NleH has been proposed as such an effector since it is similar to OspG, a Shigella effector that inhibits NF-kappaB activation [15]. However, we found that an EPEC strain, in which both nleH alleles were deleted, still inhibited IkappaB degradation, similarly to wild-type EPEC (Fig. 1D), suggesting that NleH is not required for blocking IkappaB degradation under the experimental conditions used by us.

###begin title 16
NleE and NleB are required for IkappaB stabilization
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 138 145 134 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 371 378 367 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="ppat-1000743-t001">Table 1</xref>

###xml 107 111 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 138 145 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 171 175 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 297 301 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>

###xml 1257 1261 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
To identify putative effector(s) that block IkappaB degradation, we bioinformaticly compared the genome of EPEC to that of non-pathogenic E. coli K12 and identified large EPEC-specific regions that contain, or possibly contain, effector genes. Based on this comparison, we constructed a set of 15 EPEC strains, each deleted of one EPEC-specific large chromosomal region (Table 1). Altogether, 770 EPEC-specific ORFs were deleted. We then tested the capacity of each of the deleted strains to inhibit IkappaB degradation upon TNFalpha treatment. One of the strains, deleted of the IE6 region [2], could not inhibit IkappaB degradation (data not shown). Further systematic deletion analysis defined two effector-encoding genes, nleB and nleE, required for stabilizing IkappaB (Fig. 2A, 2B and data not shown). Deletion of nleE strongly reduced the bacteria's capacity to stabilize IkappaB, but a complete deficiency in IkappaB stabilization was observed only in the strain deleted of both nleB and nleE (Fig. 2B). To corroborate the notion that NleE is required for IkappaB stabilization, we complemented a strain deleted of the nleBE region with plasmids containing nleB, nleE, or nleBE. We found that expression of NleE, but not of NleB, partially restored EPEC's capacity to stabilize IkappaB (Fig. 2C). Importantly, full IkappaB protection was achieved in strains expressing both NleB and NleE (Fig. 2C). A mutant expressing only NleB showed only low level of IkappaB protection (Fig. 2C). Taken together, these results suggest that NleB and NleE, located at the IE6 region, are necessary for stabilizing IkappaB and that this activity is contributed mainly by NleE (Fig. 2B). We therefore focused our attention on NleE.

NleE is required for EPEC inhibition of TNFalpha-induced IkappaB degradation.

###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 1186 1190 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
EPEC encode two very similar nleE alleles. One allele, identified in our screen, is located in the IE6 region and the other is in the IE2 region [2]. We initially found that deletion of the IE6 region, but not of the IE2 region, caused deficiency in inhibition of IkappaB degradation (data not shown). However, the two proteins, NleEIE2 and NleEIE6, are identical, apart from an internal deletion of 56 residues in NleEIE2 (Fig. S1), and this similarity between the two proteins urged us to determine the activity of each of the two proteins. We first tested their ability to complement IkappaB destabilization in a strain deleted of nleEIE6. To this end, we expressed each of them on a plasmid carrying an identical promoter and ribosomal binding site. Results showed that only NleEIE6, but not NleEIE2, was able to attenuate IkappaB degradation (Fig. 3A). These results indicate that NleEIE2 is either not active in the host cell or is not translocated into the host cell. To differentiate between these two possibilities, we used the above mentioned plasmid where both proteins were fused to the beta-lactamase translocation reporter protein, BlaM. The plasmids were introduced into EPEC and the ability to translocate them into infected cells was tested. We found that both NleEIE2-BlaM and NleEIE6-BlaM were expressed at similar levels in the bacteria (Fig. 3B). Importantly, however, only NleEIE6 was translocated into the host cell (Fig. 3C), suggesting that NleEIE2 is a cryptic effector. Cumulatively, these results define NleEIE6, but not NleEIE2, as the effector needed for inhibition of IkappaB degradation. Therefore, in this report the term "NleE" specifically refers to "NleEIE6".

The IL8 expression assay was found to be much more sensitive then testing translocation to the nucleus or the IkB degradation assay. Different NF-kappaB activating pathways converge at the level of IKK phosphorylation, which subsequently leads to IkappaB phosphorylation, targeting it to ubiquitination and proteasome-mediated degradation [6]. The signaling pathways induced by the TNF receptor (TNFR) is different from that induced by the IL1 or TLR receptors, but both converge at the level of IKK activation by TAK1 (Fig. 6C, [16]). ###begin title 47
Discussion
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 307 311 295 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nleE</italic>
###xml 416 420 400 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nleE</italic>
###xml 657 661 633 637 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nleE</italic>
###xml 38 42 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 232 236 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 268 272 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
In this report we showed that NleE of EPEC stabilizes the NF-kappaB inhibitor, IkappaB, via inhibition of its phosphorylation, thereby preventing NF-kappaB signaling. This activity of NleE was discovered using an unbiased screen of EPEC strains deleted of most of the EPEC-specific genes. We showed that an nleE mutant is deficient in blocking IkappaB phosphorylation and in preventing its degradation. Moreover, an nleE mutant was attenuated in blocking TNFalpha-induced NF-kappaB migration to the nucleus as well as in IL-8 expression and secretion. These abilities were restored to the mutant upon complementation with a plasmid expressing the wild-type nleE allele. Importantly, we showed that NleE expressed in HeLa cells blocks NF-kappaB translocation to the nucleus upon TNFalpha treatment. Taken together, our findings indicate that NleE is sufficient to inhibit NF-kappaB signaling by blocking IkappaB phosphorylation. Further analysis suggest that NleE blocks the NF-kappaB signaling cascade downstream to the converging point of the TNFalpha and IL1beta signaling pathways, but upstream to IkappaB phosphorylation, possibly by directly blocking TAK1 or IKKbeta activation.

###xml 904 907 892 895 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">IE6</sub>
###xml 958 965 946 953 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="ppat.1000743.s004">Fig. S1</xref>
###xml 793 797 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 822 826 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 838 850 <span type="species:ncbi:67825">C. rodentium</span>
We also show that NleB enhances NleE activity. The nleE mutant still showed residual inhibition of IkappaB degradation, which was eliminated upon further deletion of nleB. Moreover, complementation of the nleBE double mutant with a plasmid expressing nleBE was more efficient than a plasmid expressing only nleE. These results suggest that NleB plays a role in IkappaB stabilization. Similar results were obtained when expression of IL8 was used as a readout for inhibition of the NF-kappaB signaling. The mechanism underlying NleB function is not yet apparent. Nevertheless, the notion that NleB and NleE function together is supported by the facts that nleE form a putative bicistronic operon with nleB and that nleE is consistently associated with nleB in natural isolates of diarrheagenic EPEC [17]. Other isolates of EPEC, EHEC, and C. rodentium carry nleB and nleE alleles almost identical to nleBEIE6 investigated in this study (Supplemental material Fig. S1). We predict that all NleBE proteins function similarly.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 70 73 70 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Dean1">[1]</xref>
###xml 108 112 108 112 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">escV</italic>
###xml 123 128 119 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nleBE</italic>
###xml 378 384 362 368 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-1000743-g007">Fig. 7</xref>
###xml 526 530 510 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">escV</italic>
###xml 606 611 590 595 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nleBE</italic>
###xml 800 806 784 790 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-1000743-g007">Fig. 7</xref>
###xml 65 69 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 692 696 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
The phenomenon of effectors functioning in parallel is common in EPEC [1]. Interestingly, the TTSS mutants (escV) and DeltanleBE were only partially deficient in blocking the TNFalpha-induced migration of NF-kappaB to the nucleus, suggesting that an additional TTSS-independent mechanism might function in parallel to NleBE to inhibit translocation of NF-kappaB to the nucleus (Fig. 7). This activity might be related to damping of the signal due to continuous exposure to PAMPs like flagellin or LPS. Interestingly, the TTSS escV mutant was completely deficient in inhibition of IL8 expression, while the nleBE mutant was only partially deficient in this function. We therefore predict that EPEC encode additional putative effector(s) that function in parallel to NleBE by blocking IL-8 expression (Fig. 7).
###end p 50
###begin title 51
###xml 50 54 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
Schematic of inhibition of NF-kappaB signaling by EPEC.
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 752 758 733 739 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-1000743-g004">Fig. 4</xref>
Signals (indicated as lightning) sensed by different receptors elicit signal transduction cascades that converge at the level of TAK1 activation and IKK phosphorylation, which subsequently leads to IkappaB phosphorylation. This step is inhibited by NleBE. NleE inhibits IKKbeta phosphorylation, but the exact target of NleE and NleB is yet to be defined. The phosphorylated IkappaB is subjected to ubiquitination and proteasome-mediated degradation, allowing NF-kappaB translocation to the nucleus and activation of expression of target genes including IL-8. Our results predicate that in addition to NleBE, a putative non-TTSS factor and a putative TTSS effector might inhibit NF-kappaB translocation to the nucleus and IL-8 expression, respectively (Fig. 4).
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 170 178 166 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Shigella</italic>
###xml 210 214 202 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Zurawski1">[18]</xref>
###xml 332 338 324 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 338 342 330 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Zurawski1">[18]</xref>
Where as in this study we showed that NleE represses NF-kappaB activation, a recent report suggested exactly the opposite, i.e. NleE and OspZ, an NleE-homolog encoded by Shigella, activates NF-kappaB signaling [18]. The discrepancy between the two studies might result from the use of different cell lines. In their study, Zurawski et al.[18] used cell lines that activate NF-kappaB upon sensing of either TNFalpha or bacterial PAMPs including LPS. The latter might complicate the interpretation of the results. To avoid such complications, we used in this study HeLa and AGS cells, which are inert to LPS. Moreover, we adjusted the experimental conditions used in this study, so that the NF-kappaB activation by other bacterial PAMPs was insignificant. This facilitated the uncoupling of effector injection and NF-kappaB activation, which occurred only upon addition of TNFalpha. An alternative, although less likely explanation for the inconsistency between the two studies is that NleE functions differently in different types of cell or different cell lines.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 11 19 11 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Yersinia</italic>
###xml 200 204 193 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Mukherjee1">[19]</xref>
###xml 475 485 461 471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 496 504 482 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Shigella</italic>
###xml 604 608 586 590 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Haraga1">[20]</xref>
###xml 610 618 592 600 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Shigella</italic>
###xml 684 688 662 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Kim1">[15]</xref>
###xml 798 802 776 780 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nleH</italic>
###xml 938 944 908 914 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-1000743-g007">Fig. 7</xref>
###xml 1016 1020 986 990 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nleH</italic>
###xml 5 9 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 705 709 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 766 770 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
Like EPEC, Yersinia also employs an injected effector, YopJ, to block IkappaB phosphorylation. YopJ is an acetyl transferase that acetylates critical IKKbeta residues and thus prevents its activation [19]. Although both NleE and YopJ block IKKbeta phosphorylation, they are very different in sequence, which probably reflects functional differences. We are currently investigating the NleE's mode of action. Other effectors that interfere with NF-kappaB function include the Salmonella SspH, and Shigella IpaH9.8, which are targeted to the host cell nucleus and inhibit NF-kappaB-dependent transcription [20]. Shigella also uses OspG, which inhibits ubiquitination of phospho-IkappaB [15]. Interestingly, EPEC encode an OspG homolog, NleH. However, we found that an EPEC strain, mutated in its two nleH alleles, still inhibit IkappaB degradation. Yet, our analysis indicates that additional TTSS effector(s) inhibits NF-kappaB signaling (Fig. 7) and we can not exclude the possibility that this putative effector is nleH.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 118 130 118 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. rodentium</italic>
###xml 197 209 197 209 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. rodentium</italic>
###xml 321 325 321 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Kelly1">[21]</xref>
###xml 326 330 326 330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat.1000743-Wickham1">[22]</xref>
###xml 20 24 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 118 130 <span type="species:ncbi:67825">C. rodentium</span>
###xml 197 209 <span type="species:ncbi:67825">C. rodentium</span>
###xml 238 242 <span type="species:ncbi:10090|species:ncbi:10095">mice</span>
###xml 298 302 <span type="species:ncbi:10090|species:ncbi:10095">mice</span>
The role of NleE in EPEC virulence was not tested, since an animal model is not yet available, but it was tested with C. rodentium. Importantly, NleE was found to be required for full virulence of C. rodentium upon infection of wild-type mice, but this requirement was diminished upon infection of mice deficient in TLR4 [21],[22]. Our results revealed the rationale behind this intriguing phenomenon. We argue that NleE-mediated NF-kappaB repression is no longer needed if the host itself is deficient in TLR4/LPS-induced NF-kappaB signaling.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 170 174 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
###xml 347 351 <span type="species:ncbi:562">EPEC</span>
In conclusion, we show that NleE blocks IkappaB phosphorylation by IKK and thus it inhibits NF-kappaB signaling. We also show that NleB enhances NleE's activity and that EPEC probably use additional mechanisms to interfere with other constituents of the NF-kappaB signaling pathway. This is presumed to have multiple consequences on the course of EPEC infection and the maturation of both innate and adaptive host immune response.

To determine translocation levels, overnight cultures of wild-type EPEC containing plasmids expressing NleE-BlaM were diluted 1:50 in DMEM and used to infect HeLa cells for 3 h. Cells were then washed and stained with CCF2 for 2.5 h as described [26], washed in cDMEM, excited at 405 nm, and then emission at 465 nm and 535 nm was recorded (SPECTRAFluor, TECAN). The amount of translocation was determined as described [26]. As a negative control, we used EPEC expressing unfused BlaM (Vector). To determine expression levels, the unattached bacteria were harvested, washed, and lysed by repeated freezing and thawing in PBS containing 1 mM EDTA, 1 mg/ml lysozyme, and 0.1% Triton-x100. The BlaM activity in the lysate was determined using nitrocefin as substrate and the rate of product accumulation per number of bacteria (OD 600) was determined as described [25].

###begin p 69

###xml 215 216 197 198 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
HeLa cells (7x105) in 6 wells plates were inoculated with a 1:100 dilution in DMEM of bacteria grown overnight statically at 37degreesC (multiplicity of infection, MOIapproximately1:100) and incubated for 3 h (5% CO2, 37degreesC). To terminate the infection and induce IL8 expression, the medium was replaced with fresh DMEM supplemented with 2% FCS, 100ug/ul gentamicin and with or without 10 ng/ml TNFalpha and incubated for additional 3 h. Cells were than washed with 2 ml of cold TBS (20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl), scraped with 1 ml of cold TBS, collected and centrifuged, (800 g, 2 min, 4degreesC). RNA was extracted using the MasterPure Complete DNA and RNA Purification Kit (EPICENTRE Biotechnologies) and used to synthesize cDNA with the Verso cDNA kit (Thermo scientific). hHPRT transcript levels were used to normalize total RNA levels in samples. Real time analysis was than conducted using Absolute Blue QPCR SYBR Green (Thermo scientific) in a real-time cycler (Rotor-Gene 6000, Corbett).

Transfection of pCherry-nleE into HeLa cells and p65 staining

After 24 h, the medium was replaced with fresh DMEM containing, or not containing, 10 ng/ml TNFalpha. Cells were then stained using anti-p65 (SC109, Cell Signaling) antibodies (1:300 in TBS) overnight and further stained with CY-488 goat anti-rabbit (Cell Signaling) (1:1000 in TBS) for 1 h. NleE is involved in blocking IL8 secretion. HeLa cells (8x104 per well, seeded in 24-well plate) were infected for 3h with different EPEC strains as indicated or remained uninfected (N/I). After 3.5 h, supernatants were replaced with 300 ul DMEM, 2% FCS, and 50 microg/ml gentamycin with or without 10 ng/ml TNFalpha. After 16 h, 100 microl of cleared supernatant taken from each well was used for IL-8 measurements using Human CXCL8/IL-8 Quantikine immunoassay assay (R & D), according to the manufacturer's instructions. PBS and IL-8 were used as negative and positive controls for the detection assay. The relative amounts of IL-8 are shown. The experiment was done twice in duplicates and typical results are shown. Standard errors are indicated by bars. In the case of DeltaescV (indicated by a vanishing colored bar), the signal was above the upper limit of the detection levels. Untreated and uninfected cells secreted approximately300 units of IL-8, whereas TNFalpha treatment induced a 10-fold increase in IL-8 secretion (approximately3000 units) (Fig. 4B). In contrast, pre-infection with wild-type EPEC or with the DeltaIE2 mutant (WTDeltaIE2), reduced IL-8 secretion below the detection levels, even upon TNFalpha treatment. Furthermore, the TTSS escV mutant was completely deficient in blocking IL-8 secretion. In conclusion, EPEC strongly reduces IL-8 secretion by a TTSS-dependent mechanism. Importantly, we found that the DeltanleE or DeltanleEB mutants were strongly deficient in blocking IL-8 secretion but not as deficient as the TTSS mutant (escV). These results show that (i) NleE is required for full inhibition of IL-8 secretion and (ii) other putative TTSS effector(s) might function in parallel to NleE to inhibit IL-8 expression and/or secretion.
