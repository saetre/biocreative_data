Characterization of Mce4A protein of Mycobacterium tuberculosis: role in invasion and survival

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 4 9 4 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 71 74 71 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce</italic>
###xml 87 113 87 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 119 125 119 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4A </italic>
###xml 176 182 176 182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1A </italic>
###xml 233 248 233 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 280 285 280 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 425 441 425 441 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 462 467 462 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 87 113 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 233 248 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 425 440 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 527 531 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The mce4 operon is one of the four homologues of mammalian cell entry (mce) operons of Mycobacterium tuberculosis. The mce4A (Rv3499c) gene within this operon is homologous to mce1A (Rv0169), that has a role in host cell invasion by M. tuberculosis. Our earlier reports show that mce4 operon is expressed during the stationary phase of growth of the bacillus in culture and during the course of infection in mammalian hosts. M. tuberculosis carrying mutation in mce4 operon shows growth defect and reduced survival in infected mice. However, the intracellular localization of Mce4A protein and its direct role in cell entry or survival of the bacillus has not been demonstrated so far.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 141 149 141 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 197 203 197 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4A </italic>
###xml 233 239 233 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1A </italic>
###xml 270 278 270 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 414 430 414 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 141 148 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 270 277 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 281 286 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 414 435 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
By transmission electron microscopy we have demonstrated that recombinant Mce4A protein facilitates the invasion of non-pathogenic strain of E. coli into non-phagocytic HeLa cells. We observe that mce4A gene has a role comparable to mce1A in the survival of recombinant E. coli in human macrophages. Using antibodies raised against Mce4A protein, we show that the protein is localized in the cell wall fraction of M. tuberculosis H37Rv stationary phase culture only.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 115 130 115 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 174 182 174 182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 115 130 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 174 181 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Mce4A protein is expressed during the stationary phase of broth culture and localizes in the cell wall fraction of M. tuberculosis. Mce4A protein expressed in non-pathogenic E. coli enables it to enter and survive within HeLa cells and the macrophages. As Mce4A protein is expressed during later phase of mycobacterial growth, our results raise the possibility of it playing a role in maintenance of persistent tubercular infection.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 122 149 122 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 237 238 237 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 239 240 239 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 319 335 319 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 444 445 444 445 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 122 148 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 166 172 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">people</span>
###xml 319 334 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
The World Health Organization has estimated that nearly one-third of the world's population is now latently infected with Mycobacterium tuberculosis and 8-10 million people develop active disease resulting in 2 million deaths each year [1,2]. The prevalence of this infection is largely due to the extended dormancy of M. tuberculosis in the host and its ability to cause disease even in the face of a highly orchestrated host immune response [3].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 0 16 0 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 91 92 91 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 176 177 176 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 223 224 223 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 225 226 225 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 0 15 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
M. tuberculosis is believed to primarily invade and replicate within alveolar macrophages [4]. It has the ability to enter A549 cells of alveolar epithelial origin in culture [5] and also invade other non-phagocytic cells [6,7].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 98 106 98 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 253 254 253 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 255 256 255 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 319 327 319 327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 379 380 379 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 418 422 418 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce </italic>
###xml 433 449 433 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 543 545 543 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 637 641 637 641 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce </italic>
###xml 652 668 652 668 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 709 711 709 711 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 712 714 712 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 755 760 755 760 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 775 780 775 780 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1 </italic>
###xml 914 919 914 919 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 972 988 972 988 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 1006 1008 1006 1008 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 1095 1097 1095 1097 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 1098 1100 1098 1100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1257 1259 1257 1259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 1306 1311 1306 1311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 1315 1320 1315 1320 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 1331 1347 1331 1347 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 1377 1379 1377 1379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1450 1455 1450 1455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 1492 1508 1492 1508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 1543 1545 1543 1545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1604 1609 1604 1609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 1645 1661 1645 1661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 98 105 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 319 326 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 433 454 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 652 673 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 833 840 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbits</span>
###xml 845 856 <span type="species:ncbi:10141">guinea pigs</span>
###xml 972 987 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1331 1352 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 1371 1375 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1492 1507 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1537 1541 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1645 1660 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Role of Mce1A protein in the entry and survival of the pathogen is demonstrated using recombinant E. coli carrying cloned copy of the gene as well as latex microspheres coated with recombinant Mce1A protein, both of which were able to enter HeLa cells [8,9]. Additionally, it has been demonstrated that the recombinant E. coli expressing Mce1A can survive longer in these cells [8]. The continued analysis of the four mce operons of M. tuberculosis H37Rv genome has shown significant conservation of the protein sequences in the four operons [10]. The redundancy of these operons is explained by functional significance of the different mce operons of M. tuberculosis H37Rv by various studies including ours [11-15]. Earlier we reported the expression of mce4 and absence of mce1 transcripts in the specific tissues of both infected rabbits and guinea pigs during advanced disease conditions, which suggested that mce4 operon, may have a role in the long term survival of M. tuberculosis in host's tissue [11]. The survival mechanism of dormant tubercle bacilli is still under intense research [16-18]. It is suggested that mycobacterial persistence may rise as a response to environmental stress, such as reduced oxygen concentration within a host tissue [19]. It is demonstrated that deletion mutants of mce3 and mce4 operons of M. tuberculosis H37Rv are attenuated in mice [13]. By deleting eight genes and the first 250 bp of the ninth gene from mce4 operon it was shown that survival of M. tuberculosis was significantly reduced in mice [13]. However, direct involvement of individual proteins from mce4 operon in the survival or uptake of M. tuberculosis into the mammalian cells was not demonstrated.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 62 67 62 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 176 184 176 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 202 208 202 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4A </italic>
###xml 244 253 244 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 288 304 288 304 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 140 145 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 176 183 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 288 303 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
In the present study we validate the role of Mce4A protein of mce4 operon not only in cell invasion but also in survival of the pathogen in human macrophages using recombinant E. coli expressing cloned mce4A gene. The cellular localization and in vitro expression of the Mce4A protein in M. tuberculosis was also analyzed.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Methods
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Bacterial strains, cells and culture conditions
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 0 16 0 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 0 21 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 128 134 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
M. tuberculosis H37Rv was grown as shake-cultures in Middlebrook 7H9 broth (Difco) supplemented with OADC (oleic acid, albumin [bovine, fraction V], dextrose, catalase [Difco]) and 2% glycerol at 37degreesC.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 623 624 611 612 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 0 7 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 157 162 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 331 337 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 509 513 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
E. coli DH5alpha and BL-21 (DE3) cells were grown in Luria-Bertani (LB) broth or on LB agar in presence of kanamycin (25 mug/ml) where ever appropriate. The human epithelial cell line HeLa cells were grown in complete Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium (DMEM, Gibco) supplemented with 10 mM sodium pyruvate (Sigma), 10% FBS (fetal bovine serum, Gibco), HEPES (Sigma) and 10 mug of penicillin-streptomycin/ml (Sigma). THP-1 monocyte cell line was cultured in RPMI 1640 (Sigma) supplemented with 10% FCS (fetal calf serum, Gibco) and 2 mM L-glutamine (Sigma). Both the cell lines were grown at 37degreesC in presence of 5% CO2. PMA (phorbol myristate acetate, Sigma) was used for the adhesion of THP-1 cells.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Cloning, expression and purification of Mce1A and Mce4A
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 17 33 17 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 116 118 116 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 133 139 133 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1A </italic>
###xml 161 167 161 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4A </italic>
###xml 212 228 212 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 241 243 241 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 318 324 318 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1A </italic>
###xml 350 356 350 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4A </italic>
###xml 380 381 380 381 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 413 416 413 416 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sac</italic>
###xml 482 486 482 486 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hind</italic>
###xml 17 38 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 212 227 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Genomic DNA from M. tuberculosis H37Rv was extracted according to the CTAB (cetyltrimethylammonium bromide) method [20]. Full length mce1A (Rv0169, 1365 bp) and mce4A (Rv3499c, 1203 bp) genes were amplified from M. tuberculosis genomic DNA [21] by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) using the primers mce1F and mce1R for mce1A gene, mce4F and mce4R for mce4A gene as listed in Table 1. Each forward primer contained SacI restriction enzyme site whereas the reverse primers contained a HindIII restriction site.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 38 44 38 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1A </italic>
###xml 48 54 48 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4A </italic>
Primers used for PCR amplification of mce1A and mce4A gene.
###end p 19
###begin p 20
* Restriction sites in primers are indicated in bold letters.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 65 71 65 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1A </italic>
###xml 82 88 82 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4A </italic>
###xml 155 163 151 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 155 162 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The amplicons were cloned in pET28a (Novagen) to generate pET28a/mce1A and pET28a/mce4A with hexa histidine tags. Both DH5alpha and BL-21 (DE3) strains of E. coli were transformed with the recombinant plasmids for expression studies. The presence of the inserts was confirmed by restriction enzyme digestion and sequencing.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 27 35 27 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 27 34 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Overnight grown culture of E. coli BL-21 (DE3) containing the recombinant expression plasmids was diluted 1:10 in fresh LB broth containing kanamycin and grown with vigorous shaking at 37degreesC to an optical density (OD) of 0.6 at 600 nm. For transcription induction, IPTG (isopropyl thio-b-D-galactoside, Sigma) to a final concentration of 0.5 mM was added to the culture and incubation was continued for 2 h. The N-terminal His-tagged recombinant proteins were purified under denaturing condition on nitrilotriacetic acid (Ni-NTA) agarose columns (Qiagen) according to the manufacturer's instructions.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
Production of polyclonal antibodies against Mce4A protein
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 79 85 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 254 260 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
The antibodies against the Mce4A protein were raised in 5-month-old female NZW rabbit. For each immunization, 300 mug of protein in 0.5 ml PBS was mixed with 0.6 ml of incomplete Freund's adjuvant (Sigma) and administered subcutaneously at five sites in rabbit, followed by four booster injections of 300 mug each 2, 3, 4 and 6 weeks after the first injection. Serum was prepared from blood collected 2 weeks after the last booster dose. The animal handling was according to the guidelines of the Institutional Ethical Committee and Indian Council of Medical Research, India.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Preparation of subcellular fractions
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 0 16 0 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 426 428 420 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 429 431 423 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 550 566 544 560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 0 21 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 550 571 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
M. tuberculosis H37Rv was grown in one liter of 7H9 Middlebrook medium to mid-log phase (day 6) at 37degreesC on shaking (200 rpm) up to an OD of 0.6 at 600 nm. Cells were harvested by centrifugation at 10,000 g for 30 min and the culture supernatant was concentrated to obtain the culture filtrate protein (CFP). Pelleted cells were washed, resuspended in PBS and subcellular fractions were prepared as previously described [22,23]. Similarly, cellular fractions were also prepared from the stationary phase culture (day 10, OD of 1.0 at 600 nm) of M. tuberculosis H37Rv.
###end p 26
###begin title 27
Detection of protein expression
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 109 111 109 111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 686 701 686 701 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 187 191 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 197 202 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 588 595 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbits</span>
###xml 686 701 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
The protocol followed for Western blotting of purified Mce1A and Mce4A proteins was as described previously [24]. Anti-His monoclonal antibody (1:2000 dilution) and peroxidase-conjugated goat anti-mouse IgG (1:2000) were used as primary and secondary antibody respectively. Western blot was carried out to detect purified Mce4A protein and also the expression of Mce4A protein in the whole cell lysate of cells harvested on day 2 (OD-0.2 at 600 nm), day 4 (OD-0.4), day 7 (OD-0.7), and day 10 (stationary phase, OD-1.0) post inoculation. Polyclonal anti-Mce4A antibody (1:2000) raised in rabbits, was used as the primary antibody. For the intracellular localization of Mce4A protein in M. tuberculosis, Western blotting was performed with cytoplasmic (C), cell membrane (CM), cell wall (CW) fractions, culture filtrate protein (CFP) and whole cell lysate (CL) prepared from both log and stationary phase culture as described above.
###end p 28
###begin title 29
Invasion assay and Electron microscopy
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 39 41 39 41 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sup>
###xml 114 119 114 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1A</italic>
###xml 128 134 128 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4A </italic>
###xml 649 657 637 645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 864 865 846 847 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 923 931 905 913 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 649 656 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 923 930 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Invasion of HeLa cells seeded at 2 x 106 cells per well into 6-well plates by BL-21 cells transformed with pET28a/mce1A, pET28a/mce4A and empty vector pET28a was assayed. Bacterial cultures grown overnight were diluted (1:10) in fresh medium containing kanamycin and incubated further with vigorous shaking to reach an OD of 0.6 at 600 nm and induced with 0.5 mM IPTG for 2 h at 37degreesC. Following the induction, the cells were washed once with PBS and resuspended in 5 ml of PBS. Prior to the infection, monolayer of HeLa cells were charged with fresh medium (DMEM, supplemented with 10% FCS) and incubated at 37degreesC for 30 min. Recombinant E. coli cells were added to the monolayer at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 10:1 and incubated at 37degreesC for 4 h, washed three times with PBS and processed for electron microscopy as described previously [9] and examined under CM-10 electron microscope. Since the E. coli strain used is kanamycin resistant, the cells were not treated with kanamycin or gentamycin but were extensively washed post-infection to remove the extra-cellular bacteria that failed to enter the mammalian cells.
###end p 30
###begin title 31
Survival assay
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 33 35 33 35 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6 </sup>
THP-1 cells were seeded at 2 x 106 cells per well into 12-well plates. PMA (20 ng/ml) was added for the cell adhesion. Recombinant bacterial cultures induced with IPTG were incubated with THP-1 cells at a MOI of 10:1. Prior to the infection, THP-1 cells were charged with fresh medium (RPMI supplemented with 10% FCS and 2 mM L-glutamine) and incubated at 37degreesC for 30 min. Infection was continued for 1 h at 37degreesC.

###xml 744 750 744 750 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4A </italic>
###xml 776 782 776 782 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1A </italic>
###xml 806 812 806 812 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4A </italic>
###xml 839 845 839 845 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1A </italic>
###xml 873 889 873 889 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 106 121 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 468 475 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 590 597 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 725 732 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 873 888 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
The initial step in the pathogenesis by intracellular pathogens is the invasion of host cells. In case of M. tuberculosis, Mce1A protein of mce1 operon was the first protein of the pathogen to be implicated in invasion and survival inside macrophages [8]. Although Mce2A protein of mce2 operon exhibits 67% amino acid identity with Mce1A, latex beads coated with Mce2A are not internalized by HeLa cells [9]. In the present study we have demonstrated that recombinant E. coli expressing Mce4A protein can enter non-phagocytic HeLa cells. Further, Mce4A protein also enables the recombinant E. coli to survive inside THP-1 macrophages. The estimation of CFU demonstrates that the rate of intracellular survival of recombinant E. coli expressing mce4A was comparable to that of mce1A indicating thereby that mce4A may have a role similar to mce1A in invasion and survival of M. tuberculosis inside the host cells.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 180 182 180 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 183 185 183 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 425 427 425 427 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
Molecular modeling studies using bioinformatics tools have shown that cell entry epitope is exposed on the surface of Mce1A, Mce3A and Mce4A proteins but not on the Mce2A protein [26,27]. This suggests that the cell entry epitope of Mce1A, Mce3A and Mce4A, but not of Mce2A, may be available for interaction with mammalian cells. Subsequently a report appeared confirming the role of Mce3A and Mce3E in mammalian cell entry [12].
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 45 50 45 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 72 88 72 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 167 183 167 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 295 300 295 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 359 375 359 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 399 404 399 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 485 494 485 494 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 566 568 566 568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 612 617 612 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1 </italic>
###xml 699 704 699 704 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 790 795 790 795 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 843 845 843 845 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 72 87 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 167 182 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 359 374 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 678 682 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The recently emerging reports on the role of mce4 operon in survival of M. tuberculosis in host tissue indicates that Mce4 proteins may be involved in maintaining the M. tuberculosis in a nutrient deficient environment for long term survival [14]. It has been suggested that the proteins of the mce4 operon may operate as a major cholesterol import system of M. tuberculosis because strains lacking mce4 operon exhibit drastically reduced ability to take up and metabolize cholesterol in vitro and hence grow poorly when cholesterol is the primary source of carbon [14]. It was reported earlier that mutation in mce1 operon produces growth defect in early phase of infection in mice and mutation in mce4 operon produces growth defect after 3-4 weeks post infection, indicating thereby that mce4 operon is required in later phase of infection [28].
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 126 142 126 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 126 147 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
The localization study showed that the Mce4A protein was present in the cell wall fraction of the stationary phase culture of M. tuberculosis H37Rv further strengthening our results of transmission electron microscopy and cell invasion studies.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 139 155 139 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 139 154 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
We conclude that the results of the present study strongly support the role of Mce4A protein in invasion and the intracellular survival of M. tuberculosis and suggest that it may have a role in persistence of mycobacterial infection in the host tissue.
###end p 57
###begin title 58
Conclusion
###end title 58
###begin p 59
###xml 75 91 75 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 380 385 380 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 606 622 606 622 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 75 90 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 418 424 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 429 439 <span type="species:ncbi:10141">guinea pig</span>
###xml 606 621 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
There are many studies reported in the literature to elucidate dormancy in M. tuberculosis and the survival mechanism of dormant tubercle bacilli is not fully understood. It is suggested that mycobacterial persistence might arise as a response to environmental stress, such as reduced oxygen concentration within host tissue. Our earlier observation of preferential expression of mce4 operon in the internal organs of rabbit and guinea pig and also during the stationary phase in culture in conjunction with the results presented here strongly support a role for Mce4A protein in the long term survival of M. tuberculosis inside its host.
###end p 59
