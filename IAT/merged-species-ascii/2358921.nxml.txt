A proteome map of the zebrafish (Danio rerio) lens reveals similarities between zebrafish and mammalian crystallin expression
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Purpose
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 46 55 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
To characterize the crystallin content of the zebrafish lens using two-dimensional gel electrophoresis (2-DE). These data will facilitate future investigations of vertebrate lens development, function, and disease.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 6 15 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Adult zebrafish lens proteins were separated by 2-DE, and the resulting spots were identified by matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time of flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF MS). The relative proportion of each crystallin was quantified by image analysis, and phosphospecific staining was used to identify phosphorylated alpha-crystallins. The proportion of each crystallin in the soluble and insoluble fraction of the lens was also determined by resolving these lens fractions separately by 2-DE.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 75 84 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 147 156 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 200 205 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 298 307 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 355 361 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 462 471 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
alpha-, beta-, and gamma-crystallins comprised 7.8, 36.0, and 47.2% of the zebrafish lens, respectively. While the alpha-crystallin content of the zebrafish lens is less than the amounts found in the human lens, the ratio of alphaA:alphaB crystallin is very similar. The phosphorylation pattern of zebrafish alphaA-crystallins was also similar to that of humans. The most abundant gamma-crystallins were the diverse gammaMs, comprising 30.5% of the lens. Intact zebrafish crystallins were generally more common in the soluble fraction with truncated versions more common in the insoluble fraction.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusions
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 59 68 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 95 101 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 236 245 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
While the total alpha- and gamma-crystallin content of the zebrafish lens differs from that of humans, similarities in alpha-crystallin ratios and modifications and a link between crystallin truncation and insolubility suggest that the zebrafish is a suitable model for the vertebrate lens. The proteome map provided here will be of value to future studies of lens development, function, and disease.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Introduction
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 89 90 89 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 168 169 168 169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 170 171 170 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 184 185 184 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 186 187 186 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 229 231 229 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 232 234 232 234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 251 253 251 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 516 518 516 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 519 521 519 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 619 620 619 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 621 623 621 623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 624 626 624 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 627 629 627 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 934 936 927 929 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 937 939 930 932 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 940 942 933 935 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 112 121 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 505 514 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 578 587 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1076 1085 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Fishes have become a valuable tool for the comparative study of vertebrate eye diseases [1]. In particular, the zebrafish has been used to investigate eye development [2-7], glaucoma [8,9], retinal degeneration and regeneration [10,11], and cataract [12]. This species' short generation time and nearly completed genome facilitate its use as a model vertebrate. Furthermore, the genes for a large number of lens crystallins, the major structural and protective proteins of the lens, have been cloned from zebrafish [13-16]. While several studies have examined the expression of zebrafish crystallins at the mRNA level [3,12,14-16], there are few data on the relative proportions or posttranslational modifications of the proteins themselves. Previous attempts to calculate the relative proportions of each crystallin family have been hampered by the inability to separate alpha- and beta-crystallins by size exclusion chromatography [14,17,18]. A proteomics approach using two-dimensional electrophoresis (2-DE) such as the one used in this study will increase the utility of zebrafish as a model for eye disease and provide valuable data for future investigations of lens development.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 114 116 114 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 117 119 117 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 134 136 134 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 353 355 342 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 356 358 345 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 359 361 348 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
###xml 540 542 525 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 618 620 603 605 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 621 623 606 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r26">26</xref>
###xml 125 132 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 506 515 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 599 608 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 662 671 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
2-DE has been used successfully to describe the crystallin content and modifications that occur in the mammalian [19,20] and chicken [21] lens. Protein truncation and other modifications such as phosphorylation, deamidation, and methylation alter the protective chaperone activity of alpha-crystallins and the solubility of beta- and gamma-crystallins [20,22-24]. These posttranslational modifications accumulate with age and increase the likelihood of cataract. We previously performed a 2-DE analysis of zebrafish lens alpha-crystallins [16], but very few other studies have used the technique on zebrafish tissues [25,26]. A more detailed proteome map of the zebrafish lens will facilitate the study of age and stress-related changes to crystallins and their impact on lens function. Comparison of these data with those from other vertebrate species can provide additional insight into lens function and disease.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 102 111 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 384 393 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 474 483 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 572 581 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
In this study, we used 2-DE and mass spectrometry (MS) to detail the expression of crystallins in the zebrafish lens. Although we found that alpha-crystallin levels are lower than in the mammalian lens, the ratio between alphaA- and alphaB-crystallins is very similar, suggesting that these proteins play similar roles in both groups. We also show that similar to the mammalian lens, zebrafish alphaA-crystallin contains many phosphorylated isoforms and that truncations of zebrafish crystallins are correlated with protein insolubility. These data support the use of the zebrafish as a model for studying the development, function, and aging of the vertebrate lens.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Methods
###end title 14
###begin title 15
Separation and visualization of lens proteins
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 56 65 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Six to eight lenses were collected from adult wild type zebrafish and homogenized in 600 microl of sample buffer (8 M urea, 2% CHAPS, 50 mM DTT, 0.2% Bio-Lyte 3/10 ampholyte, and 0.001% bromophenol blue; Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA) to solubilize total lens protein. Fish were obtained from aquarium stores, and the exact ages were not known. All fish were euthanized using methods authorized by Ashland University's Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee. Lens homogenates were centrifuged at 15,000x g for 20 min to remove any unsolubilized material, and the protein in the supernatant was quantified using the RC DC protein assay kit (Bio-Rad). One hundred and fifty micrograms of lens homogenate were focused on immobilized pH gradient (IPG) strips (11 cm; pH 3-10 nonlinear, pH 5-8, and pH 4-7; Bio-Rad). Second dimension separation was performed on 12% SDS-PAGE gels with subsequent Coomassie staining. Gels were digitally imaged with a Kodak 440 CF Imagestation (Kodak, Rochester, NY), and the proportion of total protein found in each spot on the pH 3-10 nonlinear gels was quantified by densitometry using Kodak 1D Image Analysis software (Kodak). The proportion of each crystallin was calculated as the mean of three separate lens samples. Some gels were stained with Pro-Q Diamond phosphoprotein gel stain (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) to identify phosphorylated proteins. These gels were subsequently stained with SYPRO Ruby protein gel stain to visualize all protein spots (Invitrogen).
###end p 16

###begin title 18
Identification of two-dimensional gel electrophoresis spots by matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time of flight mass spectrometry
###end title 18
###begin p 19
Gel spots were selected for peptide mass fingerprinting. Coomassie stained spots were excised from SDS-PAGE gels, destained, and digested overnight with proteomics grade trypsin (Sigma, St. Louis, MO). Digest solutions were acidified with 0.1% trifluoroacetic acid and mixed with the matrix alpha-cyano-4-hydroxycinnamic acid. Samples were analyzed by matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time of flight (MALDI-TOF) mass spectrometry using a Bruker MicroFLEX instrument (Bruker Daltonics, Billerica, MA). Externally calibrated, positive-ion mass spectra were obtained in reflection mode. The resulting mass spectra were compared to theoretical peptide mass maps using  software and the  non-redundant protein database. Further confirmation of protein identity was performed on selected samples by the analysis of post-source decay fragments of tryptic peptide ions.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Two-dimensional gel electrophoresis analysis of soluble and insoluble protein fractions
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">Zebrafish</span>
Zebrafish lenses from 12 individuals were homogenized with a Wheaton homogenizer (Wheaton Science Products, Millville, NJ) in 800 microl of 20 mM sodium phosphate buffer containing protease and phosphatase inhibitors (Roche Molecular Biochemicals, Basel, Switzerland). The resulting homogenate was centrifuged at 15,000x g for 15 min. The supernatant containing the soluble protein fraction was removed, and the insoluble pellet was resuspended in 800 microl of 2-DE sample buffer (see above; Bio-Rad). The protein content of the soluble and insoluble fractions was quantified using a Bradford assay and RC DC protein assay, respectively (Bio-Rad). Both samples were diluted with 2-DE sample buffer to apply 150 mug to 11-cm IPG strips (pH 5-8) before isoelectric focusing.
###end p 21

###begin title 23
Results
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 192 200 192 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 337 339 337 339 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 437 445 433 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 572 580 560 568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 719 721 703 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r27">27</xref>
###xml 722 724 706 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
###xml 646 655 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Our 2-DE analysis resolved over 80 protein spots. Sixty of these spots were identified by MALDI-TOF MS as one of 28 different crystallins, and another four were identified as non-crystallins (Figure 1). The large number of crystallin spots reflects an abundance of posttranslational modifications. Similar to data from a previous study [17], we found that the gamma-crystallins were the most abundant crystallin family in the fish lens (Figure 2). However, our data are the first to show that of the gamma-crystallins, the aquatic specific gammaMs were the most abundant (Figure 2). Furthermore, the 7.8% total alpha-crystallin shown here in the zebrafish lens is lower than previously reported values for fish lenses [27,28]. Details for each crystallin family are given in the following sections.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Quantitation and modification of alpha-crystallins
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 195 197 191 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 398 407 386 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1A</xref>
###xml 512 520 484 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 522 529 494 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 639 641 603 605 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
###xml 143 152 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 258 267 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 627 632 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Few studies have calculated the proportion of each major crystallin family in the fish lens. Furthermore, since it was recently shown that the zebrafish lens contains a second alphaB-crystallin [16], no study has determined the relative amounts of all three zebrafish alpha-crystallins. We found alpha-crystallins in 13 different 2-DE gel spots, suggesting several posttranslational modifications (Figure 1A). Total alpha-crystallin content in the lens was 7.8% with a 6.4:3.6:1 ratio of alphaA:alphaBa:alphaBb (Figure 2, Table 1). This value is very similar to the 3:2 ratio of alphaA:alphaB crystallin reported for the adult human lens [29].

###xml 79 81 75 77 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r32">32</xref>
###xml 175 183 167 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f3">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 456 465 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Phosphorylation has been shown to modify the function of alpha-crystallins [30-32]. We used a phosphoprotein-specific stain to identify phosphorylated alpha-crystallin spots (Figure 3). This stain identified four phosphorylated alphaA-crystallin spots of similar mass. These spots comprised a relatively small proportion of the alphaA-crystallin on the 2-DE gels, indicating that only a small proportion of alphaA-crystallin is phosphorylated in the adult zebrafish lens. No phosphorylated alphaBa- or alphaBb-crystallin was found.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
Quantitation of beta/gamma-crystallins
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 73 75 70 72 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 214 231 211 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;A3-crystallin</italic>
###xml 346 355 334 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1A</xref>
###xml 357 364 345 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 1062 1064 1029 1031 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
###xml 1173 1180 1137 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">Zebrafish</span>
###xml 166 175 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 316 325 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 863 872 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1035 1042 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 1071 1080 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Zebrafish and mammalian beta-crystallins are similar in their diversity [13]. Both taxa have an acidic and basic group whose members are well conserved, although the zebrafish genome lacks an ortholog to mammalian betaA3-crystallin. We identified five betaA-crystallins and three betaB-crystallins comprising 36% of zebrafish lens total protein (Figure 1A, Table 1). Many of the beta-crystallins were extensively modified. betaB1 and betaB2, for example, were identified as 11 and 8 spots, respectively. Many of the beta-crystallin modifications differ in mass, suggesting that they are truncations. Other beta-crystallin modifications are of similar mass but have different isoelectric points, which may be due to phosphorylation or other modifications. The exact nature of these modifications still needs to be examined. betaB3 is the largest crystallin in the zebrafish lens due to the COOH-terminal proline-asparagine (PNPN) and proline-alanine (PAPA) repeats that are similar to the repeating PAPA sequence found in mammalian and chicken betaB1-crystallin [33]. The zebrafish betaB-crystallin genes are not found on the same chromosome as are the mammalian orthologs (Table 1).
###end p 31
###begin p 32

###xml 240 247 225 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 424 426 401 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>

Unlike the beta crystallins, zebrafish and mammalian gamma-crystallins have undergone greater evolutionary divergence [13]. Most noticeably, the gammaA-F group does not occur in the zebrafish but is replaced by at least 11 gammaM proteins (Table 1). Zebrafish also have four gammaS proteins instead of the one found in mammals. We identified protein resulting from all the gamma-crystallin genes described by Wistow et al. [13] except gammaM5 and betagammax (Figure 1B,C; Table 1), totaling 47.2% of total protein (Figure 2). In addition, we identified gammaM2D1- and gammaM2D2-crystallins. The gammaM-crystallins were the most abundant family in the zebrafish lens (30.5%; Figure 2). These were also the most basic gamma-crystallins and the most difficult to resolve similar to the gammaA-F crystallins in mammals [20]. Two large unresolved gammaM-crystallin areas appeared when focused on the pH 3-10 nonlinear IPG strips (Figure 1B). Improved resolution on pH 7-10 strips showed that these areas also contained minor amounts of gammaS3-crystallin (Figure 1C). Therefore, our calculations of gammaM-crystallin content using the pH 3-10 nonlinear strips may slightly overestimate the total gammaM-crystallin content of the zebrafish lens.

###begin title 34
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">Zebrafish</span>
Zebrafish crystallin truncation is correlated with insolubility
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 123 125 123 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
###xml 619 627 607 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f4">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 991 999 969 977 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f4">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 1272 1280 1231 1239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f4">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 156 165 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 289 298 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
The accumulation of protein modifications with age can decrease the solubility of crystallins and contribute to cataracts [20,24]. To determine whether the zebrafish lens will make a good model for studying the impact of crystallin insolubility, we separately quantified the proportion of zebrafish crystallins in the soluble and insoluble fractions of the lens. We found that putatively truncated proteins were more abundant in the insoluble fraction. For example, the only alpha-crystallin spots that were found at equal or greater abundance in the insoluble fraction were lower mass alphaA- and alphaBa-crystallins (Figure 4, white arrows). These spots were not phosphorylated, further suggesting that their insolubility is linked to truncation. All other alphaA-crystallin spots are much more abundant in the soluble fraction. The same pattern is found in betaB1- and betaB3-crystallins. Lower mass spots of these two crystallins are also found preferentially in the insoluble fraction (Figure 4). Unlike the intact alpha-and beta-crystallins, the two gammaN-crystallins were not found preferentially in the soluble fraction. gammaN1-crystallin occurred in both fractions in roughly equal amounts while gammaN2-crystallin occurred primarily in the insoluble fraction (Figure 4).
###end p 35

###begin title 37
Discussion
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 231 240 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 583 592 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
This is the first study to quantify the proportions of individual crystallins in a fish lens and investigate their posttranslational modifications. While there are some significant differences between the crystallin content of the zebrafish and mammalian lens, most notably the lower amounts of alpha-crystallin and the higher amounts and greater diversity of gamma-crystallins, there are also some significant similarities. In particular, similarities in alpha-crystallin ratios and modifications as well as the connection between crystallin truncation and insolubility support the zebrafish as a useful model for lens function and disease. This proteome map will also be of value to future studies of lens development.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 298 300 287 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 301 303 290 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 304 306 293 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 484 486 469 471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r27">27</xref>
###xml 487 489 472 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
###xml 925 927 902 904 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 1267 1269 1236 1238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
###xml 473 482 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 645 654 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 732 735 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 934 943 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1186 1195 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1548 1557 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Our calculation of alpha-crystallin content is the first accurate measurement for the fish lens. The 2-DE approach used here resolves a previously noted difficulty in separating fish alpha-and beta-crystallins by size exclusion chromatography, which made it difficult to quantify their expression [14,17,18]. Two previous studies found alpha-crystallin proportions of fish lens to be 12% and 41.6% for two different species, both higher than the 7.8% reported here for the zebrafish [27,28]. It is possible that alpha-crystallin values do differ greatly between fish species. Future studies will need to determine if the lower proportion in the zebrafish is typical among fish species. The low proportion of alpha-crystallin in the rat lens (20%) compared to other mammals has been hypothesized to be due to this species' shorter life span and subsequent reduced need for protection against age-induced protein denaturation [19]. The zebrafish has a similar lifespan of about three years. Examination of longer-lived fish species could test a possible link between alpha-crystallin content and lifespan. An alternative hypothesis is that the small percentage of alpha-crystallin in the zebrafish and rodent lenses is a reflection of their high total protein content [29]. Rodents have rigid, dense lenses that may require an increased proportion of gamma-crystallin and reduced alpha-crystallin levels. If alpha-crystallin content reflects total protein concentrations, long-lived fishes should have low alpha-crystallin proportions similar to the zebrafish.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 215 217 211 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 218 220 214 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 393 395 385 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 396 398 388 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 399 401 391 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r34">34</xref>
###xml 948 950 920 922 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
###xml 992 994 964 966 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r35">35</xref>
###xml 1010 1012 982 984 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r36">36</xref>
###xml 1153 1173 1121 1141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dissostichus mawsoni</italic>
###xml 58 67 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 605 614 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 917 922 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1132 1151 <span type="species:ncbi:36200">Antarctic toothfish</span>
###xml 1153 1173 <span type="species:ncbi:36200">Dissostichus mawsoni</span>
The large amount of gamma-crystallin (47.2%) found in the zebrafish lens is similar to amounts measured in other fish species from evolutionarily diverse groups and appears to be a general feature of the fish lens [17,18]. It has been suggested that an abundance of small, polydisperse gamma-crystallins allows tighter protein packing to produce the dense lenses needed for underwater vision [13,17,34]. Therefore, the expansion of the gammaM-crystallin family in fishes may be an adaptation for underwater vision. Our finding that gammaM-crystallins are the most abundant of the gamma-crystallins in the zebrafish lens supports this hypothesis. Both the diversity and high concentration of gammaM-crystallins suggest that they are important for producing an aquatic lens with a high refractive index. In a similar way, the dense rodent lens expresses all six mammalian gammaA-F proteins while the softer, less dense human lens only expresses two [29] and the soft avian lens expresses none [35]. Kiss et al. [36] argue that the large number of gammaM-crystallins also facilitated the evolution of cold tolerance in the lens of the Antarctic toothfish, Dissostichus mawsoni.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 212 229 212 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;A4-crystallin</italic>
###xml 249 265 246 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;B-crystallin</italic>
###xml 319 326 313 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 470 472 461 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r37">37</xref>
###xml 579 586 562 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 832 839 812 816 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;A4-</italic>
###xml 844 861 821 835 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;B1-crystallin</italic>
###xml 916 918 890 892 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r37">37</xref>
###xml 49 58 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 202 211 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 549 558 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 610 619 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 824 831 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 999 1008 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Recent efforts to complete the annotation of the zebrafish genome allow the comparison of crystallin gene chromosomal locations and linkages with other vertebrate taxa. Unlike in mammals and birds, the zebrafish betaA4-crystallin gene and the three betaB-crystallin genes are not closely linked on the same chromosome (Table 1). The linkage of these genes in mammals and birds may be due to an original clustering of the entire beta-crystallin family on one chromosome [37] as is currently seen in the mammalian gammaA-F-crystallins and most of the zebrafish gammaM-crystallins (Table 1). The dispersal of the zebrafish beta-crystallins to separate chromosomes may simply reflect the evolutionary distance from the last common ancestor of teleost fishes, birds, and mammals. The possibility that chromosomal linkage between chicken betaA4- and betaB1-crystallin results in a mechanistic linkage in their expression [37] could mean that the expression of these genes is controlled differently in the zebrafish than in other vertebrate taxa.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 47 49 47 49 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r38">38</xref>
###xml 336 338 329 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r38">38</xref>
###xml 644 651 617 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 903 905 860 862 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r38">38</xref>
###xml 1200 1208 1157 1165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 1282 1284 1239 1241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r38">38</xref>
###xml 178 187 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1063 1072 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Our study agrees with that of Vihtelic et al. [38], which used cDNA transcript abundance to infer that beta- and gamma-crystallins were the most highly expressed proteins in the zebrafish lens. However, our data also differ from this study in several ways. First, the three most abundant lens cDNA transcripts from the Vihtelic et al. [38] study were the gammaS1-, betaB3-, and betaB2-crystallins. The three most abundant crystallins in our 2-DE gels were betaA1-, betaB1-, and gammaS2-crystallin. However, since some of the 2-DE spots in our study contained several proteins, we may be underestimating the total abundance of some crystallins (Table 1). Second, this previous study found a greater abundance of transcripts for each of the two alphaB-crystallins than for alphaA-crystallin while our data indicate that alphaA-crystallin is the most abundant alpha-crystallin. And third, Vihtelic et al. [38] noted a large number of transcripts for cystatin B and suggested the possibility that this protein has been recruited as a taxon-specific crystallin in the zebrafish lens. We did not identify this protein in our 2-DE gels, although we did identify four other highly expressed non-crystallins (Figure 1), three of which were identified as cDNA transcripts by Vihtelic et al. [38] (actin, BTB domain containing protein 2, and galectin-related inter-fiber protein).
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 288 290 276 278 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r39">39</xref>
###xml 409 411 389 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 76 85 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 132 141 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 337 346 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 509 514 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 625 634 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 769 778 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 890 899 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1002 1011 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Several alpha-crystallin characteristics appear to be conserved between the zebrafish and mammalian lens. We previously showed that zebrafish alphaA-crystallin is a stronger chaperone and has greater thermal stability than alphaBa-crystallin, consistent with the pattern seen in mammals [39]. In this study, we show that even though the zebrafish lens expresses a third alpha-crystallin (alphaBb-crystallin) [16], the ratio of alphaA-crystallin to the combined alphaB-crystallins is very close to that of the human lens. These combined data support the conclusion that alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin play similar roles in the zebrafish and mammalian lens. The higher abundance and greater thermal stability of alphaA-crystallin compared to alphaB-crystallin in both the zebrafish and mammals reflect its similar role in preventing protein aggregation over the life of the lens. Furthermore, zebrafish and mammalian alphaA-crystallin share a similar phosphorylation pattern, although the exact nature of zebrafish phosphorylation still needs to be determined.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 562 564 538 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 681 683 657 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r40">40</xref>
###xml 794 796 770 772 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r41">41</xref>
###xml 797 799 773 775 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r43">43</xref>
###xml 1096 1098 1056 1058 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r39">39</xref>
###xml 1294 1296 1246 1248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 352 361 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 599 608 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 657 669 <span type="species:ncbi:70862">teleost fish</span>
###xml 776 785 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1010 1019 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1066 1075 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1190 1199 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1492 1501 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
Determining the function of alphaB-crystallin in the mammalian lens has been problematic because it is also expressed in numerous non-lenticular tissues. Therefore, any functional analysis of mammalian alphaB-crystallin might not be relevant to the lens but may instead reflect function in another tissue. The presence of two alphaB-crystallins in the zebrafish, one lens specific (alphaBa-crystallin) and one ubiquitous (alphaBb-crystallin), provides an excellent model for discriminating between lenticular and extra lenticular functions of alphaB-crystallin [16]. Gene duplications are common in zebrafish due to a genome-wide duplication event early in teleost fish evolution [40]. In many cases, the functions of a single mammalian protein become divided between the two zebrafish copies [41-43]. The higher expression of the lens specific alphaBa-crystallin compared to the ubiquitous alphaBb-crystallin (2.56% and 0.72%, respectively) suggests that alphaBa-crystallin plays a more prominent role in the zebrafish lens. The very low chaperone-like activity of zebrafish alphaBa-crystallin [39] may indicate that protective function is not a primary role for alphaB-crystallins in the zebrafish lens. The lower expression of the ubiquitous alphaBb-crystallin, which is a strong chaperone [16], further suggests that the alphaB-crystallin protective function is of greater importance outside of the lens. If the function of mammalian alphaB-crystallin had been subdivided between the two zebrafish proteins, our data support the hypothesis that chaperone activity is not a prominent role of alphaB-crystallin in the mammalian lens.
###end p 44

###xml 91 93 91 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r44">44</xref>
###xml 94 96 94 96 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r46">46</xref>

###xml 618 620 615 617 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r47">47</xref>
###xml 808 810 793 795 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r48">48</xref>
###xml 1147 1149 1116 1118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 155 164 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 654 663 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 1017 1022 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Crystallin truncation occurs with aging in the mammalian lens and contributes to cataract [44-46]. The link between insolubility and truncation in several zebrafish crystallins suggests that this species may make a good model for studying the effects of crystallin aging on lens function. It is possible that the loss of solubility seen in our data are not directly due to truncation but instead to other modifications such as deamidation or methylation [24]. Truncation of some beta-crystallins, for example, may be an adaptive response by the lens to limit the increasing insolubility caused by other modifications [47]. The preferential expression of zebrafish gammaN-crystallins in the insoluble fraction may be due to membrane interactions as has been shown for mammalian gammaE- and gammaF-crystallin [48]. These data could also result from gammaN-crystallin interaction with the cytoskeleton. Alternatively, reduced gammaN-crystallin solubility could be a feature of the protein itself as purified recombinant mouse gammaN-crystallin has reduced solubility and lower conformational stability compared to other vertebrate gamma-crystallins [13]. Future studies will need to detail the beta- and gamma-crystallin modifications that lead to insolubility.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 51 60 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 115 124 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 322 331 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 568 577 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 660 669 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
###xml 719 728 <span type="species:ncbi:7955">zebrafish</span>
This study identifies several similarities between zebrafish and mammalian crystallins that support the use of the zebrafish as a model for investigations of vertebrate lens development, function, and disease. Not only can this species be used similarly to traditional models like rodents to study the adult lens, but the zebrafish also provides the added benefit of having an externally laid egg and transparent embryo, allowing in vivo observation of lens and eye development. Furthermore, a relatively low expense and ease of maintenance adds to the utility of the zebrafish. Future studies will need to further detail the posttranslational modification of zebrafish crystallins and examine developmental changes in zebrafish lens protein expression. The proteome map provided here contributes a solid foundation for these investigations.
