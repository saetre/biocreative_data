###begin article-title 0
beta1 Integrin-Mediated Adhesion Signalling Is Essential for Epidermal Progenitor Cell Expansion
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: APC SW CB RF. Performed the experiments: APC HK HM MB CB. Analyzed the data: APC HK HM MB CB RF. Wrote the paper: APC SW RF.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 174 178 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
There is a major discrepancy between the in vitro and in vivo results regarding the role of beta1 integrins in the maintenance of epidermal stem/progenitor cells. Studies of mice with skin-specific ablation of beta1 integrins suggested that epidermis can form and be maintained in their absence, while in vitro data have shown a fundamental role for these adhesion receptors in stem/progenitor cell expansion and differentiation.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 55 59 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 171 175 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 441 445 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To elucidate this discrepancy we generated hypomorphic mice expressing reduced beta1 integrin levels on keratinocytes that developed similar, but less severe defects than mice with beta1-deficient keratinocytes. Surprisingly we found that upon aging these abnormalities attenuated due to a rapid expansion of cells, which escaped or compensated for the down-regulation of beta1 integrin expression. A similar phenomenon was observed in aged mice with a complete, skin-specific ablation of the beta1 integrin gene, where cells that escaped Cre-mediated recombination repopulated the mutant skin in a very short time period. The expansion of beta1 integrin expressing keratinocytes was even further accelerated in situations of increased keratinocyte proliferation such as wound healing.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
These data demonstrate that expression of beta1 integrins is critically important for the expansion of epidermal progenitor cells to maintain epidermal homeostasis.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 245 248 238 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Brakebusch1">[1]</xref>
Integrins are heterodimeric cell surface receptors consisting of one alpha and one beta subunit. They bind extracellular matrix (ECM) proteins and counter receptors such as VCAM, and play fundamental roles for tissue development and homeostasis [1]. The mammalian genome contains 18 alpha and 8 beta integrin genes whose proteins can give rise to 24 different integrin heterodimers. Integrins are expressed on almost all cells including keratinocytes of the skin.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 598 601 598 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Clayton1">[2]</xref>
###xml 889 892 838 841 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Watt1">[3]</xref>
###xml 1150 1153 1089 1092 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-vanderNeut1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1154 1157 1093 1096 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Raghavan1">[7]</xref>
###xml 630 636 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
The epidermis is a multilayered epithelium that protects from environmental assault and damage. The hallmark of the epidermis is its ability to self-renew throughout the entire life span of the organism. This is achieved with epidermal progenitor cells (EPCs) in the basal cell layer of the epidermis, which undergo an unlimited number of symmetric and asymmetric cell divisions, giving rise to daughter cells that either proliferate or exit the cell cycle and move to the suprabasal layer of the epidermis. There they undergo terminal differentiation and are eventually shed from the skin surface [2]. Basal keratinocytes of the murine skin normally express high levels of alpha2beta1, alpha3beta1 and alpha6beta4 integrins and alphanubeta5 that is weakly expressed. Expression of alpha5beta1, alphanubeta6 and alpha9beta1 integrins is induced upon wounding or in pathological conditions [3]. Tissue specific or constitutive deletion of these integrin chains revealed their crucial roles in skin development and homeostasis with the most striking phenotypes observed upon deletion of the beta1 integrins and the hemidesmosomal alpha6beta4 integrins [4]-[7].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 410 413 407 410 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Brakebusch2">[6]</xref>
###xml 577 580 568 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Grose1">[8]</xref>
###xml 962 965 944 947 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Jones1">[9]</xref>
###xml 966 970 948 952 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Jensen1">[11]</xref>
###xml 1259 1263 1235 1239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Wan1">[12]</xref>
###xml 1732 1736 1702 1706 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Carroll1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1738 1742 1708 1712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Romero1">[14]</xref>
###xml 7 11 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 441 445 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 691 695 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1186 1191 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Mutant mice lacking beta1 integrin in skin had multiple blisters due to impaired attachment of basal keratinocytes to the basement membrane (BM), a disorganized and hyperthickened interfollicular epidermis (IFE), impaired proliferation of interfollicular and hair matrix keratinocytes, delayed terminal differentiation in IFE, defects in the hair follicle morphology, progressive hair loss and dermal fibrosis [6]. Moreover, beta1-deficient mice showed severely delayed wound healing, which has been associated with impaired migration of beta1 integrin-deficient keratinocytes [8]. Despite the severe skin defects and complete hair loss, some of the beta1 skin specific conditional knockout mice survived up to 12 months suggesting that loss of beta1 integrin is not associated with a stem cell or progenitor cell depletion phenotype. This finding was unexpected, as previous data pointed to an important role for beta1 integrins for maintaining skin stem cells [9]-[11]. It has been shown that keratinocytes with high beta1 integrin levels display typical stem cell properties including high colony forming efficiency in vitro. Similar results were also obtained with cells from adult human palm, sole and breast skin that strongly expressed beta1 integrins [12]. Furthermore, it was shown that keratinocytes expressing high levels of beta1 integrin are slow cycling cells in vivo and cluster in the basal layer of the epidermis. Interestingly, integrin mediated adhesion to the ECM was shown to negatively regulate terminal differentiation of cultured keratinocytes in vitro, while ectopic expression of beta1 integrins in suprabasal cells leads to epidermal hyper-proliferation and perturbed keratinocyte differentiation in vivo [13], [14].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 165 170 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 369 373 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In order to clarify the discrepancy between the in vitro and in vivo results on the role of beta1 integrin in the maintenance of epidermal stem cells we generated a mouse strain with reduced beta1 integrin expression in keratinocytes of the skin. This approach allows to study the function of beta1 integrin in skin homeostasis avoiding the gross abnormalities seen in mice with skin specific ablation of beta1 integrin. Using this new model we could show that keratinocytes that express normal levels of beta1 integrin due to inefficient downregulation of beta1 integrin expression are able to quickly expand in the interfollicular epidermis and almost completely replace the mutant cells over time. This rapid expansion of cells ensures nearly normal epidermal homeostasis and strongly indicates that beta1 integrins play a very critical role for the proliferation and maintenance of EPC in vivo.
###end p 12

Results
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Generation of a beta1 integrin hypomorphic allele

###xml 1156 1166 1138 1148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0005488.s001">Figure S1C</xref>
###xml 124 129 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1026 1030 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To test how reduced expression of beta1 integrin affects skin development and maintenance we engineered a beta1 hypomorphic mouse strain by diminishing the stability of the beta1 integrin mRNA and thus the amount of beta1 integrin subunits. The hypomorphic gene mutation was obtained by introducing a 141-bp long wild-type (wt) cDNA fragment of the beta1 integrin gene coding for the entire beta1 integrin cytoplasmic tail, including the stop codon from exon 16 (called Cyto-cDNA), in frame into exon 15 (see Figure S1A in Supporting Information). In addition, a floxed neo-tk cassette was introduced into the targeting vector (hpmKIneo-tk+). The hpmKIneo-tk+ mutation was transferred into embryonic stem (ES) cells by homologous recombination (see Figure S1B, left panel, in Supporting Information), and the neo-tk selection cassette was subsequently removed by transient expression of Cre recombinase leaving one loxP site in the non coding region (hpmKIlox). The hpmKIlox/+ ES cells were used to generate germline chimeric mice (see Figure S1B, right panel, in Supporting Information). Successful germline transmission was confirmed by genomic PCR (see Figure S1C in Supporting Information) and by sequence analysis (not shown).

###xml 1411 1414 1405 1408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hpm</italic>
###xml 1416 1419 1410 1413 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">lox</sup>
The stop codon in exon 15 of the hpmKIlox allele should give rise to a transcript with a premature translation termination signal 200 nucleotides upstream of the exon-junction site generated upon splicing of exons 15 with exon 16. Consequently, this additional stop codon located 5' relative to the last exon of the beta1 integrin gene should significantly downregulate the hpmKIlox mRNA by the nonsense mediated decay (NMD) pathway [15]. To test whether the hpmKIlox mRNA was indeed subjected to NMD, we first analyzed the level of hpmKIlox mRNA in primary hpmKIlox/+ keratinocytes treated with the translation inhibitor emetine to abrogate the NMD pathway [16], [17]. As expected, incubation of subconfluent hpmKIlox/+ keratinocytes with emetine revealed that the hpmKIlox mRNA levels steadily increased with time of treatment suggesting that the hpmKIlox mRNA is short-lived and can be stabilized by emetine (Figure 1A). To test whether the emetine-mediated beta1 mRNA up-regulation is caused by inhibition of NMD or by an emetine triggered stress response [18], [19] primary hpmKIlox/+ keratinocytes were treated with the transcriptional inhibitor actinomycin D either in the presence or absence of emetine. Actinomycin D treatment influenced neither the levels of non-stabilized hpmKIlox mRNA nor the levels of emetine-stabilized hpmKIlox mRNA (Figure 1B) indicating that the increase of emetine-triggered hpmKIlox mRNA is caused by the inhibition of the NMD pathway.

To test whether the reduced expression of beta1 mRNA from the hpmKIlox allele is affecting skin homeostasis, we generated mice solely expressing the hpmKIlox allele in keratinocytes. To this end, we intercrossed the hpmKIlox/+ and floxed beta1 integrin strains with transgenic mice expressing the Cre recombinase under the Keratin 5 (K5) promoter [6], [22] to generate hpmKIlox/beta1fl; K5Cre (hpm) mice. The hpm mice lose the beta1 floxed allele in Cre expressing cells and thus, express only the hpmKIlox allele in keratinocytes of the IFE and the outer root sheath (ORS) of hair follicles (HFs). To determine the expression of integrins in 3-week-old hpm keratinocytes we measured beta1 mRNA levels with quantitative Real-Time (RT) PCR and integrin surface levels with flow cytometry. The beta1 mRNA levels were reduced by 93+/-2% in the hpm mice (Figure 1C), and the cell surface levels of beta1 and alpha2 integrins were reduced by 85.9+/-3% and 65.1+/-19%, respectively, compared to control cells (Figure 1D and see Figure S2 in Supporting Information). The expression of the hemidesmosomal integrins alpha6 and beta4 was slightly increased (see Figure S2 in Supporting Information). These data demonstrate that subjecting beta1 integrin mRNA to NMD leads to severely reduced beta1 integrin levels.

To assess whether the reduced beta1 integrin levels on hpm keratinocytes are sufficient for in vitro functions we isolated keratinocytes from 2.5-month-old wt and hpm mice and seeded them on a surface coated with a mixture of collagen I (Col1) and fibronectin [23]. While the majority of adherent wt cells began spreading one day after plating and grew to confluency 4 days later, hpm cells adhered but failed to spread and proliferate (Figure 3A). To determine the proliferation rates of wt and hpm keratinocytes we performed an ELISA-based BrdU incorporation assay and could not find proliferating hpm cells on Col1/FN surfaces (Figure 3B). To quantify adhesion, we plated wt and hpm keratinocytes on a mixture of Col1/FN or LM-332, respectively, and observed similar adhesion efficiencies in both genotypes (Figure 3C). Next we compared focal contact organization and F-actin distribution between wt and hpm cells after a culture period of 2 days on a Col1/FN mixture. Immunostaining for beta1 integrin, vinculin and paxillin, and visualization of F-actin with fluorescently labeled phalloidin revealed a complete absence of FA structures and a failure to organize F-actin into stress fibres in hpm keratinocytes (Figure 3D). The immunosignals of FA proteins were observed in the cytoplasm and F-actin appeared as clumps or fine filaments extending from the perinuclear region. Altogether these findings indicate that approximately15% of the normal beta1 integrin level is not sufficient to support normal epidermal and HF homeostasis in vivo and integrin functions in vitro.

In agreement with the external appearance (Figure 1E), histology of skin sections from 6.5 months old hpm mice revealed reduced and misshapen HFs, a mild dermal fibrosis, and dermal melanin deposits from perished HFs (Figure 2C). Surprisingly, we observed that while the hyperplasia of the IFE remained similar, the epidermal-dermal blisters almost completely disappeared (Figure 2D). We next determined if these escape mechanisms are also active in healing skin wounds and if the wound-induced hyperproliferation accelerates this process. To test this possibility we generated full-thickness excision wounds in hpm mice and wild-type littermates at the age of 8-12 weeks. Surprisingly, the rate of wound closure as well as the area of hyperproliferative epidermis was not reduced in hpm mice at day 5 after injury (see Figure S3A in Supporting Information). At day 13 after wounding, wounds in mice of both genotypes were fully reepithelialized. The only difference that we observed was an enlarged width of the healed wound and accordingly, an increased area of wound epidermis (see Figure S3B in Supporting Information). This phenotype most likely results from reduced contraction of the wound due to fibrosis in the dermis. However, the rate of reepithelialization was not affected. When sections from 13-day wounds were stained for beta-galactosidase activity, large areas of the wound epidermis above the healing wound were found to be lacZ-negative (see Figure S3C in Supporting Information), demonstrating that these cells escaped Cre-mediated recombination. This finding suggests that the necessity for rapid keratinocyte proliferation upon skin injury accelerates the selection process that we also observed in ageing skin.

###xml 732 740 705 713 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0005488-g006">Figure 6</xref>

To test whether the partial rescue of the skin phenotype in 5-week-old K5-beta1 mice was caused by the expansion of beta1 integrin-positive cells, we analyzed adjacent back skin sections from 5-day-, 3-week- and 5-week-old control and K5-beta1 mice for integrin and lacZ expression. At the age of 5 days the K5-beta1 back skin was positive for lacZ and only single cells or very small cell nests in the IFE also expressed beta1 integrin (Figure 6, and data not shown). In 3-week-old K5-beta1 mice an increasing number of beta1 integrin-positive keratinocytes was observed in lacZ-positive areas. By 5 weeks of age large regions of the K5-beta1 IFE were beta1 integrin positive. Interestingly, these areas were either lacZ-positive (Figure 6, upper panel) or negative (Figure 6, lower panel) in the adjacent sections. These results suggest that the beta1 integrin-positive cells can result from inefficient recombination of either one (beta1 integrin-positive and lacZ-positive) or both (beta1 integrin-positive and lacZ-negative) beta1 floxed alleles.

###begin title 53
Discussion
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 69 72 66 69 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Brakebusch1">[1]</xref>
###xml 74 78 71 75 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Fssler1">[20]</xref>
###xml 80 84 77 81 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Bouvard1">[24]</xref>
###xml 250 254 247 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Watt2">[25]</xref>
###xml 256 260 253 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Fuchs1">[26]</xref>
###xml 562 566 559 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Lechler1">[27]</xref>
###xml 567 571 564 568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Taddei1">[30]</xref>
###xml 937 940 925 928 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Brakebusch2">[6]</xref>
###xml 942 945 930 933 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Raghavan1">[7]</xref>
###xml 947 951 935 939 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Taddei1">[30]</xref>
###xml 952 956 940 944 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Sixt1">[33]</xref>
###xml 1329 1333 1311 1315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Taddei1">[30]</xref>
beta1 integrins are essential for tissue development and maintenance [1], [20], [24]. They are highly expressed on stem and progenitor cell populations, which orchestrate organogenesis and represent a cellular reservoir to maintain organ homeostasis [25], [26]. Experimental evidence suggests that the high expression level is critically important for supporting stem and progenitor cell properties such as adhesion to their niche, cell cycle quiescence and the appropriate orientation of the mitotic spindle to control asymmetric versus symmetric cell division [27]-[30]. Although the function of beta1 integrins for morphogenesis is undisputed, their role in stem and progenitor cell maintenance and expansion is less clear. Cell type-specific deletions of the beta1 integrin gene in the skin, mammary gland and haematopoietic system revealed a differential requirement of the beta1 integrin subfamily for stem and progenitor function [6], [7], [30]-[33]. While deletion of the beta1 integrin gene had no apparent impact on resting hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells, deletion of the beta1 integrin gene in keratinocytes reduced the proliferation rate of skin progenitor cells and in the mammary gland epithelium severely affected both, the proliferation and asymmetric division of the stem and progenitor cell population [30].
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 1335 1339 1275 1279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Fssler1">[20]</xref>
###xml 1452 1455 1392 1395 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hpm</italic>
###xml 1457 1460 1397 1400 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">lox</sup>
###xml 36 41 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 463 467 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1083 1087 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 1297 1301 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1428 1432 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In the present paper we generated a mouse strain expressing reduced beta1 integrin levels to directly investigate the role of beta1 integrin on skin formation and homeostasis, avoiding gross damages as seen in complete knockout animals. Since the beta integrin subunits are usually expressed in excess, the expression of alpha/beta integrin heterodimers on the cell surface is controlled by the available amount of alpha integrin subunits. This is the reason why mice heterozygous for the deletion of the beta1 integrin gene have normal or only slightly reduced levels of alphaxbeta1 integrins on their cells and are thus phenotypically normal. To significantly reduce the expression level of beta1 integrins we decided to impair the stability of the beta1 integrin mRNA. To this end we fused the coding sequence of the last exon to the second-last exon of the beta1 integrin gene. We thereby introduced a premature translation termination codon into the second-last exon, which in turn induced the NMD pathway and reduced beta1 integrin levels on keratinocytes to approximately15%. Mice homozygous for the hypomorphic beta1 integrin allele die between E6.0-E6.5, indicating that the remaining integrins allow development to proceed slightly further than the complete beta1 gene ablation, as such mice die at the late blastocyst stage [20]. We are currently trying to identify the specific defect(s) underlying the lethality of mice homozygous for the hpmKIlox allele.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 527 530 518 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Brakebusch2">[6]</xref>
###xml 532 535 523 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Raghavan1">[7]</xref>
###xml 537 541 528 532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Czuchra1">[34]</xref>
###xml 783 786 774 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hpm</italic>
###xml 1043 1046 1031 1034 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hpm</italic>
###xml 341 345 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 412 416 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 787 791 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1047 1051 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Skin development and maintenance depend on the presence of epithelial progenitor or stem cells, which retain their proliferative capacity throughout the entire life span of an animal. They have to replenish the interfollicular epidermis and the hair follicles and are therefore crucial to maintain the skin and the hairs. Genetic studies in mice have shown that the ablation of the beta1 integrin gene (K5-beta1 mice) or the expression of inactive beta1 integrins on keratinocytes leads to severe skin and hair coat phenotypes [6], [7], [34]. The defects include progressive hair loss, malformation of the hair follicles, hyperthickened and detached epidermis, defective LM-332 deposition at the dermal-epidermal junction, reduced keratinocyte proliferation and dermal fibrosis. The hpm mice with 15% of normal beta1 integrin levels develop similar phenotypes, albeit much less severe and delayed. Their IFE epidermis is less thick, blisters are very small and scarce and almost all HF are malformed. Primary keratinocytes isolated from young hpm mice adhered well to ECM substrates but failed to spread, to proliferate and to survive in vitro, suggesting that the reduced beta1 integrin levels are sufficient to promote adhesion but insufficient to appropriately activate signalling pathway(s) required for F-actin reorganisation, cell spreading and proliferation.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 31 34 31 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hpm</italic>
###xml 80 83 80 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hpm</italic>
###xml 251 254 251 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hpm</italic>
###xml 535 538 532 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hpm</italic>
###xml 679 682 673 676 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Brakebusch2">[6]</xref>
###xml 35 39 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 84 88 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Despite the skin abnormalities hpm mice had an apparently normal life span. Old hpm mice continued to lose their hair, but microblisters and irregular LM-332 deposition were significantly ameliorated with age. Moreover, primary keratinocytes from old hpm were able to spread and organize actin into stress fibers, to form robust and normal numbers of focal adhesions and to proliferate. The reason for the 'rescue' of the mutant phenotype in vivo and in vitro was the very rapid expansion of beta1 integrin expressing keratinocytes in hpm skin. Around 11% of the beta1 integrin expressing cells escaped Cre-mediated deletion. Similar findings were obtained in our previous study [6]. Furthermore, approximately76% of beta1 integrin expressing keratinocytes escaped or compensated for NMD-mediated degradation of beta1 integrin mRNA. These results indicate that keratinocytes with high beta1 integrin levels have an enormous advantage over cells expressing low beta1 integrin levels, leading to their rapid expansion in the mutant skin and partial reversion of the epidermal phenotype. Interestingly, this advantage becomes even more pronounced in situations where the requirement for keratinocyte proliferation and migration is particularly high such as during wound healing.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 372 375 358 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hpm</italic>
###xml 376 380 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Concomitant with the restoration of the beta1 integrin expression in the mutant animals there was also an increase in cell surface expression of its dimerization partner alpha2 integrin that was strongly reduced in young animals. On contrary the expression of the hemidesmosomal integrins alpha6 and beta4 remained relatively stable throughout the entire life span of the hpm mice slightly exceeding that of aged-matched control littermates. These data suggest that the alpha6beta4 integrins do not play a major role in the regulation of the proliferative potential of the keratinocytes, which only increases once cells expressing normal levels of beta1 integrin repopulate the skin.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 13 17 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 195 199 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 252 256 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The K5-beta1 mice carry disrupted beta1 integrin genes in keratinocytes, resulting in loss of beta1 integrin expression shortly after birth in around 60-70% of basal keratinocytes. Most K5-beta1 mice die within the first three weeks after birth. A few mice survive beyond the age of 5 weeks, despite their severe skin abnormalities. Interestingly, the epidermal phenotype of the 5 weeks old K5-beta1 mutant also improved indicated by a reduced number of blisters at the epidermal-dermal junction and normal deposition of LM-332. This phenotype improvement is also due to expansion of beta1 integrin expressing cells; already 5 days after birth the number of beta1 integrin exprerssing cells increased to approximately50%, and at the age of 5 weeks to approximately86%. The majority of these cells were still lacZ-positive indicating that in the majority of beta1 integrin-positive cells only one beta1 integrin floxed allele was excised by the K5-Cre transgene. These data indicate that the proliferative potential of beta1 integrin expressing keratinocytes is so superior that they overgrow beta1 integrin-null keratinocytes in a few weeks leading to a partial rescue of the interfollicular phenotype.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 181 184 181 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">hpm</italic>
###xml 209 213 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In contrast to the partial rescue of the interfollicular phenotype, the hair follicle phenotype was progressive in the mutant animals, leading to a complete hair loss in 1 year old hpm and 5-week-old K5-beta1 mice. This finding suggests that once a hair follicle is malformed and has lost the connection to the dermal papilla, it is no longer able to fully regenerate independently of the presence of beta1 integrin re-expressing EPCs in the skin.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 172 175 169 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Jones1">[9]</xref>
###xml 176 180 173 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Wan1">[12]</xref>
###xml 371 374 365 368 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Brakebusch2">[6]</xref>
###xml 593 597 578 582 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Jones3">[35]</xref>
###xml 642 645 624 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005488-Brakebusch2">[6]</xref>
###xml 239 243 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 412 416 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 459 463 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 612 616 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Our findings fully support previous in vitro data showing that high expression of beta1 integrins on keratinocytes is important for stem or progenitor cell-like properties [9]-[12]. Their role for stem/progenitor cells was questioned when mice with a deletion of the beta1 integrin gene in keratinocytes maintained an epidermis without premature terminal differentiation [6]. The analysis of the beta1 hypomorph mice and of surviving beta1 integrin deficient mice revealed that beta1 integrins are enormously important for the self-renewal capacity of the proliferating cells of the epidermis [35]. The K5-beta1 mice surviving beyond 5 weeks [6] benefit from the enormous proliferative potential conferred by beta1 integrin-mediated adhesion and signals. Interestingly, ablation of the floxed beta1 integrin allele differs in other cell systems such as the hematopoietic organ. Deletion of the beta1 integrins has no severe impact on hemtaopoietic stem cell/progenitor cell proliferation, lineage commitment and lineage expansion. It will require further studies to explain why keratinocytes so critically depend on the function of this integrin subfamily while blood cells apparently do not.

Mouse genomic clones used to generate the targeting construct were described earlier [20]. The loxP flanked neomycin resistance - thymidin kinase gene (neo-tk) selection cassette was inserted together with a 141 bp wt cDNA fragment of the beta1 integrin gene coding for the entire cytoplasmic tail of the beta1 integrin chain including the stop codon, into exon 15, just behind and in frame with the transmembrane coding region. The targeting construct (see Figure S1A in Supporting Information) was electroporated into R1 embryonic stem (ES) cells (129/Sv) and homologous recombinants were identified by Southern blot analysis of BamHI -digested DNA with a 5' external probe (see Figure S1B in Supporting Information ). Positive ES clones were transiently transfected with Cre recombinase to remove the neo-tk cassette, selected in 2'-fluoro-2'-deoxy-1-beta-D-arabinofuranosyl-5-iodouracil supplemented growth medium and identified with a 3' external probe after BamHI digestion (see Figure S1B in Supporting Information). Cells that lacked the selection cassette were injected into blastocysts to generate germline chimeras. Male chimeras were intercrossed with C57BL/6 females and offspring carrying the hpmKIlox allele were identified by genotyping PCR that distinguishes between the wt and the hpmKIlox allele (5'-GTCCTACTGGTCCCGAC-3', 5'-TGCTCTCAGTAATGTTTCATAAC-3'). The hpmKIlox/+ mice were intercrossed with mice carrying a floxed beta1 integrin gene [6] and mice carrying a keratin 5 promoter-driven Cre recombinase transgene [22]. For the experiments performed with this mouse line hpmKIlox/beta1fl, beta1fl/+ or beta1fl+/K5Cre age-matched control animals were used, depending on the requirement of the experiment.
