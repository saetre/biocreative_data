###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 242 285 242 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">adipocytes-type fatty acid binding protein </italic>
###xml 286 291 286 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4</italic>
###xml 294 317 294 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">liver X receptor &#945; </italic>
###xml 318 326 314 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945;</italic>
###xml 329 341 321 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cytochrome b</italic>
###xml 341 343 333 335 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sub>
###xml 344 349 336 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cyt b</italic>
###xml 349 350 341 342 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sub>
###xml 353 384 345 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">long-chain acyl-CoA synthetase </italic>
###xml 385 389 377 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ACSL</italic>
###xml 391 392 383 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</italic>
###xml 394 400 386 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ACSL4 </italic>
###xml 404 437 396 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">diacylglycerol acyltransferase 2 </italic>
###xml 438 443 430 435 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DGAT2</italic>
Fatty acid composition has become an important trait in the beef industry in terms of beef flavor and decreasing the circulating concentration of LDL cholesterol. In this study, we examined the association between polymorphisms of six genes, adipocytes-type fatty acid binding protein (FABP4), liver X receptor alpha (LXRalpha), cytochrome b5 (Cyt b5), long-chain acyl-CoA synthetase (ACSL) 1, ACSL4 and diacylglycerol acyltransferase 2 (DGAT2) and fatty acid composition.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 111 117 111 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 139 147 139 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945;</italic>
###xml 317 323 313 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 332 341 328 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 292 298 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
Sequence comparisons revealed 14 single nucleotide polymorphisms in six genes. Four of them, I74V and V110M in FABP4 and G51E and V133I in LXRalpha, were nonsynonymous substitutions. The associations between the genotypes and fatty acid compositions were analyzed by using 234 Japanese Black cattle. The genotypes of FABP4 I74V and LXRalpha V133I were significantly associated with palmitoleic acids (C16:1, P = 0.0086) and linoleic acid (C18:2, P = 0.0121) content in intramuscular fat, respectively.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 51 57 51 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 66 75 66 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 148 154 144 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 179 188 175 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 280 286 272 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 246 252 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
Our findings suggest that the two polymorphisms of FABP4 I74V and LXRalpha V133I might be genetic factors in part associated with palmitoleic acid (FABP4 I74V) and linoleic acid (LXRalpha V133I) composition in intramuscular fat of Japanese Black cattle, respectively. Especially, FABP4 I74V had highly significant effect (P < 0.01) on C16:1 proportion, indicating that the I/I homozygote exhibited 0.5% higher percentage than V/V homozygote.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 355 356 355 356 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 357 358 357 358 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 628 629 628 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 643 648 643 648 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 651 652 651 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 49 55 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 106 112 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 391 397 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 705 711 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 787 793 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
Fatty acid composition of adipose tissue in beef cattle has become an important trait in the industry. In cattle adipose tissue, higher concentrations of monounsaturated fatty acid (MUFA) in the adipocytes and lower fat-melting point are considered to contribute to favorable beef flavor and may decrease the circulating concentration of LDL cholesterol [1-3]. The fatty acid composition in cattle, unlike that for non-ruminants, is much less dependent on the diet. Microorganisms within the rumen hydrogenate a majority of the dietary unsaturated fatty acids and most dietary fatty acids are absorbed as saturated fatty acids [4]. Zembayashi et al. [5] demonstrated that adipose tissue of Japanese Black cattle contains a higher proportion of MUFA than that of Holstein, Japanese Brown cattle or Charolais. These results suggested that fatty acid composition may be controlled by genetic factors such as lipid synthesis and fatty acid metabolism related genes.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 0 24 0 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Stearoyl-CoA desaturase </italic>
###xml 25 28 25 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCD</italic>
###xml 192 196 192 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SCD </italic>
###xml 280 281 280 281 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 351 395 351 395 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sterol regulatory element-binding protein-1 </italic>
###xml 396 403 396 403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SREBP-1</italic>
###xml 460 461 460 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 609 614 609 614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 617 618 617 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 659 679 659 679 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fatty acid synthase </italic>
###xml 680 684 680 684 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FASN</italic>
###xml 746 764 746 764 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Longissimus dorsi </italic>
###xml 272 278 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 452 458 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
Stearoyl-CoA desaturase (SCD) is a key enzyme responsible for conversion of saturated fatty acids (SFA) into MUFA. In our previous study, we demonstrated that a nucleotide substitution in the SCD coding sequence is associated with fatty acid composition in Japanese Black cattle [6]. In addition, we revealed that a 84 bp insertion in intron 5 of the sterol regulatory element-binding protein-1 (SREBP-1) affected the MUFA proportion in Japanese Black cattle [7]. SREBP-1 is a transcriptional factor that plays a pivotal role in energy homeostasis by promoting glycolysis, lipogenesis and adipogenesis. Zhang et al. [8] also demonstrated that the genotype of fatty acid synthase (FASN) was significantly associated with fatty acid composition of Longissimus dorsi muscle of Angus bulls. The results suggest that the fatty acid composition is controlled by polygenic factors.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 151 189 151 189 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">adipocytes fatty acid binding protein </italic>
###xml 190 195 190 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4</italic>
###xml 198 221 198 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">liver X receptor &#945; </italic>
###xml 222 230 218 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945;</italic>
###xml 233 245 225 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cytochrome b</italic>
###xml 245 247 237 239 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sub>
###xml 248 253 240 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cyt b</italic>
###xml 253 254 245 246 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sub>
###xml 257 288 249 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">long-chain acyl-CoA synthetase </italic>
###xml 289 293 281 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ACSL</italic>
###xml 295 296 287 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</italic>
###xml 298 303 290 295 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ACSL4</italic>
###xml 309 342 301 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">diacylglycerol acyltransferase 2 </italic>
###xml 343 348 335 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DGAT2</italic>
###xml 423 429 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
The aim of this study is to discover additional genetic markers associated with fatty acid composition in beef. For this purpose, we picked six genes, adipocytes fatty acid binding protein (FABP4), liver X receptor alpha (LXRalpha), cytochrome b5 (Cyt b5), long-chain acyl-CoA synthetase (ACSL) 1, ACSL4, and diacylglycerol acyltransferase 2 (DGAT2), due to the important functions of the genes in fatty acid metabolism in cattle. For single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) discovery purposes, the coding regions of these six genes were sequenced. Statistical analysis was performed to investigate associations with polymorphisms identified and fatty acid composition.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Results
###end title 12
###begin title 13
Polymorphism identification and genotyping
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 67 72 67 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4</italic>
###xml 74 82 74 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945;</italic>
###xml 84 89 80 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cyt b</italic>

###xml 109 114 105 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DGAT2</italic>

###xml 898 903 890 895 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cyt b</italic>
###xml 903 905 895 897 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sub>
###xml 946 952 938 944 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ACSL1 </italic>
###xml 1037 1043 1029 1035 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ACSL4 </italic>
###xml 1047 1052 1039 1044 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DGAT2</italic>
###xml 188 194 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 214 220 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
We sequenced the full length of coding sequence for the six genes (FABP4, LXRalpha, Cyt b5, ACSL1, ACSL4 and DGAT2) from genome DNA or cDNA of eight animals, including five Japanese Black cattle and three Holstein cattle (primer sequences listed in Table 1). In FABP4, sequence comparison among the eight animals revealed four substitutions (220 bpA/G, 328 bpG/A, 348 bpG/C and 396 bpA/G, with the translation initiation site assigned as +1). Two of them were predicted to cause amino acid substitutions, isoleucine to valine at 220 bpA/G (I74V) and valine to methionine at 328 bpG/A (V110M). In LXRalpha, sequence comparison revealed four substitutions (45 bpT/C, 152 bpG/A, 397 bpG/A and 1,314 bpG/T). Two were predicted to cause amino acid substitutions, glycine to glutamic acid at 152 bpG/A (G51E) and valine to isoleucine at 397 bpG/A (V133I). Three synonymous substitutions were detected in Cyt b5 (195 bpT/C, 201 bpA/G and 360 bpG/A) and ACSL1 (282 bpC/T, 516 bpC/G and 1,938 bpT/G). No nucleotide substitution was identified in ACSL4 and DGAT2.

Effects of the four genotypes of FABP4 and LXRalpha on phenotypic traits (fatty acid composition and carcass traits listed in Table 2) were investigated by analysis of variance (ANOVA) (Table 2). Year of testing and sex had significant effects on the characteristics of dressed carcasses and fatty acid compositions as described in previous study [9]. FABP4 I74V had a highly significant effect on the percentage of C16:1 (P = 0.0086). ###begin title 30
Discussion
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 5 7 5 7 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5 </sub>
###xml 147 149 147 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 216 221 216 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 224 226 224 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 268 274 268 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ACSL4 </italic>
###xml 583 585 583 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
Cyt b5 is the electron carrier, participating in a series of electron-transfer processes in biological systems, including fatty acid desaturation [10]. ACSL plays a key role in the metabolism of fatty acids. Mercade et al. [11] reported the association of the porcine ACSL4 polymorphism with growth and percentage of oleic fatty acid. DGAT is the enzyme that catalyzes the final reaction in the synthesis of triglycerides. DGAT2 and SCD1 proteins are located very close to each other in the endoplasmic reticulum, which is a very important criterion for the channeling of substrate [12]. Although these genes relate to fat metabolism, no amino acid substitutions were detected in this study.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 73 79 73 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 101 109 101 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945;</italic>
###xml 458 460 454 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 691 693 683 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 694 696 686 688 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
In the other two genes, four amino acid substitutions, I74V and V110M in FABP4 and G51E and V133I in LXRalpha, were observed. FABP4 is one of cytoplasmic protein family involved in intracellular free fatty acid transport and metabolism, and binds long-chain fatty acids with high affinities. Fatty acid trafficking during lipolysis is mediated by FABP4, and the complex with hormone-sensitive lipase is the first step in an organized lipid-transfer process [13]. LXRalpha is a transcription factor regulating genes involved in cholesterol and lipid metabolism. LXR directly activates SREBP-1 gene transcription, and the SREBP-1 subsequently activates lipogenic genes such as FASN and SCD-1 [14,15]. From these points of view, the amino acid polymorphisms, found in the current study, may affect the function and be associated with fat-related traits.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 48 54 48 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 76 84 76 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945;</italic>
###xml 536 541 532 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 544 545 540 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 639 645 635 641 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 682 691 678 683 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 723 729 715 721 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 859 860 851 852 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T3">3</xref>
###xml 887 889 879 881 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 997 1003 989 995 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 1099 1108 1091 1096 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 1246 1247 1234 1235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T3">3</xref>
###xml 108 114 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 1232 1238 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 1393 1399 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
We genotyped these four SNPs (I74V and V110M in FABP4 and G51E and V133I in LXRalpha) in 234 Japanese Black cattle and investigated the effect on fatty acid composition and carcass traits. ANOVA on carcass traits and fatty acid composition revealed some significance in year and sex effects. Steers had higher dressed carcass weight, rib-eye area, rib thickness and saturated fatty acid proportion, while heifers had higher BCS (darker color) and MUFA proportion. Our results were consistent with previous study described by Zembayashi et al. [5] in terms of higher MUFA and lower SFA in heifers. We demonstrated the significant effect of FABP4 I74V on palmitoleic acid (C16:1) and LXRalpha V133I on linoleic acid (C18:2). FABP4 I74V had a significant effect on C16:1 proportion; the I/I homozygote exhibited 0.5% higher percentage than V/V homozygote (Table 3). Westerling and Hedrick [16] suggested a negative correlation between C16:1 and beef flavor or beef tenderness, so the polymorphism of FABP4 I74V might be a candidate marker in beef flavor and tenderness indices. The V/I heterozygote of LXRalpha V133I was 0.2% higher than V/V homozygote, and I/I homozygote was the lowest. Since I/I homozygote was observed in only six cattle (Table 3), we are unable to draw conclusions from such limited numbers. In order to confirm the effect, further investigations would be required in other cattle populations that include adequate frequency of allele I.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 180 182 180 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 175 178 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 437 443 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
FABP4 has been reported as a possible candidate gene for obesity since it was located within a quantitative trait loci (QTL) region contributing to the serum leptin levels in rat [17]. Leptin is a serum protein secreted from adipocytes and is involved in regulating body fat by inhibiting food intake and stimulating energy expenditure. This suggests that FABP4 play an important role in lipid metabolism and homeostasis in adipocyte of cattle.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 7 12 7 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 15 17 15 17 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 319 324 319 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 327 329 327 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 433 439 433 439 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 449 454 449 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 457 459 457 459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 489 495 489 495 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 569 575 569 575 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 33 39 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 620 626 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 683 689 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 732 738 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
Michal et al. [18] reported that bovine FABP4 genotype significantly affected the marbling score and subcutaneous fat depth in a Wagyu x Limousine F2 population. However, no FABP4 effects on any carcass traits including BMS and subcutaneous fat thickness were found in our study. Since the polymorphism found by Michal et al. [18] was detected in 3' untranslated region, the polymorphism might have different phenotypic effects than FABP4 I74V. Cho et al. [19] identified polymorphisms of FABP4 I74V, V110M and 348 bpG/C. The authors showed a putative association with FABP4 I74V and back fat thickness in Korean native cattle. Our study shows no such associations in Japanese Black cattle. The reasons may be due to differences of cattle breeds and/or standard for the measurement of BMS and fat thickness.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 10 19 10 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 95 104 91 96 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 153 155 145 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 161 166 153 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 169 171 161 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 648 657 640 645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 3 8 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 146 151 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">women</span>
###xml 237 240 <span type="species:ncbi:9823">pig</span>
###xml 705 711 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
In human, LXRalpha polymorphism in exon 5 was associated with obesity phenotypes, and relative LXRalpha mRNA expression level was higher in obese women [20]. Yu et al. [21] identified two polymorphisms influencing lean and fat growth in pig. The SNP identified in intron 8 was significantly associated with loin eye area and total lipid. Significant associations were also found between the polymorphism in exon 2 and boneless loin and marbling score. Since polymorphisms identified in our study altered amino acid changes but did not affect carcass traits, further work will be necessary to investigate the association between expression level of LXRalpha and carcass traits or fatty acid composition in cattle.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 22 28 22 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 37 46 37 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 221 223 210 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 233 239 222 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 281 290 267 272 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 342 344 324 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
Homology searches for FABP4 I74V and LXRalpha V133I among several mammalian spices indicates that the amino acid changes are conservative. The FABP4 protein consists of two alpha helix domain and 10 beta strands domains [22] and the FABP4 I74V is located on fifth beta sheets. The LXRalpha V133I is located on Zinc finger DNA binding domain [23]. These amino acid replacements may affect structure and therefore function of these proteins.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 40 46 40 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 55 64 55 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 304 306 300 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 307 309 303 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 564 566 560 562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 835 841 831 837 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 850 859 846 851 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 545 551 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 791 797 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
In this study, our results suggest that FABP4 I74V and LXRalpha V133I are in part associated with fatty acid composition. Since the fatty acid composition is a polygenic trait and QTL analysis has shown that the fatty acid composition of livestock species is regulated by many potential candidate genes [24,25], the effect of each gene may be rather small. Additionally, differential expression of adipogenic factors has been known to play a key role in regulating multiple responsive pathways processing fat development and lipid metabolism in cattle adipocytes [26]. Further analysis investigating the interaction of the genetic factors in the biochemical pathway with respect to lipid synthesis and fatty acid metabolism will bring new insight into the fat-related carcass traits in beef cattle. To evaluate the intimate effects of FABP4 I74V and LXRalpha V133I, additional analysis using different field population is needed to confirm the effects of these two polymorphisms in the future.
###end p 38
###begin title 39
Conclusion
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 64 70 64 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 79 88 79 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 142 148 138 144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 173 182 169 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 240 246 232 238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
In summary, we have identified two nonsynonymous substitutions, FABP4 I74V and LXRalpha V133I, that were significantly associated with C16:1 (FABP4 I74V) and C18:2 content (LXRalpha V133I) in intramuscular fat. In particular, the effect of FABP4 I74V might be a good DNA marker for beef quality.
###end p 40
###begin title 41
Methods
###end title 41
###begin title 42
Animals and fatty acid analysis
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 36 61 36 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Musculaus (M.) trapezius </italic>
###xml 286 298 280 292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. trapezius</italic>
###xml 181 187 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
For genomic DNA and RNA extraction, Musculaus (M.) trapezius muscle and subcutaneous adipose tissue around the right neck were collected from five Japanese Black and three Holstein cattle and stored at -80degreesC until RNA preparation. We extracted genomic DNA from the muscle sample (M. trapezius) and RNA from the adipose tissue.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 119 131 119 131 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. trapezius</italic>
###xml 462 483 458 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. longissimus dorsi </italic>
###xml 605 606 601 602 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 155 161 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 462 476 <span type="species:ncbi:280409">M. longissimus</span>
In order to investigate the association between the genotypes and fatty acid composition, we collected muscle samples (M. trapezius) of 234 Japanese Black cattle, 141 steers and 93 heifers, as part of the progeny testing performed by the Wagyu Registry Association of Japan from 2002 to 2006. The average age in months at slaughter was 28.5 +/- 1.4 (mean +/- standard deviation). The intramuscular adipose tissue samples were collected from intramuscular fat of M. longissimus dorsi to analyze fatty acid composition. The measurements of fatty acid profile were carried out according to a previous study [7]. Analyzed fatty acid methyl esters were C14:0, C14:1, C16:0, C16:1, C18:0, C18:1 and C18:2 and the composition of each fatty acid was expressed as a percentage. C20:0 and greater sized fatty acids were not included in this analysis since the percentages were lower than 0.5%.
###end p 44
###begin title 45

###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 105 106 105 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>

Total RNA extraction from fat tissues and cDNA synthesis were performed according to our previous study [6]. Genomic DNA samples were isolated by proteinase K digestion followed by phenol extraction. Sequencing for Cyt b5, ACSL1, ACSL4 and DGAT2 genes were performed using cDNA, while genomic DNA was used for sequencing in FABP4 and LXRalpha.

###xml 50 54 50 52 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174; </sup>

###begin title 49
Genotyping
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 80 86 80 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 90 93 90 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR</italic>
###xml 134 135 130 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 196 201 192 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4</italic>
###xml 225 233 221 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945;</italic>
###xml 737 743 690 696 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FABP4 </italic>
###xml 764 767 717 720 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nmu</italic>
###xml 774 777 727 730 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nla</italic>
###xml 822 831 775 780 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 857 860 806 809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hpy</italic>
###xml 917 920 866 869 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bse</italic>
###xml 966 975 915 920 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 73 79 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
We applied PCR-RFLP methods to genotype four nucleotide substitutions in bovine FABP4 and LXRalpha genes. Four PCR primer sets (Table 1) were designed for DNA fragments flanking I74V and V110M in FABP4, and G51E and V133I in LXRalpha. The PCR amplification was performed with 20 ng of genomic DNA and TaKaRaEx Taqtrade mark polymerase (TaKaRa, Kyoto, Japan). Amplification was performed with a thermal cycler, GeneAmp PCR System 9700 (Applied Biosystems, CA), with the following thermo-cycling protocol: initial denaturation at 94degreesC for 2 min, followed by 30 cycles of 94degreesC for 30 s, 57-62degreesC for 30 s, and 72degreesC for 1 min, with a final extension step, 72degreesC for 7 min. The fragment flanking I74V and V110M in FABP4 were digestible with NmuCI and NlaIII, respectively, and the fragment flanking LXRalpha V133I was digestible with HpyCH4IV. Mismatch PCR-RFLP was also adopted to introduce a BseNI recognition site for the fragment flanking LXRalpha G51E since no suitable restriction enzyme was detected in the site. The digestions were performed in a total of 20 mul volume reaction mixture with approximately 5 mug of PCR products and 3 units of each restriction enzyme. The reaction was incubated at the proper temperature for each restriction enzyme. The digested PCR products were confirmed with undigested products and sequenced homozygous and heterozygous samples by agarose gel electrophoresis.
###end p 50
###begin title 51
Statistical analysis
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 555 557 555 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
Fatty acid composition of intramuscular fat and the character of dressed carcasses (the weight of dressed carcass, rib-eye area, rib thickness, subcutaneous fat thickness, yield estimate, beef marbling standard and beef color standard) were tested statistically by ANOVA with a model that accounted for year of testing, sex, four genotypes without interaction. Tukey's HSD test was performed for further analysis. Genotypic distributions and allele frequencies were calculated and Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium test was performed using Arlequin version 3.1 [27].
