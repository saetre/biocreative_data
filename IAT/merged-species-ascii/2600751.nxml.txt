###begin article-title 0
alpha-E-catenin binds to dynamitin and regulates dynactin-mediated intracellular traffic
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 37 55 37 55 <email xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">vvasiouk@fhcrc.org</email>

###begin p 3
###xml 289 292 277 280 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 274 292 266 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;&#8211;E-catenin<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
alpha-Epithelial catenin (E-catenin) is an important cell-cell adhesion protein. In this study, we show that alpha-E-catenin also regulates intracellular traffic by binding to the dynactin complex component dynamitin. Dynactin-mediated organelle trafficking is increased in alpha-E-catenin-/- keratinocytes, an effect that is reversed by expression of exogenous alpha-E-catenin. Disruption of adherens junctions in low-calcium media does not affect dynactin-mediated traffic, indicating that alpha-E-catenin regulates traffic independently from its function in cell-cell adhesion. Although neither the integrity of dynactin-dynein complexes nor their association with vesicles is affected by alpha-E-catenin, alpha-E-catenin is necessary for the attenuation of microtubule-dependent trafficking by the actin cytoskeleton. Because the actin-binding domain of alpha-E-catenin is necessary for this regulation, we hypothesize that alpha-E-catenin functions as a dynamic link between the dynactin complex and actin and, thus, integrates the microtubule and actin cytoskeleton during intracellular trafficking.
###end p 3

###begin title 5
Introduction
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 129 148 122 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib10">Hirano et al., 1992</xref>
###xml 150 169 143 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Torres et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 171 194 164 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Vasioukhin et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 196 220 189 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib11">Kobielak and Fuchs, 2004</xref>
###xml 367 388 356 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">Bullions et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 390 413 379 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib24">Vasioukhin et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 415 432 404 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib12">Lien et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 434 459 423 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib3">Benjamin and Nelson, 2008</xref>
###xml 342 347 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
alpha-Catenin is an adherens junction (AJ) protein that binds to beta-catenin and is necessary for AJ formation and maintenance (Hirano et al., 1992; Torres et al., 1997; Vasioukhin et al., 2000; Kobielak and Fuchs, 2004). In addition, alpha-catenin is also involved in the regulation of cell proliferation, and this protein is often lost in human epithelial tumors (Bullions et al., 1997; Vasioukhin et al., 2001; Lien et al., 2006; Benjamin and Nelson, 2008). It is not clear whether all of the functions of alpha-catenin are connected to its role in intercellular adhesion or whether it may have an additional adhesion-independent role.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 438 451 438 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">Schroer, 2004</xref>
###xml 610 634 610 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Vaughan and Vallee, 1995</xref>
###xml 636 657 636 657 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Quintyne et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 659 678 659 678 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Askham et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 680 699 680 699 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">Deacon et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 923 928 923 928 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Glued</sup>
###xml 1180 1193 1180 1193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">Schroer, 2004</xref>
Normal cellular function requires constant traffic and delivery of organelles and other membrane-limited vesicles. Vesicles can use both actin and microtubule-mediated movements, and this enables them to efficiently traverse any cytoplasmic region within the cell. The microtubule-based motor proteins dynein, kinesin 2, and kinesin Eg-5 cannot directly bind to their cargoes, and they use the dynactin protein complex for this function (Schroer, 2004). Dynactin not only links a variety of cargoes to their microtubule motors, but it is also necessary for proper organization of the microtubule cytoskeleton (Vaughan and Vallee, 1995; Quintyne et al., 1999; Askham et al., 2002; Deacon et al., 2003). The dynactin complex consists of two distinct parts: a rodlike domain, which contains Arp1 filament and actin-capping proteins and is responsible for interaction with cargo, and an elongated projection containing the p150Glued homodimer, which is responsible for interaction with motor proteins and microtubules. These two parts of dynactin are bridged by p50 dynamitin, which is critical for the assembly of the entire dynactin protein complex and its association with dynein (Schroer, 2004).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
In this study, we describe a novel function for alpha-catenin in the regulation of dynactin-mediated intracellular trafficking. We show that dynamitin links dynactin with alpha-epithelial catenin (E-catenin) and that alpha-E-catenin negatively regulates dynactin-dependent microtubule traffic.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Results and discussion
###end title 9
###begin title 10
alpha-E-catenin binds to dynamitin and interacts with the dynactin protein complex
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 241 249 229 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 A</xref>
###xml 627 632 604 609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dctn2</italic>
###xml 84 89 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 481 486 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
To gain new insights into the mechanism of alpha-E-catenin function, we performed a yeast two-hybrid screen to identify novel alpha-E-catenin-interacting proteins. alpha-E-catenin contains three vinculin homology domains: VH1, VH2, and VH3 (Fig. 1 A). Although the VH1 domain is responsible for binding to beta-catenin, the functional significance of the VH2 and VH3 domains is not as well understood. We used DeltaVH1 alpha-E-catenin (291-906 aa) as a bait to screen an embryonic mouse brain cDNA library. We identified 84 individual clones, and subsequent analysis revealed that 74 of them contained cDNA encoding dynamitin (Dctn2), which is a central component of the dynactin protein complex.
###end p 11

###begin p 13
###xml 193 201 185 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 B</xref>
###xml 622 630 602 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 C</xref>
###xml 804 812 776 784 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 C</xref>
###xml 120 125 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 490 495 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
To confirm the binding and determine the domain of alpha-E-catenin responsible for interaction, we performed a targeted yeast two-hybrid assay with full-length or fragments of alpha-E-catenin (Fig. 1 B). Dynamitin showed a strong interaction with VH2 and VH2-VH3 fragments of alpha-E-catenin. Interestingly, the VH1 domain displayed an inhibitory effect for this interaction, as we found a significantly weaker interaction between dynamitin and full-length alpha-E-catenin. Consistent with yeast two-hybrid results, we found that dynamitin interacts with full-length alpha-E-catenin and its VH2 domain in mammalian cells (Fig. 1 C). Interestingly, the alpha-E-catenin-like protein vinculin did not bind dynamitin in this assay, indicating that the alpha-E-catenin-dynamitin interaction is very specific (Fig. 1 C). Thus, we conclude that alpha-E-catenin binds to dynamitin, and the VH2 domain of alpha-E-catenin is responsible for this interaction.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 221 226 213 218 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Glued</sup>
###xml 228 236 220 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 D</xref>
###xml 377 382 365 370 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Glued</sup>
To determine whether alpha-E-catenin can use dynamitin to bind to the entire dynactin complex, we analyzed alpha-E-catenin immunoprecipitates for the presence of other members of the dynactin complex such as Arp1 and p150Glued (Fig. 1 D). We found that full-length and VH2-VH3 domain-containing fragments of alpha-E-catenin pulled down not only dynamitin but also Arp1 and p150Glued proteins. Thus, not only dynamitin but also other members of the dynactin protein complex interact with alpha-E-catenin.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 260 268 256 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 E</xref>
###xml 353 361 345 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 E</xref>
###xml 661 666 642 647 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Glued</sup>
###xml 710 713 687 690 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 695 713 676 690 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;&#8211;E-catenin<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 729 737 706 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 F</xref>
###xml 890 898 863 871 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 G</xref>
###xml 201 206 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
To analyze the interaction between endogenous alpha-E-catenin and dynactin, we performed cosedimentation and immunoprecipitation (IP) experiments. We isolated the dynactin protein complex from primary mouse keratinocytes using sucrose gradient centrifugation (Fig. 1 E). Similar to dynein, only a fraction of alpha-E-catenin cosedimented with dynactin (Fig. 1 E). To determine whether endogenous alpha-E-catenin physically interacts with dynactin, we used the fractions containing dynactin for co-IP experiments. Anti-alpha-E-catenin antibodies, but not the control anti-beta-galactosidase antibodies, pulled down the dynactin proteins dynamitin, Arp1, and p150Glued from wild-type but not from alpha-E-catenin-/- keratinocytes (Fig. 1 F). In addition, an antibody directed against a different domain of alpha-E-catenin was able to co-IP dynamitin from keratinocyte total protein extracts (Fig. 1 G).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
To determine whether alpha-E-catenin can potentially link cadherin-catenin complexes to dynactin, we analyzed whether alpha-E-catenin could simultaneously interact with beta-catenin and dynamitin. We found that GST-dynamitin pulled down alpha-E-catenin but not the alpha-E-catenin-beta-catenin complexes (Fig. S1 A, available at ). Similarly, GST-beta-catenin pulled down alpha-E-catenin but not the alpha-E-catenin-dynamitin complexes (Fig. S1 B). Overall, we conclude that alpha-E-catenin, but not alpha-E-catenin-beta-catenin heterodimers, binds to dynactin.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
alpha-E-catenin and dynamitin are necessary to extend the microtubule cytoskeleton to the cell periphery and establish strong intercellular adhesion
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 91 94 83 86 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 76 94 72 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;&#8211;E-catenin<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 248 257 236 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2 A&#8242;</xref>
###xml 396 404 380 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2 A</xref>
###xml 500 503 480 483 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 485 503 469 483 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;&#8211;E-catenin<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 580 598 560 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2, B and B&#8244;</xref>
To determine localization of alpha-E-catenin and dynamitin in wild-type and alpha-E-catenin-/- keratinocytes, we performed immunofluorescence stainings. alpha-E-catenin prominently localized around the nuclei and at AJs in wild-type keratinocytes (Fig. 2 A'). Dynamitin colocalized with alpha-E-catenin around cell nuclei, and small amounts of dynamitin were found at the cell periphery and AJs (Fig. 2 A). In contrast, dynamitin was localized almost exclusively around the nucleus in alpha-E-catenin-/- cells, with very little amounts of dynamitin present at the cell periphery (Fig. 2, B and B'''). These data suggest that alpha-E-catenin is necessary to localize dynamitin to cell edges.

###xml 564 567 546 549 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>

###xml 575 589 557 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2, F&#8211;G&#8244;</xref>
It has been recently demonstrated that alpha-E-catenin is involved in the regulation of microtubule cytoskeleton (Shtutman et al., 2008). Staining with anti-beta-tubulin antibodies revealed that the microtubules extended to the cell periphery and prominently localized to AJs in wild-type but not in alpha-E-catenin-/- keratinocytes (Fig. 2, C-E; arrows). This was despite the fact that beta-catenin, which was previously implicated in the connection between AJs and microtubules (Ligon et al., 2001), continued to localize to cell-cell contacts in alpha-E-catenin-/- cells (Fig. 2, F-G'''). Thus, we conclude that alpha-E-catenin is necessary for proper intracellular localization of dynamitin and extension of microtubule cytoskeleton to the cell periphery and AJs.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 85 88 81 84 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 70 88 70 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;&#8211;E-catenin<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 205 214 201 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dynamitin</italic>
###xml 283 298 279 294 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3, A and B</xref>
###xml 341 344 333 336 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 326 344 322 336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;&#8211;E-catenin<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 352 361 344 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dynamitin</italic>
###xml 448 459 440 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3, C&#8211;E</xref>
###xml 475 484 467 476 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dynamitin</italic>
###xml 539 541 531 533 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 571 579 563 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 F</xref>
###xml 588 597 580 589 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dynamitin</italic>
###xml 773 776 761 764 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 758 776 750 764 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;&#8211;E-catenin<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
To determine whether loss of dynamitin may phenocopy the phenotype in alpha-E-catenin-/- cells, we used a short hairpin RNA (shRNA) knockdown (KD) approach. Because dynamitin is critical for cell mitosis, dynamitin KD cells displayed a decrease but not a complete loss of dynamitin (Fig. 3, A and B). Nevertheless, similar to alpha-E-catenin-/- cells, dynamitin KD cells failed to extend the microtubule cytoskeleton to the cell periphery and AJs (Fig. 3, C-E). In addition, dynamitin KD cells displayed a prominent defect in short-term Ca2+-mediated cell-cell adhesion (Fig. 3 F). Thus, dynamitin KD cells displayed the defects in organization of the microtubule cytoskeleton and strengthening of cell-cell adhesion, which were similar to the phenotypes of alpha-E-catenin-/- cells. We propose that interaction between alpha-E-catenin and dynactin may facilitate dynactin function in the extension of the microtubule cytoskeleton to the cell periphery, and this can promote cell-cell junction formation.
###end p 21

The primary function of dynactin is to facilitate the microtubule motor-mediated traffic of cell organelles and vesicles (Schroer, 2004). To examine whether alpha-E-catenin may be involved in regulation of dynactin-dependent traffic, we performed analyses of lysosome movement in live wild-type and alpha-E-catenin-/- cells. We labeled lysosomes with LysoTracker dye and followed their movements using time-lapse microscopy (n >/= 10; Videos 1 and 2, available at ). Software-mediated quantitation of the total length of movement over 300 individual lysosomes from 15-20 randomly selected cells of each genotype showed a significant increase in the distances traveled by lysosomes in alpha-E-catenin-/- cells (Fig. 4 A). This defect was rescued by expression of exogenous alpha-E-catenin in alpha-E-catenin-/- cells (n >/= 8; Fig. 4, B-D; and Videos 3 and 4). Thus, we conclude that alpha-E-catenin negatively impacts dynactin-dependent microtubule traffic and that organelle motility is increased in alpha-E-catenin-/- cells.

###begin p 28

Cellular organelles and vesicles can use both actin filaments and microtubules for intracellular transport. Usually, microtubules are used for rapid long-range movements, and actin is used for short-range movements. Because vesicles can constantly switch between actin filaments and microtubules, the disruption of actin filaments often results in a significant increase in the mobility of vesicles and organelles (Cordonnier et al., 2001). We analyzed whether the integrity of the actin cytoskeleton is disrupted in alpha-E-catenin-/- cells. Staining for filamentous actin (F-actin) revealed the presence of well-organized F-actin bundles in alpha-E-catenin-/- cells (Fig. 5, A and B). We conclude that the organization of actin filaments is not disrupted in alpha-E-catenin-/- cells, and, thus, this cannot explain stimulation in dynactin-dependent traffic in alpha-E-catenin-/- keratinocytes.

We already found that alpha-E-catenin-/- cells display an increase in dynactin-mediated organelle motility (Fig. 4 A). To determine the impact of the actin cytoskeleton, we performed quantitation of lysosome movement in cells treated with latrunculin A, which depolymerizes actin filaments (Fig. 5, C-F). As previously reported, treatment with latrunculin A resulted in a significant increase in the mobility of the lysosomes in wild-type cells (n >/= 9; Fig. 5 E and Video 7, available at ; Cordonnier et al., 2001). Remarkably, latrunculin A treatment of alpha-E-catenin-/- cells did not significantly increase lysosome movements (n >/= 12; Fig. 5 F and Video 8). The overall motility of lysosomes in vehicle-treated alpha-E-catenin-/- cells was similar to the motility of lysosomes in wild-type cells treated with latrunculin A (Fig. 5, E and F). Interestingly, although expression of full-length alpha-E-catenin rescued the lysosome movement defect in alpha-E-catenin-/- cells, expression of truncated alpha-E-catenin lacking its actin-binding domain (BD) had no effect (Fig. 5 G, Fig. S3, and Video 9), indicating that the actin-BD was necessary for alpha-E-catenin function in the regulation of lysosome traffic. Thus, we conclude that alpha-E-catenin is required for the functional connection between the dynactin-mediated organelle traffic and the actin cytoskeleton.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
To summarize, we report in this study that alpha-E-catenin binds to dynactin and regulates its function. The regulation of dynactin activities is a novel and previously unrecognized function of alpha-E-catenin, and future research will be necessary to explore the details of alpha-E-catenin function in the regulation of both the dynactin-mediated extension of the microtubule cytoskeleton to the cell periphery and dynactin-mediated intracellular transport events.
###end p 32
