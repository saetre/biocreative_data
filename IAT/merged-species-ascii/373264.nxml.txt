The major role of human AP-endonuclease homolog Apn2 in repair of abasic sites in Schizosaccharomyces pombe

The abasic (AP) sites, the major mutagenic and cytotoxic genomic lesions, induced directly by oxidative stress and indirectly after excision of damaged bases by DNA glycosylases, are repaired by AP-endonucleases (APEs). Among two APEs in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Apn1 provides the major APE activity, and Apn2, the ortholog of the mammalian APE, provides back-up activity. We have cloned apn1 and apn2 genes of Schizosaccharomyces pombe, and have shown that inactivation of Apn2 and not Apn1 sensitizes this fission yeast to alkylation and oxidative damage-inducing agents, which is further enhanced by Apn1 inactivation. We also show that Uve1, present in S.pombe but not in S.cerevisiae, provides the back-up APE activity together with Apn1. We confirmed the presence of APE activity in recombinant Apn2 and in crude cell extracts. Thus S.pombe is distinct from S.cerevisiae, and is similar to mammalian cells in having Apn2 as the major APE.

Mitochondria continuously generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) as respiration by-products. ROS are genotoxic and induce a multitude of DNA damage, including oxidized bases, abasic (AP) sites due to direct glycosylic bond cleavage in DNA, and strand breaks caused by fragmentation and oxidation of deoxyribose in the DNA backbone with generation of 3'-phosphoglycolaldehyde and 3'-phosphoglycolate termini (1). AP sites are also generated as repair intermediates after excision of alkylated and oxidatively damaged bases by DNA glycosylases and are produced due to spontaneous depurination (2,3). Oxidized base-specific DNA glycosylases possess intrinsic AP-lyase activity, cleaving the DNA backbone at AP sites and producing 3'-phospho-alpha,beta unsaturated aldehyde or 3'-phosphates. AP sites, arguably the most abundant endogenous lesions in DNA, have been shown to be cytotoxic and mutagenic (3-5). Repair of AP sites and single-strand breaks with 3'-blocked termini occurs primarily via the DNA base excision repair (BER) pathway, which is essential for mammalian survival. Inactivation of some BER proteins including the major AP-endonuclease (APE), DNA polymerase beta (Polbeta) or XRCC1 protein in the mouse causes embryonic lethality (6). Repair of AP sites is initiated with an APE which cleaves the DNA strand 5' to the AP site and generates 3'-OH and 5'-phosphodeoxyribose termini. The resulting 5'-phosphodeoxyribose group is removed by the lyase activity of Polbeta to generate a 1 nt gap. Subsequently, Polbeta fills in the gap, and DNA ligase seals the single-strand nick to complete repair [reviewed in Mitra et al. (7)]. APEs also have intrinsic 3'-phosphodiesterase activity responsible for removing the 3'-blocking groups generated at strand breaks caused by either ROS directly or due to AP lyase activity of some DNA glycosylases (8). Two major classes of APEs are present in organisms ranging from the bacteria to mammals whose prototypes are Xth (exonuclease III), representing approximately90% of the total AP repair activity, and Nfo (endonuclease IV) in Escherichia coli. In Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Apn1, the first APE to be cloned, belongs to the E.coli Nfo family (9), while Apn2 shares homology with E.coli Xth and human APE1 and APE2 (10,11). Both Apn2 and Apn1 possess APE and DNA 3'-phosphodiesterase activities (12,13). However, the apn1 but not the apn2 mutant is sensitive to the methylating genotoxicant methyl methanesulfonate (MMS), while the apn1Delta apn2Delta double mutant is much more sensitive to MMS than the apn1Delta mutant (10,11,14). These results confirm the earlier conclusion that Apn1 is the major APE in S.cerevisiae, and that Apn2 has a back-up role in repair of AP sites in the absence of Apn1.

Fission and budding yeasts diverged early in evolution and often have distinct functional differences. Phylogenetically, the fission yeast Schizaosaccharomyces pombe is generally believed to be closer to mammalian cells than S.cerevisiae, particularly in functions of many regulatory pathways (15,16). However, the BER pathway has not been studied in detail. Although the candidate genes for Apn1 and Apn2 were identified in the S.pombe genome, and the Apn1 gene was cloned (17), the APE activity in the recombinant protein was not shown, presumably because of the presence of an intron in the genomic clone. Although Uve1, a UV photoproduct-specific endonuclease identified in S.pombe, but not S.cerevisiae, was shown to possess APE activity (18), attempts to demonstrate APE activity in cell-free extracts of S.pombe were unsuccessful (17). Although Apn2 was identified in the S.pombe genome database (10,11), its repair activity was not shown. Based on these observations, it was suggested that S.pombe does not have active APE (17). We show in this report that S.pombe does possess active Apn1 and Apn2 in addition to Uve1. Furthermore, we show a distinct difference in APE activity of the fission and budding types in that, in contrast to the situation in S.cerevisiae, Apn2 is the major APE in S.pombe. Moreover, the UV photoproduct-specific Uve1, present in S.pombe but not in S.cerevisiae, provides back-up activity along with Apn1 in repair of AP sites.

Cells were grown overnight in YE or EMM medium for survival studies after treatment with MMS and phleomycin D1 (Zeocin; Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). The cells were washed with distilled water and resuspended (2 x 107/ml) in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) before addition of the appropriate amount of MMS. Growth inhibition assay

The agar diffusion assay for examining complementation (22) was used with the following modification. ###begin title 26
RESULTS
###end title 26
###begin title 27
###xml 30 34 30 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 43 50 43 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 43 50 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
Existence of an intron in the apn1 gene of S.pombe, and cloning of Apn1 cDNA
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 8 13 8 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 16 18 16 18 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c17">17</xref>
###xml 77 81 77 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 90 97 90 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 399 400 399 400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh151f1">1</xref>
###xml 425 426 425 426 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">r</sub>
###xml 463 475 463 475 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 494 500 494 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 511 512 511 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh151f2">2</xref>
###xml 516 518 516 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c25">25</xref>
###xml 90 97 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 463 475 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 494 500 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Ramotar et al. (17) predicted the presence of a 72 bp intron sequence in the apn1 gene of S.pombe, flanked by 5'- and 3'-splice sites, which is in-frame with the open reading frame (ORF) of the putative coding sequence. To confirm the coding sequence, we cloned Apn1 cDNA from a cDNA library as described in Materials and Methods. The cDNA contained an ORF with 1029 bp, and lacked the intron (Fig. 1). Apn1, with predicted Mr of 40 kDa, shares 46% identity with S.cerevisiae Apn1 and 43% with E.coli Nfo (Fig. 2A) (25).
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 40 46 40 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 219 221 212 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c26">26</xref>
###xml 290 297 283 290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 40 46 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 290 297 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
The X-ray crystallographic structure of E.coli Nfo showed it to be a single domain protein with eight parallel beta-strands surrounded by eight peripheral alpha-helices. Its active site contains a trinuclear Zn center (26). These residues and the flanking sequences are highly conserved in S.pombe Apn1 and other members of the Nfo family.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
###xml 11 18 11 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 11 18 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
Cloning of S.pombe Apn2 cDNA
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 4 12 4 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2/xth</italic>
###xml 62 69 62 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 123 125 123 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c10">10</xref>
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c11">11</xref>
###xml 129 131 129 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c27">27</xref>
###xml 933 934 933 934 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">r</sub>
###xml 951 952 951 952 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh151f1">1</xref>
###xml 62 69 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 111 116 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The apn2/xth-like sequence (SPBC3D6.10) was identified in the S.pombe genome database by homology search using human APE1 (10,11,27). Using PCR, we isolated cDNA clones containing the predicted ORF from the Machmaker cDNA library. As in the case of Apn1, we identified a putative intron sequence, based on the genomic sequence, by using primer pairs flanking the intron region. Surprisingly, all (>40) clones were found to contain the intron sequence, which could have been derived from either genomic DNA contamination or unspliced transcripts. It is clear that the intron-containing cDNA does not encode active APE because of the presence of an in-frame stop codon in the intron. We then succeeded in cloning mature cDNA, by using RT-PCR of total RNA, although nearly half of the clones still contained the intron sequence. The Apn2 cDNA contains 1572 bp, corresponding to a polypeptide with 523 amino acid residues and predicted Mr of 58 kDa (Fig. 1).
###end p 31
###begin title 32
###xml 0 25 0 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</italic>
###xml 0 25 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
Schizosaccharomyces pombe Apn2 is a member of the Xth family
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 0 25 0 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</italic>
###xml 84 90 84 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 111 123 111 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 135 136 135 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh151f2">2</xref>
###xml 141 143 141 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c10">10</xref>
###xml 144 146 144 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c11">11</xref>
###xml 353 355 353 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c24">24</xref>
###xml 400 401 400 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh151f2">2</xref>
###xml 502 504 502 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c24">24</xref>
###xml 505 507 505 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c28">28</xref>
###xml 0 25 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
###xml 84 90 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 96 101 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 111 123 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 383 388 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Schizosaccharomyces pombe Apn2 shares several conserved and essential residues with E.coli Xth, human APE1 and S.cerevisiae Apn2 (Fig. 2B.) (10,11). The hAPE1 polypeptide with 318 amino acid residues contains two distinct domains, of which the N-terminal domain is dispensable for the endonuclease activity, but is required for its regulatory function (24). This region is absent in yeast Apn2 (Fig. 2B). The C-terminal sequence of APE1, essential for the endonuclease activity, is homologous to Apn2 (24,28).
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 87 89 87 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c29">29</xref>
###xml 201 203 201 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c29">29</xref>
###xml 204 206 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c30">30</xref>
###xml 234 246 234 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 289 297 289 297 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 319 326 319 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 347 349 347 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c12">12</xref>
###xml 356 361 356 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 498 506 498 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 508 510 508 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c31">31</xref>
###xml 26 31 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 234 246 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
The C-terminal regions of yeast Apn2/hAPE2-type proteins contain a PCNA-binding motif (29) and a sequence homologous to one present in the C-terminal region of the DNA topoisomerase III (TOP3) family (29,30). The C-terminal region of S.cerevisiae Apn2 is not required for its APE activity in vitro, but is required for in vivo repair of AP sites (12). Unk et al. subsequently showed physical and functional interaction of Apn2 with PCNA, which stimulates the 3'-phophodiesterase activities of Apn2 in vitro (31).
###end p 34
###begin title 35

###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 4 8 4 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 13 17 13 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 29 36 29 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 132 136 132 136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 141 145 141 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 212 224 212 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 226 228 226 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c11">11</xref>
###xml 29 36 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 212 224 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
The apn1 and apn2 mutants of S.pombe showed no obvious phenotype with regard to growth rate or viability, which indicates that both apn1 and apn2 are dispensable under normal conditions, as was also observed for S.cerevisiae (11).
###end p 36
###begin title 37
###xml 19 22 19 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn</italic>
MMS sensitivity of apn mutants
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 123 125 123 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c32">32</xref>
###xml 178 179 178 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">N</italic>
###xml 215 217 215 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c32">32</xref>
###xml 224 228 224 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mag1</italic>
###xml 232 239 232 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 241 243 241 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c33">33</xref>
###xml 354 361 354 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 394 395 394 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh151f3">3</xref>
###xml 412 416 412 416 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 492 496 488 492 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 564 568 556 560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 573 577 561 565 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 653 657 637 641 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 232 239 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
APEs play a central role in repair of methylated bases, e.g. 3-methyladenine and 7-methylguanine which are induced by MMS (32). Excision of the alkyl base adducts with mammalian N-methylpurine-DNA glycosylase, MPG (32), and mag1 in S.pombe (33), should generate AP sites whose repair requires APE. Thus MMS sensitivity of APE mutants is a measure of the in vivo repair activity of APEs. Figure 3A shows that the apn2Delta mutant was moderately more sensitive to MMS than the wild type, while apn1Delta mutation alone had no effect on MMS sensitivity. However, the apn1Deltaapn2Delta double mutant showed significantly higher sensitivity to MMS than the apn2Delta mutant.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 289 296 289 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 309 321 309 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 415 417 415 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c11">11</xref>
###xml 289 296 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 309 321 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
These results strongly suggest that Apn2 and not Apn1 plays a rate-limiting role in protecting cells from alkylation damage. However, in the absence of Apn2, Apn1 provides protection and has a minor role in repair of AP sites. Thus there is an unexpected role reversal of Apn1 and Apn2 in S.pombe relative to S.cerevisiae in which Apn2 is completely dispensable in alkylation damage repair in the presence of Apn1 (11).
###end p 39
###begin title 40
###xml 31 35 31 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 41 45 37 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
MMS-induced mutagenesis in the apn1Delta apn2Delta mutant
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 165 167 165 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c34">34</xref>
###xml 255 258 255 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">can</italic>
###xml 258 259 258 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">r</italic>
###xml 258 259 258 259 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>r</italic></sup>
###xml 259 260 259 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</italic>
###xml 265 268 265 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn</italic>
###xml 285 286 285 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh151f3">3</xref>
###xml 303 307 303 307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 421 424 417 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">can</italic>
###xml 424 425 420 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">r</italic>
###xml 424 425 420 421 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>r</italic></sup>
###xml 425 426 421 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</italic>
###xml 456 460 452 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 511 515 503 507 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 520 524 508 512 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
Because non-instructional AP sites are highly mutagenic, a deficiency in APE leads to enhanced mutation frequency after treatment with genotoxic agents such as MMS (34). We determined the frequency of MMS-induced forward mutation to canavanin resistance (canr1) in apn mutants. Figure 3B shows that the apn1Delta mutant and wild-type strain had a similar mutagenic response, while significant enhancement in frequency of canr1 mutation was observed in the apn2Delta mutant; the frequency was even higher in the apn1Deltaapn2Delta double mutant. These mutagenesis data, which mirror the survival data, clearly show that the APE activity of Apn2, and to a lesser extent Apn1, is needed to repair AP sites and prevent mutations.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
###xml 19 23 19 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>

###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 72 79 72 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>

###xml 345 346 345 346 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c1">1</xref>

###xml 462 466 458 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 471 475 463 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 587 591 563 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 624 636 596 608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 663 665 635 637 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c10">10</xref>
###xml 796 803 768 775 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 120 127 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 624 636 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 796 803 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
We assessed the relative 3'-phosphodiesterase activity of Apn1 and Apn2 in vivo by examining the sensitivity of various S.pombe mutants to phleomycin D1, a glycopeptide antibiotic structurally similar to bleomycin. These antibiotics react with deoxyribose to cleave phosphodiester bonds, and generate 3'-phosphoglycolate, and oxidized AP sites (1). Figure 3C shows that mutation in apn1 had no effect on phleomycin sensitivity, while the apn2Delta mutant or the apn1Deltaapn2Delta double mutant was approximately3-fold more sensitive to this drug than the wild-type strain. In contrast, apn2Delta mutation did not sensitize S.cerevisiae at all to the antibiotic (10). These results strongly suggest that Apn2 is the major contributor of not only APE but also DNA 3'-phosphodiesterase activity in S.pombe.

###begin title 44

Inactivation of an alternative excision repair pathway enhances sensitivity of the apn2Delta mutant to alkylation damage
###end title 44
###begin p 45
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Uve1</italic>

###xml 19 26 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 41 53 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 171 177 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 808 815 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
Uve1, expressed in S.pombe but absent in S.cerevisiae, was shown to repair UV photoproducts on the basis of its ability to phenotypically complement UV sensitivity of the E.coli uvrA recA phr mutant (35,36). In addition to its function as a mismatch-, and UV photoproduct-specific endonuclease (18,21,37), Uve1 possesses APE activity (18). We tested the contribution of Uve1 in repair of AP sites by measuring MMS and UV sensitivity of apn and uve1 mutants. The observed lack of UV sensitivity in apn1Delta and apn2Delta mutants was expected because UV damage repair does not involve BER in which Apns play a key role (Fig. 3D). The uve1Delta mutant was shown to be moderately sensitive to UV, which is consistent with its role in UV damage repair (38). On the other hand, mutation in uve1 did not sensitize S.pombe cells to MMS (Fig. 4D). However, this mutation acted like apn1 mutation in enhancing MMS sensitivity of the apn2Delta mutant. This indicates that both Uve1 and Apn1 provide back-up functions in repairing AP sites in the genome.

###xml 34 38 34 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>

We tested phenotypic complementation of the apn1Deltaapn2Delta mutant with S.pombe Apn2 or human APE1, using their expression plasmids in pNMT1 and pESP1 vectors with the thiamine-repressible nmt1 promoter, as described in Materials and Methods. Leaky expression of these proteins was observed in the presence of thiamine (39). In any case, as shown in Figure 5A, both Apn2 and human APE1 restored MMS resistance of the apn1Deltaapn2Delta mutant to the wild-type level. In contrast, human APE2 or S.pombe Apn1 could not complement apn1 apn2 deficiency (Fig. 5A and B). We conclude from these results that S.pombe Apn2 and human APE1 have a similar range of activity in repairing AP sites, and hence human APE1 could fully complement Apn2 deficiency.

###begin title 48
###xml 68 75 68 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
PCNA-binding and TOP3-homologous region in Apn2 are dispensable for in vivo repair of alkylation damage

###xml 840 847 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
The C-terminal region in S.cerevisiae Apn2 contains a PCNA-binding motif which was shown to be required for its binding to PCNA. Such binding activates the 3'-phosphodiesterase activity of Apn2 (31). Because S.pombe and S.cerevisiae Apn2 have extensive sequence homology, including the PCNA-binding and TOP3-like motifs, we tested the role of these motifs in MMS resistance of S.pombe. We generated alanine substitution mutants at positions 402 and 403 in the PCNA-binding motif, and at positions 456-458 included in the TOP3-like motif in the Apn2 expression plasmid. These mutants were as effective in phenotypic complementation of the apn1Deltaapn2Delta mutant as wild-type Apn2 (Fig. 5B). Thus, even if the binding of PCNA to Apn2 occurs in vivo, this interaction, unlike in the case of S.cerevisiae, does not modulate Apn2 activity in S.pombe.

###begin title 50
APE activity of recombinant Apn2
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 4 11 4 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 74 81 74 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 224 225 224 225 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sub>
###xml 258 270 258 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe apn1</italic>
###xml 276 280 272 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 318 319 310 311 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sub>
###xml 475 482 467 474 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 961 962 953 954 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh151f6">6</xref>
###xml 258 265 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 475 482 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
The in vivo studies described so far strongly suggest that Apn2 is active in vivo as an APE and a 3'-phosphodiesterase. We then directly tested recombinant Apn2 for APE activity. We attempted to over-express GST-Apn2 and His6-Apn2 fusion polypeptides in the S.pombe apn1Delta apn2Delta mutant. The recombinant Apn2-His6 was found to be mostly insoluble (data not shown). Furthermore, overexpression of the GST-Apn2 fusion protein appeared to be toxic because the transformed S.pombe stopped growing and became elongated (data not shown). Nevertheless, we were able to purify the GST-Apn2 fusion polypeptide in soluble form by affinity chromatography from extracts of these cells early after transformation. GST-Apn2 showed significant APE activity with THF-containing DNA oligo as described in Materials and Methods. The formation of the predicted 30 nt product indicated that Apn2 catalyzed hydrolysis of DNA 5' to the abasic site linearly for up to 1 h (Fig. 6B). There are several possible reasons for very low specific activity of the Apn2 fusion protein. One obvious possibility is that even though present in a soluble form, a large fraction of the protein is not correctly folded, and the folding problem was exacerbated by the presence of the GST.

An earlier report raised the unlikely possibility that APE is absent in S.pombe in spite of the presence of apn genes (17). ###begin title 54
DISCUSSION
###end title 54
###begin p 55
###xml 14 26 14 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 31 38 31 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 130 137 130 137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 418 430 418 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 521 528 521 528 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 561 572 561 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe apn</italic>
###xml 699 703 699 703 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 14 26 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 31 38 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 130 137 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 176 181 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 418 430 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 521 528 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 561 568 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
Although both S.cerevisiae and S.pombe have been extensively used as model eukaryotes for elucidating various cellular processes, S.pombe, in spite of its closer similarity to human cells, has not been extensively investigated for DNA repair processes. Among various excision repair pathways, BER is involved in repair of endogenous oxidation and alkylation damage in which APE plays a central role. While two APEs in S.cerevisiae, Apn1 and Apn2, have been extensively characterized, much less is known about the Apns in S.pombe. One major problem in examining S.pombe apn genes is the presence of small introns and low efficiency of the cells to splice out these introns, especially in the case of apn2.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 8 13 8 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 16 18 16 18 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c17">17</xref>
###xml 73 85 73 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe apn1</italic>
###xml 189 196 189 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 232 244 232 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 323 325 323 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c11">11</xref>
###xml 435 439 435 439 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">APN1</italic>
###xml 512 516 512 516 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 525 532 525 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 670 677 670 677 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 960 964 960 964 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mes1</italic>
###xml 973 980 973 980 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 1025 1027 1025 1027 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c40">40</xref>
###xml 73 80 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 189 196 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 232 244 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 316 321 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 476 481 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 525 532 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 670 677 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 973 980 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
Ramotar et al. (17) predicted, but did not show experimentally, that the S.pombe apn1 gene contains a 72 bp intron. We confirmed the absence of this intron in cloned Apn1 cDNA. The Apn1 of S.pombe shares 46% identical residues with S.cerevisiae Apn1 which accounts for >90% of the APE/3'-diesterase activity in this yeast (11). This is consistent with the observed increase of spontaneous mutations as well as MMS sensitivity when the APN1 gene was inactivated in the budding yeast. In contrast, deletion of the apn1 gene in S.pombe did not affect its MMS sensitivity. Thus it appears that Apn1 is not the major contributor of APE activity. In our attempts to clone the S.pombe Apn2 cDNA, we discovered that due to inefficient splicing, the cDNA library contained mostly copies of Apn2 primary transcripts. This suggests that splicing plays a regulatory role in maintaining the Apn2 protein level. Similar regulation at the splicing level was observed for the mes1 gene in S.pombe whose product is developmentally regulated (40).
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 12 16 12 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 21 25 21 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 35 42 35 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 100 104 100 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 113 120 113 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 183 187 183 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">APN2</italic>
###xml 198 210 198 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 221 225 221 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 283 290 279 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 327 331 323 327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 568 574 560 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 584 587 576 579 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nfo</italic>
###xml 649 652 641 644 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">xth</italic>
###xml 685 691 677 683 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 758 765 750 757 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nfo/xth</italic>
###xml 842 845 834 837 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">xth</italic>
###xml 854 856 846 848 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c41">41</xref>
###xml 888 892 880 884 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 897 901 889 893 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 952 953 944 945 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 35 42 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 113 120 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 198 210 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 283 290 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 514 527 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 568 574 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 685 691 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Deletion of apn1 and apn2 genes in S.pombe did not affect its growth and viability. Deletion of the apn2 gene in S.pombe caused MMS sensitivity, in contrast to a lack of phenotype of APN2 mutant in S.cerevisiae. Although apn1Delta mutation alone did not confer sensitivity to MMS in S.pombe, it enhanced MMS sensitivity of the apn2Delta mutant. These results strongly argue for the primary role of Apn2 in the removal of AP sites, and indicate that Apn1 provides the back-up activity in the absence of Apn2 in the fission yeast. An analogous situation was observed in E.coli in which nfo mutants do not show increased sensitivity to MMS, unlike the xth mutant. Xth is the major APE in E.coli and Nfo appears to provide the back-up activity. Accordingly, the nfo/xth double mutant is more sensitive to alkylating and oxidizing agents than the xth mutant (41). As expected, inactivation of apn1 and apn2 did not sensitize the cells to UV (254 nm, 300 J/m2) because the BER pathway is not involved in repair of UV-induced pyrimidine photoproducts.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 39 45 39 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>

###xml 187 189 187 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c43">43</xref>

###xml 1294 1298 1254 1258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 1321 1333 1277 1289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 1492 1494 1448 1450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c44">44</xref>
###xml 1538 1545 1494 1501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 39 45 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 767 774 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 1150 1157 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 1321 1333 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 1538 1545 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
Structure modeling shows that Uve1 and E.coli Nfo have similar secondary structure (42). Even though in vitro studies have indicated that Uve1 generates DNA strand breaks at AP sites (18,43), its function in BER was unknown. The present studies provide the first evidence for the ability of Uve1 to repair AP sites in vivo. As in the case Apn1, inactivation of Uve1 alone did not confer sensitivity to MMS. However, deletion of the uve1 gene in the apn2Delta mutant significantly increased MMS sensitivity, suggesting that Uve1 functions independently of Apn2 in removal of AP sites to enhance cell survival. A low level of APE activity was detected in the extract of a apn1Deltaapn2Deltauve1Delta triple mutant, suggesting the presence of other unidentified APEs in S.pombe (data not shown). Interestingly, the apn1Deltaapn2Deltauve1Delta triple mutant was no more sensitive to MMS than the apn2Deltauve1Delta double mutant (unpublished observation). This suggests a common downstream pathway in repair of AP sites initiated by Uve1 and Apn1, which is distinct from the Apn2-initiated process. In any case, Apn2 appears to be the predominant APE in S.pombe while both Uve1 and Apn1 provide the back-up functions in BER. However, it should be noted that it was recently shown that the apn1Deltaapn2Delta double mutant of S.cerevisiae is viable but not in the absence of Rad1/Rad10. This suggests alternative repair of AP sites involving this nucleotide excision repair-specific endonuclease (44). An analogous situation may be present in S.pombe.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 50 57 50 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 85 87 85 87 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c17">17</xref>
###xml 291 298 291 298 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 343 345 343 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c17">17</xref>
###xml 778 782 778 782 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 788 792 784 788 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 50 57 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 291 298 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 931 944 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
An earlier attempt to demonstrate APE activity in S.pombe extracts was unsuccessful (17). Because the present studies predict the presence of APE activity in Apn polypeptides, we have shown directly that the recombinant Apn2 is an authentic APE. We were also able to measure APE activity in S.pombe extracts, in contrast to an earlier report (17). Although the reason for the discrepancy between the previous study and our experiments is not clear, it could be due to a difference in extract preparation. While we detected the APE activity in extract produced in a French press, the extract prepared with beating beads was inactive (data not shown). It is likely that Apns are easily inactivated during preparation. More importantly, a significant amount of APE activity in the apn1Delta apn2Delta double mutant could be due to Uve1, and other unknown APEs. Thus our results provide the first evidence for APE-dependent BER in the fission yeast, in line with other organisms.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 4 10 4 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 194 196 194 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c30">30</xref>

###xml 959 963 951 955 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 969 973 957 961 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 991 998 975 982 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 4 10 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 41 46 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 56 61 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 122 127 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 182 187 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 499 506 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 991 998 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
The E.coli Xth family of APEs, including yeast Apn2 and human APE1, share critical active site motifs. A newly discovered human APE, hAPE2, shares significant sequence homology with yeast Apn2 (30). Although the recombinant hAPE2 was reported to have very weak APE activity (30), we were unable to detect such activity in the recombinant protein purified from insect cells (unpublished experiment). We examined the in vivo activity of hAPE1 and hAPE2 in a phenotypic complementation assay using the S.pombe apn1Deltaapn2Delta mutant. Expression of hAPE1 in the mutant was indicated by enhanced APE activity in cell-free extract (data not shown), and increased MMS resistance of the mutant. On the other hand, no complementation was observed when hAPE2 was expressed in this mutant strain (Fig. 5A). Thus, while hAPE2 may have some unknown enzymatic activity, it does not appear to function as an APE in vivo. The reason for the lack of complementation of the apn1Delta apn2Delta mutant with S.pombe Apn1 is not obvious. However, it is possible that Apn1 does not possess the same level of distinct activities of Apn2 or hAPE1 needed for repair of MMS-induced damage. Furthermore, the low sensitivity of the growth inhibition assay may mask the small extent of complementation with Apn1.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 155 157 155 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c29">29</xref>
###xml 164 169 164 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>

###xml 294 301 294 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 340 344 340 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 349 353 345 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 392 399 384 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 579 586 571 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 643 650 635 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 143 148 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 223 235 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 392 399 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 579 586 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
The C-terminal regions in proteins of the APE2 family contain a consensus sequence for a PCNA-binding motif that was shown to be functional in human APE2 (29). Unk et al. (12) reported that the C-terminal region of Apn2 of S.cerevisiae is dispensable for its APE activity in vitro, but not for in vivo suppression of MMS sensitivity of the apn1Deltaapn2Delta mutant. In contrast, we found in S.pombe that mutation in the putative PCNA-binding and TOP3-type motifs of Apn2 did not affect MMS resistance. These results suggest that the PCNA-binding and TOP3-type motifs of Apn2 in S.pombe are not directly involved in the repair of abasic sites in vivo.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 152 153 152 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c1">1</xref>

###xml 186 190 182 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 430 432 422 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh151c10">10</xref>
###xml 539 546 531 538 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 585 589 577 581 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 615 616 607 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh151f4">4</xref>
###xml 694 698 686 690 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 792 804 784 796 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 832 839 824 831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 159 171 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 539 546 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 792 804 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 832 839 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
Phleomycin D1 produces oxidized AP sites and strand breaks with 3'-phosphoglycolate termini which prevent DNA replication or repair by DNA polymerases (1). In S.cerevisiae, the apn1Deltaapn2Delta double mutant, but not the single mutants, displays enhanced sensitivity to phleomycin D1, indicating that these enzymes functionally overlap in repair of oxidized AP sites and in the repair of strand breaks with 3'-terminal lesions (10). We have shown here that Apn2 contributes significantly to repair of phleomycin D1-induced DNA damage in S.pombe, based on enhanced sensitivity of the apn2 mutant to the drug (Fig. 4). The sensitivity did not increase further when cells were also deficient in apn1. These results and identification of 3'-phospodiesterase and 3'-5' exonuclease activities in S.cerevisiae Apn2 strongly suggest that S.pombe Apn2 possesses both of these activities.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 93 100 93 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 146 153 146 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 158 171 158 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae.</italic>
###xml 222 234 222 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 398 402 398 402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn1</italic>
###xml 404 408 404 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apn2</italic>
###xml 413 417 413 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">uve1</italic>
###xml 434 441 434 441 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 93 100 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 146 153 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
###xml 158 170 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 179 192 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 222 234 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 621 634 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
In summary, the present study provides the first evidence for the presence of active APEs in S.pombe, and for distinct functions of these APEs in S.pombe and S.cerevisiae. In the fission yeast, as in mammals but unlike in S.cerevisiae, Apn2, an APE1 ortholog, is the major APE/3'-phosphodiesterase for repairing DNA damage caused by oxidation and alkylation. Furthermore, at least three APE genes, apn1, apn2 and uve1, are functional in vivo. Whether expression of these genes is regulated by ROS, and how the proteins interact with other BER components needs to be addressed in order to elucidate the BER process in the fission yeast.
