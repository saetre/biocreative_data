###begin article-title 0
Altering the GTP binding site of the DNA/RNA-binding protein, Translin/TB-RBP, decreases RNA binding and may create a dominant negative phenotype
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 95 120 95 120 <email xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nhecht@mail.med.upenn.edu</email>
To whom correspondence should be addressed. Tel: +1 215 898 0144; Fax: +1 215 573 5408; Email: nhecht@mail.med.upenn.edu
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 733 736 717 720 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 747 750 731 734 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 878 881 862 865 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 999 1002 983 986 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 1113 1116 1097 1100 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 813 818 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
The DNA/RNA-binding protein, Translin/Testis Brain RNA-binding protein (Translin/TB-RBP), contains a putative GTP binding site in its C-terminus which is highly conserved. To determine if guanine nucleotide binding to this site functionally alters nucleic acid binding, electrophoretic mobility shift assays were performed with RNA and DNA binding probes. GTP, but not GDP, reduces RNA binding by approximately50% and the poorly hydrolyzed GTP analog, GTPgammaS, reduces binding by >90% in gel shift and immunoprecipitation assays. No similar reduction of DNA binding is seen. When the putative GTP binding site of TB-RBP, amino acid sequence VTAGD, is altered to VTNSD by site directed mutagenesis, GTP will no longer bind to TB-RBPGTP and TB-RBPGTP no longer binds to RNA, although DNA binding is not affected. Yeast two-hybrid assays reveal that like wild-type TB-RBP, TB-RBPGTP will interact with itself, with wild-type TB-RBP and with Translin associated factor X (Trax). Transfection of TB-RBPGTP into NIH 3T3 cells leads to a marked increase in cell death suggesting a dominant negative function for TB-RBPGTP in cells. These data suggest TB-RBP is an RNA-binding protein whose activity is allosterically controlled by nucleotide binding.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 199 200 199 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c1">1</xref>
###xml 543 544 543 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c2">2</xref>
###xml 545 546 545 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c5">5</xref>
RNA-binding proteins play numerous roles in the post-transcriptional regulation of gene expression including splicing and capping in the nucleus, transport of mRNAs and translation in the cytoplasm (1). Many of these processes are modulated by post-translational protein modifications, which alter binding specificities and the association-dissociation properties of RNA-protein complexes. Small molecules such as divalent metals and nucleotides also can modify protein binding to nucleic acids and influence processes such as mRNA transport (2-5).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 147 148 147 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c6">6</xref>
###xml 149 150 149 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c7">7</xref>
###xml 359 360 359 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c8">8</xref>
###xml 361 363 361 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c13">13</xref>
###xml 709 711 709 711 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c14">14</xref>
###xml 712 714 712 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c20">20</xref>
###xml 54 59 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 76 81 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
 The Testis Brain RNA-binding protein (TB-RBP) is the mouse ortholog of the human DNA-binding protein, Translin, a highly conserved 26 kD protein (6,7). As a DNA-binding protein, TB-RBP/Translin has been proposed to bind to single-stranded DNA sequences at translocation breakpoints in lymphocytes and at recombination hot-spots during meiosis in germ cells (8-13). As an RNA-binding protein, TB-RBP/Translin has been proposed to bind to conserved sequence elements, often in the 3' untranslated regions of mRNAs. Many of these mRNAs are translationally regulated and some of the mRNAs expressed in post-meiotic male germ cells require transport through intercellular bridges to maintain haploid sufficiency (14-20).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 42 43 42 43 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c8">8</xref>
###xml 44 46 44 46 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c21">21</xref>
###xml 47 49 47 49 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c22">22</xref>
###xml 234 235 234 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c8">8</xref>
###xml 358 360 358 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c23">23</xref>
###xml 408 410 408 410 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c24">24</xref>
###xml 441 443 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c24">24</xref>
###xml 487 489 483 485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c24">24</xref>
###xml 562 564 558 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c25">25</xref>
###xml 565 567 561 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c27">27</xref>
###xml 700 709 696 705 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 710 712 706 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
###xml 869 871 861 863 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c14">14</xref>
###xml 872 874 864 866 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c19">19</xref>
###xml 898 900 890 892 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c24">24</xref>
###xml 1044 1046 1036 1038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c18">18</xref>
###xml 1260 1262 1252 1254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c17">17</xref>
 TB-RBP/Translin functions as a multimer (8,21,22). The protein must form at least a dimer to bind DNA or RNA and larger complexes of tetramers and octamers have been detected by gel filtration chromatography and electron microscopy (8). In addition to forming homomultimeric complexes, TB-RBP/Translin interacts with a number of proteins including GADD 34 (23), a transitional endoplasmic reticulum ATPase (24), a cytoskeletal gamma actin (24), and Translin associated factor X (Trax) (24). Trax is a 32 kD protein with extensive amino acid homology to TB-RBP (25-27). TB-RBP forms heterodimers or oligomers with Trax, which enhance TB-RBP binding to DNA, but strongly inhibit TB-RBP binding to RNA in vitro (28). TB-RBP also serves as a linker protein, binding specific mRNAs to microtubules, and TB-RBP co-immunoprecipitates a gamma actin, suggesting a microtubule (14,19) and/or microfilament (24) based transport of mRNA for TB-RBP. Moreover, suppression of TB-RBP binding to transported mRNAs disrupts mRNA sorting in hypocampal neurons (18). In male germ cells, TB-RBP localizes in the nuclei of meiotic pachytene spermatocytes and in the cytoplasm in all subsequent germ cell stages, suggesting TB-RBP has distinct subcellular functions in the testis (17).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"> In vivo</italic>
###xml 13 21 13 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 146 148 146 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c21">21</xref>
###xml 149 151 149 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c22">22</xref>
###xml 290 292 290 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c21">21</xref>
###xml 293 295 293 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c22">22</xref>
###xml 433 435 433 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c21">21</xref>
###xml 436 438 436 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c22">22</xref>
###xml 593 595 593 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c22">22</xref>
###xml 596 598 596 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
###xml 727 729 727 729 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
 In vivo and in vitro studies with truncated and mutated forms of TB-RBP have revealed several essential domains needed for nucleic acid binding (21,22). A leucine zipper in the C-terminus is required for protein dimerization which is stabilized by a disulfide bond involving cysteine 225 (21,22). Dimerization or oligomerization of TB-RBP is required for either DNA or RNA binding, since monomers will not bind either nucleic acid (21,22). In addition, one basic domain in the N-terminus is essential for either DNA or RNA binding, while a second basic domain is also needed for RNA binding (22,28). A highly conserved leucine-rich nuclear export signal in the C-terminus is essential for the exit of TB-RBP from the nucleus (28).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
 In addition to the leucine zipper, nuclear export signal and two basic domains essential for TB-RBP function, sequence analysis suggests that TB-RBP contains a putative GTP binding domain. We demonstrate here that TB-RBP binds GTP and that GTP, but not GDP, suppresses RNA binding, but not DNA binding. Mutation of this site inhibits RNA binding and creates a dominant negative form of TB-RBP in transfected NIH 3T3 cells. To our knowledge, TB-RBP is the first example of a DNA/RNA-binding protein in a multicellular organism where GTP/GDP binding selectively affects its nucleic acid binding.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 9
###begin title 10
RNA and DNA mobility shift assays
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 135 137 135 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
###xml 302 304 298 300 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>
###xml 353 355 345 347 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>
###xml 544 545 532 533 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Electrophoretic mobility shift assays were performed with an RNA probe, transcript c or a DNA probe, Bcl-CL1, as previously described (28). The RNA-protein complexes were digested with T1 RNase (1 U) and incubated with heparin (5 mg/ml). Transcript c was transcribed from a pGEM3Z plasmid using [alpha-32P]CTP while Bcl-CL1 was radiolabeled with [gamma-32P]ATP using T4 polynucleotide kinase. Recombinant proteins were incubated with 40 000 c.p.m. of RNA or DNA probe for 10 min at room temperature in 20 microl of 20 mM HEPES pH 7.6, 3 mM MgCl2, 40 mM KC1, 2 mM DTT and 5% glycerol. Nucleic acid-protein complexes were analyzed in 4% polyacrylamide gels in TBE buffer.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Isolation of TB-RBP-RNA complexes from testis extracts by immunoprecipitation
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 101 103 101 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c19">19</xref>
Immunoprecipitation of TB-RBP and its bound mRNAs was performed essentially as described previously (19). Testis extracts (10 mg) containing RNasin (100 U/ml) were incubated with protein A-agarose beads (100 microl), pre-immune serum (10 microl), and 20 mM Tris pH 7.6, 40 mM KCl and 1.5 mM MgCl (TKM; 4 ml) containing 0.1% Tween-20, RNasin (100 U/ml) and 0.1% Empigen BB (TKM-T) for 2 h at 4degreesC. After centrifugation at 2000 r.p.m. for 2 min (Eppendorf microfuge), TKM-T (500 microl), protein A-agarose (40 ul) and preimmune serum or an affinity purified antibody to TB-RBP (15 microg) were added to the supernatant. After incubation overnight at 4degreesC, the mixtures were centrifuged at 2000 r.p.m. for 2 min, and the pellets were washed four times with 5 ml TKM-T. The pellets were then suspended in 250 microl of Tri Reagent and RNA was purified. Following ethanol precipitation, the RNA was dissolved in 20 microl DEPC-treated water and assayed by RT-PCR.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Site-directed mutagenesis of the putative GTP binding domain of TB-RBP
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 78 94 78 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 112 115 112 115 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 611 614 611 614 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 844 847 844 847 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 78 94 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Wild-type TB-RBP was subcloned into a pET28a vector for protein expression in Escherichia coli. To create TB-RBPGTP, site directed mutagenesis was carried out using the QuickChange site-directed mutagenesis kit (Stratagene, La Jolla, CA) following the manufacturer's protocol. Using synthesized oligonucleotide primers (5'-GTCAACAGTGTCACTACTAATAGCGACTACTCTCGGCCCCTTGAC-3' and 5'-GTCAAGGGGCCGAGAGTAGTCGCTATTAGTAGTGACACT-GTTGAC-3'), the putative GTP binding site, amino acid sequence VTAGD, was changed to VTNSD. DNA sequence analysis of the construct confirmed the altered sequence. Recombinant TB-RBP and TB-RBPGTP were expressed in BL21 (DE3) cells and purified by HisBind Resin (Novagen, Madison, WI) following the manufacturer's protocol. The purities of the proteins were confirmed by SDS-PAGE. For transfection, wild-type TB-RBP and TB-RBPGTP mutant cDNAs were subcloned in frame to the C-terminus of GFP in the pEGFP C2 vector.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
Yeast two-hybrid assays
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 32 35 32 35 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 69 72 69 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Eco</italic>
###xml 75 78 75 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sal</italic>
###xml 218 223 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 376 381 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Complete cDNAs of TB-RBP, TB-RBPGTP and Trax were subcloned into the EcoRI/Sall sites of pBD-GAL4cam and pAD-GAL4 (Stratagene). Pairs of binding domain and activation domain plasmid constructs were co-transfected into yeast (strain YRG-2) and transformants were selected on SD medium lacking leucine and tryptophan. Protein-protein interactions were detected by the growth of yeast on SD medium lacking leucine, tryptophan and histidine, and by the X-Gal filter lift assay on SD medium lacking leucine and tryptophan. Strength of interactions was determined by the addition of 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole (Sigma, St Louis, MO) at concentrations from 5 to 100 mM. All transformation and filter lift assay procedures were performed following the manufacturer's instructions (Stratagene).
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Cell culture, transfections and confocal fluorescence microscopy
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 84 88 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
NIH 3T3 cells were grown in Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium containing 10% fetal calf serum and streptomycin. Cells were transiently transfected using FuGENE 6 reagent (Roche) according to the manufacturer's protocol, grown on two-well chamber slides and fixed, 48 h post-transfection, using 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS. After washing three times in PBS, the slides were mounted using Vectashield mounting medium and examined by fluorescent microscopy and by confocal microscopy with a Bio-Rad MRC-1024 confocal laser scanning microscope. ApopTag staining of transfected cells was carried out using the manufacturer's protocol (Intergen, NY).
###end p 19
###begin title 20
RESULTS
###end title 20
###begin title 21
GTP reduces TB-RBP binding to RNA but not to DNA
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 50 52 50 52 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
###xml 214 215 214 215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f1">1</xref>
###xml 219 221 219 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c29">29</xref>
###xml 390 400 390 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 404 411 404 411 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus</italic>
###xml 36 41 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 335 342 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 346 351 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 390 400 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 404 411 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus</span>
###xml 416 421 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Sequence and mutational analysis of mouse TB-RBP (28) revealed several domains including a putative GTP binding site, VTAGD, which shares substantial homology to the DTAGQ sequence present in many G-proteins (Fig. 1A) (29). Comparing known TB-RBP/Translin sequences, this putative GTP binding site is identical in species ranging from chicken to human and differs by one amino acid between Drosophila or Xenopus and human (GenBank accession nos, see Fig. 1B legend.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 263 265 263 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
###xml 385 386 369 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f2">2</xref>
###xml 497 498 481 482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f2">2</xref>
###xml 653 654 617 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f2">2</xref>
###xml 806 807 770 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f2">2</xref>
###xml 892 897 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
 To determine if the GTP binding region could be a functional domain of TB-RBP, we investigated the effect of guanine nucleotides on the binding of TB-RBP to nucleic acids. In electrophoretic mobility shift assays using transcript c, an RNA known to bind TB-RBP (28), the presence of 250 microM GTP reduces recombinant TB-RBP binding to RNA to approximately50% of control values (Fig. 2, lane 3). When GDP is substituted for GTP, no significant reduction from control binding levels is seen (Fig. 2, lane 4). Addition of 250 microM GTPgammaS, a poorly hydrolyzed analog of GTP, reduces the TB-RBP-transcript c interactions by approximately10-fold (Fig. 2, lane 5). ATP or the poorly hydrolyzed analog of ATP, adenylyl imidophosphate, (data not shown) also does not decrease the RNA binding of TB-RBP (Fig. 2, lane 6). Similar decreases in RNA binding were obtained with endogeneous TB-RBP in mouse testicular extracts in the presence of GTPgammaS (see below).
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 199 200 187 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 303 304 287 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 453 455 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c19">19</xref>
###xml 731 732 711 712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 769 770 749 750 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 803 804 783 784 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 889 890 869 870 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 984 985 960 961 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 1055 1056 1031 1032 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 1117 1118 1093 1094 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 1153 1154 1129 1130 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 1303 1304 1279 1280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
 To investigate the inhibitory effect of GTPgammaS on TB-RBP binding to RNA, two approaches were taken. First, a dose response curve increasing GTPgammaS from 50 microM to 1 mM was carried out (Fig. 3A). A concentration dependent decrease in RNA binding was observed as GTPgammaS levels increased (Fig. 3A, lanes 3-6). Secondly, utilizing the ability of our affinity purified antibody to TB-RBP to selectively precipitate TB-RBP bound endogenous mRNAs (19), we analyzed the effect of GTPgammaS on mRNA-TB-RBP co-precipitation from extracts. Following immunoprecipitation of TB-RBP, RT-PCR was used to assay two mRNAs that specifically bind TB-RBP and two control mRNAs that lack the TB-RBP binding sites from the precipitate (Fig. 3B). mRNAs encoding protamine 1 (Fig. 3B, lane 2) and protamine 2 (Fig. 3B, lane 6) were precipitated by antibody to TB-RBP, but not by preimmune serum (Fig. 3B, lanes 1 and 5). When GTPgammaS was added to the extracts, either no protamine 1 mRNA (Fig. 3B, lane 3) or a low level of protamine 2 mRNA were precipitated (Fig. 3B, lane 7). In contrast, control mRNAs encoding TB-RBP (Fig. 3B, lanes 9-11) and clusterin (Fig. 3B, lanes 13-15) were not precipitated under any conditions. RT-PCR assays of testicular RNA confirmed that the primers detected all four mRNAs (Fig. 3B, lanes 4, 8, 12 and 16). These two experimental approaches establish GTPgammaS has a profound effect on TB-RBP binding to RNA.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
###xml 341 342 333 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f4">4</xref>
###xml 371 372 359 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f4">4</xref>
###xml 554 555 538 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f4">4</xref>
###xml 596 597 580 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f4">4</xref>
###xml 635 636 619 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f4">4</xref>
###xml 770 775 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
 Electrophoretic mobility shift assays using recombinant TB-RBP and Bcl-CL1, a single-stranded DNA oligonucleotide, as probe (28) were carried out to determine what effects guanine nucleotides have on TB-RBP binding to a known DNA target. In contrast to the decreases in RNA binding seen with GTP and GTPgammaS, neither 250 microM GTP (Fig. 4, lane 3) or GTPgammaS (Fig. 4, lane 5) significantly decrease TB-RBP binding to DNA. In fact, we detect a modest, but reproducible, increase for the protein-DNA complex in the presence of GTP or GTPgammaS (Fig. 4, lanes 3 and 5), but not with GDP (Fig. 4, lane 4). As seen for RNA, ATP (Fig. 4, lane 6) or adenyl imidophosphate (data not shown) do not significantly influence the binding of TB-RBP to single-stranded DNA. When mouse testicular extracts are substituted for the recombinant TB-RBP, a similar small increase in DNA binding is seen in the presence of GTP. Thus, under identical assay conditions, we detect, in the presence of GTPgammaS, a approximately10-fold decrease in TB-RBP binding to RNA and a small enhancement of DNA binding.
###end p 25
###begin title 26
TB-RBP binds to GTP
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 222 225 222 225 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 289 292 289 292 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 456 458 456 458 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>

###xml 929 935 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
In order to verify that the effect of guanine nucleotides on TB-RBP is due to a direct interaction between the protein and guanine nucleotide, we studied the binding of GTP to TB-RBP and to a mutated form of TB-RBP, TB-RBPGTP, in which the putative binding site has been altered. In TB-RBPGTP the putative GTP binding site is disrupted by site directed mutagenesis of A161N and G162S. Proteins were dotted onto nitrocellulose membranes and incubated with [32P]GTP. Control TB-RBP binds significant levels of [32P]GTP (Fig. 5A, lane 2), whereas GTP binding was greatly reduced when the putative binding site was changed to VTNSD in TB-RBPGTP (Fig. 5A, lane 4). As controls, 25 microg of a non-GTP binding protein, bovine serum albumin (Fig. 5A, lane 1), and 1 microg of Trax, a TB-RBP binding protein which does not bind to nucleic acids nor has a GTP binding domain, were used (Fig. 5A, lane 3).Only background binding of GTP to bovine serum albumin or Trax was observed (Fig. 5A, lanes 1 and 3). Similar binding to TB-RBP, but not to TB-RBPGTP, was obtained when GTP[gamma35S] was substituted for [32P]GTP, or when the nucleotide and TB-RBP binding assays were performed in solution and then spotted onto membranes (data not shown). Ponceau S staining of the proteins on the membrane after the binding assays confirmed that the proteins remained on the membrane, indicating the difference in bound radiolabeled GTP on the filter reflects true differences in GTP binding to each protein (Fig. 5B).

###begin title 28
The GTP binding site of TB-RBP is required for TB-RBP binding to RNA but not to DNA
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 129 132 129 132 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 315 316 315 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f1">1</xref>
###xml 323 324 323 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f6">6</xref>
###xml 450 451 438 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f6">6</xref>
###xml 517 518 501 502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f2">2</xref>
###xml 570 573 554 557 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 628 629 612 613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f6">6</xref>
###xml 661 664 645 648 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 719 720 703 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f6">6</xref>
###xml 841 843 825 827 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c21">21</xref>
###xml 844 846 828 830 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c22">22</xref>
###xml 847 849 831 833 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
To confirm that GTP was causing the reduction in TB-RBP binding to RNA, we compared nucleic acid binding of TB-RBP and the TB-RBPGTP mutant protein in RNA and DNA gel shifts. Using transcript c as probe in electrophoretic mobility shift assays, control TB-RBP exhibited identical RNA binding as described in Figure 1 (Fig. 6A, lanes 2-4) and GppNHp, another poorly hydrolyzed analog of GTP, decreased TB-RBP-RNA binding by approximately10-fold (Fig. 6A, lane 4), similar to the decrease observed with GTPgammaS (Fig. 2). Following removal of the GTP binding site, TB-RBPGTP does not bind significantly to transcript c RNA (Fig. 6A, lane 5). No binding of TB-RBPGTP to RNA is seen in the presence of GDP or GppNHP (Fig. 6A, lanes 6 and 7). These data indicate that in addition to the two essential basic domains and leucine zipper of TB-RBP (21,22,28), the intact structure of the GTP binding site is necessary for TB-RBP-RNA interactions.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 157 160 157 160 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 180 183 180 183 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 221 222 221 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f6">6</xref>
###xml 301 302 301 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f6">6</xref>
###xml 340 341 340 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f6">6</xref>
###xml 375 376 375 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f6">6</xref>
###xml 462 465 462 465 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 598 601 598 601 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 627 629 627 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c21">21</xref>
###xml 630 632 630 632 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c22">22</xref>
 To establish whether the altered GTP binding site in TB-RBP affects all nucleic acid binding to TB-RBP, DNA gel shifts were performed with TB-RBP and TB-RBPGTP. Recombinant TB-RBPGTP protein bound to the DNA probe (Fig. 6B, lane 5) at a comparable level to that seen with the wild-type protein (Fig. 6B, lane 2). The presence of GDP (Fig. 6B, lanes 3 and 6) or GppNHp (Fig. 6B, lanes 4 and 7) had no significant effect on the binding of control TB-RBP or TB-RBPGTP to DNA, demonstrating that GTP binding to TB-RBP greatly influences RNA but not DNA binding to TB-RBP. The need for TB-RBP or TB-RBPGTP to dimerize to bind DNA (21,22) argues that the selective loss of RNA binding in the mutant protein is not due to inability to dimerize.
###end p 30
###begin title 31
###xml 6 9 6 9 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
TB-RBPGTP homodimerizes and interacts with TB-RBP
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 72 73 72 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c8">8</xref>

###xml 625 626 622 623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f7">7</xref>
###xml 664 665 661 662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f7">7</xref>
###xml 772 773 769 770 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f7">7</xref>
###xml 812 813 809 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f7">7</xref>
###xml 817 819 814 816 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
###xml 863 864 860 861 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f7">7</xref>
###xml 444 449 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Although multimerization is essential for TB-RBP to bind to RNA or DNA (8,21,22), altering the GTP binding site of TB-RBP could indirectly change nucleic acid binding by affecting protein-protein interactions essential for TB-RBP interactions. To directly determine whether TB-RBPGTP can form dimers in cells, we investigated the in vivo interaction of TB-RBPGTP with itself, with wild-type TB-RBP and with Trax (a TB-RBP binding protein) in a yeast two-hybrid assay. Using a filter lift assay for beta-galactosidase activity, we determined that TB-RBPGTP homodimerizes (Fig. 7E), heterodimerizes with wild-type TB-RBP (Fig. 7D) and also interacts with Trax (Fig. 7B). These interactions are identical to previous reports showing that wild-type TB-RBP homodimerizes (Fig. 7A) and heterodimerizes with Trax (Fig. 7B) (28). Trax does not interact with itself (Fig. 7C).
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 139 144 139 144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HIS 3</italic>
###xml 159 161 159 161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c30">30</xref>
###xml 193 196 193 196 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 229 232 229 232 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 439 442 439 442 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
 To provide an estimate of strength of protein-protein interactions, we have used 3-amino 1,2,4 Triazole as a competitive inhibitor of the HIS 3 gene product (30). The TB-RBP homodimers, TB-RBPGTP homodimers and the TB-RBP-TB-RBPGTP heterodimers appear to bind with similar strength, since growth of each of the co-transformants is suppressed by 75 mM, but not by 60 mM 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole. No substantial difference in TB-RBP or TB-RBPGTP binding to Trax is detected by this criterion (data not shown).

###begin title 34
###xml 6 9 6 9 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
TB-RBPGTP expression in NIH 3T3 fibroblasts causes cell death
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 25 28 25 28 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>

###xml 826 827 826 827 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f8">8</xref>
###xml 878 881 878 881 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>

Since we know that TB-RBPGTP can dimerize with TB-RBP or with Trax in eukaryotic cells (Fig. 7) and TB-RBPGTP has altered nucleic acid binding properties (Fig. 6), we set out to determine if this change in TB-RBP binding to nucleic acids could have a physiological effect in cells. To do this, we transiently transfected NIH 3T3 fibroblasts with either a wild-type TB-RBP cDNA (Fig. 8A and D) or TB-RBPGTP cDNA (Fig. 8B, C and E), each cloned in frame to the C-terminus of GFP. Control constructs containing the TB-RBP cDNA alone or the pEGFP C2 vector alone were used as transfection controls. Cells expressing GFP fused to wild-type TB-RBP appeared normal (Fig. 8A), whereas transfection with identical constructs expressing the TB-RBPGTP protein instead of wild-type TB-RBP showed substantial increases in cell death (Fig. 8B). Cell death was seen in cells expressing TB- RBPGTP even at lower levels than the wild-type TB-RBP transfections (Fig. 8, compare A to B) as well as at higher levels (Fig. 8C). Punctate particles rarely seen in control transfections are often seen in the cytoplasm following TB-RBPGTP transfection (Fig. 8, compare A to B).

###begin p 36
###xml 97 100 97 100 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 338 341 326 329 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 431 434 419 422 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 537 538 525 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f8">8</xref>
###xml 580 583 568 571 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
 To quantitate the apparent increase in death of NIH 3T3 cells following transfection with TB-RBPGTP, fluorescent activated cell sorting was performed. Quantitation of dead green fluorescent NIH 3T3 cells 24 h after transfection revealed approximately2-fold (from 12 to 22%) increase in dead cells following their transfection with TB-RBPGTP-EGFP compared to transfection with TB-RBP-EGFP. This increase in cell death in the TB-RBPGTP expressing cells compared to wild-type TB-RBP expressing cells was detected by Apoptag staining (Fig. 8, compare D to E). We conclude that TB-RBPGTP has a dominant negative function in transfected cells, causing cell death.

###begin title 37
DISCUSSION
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 152 154 152 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c31">31</xref>
###xml 587 589 583 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c19">19</xref>
###xml 754 755 746 747 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f3">3</xref>
###xml 991 992 983 984 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f6">6</xref>
###xml 1069 1070 1061 1062 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c8">8</xref>
###xml 1071 1073 1063 1065 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c21">21</xref>
###xml 1083 1085 1075 1077 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c21">21</xref>
###xml 1155 1158 1147 1150 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 1193 1196 1185 1188 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 1252 1253 1244 1245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f7">7</xref>
###xml 1335 1336 1327 1328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f5">5</xref>
###xml 1502 1510 1494 1502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1544 1546 1536 1538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c32">32</xref>
###xml 1645 1647 1637 1639 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c31">31</xref>
###xml 1222 1227 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
We demonstrate here that the DNA- and RNA-binding protein, TB-RBP, contains a functional GTP binding site similar to domains found in small G proteins (31). Although RNA binding is greatly reduced by GTP, or any of its poorly hydrolyzable analogs, the DNA binding of TB-RBP is not decreased. The marked reduction of TB-RBP binding to transcript c in the presence of GTPgammaS is seen with both recombinant TB-RBP and with endogenous TB-RBP in testicular extracts. We have shown earlier that specific mRNAs can be immunoprecipitated with affinity purified antibody to recombinant TB-RBP (19). Addition of GTPgammaS to testicular extracts greatly reduces the amount of the specific TB-RBP-protamine 1 and 2 mRNA complexes that are immunoprecipitated (Fig. 3B). The influence of the GTP binding site on RNA binding is further established by the drastic reduction in RNA binding seen when two amino acids in the putative GTP binding site of TB-RBP are altered by site directed mutagenesis (Fig. 6). Since TB-RBP/Translin must be either a homodimer or oligomer to bind DNA (8,21) or RNA (21), the selective loss in RNA binding is not due to inability of TB-RBPGTP to form homodimers, because TB-RBPGTP interacts with itself in yeast two-hybrid assays (Fig. 7) and the altered TB-RBP gives DNA gel shifts identical to wild-type TB-RBP (Fig. 5). These data mirror those reported for the bacterial protein, Era-G, a widely conserved protein involved in ribosomal maturation. Era-G also forms dimers, binds RNA in vitro and contains a GTP binding site (32). As we see here for TB-RBP, mutations in the GTP binding site of Era-G also perturb RNA binding (31). These data suggest that TB-RBP and Era-G represent eukaryotic and prokaryotic proteins that modulate their RNA binding through their GTP/GDP binding sites, most likely as a result of change in native protein structure caused by nucleotide binding.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 119 121 119 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c29">29</xref>
###xml 333 335 333 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c29">29</xref>
###xml 671 673 671 673 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c24">24</xref>
###xml 692 694 692 694 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c24">24</xref>
###xml 929 931 929 931 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
###xml 1042 1050 1042 1050 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1083 1085 1083 1085 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c24">24</xref>
###xml 1091 1101 1091 1101 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1259 1261 1259 1261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c33">33</xref>
###xml 1297 1307 1297 1307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1429 1431 1429 1431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c33">33</xref>
###xml 544 549 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
###xml 726 731 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1058 1063 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1091 1101 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 1297 1307 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
 The amino acid sequence of the GTP binding site of TB-RBP is similar to that found in many members of the Ras family (29). From sequence analysis and crystallographic studies, it has been shown that a large number of proteins that bind ATP or GTP and are involved in intracellular transport share similar conserved sequence motifs (29). GTPase assays of recombinant TB-RBP have only detected basal levels of enzyme activity, suggesting the need for an activator of guanine nucleotide exchange and a GTPase activating protein (data not shown). Yeast two-hybrid assays and immunoprecipitation studies have been used to identify TB-RBP interacting proteins including Trax (24), the Ter ATPase (24) and a putative homolog of the yeast sec 7 protein. Trax does not bind DNA or RNA as a monomer or homomultimer, but forms heterodimers and/or oligomers with TB-RBP. The heterodimer does not bind to RNA, although it binds to DNA well (28). The Ter ATPase is a ubiquitously expressed protein involved in vesicle transport which binds to TB-RBP both in vitro and in yeast two-hybrid assays (24). In Drosophila, TER 94, an ortholog of the vertebrate Ter ATPase, has been proposed to facilitate the growth of cisternae from the endoplasmic reticulum by vesicle fusion (33). The Ter ATPase is present in the Drosophila fusome, a germ cell-specific organelle composed of membrane proteins and vesicles involved in germ cell differentiation (33). The identity and functions of the sec 7-like protein await further studies.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 94 104 94 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 245 246 245 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c2">2</xref>
###xml 374 375 374 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c3">3</xref>
###xml 437 438 437 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c4">4</xref>
###xml 581 590 581 590 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Notonecta</italic>
###xml 613 614 613 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c5">5</xref>
###xml 620 630 620 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 781 783 781 783 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c34">34</xref>
###xml 914 916 914 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c15">15</xref>
###xml 917 919 917 919 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c19">19</xref>
###xml 94 104 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 620 630 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
 GTP binding may play a functional role in TB-RBP mRNA transport along microtubules. Orbit, a Drosophila gene product, is a microtubule associated protein that has GTP-binding motifs and binds directly to microtubules in a GTP-dependent manner (2). Attachment of mRNA to insect ovarian microtubules is greatly reduced by the application of GTP, ATP or AMP-PNP, but not ADP (3), indicative of kinesin motor protein-mediated interactions (4). This pattern of nucleotide sensitivity is also seen for a Staufen-like RNA-binding protein that attaches to, and is also transported along, Notonecta ovarian microtubules (5). In Drosophila oogenesis, the plus end-directed microtubule motor kinesin I is required for the posterior localization of oskar mRNA and associated Staufen protein (34). Endogeneous and recombinant TB-RBP have been demonstrated to bind to reconstituted microtubules from brain and testis extracts (15,19). Recently, we have co-immunoprecipitated TB-RBP and a member of the kinesin family with antibody to TB-RBP, suggesting there is a motor protein component of a TB-RBP protein complex in testis (data not shown).
###end p 40

###xml 297 300 297 300 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>

###xml 834 835 834 835 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke588f7">7</xref>
###xml 860 863 860 863 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 1116 1119 1116 1119 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">NES</sub>
###xml 1215 1217 1215 1217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c28">28</xref>
###xml 1242 1245 1242 1245 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 1666 1668 1666 1668 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke588c35">35</xref>
###xml 1704 1707 1704 1707 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 1900 1903 1900 1903 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GTP</sub>
###xml 1507 1514 <span type="species:ncbi:1392">anthrax</span>
 The death of NIH 3T3 cells following transfection with TB-RBPGTP is quite remarkable. Previous studies transfecting wild-type TB-RBP into NIH 3T3 cells have detected a normal distribution of TB-RBP in both nuclei and cytoplasm and normal cell viability (28). In contrast, transfection with TB-RBPGTP leads to numerous cytoplasmic punctate structures and to cellular death at modest levels of expression. Co-staining TB-RBPGTP transfected cells with LysoTracker Red DND 99 dye has established that the punctate structures are not lysosomes, but particles containing TB-RBPGTP. Since we do not know the precise functions of endogeneous TB-RBP in NIH 3T3 cells, we can only speculate why clusters of TB-RBPGTP in the cytoplasm may lead to cell death. Based upon the binding of TB-RBPGTP to itself, to wild-type TB-RBP and to Trax (Fig. 7), we propose that TB-RBPGTP could be titrating interacting proteins, thereby preventing them from participating in their normal cellular functions. This appears to be specific to TB-RBP lacking the GTP binding site, since a form of TB-RBP lacking the nuclear export signal, TB-RBPNES, remains in the nucleus, but does not cause cell death in similarly transfected NIH 3T3 cells (28). We propose that TB-RBPGTP in NIH 3T3 cells sequesters TB-RBP interacting proteins such as the Ter ATPase, Trax, GADD34 or yet to be identified proteins preventing normal assembly of endogeneous TB-RBP into functional particles. This dominant negative effect is similar to the mutants of anthrax toxin that co-assemble with wild-type protein and block its ability to translocate enzymic moieties across membranes, thereby inhibiting toxin action (35). Similarly, aggregations of TB-RBPGTP into large granules in transfected NIH 3T3 fibroblasts may indicate failure in the transport of particles containing the mutated TB-RBP protein. A detailed analysis of the components of TB-RBPGTP particles is likely to shed light on how this mutation alters RNA-binding functions of TB-RBP.
