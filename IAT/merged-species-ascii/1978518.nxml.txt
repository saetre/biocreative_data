Signaling of Human Frizzled Receptors to the Mating Pathway in Yeast
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: KS. Analyzed the data: KS. Wrote the paper: KS.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 157 181 157 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 626 630 626 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</italic>
###xml 1229 1233 1225 1229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FAR1</italic>
###xml 151 156 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 157 181 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 335 340 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 406 411 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 457 462 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1049 1054 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1332 1337 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Frizzled receptors have seven membrane-spanning helices and are considered as atypical G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs). The mating response of the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae is mediated by a GPCR signaling system and this model organism has been used extensively in the past to study mammalian GPCR function. We show here that human Frizzled receptors (Fz1 and Fz2) can be properly targeted to the yeast plasma membrane, and that they stimulate the yeast mating pathway in the absence of added Wnt ligands, as evidenced by cell cycle arrest in G1 and reporter gene expression dependent on the mating pathway-activated FUS1 gene. Introducing intracellular portions of Frizzled receptors into the Ste2p backbone resulted in the generation of constitutively active receptor chimeras that retained mating factor responsiveness. Introducing intracellular portions of Ste2p into the Frizzled receptor backbone was found to strongly enhance mating pathway activation as compared to the native Frizzleds, likely by facilitating interaction with the yeast Galpha protein Gpa1p. Furthermore, we show reversibility of the highly penetrant G1-phase arrests exerted by the receptor chimeras by deletion of the mating pathway effector FAR1. Our data demonstrate that Frizzled receptors can functionally replace mating factor receptors in yeast and offer an experimental system to study modulators of Frizzled receptors.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 235 238 235 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Cadigan1">[1]</xref>
###xml 240 243 240 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Clevers1">[2]</xref>
The mammalian Wnt proteins are secreted lipid-modified signaling molecules which control aspects of embryonic development. In the adult, Wnts have a role in diseases such as cancer, osteoporosis, and psychiatric disorders (reviewed in [1], [2]). Wnts function via receptor-mediated signaling pathways including members of the Frizzled (Fz) gene family.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 647 650 638 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Nelson1">[3]</xref>
Frizzled receptors share the basic structural organization of G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) with seven transmembrane-spanning domains, and exhibit a complex N-glycosylated exofacial N-terminal region. With regard to signal transduction, the majority of literature has dealt with the so called canonical signaling pathway which is quite divergent from classical GPCR signaling. In the canonical pathway, the phosphoprotein Dishevelled (Dv1) inhibits glycogen synthase kinase 3-beta (GSK3beta) leading to stabilization and nuclear translocation of the beta-catenin protein and transcription of specific response genes (for a recent review see [3]).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 261 264 258 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Veeman1">[4]</xref>
###xml 266 269 263 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Wang1">[5]</xref>
###xml 419 420 416 417 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 610 611 600 601 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</sub>
###xml 754 757 741 744 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Chen1">[6]</xref>
###xml 873 876 860 863 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Liu1">[7]</xref>
###xml 878 881 865 868 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Liu2">[8]</xref>
###xml 938 948 925 935 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 996 997 979 980 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">o</sub>
###xml 1065 1068 1048 1051 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Katanaev1">[9]</xref>
###xml 733 738 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 851 854 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 938 948 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
Interestingly, there is also evidence that Frizzled receptors can exert functions independently of beta-catenin. These phenomena were called 'noncanonical' Wnt-Fz signaling and common features with classical GPCR signal transduction were described (reviewed in [4], [5]). This includes sensitivity of Wnt-stimulated calcium flux and other signaling events to pertussis toxin, a specific inhibitor of G proteins of the Gi-class. Furthermore, Wnt-11-stimulated Rho GTPase activity was shown to depend on Gbetagamma signaling. Frizzled receptors were also described to couple to the visual G protein transducin (Gt). Dishevelled was found to recruit beta-arrestin, a known modulator of GPCR function, to regulate Wnt5A/Fz4 signaling in human cultured cells [6]. Some reports also implicated heterotrimeric G proteins in canonical signaling stimulated by rat Frizzled-1 (Rfz1) [7], [8]. Strong evidence in this direction was also reported in Drosophila, where genetic experiments revealed that Galphao was required to transduce both canonical and non-canonical signals [9]. However, a direct role of Frizzled receptors as GDP-GTP exchange factors for Galpha proteins has not yet been demonstrated.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 310 314 299 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Dohlman1">[10]</xref>
###xml 409 413 398 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Dohlman2">[11]</xref>
###xml 707 708 692 693 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">a</bold>
###xml 959 965 922 928 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000954-g001">Fig. 1</xref>
###xml 59 64 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 319 324 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Key principles of GPCR signaling have been unraveled using yeast as a model system. This includes the genetic demonstration of signaling through Gbetagamma proteins, as well as the identification of the first member of the regulator-of-G protein-signaling (RGS) class of Galpha GTPase activators, termed Sst2p [10]. In yeast, GPCRs control the mating pathway that is used for sexual reproduction (reviewed in [11]). When haploid cells of mating type a and alpha meet, they extend a projection toward the other ("shmoo"), stop their cell cycle, start transcription of mating genes, and finally fuse to form a diploid cell. The mating process involves specific signaling proteins such as secreted pheromones (a and alpha-factor), GPCR-class pheromone receptors (Ste2p and Ste3p), a heterotrimeric G protein (Galphabetagamma), a Gbetagamma - activated MAP kinase cascade leading to expression of pheromone-responsive genes, and growth arrest via CDK-inhibition (Fig. 1).
###end p 7

###begin p 10
###xml 231 235 228 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-King1">[12]</xref>
###xml 81 86 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 108 113 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 219 224 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The first report demonstrating functional coupling of a heterologous GPCR to the yeast mating pathway, i.e. human beta2-adrenergic receptor, suggested a significant conservation of the GPCR-machineries of mammalian and yeast cells [12].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 233 257 233 257 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dictyostelium discoideum</italic>
###xml 431 435 431 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Eichinger1">[13]</xref>
###xml 440 453 440 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dictyostelium</italic>
###xml 69 74 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 106 111 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 233 257 <span type="species:ncbi:44689">Dictyostelium discoideum</span>
###xml 501 506 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 609 614 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In the present work we aimed to use the particular advantages of the yeast mating pathway system to study human Frizzled receptors. Support for this approach recently came from the whole genome sequence analysis of the social amoeba Dictyostelium discoideum, which identified predicted receptors of the frizzled/smoothened class (family 5) in this organism. Previously, such receptors were thought to be a domain of higher animals [13]. As Dictyostelium represents an evolutionary older organism than yeast, Frizzleds may be considered as archetypic receptors, suggesting that they may be compatible with the yeast GPCR- machinery.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 721 725 713 717 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FAR1</italic>
###xml 725 729 717 721 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Chang1">[14]</xref>
###xml 18 23 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 133 138 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 460 465 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
We show that both human Frizzled1 and Frizzled2, considered as canonical and noncanonical receptors, respectively, can signal to the yeast mating pathway in the absence of added ligand. Overexpression of Frizzled receptors leads to increased accumulation of cells in the G1 phase of the cell cycle, a feature of the activated mating pathway, and activation of a specific mating pathway reporter. We devised specific receptor chimeras between Frizzleds and the yeast alpha-factor receptor Ste2p for pharmacological studies with Wnt-ligands and alpha-factor pheromone, and identify chimeras mediating highly penetrant G1-phase arrests. These effects are partially reversible by deletion of the mating pathway effector gene FAR1[14].
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Results
###end title 13
###begin title 14
###xml 14 19 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 42 47 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Expression of human Frizzled receptors in yeast
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 74 84 74 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 178 182 178 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Goo1">[15]</xref>
###xml 384 391 384 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000954-g002">Fig. 2A</xref>
###xml 397 401 397 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">URA3</italic>
###xml 849 861 849 861 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000954-g002">Fig. 2A, top</xref>
###xml 1476 1480 1472 1476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Bhanot1">[16]</xref>
###xml 74 84 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 116 121 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 229 234 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 244 249 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 759 764 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
###xml 954 959 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1119 1124 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1252 1257 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
A previous study, using a topology-sensitive reporter, suggested that the Drosophila Frizzled receptor expressed in yeast has the structure of a seven-pass transmembrane protein [15]. We first explored the feasibility to express human Fz1 in a yeast wildtype strain by a related approach: We took advantage of the nutritional reporter Ura3p fused to the C-terminus of Fz1 as shown in Fig. 2A. The URA3 gene encodes orotidine-5'-phosphate decarboxylase which functions in the biosynthesis of pyrimidines in the cytosol. Used as a fusion tag it provides information on the fate of the tagged protein. Absence of growth on minimal media lacking uracil indicates insufficient cytosolic Ura3p levels, which may result from improper protein synthesis or transport. Yeast expressing our initial Fz1-Ura3p construct failed to grow in the absence of uracil (Fig. 2A, top), and we reasoned that this might be the result of improper targeting of the protein to the yeast secretory pathway. To circumvent potential problems with the N-terminal sorting information of the mammalian Fz1, we devised a chimeric receptor consisting of human Fz1 with the amino acids N-terminal to the cysteine-rich domain (CRD) replaced with the extracellular N-terminal tail from the yeast alpha-factor receptor Ste2p (termed signal sequence "s" in the following). This design was chosen to preserve the Wnt-ligand binding region that is attributed to the CRD, a characteristic feature of Frizzled receptors [16].
###end p 15

###begin p 19
###xml 232 239 232 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000954-g002">Fig. 2B</xref>
###xml 422 426 422 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Stefan1">[17]</xref>
In order to further clarify the subcellular localization of the s-Fz1 receptor, we fused enhanced green fluorescent protein (eGFP) to the receptor's C-terminus to be able to monitor expression using confocal microscopy. As shown in Fig. 2B, we found a pronounced plasma membrane rim-like GFP signal, as well as apparently perinuclear staining which is similar to the localization described previously for GFP-tagged Ste2p [17].

Growth modulatory effects and activation of a mating pathway reporter system

In growth experiments with wildtype yeast, we consistently found that the overexpression of both s-Fz1 and s-Fz2 receptors in a galactose-inducible system (GAL1 promoter) leads to a weak but significant growth inhibition (Fig. 3A). Mating pathway activation by alpha-factor induced growth arrest occured as expected (Fig. 3A, right panel).

We next set out to confirm the basal activities of s-Fz1/2 observed in the growth modulation assays using a specific mating pathway reporter system. This system employs a firefly luciferase reporter gene under the control of the pheromone-responsive FUS1 promoter (PFUS1-luciferase; Fig. 1). We used this reporter construct in a specifically engineered yeast strain background that carries a gpa1 allele (termed MC18; [19]). The system allows the use of plasmid - encoded heterologous Galpha proteins to replace the yeast Galpha Gpa1p. In the absence of exogenous Gpa1p this strain shows constitutive mating pathway activation, and we call it "ON" strain in the following. Conversely, expression of Gpa1p in the absence of any regulating GPCR fully suppresses the mating pathway ("OFF" strain). Previously, the MC18 system was used successfully to study the coupling of olfactory GPCRs to the mating pathway [20], [21]. For the present study we introduced two additional deletions into the MC18 strain background: We first carried out a STE2 deletion to be able to test pathway activation independently of the endogenous Ste2 receptor (termed MC18 ste2), and secondly we introduced a deletion of the negative pathway regulator SST2 (MC18 ste2 sst2) in order to obtain a potentially more sensitive strain ([10]; shown schematically in Fig. 1).

###xml 706 710 702 706 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GPA1</italic>

We used the PFUS1-luciferase reporter system to determine the mating pathway activation status following overexpression of the Frizzled proteins in absence of added ligand. To this end, reporter gene activity was determined under conditions of glucose-repression and galactose-induction. As shown in Fig. 4, both s-Fz1 and s-Fz2 display significant inducible activity over the OFF-strain. The three to four - fold induction levels observed are comparable to peak-signaling levels for the alpha-factor - activated Ste2-receptor (Fig. 4). Overexpression of the Ste2 or Edg2 receptors alone does not raise the signal significantly, again demonstrating specificity of action of the Frizzled receptors. For the GPA1-deficient ON-strain an approximately 5-fold induction over the OFF-strain is observed, as expected. This is consistent with peak betagamma signaling in absence of any Gpa1p. In conclusion, these data are in line with the observations in the growth modulation assays and demonstrate signaling of the s-Fz1 and s-Fz2 receptors to the yeast mating pathway.

Conversely, by introducing intracellular Ste2 loops into the Frizzled backbone, we aimed to increase the functional coupling of the Frizzled receptors to Gpa1p. The receptor chimeras are shown in Fig. 5, and the nucleotide and amino acid sequences of the fully synthetic genes are shown in the Supporting Information section of this article (Text S1). The PFUS1-luciferase reporter system did not differentiate quantitatively between the native s-Fz1/2 and chimeric receptor constructs as observed in the growth assay (compare Fig. 8A and Fig. 3). Since a major difference between these two experimental readouts is the time following induction of receptor expression (6 hours for the PFUS1-luciferase reporter system vs. up to 7 days for the plate growth assay), we decided to measure PFUS1-luciferase activity after prolonged incubation (24 hours). In better agreement with the long-term growth assays, we observed strong persistent signaling for the chimeric receptors Ste2-Fz1(i) and s-Fz1-Ste2(i) after 24 hours, whereas the signal for the s-Fz1 construct was reduced. (Fig. 8B). The reason for this apparently differential desensitization is not known.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
Growth in liquid culture and budding index
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 557 563 557 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000954-g003">Fig. 3</xref>
###xml 674 681 674 681 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000954-g009">Fig. 9A</xref>

###xml 1262 1269 1226 1233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000954-g009">Fig. 9C</xref>

###xml 2039 2043 1991 1995 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
###xml 2060 2064 2012 2016 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Konopka1">[24]</xref>
###xml 254 259 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Cell cycle arrest following mating pathway activation results in an "unbudded" cellular morphology, and eventually induction of a "shmoo"- morphology prior to diploid formation. Therefore, we were interested to investigate the morphological changes of a yeast wildtype strain following galactose-induction of s-Fz1, Ste2-Fz1(i) or s-Fz1-Ste2(i) receptor expression in liquid culture. For this purpose, we first monitored the relative growth rates of the strains grown in repressing or inducing medium. The result was consistent with the plate growth assay (Fig. 3): In the absence of receptor expression the three strains grew comparably to the empty-vector control strain (Fig. 9A). When switched to inducing medium, following a lag-phase due to adaptation, the strains grew differentially with the s-Fz1 strain showing significant growth retardation, and the strains carrying the chimeric receptors showing almost complete growth arrest. (Fig. 9B). Consistently with a differential ability of the three receptor constructs to induce G1 arrest, we determined significantly lowered budding indices down to approximately0.4 for the s-Fz1 strain, approximately0.3 for Ste2-Fz1(i) and approximately0.2 for s-Fz1-Ste2(i) after 24 hours grown in the inducing medium (Fig. 9C). Moreover, while the s-Fz1 expressing strain was able to recover from the G1 arrest after 24 hours, as indicated by an increased budding index up to approximately0.6, the receptor chimeras appeared to be permanently arrested. This is in agreement with the reduced PFUS1-luciferase activity measured 24h after receptor induction (Fig. 8B). Specific G1 arrests we further confirmed by investigating the nuclear morphologies using staining by 4',6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (data not shown). However, we did not observe "shmoo"-like shaped cells at any time point upon overexpression of any of the three receptor classes. This is similar to previous observations made for constitutively active mutants of Ste2p that showed only weak induction of projections despite high PFUS1-reporter levels [24].

###xml 941 945 933 937 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">far1</italic>
###xml 1145 1149 1137 1141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">far1</italic>
###xml 1290 1294 1282 1286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Chang1">[14]</xref>
###xml 1312 1316 1304 1308 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ste2</italic>
###xml 1359 1367 1347 1355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000954-g010">Fig. 10A</xref>

In order to further exclude growth inhibition by unspecific effects, we aimed to demonstrate genetically that the growth phenotypes conveyed by Frizzled expression are dependent on a functional mating pathway. The FAR1 gene product is required for growth arrest in the G1 phase of the cell cycle following mating pathway activation. Inactivation of the FAR1 gene leads to cell cycle progression despite activated mating pathway through antagonizing the cyclin Cln2p (Fig. 1) [14]. Therefore, we reasoned that the deletion of the FAR1 gene would result in suppression of the growth effects seen for the receptor chimeras between Ste2p and Fz1/2. We created a far1 yeast strain and tested first its responsiveness to alpha-factor in a growth assay compared to its parent wildtype strain, as well as an isogenic ste2 strain (Fig. 10A). The expected growth arrest is observed for the wildtype strain in the presence of alpha-factor, whereas the far1 strain shows the anticipated progression to the cell cycle, and unchanged survival upon prolonged incubation (7 days). The partial suppression of the mating pathway-induced growth arrest seen in the far1 strain after short incubation (2 days) is consistent with the functional redundancy of Cln2p with the two other cyclins functional in yeast [14]. Expectedly, the ste2 strain shows no response to alpha-factor (Fig. 10A).

###begin title 56
Discussion
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 217 230 217 230 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 415 428 415 428 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Dictyostelium</italic>
###xml 428 432 428 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Eichinger1">[13]</xref>
###xml 13 18 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 45 50 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 211 216 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 217 230 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
Signaling of human Frizzled receptors to the yeast mating pathway is particularly interesting from the evolutionary perspective: Our results suggest that the archetypic GPCR signal transduction machinery of the yeast S. cerevisiae may be related to Frizzled signaling in animals. It is therefore also conceivable that the recently identified Frizzled-like receptors in the genome of the evolutionary older organism Dictyostelium[13] function through a similar ancestral mechanism.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 106 109 106 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Veeman1">[4]</xref>
###xml 111 114 111 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Wang1">[5]</xref>
###xml 596 600 596 600 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</sub>
###xml 596 600 596 600 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
###xml 350 355 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 431 436 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 523 528 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Previous work has provided some evidence that Frizzled receptors could act as G protein-coupled receptors [4], [5]. However, commonly used functional tests for GPCRs in mammalian cells (e.g. modulation of adenylyl cyclase and phospholipase C activities) could not be applied successfully to Frizzled receptors. In this work, we have attempted to use yeast as a model organism to study Frizzled function. We show that overexpressed human Frizzled 1 and Frizzled 2 receptors partially induce G1 cell cycle arrest in wildtype yeast cells, a feature of the activated mating pathway, and significant PFUS1-dependent reporter gene induction.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 278 282 274 278 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</sub>
###xml 278 282 274 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
###xml 363 367 359 363 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</sub>
###xml 363 367 359 363 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
###xml 505 511 501 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000954-g006">Fig. 6</xref>
###xml 617 621 613 617 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Gazit1">[25]</xref>
###xml 623 627 619 623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Zilberberg1">[26]</xref>
###xml 633 637 629 633 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Liu3">[22]</xref>
###xml 639 643 635 639 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Slusarski1">[27]</xref>
###xml 645 649 641 645 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Ahumada1">[28]</xref>
###xml 49 54 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 864 869 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Specific receptor chimeras between Fz1/2 and the yeast alpha-factor receptor Ste2p showed that the predicted intracellular portions of Fz1/2 are sufficient to signal to the mating pathway if incorporated into Ste2p, as indicated by a highly penetrant G1 cell cycle arrest, and PFUS1-reporter activity. Moreover, the effect on growth arrest and ability to induce PFUS1-reporter activity seen for Ste2-Fz1(i) and Ste2-Fz2(i) were almost indistinguishable, which is in line with the proposed receptor model (Fig. 6): Despite their different modes of signaling reported in higher eukaryotes (canonical vs. non-canonical; [25], [26], and [22], [27], [28], respectively), the predicted intracellular portions of Fz1 and Fz2 differ by only three amino acids. The responsiveness of these chimeras to alpha-factor further supports their functionality as GPCRs signaling to yeast Gpa1p.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 38 42 38 42 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</sub>
###xml 38 42 38 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
###xml 214 219 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Strong growth arrest paralleled with PFUS1-reporter activity was also observed for the converse receptor chimeras, s-Fz1/2-Ste2(i), which employ the Ste2-intracellular portions, expected to afford good coupling to yeast Gpa1p. Overexpressed native Ste2-receptor does not show any growth effect in the absence of added alpha-factor, demonstrating that the Fz1 and Fz2 transmembrane scaffolds allow the chimeras to adopt a conformationally active state.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 185 189 185 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Martin1">[29]</xref>
Indeed, cooperative action between the transmembrane segments and connecting loops for establishing the three-dimensional structure has been described for several GPCRs including Ste2p [29]: Specific receptor fragments were capable of intragenic allelic complementation in the absence of a covalent connection of the transmembrane segments. These data support a two-stage model of GPCR folding, in which the transmembrane alpha-helices are first formed independently, and then are assembled into tertiary structures.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 130 134 130 134 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</sub>
###xml 130 134 130 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
###xml 769 772 761 764 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Nelson1">[3]</xref>
###xml 997 1001 989 993 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-DeNobel1">[30]</xref>
###xml 1429 1433 1417 1421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Baranski1">[31]</xref>
###xml 113 118 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 775 780 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
###xml 894 899 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 952 957 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1182 1187 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
We were unable to demonstrate Wnt-dependent activation of s-Fz1/2 receptors, or the s-Fz1/2-Ste2(i) chimeras, in yeast using the PFUS1-luciferase system. Different reasons may account for this result. First, the basal activities observed for both receptor classes were close to the maximal response observed for the alpha-factor-activated native Ste2 receptor, hence the response may be already saturated. On the other hand we observed alpha-factor responsiveness of the Ste2-Fz1/2(i) receptor constructs despite similar basal activity, albeit at significantly lower potency as compared to native Ste2p. More importantly, at least for the canonical Frizzled 1, the LRP5/6 co-receptors appear crucial for Wnt ligand binding and signaling in mammalian cells (reviewed in [3]). Yeast does not express known co-receptors of this type, such molecules may have to be supplemented in the heterologous yeast system as well. Additionally, despite the fact that yeast cells can secrete proteins of >200 kDa [30], it is generally believed that proteins up to 5-10 kDa can pass through the cell wall. Consequently, the lipid-modified Wnt-ligands (approximately 40 kDa) may not pass through the yeast cell wall to reach the receptors. A possible solution to this problem may be the use of an autocrine Wnt-Fz activation system, similarly as described for the autocrine activation of the C5a receptor through an alpha-factor prepro/C5a ligand [31]. Another possible solution would be to examine spheroplasts or mutants with increased cell wall permeability.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 77 78 77 78 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">s</sub>
###xml 82 83 82 83 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 89 90 89 90 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">q</sub>
###xml 113 117 113 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Seifert1">[32]</xref>
###xml 325 329 325 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Seifert1">[32]</xref>
###xml 237 242 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Constitutive activity has been described for many wildtype GPCRs coupled to Gs-, Gi and Gq-proteins (reviewed in [32]). It is frequently unmasked by recombinant systems overexpressing receptors, as is the case in the present study using yeast. Constitutive activities of GPCRs are also known to depend on cellular background [32] as cell types may express a different G protein repertoire and different regulatory proteins. To our knowledge, constitutive activity of native Frizzled receptors has not been addressed in the published literature, which is in part due to a lack of convenient pharmacological tools.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 120 124 120 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Overton1">[33]</xref>
###xml 281 285 281 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Kaupmann1">[34]</xref>
###xml 507 511 507 511 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">STE2</italic>
###xml 523 527 523 527 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</sub>
###xml 523 527 523 527 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
###xml 406 411 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Ste2p receptor oligomerization is thought to promote receptor biogenesis, G protein signaling and endocytic trafficking [33], and functionally relevant hetero-oligomerization of GPCRs has been demonstrated in higher organisms, notably for GABA(B)R1 and GABA(B)R2 receptor subunits [34]. While it is formally possible that Ste2 - Fz hetero-oligomerization contributes to the growth effects seen in wildtype yeast cells, we observed autonomous constitutive activity of the Frizzled receptor constructs in our STE2-deficient PFUS1-dependent reporter gene system.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 105 109 105 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Konopka1">[24]</xref>
###xml 111 115 111 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Sommers1">[35]</xref>
###xml 312 316 312 316 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</sub>
###xml 312 316 312 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
###xml 408 412 404 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Konopka1">[24]</xref>
###xml 666 670 662 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Konopka2">[36]</xref>
###xml 672 676 668 672 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Konopka3">[37]</xref>
###xml 3 8 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In yeast, genetic screens were used to isolate mutations that constitutively activate the Ste2p receptor [24], [35]. Interestingly, similar features to the present constitutively active receptors have been described: This includes the observation that the overexpressed Ste2-P258L, S259L -mutant receptor shows PFUS1-reporter levels comparable to the maximal response for alpha-factor-activated native Ste2p [24], despite only weak induction of the expected "shmoo"-morphology. This has been attributed to the potential inability of these constitutively active receptors to stimulate receptor functions in morphogenesis that are not mediated by G protein activation [36], [37].
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 243 247 243 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FAR1</italic>
###xml 341 352 341 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FAR1 - CLN2</italic>
###xml 433 437 433 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Chang1">[14]</xref>
###xml 421 426 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
To demonstrate that the growth arrest effects seen for the constitutively active Ste2 - Fz 1/2 receptor chimeras are a consequence of mating pathway activation we have used a genetic approach: Deletion of the cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor FAR1 partially reversed the growth arrest phenotypes. Partial reversal was expected, as there are FAR1 - CLN2 independent parallel pathways to mediate growth arrest in wild type yeast cells [14].
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 146 150 146 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Dowell1">[38]</xref>
###xml 152 156 152 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Ladds1">[39]</xref>
###xml 211 212 211 212 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">s</sub>
###xml 216 217 216 217 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 223 224 223 224 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">q</sub>
###xml 743 744 727 728 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 840 841 824 825 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">o</sub>
###xml 847 848 831 832 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</sub>
###xml 955 959 939 943 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Gazit1">[25]</xref>
###xml 961 965 945 949 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Zilberberg1">[26]</xref>
###xml 971 975 955 959 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Liu3">[22]</xref>
###xml 977 981 961 965 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Slusarski1">[27]</xref>
###xml 983 987 967 971 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Ahumada1">[28]</xref>
###xml 38 43 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 577 582 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 799 804 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Coupling of Frizzled receptors to the yeast mating pathway parallels earlier observations with several mammalian GPCRs expressed in this organism [38], [39]. Receptors coupling to various mammalian G proteins (Gs-, Gi and Gq) could be functionally expressed, suggesting a considerable degree of evolutionary conservation at the GPCR - Galpha-protein interface. However, attempts to clearly define GPCR - elements of the primary or secondary structure responsible for Galpha-coupling specificity have not been successful. This phenomenon is particularly well illustrated by the yeast Ste2 and Ste3 receptors: Despite low sequence identity (11%), both couple to the same Galpha protein, Gpa1p. Among the four mammalian Galpha - subfamilies the Gi-subfamily shares the closest sequence similarity with yeast Gpa1p. This subfamily encompasses Go and Gt, which have been proposed previously to be involved in Frizzled 1 and Frizzled 2 signaling, respectively ([25], [26], and [22], [27], [28]).
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 41 46 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 102 107 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In summary, our report demonstrates that human Frizzled receptors can signal to the mating pathway in yeast, and this coupling can be made particulary efficient by introducing intracellular sequence elements of the Ste2 mating factor receptor into the Frizzled backbone. This experimental system may prove useful for the identification and characterization of modulators of individual Frizzled receptors.
###end p 68

Mating pathway activation PFUS1-luciferase assays
###end title 97
###begin p 98
###xml 5 9 5 9 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</sub>
###xml 5 9 5 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
###xml 70 74 70 74 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Minic1">[21]</xref>
###xml 210 235 210 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ste2::hphMX4 sst2::kanMX4</italic>
###xml 322 326 322 326 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</sub>
###xml 322 326 322 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
###xml 438 442 438 442 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Crowe1">[19]</xref>
###xml 490 494 490 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Sikorski1">[48]</xref>
###xml 192 197 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The PFUS1-firefly luciferase reporter system was described previously [21], and was used here with the following modifications for two different assay modes. For both modes, the newly devised yeast strain MC18 ste2::hphMX4 sst2::kanMX4 was employed. The strain was sequentially co-transformed with pRHF-luc (carrying the PFUS1-luciferase reporter module), pRGP-Gpa1 (for Gpa1 expression under its native promoter from a low copy plasmid; [19]) or the respective empty parent plasmid pRS413 [48], and the pYES2-based receptor expression constructs as indicated in the results section. Plating was done onto repressive SD-TRP-URA-HIS minimal medium.
###end p 98
###begin p 99
###xml 87 91 87 91 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FUS1</sub>
###xml 87 91 87 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><sub>FUS1</sub></italic>
One assay mode was particularly devised for strain-to-strain comparisons of the basal PFUS1-luciferase levels: Single clones were inoculated into 10 mL SD-TRP-URA-HIS medium and incubated over night. Subsequently, the culture was divided 1:1, cells were pelleted, washed twice with 10 mL sterile water, resuspended in 5 mL SD-TRP-URA-HIS medium, or inducing 5 mL SD+galactose-TRP-URA-HIS medium, and incubated for 6 or 24 hours at 30degreesC (as indicated in the results section). Cells were pelleted again and washed with 10 mL water. The pellet was eventually resuspended in 300 microL phosphate buffer saline (PBS) supplemented with a protease inhibitor cocktail (1 complete miniTM protease inhibitory tablet from Roche per 50 mL PBS). Acid-washed glass beads (0.45 microm, Sigma) corresponding to a volume of approximately200 microL were added, and cells were disrupted using a swinging mill (Qiagen) at maximal amplitude for 5 minutes. The resulting crude protein extract was cleared by centrifugation at 3.000xg for 5 minutes at 4degreesC. 10 microL of the extract were assayed in a total volume of 100 microL PBS (including protease inhibitor cocktail) in duplicates for luciferase activity using a BIOTEK Synergy HT device to inject 100 microL of luciferase assay reagent (Promega) in 96-well format. The luciferase levels were normalized to total protein content as determined using the Biorad protein assay.
###end p 99
###begin p 100
###xml 1519 1521 1479 1481 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 763 768 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The second assay mode was used for the ligand concentration-response experiments, and represents a simplified assay procedure for increased throughput. Single clones were inoculated into 50 mL SD-TRP-URA-HIS medium and incubated to exponential growth phase. Subsequently, cells were pelleted, washed twice with 50 mL sterile water, and resuspended in inducing 50 mL SD+galactose-TRP-URA-HIS, and incubated for 4 hours at 30degreesC to allow for receptor expression. Subsequently, the optical density (OD600nm) of the culture was adjusted to 0.2-0.4 as measured in a BIOTEK Synergy HT plate reader. 500 microL aliquots of the culture were prepared in a 24-well cell culture plate (TPP, Switzerland), and treated with alpha-factor (Sigma), recombinant carrier-free mouse Wnt-3a or Wnt-5a (R&D Systems) as indicated in the results section for 4 hours at 30degreesC. For a removal of the medium, the aliquots were transferred to 2 mL Spin-X(R) centrifuge tube cellulose acetate filters (Corning Costar), and centrifuged at 3.000xg for 1 minute. The cells were resuspended in 500 microL deionized water, and 100 microL aliquots were arrayed in quadruplets in a transparent flat-bottom 96-well plate (Nunc). OD-measurement of the plate was performed optionally to control proper pipetting. From this step on, all subsequent pipetting steps were performed using a multi-channel pipette. The cell wall was digested for 30 minutes at 37degreesC by adding 50 microL of zymolyase 100T (SEIKAGAKU) stock solution in water (40 mg mL-1) supplemented with a protease inhibitor cocktail (1 complete miniTM protease inhibitory tablet from Roche per 50 mL solution). Eventually, 50 microL tissue-culture luciferase-assay lysis buffer (Sigma) was used to disintegrate the cells by repeated up-and-down pipetting. Finally, 50 microL aliquots were assayed for luciferase-activity as described above.
###end p 100
###begin title 101
Western blot analysis
###end title 101
###begin p 102
###xml 23 27 23 27 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000954-Bickle1">[41]</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
###xml 1193 1199 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
Yeast strain MH272-1da [41] transformed with constructs as indicated in the result section was cultivated in 50 mL SD-URA medium to the exponential growth phase. To induce protein expression, the cultures were transferred to galactose-containing full medium (YPGal) for 6 hours. Cells were pelleted, and resuspended in 300 microL lysis buffer (100 mM Tris-HCl pH 8.0, 20% glycerol, 1% Triton X-100, 1 mM dithiothreitol, 1 mM phenylmethylsulphonylfluoride). Cells were disrupted by vortexing at 4degreesC for 10 minutes following addition of acid-washed glass beads (0.45 mm diameter). The crude protein extract was cleared by centrifugation at 3000xg for 5 minutes. The protein concentration was determined using the Biorad protein assay. 20 microg extract were electrophoresed using a 4-12% gradient NuPAGE Bis-Tris gel system (Invitrogen). The proteins were electroblotted to a nitrocellulose membrane. Anti-c-myc detection using a monoclonal antibody from Roche (clone 9E10) was performed either through enhanced chemiluminescence (Biorad Immun-Star HRP substrate kit; and Hyperfilm from Amersham Biosciences) or chromogenic detection (Invitrogen Western Breeze kit). As loading control, a rabbit anti-Kss1p polyclonal antibody (Santa Cruz) was used at 1:200 dilution, and detected using the Invitrogen Western Breeze kit.
