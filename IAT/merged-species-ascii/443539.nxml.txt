###begin article-title 0
Translation initiation with 70S ribosomes: an alternative pathway for leaderless mRNAs
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 96 122 96 122 <email xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Isabella.Moll@univie.ac.at</email>

###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 140 148 140 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 365 373 365 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 647 654 647 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 721 728 721 728 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 747 755 747 755 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
It is generally accepted that translation in bacteria is initiated by 30S ribosomal subunits. In contrast, several lines of rather indirect in vitro evidence suggest that 70S monosomes are capable of initiating translation of leaderless mRNAs, starting with the A of the initiation codon. In this study, we demonstrate the proficiency of dedicated 70S ribosomes in in vitro translation of leaderless mRNAs. In support, we show that a natural leaderless mRNA can be translated with crosslinked 70S wild-type ribosomes. Moreover, we report that leaderless mRNA translation continues under conditions where the prevalence of 70S ribosomes is created in vivo, and where translation of bulk mRNA ceases. These studies provide in vivo as well as direct in vitro evidence for a 70S initiation pathway of a naturally occurring leaderless mRNA, and are discussed in light of their significance for bacterial growth under adverse conditions and their evolutionary implications for translation.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 262 263 262 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c1">1</xref>
###xml 264 265 264 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c3">3</xref>

###xml 1098 1101 1098 1101 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cis</italic>
###xml 1221 1222 1221 1222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c8">8</xref>
###xml 1223 1225 1223 1225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c12">12</xref>
###xml 1636 1638 1636 1638 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c13">13</xref>
###xml 1639 1641 1639 1641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c14">14</xref>
According to the general textbook view, translation initiation in prokaryotes proceeds by binding of 30S ribosomes to mRNA through interactive RNA sequences, the Shine and Dalgarno (SD) sequence on mRNA and the anti-SD sequence located at the 3'end of 16S rRNA (1-3). The conversion of a binary 30S ribosome-mRNA complex into a 30S-mRNA-initiator-tRNA ternary complex, wherein the ribosome is tethered to the mRNA by both the SD-anti-SD interaction and the codon-anti-codon interaction, is further governed by three initiation factors (IF1-IF3) which control the fidelity of this first step in translation initiation, i.e. the selection of the correct start codon (4). In addition to this pathway, there is experimental evidence for a second translation initiation pathway (tip), wherein a 30S-initiator-tRNA complex is recruited to canonical mRNAs (5,6). As recently reported by Mayer et al. (7), IF2 appears to act as a carrier for initiator-tRNA to the ribosome, and would thus augment the formation of the 30S-initiator-tRNA complex. Consistent with the latter tip is the lack of evidence that cis elements on leaderless mRNAs, starting directly with the 5' terminal start codon, contribute to ribosomal recognition (8-12). Taken together with the stimulatory effect of IF2 on leaderless mRNA translation, it has been concluded that the start codon is the only constant and necessary ribosomal recognition element on leaderless mRNAs, and that start codon recognition of leaderless mRNAs requires a ribosome-initiator-tRNA complex, an intermediate equivalent to that obligatorily formed during translation initiation in eukaryotes (13,14).

###xml 1926 1928 1926 1928 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c21">21</xref>
###xml 2231 2238 2231 2238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 2383 2385 2383 2385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c22">22</xref>
###xml 1111 1119 <span type="species:ncbi:12017">phage fr</span>
###xml 1415 1431 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 1643 1649 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 2317 2325 <span type="species:ncbi:12018">phage GA</span>
Regardless of whether the 30S ribosomal subunit binds first either to mRNA or to initiator-tRNA, it is generally accepted that translation initiation in prokaryotes proceeds via an obligatory dissociation of ribosomes and only the 30S subunit is believed to associate with natural occurring mRNAs and initiator-tRNA. While there is ample evidence for transient 30S translation initiation complexes formed at the 5' end of a mRNA (15), it is an open question whether translational re-initiation at start codons internal to a polycistronic mRNAs is brought about by non-dissociated 70S ribosomes. Typically, translation re-initiation events in bacteria occur when a downstream translational start site is in close vicinity to or overlaps with the termination codon of an upstream gene. Although a canonical SD sequence facilitates such re-initiation events, it appears not to be essential (16,17). It is unknown whether re-initiation events occur in the presence of initiation factors, especially IF3, which would restrict ribosomal P-site access to  (3). The translational coupling of the coat and lysis gene of phage fr was shown to be dependent on the non-canonical UUG start codon of the lysis gene (18). This finding could indicate that re-initiation of the lysis gene is accomplished by 70S ribosomes on which IF3 may not exert its proofreading function for initiator-tRNA. In addition, based on studies of the Escherichia coli lac (19) and str (20) operons it has been suggested that 70S ribosomes cross the boundary between translationally coupled genes. More light on potential 70S re-initiation events has been shed through the use of E.coli temperature-sensitive mutants in the gene encoding the ribosome recycling factor (RRF) (21,22). RRF together with EFG is required for disassembly of the translational post-termination complex (23-25). Inactivation of the RRF protein resulted in vitro (26) as well as in vivo (21) in unscheduled translational re-initiation downstream from a termination codon. These experiments suggested that 70S ribosome are not dislodged from the mRNA upon termination, and that they are indeed capable of translation initiation at downstream translational starts. Moreover, inactivation of RRF in vivo does not affect translational coupling at the coat/lysis gene boundary of RNA phage GA, which is again indicative for 70S re-initiation events (22).

###xml 872 873 867 868 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</italic>

###xml 221 233 <span type="species:ncbi:10710">phage lambda</span>
Using the primer extension inhibition assay (toeprinting; 27), Balakin et al. (28) have first shown, that 70S ribosomes, in contrast to 30S ribosomal subunits, show a high preference for the 5' terminal AUG of leaderless phage lambda cI repressor mRNA. Using the same assay, O'Donnell and Janssen (29) reported likewise for several leaderless mRNAs that 70S initiation complexes are intrinsically more stable than 30S initiation complexes. These findings are in agreement with filter binding assays performed in our laboratory (30) and by Udagawa et al. (31), which revealed a 5- to 10-fold higher retention of leaderless mRNAs in the presence of  with 70S ribosomes when compared to 30S subunits. Udagawa et al. (31) have used the cell-free PURE translation system to scrutinize the 70S tip for leaderless mRNAs. Although these authors observed translation of leaderless cI mRNA with 70S ribosomes in the absence of initiation factors, an mRNA containing a canonical ribosome binding site was likewise translated under the same conditions. Hence, the currently available evidence for the 70S tip of leaderless mRNAs is either rather indirect or controversial.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 89 97 89 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 261 268 261 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 365 368 365 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tip</italic>
In this study, we demonstrate the proficiency of stable non-dissociated 70S ribosomes in in vitro translation of leaderless mRNAs. Moreover, we report that leaderless mRNA translation continues under conditions where the prevalence of 70S ribosomes is mimicked in vivo, and where translation of bulk mRNA ceases. These studies provide definite evidence for a third tip in bacteria, wherein translation initiation of leaderless mRNAs is accomplished by 70S monosomes.

###begin title 13
Filter binding assays
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 265 267 265 267 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>
###xml 349 350 349 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f1">1</xref>

###xml 728 729 718 719 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f2">2</xref>
Filter binding assays were performed using a Schleicher and Schuell SRC 072/0 Minifold II Slot Blot apparatus. The binding reactions were performed under the following conditions. 30S and 50S subunits, respectively, or 70S ribosomes were incubated with the 5' end [32P]-labelled SD-oligonucleotide 5'-UAAGGAGGUG-3' at different molar ratios (Figure 1A) in a final volume of 100 mul 1x VD buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.4, 60 mM NH4Cl, 10 mM MgOAc, 6 mM beta-mercaptoethanol) at 37degreesC for 10 min before the mixture was added to the filtration apparatus. The nitrocellulose filter was equilibrated in 1x VD buffer. It should be noted that under these buffer conditions no dissociation of 70S ribosomes was observed (see Figure 2A). After loading of the samples under vacuum the slots were washed with 20 vol of VD buffer. After drying, the filter was exposed to a Molecular Dynamics PhosphorImager screen for quantification of the radioactivity bound to the ribosomes.

The ribosomal profiles of strain MC1061 and LJ14 were basically determined as described by Flessel et al. (36). At an OD600 of 1, a 25 ml sample of the respective culture was withdrawn and chloramphenicol (Cam) was added to a final concentration of 100 mug/ml. Then, the culture was chilled on ice, and the cells were collected by centrifugation at 5000 g for 5 min. The pellet was resuspended in 4.5 ml of buffer 1 (500 mM sucrose, 100 mM Tris pH 8, 100 mM NaCl, 100 muM Cam), and upon addition of EDTA and lysozyme to a final concentration of 10 mM and 100 mug/ml, respectively, the cells were incubated for 10 min on ice. Subsequently, 10 mM MgSO4 was added and centrifugation at 5000 g was performed for 5 min. The pellet was resuspended in 450 mul of lysis buffer (10 mM Tris pH 7.6, 10 mM MgSO4, 50 mM NH4Cl, 1 mM DTT, 50 mug/ml DNase I, 100 mug/ml Cam), and the cells were disrupted by adding 25 mul of 5% Brij58. Cell debris was removed by centrifugation for 10 min at 10 000 g. The supernatant was layered onto a 10-30% sucrose gradient prepared in 10 mM Tris pH 7.6, 10 mM MgSO4, 50 mM NH4Cl and 1 mM DTT.

###begin title 25
70S initiation complex formation occurs exclusively at 5'-terminal start codons
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 8 13 8 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 16 18 16 18 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c28">28</xref>
###xml 140 141 135 136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</italic>
###xml 261 266 256 261 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">68&#8211;70</sub>
###xml 277 278 267 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</italic>
###xml 337 339 327 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c40">40</xref>
###xml 648 649 638 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">r</italic>
###xml 657 658 647 648 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">b</italic>
###xml 665 666 655 656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</italic>
###xml 671 674 661 664 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbs</italic>
###xml 679 690 669 680 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli ompA</italic>
###xml 717 719 707 709 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</italic>
###xml 726 728 716 718 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c41">41</xref>
###xml 679 685 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 703 711 <span type="species:ncbi:10665">phage T4</span>
Balakin et al. (28) have shown that 70S ribosomes form a translation initiation complex at the 5' terminal start codon of leaderless lambda cI mRNA, whereas a 70S initiation complex was not formed at an internal start codon spanning nucleotides 68-70. Since AUG68-70 of lambda cI mRNA does not represent a canonical translational start (40), we have previously tested whether 70S translation initiation complex formation occurs exclusively at 5' terminal AUGs or whether 70S initiation also applies to internal translational starts. Under conditions where 70S ribosomes do not dissociate, they failed to form an initiation complex at the canonical ribosome binding site (rbs) of E.coli ompA mRNA and on phage T4 gene 32 mRNA (41).
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 8 13 8 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 16 18 16 18 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c28">28</xref>
###xml 339 342 339 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbs</italic>
###xml 363 364 363 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f1">1</xref>
###xml 698 699 698 699 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f2">2</xref>
###xml 777 780 777 780 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbs</italic>
Balakin et al. (28) have suggested that the anti-SD is not accessible in 70S ribosomes, and therefore not available for the SD-anti-SD interaction. We therefore first examined whether the inaccessibility of the anti-SD in 70S ribosomes could account for the failure of 70S particles to form a translation initiation complex at an internal rbs. As shown in Figure 1A, the RNA SD-oligonucleotide 5'-UAAGGAGGUG-3' complementary to the anti-SD bound almost equally well to 30S and 70S ribosomes, whereas only negligible binding to the 50S subunit was observed. It should be noted that the 70S ribosomes were stable under these assay conditions and did not to dissociate into their subunits (see Figure 2A). Thus, the lack of translation initiation complex formation at an internal rbs by 70S ribosomes cannot be attributed to the inaccessibility of the anti-SD in these particles. Rather, 70S initiation appeared to require a 5' proximal position of the start codon.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 73 85 73 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ompA&#916;117</italic>
###xml 99 100 95 96 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f1">1</xref>
###xml 173 176 169 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbs</italic>
###xml 180 184 176 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ompA</italic>
###xml 400 402 396 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c13">13</xref>
###xml 403 405 399 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c42">42</xref>
###xml 427 428 423 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f1">1</xref>
###xml 616 619 612 615 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbs</italic>
###xml 628 629 624 625 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f1">1</xref>
To address this question, we studied 70S initiation complex formation on ompADelta117 mRNA (Figure 1B), which contains a 5' terminal AUG codon, and downstream the canonical rbs of ompA mRNA. This model mRNA is suited to analyze conditions which affect either 5' terminal or internal translation initiation complex formation since the ribosomal choice of either AUG start codon is mutually exclusive (13,42). As shown in Figure 1B, lane 2, 70S ribosomes formed a translation initiation complex only at the 5' terminal AUG, whereas 30S subunits generated a ternary complex almost exclusively at the internal canonical rbs (Figure 1B, lane 3). Thus, 70S ribosomes preferentially form a translation initiation complex at 5' terminal starts which might be attributed to topological constraints of the 70S mRNA track rather than to an inaccessibility of their anti-SD.

###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 41 42 41 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</italic>
###xml 44 48 44 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ</italic>

Studying translation initiation by 70S ribosomes in vitro is complicated by the fact that cellular extracts are used, which contain initiation factors. Upon addition of 70S ribosomes to an E.coli S100 translation mix, the majority of the 70S ribosomes most likely dissociates, and then translation initiation commences with 30S subunits (Figure 2D; 43). Given the results shown in Figure 2F-H, we inticipated that the presence of initiation factors in S100 extracts would not lead to a dissociation of the 70S(42degreesC) monosomes. To test this, both the 70S(28degreesC) and 70S(42degreesC) ribosomes were incubated with the S100 extract for 40 min. The subsequent sedimentation profile revealed that a substantial amount of the 70S(28degreesC) ribosomes had dissociated after incubation with the S100 extract (Figure 2D), whereas the mutant 70S(42degreesC) ribosomes were retained quantitatively as 70S tight couples (Figure 2I). To test whether the 70S(42degreesC) ribosomes can accomplish translation initiation of a leaderless mRNA but not that of an mRNA with an internal canonical rbs, in vitro translation extracts were used, which contained either 70S(28degreesC) or 70S(42degreesC) ribosomes. Both extracts were programmed with E.coli ompA mRNA, the leaderless cI mRNA or both, and the in vitro translation reactions were carried out for 40 min at 28degreesC. As mentioned above, 70S ribosomes did not form a translation initiation complex on ompA mRNA in vitro (41). Consistent with this observation, 70S(42degreesC) ribosomes failed to translate ompA mRNA (Figure 2K, lanes 2 and 6). However, these 70S(42degreesC) monosomes translated the leaderless cI mRNA (Figure 2K, lanes 4 and 6). As shown in Figure 2J, the 70S(42degreesC) ribosomes did not dissociate after the translation reaction. Taken together, the selective translation of a leaderless mRNA in vivo in strain RL425 at 42degreesC (38) and the observed in vitro translation of cI mRNA by the 70S(42degreesC) tight couples supported the hypothesis that translation initiation of leaderless mRNAs can be accomplished by non-dissociated 70S ribosomes.

###xml 1349 1351 1331 1333 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1360 1361 1342 1343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f3">3</xref>
###xml 1478 1480 1460 1462 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1555 1556 1537 1538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f3">3</xref>
###xml 1702 1703 1684 1685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f3">3</xref>
###xml 1737 1745 1719 1727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1785 1786 1767 1768 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</italic>
###xml 1848 1852 1830 1834 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ompA</italic>
###xml 1866 1867 1848 1849 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f3">3</xref>
###xml 70 76 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 774 782 <span type="species:ncbi:12016">phage f2</span>
In contrast to leaderless mRNA (38), translation of canonical mRNA in E.coli requires ribosomal protein S1 (34,44). Thus, the lack of translation of the canonical ompA mRNA by 70S(42degreesC) tight couples (Figure 2K) cannot be solely attributed to their particular stability but also to the absence of the ribosomal proteins S1 and S2. Therefore, we sought for a means to verify the data shown in Figure 2K with ribosomes containing the full complement of ribosomal proteins. More than 30 years ago, Slobin (37) has crosslinked the ribosomal subunits with dimethylsuberimidate to test whether a  complex is an obligatory intermediate in protein synthesis. These crosslinked 70S ribosomes were functional in in vitro translation of poly(U), whereas they were incompetent in phage f2 mRNA translation. Therefore, the author concluded, that dissociation of the ribosome into subunits is mandatory for initiation of protein synthesis on natural mRNAs. Given that poly(U) resembles leaderless mRNAs in that it lacks ribosomal recruitment signals and has a 5' terminal start codon, we tested whether 70SCLR are likewise functional in translation of the natural leaderless cI mRNA. Upon crosslinking of the subunits, approximately70% of the ribosomes were found to be covalently joined and did not dissociate into their subunits in the presence of 1 mM Mg2+ (Figure 3A). These 70SCLR were purified by sucrose gradient centrifugation and tested for their stability in the absence of Mg2+ cations as well as in the presence of an S100 extract. As shown in Figure 3B and C, the 70SCLR monosomes remained stable under both conditions. In addition, the 70SCLR contained both ribosomal proteins, S1 and S2 (Figure 3D). When 70SCLR were added to the in vitro translation assay, only the leaderless cI mRNA was translated, whereas the 70SCLR failed to translate ompA mRNA (Figure 3E, lane 2). This result clearly demonstrated the proficiency of non-dissociated 70S ribosomes to translate leaderless mRNA.

Preferential translation of a leaderless mRNA in an E.coli frrts mutant in vivo correlates with the prevalence of 70S ribosomes
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 300 302 300 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c23">23</xref>
###xml 303 305 303 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c24">24</xref>
###xml 306 308 306 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c45">45</xref>
###xml 309 311 309 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c46">46</xref>
###xml 360 370 354 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli frr</italic>
###xml 370 372 364 366 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ts</sup>
###xml 516 518 510 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c21">21</xref>
###xml 360 366 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The ribosome recycling factor in concert with elongation factor G (EF-G) and GTP is known to catalyze the fourth step in protein synthesis, namely the disassembly of the post-termination complex of ribosome, mRNA and tRNA in order to supply free ribosomal subunits for the next cycle of translation (23,24,45,46). Upon inactivation of RRF at 42degreesC in the E.coli frrts mutant LJ14, the ribosome remained on the mRNA after termination of translation and initiated random translation downstream of the stop codon (21). In other experiments it was observed that under these conditions ribosomes are released from the 3'end of mRNA as 70S monosomes (G. Hirokawa and A. Kaji, unpublished work). We therefore reasoned that this would create a situation where wild-type 70S ribosomes are prevalent and would give us the opportunity to examine whether this correlates with a preferential translation of a leaderless mRNA when compared to canonical transcripts.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 48 51 48 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">frr</italic>
###xml 51 53 51 53 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ts</sup>
###xml 318 319 306 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f4">4</xref>

###xml 687 688 675 676 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 700 703 688 691 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">frr</italic>
###xml 703 705 691 693 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ts</sup>

###xml 767 768 755 756 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</italic>

###xml 1013 1019 1001 1007 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>

###xml 1389 1390 1353 1354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</italic>
###xml 1392 1396 1356 1360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ</italic>
###xml 1502 1503 1466 1467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f4">4</xref>
###xml 1782 1783 1740 1741 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f4">4</xref>

###xml 1013 1019 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
To test this idea, the ribosomal profile of the frrts mutant strain LJ14 was first analyzed under permissive and non-permissive conditions. Even at the permissive temperature (28degreesC) strain LJ14 contained higher levels of 70S monosomes than the isogenic wild-type strain MC1061 at either 28 or 42degreesC (Figure 4A and B). Upon shift of strain LJ14 to the non-permissive temperature the peaks corresponding to the 30S and 50S subunits were nearly absent and 70S ribosomes were predominant in the cell (Figure 4C). Next, we assessed whether the translation yield of a leaderless mRNA correlated with the determined high levels of 70S monosomes in strain LJ14. The strains MC1061(frr+) and LJ14 (frrts) were transformed with plasmid pRB381cI harboring a chimeric cI-lacZ gene. The cI-lacZ gene, which starts with the A of the initiating codon of the cI gene (12) was used for the ease of identification of the encoded fusion protein upon separation of total cellular proteins in SDS-polyacrylamide gels. Both E.coli strains were grown at 28degreesC in minimal medium and then either kept at 28degreesC or shifted to 42degreesC for 40 min. The cells were then pulse labelled for 1 min with [35S]-methionine and total proteins of equal amounts of cells were separated on a 12% SDS-polyacrylamide gel. As shown in Figure 4D, even at 28degreesC, the relative translation of the leaderless cI-lacZ gene was approximately 3-fold higher in strain LJ14 when compared to the wild-type strain MC1061 (Figure 4D, lanes 1 and 2). Upon temperature shift of strain LJ14 to 42degreesC translation of bulk mRNA was greatly diminished, whereas translation of the leaderless mRNA was hardly affected when compared to that obtained at the same temperature in the isogenic wild-type strain (Figure 4D, lanes 3 and 4). Thus, conditions that created the prevalence of 70S ribosomes in vivo clearly correlated with a preferential translation of the leaderless mRNA.

###begin title 38
DISCUSSION
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 29 35 29 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 77 79 77 79 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c13">13</xref>
###xml 184 186 184 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c25">25</xref>
###xml 461 463 461 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c14">14</xref>
###xml 620 622 620 622 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c13">13</xref>
###xml 714 716 714 716 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c47">47</xref>
###xml 883 886 883 886 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rbs</italic>
###xml 899 900 899 900 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f1">1</xref>
###xml 903 905 903 905 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c13">13</xref>
###xml 906 908 906 908 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c28">28</xref>
###xml 909 911 909 911 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c29">29</xref>
###xml 969 971 969 971 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c28">28</xref>
###xml 972 974 972 974 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c29">29</xref>
###xml 975 977 975 977 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c40">40</xref>
###xml 1040 1048 1040 1048 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1145 1152 1145 1152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1154 1156 1154 1156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c40">40</xref>
###xml 1157 1159 1157 1159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c48">48</xref>
###xml 1160 1162 1160 1162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c49">49</xref>
###xml 1373 1374 1373 1374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f5">5</xref>
###xml 29 35 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
During logarithmic growth of E.coli, leaderless mRNAs are poorly translated (13). Given that under these physiological conditions 70S ribosomes dissociate after disassembly from mRNA (25), leaderless mRNAs must most likely compete with canonical mRNAs for 30S subunits. For the following reasons leaderless mRNAs are poor substrates for 30S subunits. So far, no ribosomal recruitment signals other than the start codon have been identified on leaderless mRNAs (14). Instrumental for ribosome recruitment to these mRNAs is the interaction of the start codon with the anticodon of initiator tRNA bound to the 30S subunit (13). The stronger tethering of initiator tRNA to 70S ribosomes when compared to 30S subunits (47) can therefore readily explain the intrinsically low stability of 30S ternary complexes on 5' terminal AUGs as well as the preference of 30S subunits for a canonical rbs (see Figure 1B; 13,28,29). A number of studies have shown that IF3 discriminates (28,29,40) against 30S initiation complex formation on leaderless mRNAs in vitro, and that their translational efficiency is negatively correlated with the concentration of IF3 in vivo (40,48,49). These observations cumulated in the hypothesis that translation initiation of leaderless mRNA with 30S subunits can only be accomplished by a 30S-tRNAi-IF2 complex in the absence of IF3 (14,29,49; see Figure 5B).
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 37 44 37 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo</italic>
###xml 139 140 139 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f5">5</xref>
###xml 175 180 175 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 183 185 183 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c28">28</xref>
###xml 460 468 460 468 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 509 510 509 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f2">2</xref>
###xml 530 531 530 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f3">3</xref>
###xml 550 551 550 551 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</italic>
###xml 790 794 790 794 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ompA</italic>
###xml 817 825 817 825 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 912 913 912 913 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f1">1</xref>
###xml 1192 1194 1192 1194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c50">50</xref>
###xml 1195 1197 1195 1197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c51">51</xref>
###xml 1724 1731 1724 1731 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1778 1780 1778 1780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c52">52</xref>
In spite of the prevailing view that de novo translation initiation events in bacteria obligatorily require free 30S ribosomes (see Figure 5A), more than a decade ago Balakin et al. (28) have put forward the idea that translation initiation of leaderless mRNA commences with 70S monosomes. In this study, we have made use of stable S1/S2-deficient 70S ribosomes and of crosslinked 70S ribosomes, and have shown that these ribosomes do not dissociate under the in vitro conditions used for translation (Figure 2I and J, and Figure 3C). Translation of cI mRNA by these 70S ribosomes demonstrated for the first time the proficiency of intact 70S monosomes to initiate translation of a naturally occurring leaderless mRNA. In contrast, the failure of these ribosomes to translate the canonical ompA mRNA corroborated the in vitro toeprinting results in that 70S initiation requires a 5' terminal start codon (Figure 1B). The ability of 70S ribosomes to form initiation complexes at 5' terminal start codons but not at internal initiation sites might be explained by topological constraints of the mRNA track which is situated at the subunit interface. From the crystal structure of the ribosome (50,51) it appears that entry of the mRNA into the template channel of the 70S monomers requires its 5' end to be pulled through the channel between both subunits. Thus, it seems conceivable that a 5' terminal AUG can find its way through the tunnel until it is locked in the ribosomal P-site. The same may be less probable for an internal AUG preceded by RNA sequences that can form secondary structures. Further support for this idea comes from studies showing that the 5' proximal position of the start codon is important for the in vivo translational efficiency of leaderless mRNAs (52).
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 88 90 88 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c37">37</xref>
###xml 340 343 340 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">frr</italic>
###xml 343 345 343 345 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ts</sup>
###xml 422 423 416 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f4">4</xref>
###xml 129 137 <span type="species:ncbi:12016">phage f2</span>
###xml 450 459 <span type="species:ncbi:12022">phage MS2</span>
By viewing poly(U) as a quasi-leaderless mRNA these results can now explain why Slobin (37) observed poly(U) translation but not phage f2 mRNA translation with crosslinked 70S ribosomes, the latter of which comprises only internal ribosome binding sites. Likewise, we have observed poly(U) translation with the S30 extract derived from the frrts strain LJ14 grown at 42degreesC, when 70S ribosomes are predominant (Figure 4C), whereas translation of phage MS2 RNA was hardly discernable (A. Kaji, unpublished work). It seems therefore conceivable that earlier in evolution a stable proto-ribosome-tRNA complex simply initiated translation at the 5' terminal codon of single-stranded RNA polynucleotides, and that the contemporary leaderless mRNAs represent remnants of ancestral RNAs which are nowadays equipped with a canonical start codon.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 30 31 30 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</italic>
###xml 33 37 33 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ</italic>
###xml 113 114 113 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f4">4</xref>
###xml 143 150 143 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 413 415 413 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c53">53</xref>
###xml 416 418 416 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c55">55</xref>
###xml 440 441 440 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh663f5">5</xref>
###xml 458 462 458 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tips</italic>
###xml 720 721 715 716 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">c</italic>
###xml 743 752 738 747 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1721 tetR</italic>
###xml 754 756 749 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c56">56</xref>
###xml 878 890 873 885 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Streptomyces</italic>
###xml 1030 1032 1025 1027 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh663c57">57</xref>
Translation of the leaderless cI-lacZ mRNA was observed in strain LJ14 at the non-permissive temperature (Figure 4D), which provides the first in vivo correlation between preferential translation of a leaderless mRNA and the prevalence of 70S ribosomes. 70S monosomes are known to be prevalent under adverse conditions, like carbon source downshifts, stationary phase and slow growth, and during cold adaptation (53-55). As shown in Figure 5B and C, the two tips with IF3-deficient 30S subunits and with intact 70S ribosomes would ensure a steady translation of leaderless mRNAs under normal physiological conditions as well as at the extremes of physiology. In this way, a constant translation of the leaderless lambda cI as well as of the Tn1721 tetR (56) repressor mRNAs would, for instance, ensure silencing of both accessory genetic elements. Several leaderless mRNAs from Streptomyces species encode proteins that confer resistance to antibiotics, which, although structurally diverse, have the ribosome as a common target (57). Being leaderless could therefore provide a means for translation of these mRNAs to counteract the respective antibiotic under a variety of physiological conditions, including those where 70S ribosomes are prevalent.
