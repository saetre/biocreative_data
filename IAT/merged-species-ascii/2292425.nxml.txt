###begin article-title 0
###xml 28 32 28 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
Association analysis of the IGF1 gene with childhood growth, IGF-1 concentrations and type 1 diabetes
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Aims/hypothesis
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 171 175 171 175 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 338 342 338 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
Insulin-like growth factor-1 is a major childhood growth factor and promotes pancreatic islet cell survival and growth in vitro. We hypothesised that genetic variation in IGF1 might be associated with childhood growth, glucose metabolism and type 1 diabetes risk. We therefore examined the association between common genetic variation in IGF1 and predisposition to type 1 diabetes, childhood growth and metabolism.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Materials and methods
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 12 16 12 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 348 352 348 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 147 155 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 264 269 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">child</span>
###xml 500 508 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 557 565 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Children</span>
Variants in IGF1 were identified by direct resequencing of the exons, exon-intron boundaries and 5' and 3' regions in 32 unrelated type 1 diabetes patients. A tagging subset of these variants was genotyped in a collection of type 1 diabetes families (3,121 parent-child trios). We also genotyped a previously reported CA repeat in the region 5' to IGF1. A subset of seven tag single nucleotide polymorphism (SNPs) that captured variants with minor allele frequency (MAF) >/=0.05 was genotyped in 902 children from the Avon Longitudinal Study of Parents And Children with data on growth, IGF-1 concentrations, insulin secretion and insulin action.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Results
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 33 37 33 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 240 244 238 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 359 363 357 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 210 218 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
Resequencing detected 27 SNPs in IGF1, of which 11 had a MAF > 0.05 and were novel. Variants with MAF >/= 0.10 were captured by a set of four tag-SNPs. These SNPs showed no association with type 1 diabetes. In children, global variation in IGF1 was weakly associated with IGF-1 concentrations, but not with other phenotypes. The CA repeat in the region 5' to IGF1 showed no association with any phenotype.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Conclusions/interpretation
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 28 32 28 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
Common genetic variation in IGF1 alters IGF-1 concentrations but is not associated with growth, glucose metabolism or type 1 diabetes.
###end p 8

###begin title 11
Keywords
###end title 11
###begin title 12
Introduction
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 235 240 235 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CTLA4</italic>
###xml 312 318 312 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PTPN22</italic>
###xml 343 348 343 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IL2RA</italic>
###xml 396 401 396 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IFIH1</italic>
###xml 470 471 470 471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">1</xref>
Type 1 diabetes is a common autoimmune disorder that arises by an interaction between genes and the environment. To date, ten loci have been identified including the HLA class II genes, the cytotoxic T-lymphocyte associated protein 4 (CTLA4) locus, protein tyrosine phosphatase, non-receptor type 22 (lymphoid) (PTPN22), IL-2 receptor, alpha (IL2RA), interferon induced with helicase C domain 1 (IFIH1) and four novel loci identified by a genome-wide association study [1].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 302 303 302 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2</xref>
###xml 420 421 420 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">3</xref>
###xml 221 229 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The protein products of these genes play important roles in antigen presentation and the cellular immune response, highlighting the importance of the immune system in the pathogenesis of type 1 diabetes. However, not all patients with evidence of islet autoimmunity develop complete beta cell failure [2]. Given the evidence to suggest that early weight gain and growth in childhood are associated with type 1 diabetes [3], it is possible that factors influencing insulin action (body weight and fat mass) and islet function may predispose to or influence the presentation of type 1 diabetes.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 85 86 85 86 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">4</xref>
###xml 141 145 141 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 253 254 253 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">5</xref>
###xml 294 298 294 298 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 401 402 401 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>
###xml 417 418 417 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>
###xml 158 162 <span type="species:ncbi:10090|species:ncbi:10095">mice</span>
Lower IGF-1 concentrations predict the development of glucose intolerance in adults [4]. George et al. reported that islet overexpression of IGF1 in diabetic mice enabled islet regeneration and gradual correction of hyperglycaemia and hypoinsulinaemia [5]. A microsatellite in the region 5' to IGF1 has been associated with adult height, cardiovascular risk, osteoporosis and type 2 diabetes in some [6] but not all [7] studies.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 26 30 26 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 201 205 201 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
Since common variation in IGF1 could alter circulating IGF-1 concentrations and therefore body habitus, insulin action and beta cell secretion, we sought to determine the associations, if any, between IGF1 and circulating IGF-1 concentrations, size at birth, both fasting and postprandial insulin concentrations, and type 1 diabetes.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Materials and methods
###end title 17
###begin title 18
Polymorphism identification
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 316 317 316 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">8</xref>
DNA samples from 32 randomly selected type 1 diabetic probands were amplified using specifically designed forward and reverse primers. This provided 88% probability of detecting single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) with minor allele frequencies (MAF) of 0.033, 96% probability for MAF 0.05 and 99.8% for MAF 0.10 [8]. Resequencing included the 3 kb region 5' to the gene, all exons and exon-intron boundaries and 3' untranslated region. Four 1 kb segments in the second intron, ~10 kb intervals apart, were also resequenced.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Tag-SNP selection
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 148 149 148 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</italic>
###xml 149 150 149 150 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 261 262 261 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>
###xml 488 491 484 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="" rid="MOESM6">ESM</xref>
###xml 511 512 507 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</italic>
###xml 512 513 508 509 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 401 409 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Children</span>
The resequencing genotype data were used to select SNP subsets that predicted the genotypes of the remainder, using a coefficient of determination, R2, which measures the ability to predict each known SNP genotype by linear regression on the tag-SNP genotypes [9]. We considered only SNPs with a MAF >/= 0.10 in the type 1 diabetes collection and >/=0.05 in the Avon Longitudinal Study of Parents And Children (ALSPAC) cohort (for details see below and Electronic supplementary material [ESM]), using a minimum R2 of 0.8.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Genotyping
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 108 109 108 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p</italic>
All genotyping data were double-scored to minimise error. All genotypes were in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (p > 0.05).
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Populations studied
###end title 24
###begin p 25
All DNA samples were collected after ethics approval and informed consent had been obtained.
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 0 33 0 33 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Type 1 diabetes family collection</bold>
###xml 197 198 197 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">8</xref>
###xml 134 139 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">child</span>
Type 1 diabetes family collection Type 1 diabetes families were of white European descent, with two parents and at least one affected child. The populations studied have been described previously [8].
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 0 6 0 6 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ALSPAC</bold>
###xml 80 88 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">children</span>
###xml 119 127 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Children</span>
###xml 176 183 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">infants</span>
ALSPAC Details of this birth cohort are available on the ALSPAC website (). The children in this study are from a 10% 'Children in Focus' sub-cohort (1,335 full-term singleton infants) randomly selected from the last 6 months of recruitment for more detailed measurements of growth. Birthweight was noted from hospital records, and length and head circumference were measured after birth. At age 7 years (mean age 7.5 +/- 0.1 years), body weight and height were measured. Body composition was assessed at age 9 years by whole-body dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry. Internal SD scores were calculated for all parameters of growth to adjust for age and sex. IGF-1 concentrations were measured in cord blood samples (birth) and in venous blood (7 or 8 years of age). Fasting insulin sensitivity was assessed by the homeostatic model assessment index () and insulin secretion at 30 min post oral glucose by the insulinogenic index at age 8 years.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
Statistical analysis
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 57 61 57 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 295 296 295 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>
###xml 322 326 322 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 367 369 367 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">10</xref>
###xml 397 401 397 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 488 489 488 489 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</italic>
###xml 489 490 489 490 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 584 588 584 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 719 723 719 723 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 812 813 812 813 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>
###xml 815 816 815 816 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>
All statistical analyses to test the association between IGF1 and type 1 diabetes were performed in either Stata () or R () statistical systems. Additional routines may be downloaded (). Missing tag-SNP genotypes were imputed under the null hypothesis and were analysed using a multilocus test [9]. The microsatellite, 5' IGF1 CA repeat, was analysed using TRANSMIT [10]. The global effect of the IGF1 tag-SNP set on each outcome variable was entered into a multi-locus regression model. R2 change was taken as the contribution to the total variation in each outcome. Associations of IGF1 SNPs with growth and metabolic phenotypes were analysed using univariate ANOVA (general linear models). The association of the 5' IGF1 CA repeat was analysed by comparing the wild-type allele (192 bp) to all other alleles [6, 7].
###end p 29

###begin p 39

###xml 319 321 317 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">10</xref>
###xml 362 363 360 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p</italic>

The selected tag-SNPs were genotyped in 2,439 families (3,121 parent-child trios). The multilocus test of the IGF1 tag-SNPs provided no evidence of an association with type 1 diabetes (chi42 = 4.4; p = 0.356; Table 1). The microsatellite, 5' IGF1 CA repeat, was genotyped in 2,109 families and analysed using TRANSMIT [10]. There was no evidence of association (p = 0.358) (ESM Table 2).

###begin title 40
Wellcome Trust Case Control Consortium data
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 100 101 100 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">1</xref>
###xml 139 143 139 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 418 419 418 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="" rid="MOESM3">3</xref>
We used data from the Wellcome Trust Case Control Consortium (WTCCC) genome-wide association study [1] to test association of the extended IGF1 region with type 1 diabetes. The region contained 22 SNPs. When the WTCCC data were analysed using a logistic regression model adjusted for variation in allele frequencies across Great Britain, no evidence of association of these SNPs within the region was found (ESM Table 3).

###begin title 47
Discussion
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 171 175 171 175 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 302 303 302 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>
###xml 380 384 380 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
We used a UK birth cohort, ALSPAC, and a large family collection of type 1 diabetes to explore association with a subset of SNPs generated by in-depth resequencing of the IGF1 locus. A subset of SNPs that effectively predicted the genotype of the remainder was subsequently genotyped in these cohorts [9]. We report no evidence to support the hypothesis that genetic variation in IGF1 is associated with major susceptibility to type 1 diabetes. However, genetic variation in this locus may modestly influence circulating IGF-1 concentrations, at least at birth and during childhood.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 57 61 57 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 157 161 157 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 250 251 250 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>
###xml 253 254 253 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>
###xml 318 319 318 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>
###xml 330 331 330 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>
###xml 90 95 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Prior studies examining the role of genetic variation in IGF1 in predisposition to common human disease have focused on a microsatellite in the region 5' to IGF1. This variant has been inconsistently associated with adult height and type 2 diabetes [6, 7]. Perplexingly, the same allele has been associated with high [6] and low [7] IGF-1 concentrations. It is possible that other common genetic variants, in different degrees of linkage disequilibrium with this variant, alter IGF-1 concentrations and explain the discrepancy in the published studies.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 242 246 242 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 282 283 282 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">8</xref>
###xml 613 617 613 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 301 312 <span type="species:ncbi:7116">large white</span>
We found no association between this microsatellite and circulating IGF-1 concentrations, growth or type 1 diabetes. To exclude the possibility that other polymorphisms in the gene predispose to disease, we undertook a systematic analysis of IGF1 gene variation using tagging SNPs [8]. Our relatively large white type 1 diabetes family collection of European ancestry provided ~84% power to detect a causal allele for type 1 diabetes predisposition with MAF = 0.1 and OR of 1.2 at the 5% significance level. The study was therefore adequately powered to detect weak predisposition to type 1 diabetes conferred by IGF1. We did not directly examine whether these variants alter the age at diagnosis of type 1 diabetes, since there was no primary evidence of association.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 42 46 42 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 273 277 273 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 402 406 402 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
###xml 493 497 493 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
The possibility remains that variation in IGF1 could alter insulin secretion or action as well as childhood growth. Therefore, to examine these associations, we adopted the same strategy in an established birth cohort. While we demonstrate that common genetic variation in IGF1 modestly influences circulating IGF-1 concentrations at birth and during childhood, we did not find a major contribution of IGF1 to birthweight, growth or response to oral glucose. It remains possible that maternal IGF1 genotypes that influence maternal metabolism might alter birth size, but they are unlikely to significantly impact on childhood growth.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 58 62 58 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IGF1</italic>
In summary, this large systematic study shows that common IGF1 variants modestly influence circulating IGF-1 concentrations at birth and during childhood. However, these variants are not associated with birthweight, childhood growth and insulin secretion or action. Moreover they do not alter type 1 diabetes risk.
###end p 52
