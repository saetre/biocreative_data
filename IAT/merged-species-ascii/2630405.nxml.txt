Disruption of Nrf2 Enhances Upregulation of Nuclear Factor-kappaB Activity, Proinflammatory Cytokines, and Intercellular Adhesion Molecule-1 in the Brain after Traumatic Brain Injury

###xml 1065 1070 1039 1040 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 1076 1080 1046 1047 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>

###xml 469 473 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 987 991 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Inflammatory response plays an important role in the pathogenesis of secondary brain injury after traumatic brain injury (TBI). Nuclear factor erythroid 2-related factor 2 (Nrf2) is a key transcription factor that plays a crucial role in cytoprotection against inflammation. The present study investigated the role of Nrf2 in the cerebral upregulation of NF-kappaB activity, proinflammatory cytokine, and ICAM-1 after TBI. Wild-type Nrf2 (+/+) and Nrf2 (-/-)-deficient mice were subjected to a moderately severe weight-drop impact head injury. Electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs) were performed to analyze the activation of nuclear factor kappa B (NF-kappaB). Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assays were performed to quantify the production of tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha), interleukin-1beta (IL-1beta), and interleukin-6 (IL-6). Immunohistochemistry staining experiments were performed to detect the expression of intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1). Nrf2 (-/-) mice were shown to have more NF-kappaB activation, inflammatory cytokines TNF-alpha, IL-1beta and IL-6 production, and ICAM-1 expression in brain after TBI compared with their wild-type Nrf2 (+/+) counterparts. The results suggest that Nrf2 plays an important protective role in limiting the cerebral upregulation of NF-kappaB activity, proinflammatory cytokine, and ICAM-1 after TBI.

###begin title 4
1. INTRODUCTION
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 132 133 132 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 135 136 135 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 182 187 182 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 272 273 268 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 275 276 271 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 375 380 371 372 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 386 391 378 379 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 407 411 395 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 417 421 402 403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 545 546 527 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 567 572 549 550 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 642 643 620 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 697 702 675 676 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 705 706 679 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
Cerebral inflammation plays an important role in the pathogenesis of secondary brain injury following traumatic brain injury (TBI) [1, 2]. Proinflammatory nuclear factor kappa B (NF-kappaB) signaling pathway has been well documented in previous studies of our laboratory [3, 4]. Increased levels of inflammatory agents with the injured brain, including tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha), interleukin-1beta (IL-1beta), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and intercellular adhesion molecule 1 (ICAM-1), are believed to contribute to the cerebral damage [5]. Their mediator NF-kappaB activation enhances the transcription of proinflammatory cytokines [6], and the cytokines are known to in turn activate NF-kappaB [7]. The positive feedback is believed to serve to amplify inflammatory signals and exacerbate brain injury after TBI.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 234 235 234 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 237 238 237 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 699 701 699 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 702 704 702 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 808 810 808 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
Recent researches have demonstrated that nuclear factor erythroid 2-related factor 2 (Nrf2), a key transcription factor that regulates the cellular antioxidant response, plays a broader role in modulating acute inflammatory response [8, 9]. Under basal conditions, Nrf2 is sequestered in the cytoplasm by the cytosolic regulatory protein Keap1. In conditions of oxidative or xenobiotic stress, Nrf2 translocates from the cytoplasm to the nucleus, and sequentially binds to a promoter sequence called the antioxidant response element (ARE), resulting in a cytoprotective response which is characterized by upregulation of a group of antioxidant enzymes and decreased sensitivity to oxidative damage [10-12]. These antioxidant enzymes have also been shown to protect cells against acute inflammatory response [13].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 191 193 191 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 231 233 231 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 283 285 283 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 333 335 333 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 392 394 392 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 532 534 532 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 650 652 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
Numerous studies have reported that Nrf2 plays a critical role in counteracting inflammation in a variety of experimental models. Nrf2 protects against allergen-mediated airway inflammation [14], cigarette smoke-induced emphysema [15], dextran sulfate sodium (DSS)-mediated colitis [16], inflammation-mediated colonic tumorigenesis [17], and inflammatory responses during skin wound healing [18]. Furthermore, Nrf2 has also been reported as a crucial regulator of the innate immune response and survival during experimental sepsis [19]. In one of our previous studies, we have demonstrated that TBI could induce Nrf2-ARE pathway activation in brain [20].
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 230 235 230 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
Therefore, it may be reasonable to postulate that Nrf2 plays an important role in limiting the cerebral inflammatory response after TBI. In our study, we evaluated the influence of Nrf2 genotype on the cerebral upregulation of NF-kappaB activity, proinflammatory cytokine, and ICAM-1 after TBI.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
2. MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 9
###begin title 10
2.1. Animals
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 446 448 446 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 902 903 902 903 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</italic>
###xml 228 232 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 384 388 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 440 444 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 490 494 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 843 847 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1079 1083 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1259 1263 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Our experiments were conformed to Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals from National Institutes of Health and approved by the Animal Care and Use Committee of Nanjing University. Breeding pairs of Nrf2-deficient ICR mice were kindly provided by Dr. Thomas W. Kensler (Johns Hopkins University, Baltimore, Md, USA). Homozygous wild-type Nrf2 (+/+) and Nrf2 (-/-)-deficient mice were generated from inbred heterozygous Nrf2 (+/-) mice [10]. Genotypes of Nrf2 (+/+) and Nrf2 (-/-) mice were confirmed by PCR amplification of genomic DNA isolated from the blood. PCR amplification was carried out by using three different primers, 5'-TGGACGGGACTATTGAAGGCTG-3' (sense for both genotypes), 5'-CGCCTTTTCAGTAGATGGAGG-3' (antisense for wild-type), and 5'-GCGGATTGACCGTAATGGGATAGG-3' (antisense for LacZ). Age- and weight-matched adult male mice (6-8 weeks, 28-32 g) were separated into four groups (n = 10 per group): group I, sham wild-type (Nrf2 +/+); group II, injured wild-type (Nrf2 +/+); group III, sham-deficient (Nrf2 -/-); group IV, injured-deficient (Nrf2 -/-). The mice of sham and injured groups were subjected to identical anesthetic alone or experimental TBI, respectively. Animals were decapitated at 24 hours following sham or injury. Five mice in each group were sacrificed for electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) and enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) analysis and the others were for immunohistochemistry study.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
2.2. Induction of experimental TBI
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 50 52 50 52 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 86 88 86 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 95 99 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 721 725 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The mouse model of TBI was employed as described [21] with recent minor modification [22]. The mice were anesthetized by intraperitoneal injection with sodium pentobarbital (50 mg/kg). A round, flat, and 6 mm diameter Teflon impounder was centered between the ears and eyes. TBI was induced by a 100 g weight dropped from a 12 cm height along a stainless steel string, which translated into 1200 g/cm. Brain injury-induced apnea was then treated for 3 minutes with 100% oxygen administration and chest compression to stimulate the respiration. This model is generally associated with 20% of mortality within the first 5 minutes postinjury and no delayed mortality was observed thereafter. After operation procedures, the mice were returned to their cages. Heart rate, arterial blood pressure, and rectal temperature were monitored, and the rectal temperature was kept at 37 +/- 0.5degreesC (physical cooling if required) throughout experiments.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 236 244 236 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 42 46 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 115 119 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 316 320 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
At the 24 hours following sham or injury, mice were sacrificed for sample collection. For EMSA and ELISA analyses, mice were exsanguinated by cardiac puncture. Cortex tissue was rapidly taken from the fresh brain at the site of lesion (Figure 1), and stored in liquid nitrogen immediately. For immunohistochemistry, mice were perfused with cold saline (4degreesC), followed by 4% neutral-buffered formalin. The cortex tissue was taken, stored overnight in 4% neutral-buffered formalin, and then embedded in paraffin.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
2.3. Nuclear protein extract and EMSA
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 59 61 59 61 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 203 204 203 204 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 459 461 459 460 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 607 609 600 601 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 1055 1061 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Nuclear protein was extracted and quantified as described [23]. Briefly, frozen brain samples were homogenized in 0.8 mL ice-cold buffer A composed of 10 mmol/L HEPES pH 7.9, 10 mmol/L KCl, 2 mmol/L MgCl2, 0.1 mmol/L EDTA, 1 mmol/L dithiothreitol (DTT), and 0.5 mmol/L phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF) (all from Sigma Chemical Co., St. Louis, Mo, USA). The homogenates were incubated on ice for 30 minutes and vortexed for 30 seconds after addition of 50 muL 10% NP-40 (Sigma Chemical Co., Mo, USA). The mixture was then centrifuged for 10 minutes (5000 x g, 4degreesC). The pellet was suspended in 100 muL ice-cold buffer B composed of 50 mmol/L HEPES pH 7.9, 50 mmol/L KCl, 300 mM NaCl, 0.1 mmol/L EDTA, 1 mmol/LDTT, and 0.5 mmol/L PMSF, and 10% (v/v) glycerol and incubated on ice 30 minutes with frequent mixing. After centrifugation (12000 x g, 4degreesC) for 15 minutes, the supernatants were collected as nuclear extracts and stored at -70degreesC for further use. Protein concentration was determined using a bicinchoninic acid assay kit with bovine serum albumin as the standard (Pierce Biochemicals, Rockford, Ill, USA).
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 136 138 136 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 227 232 227 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#947;</italic>
###xml 233 235 229 231 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">32</sup>
###xml 324 326 320 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 369 371 364 365 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 463 464 457 458 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 632 634 626 627 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 636 638 629 631 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">32</sup>
###xml 768 770 761 762 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 1115 1120 1101 1102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
EMSA was performed using a commercial kit (Gel Shift Assay System; Promega, Madison, Wis, USA) following the methods in our laboratory [23]. Consensus oligonucleotide probe (5'-AGTTGA GGGGACTTTCCCAGGC-3') was end-labeled with [gamma-32P]ATP (Free Biotech., Beijing, China) with T4-polynucleotide kinase. Nuclear protein (10 mug) was preincubated in a total volume of 9 muL in a binding buffer, consisting of 10 mmol/L Tris-HCl (pH 7.5), 4% glycerol, 1 mmol/L MgCl2, 0.5 mmol/L M EDTA, 0.5 mmol/L DTT, 0.5 mmol/L NaCl, and 0.05 g/L poly-(deoxyinosinic-deoxycytidylic acid) for 15 minutes at room temperature. After addition of the 1 muL 32P-labled oligonucleotide probe, the incubation was continued for 20 minutes at room temperature. Reaction was stopped by adding 1 muL of gel loading buffer and the mixture was subjected to nondenaturing 4% polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis in 0.5 x TBE buffer (Tris-borate-EDTA). After electrophoresis was conducted at 390 V for 1 hour, the gel was vacuum-dried and exposed to X-ray film (Fuji Hyperfilm, Tokyo, Japan) at -70degreesC with an intensifying screed. Levels of NF-kappaB DNA binding activity were quantified by computer-assisted densitometric analysis.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
2.4. ELISA analysis
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 531 536 525 526 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 573 577 563 564 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 657 659 644 646 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 475 480 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Frozen brain samples were homogenized in 1 mL of buffer containing 1 mmol/L of PMSF, 1 mg/L of pepstatin A, 1 mg/L of aprotinin, and 1 mg/L of leupeptin in PBS solution (pH 7.2) with a glass homogenizer and then centrifuged at 12000 g for 20 minutes at 4degreesC. The supernatant was then collected and total protein was determined by the Bradford method. The levels of inflammatory cytokines were quantified using enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) kits specific for mouse according to the manufacturers' instructions (TNF-alpha from Diaclone Research, France; IL-1beta, IL-6 from Biosource Europe SA, Belgium) and previous study of our laboratory [24]. The cytokine contents in the brain samples were expressed as pg per milligram protein.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
2.5. Immunohistochemical staining
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 34 36 34 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#956;</italic>
###xml 240 241 239 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 372 373 365 366 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 374 375 367 368 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 107 111 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
The paraffin-embedded sections (4 mum) were used for Immunohistochemical assay, which was performed with a goat antimouse ICAM-1(CD54) antibody (diluted 1:200, R&D Systems, Inc., Minn, USA), according to previous studies of our laboratory [7]. The sections were incubated with the diluted antibody overnight at 4degreesC in a humid chamber, washed, and blocked with 1.6% H2O2 in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) for 10 minutes. After washing with PBS again, sections were then incubated with biotinylated second antibodies for 1 hour at room temperature. Diaminobenzidine (DAB) was used as chromogen and counterstaining was done with hematoxylin. The number of positive microvessels in each section was counted in 10 microscopic fields (at 100 x magnifications) and averaged for the positively immunostained vessel number of per visual field.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
2.6. Statistical analysis
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 109 110 107 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 280 281 278 279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">P</italic>
Software SPSS 13.0 was used for the statistical analysis. All data were expressed as mean +/- SEM, Student's t-test was used to analyze the differences between the sham and TBI groups within a single genotype as well as between genotypes. Statistical significance was accepted at P < .05.

###begin title 24
3. RESULTS
###end title 24
###begin title 25
###xml 17 22 17 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
3.1. EMSA for NF-kappaB
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 3 8 3 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 79 87 75 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 96 101 92 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 231 236 223 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 375 380 363 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 178 182 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 286 290 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 303 307 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
NF-kappaB activation in the nuclear extracts was assessed by EMSA. As shown in Figure 2, low NF-kappaB banding activity (weak EMSA autoradiography) was observed in sham-operated mice of both genotypes. TBI induced activation of NF-kappaB in the cortex of both Nrf2 (+/+) and Nrf2 (-/-) mice. Nrf2 (-/-) mice showed an increased susceptibility to TBI-induced activation of NF-kappaB than their wild-type Nrf2 (+/+) counterparts.

###begin title 31
4. DISCUSSION
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 101 106 101 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 112 116 108 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 179 184 172 173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 487 492 476 477 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 60 64 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The most important finding of this study is that Nrf2 (-/-) mice had more inflammatory cytokines TNF-alpha, IL-1beta and IL-6 production, ICAM-1 expression, and their mediator NF-kappaB activation in brain after TBI compared with their wild-type Nrf2 (+/+) counterparts. These findings reported here suggest for the first time that Nrf2 may play an important role in limiting the cerebral inflammatory response after TBI through modulating the proinflammatory nuclear factor kappa B (NF-kappaB) signaling pathway.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 17 22 17 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 144 145 140 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 147 148 143 144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 154 159 150 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 250 252 242 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 287 292 279 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 406 411 394 395 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 417 421 401 402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 441 442 422 423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 449 454 430 431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 561 563 538 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 570 574 547 548 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 693 695 667 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 726 731 700 701 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 887 889 857 859 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 1078 1080 1048 1050 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 1196 1201 1166 1167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 1322 1327 1288 1289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 1330 1331 1292 1293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1566 1571 1528 1529 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 1690 1695 1648 1649 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 1860 1865 1814 1815 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 2014 2019 1964 1965 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 2073 2075 2019 2021 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 1651 1655 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1986 1990 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Activation of NF-kappaB signaling pathway has been shown to be central to the pathophysiology of cerebral inflammatory response induced by TBI [6, 7]. NF-kappaB can be activated by lesion-induced oxidative stress, bacterial endotoxin, and cytokines [25]. The functional importance of NF-kappaB in inflammation is based on its ability to regulate the promoters of multiple inflammatory genes, including TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, IL-6, and ICAM-1 [4]. TNF-alpha is reported to be a major initiator of inflammation and is released early after an inflammatory stimulus [26]. IL-1beta is regarded as the prototypic "multifunctional" cytokine and is induced in a multitude of consequences of cell types [27]. IL-6 is increased after TNF-alpha and is considered to be an important proinflammatory cytokine in contribution to both morbidity and mortality in condition of "uncontrolled" inflammation [28]. ICAM-1, a member of the immunoglobulin superfamily which can be profound induced after cytokine challenge, is important in the recruitment of leukocytes during the inflammatory process [29]. This inflammatory agent network is believed to be important in the generation of acute inflammatory response. NF-kappaB activation enhances the transcription of proinflammatory cytokines, and the cytokines are known to in turn activate NF-kappaB [5]. The positive feedback is believed to serve to amplify inflammatory signals and exacerbate brain injury after TBI. In the present study, we evaluated the influence of Nrf2 genotype in the TBI-induced activation of proinflammatory NF-kappaB signaling pathway in the brain. The results showed that disruption of Nrf2 in mice caused a greater activation of NF-kappaB signaling pathway which played a critical role in the pathophysiology of cerebral inflammatory response induced by TBI. The observed interplay between Nrf2 and NF-kappaB signaling corresponds well to the results of study on experimental sepsis, which have demonstrated that Nrf2-deficient mice displayed increased NF-kappaB activation in response to lipopolysaccharide (LPS) [19].
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 151 153 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 154 156 154 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 428 433 428 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 627 629 623 625 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 653 658 649 650 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 776 778 768 770 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 990 995 982 983 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 1045 1046 1033 1034 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 1048 1050 1036 1038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 1051 1053 1039 1041 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 1411 1416 1399 1400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 1769 1774 1753 1754 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 1540 1544 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1564 1568 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Although numerous in vivo studies have reported that Nrf2 plays a critical role in counteracting the inflammation in a variety of experimental models [14-19], the findings which we have confirmed and extended in the model of TBI in the present study, the precise mechanism underlying this network is still unclear. Several lines of evidence suggest that Nrf2 regulates the inflammatory response by inhibiting proinflammatory NF-kappaB activation through maintenance of redox homeostasis. Oxidative stress from reactive oxygen species (ROS) is believed to be involved in the progression of secondary brain injury following TBI [30]. Activation of the NF-kappaB signaling pathway has been shown to be responsive to excess ROS and is important in the generation of inflammation [19]. Nrf2, as a key antioxidant transcription factor involved in the intracellular antioxidant defense systems, has been shown to play an important role in limiting ROS levels and thereby affect redox-sensitive NF-kappaB signaling pathway involved in the inflammation [8, 10-12]. The protective function of Nrf2 is mainly mediated by a group of Nrf2-regulated antioxidant and detoxifying enzymes. Therefore, the augmentation of cellular antioxidative or detoxification systems via activation of Nrf2-regulated enzymes resulting in decreased proinflammatory cytokines production and adhesion molecules expression via inactivation of NF-kappaB represents a possible anti-inflammatory mechanism for the attenuated inflammatory response seen in brains from Nrf2 (+/+) mice but not Nrf2 (-/-) mice after TBI. We then in this study postulated that Nrf2 regulates the TBI-induced cerebral inflammatory response may at least in part through modulating the cerebral redox status and proinflammatory NF-kappaB signaling pathway. Additional work is necessary to elucidate the whole mechanisms involved in these complicated networks.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 219 224 219 220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 264 269 260 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;</italic>
###xml 275 279 267 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</italic>
###xml 506 511 495 496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#954;</italic>
###xml 135 139 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 166 170 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In summary, this present study showed that Nrf2 plays a protective role in TBI-induced cerebral upregulation of inflammatory agents in mice. We found that Nrf2 (-/-) mice are more susceptible to TBI-induced cerebral NF-kappaB activation, inflammatory cytokine TNF-alpha, IL-1beta and IL-6 production, and ICAM-1 expression, which then contributed to exacerbated brain injury after TBI. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study that elucidates the interplay between Nrf2 and proinflammatory NF-kappaB signaling pathway in the brain following TBI. These findings raise the possibility that Nrf2 will be a new therapeutic target for the treatment TBI.

Concentrations of inflammatory cytokines in the cortex of sham and injured Nrf2 (+/+) and Nrf2 (-/-) mice. Concentrations of (a) TNF-alpha, (b) IL-1beta, and (c) IL-6 were determined by ELISA in the brain samples of Nrf2 (+/+) and Nrf2 (-/-) mice 24 hours after TBI. The figure indicates that concentrations of TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, and IL-6 in brain were significantly increased after TBI and were greater in Nrf2 (-/-) mice than in Nrf2 (+/+) mice. Data represents mean +/- SEM (n = 5 per group). **P < .01 versus sham control of the same genotype. #P < .05 and ##P < .01 versus injured wild-type mice.

Expression of ICAM-1 in the cortex of sham and injured Nrf2 (+/+) and Nrf2 (-/-) mice. Immunohistochemical staining for ICAM-1 was performed in the cortex tissue sections of Nrf2 (+/+) and Nrf2 (-/-) mice 24 hours after TBI. (a), (c) Sham-operated Nrf2 (+/+) and Nrf2 (-/-) mice showing few ICAM-1-immunostained cerebral microvessels. (b) Injured Nrf2 (+/+) mice showing increased number of ICAM-1 positive vessels. (d) Injured Nrf2 (-/-) mice showing larger increment in the number of ICAM-1 positive vessels compared with injured Nrf2 (+/+) mice. (e) Quantitative analysis showed that the number of ICAM-1 positive vessels in brain was significantly increased after TBI and was greater in Nrf2 (-/-) mice than in Nrf2 (+/+) mice.
