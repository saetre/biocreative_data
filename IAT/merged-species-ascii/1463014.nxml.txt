###xml 26 47 <span type="species:ncbi:813">Chlamydia trachomatis</span>
Recruitment of BAD by the Chlamydia trachomatis Vacuole Correlates with Host-Cell Survival
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 344 366 341 363 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia trachomatis-</italic>
###xml 453 468 447 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. trachomatis-</italic>
###xml 698 712 692 706 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 823 824 817 818 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 873 893 864 884 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">Chlamydia pneumoniae</named-content>
###xml 976 989 967 980 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. pneumoniae</named-content>

###xml 1704 1719 1692 1707 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. trachomatis-</italic>
###xml 344 365 <span type="species:ncbi:813">Chlamydia trachomatis</span>
###xml 453 467 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 698 712 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 873 893 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">Chlamydia pneumoniae</span>
###xml 976 989 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">C. pneumoniae</span>
###xml 1009 1023 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 1373 1387 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 1486 1505 <span type="species:ncbi:83560">Chlamydia muridarum</span>
###xml 1607 1621 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 1704 1718 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
Chlamydiae replicate intracellularly in a vacuole called an inclusion. Chlamydial-infected host cells are protected from mitochondrion-dependent apoptosis, partly due to degradation of BH3-only proteins. The host-cell adapter protein 14-3-3beta can interact with host-cell apoptotic signaling pathways in a phosphorylation-dependent manner. In Chlamydia trachomatis-infected cells, 14-3-3beta co-localizes to the inclusion via direct interaction with a C. trachomatis-encoded inclusion membrane protein. We therefore explored the possibility that the phosphatidylinositol-3 kinase (PI3K) pathway may contribute to resistance of infected cells to apoptosis. We found that inhibition of PI3K renders C. trachomatis-infected cells sensitive to staurosporine-induced apoptosis, which is accompanied by mitochondrial cytochrome c release. 14-3-3beta does not associate with the Chlamydia pneumoniae inclusion, and inhibition of PI3K does not affect protection against apoptosis of C. pneumoniae-infected cells. In C. trachomatis-infected cells, the PI3K pathway activates AKT/protein kinase B, which leads to maintenance of the pro-apoptotic protein BAD in a phosphorylated state. Phosphorylated BAD is sequestered via 14-3-3beta to the inclusion, but it is released when PI3K is inhibited. Depletion of AKT through short-interfering RNA reverses the resistance to apoptosis of C. trachomatis-infected cells. BAD phosphorylation is not maintained and it is not recruited to the inclusion of Chlamydia muridarum, which protects poorly against apoptosis. Thus, sequestration of BAD away from mitochondria provides C. trachomatis with a mechanism to protect the host cell from apoptosis via the interaction of a C. trachomatis-encoded inclusion protein with a host-cell phosphoserine-binding protein.

###begin title 2
Synopsis
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 0 21 0 21 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">Chlamydia trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 518 528 518 528 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia,</italic>
###xml 988 998 985 995 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia.</italic>
###xml 0 21 <span type="species:ncbi:813">Chlamydia trachomatis</span>
###xml 95 101 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Chlamydia trachomatis is the most common cause of sexually transmitted bacterial infections in humans. These bacteria survive and replicate within a vacuole in the infected cell called an inclusion, producing up to a thousand bacteria per inclusion within a day of infection. Despite the large size of the inclusion, the infected cell survives long enough for the pathogens to complete their infection cycle and then infect new host cells. The researchers describe a novel mechanism for protection of the host cell by Chlamydia, namely activation of enzymes involved in host-cell survival. These enzymes, called kinases, cause the phosphorylation and inactivation of a protein, BAD, which can promote "cell suicide" (apoptosis) of uninfected cells. BAD phosphorylation is accompanied by recruitment of BAD to the chlamydial inclusion, where BAD binds to a cellular adapter protein, 14-3-3beta. The adapter protein, in turn, is attracted to the inclusion by a membrane protein produced by Chlamydia. Thus, the chlamydial inclusion sequesters BAD away from mitochondria, where BAD could induce host-cell apoptosis. The cross talk between chlamydiae and host-cell signaling pathways demonstrates a novel mechanism of pathogen modulation of host-cell biology, and represents a potential therapeutic target for blocking infection by this prevalent pathogen.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Introduction
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 0 21 0 21 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">Chlamydia trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 108 122 108 122 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 182 202 182 202 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">Chlamydia pneumoniae</named-content>
###xml 266 267 266 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b001">1</xref>
###xml 268 269 268 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b006">6</xref>
###xml 0 21 <span type="species:ncbi:813">Chlamydia trachomatis</span>
###xml 108 122 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 182 202 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">Chlamydia pneumoniae</span>
###xml 223 229 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Chlamydia trachomatis is the leading cause of bacterial sexually transmitted diseases worldwide; strains of C. trachomatis responsible for ocular infections lead to blindness, while Chlamydia pneumoniae causes pneumonia in humans and may accelerate atherosclerosis [1-6].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 4 13 4 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 91 92 91 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b007">7</xref>
###xml 391 392 391 392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b008">8</xref>
###xml 490 491 490 491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b009">9</xref>
All Chlamydia species are intracellular pathogens that primarily infect epithelial mucosa [7]. Chlamydiae spend most of their developmental cycle within a membrane-bound vacuole, or inclusion, which avoids fusion with host-cell lysosomes. Despite their intravacuolar localization, the bacteria interact with the host cell to ensure that their inclusion remains a safe haven for replication [8], but at the same time allows for acquisition of essential nutrients from the host-cell cytosol [9].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 250 259 250 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 447 449 447 449 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b010">10</xref>
###xml 450 452 450 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b011">11</xref>
###xml 525 526 525 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b009">9</xref>
###xml 527 529 527 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b012">12</xref>
###xml 658 660 658 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b013">13</xref>
###xml 661 663 661 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b016">16</xref>
Many membrane-trafficking and metabolic changes in the host cell are thought to be controlled by chlamydial proteins that are inserted into the inclusion membrane or secreted into the host cytosol. Similar to other Gram-negative bacterial pathogens, Chlamydia species possess a specialized type III secretion apparatus that behaves as a molecular syringe, enabling the bacteria to inject virulence factors from the bacteria into the host cytosol [10,11]. The metabolic demands made on the host cell by the growing inclusion [9,12] may activate a number of stress-related signaling pathways, including mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAP kinases or ERKs) [13-16]. Significantly, many stress-responsive pathways are involved in adaptation to environmental changes and affect apoptosis.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b017">17</xref>
###xml 208 210 208 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b018">18</xref>
###xml 211 213 211 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b019">19</xref>
###xml 342 344 342 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b020">20</xref>
###xml 504 506 504 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b021">21</xref>
Apoptosis is required for the development of metazoans and is crucial for the maintenance of cellular homeostasis in mammals [17]. Microbial pathogens can inhibit or stimulate apoptosis of the infected cell [18,19]. In some cases, infection can result in both induction and inhibition of apoptosis at different stages of the infection cycle [20]. Likewise, it has been proposed that chlamydiae may induce cell death at the end of the infection cycle, but inhibit apoptosis at the beginning of the cycle [21].
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 133 147 133 147 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 151 164 151 164 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. pneumoniae</named-content>
###xml 252 254 252 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b022">22</xref>
###xml 255 257 255 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b025">25</xref>
###xml 348 349 348 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 537 546 537 546 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 693 695 693 695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b026">26</xref>
###xml 873 875 873 875 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b027">27</xref>
###xml 876 878 876 878 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b029">29</xref>
###xml 1019 1020 1019 1020 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 1030 1032 1030 1032 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b030">30</xref>
###xml 133 147 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 151 164 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">C. pneumoniae</span>
The first reports of an anti-apoptotic effect of chlamydial infection showed that infection of epithelial cells and macrophages with C. trachomatis or C. pneumoniae protects against externally applied insults that induce apoptosis in uninfected cells [22-25]. These studies demonstrated that chlamydial infection prevents the release of cytochrome c from mitochondria and subsequent activation of caspases, suggesting that the apoptotic pathway may be blocked upstream from mitochondria. This view was reinforced by the observation that Chlamydia-infected cells are resistant to apoptotic pathways that rely on a mitochondrial amplification step, but not when caspases are activated directly [26]. More recently, it was shown that part of the resistance to apoptosis is due to degradation in infected cells of pro-apoptotic BH3-only proteins, including BIM, PUMA, and BAD [27-29]. These mediators lie upstream of mitochondria, where they activate BAX and BAK, which oligomerize on mitochondria and stimulate cytochrome c release [30].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 419 421 419 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b031">31</xref>
###xml 550 552 547 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b032">32</xref>
###xml 623 637 620 634 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 695 697 692 694 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b032">32</xref>
###xml 1164 1166 1155 1157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b033">33</xref>
###xml 1167 1169 1158 1160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b034">34</xref>
###xml 1322 1324 1313 1315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b035">35</xref>
###xml 1325 1327 1316 1318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b036">36</xref>
###xml 1459 1460 1450 1451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 1633 1635 1624 1626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b035">35</xref>
###xml 1636 1638 1627 1629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b037">37</xref>
###xml 426 431 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 623 637 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
Nonetheless, the possibility that stress-dependent or other signal transduction pathways, in parallel with BH3-only protein degradation, may contribute to protection against apoptosis has not been investigated. Importantly, the chlamydial inclusion membrane contains abundant transmembrane proteins encoded by the chlamydial genome, named Inc proteins, which participate in the development of the chlamydial inclusion [31]. A yeast two-hybrid analysis with one of the proteins, IncG, has shown that it interacts with a host cell protein, 14-3-3beta [32]. This adapter protein is normally distributed in the cytosol, but in C. trachomatis-infected cells, it interacts and co-localizes with IncG [32]. The function for the IncG/14-3-3beta interaction was not elucidated, but in uninfected cells, 14-3-3beta can interact with several host-cell signaling pathways in a phosphorylation-dependent manner, including those involved in cell-cycle regulation, membrane trafficking, and apoptosis. The 14-3-3 isoforms can form homo- and heterodimers both in vivo and in vitro, which is thought to facilitate their interaction with different components of signaling pathways [33,34]. Significantly, in the presence of survival factors, cytosolic 14-3-3 proteins sequester phosphorylated BAD away from its targets in the mitochondria [35,36]. In the absence of survival factors, BAD is dephosphorylated and associates with mitochondria, resulting in release of cytochrome c and cell death. BAD itself is phosphorylated by AKT/protein kinase B (PKB), which is activated in the presence of survival factors by phosphatidylinositol-3 kinase (PI3K) [35-37].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 133 147 133 147 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 691 705 691 705 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. pneumoniae,</italic>
###xml 133 147 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 691 704 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">C. pneumoniae</span>
The goal of this study was therefore to determine whether stress-induced MAP kinase pathways or PI3K may contribute to resistance of C. trachomatis-infected cells to apoptosis. We found that infection leads to activation of the PI3K pathway, which results in phosphorylation of BAD and its recruitment to chlamydial inclusions that express IncG. As our results revealed a predominant role for the PI3K pathway, we evaluated involvement of AKT through RNA interference and found that AKT plays an essential role in this protection. Our results further indicate that the PI3K pathway plays no role in protection against apoptosis in the absence of BAD phosphorylation or during infection with C. pneumoniae, which does not express IncG.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Results
###end title 12
###begin title 13
###xml 39 53 39 53 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 39 53 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
The Lymphogranuloma Venereum Strain of C. trachomatis Protects Infected Cells against Staurosporine-Induced Apoptosis through a PI3K-Dependent Pathway
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 136 138 136 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b022">22</xref>
###xml 139 141 139 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b026">26</xref>
###xml 142 144 142 144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b027">27</xref>
###xml 181 195 181 195 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 533 542 533 542 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 671 680 671 680 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 748 750 748 750 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b022">22</xref>
###xml 751 753 751 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b027">27</xref>
###xml 794 803 794 803 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 856 864 856 864 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 181 195 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
As infection of HeLa cells with the lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV)/L2 serovar has been reported to inhibit apoptosis in infected cells [22,26,27], we first measured the response of C. trachomatis-infected epithelial cells to treatment with the apoptosis inducer, staurosporine (STS). Thus, cells were infected with the LGV/L2 serovar and then treated with STS before immunofluorescence staining using the DNA dye, Hoechst, to reveal segmented (apoptotic) nuclei; TUNEL (to identify apoptotic nuclei with fragmented DNA); and an anti-Chlamydia antibody to identify infected cells. While many apoptotic nuclei were present among uninfected cells, very few cells containing Chlamydia inclusions had apoptotic nuclei. Consistent with previous results [22,27], more than 95% of the cells containing Chlamydia inclusions were resistant to STS-induced apoptosis (Figure 1).
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 22 36 22 36 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 163 165 160 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b032">32</xref>
###xml 419 421 416 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b035">35</xref>
###xml 422 424 419 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b036">36</xref>
###xml 494 503 491 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 760 774 757 771 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 891 899 888 896 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 22 36 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 760 774 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
The LGV/L2 serovar of C. trachomatis produces a transmembrane protein, IncG, which localizes to the inclusion membrane and interacts specifically with 14-3-3beta [32]. In uninfected cells in the presence of survival signals, the PI3K pathway leads to phosphorylation and activation of AKT/PKB, and subsequent phosphorylation of BAD, which in its phosphorylated form is sequestered in the cytoplasm by cytosolic 14-3-3 [35,36]. In order to explore the possibility that 14-3-3 recruitment by the Chlamydia inclusion may increase resistance to apoptosis, cells that had been infected with the LGV/L2 serovar were incubated with a PI3K inhibitor (LY294002) before treatment with STS. Under conditions in which STS-mediated apoptosis was inhibited more than 95% by C. trachomatis infection, over a third of the infected cells that had been pretreated with the PI3K inhibitor underwent apoptosis (Figure 1).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 84 92 84 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g001">Figure 1</xref>
The PI3K inhibitor was added 24 h prior to treatment with STS (8 h post-infection) (Figure 1) or 6 h prior to treatment with STS (26 h post-infection) (unpublished data). Both protocols gave similar results, suggesting that the PI3K inhibitor has its effect on apoptosis at a post-entry step.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 315 317 315 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b038">38</xref>
###xml 318 320 318 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b039">39</xref>
###xml 639 647 639 647 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g001">Figure 1</xref>
To investigate the specificity of PI3K-dependent resistance to apoptosis, infected cells were treated separately with inhibitors that target components of different anti-apoptotic pathways, such as JNK1/2 (SP600125), P38 (SB203580), mTOR (rapamycin), and synthesis of heat shock proteins (KNK437), including hsp70 [38,39]. Using trypan blue exclusion assays, we verified that the drugs were not toxic to uninfected cells at the concentrations used. With the possible exception of the JNK1/2 inhibitor, which increased slightly the level of STS-induced apoptosis, none of the inhibitors had a significant effect on resistance to apoptosis (Figure 1). Thus, the PI3K pathway has a predominant role in protecting infected cells against STS-mediated apoptosis.
###end p 17

###xml 755 757 752 754 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b032">32</xref>
###xml 67 80 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">C. pneumoniae</span>
###xml 163 176 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">C. pneumoniae</span>
###xml 409 422 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">C. pneumoniae</span>
###xml 552 566 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 581 594 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">C. pneumoniae</span>
###xml 689 703 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 729 742 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">C. pneumoniae</span>
To determine whether the PI3K pathway is involved in protection of C. pneumoniae-infected cells from apoptosis, we measured resistance to STS-induced apoptosis of C. pneumoniae-infected cells in the presence and absence of LY294002 and found no change in the level of protection (unpublished data). Thus, while other mechanisms such as BH3-only protein degradation may provide protection against apoptosis of C. pneumoniae-infected cells, the PI3K pathway does not contribute to resistance. Genome sequencing has revealed an IncG homolog is present in C. trachomatis L2 but not in C. pneumoniae [11,40,41]. Consistent with this finding, the IncG-binding adaptor 14-3-3beta is localized on C. trachomatis L2 inclusions but not on C. pneumoniae inclusions [32]. Considered together with our findings, these data indicate that sequestration of 14-3-3beta to the inclusion is required for PI3K-dependent protection.

###xml 43 44 43 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 56 70 56 70 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 56 70 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
LY294002 Pretreatment Increases Cytochrome c Release in C. trachomatis-Infected Epithelial Cells
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 26 27 26 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 218 219 218 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 277 278 277 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 335 343 335 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 414 415 414 415 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 520 534 520 534 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 598 599 598 599 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 601 609 601 609 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 701 702 701 702 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 804 812 804 812 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 885 900 885 900 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. trachomatis-</italic>
###xml 1103 1104 1103 1104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 520 534 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 885 899 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
The release of cytochrome c is a critical step in the mitochondrial pathway to apoptosis, which is triggered by STS. We next tested whether the PI3K-mediated protection was located upstream or downstream of cytochrome c release. In the absence of apoptotic stimuli, cytochrome c is localized in mitochondria. Typical results, shown in Figure 2A, indicate that less than 10% of uninfected cells retained cytochrome c in their mitochondria after treatment with STS. Under the same conditions, more than 60% of STS-treated C. trachomatis-infected cells retained their mitochondrial pool of cytochrome c (Figure 2B). However, the percentage of infected cells showing retention of mitochondrial cytochrome c decreased significantly when those cells were preincubated with LY294002 before incubation with STS (Figure 2B). These results suggest that PI3K-dependent inhibition of apoptosis in C. trachomatis-infected cells is associated with the ability of the cells to maintain an intact mitochondrial membrane. The pro-survival activity of PI3K/AKT is therefore localized upstream of mitochondrial cytochrome c release.
###end p 20
###begin title 21
###xml 57 71 57 71 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 57 71 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
AKT Activation Is Downstream from PI3K Activation during C. trachomatis Infection
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 377 379 377 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b035">35</xref>
###xml 380 382 380 382 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b036">36</xref>
###xml 500 503 500 503 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">473</sup>
In order to further elucidate the anti-apoptotic mechanisms induced by infection, we measured the phosphorylation status of an essential survival-signaling mediator that is downstream from PI3K, the serine/threonine kinase, AKT/PKB. In several cell lines, PI3K phosphorylates and thereby activates AKT, which then promotes survival through phosphorylation of multiple targets [35,36]. We thus measured phosphorylation by immunoblots using antibodies against a phosphorylation site of AKT (residue Ser473) that leads to AKT activation in epithelial cells under different conditions.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 69 84 69 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. trachomatis,</italic>
###xml 145 153 145 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 432 440 432 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 470 479 470 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 69 83 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
AKT phosphorylation/activation increased after a 24-h infection with C. trachomatis, and decreased back to basal levels by 48 h after infection (Figure 3). STS treatment did not affect the AKT phosphorylation level in uninfected or infected cells. In cells infected for 24 h that were then preincubated for 6 h with LY294002 (before incubation with STS or control buffer overnight), essentially all the AKT became dephosphorylated (Figure 3). These results suggest that Chlamydia infection leads to PI3K activation, which is essential for subsequent phosphorylation and activation of the downstream effector, AKT.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 399 407 399 407 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g004">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 597 612 597 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. trachomatis,</italic>
###xml 973 981 973 981 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g004">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 1085 1094 1085 1094 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 597 611 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
In order to rule out the possibility that the PI3K inhibitor, LY294002, may have nonspecific effects and to confirm a role for AKT in the resistance to apoptosis, we inhibited AKT expression specifically in the epithelial cells by RNA interference. 48 h after transfection with AKT siRNA, more than 70% of the protein levels of AKT were reduced in uninfected cells, as verified by Western blotting (Figure 4, inset); and we verified that AKT siRNA by itself did not induce apoptosis of uninfected cells (unpublished data). The epithelial cells were first transfected with AKT siRNA, infected with C. trachomatis, and then treated with STS. In untransfected cells that contained chlamydial inclusions, very few were sensitive to STS-induced apoptosis determined by fluorescence microscopic examination for nuclear condensation. In contrast, more than half of the AKT siRNA transfected cells that contained chlamydial inclusions exhibited apoptosis after treatment with STS (Figure 4). Taken together, these results suggest that AKT plays a major role in protection against apoptosis of Chlamydia-infected cells.
###end p 24

###xml 79 93 79 93 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 79 93 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
PI3K Activation Leads to BAD Phosphorylation in Epithelial Cells Infected with C. trachomatis
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 54 57 54 57 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">136</sup>
###xml 112 114 112 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b035">35</xref>
###xml 115 117 115 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b036">36</xref>
###xml 221 235 221 235 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 221 235 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
AKT has been shown to phosphorylate BAD on residue Ser136, which causes BAD to lose its pro-apoptotic activity [35,36]. In order to determine whether phosphorylated BAD could play a role in resistance to apoptosis during C. trachomatis infection, we increased BAD protein-expression by transfecting epithelial cells with a BAD eukaryotic expression plasmid prior to infection and observing the phosphorylation of BAD by Western blotting. Although BAD was expressed at high levels in both infected and uninfected cells, the sensitivity to STS-induced apoptosis of the cells was similar to the sensitivity of cells expressing normal levels of BAD (unpublished data).
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 77 85 77 85 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 226 228 226 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b027">27</xref>
###xml 229 231 229 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b028">28</xref>
###xml 413 421 413 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 534 542 534 542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g005">Figure 5</xref>
The total amount of BAD decreased in infected cells after 32 h of infection (Figure 5A), consistent with our previous results obtained at a higher multiplicity of infection (MOI) with cells that expressed only endogenous BAD [27,28]. There was little basal phosphorylation in uninfected cells, but BAD phosphorylation increased 8 h post-infection, peaked between 24-32 h post-infection, and decreased thereafter (Figure 5A). A similar time course for BAD phosphorylation was observed in infected cells expressing only endogenous BAD (Figure 5B), suggesting that the total level of BAD protein does not affect the kinetics of BAD phosphorylation.
###end p 27

###xml 841 854 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">C. pneumoniae</span>

IncG is also expressed by the genome of Chylamdia muridarum, and 14-3-3beta is recruited to C. muridarum inclusions [32]. However, C. muridarum infection protects the host cell against apoptosis poorly, if at all [42,43]. To investigate whether BAD might interact with inclusion-bound 14-3-3beta in C. muridarum-infected cells, cells over-expressing BAD were infected with C. muridarum and the level of AKT and BAD phosphorylation was measured (Figure 5C). There may be an increase in the extent of AKT phosphorylation in infected cells, but the level of BAD phosphorylation peaked within 5 h of infection and decreased by 8 h after infection (Figure 5C). Almost no phosphorylated BAD remained by 16 h of infection. Thus, no phosphorylated BAD remains in cells infected with C. muridarum at times when cells infected with C. trachomatis and C. pneumoniae begin to exhibit resistance to apoptosis [22,25,27], suggesting that rapid dephosphorylation of BAD may account for the poor protection against apoptosis conferred by C. muridarum infection.

###begin p 29
###xml 80 94 80 94 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 257 265 257 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 446 448 446 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b044">44</xref>
###xml 593 601 593 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 80 94 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
The potential role of the PI3K pathway in activating BAD in cells infected with C. trachomatis was investigated by Western blot. Phosphorylated BAD was detected in both infected and uninfected cells, but disappeared after STS treatment of uninfected cells (Figure 6). Moreover, the levels of total BAD decreased with STS treatment in both uninfected and infected cells, consistent with previous studies showing cleavage of BAD during cell death [44]. Conversely, the level of phosphorylated BAD remained high in infected cells treated with STS, unless the cells were pretreated with LY294002 (Figure 6). Thus, infection inhibits BAD dephosphorylation, which is downstream from PI3K/AKT activation.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 31 45 31 45 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 121 123 121 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b027">27</xref>
###xml 124 126 124 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b028">28</xref>
###xml 275 305 275 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. trachomatis. C. trachomatis</italic>
###xml 31 45 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 275 289 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 291 305 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
We have recently reported that C. trachomatis infection leads to degradation of several pro-apoptotic BH3-only proteins [27,28]. In order to determine if inhibition of PI3K by LY294002 specifically affected degradation of BH3-only proteins, we infected epithelial cells with C. trachomatis. C. trachomatis infection resulted in degradation of both BIM and BAD, but the PI3K inhibitor had no effect on infection-induced degradation of either BIM or BAD (unpublished data), suggesting that phosphorylation of BAD may be acting as an alternate anti-apoptotic pathway, in parallel with induction of BH3-only protein degradation.
###end p 30
###begin title 31
###xml 15 29 15 29 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 15 29 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
Infection with C. trachomatis Causes Phosphorylated BAD to Become Localized on Chlamydial Inclusions
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 158 167 158 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 289 297 289 297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 440 449 440 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 522 530 522 530 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g007">Figure 7</xref>
In the absence of apoptotic stimuli, BAD is uniformly distributed in the cytosol (unpublished data). However, in infected cells, most BAD is localized to the Chlamydia inclusion, in the presence or absence of STS, suggesting that the pro-apoptotic protein is sequestered by the inclusion (Figure 7A, first and second rows). Incubation of infected cells with LY294002 prior to STS treatment resulted in a decrease in BAD associated with the Chlamydia inclusion, which coincided with nuclear condensation in infected cells (Figure 7A, third row).
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 69 71 69 71 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b035">35</xref>
###xml 72 74 72 74 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b036">36</xref>
###xml 137 146 137 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 201 216 201 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. trachomatis,</italic>
###xml 341 344 341 344 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">136</sup>
###xml 525 533 525 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 992 1000 992 1000 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 201 215 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
The adapter protein 14-3-3 binds only to BAD that is phosphorylated [35,36]. To determine whether phosphorylated BAD is recruited by the Chlamydia vacuole, cells over-expressing BAD were infected with C. trachomatis, and phosphorylated BAD was localized by immunofluorescence using antibodies that recognize BAD phosphorylated on residue Ser136. To exclude the possibility of artifactual co-localization between phosphorylated BAD and chlamydiae due to the large size of mature inclusions, cells were infected for only 15 h. Figure 7B (top row) shows that there is co-localization between phosphorylated BAD and the early chlamydial inclusion. Similar results were found in cells infected for longer times (unpublished data). In order to examine whether phosphorylation of BAD is required for the co-localization, the infected cells were also treated with the PI3K inhibitor, LY294002. Longer exposures failed to reveal co-localization between the inclusion and remaining phosphorylated BAD (Figure 7B, bottom row).
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 66 79 66 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. muridarum,</italic>
###xml 174 187 174 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. muridarum.</italic>
###xml 213 225 213 225 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. muridarum</named-content>
###xml 353 361 353 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 526 538 526 538 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. muridarum</named-content>
###xml 66 78 <span type="species:ncbi:83560">C. muridarum</span>
###xml 174 186 <span type="species:ncbi:83560">C. muridarum</span>
###xml 213 225 <span type="species:ncbi:83560">C. muridarum</span>
###xml 526 538 <span type="species:ncbi:83560">C. muridarum</span>
Given that BAD was quickly dephosphorylated during infection with C. muridarum, we also measured the localization of BAD in cells over-expressing BAD that were infected with C. muridarum. Cells were infected with C. muridarum for 15 h, at which time little BAD was still phosphorylated. There was no co-localization between BAD and the early inclusion (Figure 7C), although similar results were also obtained with larger inclusions (unpublished data). The PI3K inhibitor, LY294002, had no effect on the distribution of BAD in C. muridarum-infected cells (unpublished data).
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 97 99 97 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b035">35</xref>
###xml 189 203 186 200 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 231 233 228 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b032">32</xref>
###xml 441 455 432 446 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 560 562 548 550 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b032">32</xref>
###xml 669 677 654 662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g008">Figure 8</xref>
###xml 809 817 791 799 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g008">Figure 8</xref>
###xml 954 968 936 950 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 1177 1186 1156 1165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 189 203 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 441 455 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 954 968 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
Since BAD phosphorylation leads to sequestration of BAD by cytosolic 14-3-3 in uninfected cells [35], and the 14-3-3beta protein has been previously shown to localize on the surface of the C. trachomatis vacuole in infected cells [32], we examined the distribution of 14-3-3beta in infected and uninfected cells, with or without STS treatment. As expected, 14-3-3beta was distributed uniformly in uninfected cells (unpublished data). During C. trachomatis infection, 14-3-3beta was localized in a ring around the inclusion, in agreement with previous results [32] (unpublished data). Interestingly, 14-3-3beta remained localized in the ring despite treatment with STS (Figure 8, top row). But 14-3-3beta distributed throughout infected cells that had been pretreated with LY294002 before incubation with STS (Figure 8, bottom row), which could be due in part to condensation of the dying host-cell and fragmentation of the inclusion. Thus, protection of C. trachomatis-infected cells against apoptosis is due at least partly to activation of PI3K and AKT, which correlate with BAD phosphorylation and the sequestration of phosphorylated BAD by 14-3-3beta on the surface of the Chlamydia inclusion.

###begin title 36
Discussion
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 79 93 79 93 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 79 93 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
We demonstrate here that the PI3K pathway plays a prominent role in protecting C. trachomatis-infected cells from apoptosis. Among different signaling pathways considered, only inhibition of the PI3K pathway removed the protection provided by infection against apoptosis. Inhibitors targeting JNK1/2, P38, mTOR or synthesis of heat shock proteins were unable to reverse protection against apoptosis, although the JNK1/2 inhibitor had a slight effect.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 59 73 59 73 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 351 352 351 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 609 623 609 623 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 625 627 625 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b022">22</xref>
###xml 885 887 879 881 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b032">32</xref>
###xml 1059 1061 1050 1052 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c.</italic>
###xml 1166 1174 1157 1165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g009">Figure 9</xref>
###xml 59 73 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 609 623 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
The PI3K pathway leads to phosphorylation of AKT, which in C. trachomatis-infected cells remains phosphorylated even after treatment with STS. Consistent with a role for this pathway in resistance to apoptosis, inhibition of PI3K or depletion of AKT causes STS-treated infected cells to die through apoptosis, through a mechanism involving cytochrome c release. AKT activation also results in BAD phosphorylation, which can be blocked in infected cells by incubation with the PI3K inhibitor, and with a time course that is consistent with the appearance of protection against apoptosis of cells infected with C. trachomatis [22]. BAD co-localizes with the adapter protein, 14-3-3beta, on the surface of the chlamydial inclusion membrane even in infected cells that had been treated with STS. As 14-3-3beta co-localizes with the chlamydial protein IncG on the surface of the inclusion [32], these results suggest that this IncG-14-3-3beta interaction allows the inclusion to sequester BAD away from mitochondria, where it could stimulate release of cytochrome c. Simultaneous activation of the PI3K pathway during infection maintains BAD in its phosphorylated state (Figure 9).
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 161 176 161 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. trachomatis,</italic>
###xml 208 222 208 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C. pneumoniae,</italic>
###xml 392 404 392 404 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. muridarum</named-content>
###xml 473 485 473 485 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. muridarum</named-content>
###xml 502 504 502 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b032">32</xref>
###xml 161 175 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 208 221 <span type="species:ncbi:83558">C. pneumoniae</span>
###xml 392 404 <span type="species:ncbi:83560">C. muridarum</span>
###xml 473 485 <span type="species:ncbi:83560">C. muridarum</span>
A role for IncG in PI3K-dependent protection against apoptosis is suggested by the observation that the PI3K inhibitor removed protection in cells infected with C. trachomatis, but not in cells infected with C. pneumoniae, which does not express the IncG protein. Phosphorylation of BAD also appears to be necessary for co-localization with the inclusion, since BAD does not co-localize with C. muridarum inclusions at a time when little BAD is phosphorylated, even though C. muridarum expresses IncG [32].
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 18 27 18 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 92 94 92 94 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b027">27</xref>
###xml 95 97 95 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b028">28</xref>
###xml 156 164 156 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="ppat-0020045-g009">Figure 9</xref>
###xml 393 395 390 392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b032">32</xref>
###xml 519 521 513 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b032">32</xref>
###xml 688 697 682 691 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
For safe measure, Chlamydia infection also leads to degradation of most of the BAD protein [27,28], whose degradation is measurable after 1 d of infection (Figure 9). Any remaining, but presumably functional, BAD could be sequestered away from mitochondria by 14-3-3beta, via the chlamydial protein IncG, whose expression begins within 2 h after internalization of the bacteria by host cells [32]. IncG is also phosphorylated in infected cells, and the phosphorylation is required for IncG interaction with 14-3-3beta [32]. Further exploration of the cross talk between chlamydial inclusion membrane proteins and host-cell signaling pathways will likely yield valuable new information on Chlamydia-dependent modulation of host-cell biology.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 68 82 68 82 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 367 369 367 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b048">48</xref>
###xml 546 548 546 548 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b049">49</xref>
###xml 549 551 549 551 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b053">53</xref>
###xml 560 579 560 579 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">Salmonella enterica</named-content>
###xml 700 702 700 702 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b053">53</xref>
###xml 68 82 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
###xml 560 579 <span type="species:ncbi:28901">Salmonella enterica</span>
It is still unknown what mechanism could lead to PI3K activation in C. trachomatis-infected cells. An obvious possibility would be that chlamydiae secrete anti-apoptotic factors through their type III secretion apparatus. Secreted bacterial proteins of other pathogens can stimulate or interfere with specific host-cellular processes for the benefit of the pathogen [48]. In particular, bacterial proteins secreted through the type III secretion apparatus by a number of pathogens have already been shown to modulate cell death during infection [49-53]. Thus, Salmonella enterica protects infected epithelial cells from apoptosis through secretion of SopB, which induces sustained activation of AKT [53].
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 199 201 199 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b038">38</xref>
###xml 202 204 202 204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b054">54</xref>
###xml 205 207 205 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b058">58</xref>
###xml 292 302 292 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia,</italic>
###xml 381 383 381 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b059">59</xref>
###xml 384 386 384 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b061">61</xref>
###xml 506 508 506 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b062">62</xref>
###xml 559 561 559 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b063">63</xref>
###xml 562 564 562 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b064">64</xref>
###xml 723 725 723 725 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b065">65</xref>
###xml 726 728 726 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b066">66</xref>
###xml 752 761 752 761 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
Alternatively or concurrently, a host-cell stress response could result in PI3K activation. Signaling through AKT is known to play a key role in protection of cells from a variety of toxic stresses [38,54-58], as may occur during infection by intracellular pathogens. In the specific case of Chlamydia, infection is known to increase mitochondrial metabolism and oxidative stress [59-61]. In cells exposed to oxidative stress, down regulation of the PI3K/AKT pathway results in higher levels of apoptosis [62] and oxidants stimulate the AKT survival pathway [63,64]. Interestingly, mechanical stress, which could be caused by the presence of a growing inclusion, induces rapid phosphorylation of AKT in various cell types [65,66]. We thus propose that Chlamydia could secrete specific, as-yet-unidentified proteins that could activate the PI3K pathway directly, or that oxidative or mechanical stress induced by infection leads to activation of the host cell PI3K/AKT pathway, resulting in protection from apoptosis.

HeLa cells growing at 70% confluence on tissue culture flasks (Costar) were infected with the LGV/L2 strain of C. trachomatis, C. muridarum, or C. pneumoniae at an MOI of 1.0 and incubated for the indicated times in an incubator at 37 degreesC under 5% CO2. Under these conditions, > 80% of the cells contained chlamydial vacuoles after 1 d of infection. In order to induce apoptosis, infected and control uninfected cells were treated overnight with 2 muM STS (Sigma). When indicated, cells were incubated with LY294002 before STS treatment. Both adherent cells and cells in suspension were collected for either flow cytometric or Western blot analysis, while only adherent cells were fixed for fluorescence microscopy.

###begin title 48
Transfections.
###end title 48
###begin p 49
As BAD is not visible by immunofluorescence in cells expressing endogenous levels of BAD, BAD was over-expressed where indicated. The BAD plasmid was transfected using Lipofectamine Plus reagent (Invitrogen Life Technology, Carlsbad, California, United States) following manufacturer's instructions. Briefly, 12 h after trypsinization and seeding into 24-well plates, HeLa cells were incubated for 4 h at 37 degreesC with 250 mul of DMEM containing 400 ng of pEBG-mBAD (Cell Signaling Technology, Beverly, Massachusetts, United States), 1 mul of Lipofectamine, and 4 mul of Plus reagent. Serum-containing medium was added into each well, and cells were incubated for up to 48 h.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
Transfection of siRNA for AKT was performed as follows: HeLa cells were plated in 24 well-plates for 12 h before transfection in order to reach 70% confluence on the following day, when the medium was replaced by 0.25 ml of serum-containing medium. The siRNA complex was formed immediately before transfection by adding 2.5 mul of the TransIT-TKO transfection reagent (Mirus Corporation, Madison, Wisconsin, United States) drop-wise into 50 mul of serum-free RPMI 1640 (Invitrogen Life Technology). After gentle mixing, the incubation was performed for 10 min at room temperature. 50 nM siRNA AKT (final concentration) was added to the diluted transfection reagent, mixed gently, and incubated for 10 min at room temperature. Finally, 50 mul of this mixture was added to each well, the plate was rocked gently, and further incubated for 48 h at 37 degreesC. The sequences of the siRNA AKT were: sense, 5'-UGCCCUUCUACAACCAGGATT; antisense, 5'-UCCUGGUUGUAGAAGGGCATT.
###end p 50

###xml 80 89 80 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>

For pEGB m-BAD- or siRNA AKT-transfected and infected cells, the infection with Chlamydia was begun 8 h after transfection. As a negative control for siRNA AKT, cells were transfected with the same amount of irrelevant RNA, before infection with Chlamydia.

Flow cytometric analysis of cytochrome c release.
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 191 192 190 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 603 604 590 591 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 992 994 979 981 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b068">68</xref>
###xml 801 805 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 811 816 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
HeLa cells were harvested with PBS containing 1% trypsin and 1 mM EDTA, and then treated with digitonin (200 mug/ml) (Sigma) in PBS for 10 min on ice in order to release cytosolic cytochrome c. Cells were washed with three equivalent volumes of PBS containing 0.3% BSA, centrifuged (1,000 g, 10 min, 4 degreesC), and fixed in 4% neutral-buffered paraformaldehyde for 20 min at room temperature. Cells were then washed again in PBS and incubated in blocking buffer (3% BSA, 0.1% Triton X-100 in PBS) for 1 h. Finally, cells were incubated overnight at 4 degreesC with a 1:500 dilution of anti-cytochrome c monoclonal antibody (clone 6H2B4, BD Pharmingen, San Diego, United States). On the following day, cells were washed three times with blocking buffer, and incubated for 1 h with an FITC-conjugated goat anti-mouse antibody (Caltag Laboratories, Burlingame, California, United States). The cells were washed three times in PBS and analyzed by flow cytometry (FL1), as previously described [68].

###begin title 54
Detection of apoptotic nuclei by Hoechst or TUNEL staining.
###end title 54
###begin p 55
###xml 374 376 374 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ppat-0020045-b069">69</xref>
###xml 484 493 484 493 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Chlamydia</italic>
###xml 633 637 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 643 648 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
HeLa cells were grown on glass slides in 24-well plates, washed with PBS, and fixed in 4% neutral-buffered paraformaldehyde for 30 min. DNA strand breaks were identified using a terminal deoxynucleotidyltransferase-mediated dUTP nick end labeling (TUNEL) assay kit, following the manufacturer's protocol (Roche Applied Science, Basel, Switzerland), as previously described [69]. In order to detect infected and apoptotic cells simultaneously, slides were counterstained using an anti-Chlamydia genus monoclonal antibody (clone C5/C8, Argene Biosoft, Varilhes, France) at 1:400 dilution, and a Texas Red-conjugated or FITC-conjugated goat anti-mouse polyclonal antibody (1:200 dilution) plus Hoechst (1 mug/ml) in PBS. Slides were mounted in antifade medium (Dako, Glostrup, Denmark) and examined using an epifluorescence microscope (Zeiss Axioskope) equipped with band pass optical filter sets appropriate for TRITC, FITC, and DAPI dyes. The images were captured by multiple exposures using a cooled CCD camera controlled by Metamorph software.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
Cells were also stained with 1 mug/ml Hoechst, which detects both host-cell and chlamydial DNA. In order to quantify the number of apoptotic or non-apoptotic nuclei in both infected and uninfected cells in each cell population, cells were counted in each microscope field, and the average and standard deviation per condition was calculated for the % of apoptotic cells with co-localizing inclusions in at least ten fields, containing an average of 100 cells per field.
###end p 56

###begin p 60

Summary of the Effects of C. trachomatis Infection on BAD Phosphorylation, BAD Degradation, and Host-Cell Death
###end title 163
###begin p 164
###xml 15 29 15 29 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" content-type="genus-species">C. trachomatis</named-content>
###xml 15 29 <span type="species:ncbi:813">C. trachomatis</span>
Infection with C. trachomatis leads to activation of PI3K, which in turns results in AKT activation and subsequent BAD phosphorylation. In parallel, infection causes degradation of most unphosphorylated BAD. Simultaneous expression of IncG on the chlamydial inclusion and binding of 14-3-3beta by IncG allows the inclusion to recruit phosphorylated BAD, preventing the ability of the remaining BAD to translocate to the mitochondria and induce host-cell death.
###end p 164
