GSTM1 and GSTP1 Polymorphisms as Potential Factors for Modifying the Effect of Smoking on Inflammatory Response
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 251 256 251 256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 261 266 261 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 551 556 547 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 565 570 561 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 774 779 766 771 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 789 794 781 786 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 893 894 881 882 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 1025 1030 1009 1014 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 1035 1040 1019 1024 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 173 180 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
###xml 335 342 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
###xml 629 636 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">Tobacco</span>
Inflammation has been known to be an important underlying condition for development of various diseases including cancer. The aims of this study were to investigate whether tobacco smoke exposure increases the level of inflammation biomarkers and the GSTM1 and GSTP1 gene polymorphisms are associated with inflammatory response due to tobacco smoke exposure. We measured urinary cotinine level in 300 healthy university students. Total serum TNF-alpha levels and blood WBC counts were determined to evaluate inflammatory response. Allelic loss of the GSTM1 and the GSTP1 (Ile105Val) polymorphism were determined by PCR and RFLP. Tobacco smoke exposure was found to be associated with increase of both TNF-alpha level and WBC count. Particularly, smokers with combination of GSTM1 null and GSTP1 AG or GG genotypes showed higher TNF-alpha level than those with the other genotype combinations (p=0.07). This result suggests that smoking may induce inflammation measured as TNF-alpha level or WBC count and combinations of the GSTM1 and GSTP1 polymorphisms may modify the effect of smoking on serum TNF-alpha level.

###begin title 2
INTRODUCTION
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 125 126 125 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 261 262 261 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 263 264 263 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 386 387 386 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 515 516 515 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 518 519 518 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 826 827 814 815 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 828 830 816 818 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1021 1023 1009 1011 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 1212 1214 1200 1202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 1400 1402 1384 1386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 890 897 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">persons</span>
###xml 990 997 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">persons</span>
Inflammation is a dynamic response of vascularized tissues to injury and an important part of the body's defense mechanisms (1). Although inflammation is importantly required for homeostasis, excessive inflammation may induce various diseases including cancer (2-5). Cigarette smoking has been known to induce cancer in a variety of tissues including lung through chronic inflammation (6). Previous studies have shown that cigarette smoking induces proteins related in inflammation, so called inflammation markers (7, 8). Tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-alpha) and white blood cells (WBCs) are well known inflammatory markers. Cytokines such as TNF-alpha are important inducers of inflammatory response, and the existing evidence implicates the role of TNF-alpha in inflammatory pathways that increase a variety of diseases (9-13). In one large prospective study (Honolulu Heart Program), persons with WBC count in highest tertile had 30% increased risk of dying from cancer compared with persons in the lowest tertile (14). Another prospective study has shown that elevation in WBC count within the clinically normal range is associated with increased risk of several chronic diseases related to inflammation (15). Therefore, it is believed that increased TNF-alpha level and WBC count play important roles in smoking-induced inflammation which could be associated with increased risk of diseases (16).
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 176 178 176 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 180 182 180 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 358 360 358 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 362 364 362 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 494 496 494 496 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 498 500 498 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 732 734 732 734 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 343 350 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
###xml 717 724 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
Glutathione S-transferase (GST) M1 and P1 are detoxification enzymes, which have a pivotal role in catalyzing the conjugation of glutathione (GSH) to electrophilic substrates (17, 18). Because of this function of GSTM1 and GSTP1, they may protect against inflammation and oxidative stress through detoxification of various toxic substances in tobacco smoke (19, 20). Several studies have demonstrated that individuals lacking the GSTM1 activity have increased risk of smoking-related diseases (21, 22). Moreover, the GSTP1 protein was reported to be very important in metabolism of many carcinogenic compounds including benzo[a]pyrene diolepoxide (BPDE) which is one of the most important carcinogenic metabolites in tobacco smoke (20).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 10 15 10 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 125 127 125 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 233 238 233 238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 371 373 371 373 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 393 398 393 398 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 403 408 403 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 527 532 527 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 537 542 537 542 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 856 861 852 857 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 866 871 862 867 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 778 785 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
###xml 940 947 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
The human GSTM1 is polymorphic and its deletion is responsible for the existence of a null type lacking the enzyme function (23). Furthermore, a polymorphic site at codon 105 (A to G substitution replacing isoleucine with valine) of GSTP1 is known to change the kinetic property of the enzyme in binding with a few of electrophilic substrates including PAH diolepoxides (24). For this reason, GSTM1 and GSTP1 gene polymorphisms may contribute to susceptibility to inflammation-related diseases. These results also suggest that GSTM1 and GSTP1 gene polymorphisms may be involved in the detoxification of toxic substances in cigarette smoke to change the levels of TNF-alpha and WBC, which are inflammatory markers. Therefore, in the present study we tried to investigate whether tobacco smoke exposure increases the level of inflammation biomarkers and the GSTM1 and GSTP1 gene polymorphisms are associated with inflammatory response due to tobacco smoke exposure.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 6
###begin title 7
Study population and sampling
###end title 7
###begin p 8
A total of 300 students at Inha University, Incheon, Korea, were recruited during September 2002 after exclusion of students having conditions affecting inflammatory biomarkers such as inflammatory disorder or medication of anti-inflammatory drugs. Information on demographic characteristics and lifestyle habits including smoking and alcohol consumption were collected by trained interviewers using a structured questionnaire. We controlled fasting state by asking them for overnight fasting to participate in this study and samplings of blood and urine were done in the morning. After collecting venous blood samples from subjects, whole blood in a heparinized tube was kept at 4℃ for counting of blood WBC and serum in a plain tube was separated by centrifugation within 4 hr and frozen at -70℃ for the analysis of TNF-alpha level. In addition, urine was kept frozen at -20℃ for the analysis of cotinine level. The study protocol was approved by the institutional review board at Inha University Hospital and written informed consent was provided by all study subjects.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Measurement of serum TNF-alpha levels and blood WBC counts
###end title 9
###begin p 10
Serum TNF-alpha level was determined by enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) according to the manufacturer's instructions (R&D Systems, Minneapolis, MN, U.S.A.). The detection limit of the assay was 0.12 pg/mL. In addition, the count of blood WBC was measured using cell counter.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
Measurement of urinary cotinine levels
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 292 295 292 295 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">125</sup>
###xml 55 62 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
Urinary cotinine levels were determined for monitoring tobacco exposure. After urine samples were centrifuged to remove particulate matter, cotinine level was analyzed by a liquid phase radioimmunoassay method using the fundamental principles of a competitive reaction between antibodies and 125I-labeled cotinine and urinary cotinine (DPC, Los Angeles, CA, U.S.A.). After allowing antigen-antibody reaction for 30 min, the fraction bound to antibody was precipitated and its radioactivity was measured for 1 min using Cobra D5010 Quantum, a gamma-counter (Packard, Meriden, CT, U.S.A.). Urinary cotinine level was adjusted to urine creatinine.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Genotyping assays
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 169 174 169 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 179 184 179 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 249 254 245 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 259 264 255 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 342 343 338 339 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 377 380 373 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Taq</italic>
Genomic DNA was extracted from peripheral blood lymphocytes using a QIAamp DNA Blood Mini Kit (Qiagen, Valencia, CA, U.S.A.), and analyzed using PCR and RFLP method for GSTM1 and GSTP1 gene polymorphisms. The 20 microL PCR reaction mixture used for GSTM1 and GSTP1 genotyping contained 10 mM of Tris-HCl (pH 9.0), 40 mM of KCl, 1.5 mM of MgCl2, 0.25 mM of each dNTP, 1 unit of Taq polymerase (Bioneer, Seoul, Korea), 20 pmole of the forward and reverse primers, and 50 ng of genomic DNA as a template. Amplifications were performed using the following conditions; initial denaturation at 94℃ for 5 min, 35 cycles with denaturation at 94℃ for 1 min, annealing at 65℃ for 1 min, and extension at 72℃ for 1 min, and then completion at 72℃ for 7 min. PCR amplification of reaction mixtures was carried out using a thermal cycler, PTC-200 (MJ Research, Watertown, MA, U.S.A.).
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 10 15 10 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 88 93 88 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 98 103 98 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 179 184 179 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 224 229 224 229 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 269 274 269 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 312 317 312 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 410 414 410 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">BsmA</italic>
###xml 437 442 437 442 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 579 584 579 584 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 674 679 674 679 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
GSTM1 and GSTP1 genotypes were determined using a multiplex PCR method. Primer sets for GSTM1 and GSTP1 genes were used for the amplification reaction. The primers designed were: GSTM1-forward, 5'-GAACTCCCTGAAAAGCTAAAGC-3', GSTM1-reverse, 5'-GTTGGGCTCAAATATACGGTGG-3'; GSTP1-forward, 5'-CCAGTGACTGTGTGTTGATC-3', GSTP1-reverse, 5'-CAACCCTGGTGCAGATGCTC-3'. The PCR products were digested with restriction enzyme BsmA I at 55℃ for 3 hr for GSTP1 genotyping and detected by electrophoresis on 3% 3:1 NuSieve/agarose gel (Cambrex Bio Science, Rockland, ME, U.S.A.). Genotyping of the GSTM1 gene was determined based on the presence of a 215-bp product. For the genotyping of the GSTP1 gene, the 189-bp PCR product remained intact for the A allele, but was cleaved into smaller fragments of 149-bp and 40-bp in the case of the G allele. For confirming the analyses, ten percent of the samples were randomly chosen and genotyped again, producing identical results.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Statistical analysis
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 326 331 322 327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 336 341 332 337 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
Means and the distributions of characteristic variables according to smoking status were compared using the t-test or chi-square test. The t-test was used to analyze relationships between TNF-alpha level or WBC count and subject characteristics such as age, sex, body mass index (BMI), smoking status, cotinine level, and the GSTM1 and GSTP1 genotypes. The effects of smoking on total serum TNF-alpha level and blood WBC count were evaluated by linear regression analysis. Correlation between smoking amounts obtained from questionnaire and urinary cotinine level was evaluated by Spearman's and Kendall's rank correlations as well as Pearson's correlation analysis. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to evaluate the effects of genotype combinations on inflammatory markers according to smoking amount or cotinine level. For smokers, pack year value was calculated to indicate cumulative cigarette dose (pack years=[cigarettes/day/20]xyears of smoking). A probability level of 0.05 was used as the criterion of statistical significance based on two-sided test. SAS, version 8.1 (SAS institute Inc., Cary, NC, U.S.A.) was used for the statistical analysis.
###end p 17

Total serum TNF-alpha levels and blood WBC counts according to age, sex, BMI, alcohol consumption, and GSTM1 and GSTP1 genotypes are listed in Table 2. Age, sex, and alcohol consumption were not found to be significantly related to TNF-alpha level or WBC count. Obesity was found to be significantly associated with increase of WBC count (p<0.01), but the observed increase of TNF-alpha with obesity did not reach statistical significance (p=0.08). Subjects with the GSTM1 null genotype had significantly higher TNF-alpha level than those with the GSTM1 present genotype (p=0.04). However, the GSTM1 genotype did not affect WBC count (p=0.82). Subjects with the GSTP1 AG or GG genotype had significantly higher WBC count than those with GSTP1 AA genotype (p=0.04). TNF-alpha level was also higher in subjects with the GSTP1 AG or GG genotype than those with the GSTP1 AA genotype, although this difference did not reach statistical significance (p=0.38).

Since the metabolism of the toxins from tobacco smoke is extremely complex, single gene may not have enough impact on inflammation. In an attempt to address this complexity, we constructed and analyzed composite genotypes (Fig. 1, 2). Combination of GSTM1 null and GSTP1 AG or GG genotypes in subjects with pack year or cotinine level over zero showed higher TNF-alpha level than the other genotype combinations, although the differences were not statistically significant (ANOVA, pack year, p=0.07; and cotinine, p=0.15, respectively) (Fig. 1). ###begin title 23
DISCUSSION
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 205 206 201 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 207 209 203 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 513 514 497 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 611 613 591 593 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 916 921 884 889 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 931 936 899 904 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 1218 1223 1186 1191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 1233 1238 1201 1206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 1601 1603 1569 1571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 1605 1607 1573 1575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 1609 1611 1577 1579 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 1716 1718 1684 1686 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 1720 1722 1688 1690 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 2061 2063 2029 2031 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 118 125 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
###xml 981 988 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
###xml 1860 1867 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
TNF-alpha protein has been known to play an important role in a variety of diseases through inflammatory reaction and tobacco smoke has been known to induce inflammation-related diseases including cancer (6-11). Therefore, inflammationrelated diseases caused by smoking may be promoted through increase of TNF-alpha level. However, the effect of smoking on TNF-alpha level was inconsistent in previous reports. Kuschner and coworkers reported that smokers had higher level of pulmonary TNF-alpha than nonsmokers (6), but Keatings and coworkers did not show the elevated TNF-alpha level in asymptomatic smokers (25). Our data also did not support the significant association between smoking amount and TNF-alpha level. However, when we analyzed the effect of smoking on TNF-alpha level according to genotype combination, smoking showed marginally significant effect on TNF-alpha level in subjects with combination of GSTM1 null and GSTP1 AG or GG genotypes in spite of low level of tobacco smoke exposure. Moreover, WBCs were significantly increased by smoking regardless of genotypes. These results indicate that smoking can increase the levels of inflammation biomarkers, particularly in subjects with combination of GSTM1 null and GSTP1 AG or GG genotypes. The relationship between smoking and inflammation-induced carcinogenesis is biologically plausible. Oxidative stress such as the formation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) is a key component of inflammation, and they lead to damage of macromolecules such as DNA, protein, and lipid, eventually cancer when ROS are generated in large amounts (19, 20, 26). Moreover, GST enzymes have been reported to protect against endogenous or exogenous oxidative damage (27, 28). These facts support that GST enzymes may protect against inflammation through oxidative damage induced by toxic chemicals contained in tobacco smoke. Although host antioxidant defences such as GSTs detoxify ROS, individuals differ in their ability to deal with oxidant burden, and such differences are, in part, genetically determined (29). Inability to detoxify ROS due to genetic variation is likely to perpetuate the inflammatory process which could be associated with a variety of diseases.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 250 255 242 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 265 270 257 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 424 429 412 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 434 439 422 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 501 506 489 494 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 516 521 504 509 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 718 723 706 711 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 728 733 716 721 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 757 759 745 747 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 807 812 795 800 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 817 822 805 810 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 898 903 886 891 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 913 918 901 906 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 1601 1602 1581 1582 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 884 892 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1707 1714 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
We hypothesized that elevated TNF-alpha is a good indicator of inflammation caused by smoking and serum TNF-alpha level would be associated with GST polymorphisms in relation to smoking. The present study showed that subjects with the combination of GSTM1 null and GSTP1 AG or GG genotypes have higher TNF-alpha level than those with the other genotype combinations. This result suggests that there are interactions between GSTM1 and GSTP1 genetic polymorphisms, thus subjects with the combination of GSTM1 null and GSTP1 AG or GG genotypes are more susceptible to smoking-induced inflammation than those with the other genotype combinations. The previous study by Ryberg and coworkers support the interaction between GSTM1 and GSTP1 genetic polymorphisms (30). In their study, when the effects of combined GSTM1 and GSTP1 genotypes on DNA adduct formation were examined, lung cancer patients with GSTM1 null and GSTP1 AG or GG genotype combinations had significantly higher carcinogen-derived DNA adduct level in lung tissue than those with the other genotype combinations. This genotype combination effect was not found in WBC count in our study. The reason may be that the elevation of WBC count may be relatively late and consequential event rather than causal event in inflammatory pathway while TNF-alpha is considered as a proinflammatory cytokine and a causal factor of inflammation because the binding of TNF-alpha to its receptors results in the activation of major transcription factors such as AP-1 and NFkB which in turn induce genes involved in acute and chronic inflammatory responses (9). Moreover, TNF-alpha may be induced through excessive oxidative stress by toxic chemicals contained in tobacco smoke although the level may be modified by GSTM1 and GSTP1 enzymes. For this reason, TNF-alpha may be more relevant as a biomarker indicating inflammation which could be associated with a variety of diseases than WBC count, although both TNF-alpha and WBC are indicators of inflammation.
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 909 914 893 898 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 919 924 903 908 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 217 224 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
In our study, questionnaire information of smoking showed better relationship with TNF-alpha level or WBC count than urinary cotinine level, although cotinine is widely considered to be the best marker for monitoring tobacco exposure. Study subjects sometimes intend to stop smoking before study and half life of cotinine in the body is very short (about 17 hr). Therefore, change of smoking habit before study may lead to the change of urinary cotinine level, which may undermine relationship between urinary cotinine level and TNF-alpha level or WBC count. In addition, our study showed borderline significance of smoking on TNF-alpha level, probably due to small sample size. Therefore, this study results should be confirmed through studies of larger subjects. In conclusion, our study findings suggest that smoking may induce inflammation measured as TNF-alpha level or WBC count and combinations of the GSTM1 and GSTP1 polymorphisms may modify the effect of smoking on serum TNF-alpha level, indicating genetic susceptibility to smoking-related diseases.
