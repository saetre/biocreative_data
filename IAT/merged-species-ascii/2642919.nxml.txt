Subfertility in mice harboring a mutation in betaB2-crystallin

###begin title 3
Purpose
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 382 386 376 380 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Phil</sup>
###xml 376 386 370 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Crybb2<sup>Phil</sup></italic>
###xml 357 361 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 465 470 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
betaB2-crystallin is one of the most abundant proteins of the adult ocular lens of mammals although it is expressed at lower levels in several extralenticular locations. While mutations in betaB2-crystallin are known to result in lens opacities, alterations in tissues besides the lens have not been previously investigated in these mutants. Since we found mice harboring the Crybb2Phil mutation bred poorly, here we assess the contribution of betaB2-crystallin to mouse fertility and determine the expression pattern of betaB2-crystallin in the testis.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Methods
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 162 166 159 163 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Phil</sup>
###xml 156 166 153 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Crybb2<sup>Phil</sup></italic>
###xml 167 171 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The expression pattern of betaB2-crystallin in the testis was analyzed by rt-PCR, western blotting, and immunohistochemistry. The fecundity of wildtype and Crybb2Phil mice was analyzed by quantitative fertility testing. The morphology of testes and ovaries was assessed by hematoxylin and eosin staining.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Results
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 7 12 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 251 255 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 257 263 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 269 275 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 395 399 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 598 602 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In the mouse testis, betaB2-crystallin mRNA is found at low levels at birth, but its expression upregulates in this tissue as the testis is primed to initiate spermatogenesis. Western blotting detected betaB2-crystallin protein in sperm obtained from mice, cattle, and humans while immunolocalization detected this protein in developing sperm from the spermatocyte stage onward. Male and female mice homozygous for a 12 nucleotide inframe deletion mutation in betaB2-crystallin are subfertile when analyzed on a Swiss Webster derived background due to defects in egg and sperm production. However, mice harboring the same mutation on the C57Bl/6 genetic background did not exhibit any defects in reproductive function.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Conclusions
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 66 70 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 376 381 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
betaB2-crystallin is expressed in developing and mature sperm and mice of both sexes harboring the Philly mutation in the betaB2-crystallin gene are subfertile when analyzed on a Swiss Webster genetic background. While these data are suggestive of a role for betaB2-crystallin in fertility, definitive determination of this will await the creation of a betaB2-crystallin null mouse.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
Introduction
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 199 200 199 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 201 202 201 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 368 369 368 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 567 568 567 568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 890 891 882 883 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
The ocular lens is a transparent tissue that accumulates cytoplasmic proteins at concentrations approaching 450 mg/ml in order to reach the refractive index necessary to focus light onto the retina [1,2]. These abundant proteins are named as crystallins if their individual concentration approaches 5% or more of the water soluble proteins of the lens of any species [3]. In many cases, crystallins are either transcribed from the same gene as a common metabolic enzyme or have arisen from a recent gene duplication from such a gene (the taxon specific crystallins) [4]. Further, all vertebrates have two genes for alpha-crystallins which are members of the small heat shock protein family. One of these, alphaB-crystallin, is expressed at appreciable levels in multiple tissues besides the lens where it has been shown to be protective against and/or induced by numerous stress responses [5].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 110 111 103 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 336 337 319 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 426 427 402 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 584 585 553 554 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 586 588 555 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 725 743 694 712 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Myxococcus xanthus</italic>
###xml 745 747 714 716 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 409 413 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 418 424 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 725 743 <span type="species:ncbi:34">Myxococcus xanthus</span>
All vertebrates also express members of the beta/gamma-crystallin superfamily at high levels in their lenses [6]. These proteins are defined by sequence similarities that result in the presence of two or more "beta/gamma-crystallin Greek key motifs", a characteristic anti-parallel beta-sheet conformation, in their tertiary structure [7]. Mutations in beta- or gamma-crystallin genes cause cataracts in both mice and humans [8] showing that they are required to maintain lens transparency. However, even though many beta/gamma-crystallins are expressed at sites outside of the lens [9,10], and a prokaryotic member of this family, Protein S, is a calcium binding protein that forms the spore coat of the colonial prokaryote Myxococcus xanthus [11], the extralenticular functions of vertebrate beta/gamma-crystallins are not known.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 73 75 70 72 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 76 78 73 75 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 210 211 201 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 212 214 203 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 247 249 238 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 529 530 514 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 635 637 617 619 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 769 771 751 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
betaB2-crystallin is a major component of the postnatal vertebrate lens [12-14] and it is the most abundant beta-crystallin of adult lenses in many species. betaB2-crystallin is also the most thermally stable [9,15] and resistant to modification [16] of all of the beta-crystallins and has been hypothesized to be crucial to maintain the solubility of other crystallins in the adult lens. It has been previously reported that betaB2-crystallin is expressed in many extra-lenticular sites including the retina, brain, and testis [9]. Recently, betaB2-crystallin was shown to be localized in retinal ganglion cells (RGCs) in the retina [17] and the protein was reported to be secreted, then internalized by RGCs to promotes neurite outgrowth during retinal regeneration [18].
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 169 173 166 170 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">phil</sup>
###xml 163 173 160 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">crybb2<sup>phil</sup></italic>
###xml 258 260 255 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 261 263 258 260 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 391 395 388 392 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">phil</sup>
###xml 385 395 382 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">crybb2<sup>phil</sup></italic>
###xml 626 630 623 627 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">phil</sup>
###xml 620 630 617 627 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">crybb2<sup>phil</sup></italic>
###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 396 400 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 631 635 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Mice exhibiting autosomal dominant cataracts caused by a 12 nucleotide in frame deletion removing four amino acids from the fourth Greek key of betaB2-crystallin (crybb2phil) arose spontaneously in an outbred Swiss Webster breeding colony in the late 1970s [19,20] and were maintained as homozygotes for many years in a small breeding colony until being cryopreserved. Once recovered, crybb2phil mice were found to be poor breeders and here we show that both males and females are subfertile as compared to Swiss Webster outbreds due to defective sperm and egg production. However, subfertility was not observed in male crybb2phil mice backcrossed onto a C57Bl/6NHsd genetic background suggesting that the poor reproductive performance of the original mutants is either strain dependent or due to inbreeding depression of the founding outbred Swiss Webster stock.
###end p 15

Homozygous STOCK Crybb2Phil mice on a Swiss Webster derived background were obtained from the National Institute of Health, Animal Genetics Resource, Veterinary Resources Program (Bethesda, MD). This yields a product of 132 nucleotides from the Crybb2+ allele and one of 120 nucleotides from the Crybb2Phil allele which resolve well by electrophoresis on 10% polyacrylamide gels.

###xml 746 750 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 934 938 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 996 1001 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
###xml 1069 1073 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1118 1122 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
All mice were maintained and bred in the University of Delaware animal facility under a 14/10 h light/dark cycle. Quarterly serological monitoring is conducted in every animal room on sentinel animals for Sendai, mouse hepatitis, pneumonia, minute, polyoma, epizootic diarrhea, pneumonitis, mouse parvo, ectromelia, reovirus, and lymphocytic choriomeningitis viruses as well as Mycoplasma species and Helicobacter using the Charles River Laboratories Health Monitoring Service (Wilmington, MA). The colony has tested negative for all of these pathogens for over the past six years. Monitoring for internal and external parasites is also performed and environmental monitoring of walls and floors for bacterial contamination is completed monthly. Mice were housed in open top cages (Ancare; Belmore, NY) with Beta-chip bedding (Harwood Laboratory Bedding Northeastern Products; Warrenburg, NY) changed at least once per week. Breeding mice were fed a high-energy, 19% fat variety of LabDiet, 5015 Mouse Diet (PMI Nutrition International, Richmond, IN) while nonbreeding mice were fed LabDiet, 5P00 Prolab RMH 3000. All mice are provided filtered drinking water ad libitum.
###end p 20
###begin title 21
Fertility testing
###end title 21
###begin p 22
Fertility was assessed by setting up natural matings between a single male and female and recording the number of litters and pups born to each mating over a three month period. Fecundity was calculated as the total number of pups produced per mating per 30 day period. The data was analyzed statistically by unpaired Student's t-test, and subfertility was considered to be significant when p is <0.05.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
Assessment of testis and ovary
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 36 40 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Testes and ovaries from age-matched mice were dissected and their gross anatomy observed under a Zeiss Stemi SV II Apo dissecting microscope fitted with a Nikon 4500 camera. Tissue weights were determined and the tissues fixed in Bouin's overnight, then transferred to 70% ethanol until embedment in paraffin. Six mum paraffin sections were created, then stained with hematoxylin and eosin using standard methods. Tissue morphology was observed under a light microscope, photographed, and transferred to Adobe Photoshop for comparative morphometric analysis. Statistical differences were determined by an unpaired Student's t-test.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Sperm counts and motility assays
###end title 25
###begin p 26
Cauda epididymides were minced in 2 ml of modified BWW medium (Irvine Scientific, Santa Ana, CA) with 3% BSA and sperm were allowed to swim out for ten min. For sperm counts, 50 mul of the sperm suspension was mixed with an equal volume of 2% paraformaldehyde and sperm counts assessed with a hemacytometer. For motility assays, 10 mul of the sperm suspension (or a 1:10 dilution of the suspension) was loaded on to the hemacytometer and the motility of 200 sperm assessed per sample. The data was analyzed statistically by an unpaired Student's t-test, and reduced sperm counts and motility were considered to be significant when p is <0.05.
###end p 26
###begin title 27
Apoptosis detection
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 66 70 66 70 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Phil</sup>
###xml 60 70 60 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Crybb2<sup>Phil</sup></italic>
###xml 89 93 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 183 188 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
###xml 414 425 <span type="species:ncbi:3704">horseradish</span>
Apoptotic activity in the testis was compared between STOCK Crybb2Phil and Swiss-Webster mice using an Apoptag Peroxidase Detection Kit S7100 with minor adjustments in staining time. Mouse testes were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for one h, transferred to 70% ethanol, embedded in paraffin, and sectioned. The slides were deparaffinized and digested with proteinase K, labeled with TdT-enzyme and anti-digoxigenin-horseradish peroxidase conjugate, stained with diaminobenzidine for 10 min, and counterstained with methyl green for 7 min. After dehydration through 100% N-butanol and citrus-based cleaning solvent, a drop of Cytoseal 60 (Electron Microscopy Sciences; Fort Washington, PA) was added and the slides coverslipped. Slides were observed under a light microscope and the number of apoptotic nuclei per seminiferous tubule recorded.
###end p 28
###begin title 29
RNA analysis
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 52 56 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Total RNA was prepared from testes of Swiss-Webster mice at various developmental stages and adult lens using Promega's SV Total RNA Isolation System, according to the manufacturer's instructions. The forward primer (5'-TCT GAG GCC CAT CAA AGT GGA CAG CC 3') was designed over the splice site between exon 3 and exon 4 of the betaB2-crystallin sequence and the reverse primer (5'-ACG CAC GGA AGA CAC CTT TTC CTG GTA 3') is the reverse complement of position 430-455 of the betaB2-crystallin sequence, yielding a product size of 147 bp. RT-PCR reactions were performed using the One-Step RT-PCR System (Qiagen) with denaturation at 94 degreesC for 15 min, followed by 35 cycles of 94 degreesC for 30 s, 66 degreesC for 30 s, and 72 degreesC for 1 min, and final extension at 72 degreesC for 10 min. The RT-PCR products were electrophoretically separated on a 5% acrylamide gel and stained with ethidium bromide.
###end p 30
###begin title 31
Preparation of tissue extracts for SDS-PAGE and western blot analysis
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 43 47 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Tissues were excised from euthanized adult mice and homogenized in 300 mul of RIPA buffer (285 mul of 1X PBS, 1% Igepal CA-630, 0.5% sodium deoxycholate, 0.1% SDS with 3 mul of 100 mu g/ml PMSF, 9 mul of 45 mug/ml aprotinin, and 3 mul of 1 mM sodium orthovanadate). The homogenates were cleared by centrifugation at 10,000x G for 10 min at 4 degreesC. The supernatant was then removed, the protein concentration determined by the DC Protein Assay (Bio-Rad, Hercules CA) and stored at -20 degreesC prior to analysis.
###end p 32
###begin title 33
Preparation of sperm for SDS-PAGE
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 67 69 67 69 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 173 177 173 177 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Phil</sup>
###xml 178 182 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 434 439 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 530 535 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 604 609 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 760 766 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">Bovine</span>
Protein extracts from sperm were prepared as previously described [21]. Briefly, minced epididymides from age matched, sexual mature male Swiss-Webster and homozygous Crybb2Phil mice, were incubated separately with gentle agitation at room temperature in 2 ml of sperm suspension buffer (50 mM Tris, 20 mM EDTA, 1 mM p-hydroxy-mercurobenzenzoate, 5 mM N-ethylmaleimide, 1 mM benzamidine, pH 7.2) for 10 min to disperse the sperm. The mouse sperm suspension was then centrifuged at 1,000 rpm for one min to pellet the tissues, and mouse sperm was collected by centrifuging at 2,500-3,000 rpm for ten min. Human sperm was provided from retired donors as a gift from the Andrology/Embryology Laboratory of the Oregon Health and Science University (Portland, OR). Bovine sperm was purchased from ABS Global (Deforest, WI).
###end p 34
###begin title 35
SDS-PAGE and western blot
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 160 161 160 161 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>

Mice homozygous for the Crybb2Phil mutation were recovered from cryopreserved stocks held by the Animal Genetics Resource, National Institutes of Health, and found to be poor breeders. ###begin title 55
Expression of betaB2-crystallin in the testis and sperm
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 78 79 75 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 506 508 497 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>

###xml 781 790 766 775 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f2">Figure 2B</xref>
###xml 66 69 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Previous studies have purified betaB2-crystallin protein from the rat testis [9], although neither the developmental expression profile nor the cellular distribution of this protein was reported. By immunohistochemistry using a polyclonal antibody raised against betaB2-crystallin, we detected betaB2-crystallin immunoreactivity in the basal cell layer of some but not all seminiferous tubules in cells that have the round nuclei and scant cytoplasm diagnostic of the leptotene and zygotene spermatocytes [22] (Figure 2A,B) while betaB2-crystallin was only weakly detected in basal cells with ovoid nuclei characteristic of spermatogonia (Figure 2B). betaB2-crystallin immunoreactivity is also observed in Golgi/rounded acrosomal vesicles in contact with the nuclei of spermatids (Figure 2B), in addition to the head of some but not all maturing sperm (data not shown).

RNA samples were prepared from day 1, 3, 8, 12, 15, 17, and adult Swiss-Webster testis and analyzed by semi-quantitative RT-PCR using betaB2-crystallin-specific primers. betaB2-crystallin transcripts were detected in the testis from birth throughout postnatal life (Figure 2C). Ovaries were harvested from Swiss-Webster and STOCK Crybb2Phil homozygous mice and viewed under a dissecting microscope revealing an evident difference in ovary size (Figure 3A,B). Further, quantitative determination of ovary weights demonstrated that the STOCK Crybb2Phil homozygotes have a 70% reduction in ovarian mass. Mean ovarian weight for wild type mice (N=4) was 9.7 mg+/-2.1 mg while Crybb2Phil homozygotes (N=4) averaged 2.9+/-1.9 mg, p less than or equal to 0.0001. Histological analysis indicated that this reduction in ovarian size was due to a reduction in the number of developing follicles (Figure 3C,D). Swiss-Webster mice produced an average of 25.5 eggs (N=4) while STOCK Crybb2Phil homozygous mice produced an average of 1.6 eggs (N=5), p less than or equal to 0.0006. Both rt-PCR and western blotting detected betaB2-crystallin expression in the adult ovary (data not shown), although attempts to localize this protein in the ovary were inconclusive.

While the current data indicate that betaB2-crystallin plays a role in male and female fertility, there is still the possibility that the subfertility observed in STOCK Crybb2phil mice arose from inbreeding depression occurring over the 20-30 years these animals were isolated from the original outbred Swiss Webster line or from a new mutation in this strain. Male mice on the C57Bl/6NHsd background either wildtype or homozygous for the Crybb2Phil mutation were then mated to ICR females and their reproductive performance assessed over a three month period (Table 5). ###begin title 66
Discussion
###end title 66
###begin p 67
###xml 102 103 99 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 573 575 564 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 635 637 626 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
###xml 735 737 723 725 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 791 793 779 781 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 426 430 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Previous studies have demonstrated that betaB2-crystallin mRNA and protein are present in the testis [9], although its cellular distribution and function were not described. Here we demonstrate that betaB2-crystallin is a component of mammalian sperm and is present in developing sperm from the spermatocyte stage onward. In order to identify the function of betaB2-crystallin in the testis, we then assessed the fecundity of mice harboring an in-frame 12 nucleotide deletion which would be expected to disrupt the structure of the fourth "Greek key" motif of the protein [19] which is necessary for its normal association properties [24]. In lenses, this mutation has been demonstrated to result in insolubility of betaB2-crystallin [25] and the development of autosomal dominant cataract [20].
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 28 32 28 32 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Phil</sup>
###xml 22 32 22 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Crybb2<sup>Phil</sup></italic>
###xml 150 154 150 154 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Phil</sup>
###xml 144 154 144 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Crybb2<sup>Phil</sup></italic>
###xml 695 697 686 688 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r26">26</xref>
###xml 753 755 744 746 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r27">27</xref>
###xml 935 939 923 927 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Phil</sup>
###xml 929 939 917 927 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Crybb2<sup>Phil</sup></italic>
###xml 33 37 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 155 159 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 940 944 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Fertility analysis of Crybb2Phil mice on the original Swiss Webster derived genetic background demonstrated the both male and female homozygous Crybb2Phil mice are subfertile, suggesting that betaB2-crystallin plays an important role in testis and ovary function. In the testis, the fertility defect was traced to reductions in both epidydimal sperm counts resulting from increased apoptosis of developing sperm and sperm motility. Interestingly, it was recently reported that betaB2-crystallin copurifies with microtubules isolated from lens epithelial cells, leading to the hypothesis that betaB2-crystallin is a microtubule associated protein that may protect microtubules from denaturation [26]. Since microtubules are essential for sperm motility [27], this may suggest a functional connection between mutation of betaB2-crystallin and infertility. In the ovary, the number of developing follicles was reduced in homozygous Crybb2Phil mice, resulting in drastic reductions in the number of eggs produced in response to superovulation. Overall, these data suggest an important role for betaB2-crystallin in the maturation of both eggs and sperm.
###end p 68

###xml 88 92 88 92 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Phil</sup>
###xml 82 92 82 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Crybb2<sup>Phil</sup></italic>

###xml 93 97 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 220 224 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>

###xml 902 909 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbits</span>

###xml 1060 1064 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1305 1309 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In contrast, no defects were observed in either the testis or ovary of homozygous Crybb2Phil mice on the C57Bl/6NHsd genetic background (data not shown). Further, no reductions in fecundity were noted in homozygous male mice on this strain. There are at least three possible explanations for these conflicting results. First, the Crybb2Phil mutation arose spontaneously in a breeding colony of outbred Swiss-Webster mice [20] and was then housed for over 20 years in a small breeding colony with no attempt to maintain the original genetic diversity of the strain. Although no directed attempts at inbreeding were made, it is very likely that the genetic diversity found in the founder colony was largely lost during this husbandry period. Since inbreeding has been associated with impaired reproductive fitness due to reductions in testicular mass, sperm concentration, and sperm quality in both wild rabbits and old field mice [28,29], inbreeding of the founding Crybb2Phil population is a plausible explanation for the reduced fecundity of STOCK Crybb2Phil mice in comparison to outbred Swiss Webster controls. Second, cryopreservation of STOCK Crybb2Phil embryos may have caused a de novo mutation in a recessive gene required for fertility. In these cases, the subfertility noted in STOCK Crybb2Phil mice is unrelated to the presence of the Crybb2Phil mutation and instead results from either a new independent mutation affecting fecundity or the loss of heterozygosity at numerous loci as has been observed in endangered wildlife [30]. Conversely, it is possible that betaB2-crystallin is important for sperm and egg production but the C57Bl/Hsd strain harbors a genetic locus able to suppress the effect of this mutation.

###xml 601 603 595 597 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 604 606 598 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r31">31</xref>
###xml 607 609 601 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r35">35</xref>
###xml 274 278 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 502 506 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 511 517 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 825 829 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Overall, this study has demonstrated that betaB2-crystallin protein is present in mature mammalian sperm and its expression pattern in the testis is consistent with a role in sperm development. We have also demonstrated that both male and female homozygous STOCK Crybb2Phil mice are subfertile due to defects in sperm and egg production, although C57Bl/6NHsd-Crybb2Phil homozygotes have apparently normal gonads. It is interesting to note that all known mutations in the betaB2-crystallin gene in both mice and humans disrupt only the fourth Greek key of the protein and result in dominant cataracts [19,31-35]. Since no complete null mutation in the betaB2-crystallin gene has ever been identified, definitive elucidation of the extralenticular function of betaB2-crystallin will await characterization of targeted knockout mice.
