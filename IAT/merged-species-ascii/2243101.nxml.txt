###begin article-title 0
Crystallization of quinoprotein glucose dehydrogenase variants and homologues by microseeding
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
This is an open-access article distributed under the terms described at http://journals.iucr.org/services/termsofuse.html.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 88 95 88 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 100 113 100 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. coelicolor</italic>
###xml 88 95 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 100 113 <span type="species:ncbi:1902">S. coelicolor</span>
Three mutants of the soluble quinoprotein glucose dehydrogenase and two homologues from E. coli and S. coelicolor have been crystallized using an efficient microseeding method.
###end p 2
###begin p 3
###xml 361 377 361 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 382 405 382 405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Streptomyces coelicolor</italic>
###xml 361 377 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 382 405 <span type="species:ncbi:1902">Streptomyces coelicolor</span>
The soluble quinoprotein glucose dehydrogenase oxidizes glucose, maltose and a variety of other monosaccharides and disaccharides to the corresponding lactones. An efficient microseeding protocol is reported to produce crystals of three variants that display reduced activity towards maltose. Similar cross-seeding protocols to grow crystals of homologues from Escherichia coli and Streptomyces coelicolor are described.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Introduction
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 343 345 343 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb12"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 395 397 395 397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb4"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 485 487 485 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb2"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 510 512 510 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb5"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 521 527 521 527 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 533 535 533 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb16"> &#9654;</xref>
Quinoproteins constitute a class of proteins that use one of five different quinone-containing cofactors for the two-electron oxidation of alcohols and amines. Quinoproteins containing pyrroloquinoline quinone (PQQ) are present in a variety of different prokaryotes, including Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria and archaea (Oubrie, 2003 ▶), but the alleged presence (Kasahara & Kato, 2003 ▶) of PQQ-dependent dehydrogenases in eukaryotes is controversial (Felton & Anthony, 2005 ▶; Kasahara & Kato, 2005 ▶; Rucker et al., 2005 ▶).
###end p 5

###xml 1307 1309 1304 1306 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb15"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 1405 1411 1402 1408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1417 1419 1414 1416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb15"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 1488 1490 1485 1487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb13"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 1648 1654 1645 1651 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1660 1662 1657 1659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb15"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 88 115 <span type="species:ncbi:471">Acinetobacter calcoaceticus</span>
The PQQ-dependent soluble glucose dehydrogenase (sGDH) from the Gram-negative bacterium Acinetobacter calcoaceticus is the prototype of a subclass of enzymes with a six-bladed beta-propeller structure (Oubrie, 2003 ▶) and the only protein of its class for which a detailed biochemical characterization has been carried out. The enzyme functions as a homodimer of 100 kDa (454 amino acids per monomer after cleavage of the signal peptide that directs it to the periplasm) and binds three calcium ions and one PQQ per subunit (Olsthoorn & Duine, 1996 ▶; Olsthoorn et al., 1997 ▶; Oubrie, Rozeboom, Kalk, Duine et al., 1999 ▶; Oubrie, Rozeboom, Kalk, Olsthoon et al., 1999 ▶). The sGDH catalyzes the conversion of monosaccharides and disaccharides as well as the trisaccharide maltotriose into their corresponding lactones (Olsthoorn & Duine, 1998 ▶; Igarashi et al., 2004 ▶). The kinetic mechanism of the reductive half-reaction includes an initial reversible fast step leading to formation of a fluorescing intermediate followed by an irreversible and rate-limiting step (Olsthoorn & Duine, 1998 ▶). Crystal structures have been determined of the sGDH in different states, i.e. in the apo form (Oubrie, Rozeboom, Kalk, Duine et al., 1999 ▶), in complex with PQQ (Oubrie, Rozeboom, Kalk, Olsthoon et al., 1999 ▶) and in ternary complexes with PQQ and the substrate glucose (Oubrie, Rozeboom, Kalk, Olsthoon et al., 1999 ▶) or the inhibitor methylhydrazine (Oubrie, Rozeboom & Dijkstra, 1999 ▶). The combined structural and biochemical data are consistent with a catalytic mechanism involving direct hydride transfer (Oubrie, Rozeboom, Kalk, Olsthoon et al., 1999 ▶).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 350 356 350 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 362 364 362 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb17"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 711 727 711 727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 753 776 753 776 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Streptomyces coelicolor</italic>
###xml 711 731 <span type="species:ncbi:83333">Escherichia coli K12</span>
###xml 753 776 <span type="species:ncbi:1902">Streptomyces coelicolor</span>
The enzyme has been implemented on test strips that are used by diabetics to monitor their blood glucose levels. Improvement of the substrate specificity of the sGDH could improve the reliability of these strips, as high levels of maltose in blood, only relevant in some specific clinical conditions, can interfere with the glucose diagnostics (Wens et al., 1998 ▶). For this purpose, three generations of sGDH variants with successively reduced activity towards maltose have been generated. Here, we describe an efficient and reproducible microseeding method that was used to grow crystals of each of the three variants. Using the same method, we also succeeded in growing crystals of the sGDH homologues from Escherichia coli K12 (Gene ID 945467) and Streptomyces coelicolor A32(2) (Gene ID 1100948).

Samples of wild-type and three variants of A. calcoaceticus sGDH were generously supplied by Roche Diagnostics (Penzberg, Germany) as a freeze-dried powder. The variants are called mutant A (T348G), mutant B (T348G, N428P) and mutant G (Y171G, E245D, M341V, T348G, N428P) (Kratzsch et al., 2002 ▶). Before use, samples were resuspended in a buffer solution containing 3 mM CaCl2, 120 mM NaCl, 10 mM HEPES pH 7.5. Methods to clone, express and purify the sGDH homologues from E. coli and S. coelicolor as well as a biochemical characterization of these proteins will be published elsewhere.

###begin title 11
Production of microseeds containing stock solutions
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 149 150 145 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</italic>
###xml 155 156 151 152 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 163 164 159 160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</italic>

###xml 416 417 408 409 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">0</sup>
###xml 453 454 445 446 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 504 511 496 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 516 529 508 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. coelicolor</italic>
###xml 623 624 615 616 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</italic>
###xml 504 511 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 516 529 <span type="species:ncbi:1902">S. coelicolor</span>
To obtain microseeds, a single crystal was transferred to an Eppendorf cup containing 5 microl of a stabilizing solution containing 30% PEG 6000, 3 mM CaCl2, 120 mM NaCl and 50 mM CHES pH 9.2. The crystal was crushed manually using a polypropylene pellet pestle (Sigma) while keeping the sample on ice. The sample was then diluted with the stabilizing solution to an end volume of 200 microl to obtain the 1/1 (or 100) stock. Serial dilutions up to 1/106 were produced and stored at 277 K. Microseeds of E. coli and S. coelicolor sGDH homologues were produced in the same way using stabilizing solutions buffered with 100 mM of either CHES pH 9.2 or HEPES pH 7.5.

###begin title 13
Microseeding
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 168 170 168 170 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 248 249 248 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</italic>
###xml 254 255 254 255 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>

###xml 878 879 866 867 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 886 887 874 875 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</italic>
###xml 899 900 887 888 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</italic>
###xml 718 725 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 730 743 <span type="species:ncbi:1902">S. coelicolor</span>
Crystallization conditions were screened using the hanging-drop vapour-diffusion method (Q Plate II, Hampton Research) at 289 K. A resuspended protein solution (5 mg ml-1) was mixed with an equal volume of precipitant solution (17-22% PEG 6000, 1 mM CaCl2, 120 mM NaCl, CHES pH 9.2). A total of eight drops of 2 microl of this mixture were placed on a cover slip. To seven of these drops, 1 microl of one of the seven seed-stock solutions (100 to 106 dilution series) was added, whilst 1 microl stabilizing solution was added to the last drop as a control to check for crystallization in the absence of microseeds. The drops were equilibrated against a 1 ml reservoir of precipitant solution. The sGDH homologues from E. coli and S. coelicolor were screened using wild-type seeds in the same way, but a precipitant solution buffered at a different pH (17-22% PEG 6000, 1 mM CaCl2, 120 mM NaCl, 100 mM HEPES pH 7.5) was also used.

Crystals of A. calcoaceticus sGDH variants were flash-frozen in a solution containing 20%(v/v) glycerol or ethylene glycol in addition to 30%(w/v) PEG 6000, 120 mM NaCl, 3 mM CaCl2, 100 mM CHES pH 9.2. Crystals of the E. coli sGDH homologue were cryocooled in a solution containing 20%(v/v) ethylene glycol in addition to 30%(w/v) PEG 6000, 120 mM NaCl, 3 mM CaCl2, 100 mM CHES pH 9.2, while crystals of the S. coelicolor homologue were frozen in a cryoprotectant containing 20%(v/v) glycerol in addition to 30%(w/v) PEG 6000, 120 mM NaCl, 3 mM CaCl2, 100 mM HEPES pH 7.5. Data sets were collected at station XRD1 of the ELETTRA synchrotron-radiation source in Trieste, Italy, at station 14.2 at the Synchrotron Light Source (SLS) in Daresbury, United Kingdom and at station BL-2 of the Protein Structure Factory at BESSY in Berlin, Germany. Data sets were processed and reduced using either the HKL2000 package (Otwinowski & Minor, 1997 ▶) or MOSFLM (Leslie, 1992 ▶) and programs from the CCP4 suite (Collaborative Computational Project, Number 4, 1994 ▶).

###begin title 17
Results and discussion
###end title 17
###begin title 18
Crystallization using microseeding
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 156 157 156 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</italic>
###xml 162 163 162 163 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 170 171 170 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</italic>
###xml 183 184 183 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</italic>
###xml 239 245 239 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 251 253 251 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb15"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 820 826 820 826 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 832 834 832 834 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb14"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 869 871 869 871 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bb13"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 1023 1039 1023 1039 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">A. calcoaceticus</italic>
###xml 1023 1039 <span type="species:ncbi:471">A. calcoaceticus</span>
Crystals of wild-type sGDH were previously obtained using the hanging-drop vapour-diffusion method with a reservoir solution containing 16-23% PEG 6000, 3 mM CaCl2, 120 mM NaCl, 100 mM Tris-glycine pH 9.2 (Oubrie, Rozeboom, Kalk, Olsthoon et al., 1999 ▶). These crystallization conditions were also screened for the sGDH variants, but results were erratic in terms of reproducibility and crystal morphology. A second drawback of these conditions is the presence of Tris, which for reasons that are only poorly understood prevents reconstitution of the holoenzyme in the crystals upon soaking with the PQQ cofactor. In order to obtain structural information on cofactor- and substrate-bound states, it has therefore been found necessary to transfer the crystals to a buffer containing CHES (Oubrie, Rozeboom, Kalk, Duine et al., 1999 ▶; Oubrie, Rozeboom & Dijkstra, 1999 ▶). Unfortunately, few crystals survive this treatment and crystallization in a buffer containing CHES has so far been unsuccessful, even with wild-type A. calcoaceticus protein.
###end p 19
###begin p 20
###xml 78 80 78 80 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig1"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 94 110 94 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">A. calcoaceticus</italic>
###xml 427 429 427 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 94 110 <span type="species:ncbi:471">A. calcoaceticus</span>
These drawbacks could be circumvented by using the microseeding method (Fig. 1 ▶). Initially, A. calcoaceticus wild-type sGDH seeds were used to obtain crystals of all three variants as well as the two homologues. In subsequent crystallization experiments, seeds of the target proteins themselves were used. The microseeding method facilitated efficient and reproducible crystal growth of all sGDH variants tried so far (Fig. 2 ▶). An additional advantage of the microseeding method in CHES- or HEPES-containing solutions is that no buffer exchanges are required to facilitate soaking with PQQ, which has made it more straightforward to obtain crystals of PQQ-bound enzyme forms.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 134 141 134 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E.&#160;coli</italic>
###xml 146 159 146 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. coelicolor</italic>
###xml 197 199 197 199 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2"> &#9654;</xref>
###xml 302 315 302 315 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. coelicolor</italic>
###xml 382 389 382 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 134 141 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 146 159 <span type="species:ncbi:1902">S. coelicolor</span>
###xml 302 315 <span type="species:ncbi:1902">S. coelicolor</span>
###xml 382 389 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Using this method, it has also been possible to reproducibly obtain crystals with a typical maximal size of 0.1 mm and 0.3 mm for the E. coli and S. coelicolor sGDH homologues, respectively (Fig. 2 ▶). In most cases, crystals were clearly visible after 3 h and were fully grown after a couple of days. S. coelicolor control drops did not produce crystals, whereas a small number of E. coli sGDH crystals grew in some control drops after several weeks.
###end p 21
