###begin article-title 0
###xml 2 7 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
A human RNA polymerase II subunit is encoded by a recently generated multigene family
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Background
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 27 32 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The sequences encoding the yeast RNA polymerase II (RPB) subunits are single copy genes.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Results
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 239 246 239 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 251 258 251 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b</italic>
###xml 525 531 518 524 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hPMS2L</italic>
###xml 616 623 609 616 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 628 629 621 622 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">b</italic>
###xml 845 850 834 839 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RPB11</italic>
###xml 41 46 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 659 664 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 775 780 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 827 833 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
While those characterized so far for the human (h) RPB are also unique, we show that hRPB subunit 11 (hRPB11) is encoded by a multigene family, mapping on chromosome 7 at loci p12, q11.23 and q22. We focused on two members of this family, hRPB11a and hRPB11b: the first encodes subunit hRPB11a, which represents the major RPB11 component of the mammalian RPB complex ; the second generates polypeptides hRPB11balpha and hRPB11bbeta through differential splicing of its transcript and shares homologies with components of the hPMS2L multigene family related to genes involved in mismatch-repair functions (MMR). Both hRPB11a and b genes are transcribed in all human tissues tested. Using an inter-species complementation assay, we show that only hRPB11balpha is functional in yeast. In marked contrast, we found that the unique murine homolog of RPB11 gene maps on chromosome 5 (band G), and encodes a single polypeptide which is identical to subunit hRPB11a.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Conclusions
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 9 16 9 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b</italic>
The type hRPB11b gene appears to result from recent genomic recombination events in the evolution of primates, involving sequence elements related to the MMR apparatus.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Background
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 191 192 191 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 193 194 193 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 519 520 519 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 521 522 521 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 588 589 588 589 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 590 592 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 107 112 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 126 131 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
###xml 226 231 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 304 309 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 385 390 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 433 438 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 577 582 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In eukaryotes, mRNAs are transcribed by RNA polymerase II (RPB). To date, most studies have focused on the yeast polymerases. Yeast RPB consists of 12 polypeptides ranging from 220 to 6 kDa [1-3]. Much less is known about the human (h) RPB, although the sequences encoding the subunits homologous to the yeast RPB have been determined. Complementation experiments have shown that many yeast subunits may be replaced in vivo by their human counterparts indicating a remarkable functional conservation through evolution [4-8]. This supports the view that the 3D structure of the yeast RPB [9,10] can most likely be extended to other eukaryotic nuclear RPB molecules.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 119 124 119 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB1</italic>
###xml 150 152 144 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 155 160 149 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB2</italic>
###xml 185 190 173 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB3</italic>
###xml 215 220 197 202 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB4</italic>
###xml 243 244 219 220 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 247 252 223 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB5</italic>
###xml 277 282 247 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB6</italic>
###xml 307 309 271 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 312 317 276 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB7</italic>
###xml 340 342 298 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 345 350 303 308 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB8</italic>
###xml 378 383 330 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB9</italic>
###xml 406 408 352 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 411 422 357 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB10&#945;</italic>
###xml 447 458 383 391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB10&#946;,</italic>
###xml 484 486 411 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 523 529 450 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11</italic>
###xml 47 52 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We have undertaken the characterisation of the human RPB subunits. All the subunit genes identified so far are unique: hRPB1 (Ac Ndegrees X74870-74) [11], hRPB2 (Ac Ndegrees AC068261), hRPB3 (Ac Ndegrees AC004382), hRPB4 (Ac Ndegrees U89387) [7], hRPB5 (Ac Ndegrees AC004151), hRPB6 (Ac Ndegrees AF006501) [12], hRPB7 (Ac Ndegrees U52427) [13], hRPB8 (Ac Ndegrees AJ252079-80), hRPB9 (Ac Ndegrees Z23102) [14], hRPB10alpha (Ac Ndegrees AJ252078), hRPB10beta, (Ac Ndegrees Z47728-29) [15]. The present report focuses on the hRPB11 gene which remained to be characterised.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 204 206 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 210 211 210 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 212 214 212 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 403 410 403 410 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 411 413 411 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 414 416 414 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 230 235 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 334 339 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 387 392 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
It has been shown in many systems that the RPB11 subunit is able to heterodimerize with RPB3, evoking the alpha dimer in bacteria that directs the assembly of the two largest subunits of the RPB complex [16-22,9,10]. We show that human homologs of RPB11 are encoded by a multi-gene family. We shall refer to the previously identified human gene and cDNA encoding a protein homologous to yeast RPB11, as hRPB11a[23-25]. We have characterised additional members of this family and discuss their properties.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
Results
###end title 11
###begin title 12
###xml 20 25 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Characterisation of human genomic sequences encoding RPB11-related proteins a and b
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 44 50 44 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11</italic>
###xml 72 79 72 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 170 172 170 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 173 175 173 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 184 185 184 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 128 133 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In addition to the previously characterised hRPB11 cDNA, referred to as hRPB11a in the present work, a series of highly related human cDNAs were found in the databases ([24,25], Table 1). We show that these cDNAs were transcribed from a family of genomic sequences.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 21 26 21 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RPB11</italic>
Accession numbers of RPB11 sequences
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 99 101 99 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 174 181 174 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 225 232 225 232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 273 281 273 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a.</italic>
###xml 362 369 362 369 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 376 378 376 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 386 387 386 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 496 497 496 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 595 596 595 596 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">b</italic>
###xml 598 605 598 605 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 610 611 610 611 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">b</italic>
###xml 661 663 661 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
The screening of our genomic DNA library yielded several clones. Analysis of lambda clone 27 (Fig. 1A), revealed four coding exons within a 5.5 kb DNA sequence that we named hRPB11a gene, according to their identity with the hRPB11a cDNA. Lambda clone 11 was distinct from hRPB11a. Three exons were identified by their strong homology with exons 1, 2 and 3 from hRPB11a (Fig. 1A, Table 1). The fourth exon was identified by comparing this genomic sequence with two cDNAs from the database (Table 1). This exon 4 sequence was specific to a subset of genomic sequences that we referred to as type b. hRPB11a and b genomic sequences diverged within intron 3 (Fig. 1A).
###end p 15

###begin title 17
###xml 25 32 25 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b</italic>
Differential splicing of hRPB11b transcripts
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 69 76 69 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b</italic>
###xml 157 169 157 165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b&#945;</italic>
###xml 174 186 170 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b&#946;,</italic>
###xml 206 208 199 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 216 217 209 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 327 343 320 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b&#946; into</italic>
We characterised two types of cDNAs from HeLa cells corresponding to hRPB11b transcripts and differing by the presence or absence of exon 3: they were named hRPB11balpha and hRPB11bbeta, respectively (Fig. 1B, Table 1). The absence of exon 3 switches the reading frame of exon 4, thereby extending the coding sequence (CDS) of hRPB11bbeta into an additional exon 5, identified in another genomic sequence (Ac Ndegrees AC004951).
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 57 58 57 58 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 125 132 125 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 137 138 137 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">b</italic>
###xml 272 283 272 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b&#946;</italic>
###xml 12 17 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Most of the human cDNAs and ESTs in the databases (Table 1) perfectly matched the cDNAs reconstituted from the exons of both hRPB11a and b genes, indicating that these sequences are transcribed in vivo. Exon 3 being present in all the genomic clones, we conclude that the hRPB11bbeta cDNA is produced by differential splicing resulting in exon 3 skipping.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
###xml 43 49 43 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11</italic>
Three types of proteins are encoded by the hRPB11 genes
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 4 11 4 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 191 193 191 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 273 285 273 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b&#945;</italic>
###xml 290 302 286 295 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b&#946;,</italic>
###xml 126 131 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The hRPB11a gene yields one type of mRNA that encodes the hRPB11a protein which was previously identified as a subunit of the human RPB complexes in Western-blots of immunoprecipitated RPB ([26] and our unpublished data). We have presently identified two additional cDNAs, hRPB11balpha and hRPB11bbeta, as distinct members of the same family.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 200 202 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1C</xref>
###xml 465 472 458 465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 602 604 591 593 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1C</xref>
Strikingly, as predicted from their sequences, the hRPB11a, balpha and bbeta polypeptides have similar sizes: 117, 115 and 116 residues, with calculated M.W. of 13.3, 13, 12.7 kDa, respectively (Fig. 1C). The N-terminal part of hRPB11a subunit differs only from the hRPB11b polypeptide by the presence of an additional Lys encoded at the junction between exons 1 and 2. By contrast, the C-terminal portions of these polypeptides differ drastically: while exon 4 of hRPB11a encodes a hydrophilic 11-residue peptide, it generates a rather hydrophobic 10-residue peptide in the case of hRPB11balpha (Fig. 1C); concerning hRPB11bbeta, due to exon 3 skipping, an unrelated peptide, rich in Pro (16%), Ala (14.5%), Gln (9%), His (9%) and Cys (7%) residues, is produced.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
###xml 0 6 0 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11</italic>
###xml 38 43 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
hRPB11 maps to three distinct loci on human chromosome 7
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 17 23 17 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11</italic>
###xml 144 151 144 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 162 164 162 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 187 194 187 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 199 200 199 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">b</italic>
We localised the hRPB11 genomic sequences on metaphasic chromosomes with a fluorescent genomic probe encompassing the conserved exons 1 to 3 of hRPB11a (see Fig. 1A), thus revealing both hRPB11a and b genomic sequences. 50 metaphases were analysed: 90 % showed specific signals on chromosome 7, at positions q11.23 and q22, and about 80% at position p12.
###end p 24

In marked contrast to the human system, a single locus is detected on the murine chromosome 5, at cytogenetic band G (Fig. 2D).

###begin title 40
Discussion
###end title 40
###begin title 41
A multigene family encodes the hRPB11 but not the mRPB11 subunit
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 98 105 98 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 215 217 215 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 253 270 253 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a, hRPB11b,</italic>
###xml 284 285 284 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">b</italic>
###xml 335 336 329 330 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</sup>
###xml 438 444 432 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11</italic>
###xml 500 506 494 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11</italic>
###xml 45 50 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 190 195 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 524 529 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our results demonstrate the existence in the human genome of a family of sequences related to the hRPB11a gene. Three distinct loci were detected using these genomic sequences as a probe on human chromosome 7 (Fig. 1A). Four distinct genomic sequences, hRPB11a, hRPB11b, and two type b-related sequences not described here (Ac Ndegreess AC004951 and AC004084), were identified. Quantitative PCR measurements of the genomic copy number of hRPB11 exon 3 suggested the presence of about twelve distinct hRPB11 sequences in the human haploid genome (not shown).
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 67 73 67 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mRPB11</italic>
###xml 201 203 201 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 204 206 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 56 61 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 180 185 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 229 235 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 421 426 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 431 451 <span type="species:ncbi:9534">african green monkey</span>
In sharp contrast, such a gene family does not exist in mouse. The mRPB11 gene is unique, maps to a unique locus at 5G which was previously identified as a region synthenic to the human locus 7q11.23 [27,28] and encodes a single murine mRPB11 protein identical to hRPB11a. The amplification of these genomic sequences may therefore represent a recent evolutionary event, that may be restricted to the primates, including human and african green monkey, as both RPB11 b-type mRNAs were present in COS-7 and CV1 cells (not shown).
###end p 43
###begin title 44
These genomic sequences yield stable mRNAs
###end title 44
###begin p 45
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 12 19 12 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b</italic>
###xml 174 175 174 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 337 338 337 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 345 357 345 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b&#945;</italic>
###xml 362 367 358 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">b&#946;</italic>
###xml 452 459 445 452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 70 75 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
hRPB11a and hRPB11b transcripts were detected as stable mRNAs from 16 human tissues with, in some cases, a clear expression specificity, as shown by both Northern-blot (Fig. 3) and RT-PCR experiments (not shown). This is further confirmed by the fact that they have also been isolated from cDNA libraries from various tissues (see Table 1). The hRPB11balpha and bbeta CDS result from a differential splicing mechanism which we have not observed in any hRPB11a transcript. It is tempting therefore to speculate that a selective pressure maintains both isoforms of hRPB11b messenger RNAs.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 121 123 121 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 92 97 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Using specific antibodies, the hRPB11a protein was readily detected in extracts from either human tissues or cell lines [19]. By contrast, the hRPB11balpha or beta proteins have not been detected so far, suggesting that their expression may be regulated at the translational level. We conclude that the hRPB11b proteins are either present at very low levels in these cells, or restricted to specific cell lines and/or situations that remain to be identified.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
The hRPB11 proteins exhibit distinctive properties
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 229 231 225 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 232 234 228 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 375 376 371 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 377 379 373 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 824 833 808 817 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bona fide</italic>
###xml 934 959 918 943 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</italic>
###xml 989 991 973 975 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 154 159 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 769 774 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 834 839 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 874 879 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 920 933 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 934 959 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
###xml 1041 1046 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Both hRPB11a and balpha proteins were found to contact exclusively hRPB3 in coexpression assays, consistent with previous results (see Introduction). The yeast ScRPB3/ScRPB11 heterodimer has been modelled as an alpha-like dimer [29,22], in which both C-terminal domains consist of two long alpha helices that cross each other and point toward the outside of the RPB complex [9,10]. The hRPB11balpha protein differs from hRPB11a at the very C-terminal end of this structure: its incorporation into the RPB complex instead of hRPB11a may therefore alter the interactions with the surrounding molecules. Despite this difference, both hRPB11a and balpha can indeed integrate the RPB complex in vivo. We show that hRPB11balpha is able to functionally replace ScRPB11 in the yeast RPB. Strikingly, the hRPB11a protein, known as a bona fide human RPB subunit, is not functional in yeast, whereas RPB11 of the distantly related fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe can replace ScRPB11 in vivo [30]. Why only hRPB11balpha protein is functional in yeast may be related to the fact that its C-terminal domain exhibits a higher homology to the one of ScRPB11, both being rather hydrophobic, than the hydrophilic C-terminal domain of hRPB11a. The hRPB11balpha protein may therefore be able to make, although weakly, critical contacts that the hRPB11a protein cannot make. These data point to a critical function of this C-terminal domain, that is encoded by a separate specific exon in mammals, in vivo.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 203 204 200 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 192 197 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The observation that the hRPB11bbeta protein exhibits a completely distinct set of interactions with the other RPB subunits is presently difficult to integrate into the available model of the yeast RPB [9]. It is possible that hRPB11bbeta establishes multiple but transient contacts with various subunits during theRPB assembly and that these interactions are revealed in our binary protein binding assay.
###end p 49

###xml 687 689 687 689 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 713 720 713 720 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b</italic>
###xml 823 829 823 829 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hPMS2L</italic>
###xml 1098 1100 1098 1100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 1101 1103 1101 1103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 246 251 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 291 297 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 519 524 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 996 1001 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1006 1010 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1254 1259 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1260 1268 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The b types of RPB11 genes may result from recombination events between a hRPB11a gene and at least two other genes, recruiting new exons 4 and 5, respectively. While the origin of exon 5 remains to be identified, exon 4 of hRPB11b is present in human PMS2L genes [31,32] that have no known murine homolog. Although the function of these hPMS2L genes is still elusive, they share five coding exons with the PMS2 gene (b to f, Fig. 6) which plays a critical role in the mismatch repair (MMR) machinery and is located on human chromosome 7p22 [32,33]. The hPMS2L and hRPB11 genes are located close to each other at positions 7p12, 7q11.23 and 7q22, supporting a recombinational origin [31,32]. The primate specific hRPB11b gene products may provide a new link between the transcription and MMR machineries, together with the hPMS2L gene products. Thus, it will be of interest to explore the potential contribution of this species-specific gene rearrangement to the phenotypical differences between human and mice mutants which, when affected in their MMR activity, exhibit different types of tumors [34,35]. Because of the presence of these primate-specific variants, drugs which are often tested in rodents may be mis-evaluated regarding their effects on human patients. The present findings indicate that more surprises may arise from studies of fundamental cellular processes, even in closely related species.
###end p 51
###begin title 52
Conclusions
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 68 75 68 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11a</italic>
###xml 210 216 210 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mRPB11</italic>
###xml 311 318 311 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hRPB11b</italic>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 166 172 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
The human genome contains a family of genes that includes the gene (hRPB11a) encoding subunit 11 of the hRPB complex. Strikingly, such a family does not exist in the murine genome which contains a unique gene (mRPB11) encoding a protein which is identical to hRPB11a. Our observations strongly suggest that the hRPB11b genes have been engineered by evolution in the primate genomes to produce proteins with novel properties, required only under specific circumstances, the nature and role of which remain to be identified.
###end p 53
###begin title 54
Materials and methods
###end title 54
###begin title 55
Cloning of genomic sequences
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 0 3 0 3 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mbo</italic>
###xml 66 69 66 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bam</italic>
###xml 167 168 167 168 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sup>
###xml 259 261 259 261 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>
###xml 272 275 272 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Nhe</italic>
###xml 277 280 277 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Spe</italic>
###xml 327 328 327 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 601 604 601 604 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sau</italic>
###xml 636 639 636 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bam</italic>
###xml 723 724 723 724 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 128 135 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 208 213 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
MboI partially-digested placenta DNA was inserted into the unique BamHI site of lambda GEM12, yielding, after transformation of E. coli TAP90, a library of about 1.2106 independent phages, equivalent to five human genomes. This library was screened using the 32P-labelled NheI-SpeI fragment from pBSK-hRPB11a as a probe (Table 2). One hundred positive phages were isolated and characterised by Southern blot analysis indicating the existence of several distinct restriction profiles (data not shown). For further sequence analysis, the DNA inserts of two phages, 27 and 11, were partially digested by Sau3AI and subcloned in the unique BamHI site of pBSK yielding pBSK-hRPB11a-gen and pBSK-hRPB11b-gen, respectively (Table 2). Alternatively, DNA fragments were directly sequenced after PCR amplification from several phages.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
Strains and plasmids
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 128 129 128 129 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sup>
###xml 195 196 195 196 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sup>
###xml 591 594 591 594 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Not</italic>
###xml 642 645 642 645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Not</italic>
###xml 728 729 728 729 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 2 7 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 62 67 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 167 173 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 245 250 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
A mouse SV129 D3 genomic library was similarly generated from mouse ES cells in lambda GEM12, yielding a library of about 2.5 106 independent phages, equivalent to 10 murine genomes. About 1.2 106 clones were screened as described above for the human genomic library. 26 positive clones were obtained. A Southern-blot analysis was performed on 12 independent clones (not shown) that revealed an identical restriction pattern indicating that they corresponded to a unique gene sequence. For further sequence analysis, the DNA inserts of two independent phages were excised using the flanking NotI restriction sites and subcloned in the unique NotI site of pBSK yielding pBSK-mRPB11-gen1 and pBSK-mRPB11-gen2, respectively (Table 2). Both of these genomic sequences were identical to the sequence that is present in the database (Ac Ndegrees AC087420).
###end p 58
###begin title 59
cDNA cloning
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 429 430 429 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
The cDNA fragments were amplified by RT-PCR from total HeLa cell RNA using the appropriate primers and inserted in either pBSK or PCRII vectors. In each case, unique restriction sites were introduced in front of the ATG and after the stop codons. Several independent clones of each cDNA were sequenced. Restriction fragments spanning the complete coding sequences (CDS) were then transferred to various expression vectors (Table 2).
