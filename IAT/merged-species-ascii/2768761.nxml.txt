Abundant L-type calcium channel Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit mRNA is detected in rod photoreceptors of the mouse retina via in situ hybridization
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Purpose
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 17 24 17 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CACNA1F</italic>
###xml 81 82 81 82 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 361 362 357 358 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 464 465 456 457 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 787 788 775 776 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 107 113 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 817 822 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Mutations in the CACNA1F gene encoding the L-type calcium channel pore-forming Cav1.4 (alpha1F) subunit in humans result in an incomplete form of congenital stationary night blindness (CSNB2) with residual photoreceptor function. It has been postulated that this residual function, at least in part, may be mediated by another L-type calcium channel subunit, Cav1.3 (alpha1D), expressed within cone photoreceptors. However, the expression of the calcium channel Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit within photoreceptors remains debatable due to discrepancies among the immunohistochemical studies reported in the literature. In order to get around the innate complications of utilizing unproven antibodies and to shed light on this discussion, we investigated the mRNA expression profile for the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit in the mouse retina.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Methods
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 130 131 130 131 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 259 260 255 256 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 332 333 324 325 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 49 54 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 395 400 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
In situ hybridization was performed on wild type mouse retinal sections with two independent sets of digoxigenin-11-UTP-labeled Cav1.3 (alpha1D)-specific sense and antisense cRNA probes. The two probe sets employed correspond to two distinct regions of the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit mRNA, each encoding a different fragment of the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) polypeptide. In situ hybridization of wild type mouse brain sections with these same probes was performed as an additional control for specificity.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Results
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 34 35 34 35 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 152 153 148 149 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 246 247 238 239 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 507 508 495 496 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 623 628 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Abundant L-type calcium channel Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit mRNA expression was confirmed in most cells of the outer nuclear layer using two independent Cav1.3 (alpha1D)-specific antisense cRNA probes, confirming expression in rod photoreceptors. Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA expression was also observed within most cells of the inner nuclear layer and ganglion cell layers using these same antisense cRNA probes. No labeling of tissue was observed using either sense cRNA probe. In situ detection of concentrated Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA expression within the hippocampus and Purkinje and granule cells of the cerebellum of wild type mouse brain with these same probes confirmed specificity of the probes.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Conclusions
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 58 59 58 59 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 288 289 284 285 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 405 406 397 398 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 532 533 520 521 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 577 578 561 562 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 702 703 682 683 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 759 760 735 736 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 828 835 800 807 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cacna1f</italic>
###xml 999 1000 971 972 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 1307 1308 1275 1276 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 262 266 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 319 325 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 517 521 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 843 847 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 851 857 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Our finding of expression of the L-type calcium channel Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit mRNA in rods substantiates the possibility that this pore-forming subunit may be a competent component of channels mediating the residual photoreceptor responses observed in mutant mice lacking functional Cav1.4 (alpha1F) subunits and in humans with CSNB2. Furthermore, the combined observations of abundant expression of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA in wild type rods and the large reduction in the transmission of photoreceptor responses in mice lacking Cav1.4 (alpha1F) raises the possibility that Cav1.3 (alpha1D) protein expression levels, localization, or functioning might be concomitantly altered by disruption of the Cav1.4 (alpha1F) subunit in rods. To date, no studies of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA nor protein expression levels or localization in cacna1f mutant mice or humans with CSNB2 have been published. Our findings warrant such studies to address the abovementioned possibilities. Finally, the observation of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA expression in multiple retinal cell types suggests the potential for a broader role for this L-type calcium channel subunit in overall functioning of the normal retina than previously appreciated. We therefore suggest that lesions in either the gene encoding the L-type calcium channel Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit or other molecules that interact with and regulate it may underlie one or more retinopathies with currently unidentified molecular etiologies.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Introduction
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 375 376 375 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 443 444 443 444 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 609 610 605 606 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 611 612 607 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 733 734 729 730 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 735 736 731 732 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 876 883 872 879 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cacna1f</italic>
###xml 903 904 899 900 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 937 944 933 940 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cacna1f</italic>
###xml 959 963 955 959 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nob2</italic>
###xml 965 966 961 962 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 115 121 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 396 401 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 745 748 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 847 862 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 952 957 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Incomplete X-linked congenital stationary night blindness (CSNB2) is a recessive, nonprogressive visual disease in humans. CSNB2 was originally described as a distinct form of night blindness characterized by an extremely reduced (negative type) electroretinogram (ERG) b wave due to impaired transduction of the photoreceptor response to second order neurons of the retina [1]. Mutations in the human CACNA1F gene encoding the pore-forming Cav1.4 (alpha1F) subunit of voltage gated L-type calcium channels have been firmly linked to CSNB2 and are thought to be the underlying molecular lesion causing CSNB2 [2,3]. The immunohistochemical localization of this subunit to putative active zones of the terminals of rod photoreceptors [4,5] of the rat retina supports this notion. The recently reported histological and ERG abnormalities observed in transgenic mice in which the cacna1f gene is disrupted [6] and in the naturally occurring cacna1f mutant mouse, nob2 [7], add further support for this hypothesis.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 43 44 43 44 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 109 113 109 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nob2</italic>
###xml 370 371 370 371 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 436 437 432 433 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 576 577 568 569 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 635 636 623 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 637 639 625 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 670 671 658 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 114 118 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 123 129 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 481 486 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 616 626 <span type="species:ncbi:9395">tree shrew</span>
In a recent communication, Morgans et al. [8] suggested that the residual photoreceptor function observed in nob2 mice and humans with CSNB2 may be the result of rod signals transferred to cones through rod-cone coupling and subsequent forwarding via neurotransmitter release from the cone synapse controlled by another L-type calcium channel subunit isoform, such as Cav1.3 (alpha1D). In this same report, Morgans et al. demonstrate Cav1.3 (alpha1D) immunoreactivity in wild type mouse cone pedicles, consistent with previously reported immunohistochemical localization of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) to cone terminals within tree shrew retina [9,10]. In this same communication [8], however, Morgans et al. acknowledge a second and more parsimonious explanation: the presence of another calcium channel alpha1 subunit isomer within rod photoreceptors that may mediate neurotransmitter release from the terminals of rods.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 71 72 71 72 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 151 152 147 148 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 255 257 247 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 364 365 356 357 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 421 422 409 410 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 461 463 449 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 758 759 746 747 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 819 820 803 804 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 902 903 882 883 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 1072 1079 1048 1055 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">cacna1d</italic>
###xml 1100 1101 1076 1077 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 1233 1234 1205 1206 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 1597 1598 1565 1566 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 89 94 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 243 246 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 433 438 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 474 477 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1035 1039 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1259 1264 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1628 1633 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Although Morgans' immunohistochemical data support the expression of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) in mouse cones and not rods, Xu et al., using a different anti-Cav1.3 (alpha1D) antibody, reported intense immunohistochemical labeling of rod somata in the rat retina [11]. There are 2 potential explanations for the discrepancy in these reports regarding the localization of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) within photoreceptors. As Morgans et al. [8] examined mouse retina and Xu et al. [11] examined rat retina, the difference may simply reflect a species variation in calcium channel subunit expression. This difference in results could also be the consequence of using different antibodies. As the antibodies used in the two studies were generated against distinct regions of the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit, they may actually detect different Cav1.3 (alpha1D) polypeptides translated from different mRNA spice variants of the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) gene. Such reliance upon antibodies, the specificity of which has not been rigorously demonstrated using tissues from mice with a targeted deletion of the cacna1d gene encoding the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit, is a limitation of both studies. For this reason, we decided to investigate the mRNA expression profile of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) within the mouse retina using in situ hybridization, with the goal of enlightening the discussion of its potential contribution to forwarding of light signals from rod photoreceptors to second order neurons within the retina. Portions of this study have been previously reported in abstract form (Steele, EC Jr. Analysis of L-type calcium channel Cav1.3 subunit expression in the mouse retina. Invest Ophthalmol Vis Sci 2006; 47:E-Abstract 1046).
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Methods
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Animals
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 19 23 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Wild type C57Bl/6J mice were obtained from Jackson Lab for these studies. All animal procedures conformed to the humane treatment of animals as prescribed by the Institute for Laboratory Animal Research (Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals) and by the Association for Research in Vision and Ophthalmology (ARVO) and were approved by the Morehouse School of Medicine Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
In situ hybridization of retinal and brain sections
###end title 16
###begin p 17
Eyes and brains were immediately dissected from animals following euthanasia. Corneas and lenses were immediately dissected from freshly enucleated eyes. Eyecups were then fixed by immersion in 1X phosphate buffered saline (PBS) containing 4% paraformaldehde and diethylpyrocarbonate (DEPC) for 15 min at room temperature. Brains were transected along the midline and fixed by immersion in the same fixative overnight at 4 degreesC. Eyecups and brains were then rinsed 3 times for 5 min each at room temperature with 1X PBS containing DEPC before they were cryoprotected in 1X PBS containing 30% sucrose and DEPC at 4 degreesC overnight.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
Cryprotected eyes and brains were mounted in Neg50 (Richard Allen Scientific) and flash frozen in liquid nitrogen. A Microm HM 550 OMVP cryostat was then used to cut 14 mum sections, which were subsequently mounted onto Superfrost Plus coated slides (Fisher Scientific).
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 207 208 207 208 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 287 288 283 284 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 260 266 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
Total RNA was isolated from 2 wild type C57BL/6J retinae using Trizol Reagent (Invitrogen) according to the manufacturer's specifications and mixed. Two independent fragments of the L-type calcium channel Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit RNA were amplified from total murine retinal RNA using Cav1.3 (alpha1D)-specific oligos in conjunction with the One-Step RT-PCR amplification kit (Invitrogen). Probe A corresponded to nucleotides 189-715 of GenBank accession number NM_028981 and was amplified using the following oligos: sense, 5'-AGG CAA ACT ATG CAA GAG GCA CCA G-3' and antisense, 5'-ATT CCA TCC ATT CCT AAC GTA AGC-3'.
###end p 19
###begin p 20
Probe B corresponded to nucleotides 2486-2742 of GenBank accession number  and was amplified using the following oligos: sense, 5'-AAC AAA CCA GAA GTC AAC CAG ATA GCC-3' and antisense, 5'-GGG TTG GTC TTG CTA AGA ATG AAG-3'. These fragments were subcloned into the pCRII-TOPO vector using the TOPO TA Cloning Kit Dual Promoter (Invitrogen) and sequenced to confirm identity. These constructs were then linearized and used to generate antisense and sense probes with the SP6/T7 DIG RNA Labeling kit (Roche) and digoxigenin-11-UTP (Roche) according to the manufacturer's specifications. Probe labeling efficiency was assayed according to the manufacturer's specifications by preparing serial dilutions and spotting onto a nitrocellulose membrane and detecting with alkaline phosphatase-conjugated anti-DIG antibody. The resulting intensities were then used according to the labeling kit manufacturer's specifications to estimate labeled probe concentrations.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
Slides with freshly cut sections were air dried for 30 min at room temperature and then prehybridized for 2 h at room temperature in a humidified chamber with hybridization buffer composed as follows: 50% formamide (Sigma-Aldrich), 5X SSC, 5X Denhardt's solution (Invitrogen), 250 mug/ml MRE 600 tRNA (Roche), DEPC-treated water. Hybridization buffer containing about 500 ng /ml DIG-11-UTP-labeled RNA probe, which was preheated to 80 degreesC for 5 min and then placed on ice, was added to sections before covering with DEPC-treated coverslips and incubating overnight at 61 degreesC (Probe A) or 58 degreesC (Probe B) in a humidified chamber. For each probe pair, a range of hybridization temperatures 55 degreesC, 58 degreesC, and 61 degreesC was tested to empirically determine the hybridization temperature which yielded strong staining of tissues with antisense probe following a short colorometric detection reaction time but no staining with sense probe following the same colorometric detection reaction time.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 941 942 929 930 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 501 505 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
Following hybridization, slides were washed in 0.2X SSC 2 times for 30 min 1 h at 68 degreesC to remove unbound probe. Slides were then treated with RNase (10 mg/ml) in 0.5M NaCl, 10 mM Tris pH7.5, 5 mM EDTA for 30 min at 37 degreesC. After RNase treatment, slides were rinsed with 0.2X SSC for 5 min at room temperature, followed by a rinse in 0.1 M Tris pH7.5, 0.15 NaCl for 5 min at room temperature. Slides were then blocked in 0.1 M Tris pH7.5, 0.15 NaCl containing 1-10% heat inactivated normal goat serum for 1 h at room temperature. Slides were then incubated in this same blocking buffer containing alkaline phosphatase-conjugated anti-DIG antibody (diluted 1:400) for 2 h at room temperature. Following incubation with antibody, slides were rinsed 3 times for 7 min with blocking buffer at room temperature to remove unbound antibody. Slides were then equilibrated with a buffer containing 0.1 M Tris pH 9.5, 0.1 M NaCl, 50 mM MgCl2 for 5 min at room temperature, before staining with BCIP/NBT in the dark (Vector Labs) according to the manufacturer's recommendations. The colorometric development reaction was stopped by rinsing with TE Buffer (10 mM Tris, 1 mM EDTA, pH 8.0), then with water.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
Stained slides were mounted with Vectashield mounting medium (Vector Labs) and images were acquired using a Zeiss Axioskop 2 plus overhead microscope equipped with a Zeiss Axiocam HRc CCD camera with AxioVision acquisition and analysis software. Microsoft Powerpoint and Adobe Photoshop were subsequently employed to assemble images into final figure format.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
Results
###end title 24
###begin title 25
###xml 97 98 97 98 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 25 31 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
In situ hybridization of murine retinal sections confirms expression of L-type calcium channel Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit in rod photoreceptors of the outer nuclear layer as well as the inner nuclear and ganglion cell layers
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 38 39 38 39 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 157 158 153 154 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 321 329 313 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 413 414 405 406 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 508 516 496 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 764 765 752 753 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 1051 1052 1035 1036 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 232 238 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
We utilized two independent sets of Cav1.3 (alpha1D)-specific cRNA probes, corresponding to mRNA encoding 2 distinct regions of the L-type calcium channel Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit, in our in situ hybridization analyses of wild type murine retinal sections. One set of sense and antisense probes, designated as Probe A in Figure 1, corresponds to a 5' fragment of the mRNA encoding an N-terminal fragment of the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit. The second set of sense and antisense probes, designated as Probe B in Figure 1, corresponds to an internal fragment of the mRNA encoding a fragment of the intracellular loop between the repeat domains II and III of the polypeptide. Probe B encompasses the peptide used as antigen to generate the commercially available anti-Cav1.3 (alpha1D) antibody employed in many of the previously published immunohistochemical studies of retina, making our mRNA results directly comparable to these studies. Because most, if not all, cells within each lamina of the retina appear to be expressing some Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA, the specificity of the probes was called into question. As shown in Figure 3A and Figure 3B, we observed a virtually identical concentrated expression of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) within subsets of neurons within the hippocampus and cerebellum using antisense cRNA corresponding to the Probe A (Figure 3A) or Probe B (Figure 3B) region of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA. ###begin p 34
###xml 93 94 93 94 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>

###xml 489 490 477 478 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 584 585 568 569 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 725 726 705 706 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 782 784 758 760 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 477 480 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
A limitation of the previously reported immunohistochemical studies of the localization of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) is the reliance upon anti-Cav1.3 (alpha1D) polyclonal antibodies based upon the original one of Hell et al. [13]. The antigenic sequence utilized to generate these antibodies is a 20 amino acid sequence corresponding to a unique peptide located within the intracellular loop between repeat domains II and II of the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) polypeptide and was derived from the rat brain Cav1.3 (alpha1D) polypeptide sequence. As with most peptide antigen-based antibodies, the anti-Cav1.3 (alpha1D) antibodies may have limitations to their usefulness, particularly since specificity has not been proven on tissues from the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) null animals generated by Platzer et al. [14]. In order to avoid these complications, we chose to examine mRNA analysis and utilize two probe sets corresponding to two different regions of the mRNA sequence encoding two distinct regions of the polypeptide.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 60 61 60 61 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 251 253 247 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 320 321 316 317 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 403 404 395 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 558 559 550 551 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 738 739 726 727 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 802 803 786 787 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 114 117 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 659 664 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
While Xu et al. previously reported immunolocalization of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) protein to rod photoreceptor somata in rat retinal sections, it could not be ruled out that this staining represented processes of the Muller cells ensheathing the rod somata [11]. Using an antibody generated against a different region of the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit, Morgans et al. detected immunoreactivity in cone pedicles [8]. The prominent labeling of most, if not all, cells in the outer nuclear layer of retinal sections in the present study confirms the expression of the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) calcium channel subunit in rod photoreceptors. The very small numbers of cones in the mouse retina preclude us from making any comment regarding the expression of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA in cones or the potential contribution of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) within cones to transmission of light signals derived via rod-cone coupling to second order neurons of the retina.
###end p 35

The observation of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA expression in rods raises some other interesting possibilities worthy of serious consideration in interpreting the reported observations from cacna1f null mice [6] and the nob2 mutant mice with a naturally occurring mutation in cacna1f, the gene encoding Cav1.4 (alpha1F) [7]. Specifically, it warrants concern that the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit protein expression level, localization, or functioning might be altered in addition to that of Cav1.4 (alpha1F). Cav1.3 (alpha1D) protein expression, localization, or functioning may be coupled to Cav1.4 (alpha1F) expression, localization, or functioning, and be compromised as a direct result of the Cav1.4 (alpha1F) mutations and concomitant aberration in rod-bipolar cell synapse formation.
###end p 36

Along this same vein of thinking, it is interesting that mice null for the auxiliary b2 subunit of voltage-dependent calcium channels have a disrupted outer plexiform synaptic layer and have abnormal ERG b waves. Consistent with the established role of auxiliary betasubunits in targeting the pore-forming a subunits to the plasma membrane [15], the phenotype of the beta2 null mice is presumed to be due to the observed inefficiency in targeting of Cav1.4 (alpha1F) subunits to the synapses of rod photoreceptors. In contrast to mice null for the beta2 subunit, mice null for the beta1, beta3, or beta4 subunit do not exhibit histopathological or functional consequences in the retina [16], suggesting that the beta2 auxiliary subunit plays the most prominent role in targeting the pore-forming calcium channel alpha1 subunits to the membranes of the synapses within the retina. To date, no data regarding the identity of the auxiliary betasubunit associated with the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit in photoreceptors has been published. Based upon our finding s in the present study and the evidence from mice null for the calcium channel beta2 subunit, it is highly likely that the auxiliary beta2 subunit associates with Cav1.3 (alpha1D) in addition to Cav1.4 (alpha1F) in rods and that calcium channels containing the Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit are substantially reduced in the retinas of mice lacking the beta2 subunit. To date, no studies of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA nor protein expression levels or localization in cacna1f mutant mice, the beta2 null mice, nor post mortem retinae from humans with CSNB2 have been published. Our findings warrant such studies to make sure these possibilities are not overlooked.

Finally, in addition to abundant expression in rod photoreceptors, we noted extensive Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA expression in most, if not all, neurons within all three nuclear layers of the mouse retina. This observation is consistent with the previously published results of Kamphuis et al. [17] using radiolabeled cRNA probes for in situ analysis of rat retinal sections. The observation of extensive labeling of cells within the inner nuclear layer is also consistent with previous reports of immunohistochemical localization of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) to the terminals of rat bipolar cells [18] and putative Muller cell processes in rat retina [11] and salamander retina [19,20] and single-cell RT-PCR detection of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA expression in acutely isolated human Muller cells [21] and mouse AII amacrine cells [22]. Further experiments using combined in situ hybridization and immunocytochemical analysis of retina or acutely dissociated cells with cell specific markers are required for precise demonstration of the expression of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA within specific cell types of the inner nuclear layer of the retina. Although not uninteresting, the question of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) expression in these other cell types is beyond the scope of this study, which was to investigate the potential expression of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) in rod photoreceptors as a potential explanation for the observed residual photoreceptor function in patients with CSNB2. The apparently ubiquitous expression pattern of Cav1.3 (alpha1D) mRNA we observed in the mouse retina suggests the potential for a much broader role for this L-type calcium channel subunit in overall functioning of the normal retina than previously appreciated. We therefore suggest that lesions in the gene encoding the L-type calcium channel Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit or other molecules that interact with or regulate the L-type calcium channel Cav1.3 (alpha1D) subunit may underlie one or more retinopathies with currently unidentified molecular etiologies.
