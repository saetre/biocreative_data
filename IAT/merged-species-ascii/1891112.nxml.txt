A non-sense mutation in the putative anti-mutator gene ada/alkA of Mycobacterium tuberculosis and M. bovis isolates suggests convergent evolution

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 271 275 271 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 350 376 350 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacteriumtuberculosis </italic>
###xml 433 442 433 442 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 446 449 446 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt</italic>
Previous studies have suggested that variations in DNA repair genes of W-Beijing strains may have led to transient mutator phenotypes which in turn may have contributed to host adaptation of this strain family. Single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) in the DNA repair gene mutT1 was identified in MDR-prone strains from the Central African Republic. A Mycobacteriumtuberculosis H37Rv mutant inactivated in two DNA repair genes, namely ada/alkA and ogt, was shown to display a hypermutator phenotype. We then looked for polymorphisms in these genes in Central African Republic strains (CAR).
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 92 101 92 101 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 105 109 105 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt </italic>
###xml 155 171 155 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 226 235 226 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 267 276 267 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 377 386 377 386 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 393 409 393 409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 155 170 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 267 275 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 377 385 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 393 408 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
In this study, 55 MDR and 194 non-MDR strains were analyzed. Variations in DNA repair genes ada/alkA and ogt were identified. Among them, by comparison to M. tuberculosis published sequences, we found a non-sense variation in ada/alkA gene which was also observed in M. bovis AF2122 strain. SNPs that are present in the adjacent regions to the amber variation are different in M. bovis and in M. tuberculosis strain.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 32 41 32 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 60 76 60 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 92 101 92 101 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 60 75 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 92 100 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
An Amber codon was found in the ada/alkA locus of clustered M. tuberculosis isolates and in M. bovis strain AF2122. This is likely due to convergent evolution because SNP differences between strains are incompatible with horizontal transfer of an entire gene. This suggests that such a variation may confer a selective advantage and be implicated in hypermutator phenotype expression, which in turn contributes to adaptation to environmental changes.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 149 176 149 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 287 288 287 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 512 528 512 528 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 646 647 646 647 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 648 649 648 649 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 149 175 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 208 213 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 512 527 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
With more than 2 million deaths a year, tuberculosis (TB) remains a major public health problem. As one-third of the world's population is infected, Mycobacterium tuberculosis is probably the most widespread human pathogen and the number of TB cases is growing at a rate of 2% per year [1]. This bacterium is remarkably well-adapted to its host, in which it can persist for years without inducing symptoms. Despite its worldwide dissemination and presence in diverse host populations, the genetic variability of M. tuberculosis appears to be very low with the exception of hot spot regions in the genome that are associated with mobile elements [2-5].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 102 107 102 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT2</italic>
###xml 109 115 109 115 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT4 </italic>
###xml 119 122 119 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt</italic>
###xml 382 383 382 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
Previous studies on W-Beijing family strains have identified variations in putative DNA repair genes (mutT2, mutT4 and ogt). These variations are found only in the W-Beijing family; they can therefore be considered to be specific markers of this family and suggest that a mutator phenotype, at least transient, may have contributed to the host adaptation of this family of strains [6].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 16 17 16 17 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 59 62 59 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada</italic>
###xml 63 68 63 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">alkA </italic>
###xml 72 75 72 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt</italic>
Durbach et al. [7] co-inactivated DNA repair genes, namely ada/alkA and ogt, in strain H37Rv and showed that the resulting mutant displayed a mutator phenotype under nitrosative stress.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 40 56 40 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 227 231 227 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 234 235 234 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 308 309 308 309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 310 311 310 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 365 374 365 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 40 55 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
While studying the genetic diversity of M. tuberculosis isolates in the Central African Republic (CAR), we described an MDR-prone family characterized by a T family spoligotype and a synonymous variation in the DNA repair gene mutT1 [8]. This prompted us to screen our set of well-defined isolates from CAR [8,9] by sequencing putative anti-mutator genes including ada/alkA to identify other variations that could be markers of relevant clinical strains.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 52 68 52 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 138 147 138 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 215 224 215 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 228 244 228 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 316 318 316 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 319 321 319 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 379 386 379 386 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 441 450 441 450 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 454 470 454 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 52 75 <span type="species:ncbi:77643">M. tuberculosis complex</span>
###xml 138 146 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 215 223 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 228 243 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 379 386 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 441 449 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 454 469 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Comparisons of CAR strain sequences with published "M. tuberculosis complex" genome sequences revealed an SNP common to these strains and M. bovis strain AF2122. This suggests either horizontal DNA transfer between M. bovis and M. tuberculosis CAR strains or convergent evolution. Medigue et al. and Denamur et al. [10,11] demonstrated horizontal transfer of DNA repair genes in E. coli. To determine whether there has been transfer between M. bovis and M. tuberculosis strains, we looked at polymorphism in the regions upstream and downstream from this common SNP by PCR sequencing. We provide evidence that this SNP may have occurred twice independently and might therefore be an example of convergent evolution.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
###xml 18 26 18 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA</italic>
###xml 31 34 31 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt</italic>
Variations in the ada/alkA and ogt genes in a series of MDR isolates
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 14 23 14 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 27 31 27 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt </italic>
###xml 147 156 147 156 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 183 187 183 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt </italic>
###xml 331 335 331 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt </italic>
###xml 348 357 348 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 364 365 364 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Variations in ada/alkA and ogt genes were assessed in fifty-five MDR strains collected between 1993 and 2001 in CAR. PCR products corresponding to ada/alkA and those corresponding to ogt were obtained for each MDR CAR strain in our collection. Direct sequencing of PCR products revealed four variations in DNA repair genes: one in ogt and three in ada/alkA (table 1).
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 4 8 4 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt </italic>
###xml 302 303 298 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 304 306 300 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
The ogt variant is a C to G substitution causing the replacement of a Threonine by a Serine at position 15 in Ogt, noted A CC-->A GC (Thr15-->Ser). This variation is carried by three CAR isolates. It has been previously observed in other strains and considered to be characteristic of Haarlem strains [6,12].
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 23 24 23 24 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 29 30 27 28 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 100 101 96 97 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 106 107 100 101 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 204 219 196 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 204 219 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Nine strains carry an ACT-->AAT (Thr337-->Asn) variation, and five of these strains also display a TGG-->TAG (Trp79-->AMBER) variation. This is the first report of a variant involving a nonsense codon in M. tuberculosis. This raised the question of whether such variations also occurred in non-MDR strains.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
###xml 16 24 16 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA</italic>
###xml 63 64 63 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Polymorphism of ada/alkA in a series of non-MDR strains (Table 1)
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 92 93 92 93 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 98 99 96 97 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 127 136 123 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 139 143 135 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt </italic>
###xml 234 237 230 233 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">th </sup>
###xml 250 253 246 249 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">th </sup>
###xml 301 302 297 298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 374 375 370 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 416 417 412 413 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 422 423 416 417 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 488 489 480 481 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 494 495 484 485 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
Using the same sequencing approach, we searched for the presence of the nonsense variation TGG-->TAG (Trp79-->AMBER) and other ada/alkA or ogt polymorphisms in a set of 194 well-defined non-MDR strains isolated in CAR between April 15th and August 15th 1998 from the cohort studied by Espinal et al. [9]: 138 of these isolates were found to carry sequence variations (Table 1). Twenty-four of the strains carry the ACT-->AAT (Thr337-->Asn) variation, including three that also carry the TGG-->TAG (Trp79-->AMBER).
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 12 21 12 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 45 61 45 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 102 103 102 103 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 108 109 106 107 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 140 149 136 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 211 212 207 208 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 217 218 211 212 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 277 293 269 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 410 426 402 418 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 587 588 579 580 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 593 594 583 584 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 612 621 600 609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 45 68 <span type="species:ncbi:77643">M. tuberculosis complex</span>
###xml 140 148 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 277 292 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 410 433 <span type="species:ncbi:77643">M. tuberculosis complex</span>
We searched ada/alkA sequences in available "M. tuberculosis complex" genome sequences and found the TGG-->TAG (Trp79-->AMBER) variation in M. bovis strain AF2122. The spoligotypes of the CAR isolates with the TGG-->TAG (Trp79-->AMBER) variation indicate that they are Haarlem M. tuberculosis strains. This suggests that this Amber mutation has occurred independently in at least two different species of the "M. tuberculosis complex" or that it has been acquired by horizontal DNA transfer. To test these possibilities, we studied the polymorphisms of chromosomal regions flanking the TGG-->TAG (Trp79-->AMBER) ada/alkA SNP.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 52 68 52 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 230 239 228 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 336 345 334 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>

###xml 469 470 467 468 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 475 476 471 472 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 526 527 520 521 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 532 533 524 525 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 562 571 552 561 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>

###xml 695 703 685 693 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA</italic>
###xml 759 760 749 750 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 838 839 828 829 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 844 845 832 833 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 890 899 876 885 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 938 953 924 939 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 961 962 947 948 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 967 968 951 952 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 1013 1022 995 1004 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 1062 1071 1044 1053 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 1096 1112 1078 1094 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 1145 1146 1127 1128 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 1151 1152 1131 1132 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 1292 1308 1270 1286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 1361 1370 1339 1348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 1391 1392 1369 1370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 1557 1566 1535 1544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 52 67 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 230 238 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 562 570 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 890 898 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 938 953 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1013 1021 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 1062 1070 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 1096 1111 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1292 1307 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1361 1369 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
We compared regions flanking the Amber variation in M. tuberculosis strains including (I) CAR isolates carrying the Thr337-->Asn variation with or without the Amber variant, (II) H37Rv, (III) CDC1551 and (IV) W-Beijing 210 and in M. bovis strains including (V) RCA isolates, (VI) strain AF2122 and (VII) BCG Pasteur. In addition to the ada/alkA gene variations previously found in MDR CAR strains, we identified two further SNPs affecting the ada/alkA coding sequence: GCG-->ACG (Ala11-->Thr) only found in W-Beijing210 and CTG-->CTA (Leu269-->Leu) harboured by M. bovis AF2122 and BCG Pasteur. We also found six SNPs outside of the ada/alkA coding sequence: SNP1, SNP2 and SNP3 downstream from ada/alkA, and SNP4, SNP5 and SNP6 upstream from the gene (table 2). SNPs 2, 4, 5 and 6 were found in all strains except in H37Rv. SNP1 and the ATC-->GTC (Ile12-->Val) variation were found in all M. bovis strains but not in any CAR isolates of M. tuberculosis. The CTG-->CTA (Leu269-->Leu) variation is present only in M. bovis AF2122 and BCG Pasteur and SNP3 only in M. bovis AF2122. In contrast, CAR M. tuberculosis Amber variants also harbour the ACT-->AAT (Thr337-->Asn) variation and do not carry SNP1 or SNP3, or codon 12 and 269 modifications. Therefore, DNA polymorphisms identified in CAR M. tuberculosis Amber variant isolates differ from those observed in M. bovis strain AF2122 (Table 2). This is not compatible with the occurrence of the Amber variation in both groups being a consequence of horizontal transfer and allelic replacement of the entire ada/alkA gene. It is therefore more likely that independent events are responsible for the appearance of this variation in the two species, thus suggesting convergent evolution.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
###xml 16 31 16 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 16 31 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Spoligotypes of M. tuberculosis strains and relatedness
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 76 77 76 77 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 187 189 187 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 562 563 562 563 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 568 569 566 567 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 800 801 796 797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
Spoligotyping of all these CAR isolates has been reported in Nouvel et al. [8]. Spoligotype clusters were named by their Shared Type number according to the spoligotype database SpolDB3 [13]. We considered the distribution of CAR Amber variant isolates among the clusters. They were found in three different clusters: (I) "ST047" (composed of 50 strains of which only one carries the Amber variation), "ST316 minus spacer 13" (five strains, all carrying the Amber variant, four of them being MDR) and "ST316" (22 strains, two carrying the Amber variation). The ACT-->AAT (Thr337-->Asn) variation alone (not associated with the Amber variant) is present in strains of clusters "ST047", "ST316", "ST312" and "ST312 minus spacers 42-43". These clusters share 93.47 to 98.4% spoligotype identity (Figure 1) and could reasonably be considered as a single family.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Discussion
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 39 48 39 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 62 63 62 63 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 68 69 66 67 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 143 144 139 140 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 149 150 143 144 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 203 204 195 196 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 209 210 199 200 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 257 258 245 246 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 263 264 249 250 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
In our collection of CAR isolates, the ada/alkA gene variant ACT-->AAT (Thr337-->Asn) was found in 16 isolates, eight of which also carry the TGG-->TAG (Trp79-->AMBER) variation. This suggests that the ACT-->AAT (Thr337-->Asn) mutation appeared before the TGG-->TAG (Trp79-->AMBER) mutation.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 37 38 37 38 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 43 44 41 42 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 213 214 209 210 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 219 220 213 214 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 242 243 234 235 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 248 249 238 239 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 525 526 513 514 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 531 532 517 518 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 580 581 564 565 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 586 587 568 569 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</underline>
###xml 642 645 622 625 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt</italic>
###xml 749 753 729 733 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ogt </italic>
###xml 867 876 847 856 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 889 890 869 870 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
Moreover, strains carrying only the ACT-->AAT (Thr337-->Asn) variation show spoligopatterns "ST047", "ST312", "ST314", "ST316", "ST312 without spacers 42-43", or "ST314 without spacers 39". Strains carrying both ACT-->AAT (Thr337-->Asn) and TGG-->TAG (Trp79-->AMBER) belong to "ST047", "ST316" or "ST316 without spacer 13". All these Shared Types are similar and differ by a small number of spacers. The spoligopatterns of these 16 strains classify them into the Haarlem family (similar to ST047). However, the strains with ACT-->AAT (Thr337-->Asn) variation with or without the TGG-->TAG (Trp79-->AMBER) variation do not carry variations in ogt: this suggests they are all derived from an ancient Haarlem family strain before the appearance of the ogt variants. We propose a genealogy of strains showing a Haarlem spoligotype and carrying the Amber variation in the ada/alkA gene (Figure 2). Further studies will be necessary to assess whether these strains are an emerging cluster in CAR.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 23 32 23 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alka </italic>
###xml 49 58 49 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 117 126 117 126 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 188 197 188 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 278 287 278 287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 315 320 315 320 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 323 325 323 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 388 396 388 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 494 503 494 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 569 585 569 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 712 720 712 720 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis</italic>
###xml 811 820 811 820 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alka </italic>
###xml 49 57 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 117 125 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 188 196 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 278 290 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
###xml 388 395 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 569 584 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
The Amber variation in ada/alka is also found in M. bovis strain AF2122. This Amber variation is absent from the two M. bovis strains isolated in CAR analysed in this study (identified as M. bovis by biochemical tests and confirmed by spoligotyping). It is also absent from the M. bovis BCG Pasteur strain. Denamur et al. [11] demonstrated horizontal transfer of DNA repair genes between E. coli strains and showed that this led to the appearance of mutator phenotypes. Our SNP analysis of the ada/alkA DNA region excludes this possibility for the Amber variation. All M. tuberculosis strains carrying the Amber variant in our study population harbour the same SNPs; these SNPs are different from those found in M. bovisAF2122. This argues strongly against horizontal transfer of a region comprising the entire ada/alka gene. Therefore, the Amber variation probably occurred more than once independently and thus constitutes a case of convergent evolution. The acquisition of this variation may confer a selective advantage on the strain.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 95 104 95 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 112 128 112 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 146 150 146 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada </italic>
###xml 154 159 154 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">alkA </italic>
###xml 168 175 168 175 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 224 226 224 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 303 312 303 312 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 400 409 400 409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 536 552 536 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 582 590 582 590 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 591 592 591 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 625 634 625 634 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 732 739 732 739 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 874 882 874 882 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 883 885 883 885 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 949 956 949 956 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1014 1016 1014 1016 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 112 127 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 168 175 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 303 311 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 536 551 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 874 881 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
In several bacteria, the Ada and AlkA proteins are encoded by two distinct genes. The putative ada/alkA gene of M. tuberculosis is similar to the ada and alkA genes of E. coli, and is likely to be a fusion of the two genes [14]. Interestingly, the non-sense mutation we described in CAR isolates and in M. bovis AF2122 affects the Ada part of the gene. Presumably, the Amber mutation inactivates the ada/alkA fused gene and results in a mutator phenotype. Indeed, inactivation of this gene results in an increased mutation frequency in M. tuberculosis without any growth impairment in vivo [7]. Thus, the naturally occurring ada/alkA mutants we report here may have the advantage of a high mutation rate without any associated cost in vivo. Alternatively, the production of a truncated Ada protein may constitutively activate adaptive responses, as has been demonstrated in E. coli [15]. In particular, it may provide an improved response to stress in vivo, for instance in the phagosome of activated macrophages [16].
###end p 29
###begin title 30
Conclusion
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 23 32 23 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 53 69 53 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 107 116 107 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis </italic>
###xml 261 277 261 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 281 289 281 289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. bovis</italic>
###xml 378 387 378 387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ada/alkA </italic>
###xml 404 420 404 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 473 474 473 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 626 642 626 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 53 68 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 107 115 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 261 276 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 281 289 <span type="species:ncbi:1765">M. bovis</span>
###xml 404 419 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 626 641 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
An Amber stop-codon in ada/alkA was detected in some M. tuberculosis Haarlem isolates from CAR and also in M. bovis strain AF2122. This Amber variation appears not to have spread by horizontal gene transfer; this could be a case of convergent evolution between M. tuberculosis and M. bovis. This suggested that acquisition of the variation may confer a selective advantage. The ada/alkA genetic locus of M. tuberculosis has been implicated in mutator phenotype expression [7]. The variant we report may therefore contribute to adaptation to environmental changes. In addition, we describe polymorphisms in DNA repair genes in M. tuberculosis isolates and these could be used for strain characterization.
###end p 31

###begin title 39
Computer-assisted analysis of dendrograms
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 479 481 479 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 482 484 482 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>

Autoradiographs were scanned, and the resulting spoligotype data for each mycobacterial isolate was analysed using the Windows version of the computer-assisted BioNumerics software (Applied Maths, Kortrijik, Belgium). Dendrograms were constructed for the spoligotypes according to degree of similitude (Dice's coefficient) and comparison with known spoligotypes [13,21,22].
