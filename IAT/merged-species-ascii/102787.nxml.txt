Human RecQ5beta, a large isomer of RecQ5 DNA helicase, localizes in the nucleoplasm and interacts with topoisomerases 3alpha and 3beta
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 97 102 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1353 1358 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The RecQ helicase superfamily has been implicated in DNA repair and recombination. At least five human RecQ-related genes exist: RecQ1, BLM, WRN, RecQ4 and RecQ5. Mutations in BLM, WRN and RecQ4 are associated with Bloom, Werner and Rothmund-Thomson syndromes, respectively, involving a predisposition to malignancies and a cellular phenotype that includes increased chromosome instability. RecQ5 is small, containing only a core part of the RecQ helicase, but three isomer transcripts code for small RecQ5alpha (corresponding to the original RecQ5 with 410 amino acids), new large RecQ5beta (991 amino acids) and small RecQ5gamma (435 amino acids) proteins that contain the core helicase motifs. By determining the genomic structure, we found that the three isoforms are generated by differential splicing from the RecQ5 gene that contains at least 19 exons. Northern blot analysis using a RecQ5beta-specific probe indicates that RecQ5beta mRNA is expressed strongly in the testis. Immunocytochemical staining of three N-terminally tagged RecQ5 isomers expressed in 293EBNA cells showed that RecQ5beta migrates to the nucleus and exists exclusively in the nucleoplasm, while the small RecQ5alpha and RecQ5gamma proteins stay in the cytoplasm. Immunoprecipitation and an extended cytochemical experiment suggested that the nucleoplasmic RecQ5beta, like yeast Sgs1 DNA helicase, binds to topoisomerases 3alpha and 3beta, but not to topoisomerase 1. These results predict that RecQ5beta may have an important role in DNA metabolism and may also be related to a distinct genetic disease.

###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4

DNA helicases have important roles in cellular DNA events, such as replication, recombination, repair and transcription, by unwinding the duplex DNA (1-3). Multiple DNA helicase families with seven consensus motifs have been found and members within each helicase family also share sequence homologies between motifs (4). The RecQ helicase family includes helicases that have extensive amino acid sequence homologies to the Escherichia coli DNA helicase RecQ implicated in double-strand break repair and suppression of illegitimate recombination (5,6). The human RecQ DNA helicase gene family has five members, RecQ1 (also referred to as RecQL), BLM, WRN, RecQ4 and RecQ5, located on human chromosomes 12p12, 15q26.1, 8p12-11.2, 8q24.3 and 17q25.2-25.3, respectively (7-10). Mutations in BLM, WRN and RecQ4 cause Bloom syndrome (BS), Werner syndrome (WS) and Rothmund-Thomson syndrome (RTS), respectively (8,9,11), genetic disorders that increase the rate of generation of genetic instability in patient cells, resulting in a predisposition to cancer and premature aging (12).
###end p 4

###xml 131 137 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>

RecQ-type helicases from other organisms are SGS1 of Saccharomyces cerevisiae and rqh1+ of Schizosaccharomyces pombe (13,14). Like E.coli, these unicellular organisms contain a single species of RecQ-type helicase. The Sgs1 protein exists in the nucleolus when the cells are young and migrates to the nucleoplasm as the cells senesce and the nucleolus fragments (15). Mutation of SGS1 suppresses the slow growth phenotype of mutant yeast cells with a mutation in the topoisomerase 3 gene and causes missegregation of the chromosome during meiosis and mitosis (13,16). Biochemically, the Sgs1 protein binds to topoisomerases 2 and 3 (13,16). The rqh1+ gene of S.pombe was cloned by complementation of the UV sensitivity of a rqh1-h2 mutant; rqh1- cells arrested by hydroxyurea in S phase are unrecoverable because of high recombination (14).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 139 140 139 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c8">8</xref>
###xml 141 142 141 142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c9">9</xref>
###xml 143 145 143 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c11">11</xref>

###xml 852 854 852 854 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c10">10</xref>
###xml 951 952 951 952 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c7">7</xref>
###xml 953 955 953 955 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c10">10</xref>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 542 547 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 622 627 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Human WRN, BLM and RecQ4 DNA helicases and their disease-causing defective mutations have been extensively characterized by us and others (8,9,11,12). The DNA helicase RecQ5, however, has not been characterized for its gene structure, biochemical nature of the encoded helicase, subcellular localization nor the disease caused by mutation. Our previous phylogenetic study (10) on the sequence of the helicase domain indicated that the product of the RecQ5 gene is evolutionarily close to the small helicase RecQ1, which is characterized as a human progenitor of the RecQ family helicases (7), but has no known relation to human disease. We noted that two size classes of the RecQ helicase family exist, large and small helicases: the large helicases are BLM, WRN and RecQ4 in the nucleus, which contain 1417, 1432 and 1208 amino acids, respectively (8-10); the small helicases are RecQ1 and RecQ5, which contain 649 and 410 amino acids, respectively (7,10).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 209 211 209 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c17">17</xref>
###xml 212 214 212 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c19">19</xref>

###xml 947 949 943 945 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c30">30</xref>
###xml 1043 1045 1039 1041 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c31">31</xref>
###xml 129 135 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 259 266 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patient</span>
###xml 643 650 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patient</span>
###xml 1018 1023 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Extended studies have been mostly with the large helicase members of the RecQ family whose mutation causes disease phenotypes in humans. We and others found that: (i) all three helicases exist in the nucleus (17-19); (ii) their mutation causes the genomes of patient cells to become cytogenetically unstable, but differentially: defective BLM increases the rate of sister chromatid exchange (20), while defective WRN results in variegated translocation mosaicism (21) and defective RecQ4 (RTS) causes trisomerization, although a limited number of case reports exist (22); (iii) notable down-regulation of mRNAs and helicase proteins occurs in patient cells (19,23,24), due perhaps to early termination codon-mediated regulation (25); (iv) only WRN contains exonuclease activity in its N-terminus, whereas its 3'-->5' or 5'-->3' directionality remains to be unequivocally defined (26-29); (v) WRN unwinds DNA/RNA heteroduplexes in addition to DNA (30); (vi) intact BLM and WRN partially suppress hyper-recombination in yeast sgs1 mutant cells (31). These findings suggest that the three disease-causing helicases have different functions in cellular DNA metabolism that cannot be complemented by each other, although the molecular mechanisms behind each helicase reaction need to be clarified.
###end p 7

###xml 300 302 300 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c10">10</xref>

###xml 782 792 771 781 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 526 531 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 749 754 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 815 820 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In this paper we describe the genomic structure of RecQ5, a candidate for the fourth disease-causing RecQ-type helicase gene, regarding new cDNA genes generated by differential splicing and the biochemical nature of proteins coded for by the three gene isoforms. The RecQ5 reported previously by us (10) corresponds to RecQ5alpha in this paper. Two newly identified isomer genes are referred to here as RecQ5beta and RecQ5gamma. During the preparation of this manuscript, Sekelsky et al. (32) reported that the Drosophila and human RecQ5 cDNA genes exist in different isoforms generated by alternative splicing. Particularly, three Drosophila isoforms, dRecQ5a-c, which they described in their paper, are consistent in size with the findings of the human isoforms in this study and Drosophila RecQ5a corresponds to human RecQ5beta.

###begin title 9
MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 9
###begin title 10
###xml 13 18 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Isolation of human RecQ5 isoform cDNAs
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 117 123 117 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 146 147 146 147 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sup>
###xml 456 458 452 454 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>
###xml 2 7 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 117 123 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 383 388 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
A human testis cDNA library constructed with the pAP3-neo plasmid was purchased from Takara (Kyoto, Japan). DNA from E.coli transformants (~1 x 106 clones) made with recombinant plasmids grown on nitrocellulose filters (Millipore, Japan) was replicated on nylon membranes (Pall, UK) and screened by hybrizidation using a 1.3 kb cDNA fragment covering the open reading frame (ORF) of human RecQ5 as probe. In brief, membranes were hybridized with an [alpha-32P]dCTP-labeled probe DNA in 5x [0.15 M NaCl, 0.15 M sodium citrate buffer, pH 7.0, (SSC)] containing 10x Denhardt's solution at 65degreesC for 20 h. The membranes were washed at 65degreesC in 0.5x SSC, 0.1% SDS, followed by further washing with 0.1x SSC, 0.1% SDS solution. The membranes were exposed to X-ray films (Kodak) with intensifying screens at -70degreesC overnight. Colonies on the original nitrocellulose filters corresponding to positive signals were selected. After repeated hybridization, five independent clones were selected that contained the RecQ5alpha, beta and gamma cDNAs of almost full size. The sequences of cDNAs were determined by PCR-based cycle sequencing using an automated DNA sequencer (Applied Biosystems).
###end p 11
###begin title 12
###xml 46 51 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Determination of the genomic structure of the human RecQ5 gene
###end title 12
###begin p 13
Two P1 clones, 15033 and 21570, that partially overlap and cover the contour length of the full-sized RecQ5 gene, were obtained from Genome Systems Inc. (St Louis, MO) after screening using a PCR-based method with two primer sets, 5'-GAGCAGCCTTGTGTTTAGACCTGG-3'/5'-ATCCCCC-ATGTCCAATGTGTCTGG-3' and 5'-GATATAAGATTGC-GTGGGTTCTGC-3'/5'-CGTGGTCCGCCCAAGAATTAAA-GG-3', corresponding, respectively, to the 5'- and 3'-untranslated regions of the RecQ5alpha gene. The exon-intron boundary sequences of the RecQ5 genome were determined by sequencing the P1 DNAs directly using primers prepared from the sequences of the RecQ5 isoform cDNAs.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Northern blot analysis
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 145 147 138 140 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>
###xml 181 184 171 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Eco</italic>
###xml 187 190 177 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xho</italic>

###xml 548 550 525 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c10">10</xref>
The expression of RecQ5beta mRNA was studied using multiple tissue northern blot analysis (Clontech). The filters were hybridized with an [alpha-32P]dCTP-labeled RecQ5beta-specific EcoRV-XhoI cDNA fragment, corresponding to exons 14-19, prepared from a 6x hemagglutinin (HA)-RecQ5beta plasmid at 42degreesC overnight in 5x SSPE buffer containing 50% formamide, 2% SDS, 10x Denhardt's solution and 100 microg/ml depurinated salmon sperm DNA. Washing was under highly stringent conditions that were essentially the same as described by Kitao et al. (10): three times with 2x SSC, 0.1% SDS at room temperature and once with 0.2x SSC, 0.1% SDS for 30 min at 65degreesC. The filters were exposed to X-ray films that were analyzed using a BAS1500 system (Fuji film).

RecQ5alpha, beta or gamma cDNA fragments were excised with EcoRI and NotI from pAP3-neo vector DNAs and subcloned into pBluescript II KS+. These plasmids were modified in the 5'-flanking sequences of the first ATG of the RecQ5 gene to isolate the intact ORF using NheI and XhoI digestions. The NheI- and XhoI-digested DNA fragments derived from RecQ5alpha, beta and gamma cDNAs were subcloned into the NheI-XhoI sites of the pcDNA3 mammalian expression plasmid containing a 6xHA epitope sequence. The 6xHA epitope was introduced by repeated ligation of oligonucleotides encoding the epitopic amino acid sequence to the 5' NheI site. To construct the 6xHA-RecQ5betac expression plasmid, the 6xHA-RecQ5beta plasmid DNA was digested with NheI to remove the 5'-region upstream of an endogenous NheI site and was self-ligated to code for a C-terminal 246 amino acid polypeptide of RecQ5beta.

###begin p 18
###xml 552 555 538 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xba</italic>

The cDNAs of human topoisomerases 1, 3alpha and 3beta comprising the full ORF were amplified by PCR using primer sets 5'-TATTCTAGAATGAGTGGGGACCACCTCCACA-ACG-3'/5'-TATGTCGACGCTAAAACTCATAGTCTTCA-TCAGCC-3' for topoisomerase 1, 5'-TATACTAGTATG-ATCTTTCCTGTCGCCCGCTACG-3'/5'-TATCTCGAGGC-TCATCTGTTCTGAGGACAAAAGG-3' for topoisomerase 3alpha and 5'-TATGCTAGCATGAAGACTGTGCTCATGGTTGCTG-3'/5'-TATCTCGAGGGACAGGGTC-ATCATACA-AAGTAGG-3' for topoisomerase 3beta. The amplified cDNA fragments were digested with restriction enzymes whose sites were tagged with primers: XbaI and SalI for topoisomerase 1, SpeI and XhoI for topoisomerase 3alpha and NheI and XhoI for topoisomerase 3beta. The fragments were subcloned downstream of a sequence encoding the N-terminally tagged Flag epitope in a pcDNA3-based vector and were sequenced.

###begin title 19
Transfection and expression in 293EBNA cells
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 208 209 202 203 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 132 136 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
Human 293EBNA cells (Invitrogen) were maintained in Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated fetal calf serum. The cells were cultured at 37degreesC in an incubator with 5% CO2. Confluent cells (70%) cultured in 60 mm dishes were transfected with 3 microg of the expression plasmid DNA using Lipofectamine (Gibco BRL) and the transfected cells were grown for 48 h at 37degreesC.

###begin title 21
Immunoprecipitation and western blot analysis
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 863 869 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 914 919 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1007 1012 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>

After 48 h transfection in a 60 mm dish, the cells were harvested, washed with phosphate-bufferred saline (PBS) and lysed in 300 microl of lysis buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 150 mM NaCl, 0.5% NP-40, 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride). The cell lysate was obtained by centrifugation at 15 000 r.p.m. for 30 min at 4degreesC. For immunoprecipitation, the cell lysate was incubated with 20 microl of M2 agarose (Eastman Kodak) overnight at 4degreesC. The protein-bound agarose beads were washed four times with PBS containing 0.05% Tween 20 and the proteins eluted with 20 microl of lysis buffer containing 100 microg/ml of Flag peptide. The proteins were fractionated using SDS-PAGE, transferred to polyvinylidene difluoride membranes and treated with PBS containing 5% (w/v) skimmed milk. The membranes were then incubated for 60 min at 4degreesC with anti-HA rabbit polyclonal IgG (Santa Cruz) or anti-Flag M2 mouse monoclonal IgG (Eastman Kodak), washed and then incubated for 60 min at 4degreesC with horse radish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated anti-rabbit IgG (Amersham) or HRP-conjugated anti-mouse IgG (Dako, Denmark). After incubation, the membranes were washed and developed using ECLtrade mark (Amersham Life Science, UK).

###begin title 25
RESULTS
###end title 25
###begin title 26
Characterization of the RecQ5 gene and identification of three RecQ5 isomer transcripts generated by differential exon use
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 104 105 104 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f1">1</xref>
###xml 115 116 115 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd270tb1">1</xref>
###xml 286 288 286 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c10">10</xref>
###xml 582 584 578 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c10">10</xref>
###xml 772 773 764 765 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f1">1</xref>
###xml 938 939 924 925 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f1">1</xref>
###xml 1108 1109 1086 1087 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f1">1</xref>
###xml 1283 1284 1261 1262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f1">1</xref>
###xml 1439 1445 1414 1420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 1488 1489 1463 1464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f1">1</xref>
###xml 1512 1517 1487 1492 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 1520 1522 1495 1497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c32">32</xref>
###xml 1565 1575 1540 1550 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1874 1875 1843 1844 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f2">2</xref>
###xml 344 349 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1439 1445 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 1715 1720 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Characterization of the RecQ5 gene using P1 phage DNAs showed that it contained at least 19 exons (Fig. 1A). Table 1 shows the nucleotide sequences at the exon-intron junctions. Previously, we showed that RecQ5 mRNA consists of two transcripts having different sizes of 3.6 and 3.8 kb (10). Our further studies that cloned new RecQ5 cDNAs from human testis mRNAs and determined the sequences, showed that at least three different transcripts code for RecQ5 DNA helicases of different sizes. The original RecQ5 gene (referred to hereafter as RecQ5alpha) that we reported previously (10) was the largest subset of transcripts (3715 bases) among the three RecQ5 transcript species, consisting of exons 1-7, but encoded a small polypeptide RecQ5alpha of 410 amino acids (Fig. 1a and b). The second largest transcript, RecQ5beta (3703 bases), formed by exons 1-7a and 8-19, coded for the largest polypeptide RecQ5beta of 991 amino acids (Fig. 1a and b). The third transcript, RecQ5gamma, was the smallest in size (1749 bases), formed by exons 1-7a and exon 7b and coded for the small polypeptide RecQ5gamma (Fig. 1a and b). The protein structures of all three polypeptides predicted from the defined genomic sequence share seven motifs conserved for DNA helicases (darkened areas in Fig. 1b). In contrast, the largest polypeptide RecQ5beta contained a large C-terminal region that includes a domain homologous to the non-helicase domain of the E.coli RecQ DNA helicase (hatched region in Fig. 1b). Recently, Sekelsky et al. (32) cloned the RecQ5 homolog gene dRecQ5a in Drosophila that encodes a polypeptide of an equivalent size (1057 amino acids) to RecQ5beta. A comparison of the amino acids sequences of dRecQ5a and human RecQ5beta showed that these two polypeptides share considerable homology, particularly in the N-terminal helicase domain and the C-terminal region (Fig. 2).
###end p 27
###begin title 28
###xml 48 53 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Northern blot analysis of the RecQ5beta mRNA in human tissues
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 402 404 398 400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c10">10</xref>
###xml 734 735 720 721 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f3">3</xref>
###xml 139 144 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our previous studies using a DNA probe prepared from the helicase domain of RecQ5alpha showed that: (i) the profile of RecQ5 expression in human tissues largely resembles that of RecQ1, ubiquitously expressed in all tissues examined, with a notably strong expression in the pancreas and testis, similar to some extent to that of WRN; (ii) at least two major isomer transcripts of 3.6 and 3.8 kb exist (10). To determine whether the expression profile of the new large RecQ5beta mRNA differs from that of the original RecQ5 (now renamed RecQ5alpha), we prepared a DNA probe from the 3'-terminus (exons 14-19) of RecQ5beta and carried out northern blot analysis for expression in various tissues under highly stringent conditions (Fig. 3). Again, RecQ5beta was expressed in all the tissues and organs examined, with strong expression in the testis. The expression profile was, however, distinct from that of RecQ5alpha, with reduced expression in the pancreas and increased expression in the kidney. Two major transcripts of 3.6 and 3.8 kb were detected, consistent with our previous findings, but there were other small and large RecQ5beta-related RNA species found in the placenta and kidney (indicated by asterisks). These results suggest that the tissue-specific expression of RecQ5beta is by selective exon use and perhaps other unidentified RecQ5beta-related transcripts exist in the placenta and kidney.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
Nuclear localization of RecQ5beta helicase but not of RecQ5alpha and RecQ5gamma helicases
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 69 71 69 71 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c17">17</xref>
###xml 72 74 72 74 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c19">19</xref>
###xml 138 140 138 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c33">33</xref>
###xml 620 621 614 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f4">4</xref>
###xml 804 805 798 799 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f4">4</xref>
###xml 1239 1240 1219 1220 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f5">5</xref>
###xml 1394 1395 1370 1371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f5">5</xref>
###xml 1425 1426 1397 1398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f5">5</xref>
###xml 1520 1521 1489 1490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f5">5</xref>
###xml 1640 1641 1603 1604 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f5">5</xref>
###xml 1131 1137 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
BLM, WRN and RecQ4 helicases migrate to and localize in the nucleus (17-19), while RecQ1 helicase localizes in the nucleus and cytoplasm (33). To understand the subcellular localization of the three RecQ5 helicase isomers, we expressed the genes N-terminally tagged with epitope sequence (YPYDVPDYASL) of influenza virus hemagglutinin (HA) repeated six times in 293EBNA cells. As our preliminary experiments showed that expressed RecQ5beta migrates into the nucleus, we also expressed a part of the C-terminus (246 amino acids, referred to as RecQ5betac) to locate the nuclear localization signal (NLS) sequence. Figure 4a shows structures of the expressed proteins. A total of four recombinant proteins were expressed with the sizes confirmed by western blot analysis using a HA-specific antibody (Fig. 4b). The molecular weights of the individual proteins were estimated as 53, 140, 60 and 49 kDa for RecQ5alpha, RecQ5beta, RecQ5gamma and RecQ5betac, respectively. The subcellular locations of the expressed proteins were next examined by immunocytochemical staining using the HA-specific antibody and a biotinylated antibody to rabbit immunoglobulin and the expressed proteins were stained green with streptavidin-conjugated FITC (Fig. 5). To locate the position of the nuclei, the same cells were stained with DAPI, which stains duplex DNA blue. The data clearly show that RecQ5alpha (Fig. 5a and b) and RecQ5gamma (Fig. 5e and f) are localized in the cytoplasm. In contrast, RecQ5beta migrates to the nucleus (Fig. 5c and d), suggesting that RecQ5beta is a nuclear helicase. RecQ5betac also migrated exclusively into the nucleus (Fig. 5g and h), indicating that the NLS of the RecQ5beta protein is within this small C-terminal fragment.

###begin title 32
RecQ5beta protein interacts with topoisomerases 3alpha and 3beta
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 30 36 30 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 439 451 430 442 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 572 574 563 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c13">13</xref>
###xml 666 668 657 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c34">34</xref>
###xml 1554 1555 1516 1517 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f6">6</xref>
###xml 1576 1577 1538 1539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f6">6</xref>
###xml 2002 2003 1951 1952 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f6">6</xref>
###xml 30 36 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 439 451 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 727 732 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 815 820 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The high sequence homology to E.coli RecQ DNA helicase and a clear biochemical nature to localize in the nucleus strongly predict that RecQ5beta is most probably a helicase involved in DNA unwinding. Although such a DNA helicase activity of the RecQ5beta protein remains to be substantiated using the purified protein, we examined whether the expressed RecQ5beta protein interacts with type I topoisomerases that cooperate with helicases. S.cerevisiae Sgs1 DNA helicase, for example, binds to topoisomerase 3 and the two enzymes cooperate to suppress hyper-recombination (13), and sgs1 mutant cells have a poor growth rate in a topoisomerase 1-defective background (34). To determine whether RecQ5beta is capable of binding to human type I topoisomerases, we carried out a series of co-expression experiments using human topoisomerases 1, 3alpha and 3beta in 293EBNA cells. In this experiment the RecQ5beta protein was expressed as a form N-terminally tagged with influenza virus HA (6xHA-RecQ5beta). Topoisomerases were expressed individually as N-terminal Flag-tagged forms (Flag-Top1, Flag-Top3alpha or Flag-Top3beta). After co-expression with individual topoisomerases, RecQ5beta protein bound to the topoisomerases and co-immunoprecipitated with anti-Flag antibody was analyzed by western blot analysis using anti-HA antibodies. In these co-expression experiments, 6xHA-RecQ5beta and the three topoisomerase proteins were expressed at their expected sizes in 293EBNA cells, as measured by western blot analysis using antibodies specific to HA (Fig. 6a) and to Flag (Fig. 6b). Individual topoisomerases in the cell extracts were then immunoprecipitated by anti-Flag antibody-conjugated agarose beads, eluted by Flag peptide and the 6xHA-RecQ5beta protein potentially bound to each topoisomerase was analyzed by western blot analysis using anti-HA antibodies. The results clearly show that 6xHA-RecQ5beta was co-immunoprecipitated with Flag-Top3alpha and Flag-Top3beta, but not with Flag-Top1 (Fig. 6c), suggesting that RecQ5beta helicase binds specifically to topoisomerases 3alpha and 3beta.
###end p 33

###begin title 36
DISCUSSION
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 456 457 434 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f5">5</xref>
###xml 540 544 518 522 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">KRPR</underline>
###xml 555 559 533 537 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">KRPR</underline>
###xml 914 916 881 883 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c35">35</xref>
###xml 994 999 961 966 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RKRKK</underline>
###xml 1006 1010 973 977 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">KRRK</underline>
###xml 1036 1039 1003 1006 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">KRR</underline>
###xml 1051 1055 1018 1022 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">KRSK</underline>
###xml 1103 1105 1070 1072 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c17">17</xref>
###xml 1116 1118 1083 1085 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c18">18</xref>
###xml 1471 1473 1438 1440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c18">18</xref>
###xml 1558 1560 1525 1527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c36">36</xref>
###xml 1589 1591 1556 1558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c19">19</xref>
###xml 1636 1641 1603 1608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 1644 1646 1611 1613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c37">37</xref>
###xml 429 434 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1376 1384 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
In this paper we have defined the exon-intron structures of the RecQ5 gene, and we show that three RecQ5 helicase isomers, RecQ5alpha, RecQ5beta and RecQ5gamma, are generated as a result of differential splicing of the RecQ5 gene transcript. Two of these isomer gene products, RecQ5alpha and RecQ5gamma, are small and localize in the cytoplasm, while RecQ5beta migrates into the nucleus and exists in the nucleoplasm, like other human RecQ helicases (Fig. 5). Our amino acid homology search indicated that a potential NLS with the sequence KRPRSQQENPESQPQKRPR exists in the C-terminus (residues 854-872 amino acids) of RecQ5beta, which is absent from both RecQ5alpha and RecQ5gamma. A similar bipartite NLS motif comprising two arrays of basic amino acids with a spacer region of any 10 amino acids between them has been found in many nuclear proteins, including nucleoplasmin, p53 and topoisomerases (reviewed in 35). Previously, we identified the NLS as having an array of basic amino acids, RKRKKMPASQRSKRRK (residues 1334-1349) and KRRCFPGSEEICSSSKRSK (residues 1371-1389), in the C-termini of BLM (17) and WRN (18), respectively, and found that most mutations generate truncated helicases lacking the NLS, causing WRN and BLM not to be transported to the nucleus, where the DNA helicases are presumed to function. Importantly, this finding clearly explains why WS and BS patients show a set of similar clinical phenotypes no matter what type of mutation they carry (18). More recently, we showed that WRN helicase can exist not only in the nucleoplasm (36) but also in the nucleolus (19), confirming the previous data of Marciniak et al. (37).
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 534 535 508 509 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f6">6</xref>
###xml 891 893 855 857 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c13">13</xref>
###xml 894 896 858 860 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c16">16</xref>
###xml 1234 1235 1178 1179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd270f7">7</xref>
###xml 1283 1288 1227 1232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 1291 1293 1235 1237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c13">13</xref>
###xml 803 808 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1325 1330 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The expression profile of RecQ5beta is largely the same as that we previously reported for RecQ5alpha, although stronger expression of RecQ5beta in the testis was noted in this study. These data predict that a mutation in RecQ5beta may cause a disease that shows the phenotypes shared by BS, WS and RTS, although whether RecQ5beta is a disease-causing gene remains to be investigated. Our studies using immunoprecipitation showed that the expressed RecQ5beta binds to topoisomerases 3alpha and 3beta, but not to topoisomerase 1 (Fig. 6). Although the results should be confirmed by the endogenous proteins and their specific antibodies, the data of this study at least support the view that overexpressed RecQ5beta shows preferential binding to overexpressed topoisomerases 3alpha and 3beta, similar to yeast Sgs1, which binds to overexpressed topoisomerases 2 and 3 in two-hybrid analyses (13,16). The fact that the expressed RecQ5beta failed to bind to overexpressed topoisomerase 1 suggests that the apparent preferential binding to topoisomerases 3alpha and 3beta is unlikely to be mediated simply by DNA. Further immunocytochemical double staining of expressed RecQ5beta and topoisomerases 3alpha or 3beta in 293EBNA cells (Fig. 7) was consistent with this conclusion. Gangloff et al. (13) showed that the N-terminus of yeast Sgs1 helicase binds to topoisomerase 3. Whether the interaction between RecQ5beta and the two topoisomerases is also mediated by the RecQ5beta N-terminus remains to be studied.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 71 73 71 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c38">38</xref>
###xml 144 146 144 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c39">39</xref>
###xml 196 198 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c13">13</xref>
###xml 199 201 199 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c16">16</xref>
###xml 363 368 363 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et&#160;al</italic>
###xml 371 373 371 373 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd270c40">40</xref>
###xml 396 402 396 402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 80 84 <span type="species:ncbi:10633?0.9966199903428296">SV40</span>
###xml 152 157 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 396 402 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Topoisomerases remove positive and/or negative superhelicity from DNA (38). The SV40 T antigen, a viral DNA helicase, binds to topoisomerase 1 (39) and yeast Sgs1 binds to topoisomerases 2 and 3 (13,16), perhaps to cooperatively regulate the superhelicity and relax the distorted DNA structure generated as a result of DNA unwinding. Consistent with this, Harmon et al. (40) recently showed that E.coli RecQ helicase specifically stimulates topoisomerase 3 to fully catenate double-stranded DNA molecules by unwinding a covalently closed DNA, proposing that these two proteins functionally interact and control cellular recombination. In the absence of cooperation between helicase and topoisomerases, an imbalance may occur, yielding potential recombinogenic lesions. It is thus intriguing that RecQ5beta is capable of binding to topoisomerases 3alpha and 3beta. This finding strongly supports the view that RecQ5beta is probably an active DNA helicase and is involved in nucleoplasmic DNA metabolism, but this remains to be clarified.
