A mutation in GJA8 (p.P88Q) is associated with "balloon-like" cataract with Y-sutural opacities in a family of Indian origin
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Purpose
###end title 2
###begin p 3
To detect the underlying genetic defect in a family with three members in two generations affected with bilateral congenital cataract.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 119 124 115 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYAA</italic>
###xml 146 152 139 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYBA1</italic>
###xml 174 180 164 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYBB2</italic>
###xml 210 222 192 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGA, CRYGB</italic>
###xml 224 229 206 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 235 240 217 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGD</italic>
###xml 256 260 238 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GJA3</italic>
###xml 280 284 262 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GJA8</italic>
Detailed family history and clinical data were recorded. Mutation screening in the candidate genes, alphaA-crystallin (CRYAA), betaA1-crystallin (CRYBA1), betaB2-crystallin (CRYBB2), gammaA-gammaD-crystallins (CRYGA, CRYGB, CRYGC, and CRYGD), connexin-46 (GJA3), and connexin-50 (GJA8), was performed by bidirectional sequencing of the amplified products.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 186 190 186 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GJA8</italic>
Affected individuals had "balloon-like" cataract with prominent Y-sutural opacities. Sequencing of the candidate genes showed a heterozygous c.262C>A change in the gene for connexin 50 (GJA8), which is localized at 1q21, that resulted in the replacement of a highly conserved proline by glutamine (p.P88Q). This sequence change was not observed in 96 ethnically matched controls.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusions
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 31 35 31 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GJA8</italic>
We report a p.P88Q mutation in GJA8 associated with Y-sutural cataract in a family of Indian origin. Mutations of the same codon have previously been described in British families with pulverulent cataract, suggesting that modifying factors may determine the type of cataract.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Introduction
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 219 220 219 220 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 221 222 221 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 446 447 446 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 448 449 448 449 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 521 522 521 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 684 685 684 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 1126 1127 1126 1127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 1145 1146 1145 1146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
Congenital cataract is one of the most significant causes of visual impairment and childhood blindness worldwide. The prevalence of non-syndromic congenital cataract is estimated to be 1-6 cases per 10,000 live births [1,2]. It is a clinically and genetically heterogeneous ocular lens disorder. Interfamilial and intrafamilial phenotypic variation is quite significant in congenital cataract, and various types and sub-types have been reported [3-5]. Approximately one-third of the cases show a positive family history [6]. All three Mendelian modes of inheritance exist for congenital cataract. However, an autosomal dominant mode of transmission is reported to be the most common [5]. At least 27 loci have been mapped. The identified genes encode proteins such as crystallins, the major structural lens proteins (CRYAA, CRYAB, CRYBA1/A3, CRYBB1, CRYBB2, CRYBB3, CRYGC, CRYGD, and CRYGS), gap junctional proteins, the connexins (GJA3 and GJA8), major intrinsic protein (MIP/MIP26), lens integral membrane protein 2 (LIM2), beaded filament proteins (BFSP1, BFSP2), and heat shock protein (HSF4) as reviewed by Reddy et al. [2] and Hejtmancik [7].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 145 146 145 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 242 243 242 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 372 376 372 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GJA8</italic>
###xml 392 394 392 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 731 735 731 735 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GJA8</italic>
###xml 804 811 804 811 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
Pulverulent cataract was the first autosomal disease locus mapped. In 1963, Renwick and Lawler described linkage to the Duffy blood group locus [8], which was found to cosegregate with an uncoiler element of chromosome 1q by Donahue in 1968 [9]. Thirty years later, Shiels analyzed the same cataract family and reported a missense mutation in codon 88 of the connexin 50 (GJA8) gene at 1q21 [10], leading to the substitution of proline by serine (p.P88S). Here, we report a cataract family of Indian origin with three affected members in two generations with a proline to glutamine mutation of the same codon (p.P88Q) but with balloon-like cataract with prominent Y-sutural opacities. Now, more than a dozen different mutations in GJA8 associated with different cataract phenotypes have been identified (Table 1).
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Mutations identified in connexin 50 in association with different cataract phenotypes in different congenital cataract families.
###end title 13
###begin p 14
This table provides information on specific mutations identified in different domains and regions of connexin 50 in association with congenital cataract in families belonging to different ethnic groups. Most of these mutations are missense mutations, and the cataract phenotypes are zonular/nuclear pulverulent type with powdery dust like opacities. However, the phenotype balloon-like cataract with Y-sutural opacities linked with p.P88Q substitution observed in present family had no pulverized dust like opacities in the lens. The feathery opacities in between the Y-sutural opacities and three riders at the perimetry of the opaque fetal nucleus are very much prominent in the affected lenses in the present family.
###end p 14

###begin title 19
Mutation analysis
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 301 303 301 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 395 400 395 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYAA</italic>
###xml 422 428 422 428 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYBA1</italic>
###xml 452 458 452 458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYBB2</italic>
###xml 480 485 480 485 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGA</italic>
###xml 508 513 508 513 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGB</italic>
###xml 536 541 536 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGC</italic>
###xml 564 569 564 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CRYGD</italic>
###xml 592 596 592 596 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GJA3</italic>
###xml 624 628 624 628 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GJA8</italic>
###xml 780 782 780 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 783 785 783 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 786 788 786 788 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 968 970 968 970 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">TM</sup>
Informed consent was obtained for each individual studied. This study was approved by the ethics review board of the Guru Nanak Dev University (Amritsar, Punjab, India), consistent with the provisions of the Declaration of Helsinki. Blood was drawn and DNA isolated using method of Adeli and Ogbonna [11]. Mutation screening was performed in the exonic regions of the following candidate genes: CRYAA (21q22.3; GenBank ), CRYBA1 (17q11-q12; GenBank ), CRYBB2 (22q11.2; GenBank ), CRYGA (2q33-q35; GenBank ), CRYGB (2q33-q35; GenBank ), CRYGC (2q33-q35; GenBank ), CRYGD (2q33-q35; GenBank ), GJA3 (13q11-q13; GenBank ), and GJA8 (1q21-q25; GenBank ). The coding regions and exon-intron boundaries of the candidate genes were amplified using previously published primer sequences [10,12-16]. Genomic DNA from all three affected and one unaffected individual was amplified. Purified polymerase chain reaction (PCR) products were sequenced bidirectionally with ABI BigDyeTM Terminator Cycle Sequencing Ready Reaction Kit version 3.1 (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA) for a 10 microl final volume containing 5.0 microl of purified PCR product, 4.0 microl of BigDye Terminator ready reaction mix, and 3.2 pmol of primer. Cycling conditions were: 95 degreesC for 2 min, 25 cycles at 95 degreesC for 30 s, 52 degreesC for 15 s, and 60 degreesC for 4 min. The sequencing reaction products were purified by the isopropanol precipitation method (ABI protocol; Applied Biosystems), resuspended in 10 microl of loading buffer (5:1 ratio of deionized formamide and 25 mM EDTA with blue dextran [50 mg/ml]), denatured at 95 degreesC for 5 min, and electrophoresed on 4% denaturing polyacrylamide gels on the DNA sequencer (ABI-Prism 377; Applied Biosystems). Sequencing results were assembled and analyzed using the SeqMan II program of the Lasergene package (DNA STAR Inc., Madison, WI).
###end p 20
###begin title 21
Results
###end title 21
###begin title 22
Phenotype description
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 268 277 268 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f2">Figure 2A</xref>
Slit lamp examination of the lenses in affected individuals (III:1, III:2) showed that the cataract affected the fetal nucleus with additional changes on its surface. The fetal nucleus appeared semi-opaque. The Y-sutures on the anterior surface seem sharp and narrow (Figure 2A). In between the Y-sutures, there appeared feathery opacities extending up to three-fourths of the length of the Y-sutures. Along the perimeter of the fetal nucleus, three prominent riders were present. The posterior Y-sutures were not clearly seen through the anterior central feathery opacification, but its presence is in no doubt. Inside the semi-opaque fetal nucleus, there appeared no opacities.
###end p 23

###begin title 25
Mutation screening
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 105 113 105 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f3">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 147 151 147 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GJA8</italic>
Bidirectional sequencing of the coding regions of the candidate genes showed a heterozygous change, C>A (Figure 3), at position 262 (c.262 C>A) in GJA8 in all three affected individuals. This substitution was not seen in the unaffected mother or in 96 unrelated control subjects (192 chromosomes) from the same North Indian population as tested by bidirectional sequence analysis (data not shown). The substitution replaces an evolutionarily highly conserved proline by glutamine at amino acid position 88 (p.P88Q) in the second alpha-helical transmembrane domain 2 (M2) of connexin 50.

###begin title 28
Discussion
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 137 138 137 138 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 260 262 260 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 263 265 263 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 428 430 428 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 188 194 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Connexins are integral membrane proteins with four transmembrane domains, two extracellular loops, and an intracellular loop with both NH2- and COOH-termini localized in the cytoplasm. In humans, at least 20 connexins classified into three families are known [17,18]. Connexin 46 and connexin 50 are responsible for joining the lens cells into a functional syncytium. In addition, connexin 50 is also important for lens growth [19]. The observed p.P88Q substitution is centrally located within the second alpha-helical transmembrane domain (M2) of connexin 50 and replaces the highly conserved hydrophobic proline by the non-polar glutamine at position 88, in association with the congenital cataract in the present family.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 122 124 122 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 431 432 431 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 433 435 433 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 623 625 623 625 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 626 628 626 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
The same mutation was seen in a British family. However, this mutation was associated with lamellar pulverulent cataract [20]. In another family of English descent, a mutation of the same codon lead to the substitution of proline by serine (p.P88S), which is associated with nuclear pulverulent cataract, characterized by innumerable powdery opacities located in the nuclear (central) and perinuclear (lamellar) zones of the lens [8,10]. Further, functional analyses revealed that mutants p.P88S and p.P88Q act as dominant negative inhibitors and significantly decreases the activity of co-expressed wild type connexin 50 [20,21].
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 267 268 267 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 269 270 269 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 460 461 460 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 462 463 462 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 464 465 464 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 593 600 593 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 672 674 672 674 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 821 830 821 830 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f2">Figure 2B</xref>
###xml 1061 1068 1061 1068 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 1170 1172 1170 1172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 98 104 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 119 123 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 128 134 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Mutations in connexin 46 and connexin 50 have been reported to be linked with cataractogenesis in humans as well as in mice. In humans, over a dozen mutations in each connexin 46 and connexin 50 gene have so far been detected in association with congenital cataract [2,7] and significant interfamilial phenotypic variability. The phenotype in most cases with mutations in connexin 46 and connexin 50 has been described as zonular/nuclear pulverulent cataract [3,5,7]. The cataract phenotype in the present family differs from these as no "pulverized" dust-like opacities are seen in the lens (Table 1). It also differs from the British family with the identical mutation [20], which showed linear dense vertical opacities inside the fetal nucleus with the embryonic nucleus remaining clear and without sutural opacities (Figure 2B). In the present family, Y-sutural opacities are very prominent, comparable with the p.V79L mutation in the second transmembrane domain (M2) of connexin 50, which is linked with "full moon" like cataract with Y-sutural opacities (Table 1) in another Indian family having 15 affected members in three generations reported previously by us [22].
###end p 31
###begin p 32
In summary, we describe a heterozygous p.P88Q mutation in connexin 50 that showed marked phenotypic differences to previously reported cases affecting the same codon. Thus, variants in other genes might act as modifiers of the cataract phenotype.
