Targeted Deletion of HIF-1alpha Gene in T Cells Prevents their Inhibition in Hypoxic Inflamed Tissues and Improves Septic Mice Survival
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 252 256 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Conceived and designed the experiments: MS MT. Performed the experiments: SK MT SK. Analyzed the data: MT. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: DL AL PC. Wrote the paper: MS SK MT SK. Other: Isolated T-cells: AO. Performed ELISA: PS SK. Bred mice: CC. Performed cell cycle analyses: CC.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 7 15 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Sepsis patients may die either from an overwhelming systemic immune response and/or from an immunoparalysis-associated lack of anti-bacterial immune defence. We hypothesized that bacterial superantigen-activated T cells may be prevented from contribution into anti-bacterial response due to the inhibition of their effector functions by the hypoxia inducible transcription factor (HIF-1alpha) in inflamed and hypoxic areas.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 124 131 120 127 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 40 44 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 318 322 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Using the Cre-lox-P-system we generated mice with a T-cell targeted deletion of the HIF-1alpha gene and analysed them in an in vivo model of bacterial sepsis. We show that deletion of the HIF-1alpha gene leads to higher levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines, stronger anti-bacterial effects and much better survival of mice. These effects can be at least partially explained by significantly increased NF-kappaB activation in TCR activated HIF-1 alpha deficient T cells.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
T cells can be recruited to powerfully contribute to anti-bacterial response if they are relieved from inhibition by HIF-1alpha in inflamed and hypoxic areas. Our experiments uncovered the before unappreciated reserve of anti-bacterial capacity of T cells and suggest novel therapeutic anti-pathogen strategies based on targeted deletion or inhibition of HIF-1 alpha in T cells.
###end p 7

###xml 2415 2419 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The surprisingly low incidence of serious complications after normal immune response to pathogens suggests the functioning of regulatory mechanisms that limit the collateral inflammatory damage to normal cells. It was suggested that it is the damage to the microvasculature and the ensuing decrease in oxygen supply (local tissue hypoxia) that may serve as primary signals of excessive tissue damage and need to de-activate immune cells [1]. One of the hypoxia-generated signals of excessive inflammation could be the accumulation of extracellular adenosine [2], while the second signal could be provided by a hypoxia-sensing molecule [1]. It was proposed that the overactive T-cells in inflamed and hypoxic areas are down-regulated by A2A adenosine receptors [1] and hypoxia inducible factor 1alpha (HIF-1alpha) [3], [4] which may act in concert [5], [6]. While the genetic evidence for the critical role of A2A adenosine receptors in down-regulation of activated immune cells in vivo has been provided using A2A receptor deficient mice [2], [6], thereby confirming the overall validity of this "anti-inflammatory hypoxia" hypotheses, the anti-inflammatory role of HIF-1alpha in T cells in vivo remained to be proven using appropriate genetic models. The hypothesis of HIF-1alpha being anti-inflammatory in T cells seems to be counter-intuitive, since the myeloid cell-targeted deletion of HIF-1alpha resulted in the loss of inflammatory response by myeloid cells leading to the impression that HIF-1alpha in myeloid cells is pro-inflammatory[7]. Despite these findings, we reasoned that T cells may be different from macrophages and neutrophils, which are known to be much more dependent on glycolysis-derived ATP and therefore on glycolysis-controlling HIF-1alpha- [7]. We considered that since HIF-1alpha is not as critical for activated T cells' short term survival as it is for myeloid cells, it may play an important T cell-down regulating role in inflamed and hypoxic tissue microenvironments in vivo by acting in concert with other hypoxia-triggered mechanisms [1], [5]. To directly test this we used mice with T cell targeted deletion of HIF-1alpha gene. The present studies of bacterial sepsis show that T-cell specific deletion of HIF-1 alpha in mice results in: i) higher levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines; ii) stronger anti-bacterial effects of T cells and granulocytes and iii) better survival of mice from cecal ligation and puncture (CLP)-induced bacterial sepsis [8]. In vitro studies to clarify the possible mechanism of the in vivo observations revealed much faster cell proliferation and enhanced activation of NF-kappaB in HIF-1alpha deficient T cells. Quantitative real-time RT-PCR showed higher NF-kappaB p50 subunit expression in TCR stimulated T cells upon HIF-1alpha deletion, suggesting that the HIF-1alpha-mediated pathway is at least in part responsible for the suppression of NF-kappaB dependent cell proliferation and pro-inflammatory cytokine production. These experiments uncover the before unappreciated reserve of pro-inflammatory capacity of T cells in vivo and suggest novel therapeutic strategies when there is a need to enhance activity of T cells in hypoxic and/or inflamed tissues. We propose to inhibit HIF-1alpha in order to relieve from inhibition the "anti-bacterial" T cells during immune response to drug-resistant bacteria.

###begin title 10
Results and Discussion
###end title 10
###begin title 11
T-cells are present in hypoxic inflamed peritoneum but do not seem to contribute to anti-bacterial immune response
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 93 97 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The comparison of survival rates of WT (n = 10) vs. T cell deficient RAG-2 knockout (n = 10) mice revealed no differences suggesting that T cells do not contribute to an anti-sepsis immune response in our model of sepsis (data not shown). We tested whether this could be explained by i ) the lack of T cells' presence in hypoxic inflamed areas or ii) by hypoxic inhibition of activated T cells in the local inflamed microenvironment.

###begin p 16
###xml 405 409 393 397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Caldwell1">[10]</xref>
###xml 238 242 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To test whether the failure to observe T cells' contribution in anti-bacterial response and pathogen destruction was due to their negative regulation by hypoxia-stabilized and TCR-activation induced HIF-1alpha, we studied newly developed mice with T cell targeted deletion of the HIF-1 alpha gene. We hypothesized that if HIF-1alpha was indeed one of the mediators of immunosuppressive effects of hypoxia [10] by inhibiting T cells, then it would be expected that HIF-1alpha gene-deficient T cells would be resistant to inhibition in inflamed and hypoxic areas of peritoneum. This, in turn, would be expected to result in uninhibited production of e.g. IFN-gamma and of other pathogen-destroying cytokines by activated T cells, as well as in the overall enhancement of the anti-bacterial host response and better survival.
###end p 16

###xml 55 59 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Selective disruption of HIF-1 alpha in T cells rescues mice from septic death and decreases bacterial burden-associated tissue damage
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 341 344 329 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Ebong1">[8]</xref>

The possible contribution of T cells may have been overlooked in models of sepsis with early (<24 hour) lethal outcomes, since T cells may require longer than 24 hrs to be recruited to and activated in inflamed environment. Therefore, we have adapted a more long-lasting model of sepsis with approximately50% mortality after 72 hours of CLP [8] to allow sufficient time for T cell recruitment and activation in inflamed peritoneum so that T cells will have an opportunity to contribute to the anti-bacterial response. Comparative studies of mice with the confirmed HIF-1 alpha deletion in their T cells (Fig. 1B) supported the view that HIF-1alpha in T cells did inhibit them during sepsis. This was reflected in a statistically significant increase in survival of mice with HIF-1alpha-deficient T cells as compared with their HIF-1 alpha expressing littermates (Fig. 1C). Survival between lck-Cre (+) and lck-Cre (-) control mice was not different, demonstrating that the difference in survival between lck-Cre (+) HIF-1 alpha loxP and lck-Cre (-) HIF-1 alpha loxP mice was indeed due to Cre mediated HIF-1 alpha knock-down (data not shown). In another control, the sham CLP surgery did not result in any mouse mortality (data not shown).

The protective effects of genetic inactivation of HIF-1alpha in T cells are most likely due to the relief from the negative regulation by HIF-1alpha of pro-inflammatory, anti-bacterial functions of T cells. This is reflected in the strong attenuation of bacterial burden in spleen and liver of HIF-1 alpha-deleted mice (Fig. 2A). The quantitative data showing that the decrease in bacterial counts are paralleled by a dramatic decrease in the number of gas-forming bacteria in HIF-1 alpha-deficient mice. For instance, Fig. 2B shows that masses of gas-forming bacteria formed rings around gas bubbles that also contained free bacteria in the spleen of HIF-1 alpha-expressing mice. Such intensive bacterial growth was not observed in littermates with HIF-1 alpha-deficient T cells. These data provide direct microbiological and histological evidence that HIF-1 alpha inhibits anti-bacterial activities of T cells (Fig. 2A,B). Inactivation of HIF-1 alpha in T cells might contribute to amplification of bactericidal functions of cells of the innate immune system. Accordingly, granulocytes isolated from septic mice with T cell specific HIF-1 alpha deficiency were much stronger activated as indicated by levels of CD11b cell surface expression and H2O2 production (Fig. 2C). In agreement with the general view that IFN- gamma is a likely candidate for T cell dependent enhancement of bactericidal functions of granulocytes, the observed rescue from septic death and the decrease in the bacteria-mediated tissue damage (Fig. 1C and 2A,B) in mice with HIF-1 alpha protein deficiency in T cells suggested to test for a possible HIF-1alpha deficiency-induced "de-inhibition" of secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines in TCR-activated T cells.

Genetic deletion of HIF-1alpha in T cells resulted in increased TCR triggered cell proliferation as shown by significantly higher percentages of cells which had undergone three or four cell divisions upon anti-CD3 mediated activation (Fig. 3A). Although enhanced cell proliferation may result from direct relief of cell cycle arrest as induced by loss of HIF-1alpha e.g. in B cells [11], the increase in IL-2 production we found in HIF-1alpha deficient T cells might account for enhanced T cell proliferation as well (Fig. 3B). Besides IL-2, IFN-gamma secretion was significantly increased with the latter confirmed at the intracellular level in HIF-1alpha deficient peritoneal T cells harvested 72 h after CLP (Fig. 3C). No extracellularly secreted IFN-gamma could be detected in blood serum or peritoneal lavage fluid neither of HIF-1alpha KO or HIF-1alpha WT mice. However, in agreement with IFN-gamma acting as an autocrine or paracrine biological immune response modifier which enhances the production of other pro-inflammatory cytokines [12], levels of TNF-alpha and IL-6 after cecal ligation and puncture were higher in peritoneal lavage fluid and blood serum in HIF-1alpha KO than in HIF-1alpha WT mice (Fig. 3D).

###begin title 29
Deletion of HIF-1alpha enhances TCR stimulated NF-kappaB activation
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 91 95 87 91 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Andreakos1">[16]</xref>
###xml 381 385 365 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Walmsley1">[17]</xref>
###xml 610 617 578 585 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Fig. 4A</bold>
###xml 610 617 578 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000853-g004"><bold>Fig. 4A</bold></xref>
###xml 738 742 698 702 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Walmsley1">[17]</xref>
###xml 859 863 815 819 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Zheng1">[18]</xref>
###xml 1165 1172 1109 1116 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Fig. 4B</bold>
###xml 1165 1172 1109 1116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000853-g004"><bold>Fig. 4B</bold></xref>
###xml 1384 1391 1316 1323 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Fig. 4C</bold>
###xml 1384 1391 1316 1323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0000853-g004"><bold>Fig. 4C</bold></xref>
###xml 1622 1626 1542 1546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Chen1">[19]</xref>
###xml 1639 1643 1559 1563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Eltzschig1">[20]</xref>
###xml 1742 1746 1662 1666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Ten1">[21]</xref>
The NF-kappaB transcription factors are key regulators of inflammatory and immune response [16]. We hypothesized that observed effects of HIF-1alpha deletion in T cells may be due to HIF-1alpha affecting T cells by acting through NF-kB. Indeed, studies of neutrophils did suggest that NF-kappaB could be hypoxia-regulated and a HIF-dependent target to increase neutrophil survival [17]. Accordingly, we studied the effects of HIF-1alpha deletion on NF-kappaB activity in T cells. As shown by EMSA, NF-kappaB DNA binding activity was enhanced in nuclear extracts of TCR stimulated HIF-1alpha deficient T cells (Fig. 4A). While in neutrophils NF-kappaB p65 expression plays a major role in HIF-1alpha dependent regulation of NF-kB activity [17], the TCR-stimulated NF-kappaB activation in T cells comprises p50+RelA and p50+cRel heterodimers and p50 homodimers [18]. NF-kappaB family profiling (p65, p50, c-Rel, p52 and RelB ) of DNA binding activity of T cell nuclear extracts showed that upon TCR activation the activity of both the p50 and the p65 subunits significantly increased in HIF-1alpha deficient cells as compared to T cells with intact HIF-1alpha genes (Fig. 4B). To find out whether these findings are due to NF-kappaB transcription, we performed quantitative RT-PCR which confirmed a significant increase in NF-kappaB p50 mRNA expression in HIF-1alpha deficient T cells (Fig. 4C). Thus, HIF-1alpha is likely to suppress TCR stimulated T cell responses by preventing p50 transcription and thereby inhibiting NF-kappaB activation. Although HIF-1alpha is capable to directly inhibit transcription (e.g. CAD gene [19] or ENT gene [20]), we were not able to identify putative HRE sites in the promoter region of the p50 encoding gene [21] by analyses using a hidden Markov model (HMM) algorithm. Alternatively, HIF-1alpha might affect a multiplicity of yet unknown proteins or transcription factors influencing the p50 transcription and therefore may indirectly suppress p50 expression.

###begin p 33
###xml 281 285 273 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Caldwell1">[10]</xref>
###xml 391 395 379 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Kojima1">[14]</xref>
###xml 486 494 470 478 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 494 498 478 482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Lukashev1">[15]</xref>
The reported here consideration and studies of an inhibitory role of HIF-1alpha in regulation of activated T cells have been prompted by several lines of earlier observations: i) hypoxia inhibited the TCR-induced pro-inflammatory cytokine (e.g. IFN- gamma) accumulation by T cells [10]; ii) the deletion of HIF-1alpha in T and B cells resulted in an increased tissue damage and autoimmunity [14] and iii) the deletion of HIF-1alpha in T cells resulted in enhanced activation of T cells in vitro[15].
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 129 136 125 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
The data presented here provide the first direct genetic evidence for the negative regulation of T cell activities by HIF-1alpha in vivo. Furthermore, we show that the observed anti-inflammatory effect of HIF-1alpha during anti-bacterial response results from suppression of T cell proliferation and pro-inflammatory cytokine production most likely due to inhibition of NF-kappaB/p50 transcription and subsequent reduction of NF-kappaB activity.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 232 239 224 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1028 1030 1008 1010 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2A</sub>
###xml 1051 1055 1031 1035 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Ohta2">[22]</xref>
Of potential clinical significance, these observations offer a new approach to enhance the immune response by de-inhibiting T cells from HIF-1alpha-mediated immunosuppression and thereby enhancing production of IFN-gamma by T cells in vivo. This may-in turn-lead to a greater activation of cells of the innate immune system, which then play a key role in the early bacterial elimination. Our findings may re-invigorate the attention to the anti-bacterial effects of T cells and appeal to recruit T cells' powerful, but otherwise unavailable anti-bacterial capacity in novel anti-sepsis therapies. Moreover, our observations of T cells inhibition by HIF-1alpha in hypoxic inflamed peritoneum suggest that activated anti-tumor T cells may be also inhibited near or within hypoxic tumors by HIF-1alpha. The HIF-1alpha-mediated inhibition may be additive or synergistic with immunosupression caused by tumor hypoxia-produced extracellular adenosine that is protecting tumors by inhibiting the incoming anti-tumor T cells via their A2A adenosine receptors [22].
###end p 35

Materials and Methods
###end title 36
###begin title 37
###xml 53 57 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Generation of lck-Cre+/HIF1-alpha loxP floxed/floxed mice
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 91 95 87 91 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Lukashev1">[15]</xref>
###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 180 184 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 335 340 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 402 407 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Mice with T cell-targeted deletion of HIF-1 alpha gene have been generated as described in [15]. This allows for a T cell lineage specific HIF-1 alpha deletion in lck-Cre positive mice, while the lck-Cre negative littermates are HIF-1 alpha positive and can be used as controls. The gross phenotype of the lck-Cre (+) HIF-1 alpha loxP mouse was not markedly different from lck Cre (-) HIF-1 alpha loxP mouse (HIF-1 alpha expressing littermates) as judged by viability, weight, or limb deformities.

###begin title 39
Determination of deletion efficiency
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 254 258 246 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Lukashev2">[23]</xref>
###xml 73 77 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
For the measurement of deletion efficiency of HIF-1 alpha in lck-Cre (+) mice the purified T cells were obtained by positive selection on AutoMACS cell separator using anti-CD3 mAb and HIF-1 alpha mRNA expression was determined by real time RT-PCR as in [23] using primers spanning the targeted region as well as primers for an undeleted control gene for normalization.
###end p 40
###begin title 41
Cecal ligation and puncture
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 219 222 219 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Ebong1">[8]</xref>
###xml 112 118 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 565 569 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To enable the investigation of the possible role of T cells in clearing bacterial infections, we have adapted a murine model of sepsis, which results in 50% mortality after 72 hours of cecal ligation and puncture (CLP) [8]. By avoiding the early (<24 hour) lethal events of sepsis enabled us to study the behavior of T cells, which may have required longer than 24 hrs to be recruited and activated in inflamed environment. CLP was performed always by the same scientist who was blinded to the experimental design until the final time point of experiment (death of mice). Sham-treated controls underwent the same surgical procedures (i.e. laparotomy and resuscitation), but the cecum was neither ligated nor punctured.
###end p 42

Bacterial counts were performed on aseptically harvested spleens, lungs and livers as described previously [24]. The tissues were weighed, homogenized, and cellular debris pelleted. Isolation of mononuclear cells from spleen, lymph nodes and peritoneum

Spleens (lymph nodes) were homogenized using a 70 microm cell strainer. Cells from the peritoneum were collected by insertion and withdrawing of 10 ml of sterile PBS from the peritoneal space. ###xml 256 260 256 260 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0000853-Kuboki1">[26]</xref>

Quantitative RT-PCR

###begin p 64
Spleen samples were homogenized before Total RNA isolation using the RNeasy mini kit (Qiagen) including DNAse treatment following the manufacturer's protocol. Real-time qPCR was performed in duplicates with the Light Cycler480 instrument (Roche Diagnostics) using the Roche's SYBR-Green mastermix and prevalidated primer pairs (Qiagen). The thermal cycler conditions were 35 cycles of 94degreesC for 20 s, 55degreesC for 20 s, and 72degreesC for 30 s. Relative mRNA expression was calculated with the Relative Quantification software (Roche Diagnostics) using an efficiency-corrected algorithm with standard curves and 18S ribosomal RNA as an endogenous reference.
