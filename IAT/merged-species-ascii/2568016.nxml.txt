SPDL-1 functions as a kinetochore receptor for MDF-1 in Caenorhabditis elegans
###end article-title 0

###begin p 3
###xml 257 267 257 267 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1/MAD3</italic>
###xml 280 286 280 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spdl-1</italic>
###xml 290 312 290 312 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caenorhabditis elegans</italic>
###xml 290 312 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
The spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) ensures faithful chromosome segregation by delaying anaphase onset until all sister kinetochores are attached to bipolar spindles. An RNA interference screen for synthetic genetic interactors with a conserved SAC gene, san-1/MAD3, identified spdl-1, a Caenorhabditis elegans homologue of Spindly. SPDL-1 protein localizes to the kinetochore from prometaphase to metaphase, and this depends on KNL-1, a highly conserved kinetochore protein, and CZW-1/ZW10, a component of the ROD-ZW10-ZWILCH complex. In two-cell-stage embryos harboring abnormal monopolar spindles, SPDL-1 is required to induce the SAC-dependent mitotic delay and localizes the SAC protein MDF-1/MAD1 to the kinetochore facing away from the spindle pole. In addition, SPDL-1 coimmunoprecipitates with MDF-1/MAD1 in vivo. These results suggest that SPDL-1 functions in a kinetochore receptor of MDF-1/MAD1 to induce SAC function.
###end p 3

###begin title 5
Introduction
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 208 213 208 213 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CDC20</sup>
###xml 345 367 345 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">May and Hardwick, 2006</xref>
###xml 369 395 369 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib24">Musacchio and Salmon, 2007</xref>
To ensure faithful chromosome segregation, the spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) monitors the status of kinetochore-microtubule binding and inhibits activity of the anaphase-promoting complex/cyclosome (APC/CCDC20), thereby delaying anaphase onset until all sister kinetochores have properly attached to bipolar mitotic spindles (for review see May and Hardwick, 2006; Musacchio and Salmon, 2007).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 169 186 169 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib11">Hoyt et al., 1991</xref>
###xml 188 207 188 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Li and Murray, 1991</xref>
###xml 475 494 475 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib29">Sironi et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 496 518 496 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib3">De Antoni et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 709 721 709 721 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Karess, 2005</xref>
###xml 782 800 782 800 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib33">Starr et al., 1998</xref>
###xml 904 921 904 921 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Kops et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 1002 1025 1002 1025 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib6">Famulski and Chan, 2007</xref>
###xml 162 167 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The SAC signaling pathway is mediated by highly conserved proteins such as MAD1-3, BUB1, and BUB3, first identified by two independent genetic screens in budding yeast (Hoyt et al., 1991; Li and Murray, 1991). These proteins temporally associate with kinetochores that have not achieved bipolar attachment. MAD1 specifically localizes and recruits MAD2 to microtubule-free kinetochores and facilitates binding of MAD2 and CDC20, an APC/C activator, thereby inhibiting APC/C (Sironi et al., 2001; De Antoni et al., 2005). However, how MAD1 is specifically targeted to unattached kinetochores is yet unanswered. In metazoan cells, the ROD-ZW10-ZWILCH (RZZ) complex is crucial in the SAC pathway (for review see Karess, 2005). The RZZ complex recruits dynein/dynactin to kinetochores (Starr et al., 1998). Although the RZZ complex is required to regulate levels of MAD1 and MAD2 at unattached kinetochores (Kops et al., 2005), it localizes to not only microtubule-free but also tension-free kinetochores (Famulski and Chan, 2007).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 3 25 3 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caenorhabditis elegans</italic>
###xml 187 210 187 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Kitagawa and Rose, 1999</xref>
###xml 243 262 243 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Nystul et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 264 285 264 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">Encalada et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 3 25 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
In Caenorhabditis elegans, MDF-1/MAD1 is required for SAC-dependent delay of mitotic progression induced by chemical or mutational disruption of microtubules in proliferating germ cells (Kitagawa and Rose, 1999) and early-stage embryogenesis (Nystul et al., 2003; Encalada et al., 2005). However, MDF-1 loss hardly affects cell cycle progression during early embryogenesis.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 109 128 109 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Nystul et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 162 172 162 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 186 191 186 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1</italic>
###xml 232 250 232 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib34">Stein et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 320 330 320 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 370 375 370 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdf-1</italic>
###xml 379 389 379 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdf-2/MAD2</italic>
###xml 450 473 450 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Kitagawa and Rose, 1999</xref>
###xml 493 498 493 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdf-1</italic>
###xml 561 580 561 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Furuta et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 582 603 582 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">Kitagawa et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 605 626 605 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib35">Tarailo et al., 2007a</xref>
###xml 666 671 666 671 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CDC20</sup>
###xml 831 836 831 836 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdf-1</italic>
###xml 838 843 838 843 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdf-2</italic>
###xml 848 853 848 853 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1</italic>
###xml 948 966 948 966 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib34">Stein et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 162 172 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 320 330 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
SAN-1/MAD3 is also essential for microtubule defect- or anoxia-induced mitotic delay in early-stage embryos (Nystul et al., 2003) but not for animal viability in C. elegans; deletion of san-1 causes no severe developmental defects (Stein et al., 2007). In contrast, despite being dispensable during early embryogenesis, C. elegans strains carrying deletion mutations in mdf-1 or mdf-2/MAD2 exhibit severe defects in larval and germ cell development (Kitagawa and Rose, 1999). Lethality of the mdf-1 deletion strain is suppressed by reduction of APC/C activity (Furuta et al., 2000; Kitagawa et al., 2002; Tarailo et al., 2007a), suggesting that MDF-1 regulates APC/CCDC20 activity during development. The defect in metaphase-to-anaphase transition in meiosis I caused by APC/C mutants can be suppressed by hypomorphic mutations in mdf-1, mdf-2, or san-1, suggesting that these SAC components are required to regulate APC/C activity during meiosis (Stein et al., 2007).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 103 109 103 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spdl-1</italic>
###xml 326 336 326 336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 326 336 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
An RNAi-based genomewide screen for synthetic genetic interactors with known SAC components identified spdl-1, which is required to activate the SAC pathway, particularly MDF-1-mediated signaling pathways. Our findings suggest that SPDL-1 functions in the SAC pathway as a constituent of the kinetochore receptor for MDF-1 in C. elegans.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
Results and discussion
###end title 11
###begin title 12
###xml 24 29 24 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1</italic>
SPDL-1, identified as a san-1/MAD3 synthetic genetic interactor, is required for SAC activation
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 140 145 140 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1</italic>
###xml 156 166 156 162 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1&#916;</italic>
###xml 210 229 206 225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Kamath et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 356 361 352 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1</italic>
###xml 563 573 559 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 563 573 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
The RNAi screen for genes whose depletion caused phenotypes that were embryonic lethal or had reduced number of progeny in combination with san-1 deletion (san-1Delta) by using Ahringer's RNAi feeding library (Kamath et al., 2003) identified known components of spindles, kinetochore, and cohesion as well as other SAC components (unpublished data). Thus, san-1 synthetic lethal genes include genes whose depletion activates the SAC and those required for SAC activation. (To avoid confusion caused by the inconsistency of nomenclature among organisms, names for C. elegans genes used in this study are listed in Fig. S1 A, available at .)
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 16 21 16 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1</italic>
###xml 293 301 293 301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 A</xref>
###xml 386 394 386 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 A</xref>
###xml 490 515 490 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib1">Cheeseman and Desai, 2008</xref>
###xml 685 705 685 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Griffis et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 732 738 732 738 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spdl-1</italic>
Uncharacterized san-1 synthetic lethal genes were further screened for genes whose depletion by RNAi bypasses the mitotic delay induced by ZYG-1 deficiency in two-cell-stage embryos (see Materials and methods). The mitotic delay in ZYG-1-depleted embryos was bypassed by codepletion of MDF-1 (Fig. 1 A), indicating the delay was SAC dependent. This secondary screen identified C06A8.5 (Fig. 1 A), which encodes a protein-sharing sequence similarity with Spindly family proteins (Fig. S1 B; Cheeseman and Desai, 2008). Although our finding that SAC activation needs C06A8.5 does not support that it behaves as an orthologue of Spindly, which silences the SAC rather than activating it (Griffis et al., 2007), we designated this gene spdl-1 as assigned by the Caenorhabditis Genetic Center to be consistent in gene naming.
###end p 14

###xml 121 131 117 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdf-2&#916;</italic>
###xml 320 330 312 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1&#916;</italic>
###xml 361 366 349 354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdf-1</italic>
###xml 370 380 358 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdf-2&#916;</italic>
###xml 431 441 415 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1&#916;</italic>
###xml 459 464 439 444 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdf-1</italic>
###xml 479 489 459 465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdf-2&#916;</italic>
RNAi of spdl-1 caused synthetic lethality with san-1Delta but did not affect viability of the mdf-1 mutant allele or the mdf-2Delta allele (Fig. S1 C). This result was in contrast to that obtained for other candidates of the screen, such as HCP-1, BUB-3, or GOA-1, whose depletion was synthetically lethal with not only san-1Delta but also the mutant allele of mdf-1 or mdf-2Delta (Fig. S1 C). MDF-1 depletion was also lethal with san-1Delta but not with the mdf-1 mutant or the mdf-2Delta allele (Fig. S1 C), suggesting that SPDL-1 functions in an MDF-1-mediated pathway.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 47 53 47 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spdl-1</italic>
###xml 241 251 241 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1&#916;</italic>
###xml 301 307 297 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spdl-1</italic>
RNAi by feeding the bacterial clone expressing spdl-1 double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) only partially depleted SPDL-1, with little effect on brood size or number of progeny of wild-type worms, but considerably reduced viability of progeny in the san-1Delta strain (Fig. S1 C). However, microinjection with spdl-1 dsRNA into gonad arms of wild-type adult hermaphrodites caused substantial reduction of SPDL-1 (Fig. S1 D) and the embryonic lethal phenotype at high penetrance (not depicted). Thus, SPDL-1 is indispensable for embryogenesis.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 172 180 169 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 B</xref>
###xml 446 454 443 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 C</xref>
###xml 540 563 537 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib31">Sonnichsen et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 735 743 732 740 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 C</xref>
###xml 989 997 986 994 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 C</xref>
###xml 999 1017 996 1014 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Desai et al., 2003</xref>
During chromosome segregation in embryos expressing GFP-histone H2B and GFP-beta-tubulin, depletion of SPDL-1 caused severe chromosome missegregation at the first mitosis (Fig. 1 B). In SPDL-1-depleted embryos, chromosome congression was incomplete and sister chromatids being separated were often connected by lagging chromosomes. Regardless of the presence of lagging chromosomes, cytokinesis occurred at the same time as in untreated embryos (Fig. 1 C), leading to karyomere formation at a frequency similar to that reported previously (Sonnichsen et al., 2005). Time course analysis revealed that depletion of SPDL-1 induced rapid and premature centrosome separation, which reflects a defect in kinetochore-microtubule attachment (Fig. 1 C), suggesting that SPDL-1 plays a role in proper kinetochore-microtubule attachment in addition to the SAC function. However, the defect was less severe than that caused by depletion of NDC-80, a constituent of the core microtubule-binding site (Fig. 1 C; Desai et al., 2003).
###end p 18

We next tested the genetic interaction of SPDL-1 and the APC/C, the downstream target of the SAC. At 24degreesC, mat-3/APC8 temperature-sensitive allele, mat-3(or180) (referred to as mat-3ts), causes embryonic arrest at metaphase of meiosis I at high penetrance (Table I; Golden et al., 2000). When mat-3ts embryos were depleted of SPDL-1, they exited meiosis and entered the mitotic cycle with abnormal chromosome segregation, resulting in arrested embryos with multiple cells, which resembles the previously described suppression effect of the mdf-1Delta allele on the mat-3ts phenotype (Tables I and II; Stein et al., 2007). Thus, SPDL-1 depletion bypassed the meiosis I metaphase arrest of mat-3ts, possibly by decreasing the SAC activity to inhibit the APC/C.

###xml 1145 1156 1121 1128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spdl-1&#916;</italic>
###xml 1185 1196 1157 1164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spdl-1&#916;</italic>

###xml 1268 1279 1236 1243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spdl-1&#916;</italic>
###xml 1369 1377 1333 1341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">emb-30ts</italic>
###xml 1462 1473 1426 1433 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spdl-1&#916;</italic>
The spdl-1 deletion allele spdl-1(ok1515) (referred to as spdl-1Delta) is a recessive lethal allele. The spdl-1Delta homozygotes that were segregated from spdl-1Delta hemizygotes survived through embryogenesis may be due to maternal contribution but showed larval lethal or sterile phenotype (unpublished data). Sterile spdl-1Delta homozygotes had oocytes that prematurely exited meiotic prophase, resulting in endomitosis (Emo phenotype; Fig. S2 A, available at ; Iwasaki et al., 1996). Oocytes having extra chromosomes were also identified (Fig. S2, B and C). Sterility was suppressed by ectopic expression of GFP-SPDL-1 (unpublished data), confirming that the germline defect was caused by loss of SPDL-1 function. mdf-1Delta homozygotes also showed an abnormal number of bivalents in oocytes and the Emo phenotype (Kitagawa and Rose, 1999). As the sterile phenotype of mdf-1Delta homozygotes is suppressed by the temperature-sensitive allele of emb-30/APC4, emb-30(tn377) (referred to as emb-30ts; Furuta et al., 2000; Kitagawa et al., 2002; Tarailo et al., 2007a), we tested whether an emb-30ts mutation suppresses the sterile phenotype of spdl-1Delta homozygotes. In contrast to spdl-1Delta homozygotes that showed sterility at high penetrance, 90% of emb-30ts; spdl-1Delta homozygotes laid eggs (Fig. S2 D). However, these embryos were arrested, suggesting that emb-30ts mutation suppressed the oocyte maturation defect but not the embryonic lethality of spdl-1Delta homozygotes. These results strongly suggest that SPDL-1 plays a role in regulating APC/C activity during oogenesis presumably via its SAC function.

###begin title 25
Kinetochore localization of SPDL-1 depends on KNL-1 and CZW-1 but not on NDC-80 or BUB-1
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 213 221 213 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 D</xref>

Immunofluorescence microscopy using a SPDL-1-specific antibody (Fig. S2 E) revealed that SPDL-1 localized to microtubules through mitosis and to kinetochores upon nuclear envelope breakdown (NEBD) until anaphase (Fig. 1 D and Fig. S2 F). We confirmed this localization pattern in living embryos expressing GFP-SPDL-1 in the spdl-1Delta background (Video 1, available at ).

###xml 140 158 140 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Desai et al., 2003</xref>

We analyzed the molecular dependency of kinetochore localization of SPDL-1 on other kinetochore proteins. RNAi-mediated depletion of KNL-1 (Desai et al., 2003), a core outer kinetochore component, or CZW-1/ZW10 (Starr et al., 1997), a component of the RZZ complex, significantly reduced the targeting of SPDL-1 on chromosomes (Fig. 2 A) without affecting localization of SPDL-1 on microtubules (Fig. S3 A, available at ) and total amount of cellular SPDL-1 in whole-worm lysate (Fig. 2 B), suggesting that both are specifically required for SPDL-1 targeting to kinetochores. In reciprocal experiments, we observed kinetochore localization of KNL-3 (Cheeseman et al., 2004), a component of the MIS-12 complex; KBP-4 (Cheeseman et al., 2004), a component of the NDC-80 complex; CZW-1; and BUB-1 in SPDL-1-depleted embryos in metaphase (Fig. 2 C). ###begin title 30
SPDL-1 localizes and targets MDF-1 to the kinetochores that face away from the pole in embryonic cells with monopolar spindle
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 345 353 345 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 A</xref>
###xml 470 478 470 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 A</xref>
###xml 578 586 578 586 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 A</xref>
###xml 1041 1062 1041 1062 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib30">Skoufias et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 1064 1087 1064 1087 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib6">Famulski and Chan, 2007</xref>
###xml 1141 1149 1141 1149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 A</xref>
###xml 1211 1219 1211 1219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 A</xref>
###xml 1320 1328 1320 1328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 B</xref>
###xml 1546 1554 1546 1554 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 A</xref>
###xml 1894 1902 1894 1902 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2 A</xref>
###xml 1979 1987 1979 1987 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 B</xref>
We next tested whether depletion of SPDL-1 affects MDF-1 localization at unattached kinetochores. MDF-1-kinetochore association was barely detectable during normal mitosis (unpublished data). In two-cell-stage embryos depleted of ZYG-1, condensed chromosomes formed an arc around the single microtubule aster nucleated from the mono centrosome (Fig. 3 A). In these cells, MDF-1 accumulated along the outer side of the chromosome arc that faces away from the centrosome (Fig. 3 A). In contrast, KNL-1 and NDC-80 localized to both the inner and outer sides of the chromosome arc (Fig. 3 A). These results suggest that sister centromeres were resolved and the outer kinetochore was assembled on both sister chromatids, but one assembled on the side facing away from the centrosomes is not captured, or only laterally captured, by microtubules. Monotelic microtubule attachment could cause the lack of tension on both sister kinetochores. Consistent with the previous finding that BUB1 and the RZZ complex localize to tension-free kinetochores (Skoufias et al., 2001; Famulski and Chan, 2007), BUB-1 and CZW-1 localized as KNL-1 and NDC-80 did (Fig. 3 A). In contrast, SPDL-1 asymmetrically localized as MDF-1 did (Fig. 3 A). Furthermore, MDF-1 associating with chromosomes was substantially reduced by depletion of SPDL-1 (Fig. 3 B and Fig. S3 C). These observations strongly suggest that SPDL-1 is specifically required for MDF-1 targeting to unattached kinetochores. Although CZW-1 localized at both sister kinetochores in ZYG-1-depleted embryos (Fig. 3 A), codepletion of CZW-1 and ZYG-1 substantially reduced localization of SPDL-1 and MDF-1 on the kinetochores facing away from the centrosome (Fig. S3 D), suggesting that SPDL-1 requires CZW-1 for its accumulation on unattached kinetochores as it does in normal mitosis. Depletion of BUB-1 did not affect kinetochore localization of SPDL-1 (Fig. 2 A and Fig. S3 A) but reduced MDF-1 on kinetochores in ZYG-1-depleted embryos (Fig. 3 B). Thus, MDF-1 localization depends on two independent molecules, SPDL-1 and BUB-1.

###begin title 33
SPDL-1 physically associates with MDF-1
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 200 208 200 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 C</xref>
###xml 304 312 304 312 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 C</xref>
###xml 391 392 391 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;</italic>
###xml 409 417 409 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 C</xref>
###xml 419 441 419 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">O'Connell et al., 2001</xref>
We next analyzed the physical interaction between SPDL-1 and MDF-1. There was a small but detectable amount of MDF-1 and MDF-2 in SPDL-1 immunoprecipitates from whole-worm lysate of wild-type strain (Fig. 3 C); reciprocally, SPDL-1 and MDF-2 were detected in immunoprecipitates with anti-MDF-1 antibody (Fig. 3 C). Coimmunoprecipitation of MDF-1 and SPDL-1 increased in the lysate from ZYG-1-deficient worms (Fig. 3 C; O'Connell et al., 2001), suggesting that the MDF-1-MDF-2 complex associates more stably with SPDL-1 at unattached kinetochores.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 19 24 19 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">san-1</italic>
###xml 241 249 241 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 D</xref>
###xml 588 593 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In conclusion, our san-1 synthetic lethal screen identified that SPDL-1 is required for spindle defect-induced activation of the SAC. SPDL-1 localizes at the kinetochore during mitosis in a CZW-1/ZW10-dependent and BUB-1-independent manner (Fig. 3 D). In embryonic cells with the monopolar spindle, SPDL-1 localizes to kinetochores on the side of the chromosome that faces away from the mono centrosome, thereby recruiting MDF-1 via its physical association. MDF-1 localization to unattached kinetochores also depends on BUB-1. We could not show direct interaction of SPDL-1 and MDF-1 by yeast two-hybrid analysis (unpublished data), suggesting that the proteins need additional components for their interaction, or MDF-1 may be modified in a BUB-1 kinase-dependent manner for its interaction with SPDL-1.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 213 221 213 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 D</xref>
###xml 483 503 483 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Griffis et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 796 806 796 806 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 796 806 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
Whether SPDL-1 is actively recruited to unattached kinetochores by interacting with other proteins or specifically excluded from attached kinetochores needs study. High amounts of SPDL-1 on metaphase chromosomes (Fig. 1 D) imply that SPDL-1 is removed from kinetochores after microtubule attachment. SPDL-1 is structurally related to Spindly, which plays a role in recruiting dynein to kinetochores and is translocated from kinetochores to microtubules in a dynein-dependent manner (Griffis et al., 2007). Therefore, dynein may remove SPDL-1 from kinetochores attached to microtubules. Although Spindly does not play the same role as SPDL-1 in MAD1 recruitment in other organisms because the property of MAD1 to localize to unattached kinetochores is highly conserved among eukaryotes (including C. elegans), other organisms might also have a similar regulatory mechanism in which other proteins may play the role of SPDL-1 in recruiting MAD1 to unattached kinetochores. Therefore, elucidating how SPDL-1 accumulates specifically at unattached kinetochores should help unravel the molecular mechanisms by which microtubule attachment status of the kinetochore is sensed to initiate SAC function.

Each RNAi bacterial clone was grown overnight at 37degreesC in low-salt LB (0.5% NaCl) containing 50 mug/ml ampicillin in each well of 96-deep well plates, induced with 1 mM isopropyl beta-d-thiogalactoside at 37degreesC for 4 h. About 10 synchronized L1 larvae of wild-type or san-1Delta strain in 100 mul of complete S medium containing 50 mug/ml ampicillin and 1 mM isopropyl beta-d-thiogalactoside were fed 50 mul of induced bacterial culture in each well of 96-well plates. Wells for wild-type and san-1Delta strains were placed adjacent to each other to allow easy comparison. After 5-8 d of incubation, densities of F1 worms in each well were scored by visual inspection under a microscope. Six-level scores were defined by the densities of F1 worms. If the score level of F1 density in the san-1Delta strain was reduced by more than two levels compared with the wild-type strain, the RNAi clone was considered a positive gene. Positive results were confirmed by repeating the experiments at least three times. Insert DNA sequences of positive clones were confirmed by sequencing. ###begin title 48
Induction of SAC-dependent mitotic delay by RNAi-mediated depletion of ZYG-1
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 130 152 130 152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">O'Connell et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 466 471 466 471 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">zyg-1</italic>
###xml 509 514 509 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">zyg-1</italic>
###xml 868 876 868 876 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 A</xref>
SAC-dependent mitotic delay was induced by RNAi-mediated depletion of ZYG-1, a protein required for daughter centriole formation (O'Connell et al., 2001), which results in monopolar spindle formation in the second embryonic division. Consequently, sister kinetochores capture only the mono-oriented microtubule attachment, causing SAC-dependent mitotic delay. Embryos expressing GFP-histone H2B and GFP-tubulin were dissected from adult hermaphrodites injected with zyg-1 dsRNA only or a mixture of dsRNAs of zyg-1 and other genes, and subjected to time-lapse differential interference contrast and fluorescent microscopy. The duration of mitosis was defined as an interval between NEBD and chromosome decondensation. Under our experimental conditions, the duration of mitosis of ZYG-1-depleted cells was extended to 600 s, the duration of normal mitosis being 200 s (Fig. 1 A).

For immunostaining of proteins except SPDL-1 or CZW-1, embryos dissected or isolated by the alkaline hypochlorite method (Lewis and Fleming, 1995) were transferred onto poly-l-lysine-coated slides, frozen on dry ice, cracked, fixed with -20degreesC methanol for 10 min, dehydrated with -20degreesC acetone for 10 min, and rehydrated with PBS, pH 8.0, for 10 min. For SPDL-1 or CZW-1 staining, embryos prepared by the alkaline hypochlorite method were collected in a tube, fixed with -20degreesC methanol for 10 min, and washed with PBS. Embryos were then blocked with blocking solution (10% skimmed milk in PBS) for 1 h; incubated overnight at 4degreesC with blocking solution containing rat anti-alpha-tubulin YL1/2 (1:100; AbD Serotec) and anti-SPDL-1 (1:200), anti-KNL-1 (1 mug/ml; Desai et al., 2003), anti-NDC-80 (1 mug/ml; Desai et al., 2003), anti-BUB-1 (1 mug/ml; Oegema et al., 2001), anti-MDF-1 (1:2,000; Watanabe et al., 2008), or anti-CZW-1 antibodies (1:200); washed with PBST (0.1% Tween-20 in PBS); incubated for 3 h with blocking solution containing Alexa 594-conjugated anti-rabbit IgG (1:2,000; Invitrogen), Alexa 488-conjugated anti-rat IgG (1:2,000; Invitrogen), and 3 mug/ml DAPI; washed with PBST; and mounted on slides.
