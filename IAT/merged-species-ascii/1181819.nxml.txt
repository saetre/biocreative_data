lin-12 Notch functions in the adult nervous system of C. elegans

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 248 258 248 258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 446 476 446 476 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caenorhabditis elegans lin-12 </italic>
###xml 239 243 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 446 468 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
Notch signaling pathways are conserved across species and traditionally have been implicated in cell fate determination during embryonic development. Notch signaling components are also expressed postdevelopmentally in the brains of adult mice and Drosophila. Recent studies suggest that Notch signaling may play a role in the physiological, rather than developmental, regulation of neurons. Here, we investigate a new non-developmental role for Caenorhabditis elegans lin-12 Notch signaling in neurons regulating the spontaneous reversal rate during locomotion.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 33 44 33 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 87 94 87 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 239 246 239 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 324 331 324 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 373 380 373 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 448 455 448 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 606 612 606 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 623 630 623 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 777 784 777 784 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 927 934 927 934 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 974 980 974 980 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lag-2 </italic>
###xml 1013 1018 1013 1018 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lag-1</italic>
###xml 1033 1044 1033 1044 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 1084 1090 1084 1090 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 1129 1135 1129 1135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 1153 1159 1153 1159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 1215 1222 1215 1222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 33 43 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 1033 1043 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
The spontaneous reversal rate of C. elegans during normal locomotion is constant. Both lin-12 gain and loss of function mutant animals had significantly increased reversal rates compared to wild type controls. These defects were caused by lin-12 activity, because the loss of function defect could be rescued by a wild type lin-12 transgene. Furthermore, overexpression of lin-12 recapitulated the gain-of-function defect. Increasing or decreasing lin-12 activity in the postdevelopmental adult animal was sufficient to rapidly and reversibly increase reversals, thereby excluding a developmental role for lin-12. Although lin-12 is expressed in the vulval and somatic gonad lineages, we find that these tissues play no role in regulating reversal rates. In contrast, altering lin-12 activity specifically in the nervous system was sufficient to increase reversals. These behavioral changes require components of the canonical lin-12 signaling cascade, including the ligand lag-2 and the transcriptional effector lag-1. Finally, the C. elegans AMPA/kainate glutamate receptor homolog glr-1 shows strong genetic interactions with lin-12, suggesting that glr-1 and/or other glutamate gated channels may be targets of lin-12 regulation.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 44 51 44 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 60 71 60 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 88 95 88 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 60 70 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
Our results demonstrate a neuronal role for lin-12 Notch in C. elegans and suggest that lin-12 acutely regulates neuronal physiology to modulate animal behavior, without altering neuronal cell fate specification or neurite outgrowth. This is consistent with a role for Notch signaling in neurological disease with late onset symptoms.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 178 179 178 179 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D</underline>
###xml 184 185 184 185 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</underline>
###xml 192 193 192 193 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L</underline>
###xml 215 216 215 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 217 218 217 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 336 337 336 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 338 340 338 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 404 405 404 405 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</underline>
###xml 409 410 409 410 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</underline>
###xml 415 416 415 416 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L</underline>
###xml 432 434 432 434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 435 437 435 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 443 453 443 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 528 530 528 530 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 531 533 531 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 603 605 603 605 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 606 608 606 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 700 702 700 702 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 443 453 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
The conserved Notch signaling pathway has well established roles in cell fate determination during development. Transmembrane Notch receptors are activated by transmembrane DSL (Delta/Serrate/LAG-2) family ligands [1-6]. The intracellular (IC) domain of Notch is proteolytically released by presenilins and translocates to the nucleus [7-11], where it acts as a transcriptional activator abetted by CSL (CBF1/Su(H)/LAG-1) proteins [12-15]. In C. elegans, the LIN-12 Notch receptor is activated by LAG-2 and related DSL ligands [16-19], and proteolytically processed by the presenilins SEL-12 and HOP-1 [20-22]. The CSL protein LAG-1 interacts with LIN-12IC to activate transcription of target genes [23].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 71 73 71 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 74 76 74 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 97 108 97 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 486 488 486 488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 489 491 489 491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 112 116 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Notch receptors and ligands are expressed in adult vertebrate neurons [24,25]; recent studies in Drosophila and mice suggest that altering Notch signaling results in defective neuronal function [Costa, 2003 #50; Ge, 2004 #46; Presente, 2004 #31; Saura, 2004 #32; Wang, 2004 #51;Yoon, 2005 #69]. The importance of these findings is underscored by the fact that several genetic diseases associated with neuronal defects and/or late onset symptoms map to mutations in Notch pathway genes [32-36]. However, it remains unclear from these studies whether Notch signaling is acutely affecting neuronal physiology or if it is causing permanent changes in cell fate and/or structure due to developmental defects or aberrant growth.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 31 38 31 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 61 72 61 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 141 152 141 152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 256 263 256 263 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 395 401 395 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 480 487 480 487 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 628 635 628 635 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 714 721 714 721 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 791 796 791 796 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1</italic>
###xml 896 902 896 902 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 965 972 965 972 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 61 71 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 141 151 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
Here, we report a new role for lin-12 signaling in the adult C. elegans nervous system, using behavior as an indicator of neuronal activity. C. elegans predominantly move forward, but they spontaneously initiate backward locomotion. Genetically modulating lin-12 activity alters the rate of initiation of spontaneous reversals. Using inducible RNAi and a conditional, gain-of-function allele of lin-12, we show that this behavioral change can occur within a few hours of altering lin-12 activity in post-developmental adults. We also show that these inducible behavioral changes are rapidly reversible, strongly suggesting that lin-12 mediated behavioral changes are unlikely due to changes in cell fate. Altering lin-12 activity in a subset of interneurons is sufficient to alter behavior. glr-1, an AMPA/kainate receptor homolog gene expressed in these interneurons, genetically interacts with lin-12. Our results demonstrate a novel, post-developmental role for lin-12 signaling that is clearly distinct from its role in cell fate determination.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Results
###end title 12
###begin title 13
###xml 9 16 9 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
Altering lin-12 activity increases spontaneous reversals during locomotion
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 21 28 21 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 123 130 123 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 151 152 151 152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 312 318 312 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 319 323 319 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n941</italic>
###xml 378 385 378 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 425 431 425 431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 505 512 505 512 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 531 538 531 538 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 548 550 548 550 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 566 572 566 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 689 696 689 696 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
To assess a role for lin-12 Notch signaling in behavior, we first examined spontaneous reversal rates during locomotion in lin-12 mutant animals (Fig. 1). Normal animals moving forward consistently initiate backward locomotion approximately 10 times per 3 minutes. Reversal rates were significantly increased in lin-12(n941) loss of function (lf) animals, which completely lack lin-12 gene function. The behavioral defect of lin-12(lf) animals was rescued by a previously described transgene containing a lin-12 cDNA driven by the lin-12 promoter [37]. Furthermore, lin-12(lf) behavioral defects could be recapitulated by RNAi (see below). These results indicated that loss of function in lin-12 caused increased reversal rates.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 24 31 24 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 82 88 82 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 89 97 89 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n137n460</italic>
###xml 105 106 105 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 159 161 159 161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 162 164 162 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 213 219 213 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 488 494 476 482 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 530 537 518 525 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 594 609 582 597 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 685 692 673 680 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
The effect of increased lin-12 activity on reversal rates was then assessed using lin-12(n137n460) (Fig. 1), a gain of function, cold sensitive (gfcs) allele [38,39]. Reversals were not significantly increased in lin-12(gfcs) animals raised at the permissive temperature (25degreesC), but were dramatically increased in animals raised at the restrictive temperature (15degreesC). Cultivation temperature had no effect on reversal rate in wild type animals. The increased reversal rate of lin-12(gfcs) animals was due to increased lin-12 activity, as transgenic animals that overexpress LIN-12 (lin-12p::lin-12(OE)) also had increased reversals. Thus, both gain and loss of function in lin-12 causes increased reversal rates.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
###xml 21 28 21 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
An allelic series of lin-12 mutants reveals complex regulation of behavior
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 49 56 49 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 117 124 117 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 216 217 216 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 220 227 220 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 370 383 370 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n941) </italic>
###xml 551 566 551 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n941)/+ </italic>
###xml 607 620 607 620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n941) </italic>
###xml 643 655 643 655 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n302)</italic>
###xml 657 669 657 669 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n379)</italic>
###xml 675 688 675 688 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n676) </italic>
###xml 723 725 723 725 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 810 827 810 827 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137n460) </italic>
###xml 1005 1018 1005 1018 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137) </italic>
###xml 1022 1035 1022 1035 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n427) </italic>
###xml 1157 1183 1157 1183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137)/lin-12(n941) </italic>
###xml 1274 1291 1274 1291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137n460) </italic>
###xml 1309 1324 1309 1324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 1335 1336 1335 1336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 1382 1389 1382 1389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1399 1401 1399 1401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5B</xref>
###xml 1429 1436 1429 1436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1495 1511 1495 1511 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12p::lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1609 1616 1609 1616 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1733 1749 1733 1749 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12p::lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1818 1825 1818 1825 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
To further characterize the relationship between lin-12 activity and reversal rates, we assessed behavior across the lin-12 allelic series ordered based on the severity of previously determined vulval defects (Table 1). lin-12 alleles can be grouped into 4 classes: strong loss of function, weak gain of function, moderate gain of function, and strong gain of function. lin-12(n941) null animals are sterile and display protruding vulva that usually burst in adult animals; these animals had increased reversals. The vulval and reversal phenotypes of lin-12(n941)/+ animals were normal, indicating that the lin-12(n941) mutation is recessive. lin-12(n302), lin-12(n379), and lin-12(n676) are weak gain of function alleles [39]; these animals were fertile, vulvaless, and had slightly decreased reversal rates. lin-12(n137n460) acts as a moderate gain of function allele; these animals are cold-sensitive, display multiple pseudovulvae at the restrictive temperature, and had increased reversals. Finally, lin-12(n137) and lin-12(n427) are strong gain of function alleles that display multiple pseudovulvae and had strongly decreased reversals. We note that lin-12(n137)/lin-12(n941) hemizygote animals have multiple pseudovulvae and have high reversal rates consistent with lin-12(n137n460) phenotypes. Since lin-12p::lin-12(OE) (Fig. 1) and other transgenic animals overexpressing lin-12 (see Fig. 5B) recapitulate the moderate lin-12 gain of function allele, we expected that injection of the lin-12p::lin-12 construct at a higher concentration would lead to transgenic animals that recapitulate the strong lin-12 gain of function alleles. However, we were unable to generate viable transgenic lines using higher concentrations of lin-12p::lin-12 (data not shown; see Methods for details). We conclude that altering lin-12 activity results in complex changes in the pattern of reversal behavior; the implications of this allelic series are discussed below.

###begin title 18
###xml 9 16 9 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
Altering lin-12 activity in adult animals is sufficient to increase reversal rates
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 109 116 109 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 143 150 143 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 192 199 192 199 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 266 273 266 273 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 344 351 344 351 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 369 380 369 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hsp::lin-12</italic>
###xml 430 432 430 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 445 456 445 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hsp::lin-12</italic>
###xml 898 899 892 893 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G</underline>
###xml 915 916 909 910 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">r</underline>
###xml 924 925 918 919 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">k</underline>
###xml 898 940 892 934 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><underline>G</underline>protein coupled <underline>r</underline>eceptor <underline>k</underline>inase-2 grk-2) </italic>
###xml 987 997 981 991 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hsp::grk-2</italic>
###xml 1006 1012 1000 1006 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">grk-2 </italic>
###xml 1053 1055 1047 1049 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 1136 1146 1130 1140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hsp::grk-2</italic>
###xml 1365 1372 1359 1366 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
lin-12 Notch plays well established roles in development. Therefore, we asked whether increased reversals in lin-12 mutant animals depended on lin-12 activity during development or in adults. lin-12 loss of function was induced by expressing an inverted repeat of a lin-12 cDNA fragment under the control of a heat shock promoter to knock down lin-12 activity by RNAi (hsp::lin-12(RNAi)) in otherwise normal adult animals (Figure 2A). Uninduced hsp::lin-12(RNAi) adult animals (filled triangles) raised at 25degreesC had normal reversal rates, while heat shock induction resulted in dramatically increased reversal rates within 4 hours. Reversals returned to near basal levels after overnight recovery (approx. 14 hours). Heat shock had no effect on wild type control animals (filled circles). As a control, we generated transgenic animals containing an inverted repeat of a cDNA fragment from the Gprotein coupled receptor kinase-2 grk-2) gene under control of the heat shock promoter (hsp::grk-2(RNAi)). grk-2 loss of function causes sensory defects [40], but had no effect on reversal rates (data not shown). Heat shock induction of hsp::grk-2(RNAi) did not alter reversal rates (open squares), indicating that neither the presence of the heat shock vector nor overexpression of an unrelated dsRNA influenced reversal rates. We conclude that loss of function of lin-12 in adult animals is sufficient to alter behavior.

###begin p 20
###xml 12 18 12 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 74 76 74 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 79 85 79 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 442 448 430 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 699 705 675 681 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 1064 1071 1028 1035 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1189 1196 1153 1160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
We examined lin-12(gfcs) animals in temperature shift experiments (Figure 2B). lin-12(gfcs) adults raised at the restrictive temperature 15degreesC (filled square, t = 0) initially had increased reversal rates. When these animals were moved to the permissive temperature of 25degreesC (dotted line with filled squares), reversal rates gradually decreased, and after 3 hours reversals decreased to wild type levels. In reciprocal experiments, lin-12(gfcs) adults raised at 25degreesC (open square, t = 0) initially had almost normal reversal rates. When they were moved to 15degreesC (solid line with open squares), reversal rates gradually increased until they reached levels comparable to those of lin-12(gfcs) animals raised at 15degreesC. When these animals were moved back to 25degreesC (dotted line with open square), reversal rates decreased to original levels within 2 hours. Temperature shifts and cultivation temperature had only minimal effects on control wild type animals (open and filled circles). Taken together, these data demonstrate that altering lin-12 Notch activity for a few hours in post-developmental adult animals is sufficient to change behavior and suggests that lin-12 activity is regulating a physiological, not a developmental, process.
###end p 20
###begin title 21
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
lin-12 is not required in the vulval lineage to regulate reversals
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 11 18 11 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 162 174 162 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12::gfp </italic>
###xml 185 187 185 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 201 208 201 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 537 539 537 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
Where does lin-12 function to regulate reversal rates? LIN-12 is expressed in the somatic gonad and vulval lineages, based on previous studies using a functional lin-12::gfp transgene [37]. To test if lin-12 activity in these tissues regulated reversal rates, we eliminated the somatic gonad and the vulva by killing the progenitor cells of these lineages using a laser and then determining the reversal rates of the operated animals. Vulval development depends on cell-cell signaling from the anchor cell to the vulval precursor cells [41]. The anchor cell (and somatic gonad) is derived from one of two equipotent cells called Z1 and Z4 in L1 larvae; thus, killing Z1 and Z4 results in animals that lack gonads and vulvae.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 166 167 166 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 256 262 256 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 299 314 299 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 341 347 341 347 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 358 373 358 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 478 485 478 485 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
Wild type Z1-Z4-killed adult animals had normal reversal rates, indicating that these tissues play no role in regulating the reversal rate in wild type animals (Fig. 3). We tested the role of the vulval and gonadal lineages in regulating reversal rates in lin-12(RNAi) (see Methods for details) and lin-12p::lin-12(OE) animals. Z1-Z4-killed lin-12(RNAi) and lin-12p::lin-12(OE) animals maintained high reversal rates comparable to mock treated animals. These data indicate that lin-12 must function outside of the somatic gonad and vulva to regulate reversal rates.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 77 84 77 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 108 115 108 115 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 125 131 125 131 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">per se</italic>
###xml 178 184 178 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 234 240 234 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 370 376 364 370 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 385 391 379 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 493 494 487 488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 506 512 500 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 523 538 517 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 741 748 735 742 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 837 844 831 838 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
We considered the possibility that the gross morphological vulval defects in lin-12 mutant animals, but not lin-12 signaling per se, might account for changes in reversal rates. lin-12(lf) animals have a large protruding vulva, while lin-12(gfcs) animals raised at the restrictive temperature 15degreesC have multiple pseudovulvae. We measured basal locomotion rates in lin-12(lf) and lin-12(gfcs) animals and found that they had slight but significant decreases in basal movement rate (Table 2). However, lin-12(RNAi) and lin-12p::lin-12(OE) animals, which phenocopy the reversal phenotypes but not the vulval phenotypes of the mutant animals, had normal basal movement rates. These data indicate that morphological defects of the vulva in lin-12 mutant animals cannot account for the altered behavior. Taken together, we conclude that lin-12 expressed in the vulva and somatic gonad does not contribute to the regulation of reversal rates during locomotion.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 27 33 27 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
lin-12 acts in a subset of glr-1 expressing neurons to regulate reversals
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 47 58 47 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 283 285 283 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 356 358 356 358 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 369 371 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 372 374 372 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 519 525 519 525 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 531 537 531 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 547 553 547 553 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 554 558 554 558 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n137</italic>
###xml 630 632 630 632 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 669 676 669 676 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 979 980 979 980 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 1272 1279 1272 1279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 47 57 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
lin-12 might act in or upon neurons to control C. elegans behavior. Previous studies have implicated the command interneurons AVA, AVB, AVD, AVE and PVC in regulating normal forward and backward locomotion [42]. The intrinsic activation of these interneurons affects reversal rates [43]. Other neurons presynaptic to the command interneurons, such as ASH [43] and AIY [44-46], can affect reversal rates as well. We did not detect any overt cell fate changes or morphological defects in any of these or other neurons in lin-12(lf), lin-12(gfcs) or lin-12(n137gf) mutant animals (data not shown), consistent with a previous report [47] and supporting our conclusion that lin-12 mediated behavioral changes were not due to developmental defects. LIN-12 expression was not detected in any of these neurons either by immunohistochemical analysis or by GFP fluorescence (data not shown). However, occasional LIN-12::GFP expression was observed in the RIG neurons of young larvae (Fig. 4). These observations and the preceding temperature shift experiments suggested that LIN-12 may be expressed in adult neurons at levels too low to detect. Increasing LIN-12::GFP levels further caused lethality (data not shown); therefore, a functional approach was taken to determine whether lin-12 acts in the nervous system.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 78 85 78 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 116 122 116 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>

###xml 328 330 328 330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 399 406 399 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 441 454 441 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1::lin-12</italic>
###xml 491 503 491 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12::gfp </italic>
###xml 579 586 579 586 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>

###xml 1136 1142 1136 1142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 1167 1178 1167 1178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1p::(0)</italic>
###xml 1205 1209 1205 1209 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gfp </italic>
###xml 1241 1247 1241 1247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 1294 1296 1294 1296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5B</xref>
###xml 1332 1346 1332 1346 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 1436 1443 1436 1443 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1455 1461 1455 1461 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 1511 1522 1511 1522 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(lf) </italic>
lin-12 activity was knocked down by RNAi in a subset of neurons by expressing lin-12 dsRNA under the control of the glr-1 promoter, which drives expression in the aforementioned command interneurons and twelve other classes of neurons including RIG [48,49] (glr-1p:::lin-12(RNAi)). Because RNAi effects can spread systemically [50], we first validated the cellular specificity of this approach. The lin-12 cDNA fragment used to generate the glr-1::lin-12(RNAi) constructs was derived from a lin-12::gfp fusion; thus, the dsRNA expressed in these transgenic animals contains both lin-12 and gfp sequences. When glr-1p::lin-12(RNAi) constructs were injected into strains that express GFP in the intestine or in ASH sensory neurons (which are physically close to glr-1 expressing neurons), no decreases in GFP fluorescence were observed (Figure 5A). Furthermore, these transgenic animals had grossly normal fertility and vulval morphology (data not shown). Thus, RNAi effects did not appear to spread from glr-1 expressing neurons to nearby neurons, the intestine, or to the vulva. Also, we found that transgenic animals injected with the glr-1 promoter fragment alone (glr-1p::(0)) or constructs expressing gfp only dsRNA under control of the glr-1 promoter had no effect on reversal rates (Fig. 5B). When we examined the behavior of glr-1p::lin-12(RNAi) animals, we found that reversal rates increased significantly. Thus, knocking down lin-12 activity in glr-1 expressing neurons was sufficient to recapitulate lin-12(lf) behavioral defects.

###xml 20 27 20 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 85 92 85 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 118 124 118 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>

###xml 155 162 155 162 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>

###xml 288 294 288 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 304 318 304 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 327 343 327 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1p::lin-12IC</italic>
###xml 418 424 418 424 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 434 445 434 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1p::gfp</italic>
###xml 480 482 480 482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5B</xref>
###xml 535 542 535 542 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 572 578 572 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 588 605 588 605 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1p::lin-12(+)</italic>
###xml 641 647 641 647 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>

###xml 748 755 748 755 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 767 773 767 773 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
The requirement for lin-12 activity in the nervous system was also tested by driving lin-12 cDNA expression using the glr-1 promoter (Fig. 5B). Increasing lin-12 activity by overexpressing either a full length lin-12 cDNA or a truncated, activated form of lin-12 under the control of the glr-1 promoter (glr-1p::lin-12(OE) and glr-1p::lin-12IC, respectively) also increased reversal rates. Expression of GFP using the glr-1 promoter (glr-1p::gfp) as a control had no effect (Fig. 5B). Finally and most significantly, expression of the lin-12 cDNA under the control of the glr-1 promoter (glr-1p::lin-12(+)) rescued the behavioral defects of lin-12(lf) animals, restoring reversal rates to wild type levels (Fig. 5C). These results demonstrate that lin-12 activity in glr-1 expressing neurons is sufficient to regulate reversal rates.

###xml 10 17 10 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
Increased lin-12 activity affects reversal rates via RIG neurons
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 75 80 75 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1</italic>
###xml 331 337 331 337 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 393 408 393 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12p::lin-12</italic>

The RIG neurons, in which we observed weak LIN-12::GFP expression, express glr-1. Interestingly, the RIG neurons are presynaptic to command interneurons. Despite the fact that lin-12 overexpression recapitulated lin-12(gfcs) behavioral defects, we considered the possibility that the lin-12p::lin-12(OE) transgene might act ectopically or during development to alter reversal rates. ###begin title 32
###xml 25 32 25 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
Genes that interact with lin-12 to regulate reversals
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 30 37 30 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 170 177 170 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 199 205 199 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lag-1 </italic>

###xml 531 548 531 548 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1p::lin-12IC </italic>
###xml 590 597 590 597 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 624 630 624 630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lag-1 </italic>
###xml 656 663 656 663 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>

###xml 747 754 747 754 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>

###xml 959 965 959 965 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lag-1 </italic>
To determine if the canonical lin-12 signaling pathway regulates spontaneous reversal rates, we examined the reversal rates of animals that are defective in genes of the lin-12 pathway, specifically lag-1 (which encodes a transcriptional cofactor that is the major effector for lin-12 signaling) and lag-2 (which codes for a Notch ligand) (Fig. 7A). The reversal rates of partial loss-of-function lag-1 and lag-2 mutant animals were relatively normal. However, partial loss of lag-1 function suppressed increased reversal rates in glr-1p::lin-12IC animals that had constitutively activated lin-12 signaling, consistent with lag-1 functioning downstream of lin-12 regulating reversal rates. Also, a semidominant allele of lag-2 that suppresses the lin-12 gain-of-function multivulval phenotype caused increased reversal rates. Although strong loss of function alleles could not be tested due to embryonic lethality, our results suggest that lin-12, lag-2, and lag-1 likely act together in the nervous system to regulate reversals.

###xml 301 306 301 306 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1</italic>
###xml 351 353 351 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 397 403 397 403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 404 417 404 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1p::glr-1</italic>
###xml 532 534 532 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 629 634 629 634 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1</italic>
###xml 638 654 638 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">;lin-12p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 663 668 663 668 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1</italic>
###xml 672 687 672 687 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">;glr-1p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 829 841 829 841 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1p::gfp </italic>
###xml 869 876 869 876 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 942 948 942 948 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 1005 1012 1005 1012 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
Finally, given the previously described role of the glr-1 AMPA receptor in the command interneurons [43,48,49,51], we examined more closely the role of glr-1 in spontaneous reversals and lin-12 mediated behavioral changes (Fig. 7B). Consistent with a previous report, complete loss of glr-1 function (glr-1(lf)) alone had no effect on reversal rates [43]. However, we found that overexpression of glr-1 (glr-1p::glr-1(OE)) increased spontaneous reversal rates. We note that different constructs are used here than previous studies [43] (see Methods for details) and that reversal rates can be dependent on assay conditions. Both glr-1(lf);lin-12p::lin-12(OE) and glr-1(lf);glr-1p::lin-12(RNAi) animals had dramatically decreased reversal rates (below wild type levels). Yet, there were no dramatic changes in the expression of a glr-1p::gfp transcriptional reporter in lin-12 (gfcs) or (lf) animals (data not shown). Our results suggest that glr-1 AMPA receptor activity, but not levels, are modulated by lin-12 signaling to regulate reversals.
###end p 34
###begin title 35
Discussion
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 58 65 58 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 110 121 110 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 131 138 131 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 259 266 259 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 300 307 300 307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 310 316 310 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 388 395 388 395 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 525 532 525 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 563 571 563 571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo </italic>
###xml 695 702 695 702 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 110 120 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
In this study we demonstrate a non-developmental role for lin-12 Notch in the adult nervous system regulating C. elegans behavior. lin-12 mediated behavioral changes can be rapidly induced within a few hours in adult animals and are reversible. Knocking down lin-12 activity by RNAi or by activating lin-12 in glr-1 expressing neurons is sufficient to reproduce the behavioral defects of lin-12 mutant animals. The rapidity with which behavioral changes can be induced in post-developmental adult animals argues that neither lin-12 mediated cell fate changes nor de novo neurite outgrowth are the likely mechanisms for altering behavior. Rather, our results are consistent with a novel role for lin-12 signaling acutely regulating neuronal physiology via transcriptional activation, clearly distinct from previously described roles in cell fate specification.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 294 296 294 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 386 396 386 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 575 577 575 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 578 580 578 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 689 691 689 691 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 839 841 839 841 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 1023 1025 1023 1025 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 1244 1246 1244 1246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 1247 1249 1247 1249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 1262 1264 1262 1264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 586 590 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 711 715 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 920 924 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Signaling pathways used to pattern the developing nervous system can also play important roles in the adult nervous system. For example, ephrins and Eph receptors function both in nervous system patterning during development and in synaptic plasticity in the adult nervous system (reviewed in [52]). Recent studies suggest that Notch signaling may also play a role in adult neurons. In Drosophila, adult animals harboring temperature sensitive, loss-of-function Notch alleles are defective for long term memory formation after one to two days at the restrictive temperature [27,28]. In mice, Notch1 and CBF1 heterozygous adult animals have specific defects in spatial learning and memory [26]. Similarly, adult mice in which Notch protein levels have been partially depleted by antisense RNA are defective in long term potentiation (LTP) [30]. Conditional knockout of both presenilin genes in the postnatal forebrain in mice results in defects in long-term contextual memory and LTP, when assayed in two month old animals [29]. Our heat shock and temperature shift experiments indicate that behavioral defects appear within hours, suggesting that Notch mediated alterations in neuronal function can occur on a much shorter timescale than days [27,28] or months [29] as previously reported.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 4 11 4 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 72 89 72 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137n460) </italic>
###xml 221 226 221 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n427 </italic>
###xml 230 234 230 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n137</italic>
###xml 291 308 291 308 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137n460) </italic>
###xml 479 495 479 495 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137n460)</italic>
###xml 557 569 557 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137)</italic>
###xml 607 612 607 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n460 </italic>
###xml 684 689 684 689 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n137 </italic>
###xml 690 692 690 692 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 693 695 693 695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 707 712 707 712 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n137 </italic>
###xml 720 729 720 729 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n137n460 </italic>
###xml 782 799 782 799 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137n460) </italic>
###xml 834 846 834 846 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137)</italic>
###xml 876 883 876 883 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1006 1013 1006 1013 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1014 1029 1014 1029 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 1141 1154 1141 1154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137) </italic>
###xml 1182 1207 1182 1207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12(n137/lin-12(n941) </italic>
The lin-12 allelic series for reversal rates is complex. In particular, lin-12(n137n460) gain-of-function hemizygotes, heterozygotes, and homozygotes all have high reversal rates, while stronger gain-of-function alleles (n427 and n137) have decreased reversals, raising the possibility that lin-12(n137n460) could be a neomorphic allele. Several lines of evidence argue against this hypothesis. First, based on vulval phenotypes, there is no evidence of any neomorphic activity. lin-12(n137n460), which is a recessive hypermorphic allele, is a revertant of lin-12(n137), a dominant hypermorphic allele; the n460 mutation confers a temperature sensitive, partial loss of function onto n137 [38,39]. Both the n137 and the n137n460 alleles cause multiple pseudovulvae, indicating that lin-12(n137n460) is simply a weaker hypermorph than lin-12(n137). Second, modestly increasing lin-12 activity through several other independent means also caused increased reversals. These include moderate overexpression of lin-12 (lin-12p::lin-12(OE)) at levels that do not affect fertility and vulval development, and placing the strong hypermorphic allele lin-12(n137) over the null allele (i.e., lin-12(n137/lin-12(n941) animals).
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 48 55 48 55 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 203 210 203 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 405 412 405 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 677 684 677 684 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 762 769 762 769 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 810 817 810 817 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 985 990 985 990 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glp-1</italic>
###xml 1000 1011 1000 1011 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 1091 1098 1091 1098 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1000 1010 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
We favor the hypothesis that the unconventional lin-12 allelic series for reversal rates reflects the underlying complexity of Notch signaling and the neuronal signaling pathways that regulate behavior. lin-12 acts at multiple places during vulval cell fate specification, specifically the AC/VU decision and VPC lateral inhibition, resulting in a complex allelic series for vulval phenotypes. Similarly, lin-12 gain and loss of function may have different cellular foci for action in the nervous system, making it difficult to predict the behavioral output based on simple genetic rules. This is partially supported by the RIG ablation studies, wherein killing RIG neurons in lin-12 gain of function animals ameliorated reversal increases, but had no effect in lin-12 loss of function animals. Alternatively, lin-12 may act coordinately with other genes to regulate reversals. Further genetic studies may lead to a clearer picture. Consistent with this hypothesis, we have found that glp-1, another C. elegans Notch homolog, modulates reversal rates (in preparation). Our data suggest that lin-12 regulates reversal rates in a complex fashion.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 35 42 35 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 147 154 147 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 162 168 162 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 450 452 450 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 453 455 453 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 489 495 489 495 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1 </italic>
###xml 514 521 514 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 534 541 534 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 576 581 576 581 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glr-1</italic>
###xml 596 603 596 603 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 746 752 746 752 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">avr-15</italic>
###xml 784 795 784 795 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 863 865 863 865 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 900 907 900 907 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">avr-15 </italic>
###xml 1033 1035 1033 1035 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 1138 1143 1138 1143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nmr-1</italic>
###xml 1239 1241 1239 1241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 1260 1266 1260 1266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nmr-1 </italic>
###xml 1298 1304 1298 1304 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 1402 1409 1402 1409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 444 448 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 784 794 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
The behavioral changes observed in lin-12 animals are dramatically dependent on GLR-1 AMPA receptor function. Taken together with our finding that lin-12 acts in glr-1 expressing neurons to regulate reversals, it suggests a possible relationship between AMPA receptors and Notch receptors in post-developmental synaptic plasticity. This is consistent with a recent study that demonstrated that altering Notch signaling caused defects in LTP in mice [29,30]. Based on our genetic analysis, glr-1 may be a target of lin-12 signaling or lin-12 signaling may act in parallel with glr-1. For example, lin-12 signaling may modulate other glutamate-gated currents to influence membrane excitability. Consistent with this hypothesis, loss of function in avr-15, one of several semi-redundant C. elegans genes encoding conserved glutamate-gated chloride channel subunits [53], results in increased reversals. avr-15 is expressed in the AVA command interneurons (data not shown) and chloride currents have been observed in these interneurons [51], making AVR-15 a candidate target for regulation by LIN-12 signaling. Similarly, loss of function of nmr-1, which encodes an NMDA glutamate receptor subunit, results in decreased spontaneous reversals [54], suggesting that nmr-1 activity could be influenced by lin-12. Additional behavioral and genetic analysis will be required to further delineate the targets of lin-12 signaling in adult neurons.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 280 282 280 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 515 517 515 517 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 518 520 518 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 627 629 627 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 630 632 630 632 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 182 187 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
It should be noted that defects in Notch signaling can result in pleiotropic developmental disorders and nervous system dysfunction. CADASIL syndrome is associated with mutations in human Notch3 and is characterized by seizures, late onset neurodegeneration and vascular defects [32]. Mutations in Jagged1 (a DSL protein family member) are implicated in Alagille syndrome, which is characterized by defects in liver, cardiac, and skeletal tissues, and less frequently, neurovascular defects and mental retardation [34,35]. Familial, early onset Alzheimer's disease is often caused by mutations in presenilin 1 or presenilin 2 [33,36]. The developmental defects associated with CADASIL and Alagille syndromes make it difficult to establish a role for Notch signaling in neurons, but it may play a role in the defects observed in some of the late-onset symptoms. Given the emerging role for Notch signaling in the adult nervous system, a role for defective Notch signaling in these and other neurological disorders warrants further investigation.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
Conclusion
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 38 45 38 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 54 77 54 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caenorhabditis elegans </italic>
###xml 115 122 115 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 225 232 225 232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 279 286 279 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 387 394 387 394 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 456 463 456 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 546 552 546 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lag-2 </italic>
###xml 580 585 580 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lag-1</italic>
###xml 612 619 612 619 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 54 76 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
We have demonstrated a novel role for lin-12 Notch in Caenorhabditis elegans in the adult nervous system. Changing lin-12 activity postdevelopmentally in adult animals alters the spontaneous reversal rates during locomotion. lin-12 activity in the vulva and somatic gonad, where lin-12 expression was previously reported, is not required to control reversal rates. In contrast, altering lin-12 activity in specific neurons is sufficient to alter behavior. lin-12 likely acts through the canonical Notch signaling pathway that includes the ligand lag-2 and the downstream effector lag-1. The neuronal function of lin-12 is clearly independent from cell fate specification during development.
###end p 43
###begin title 44
Methods
###end title 44
###begin title 45
Behavioral assays
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 99 101 99 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 102 104 102 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 192 200 192 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1534 1541 1516 1523 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1611 1618 1593 1600 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 192 199 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Spontaneous reversals are modulated by sensory input, environmental conditions and feeding status [43,55]. To control these variables, animals were cultured on NGM agar plates containing OP50 E. coli at 25degreesC, except in temperature shift experiments, in which animals were cultured at 15degreesC and moved to room temperature 30 minutes prior to assays. Young adults (containing at least 4 eggs) were moved from the bacterial lawn of an uncrowded plate to an NGM plate lacking food, allowed to crawl around briefly to remove bacterial residue, then quickly transferred to another NGM plate lacking food for assays. Spontaneous initiation of backward locomotion was recorded over three minutes during the next 1.5 to 10.5 minutes with the lid on. Up to three animals per assay plate per trial were used; no effect on reversal rates was observed for up to three animals per plate. Freshly poured NGM agar plates were dried in a laminar flow hood for approx. 2 hours, sealed with Parafilm, then stored at 4degreesC at least overnight. Plates were allowed to warm up to room temperature for at least 30 minutes prior to use. Several assay plates were tested until a plate that resulted in an average of 10 reversals in 3 minutes was observed for N2 control animals; this plate was then used for all subsequent assays on that day. Each initiation of backward locomotion was scored as one reversal; omega turns without reversals were not scored. A subset of animals was scored blind as to genotype and/or transgene to confirm results. lin-12 mutants have defective vulvae, which are visually obvious; therefore, lin-12 mutant animals were scored independently by two observers. Statistical analysis was performed using the two tailed Student's t test.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
Laser ablations
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 56 58 56 58 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 169 176 169 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nyIs60 </italic>
###xml 195 208 195 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">flp-18p::GFP </italic>
###xml 209 211 209 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 290 296 290 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 401 407 401 407 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 556 562 556 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 760 773 748 761 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">flp-18p::gfp </italic>
###xml 828 834 816 822 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 1116 1123 1104 1111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
Laser ablations were performed as previously described [56] using a Micropoint ablation system (Photonic Instruments, St. Charles, IL). RIG ablations were undertaken in nyIs60 animals expressing flp-18p::GFP [57]. These animals are uncoordinated but have normal spontaneous reversal rates. lin-12(lf) mutant animals were not subjected to laser microsurgery because they rarely survived the procedure. lin-12(gfcs) mutant animals did not survive laser microsurgery as L1 larvae, but most survived when operated on as L2-L3 larvae. After laser microsurgery, lin-12(gfcs) animals were allowed to recover at the permissive temperature 25degreesC for 1-2 days, then were shifted to the restrictive temperature 15degreesC for 4 hours prior to behavioral assays. The flp-18p::gfp transgene did not affect the temperature dependence of lin-12(gfcs) phenotypes (data not shown). After behavioral assays were completed, successful ablation of the RIG neurons was scored by the lack of GFP labeled neuronal cell bodies in the retrovesicular ganglion. In nearly all laser ablation experiments, mock treated animals with altered lin-12 activity had slightly lower reversal rates than untreated animals. However, they still had significantly higher reversal rates than wild type mock treated animals.

Molecular biology
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 45 60 45 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12p::lin-12</italic>
###xml 80 91 80 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hsp::lin-12</italic>
