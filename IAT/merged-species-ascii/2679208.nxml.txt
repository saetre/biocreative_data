Exploring Functional beta-Cell Heterogeneity In Vivo Using PSA-NCAM as a Specific Marker
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: MK CTC AK CM CK. Performed the experiments: MK JC CTC MB AG MD SC MR PC BL. Analyzed the data: MK CTC CM CK. Wrote the paper: MK SL JL CM CK.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 344 348 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
The mass of pancreatic beta-cells varies according to increases in insulin demand. It is hypothesized that functionally heterogeneous beta-cell subpopulations take part in this process. Here we characterized two functionally distinct groups of beta-cells and investigated their physiological relevance in increased insulin demand conditions in rats.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 98 102 95 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i.e.</italic>
###xml 107 111 101 105 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 120 123 111 114 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 150 152 141 143 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 334 338 322 326 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 347 350 332 335 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 4 7 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 496 499 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 529 532 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Two rat beta-cell populations were sorted by FACS according to their PSA-NCAM surface expression, i.e. betahigh and betalow-cells. Insulin release, Ca2+ movements, ATP and cAMP contents in response to various secretagogues were analyzed. Gene expression profiles and exocytosis machinery were also investigated. In a second part, betahigh and betalow-cell distribution and functionality were investigated in animal models with decreased or increased beta-cell function: the Zucker Diabetic Fatty rat and the 48 h glucose-infused rat.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 83 86 77 80 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 98 102 89 93 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 218 221 203 206 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 299 303 281 285 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 58 61 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
We show that beta-cells are heterogeneous for PSA-NCAM in rat pancreas. Unlike betalow-cells, betahigh-cells express functional beta-cell markers and are highly responsive to various insulin secretagogues. Whereas betalow-cells represent the main population in diabetic pancreas, an increase in betahigh-cells is associated with gain of function that follows sustained glucose overload.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusion
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 109 116 106 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
Our data show that a functional heterogeneity of beta-cells, assessed by PSA-NCAM surface expression, exists in vivo. These findings pinpoint new target populations involved in endocrine pancreas plasticity and in beta-cell defects in type 2 diabetes.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Introduction
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 199 203 193 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i.e.</italic>
###xml 302 305 293 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Prentki1">[1]</xref>
###xml 499 502 487 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Ahrn1">[2]</xref>
###xml 753 756 738 741 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Parsons1">[3]</xref>
###xml 758 761 743 746 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Klppel1">[4]</xref>
Pancreatic beta-cells synthesize and release insulin with a remarkable degree of plasticity over time that allows adaptation to the metabolic environment. This involves increased beta-cell function, i.e. an increase both in insulin production and secretion as well as enlargement of the beta-cell pool [1]. The functional beta-cell mass varies according to changes in insulin sensitivity. Thus, increased insulin sensitivity displayed by master athletes is associated with reduced insulin secretion [2]. On the contrary, insulin resistance as part of pregnancy or obesity is compensated via an increase in both beta-cell number and responsiveness to glucose. This mechanism allows the maintenance of euglycemia in spite of decreased insulin sensitivity [3], [4].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 119 122 116 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-VanSchravendijk1">[5]</xref>
###xml 193 196 190 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Schuit1">[6]</xref>
###xml 198 201 195 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Bosco1">[7]</xref>
###xml 203 206 200 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Kiekens1">[8]</xref>
###xml 300 303 297 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-VanSchravendijk1">[5]</xref>
###xml 305 308 302 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Kiekens1">[8]</xref>
###xml 310 313 307 310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Pipeleers1">[9]</xref>
###xml 315 319 312 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Hiriart1">[10]</xref>
###xml 321 325 318 322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Ling1">[11]</xref>
###xml 327 331 324 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Hiriart2">[12]</xref>
The concept of functional heterogeneity among beta-cells proposes that each cell differs in its sensitivity to glucose [5] and is recruited in a glucose-dependent manner into both biosynthetic [6], [7], [8] and secretory active states in order to adapt insulin secretion to the metabolic environment [5], [8], [9], [10], [11], [12]. Therefore characterization of such beta-cell subpopulations with different metabolic sensitivities would lead to the development of new therapeutic strategies.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 183 187 180 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-BernardKargar1">[13]</xref>
###xml 189 193 186 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Theodosis1">[14]</xref>
###xml 195 199 192 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Kiss1">[15]</xref>
###xml 315 319 309 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i.e.</italic>
###xml 339 343 333 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Kiss1">[15]</xref>
###xml 575 579 563 567 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-BernardKargar1">[13]</xref>
###xml 570 574 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
The sialylated form of the Neural Cell Adhesion Molecule (PSA-NCAM) is only expressed in structures undergoing functional changes in the adult such as brain and pancreatic beta-cells [13], [14], [15]. In adult beta-cells, the regulation of PSA-NCAM abundance at the cell surface is controlled by cellular activity, i.e. insulin exocytosis [15]. Interestingly, we have previously identified PSA-NCAM as a marker of beta-cell functionality. According to the level of surface PSA-NCAM, beta-cells were divided in two subpopulations with different glucose responsiveness in rats [13].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 367 371 361 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Topp1">[16]</xref>
###xml 562 566 553 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Bernard1">[17]</xref>
###xml 335 338 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 406 409 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Our present study aimed to characterize these two groups of beta-cells and to investigate the basis supporting their different insulin secretory capacities. Furthermore, we correlated the PSA-NCAM labeling to the functional beta-cell mass in animal models where it is either decreased or increased: (i) the Zucker Diabetic Fatty (ZDF) rat, a model of type 2 diabetes [16] and (ii) the 48 h glucose-infused rat (HG/HI), a model in which a long-term imposed hyperglycemia leads to pancreatic overactivity and to an impressive increase of functional beta-cell mass [17].
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 182 189 176 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 334 338 325 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i.e.</italic>
Taken together, our data (i) confirm that PSA-NCAM is a prominent marker of functional beta-cells, (ii) provide proof of concept that a functional heterogeneity of beta-cells exists in vivo and (iii) explore mechanistic insights of heterogeneity. Moreover, they support the idea that an alteration of pancreatic beta-cell plasticity, i.e. an inability to recruit fully functional beta-cells, could contribute to the impairment of insulin secretion in type 2 diabetes.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Materials and Methods
###end title 16
###begin title 17
Animals
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 359 369 359 369 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ad libitum</italic>
###xml 664 668 664 668 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Bernard1">[17]</xref>
###xml 670 677 670 677 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vivo</italic>
###xml 50 61 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Wistar rats</span>
###xml 109 113 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 233 237 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Rats</span>
###xml 440 444 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 458 469 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Wistar rats</span>
###xml 611 622 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Wistar rats</span>
Experiments were carried out on 12 weeks old male Wistar rats and on 12 weeks old male Zucker Diabetic Fatty rats (ZDF fa/fa) and their age-matched lean littermates (ZDF fa/+). Animals were purchased from Charles River laboratories. Rats were housed under a 12 h light-dark cycle with free access to water. During the acclimatization period, animals were fed ad libitum on standard diet or on Purina 5008 chow (Charles River) for ZDF fa/fa rats. A subset of Wistar rats were infused with 30% glucose during 48 h to induce hyperglycemia and hyperinsulinemia (Wistar HG/HI) and compared to 48 h 0.9% NaCl-infused Wistar rats (Wistar control) as previously described [17]. In vivo insulin secretion in response to glucose represented by the insulinogenic index (DeltaI/DeltaG) was evaluated during oral glucose tolerance tests (OGTT). Insulin and glucose responses during OGTT (3 g/kg) were calculated as the incremental plasma insulin values integrated over a period of 30 min after the glucose gavage (DeltaI) and the corresponding increase in glucose concentration (DeltaG). The insulinogenic index represents the ratio of these two parameters. All procedures were performed according to the French ethical rules for animal experimentation.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
Islet cell preparation
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 184 188 184 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Emre1">[18]</xref>
###xml 373 377 373 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-BernardKargar1">[13]</xref>
###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">Rats</span>
Rats were anesthetized with pentobarbital (Sanofi; 4 mg/100 g body weight i.p.). Islets of Langerhans were isolated after collagenase digestion of the pancreas as previously described [18]. Islets were trypsinized with 0.1 mg/ml trypsin (1:250, Difco) and digestion was stopped with cold Krebs-Ringer-Bicarbonate-Hepes (KRBH) buffer-0.5% BSA (Interchim) and 5.5 mM glucose [13]. Cell suspensions were used for immunocytochemistry or for beta-cell sorting.
###end p 20
###begin title 21
Fluorescence-activated cell sorting of beta-cells
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 15 19 15 19 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">plus</sup>
###xml 252 256 243 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-VanDeWinkel1">[19]</xref>
###xml 258 262 249 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Nielsen1">[20]</xref>
Using a FACStarplus (Becton Dickinson), beta-cells were distinguished from non-beta cells and sorted based on their autofluorescence (FAD content) and cell size, resulting in a population with 95% (insulin-positive) beta-cells, as previously described [19], [20].
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 342 346 333 337 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 405 408 390 393 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 552 556 534 538 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 571 574 550 553 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 73 78 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 123 128 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 248 252 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 440 443 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 525 529 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
beta-cells were analyzed for their surface PSA-NCAM expression using the mouse anti-PSA-NCAM antibody (AbCys) and the anti-mouse PE-conjugated secondary antibody (Invitrogren). The geometric mean fluorescence for PSA-NCAM was determined in control rats and used to arbitrarily separate between high PSA-NCAM-labeled beta-cell population (betahigh-cells) and low PSA-NCAM-labeled beta-cell population (betalow-cells). In HG/HI and ZDF fa/fa rat models, the geometric mean of respective controls (ZDF fa/+ lean or NaCl-infused rats) was used to sort betahigh-cells and betalow-cells.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 45 49 45 49 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">plus</sup>
###xml 270 271 270 271 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 188 192 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 225 229 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
Data were analyzed using the attached FACStarplus analysis software (Becton Dickinson). Histograms are representative of acquisitions performed on 7500 events for Wistar control and HG/HI rats and 3500 for ZDF lean and fa/fa rats. Each experiment was performed on 1-2.106 cells.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Culture of sorted pancreatic beta-cells
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 394 395 372 373 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 401 402 379 380 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 7 10 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Sorted rat beta-cells were resuspended in RPMI 1640 (MP Biomedicals) supplemented with 5.5 mM glucose, 10% heat-inactivated FCS, 100 microg/ml gentamycin, 2 mM L-glutamine and 10 mM Hepes and plated in miniculture dishes. For aggregates formation, beta-cells were incubated during 1 h in a rotary incubator (30 cycles/min) at 37degreesC. Cells were then cultured for 18 h at 37degreesC in 95% O2/5% CO2 and saturated humidity and washed in KRBH buffer containing 5.5 mM glucose and 0.05% FAF-BSA (fraction V, Roche) before use. The cell viability rate was assessed by neutral red and was always between 90-95%.
###end p 26
###begin title 27
Insulin content
###end title 27
###begin p 28
Freshly sorted beta-cells were homogenized in acid alcohol solution (75% ethanol, 1.5% HCl 12N, 23.5% distilled water) and stored at -80degreesC until insulin determination.
###end p 28
###begin title 29
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vitro</italic>
In vitro analysis of insulin secretion
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 0 8 0 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vitro</italic>
###xml 123 124 120 121 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sup>
In vitro insulin release was assayed either by perifusion or under static incubation on aggregated sorted beta-cells (80x103) after overnight culture.
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 192 196 192 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-BernardKargar1">[13]</xref>
###xml 18 21 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
The perifusion of rat cells was performed using low (5.5 mM) or stimulating (8.3 mM and/or 16.7 mM) glucose concentrations or KCl (50 mM) in KRBH buffer-0.05% FAF-BSA, as described previously [13]. The eluates were collected for insulin quantification. Incremental insulin response (DeltaI) to stimulating glucose concentrations or KCl above basal release was obtained by planimetry of perifusion profiles and was expressed as the difference in insulin secretion rate relative to the mean hormonal output recorded at the pre-stimulation period with 5.5 mM glucose.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 23 26 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
For static incubation, rat cells were pre-incubated for 60 min at 37degreesC in silicone-coated glass tubes in a shaking water bath containing KRBH buffer-0.05% FAF-BSA and 5.5 mM glucose. Thereafter, the medium was replaced by KRBH-BSA containing 5.5 mM or 16.7 mM without or with 10 nM GLP-1, 5 mM db-cAMP, 19 mM arginine or 10 mM leucine and beta-cells were further incubated for 30 min at 37degreesC. The supernatant was stored at -20degreesC until assayed for insulin.
###end p 32
###begin title 33
Intracellular free calcium measurements
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 58 59 55 56 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sup>
###xml 284 285 271 272 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 296 297 283 284 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 308 309 295 296 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 603 604 584 585 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 617 618 598 599 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 787 789 768 770 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 790 791 771 772 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 919 921 894 896 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 37 40 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Overnight-cultured aggregated sorted rat beta-cells (80x103) were allowed to attach on polylysine-treated cover-glass then loaded for 1 h with 1.5 microM Fura-2/AM (Molecular Probes) at 37degreesC in Krebs-Ringer-Bicarbonate (KRB) buffer containing (115 mM NaCl, 5 mM KCl, 24 mM NaHCO3, 1 mM CaCl2, 1 mM MgCl2, 5.5 mM glucose and) 0.05% FAF-BSA. Thereafter they were transferred to a perifusion chamber placed on the stage of an inverted fluorescent microscope (Nikon Diaphot) and were perifused with 25 mM Hepes-buffered medium maintained at 37degreesC containing (125 mM NaCl, 5.9 mM KCl, 1.28 mM CaCl2, 1.2 mM MgCl2,) 0.1% FAF-BSA and supplemented with 5.5 mM or 16.7 mM glucose. The 340 to 380 fluorescence ratios (F340/F380) reflects the intracellular free calcium concentration [Ca2+]i as previously described. The perifusion fluid was collected and stored at -20degreesC until assayed for insulin. Incremental Ca2+ response to stimulating glucose (DeltaR) was obtained in the same manner as described above for insulin (DeltaI).
###end p 34

###begin title 39
Measurement of cAMP content
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 165 169 159 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Dachicourt1">[21]</xref>

We next investigated by RT-qPCR the expression of genes considered as highly representative of beta-cell function, differentiation and survival (Table S1). The mRNA levels of transcription factors such as Neurod1, Pdx1, Pax6 and Nkx6.1 were 2.6 to 3.3 times more important in betahigh-cells than in betalow-cells. Meanwhile, betalow-cells expressed 2.6 to 3.6 higher levels of genes involved in early beta-cell differentiation such as Ngn3 and Tcf7l2. Regarding metabolism, some enzymes normally absent in beta-cells, such as Hk1or Ldha, were highly expressed in betalow-cells compared with betahigh-cells (12.1 to 16.7 times). At the same time several beta-cell-related metabolic enzymes such as Glut2, Gck, Pk, mtGPDH and Pcx were expressed to a lesser extent in betalow-cells (2.8 to 3.3 times). In addition, the levels of several pumps/ion channels (Kir6.2, Sur1, Cav1.2, Kv2.1 and SERCA2/3) were 2 to 4 times higher in betahigh-cells compared with betalow-cells. Similar results were obtained with genes implicated in the cAMP pathway (Gcgr, Glp1r, PKA, Rap1a and Rab3a). Expression of genes involved in insulin synthesis, maturation and secretion (Ins itself, Iapp, PC1/3, PC2, Chga, Cx36 and Slc30a8) or exocytosis (Munc13.1, Stx1a, Snap25, Vamp2 and Rim2) was downregulated in betalow-cells up to 3.8 times compared with betahigh-cells.

In order to evaluate the physiological relevance of PSA-NCAM distribution, we measured the proportion of betahigh and betalow-cells and their insulin secretory capacity in response to increased insulin demand in two animal models. We first used the ZDF fa/fa rat, a model for type 2 diabetes with impaired functional beta-cell mass [16]. In vivo insulin secretion in response to glucose is markedly impaired in these animals at 12 week age, as reflected by a 70% decrease of the insulinogenic index compared with ZDF lean controls (Fig. 7A). The geometric mean of PSA-NCAM fluorescence of beta-cells of ZDF fa/fa rats (red arrow, Fig. 7B) was strongly left-shifted compared to that of ZDF fa/+ lean controls (blue arrow, Fig. 7B), indicating a striking decrease of the expression of PSA-NCAM on beta-cells of ZDF fa/fa rats (Fig. 7B). The betalow-cell population was strongly predominant in ZDF fa/fa rats (74.6%+/-4.3% of total beta-cells) in remarkable contrast to the age-matched ZDF lean controls in which the betalow population only represented 37.2%+/-1.7% of total beta-cells (Fig. 7B). In addition, betahigh-cells from ZDF fa/fa rats were significantly less responsive to glucose compared to the ZDF lean controls (Fig. 7C) whereas the insulin secretion of betalow-cells was unchanged (Fig. 7C).

###xml 471 475 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 602 606 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>

The second model was the HG/HI rat in which the functional beta-cell mass is improved [17]. The insulinogenic index was 2-fold increased compared with control saline-infused Wistar rats (Fig. 7D). The geometric mean of PSA-NCAM fluorescence of beta-cells of HG/HI rats (red arrow, Fig. 7E) was strongly right-shifted compared to that of saline-infused Wistar rats (blue arrow, Fig. 7E), indicating a striking increase of the expression of PSA-NCAM on beta-cells of HG/HI rats (Fig. 7E). beta-cells were redistributed in favor of betahigh-cells, now representing 73%+/-3.0% of total beta-cells in HG/HI rats as compared to betahigh-cells of controls (55.3%+/-1.7% of total beta-cells) (Fig. 7E). betahigh and betalow-cells from HG/HI rats were more responsive than betahigh and betalow-cells of control rats (Fig. 7F).

###begin p 96
###xml 45 49 42 46 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>

These data show that the distribution of betahigh and betalow-cells correlates with physiological or pathological states regarding functional beta-cell mass. Therefore surface expression of PSA-NCAM reflects the ability of the pancreas to secrete insulin.
###end p 96
###begin title 97
Discussion
###end title 97
###begin p 98
###xml 110 117 107 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 257 261 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
The main outcome of our study is the demonstration of the existence of the functional beta-cell heterogeneity in vivo. Based on the specificity of PSA-NCAM expression in pancreatic beta-cells, we correlated this marker with beta-cell functional activity in rats and explored the differences between both groups of cells.
###end p 98
###begin p 99
###xml 104 107 101 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-VanSchravendijk1">[5]</xref>
###xml 109 113 106 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Hiriart2">[12]</xref>
###xml 240 243 237 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-VanSchravendijk1">[5]</xref>
###xml 245 248 242 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Kiekens1">[8]</xref>
###xml 425 429 416 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Bosco2">[26]</xref>
###xml 502 506 493 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0005555-Bosco2">[26]</xref>
###xml 717 721 702 706 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 763 766 745 748 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 782 786 764 768 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i.e.</italic>
###xml 791 795 770 774 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 848 851 824 827 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 776 780 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
beta-cells are heterogeneous in their sensitivity to glucose and to non-glucidic nutrient secretagogues [5], [12]. Their metabolic redox state differs when stimulated with glucose and it is correlated with their capacity to secrete insulin [5], [8]. Recently expression of E-cadherin at the surface of beta-cells was correlated with their insulin secretory capacity, promoting E-cadherin as a functional marker of beta-cells [26]. However E-cadherin is expressed in both exocrine and endocrine tissues [26] in contrast to PSA-NCAM which is specific to beta-cells as shown here. In the present study, using the arbitrary parameter of the geometric mean for PSA-NCAM fluorescence, we discriminated fully functional betahigh-cells from poorly glucose responsive betalow-cells in rats, i.e. betahigh-cells were highly responsive to glucose whereas betalow-cells showed weaknesses in insulin secretion.
###end p 99

The most striking difference between betahigh and betalow-cells is the small amplitude of glucose-induced raises in [Ca2+]i and insulin secretion in betalow-cells, reflecting a ticking over glucose metabolism in betalow-cells. The effects of the metabolizable amino acid leucine were similar to those of glucose which reinforces the hypothesis of metabolic impairments in betalow-cells. Glut2, Gck, Pk, mtGPDH and Pcx are metabolic enzymes implicated in the delivery of metabolites to mitochondria and thus the generation of metabolic signals, such as ATP, required for an appropriate insulin secretory response to glucose [27], [28]. The expression of these genes as well as the ATP levels were decreased in betalow-cells while normally suppressed metabolic genes (Ldha, Hk1) were upregulated. Such alterations may be sufficient to interfere with glucose recognition mechanism by regulating metabolic pathways diversionary to normal beta-cell metabolism [29]. betalow-cells also showed deficiencies in cAMP-dependent pathways as evidenced by the use of GLP-1. Although the expression of the GLP-1 receptor and ATP content were diminished in betalow-cells, the differential effects of db-cAMP also imply differences downstream of cAMP generation. Indeed expression of genes of the cAMP pathway such as Gcgr, Glp1r, PKA and Rap1a were decreased in betalow-cells. Finally betalow-cells expressed high levels of markers of progenitor cells Ngn3 and Tcf7l2[30] while islet-associated transcription factors such as Neurod1, Pdx1, Nkx6.1, Pax6 were downregulated. None of these transcription factors was completely shut off in betalow-cells but modest reductions have wide repercussions [31], [32] including insulin secretion.

###xml 1242 1245 1221 1224 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 1347 1350 1323 1326 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 1437 1441 1410 1414 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
betahigh and betalow-cells also differ from a mechanistic point of view. First they showed different cellular complexity, implying differences in intracellular components. betahigh-cells exhibited a fragmented distribution of F-actin. Conversely betalow-cells displayed a strong and continuous signal as a ring beneath the plasma membrane. This physical barrier modulates the access of insulin vesicles to the plasma membrane hence hampering exocytosis [25], [33]. In addition several proteins required in distal steps of exocytosis and its calcium regulation [34], such as SYT9, STX1, SNAP25 and VAMP2, were considerably downregulated in betalow-cells as well as key pumps and ion channels such as Kir6.2, Sur1 and Cav1.2, mediators of insulin secretion. The similar amount of released insulin in response to the depolarizing agents arginine and KCl may result from the fact that dynamics of insulin granules differs between K+ stimulation and glucose stimulation [35]. Indeed most of the granules responsible for fusion events induced by K+ stimulation consist of already docked granules to the plasma membrane whereas fusion in glucose stimulation implies newly recruited granules [35]. Collectively our data suggest that betahigh and betalow-cells may differ in their capacity to recruit new insulin granules to the cell membrane and that betalow-cells are not able to properly respond to glucose in contrast to fully functional betahigh-cells. Therefore, in normal animals, PSA-NCAM is a specific marker to detect glucose responsive beta-cells.

The physiological relevance of such functional heterogeneity is underscored by our findings in two animal models in which insulin demand is increased. In the HG/HI rat, a 48 h glucose infusion results in an increased functional beta-cell mass [17]. In this model, insulin release in response to glucose of both betahigh and betalow groups of cells was strongly enhanced compared to cells from saline-infused control rats. In the meantime the proportion of betahigh-cells rose to 75% of total beta-cells compared to 55% in control rats. This expansion of fully functional betahigh-cells and of their capacity to release insulin easily explains the functional improvement in HG/HI rats [17]. It could be hypothesized that betalow-cells may be a pool of cells recruited in presence of hyperglycemia as a component of endocrine pancreas plasticity. In ZDF fa/fa rats, a genetic model of type 2 diabetes with decreased functional beta-cell mass [16], insulin release of betahigh-cells from ZDF fa/fa rats was strongly diminished compared to betahigh-cells from control ZDF lean rats. Moreover the distribution of betahigh and betalow-cells was completely inversed in ZDF fa/fa rats, poorly functional betalow-cells becoming the predominant population (75% of total beta-cells versus 37% in ZDF lean rats). These data perfectly fits with the failure of beta-cell function in diabetic rats, i.e. impaired control of glucose homeostasis and onset of permanent hyperglycemia. Furthermore they suggest that an alteration of pancreatic beta-cell plasticity, i.e. inability to recruit fully functional beta-cells, is a key component in the development of type 2 diabetes [36]. Several reasons may account for the increased number of betalow-cells in ZDF fa/fa rats. ZDF rats have a decreased beta-cell mass due to massive apoptosis [37]. So the glucolipotoxic environment may induce the death of highly glucose responsive betahigh-cells. Otherwise, it may induce a loss of their activity favoring the loss of PSA-NCAM mobilization to the cell surface [15] which increases the number of betalow-cells.

###xml 26 34 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 588 594 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 684 687 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
The follow-up of diabetic patients and anti-diabetic strategies requires reliable methods to assess the beta-cell mass with non-invasive imagery procedures. Such approaches rely on the specific labeling of beta-cells using enzymes, cell receptors or surface structures that are expressed on beta-cells. Several beta-cell structures, including GLP-1 receptors, sulfonylurea receptors, vesicular amin transporter 2 (VMAT2) or gangliosides have been used as labeling targets. However none of them has yet provided an accurate estimation of beta-cell mass to justify a routine application in humans [38]. Because PSA-NCAM is a surface marker specific of functional beta-cells at least in rat, it can be proposed as a potential tag to investigate functional beta-cell mass by imagery approaches in vivo.
###end p 103
###begin p 104
###xml 144 147 138 141 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
###xml 221 225 212 216 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 265 269 253 257 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">high</sup>
###xml 278 281 263 266 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">low</sup>
Here we characterized two groups of beta-cells with different functional and gene expression profiles. Taken together the data suggest that betalow-cells are poorly glucose responsive in contrast to highly responsive betahigh-cells. Moreover, the proportion of betahigh and betalow-cells varied according to the insulin demand and was correlated with the progression of diabetes. PSA-NCAM constitutes definitively a powerful functional beta-cell marker which may provide further insight in beta-cell plasticity and lead to the identification of new targets in order to improve the functional status of beta-cells with low secretory capacities such as in advanced type 2 diabetes.
