Genome-wide Association of Hypoxia-inducible Factor (HIF)-1alpha and HIF-2alpha DNA Binding with Expression Profiling of Hypoxia-inducible Transcripts*
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 1051 1057 1027 1033 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
###xml 1425 1431 1385 1391 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
Hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) controls an extensive range of adaptive responses to hypoxia. To better understand this transcriptional cascade we performed genome-wide chromatin immunoprecipitation using antibodies to two major HIF-alpha subunits, and correlated the results with genome-wide transcript profiling. Within a tiled promoter array we identified 546 and 143 sequences that bound, respectively, to HIF-1alpha or HIF-2alpha at high stringency. Analysis of these sequences confirmed an identical core binding motif for HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha (RCGTG) but demonstrated that binding to this motif was highly selective, with binding enriched at distinct regions both upstream and downstream of the transcriptional start. Comparison of HIF-promoter binding data with bidirectional HIF-dependent changes in transcript expression indicated that whereas a substantial proportion of positive responses (>20% across all significantly regulated genes) are direct, HIF-dependent gene suppression is almost entirely indirect. Comparison of HIF-1alpha- versus HIF-2alpha-binding sites revealed that whereas some loci bound HIF-1alpha in isolation, many bound both isoforms with similar affinity. Despite high-affinity binding to multiple promoters, HIF-2alpha contributed to few, if any, of the transcriptional responses to acute hypoxia at these loci. Given emerging evidence for biologically distinct functions of HIF-1alpha versus HIF-2alpha understanding the mechanisms restricting HIF-2alpha activity will be of interest.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 466 467 466 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 467 467 467 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2"/>
###xml 468 469 468 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 566 567 566 567 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
###xml 566 567 566 567 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fn" rid="FN3"><sup>2</sup></xref>
###xml 670 671 666 667 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 673 674 669 670 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 754 755 750 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 755 755 751 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6"/>
###xml 756 757 752 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
Cells respond to changes in environmental oxygen levels through the coordinated regulation of the expression of a large number of genes with key functions in processes as diverse as proliferation, differentiation, apoptosis, energy metabolism, and growth factor production that are important in physiological and pathophysiological processes spanning embryonic development, adaptation to altitude, wound healing, inflammation, ischemic vascular disease, and cancer (1-3). Central to many of these responses is the transcription factor hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF),2 which is regulated by oxygen through enzymatic post-translational hydroxylation of the alpha-subunit (4, 5), which in turn regulates its stability and its interaction with coactivators (5-7).
###end p 2

###xml 136 137 124 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>

###xml 376 378 364 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 502 504 490 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 504 504 492 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20"/>
###xml 504 504 492 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21"/>
###xml 504 504 492 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22"/>
###xml 504 504 492 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23"/>
###xml 504 504 492 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24"/>
###xml 505 507 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 555 561 543 549 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
Analyses of HIF-DNA interactions at approximately50 gene loci have defined a core hypoxia response element (HRE), RCGTG that binds HIF (8). However, genome-wide transcript analyses using microarrays have indicated that a much larger number of genes respond to HIF signaling, with significant positive and negative responses extending across several hundreds of transcripts (9-18). Furthermore, recent studies indicate that HIF may affect gene expression profiles indirectly through diverse mechanisms (19-25), raising questions as to the extent of direct versus indirect effects of HIF.
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 277 279 269 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 281 283 273 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 363 365 355 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 365 365 357 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29"/>
###xml 365 365 357 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30"/>
###xml 366 368 358 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 543 545 535 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 547 549 539 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 549 549 541 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33"/>
###xml 549 549 541 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34"/>
###xml 550 552 542 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 763 765 747 749 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 767 769 751 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 771 773 755 757 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
Further complexity is generated by the existence of multiple HIF isoforms, with the best understood being HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha. These have similar domain architectures and mechanisms of regulation, and both bind to HREs and efficiently activate HRE-linked reporter genes (26, 27). However, they generate different developmental phenotypes upon inactivation (28-31). Although, differential expression may contribute to these differences, recent studies have demonstrated that each isoform appears to have distinct transcriptional targets (11, 32-35). Mechanisms of HIF-alpha transcriptional selectivity are poorly understood and chromatin immunoprecipitation studies at a limited number of loci have not shown selective binding of the two HIF-alpha isoforms (12, 36, 37). However, to date, such studies have examined only a small set of gene loci and it remains unclear how HIF-alpha binding correlates with functional effects on gene expression across the genome.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 378 379 354 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 435 441 411 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
###xml 238 243 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
To address this we have undertaken a genome-wide analysis of HIF-alpha DNA binding in MCF-7 cells using chromatin immunoprecipitation. Here we report on the distribution of HIF-1alpha- and HIF-2alpha-binding sites across more than 25,500 human gene promoters, and on the correlation of HIF-alpha binding with functional responses to HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha across the genome (9). The work provides an estimate of the scale of direct versus indirect effects of HIF on early changes in gene expression in response to HIF activation. Despite a large degree of overlap in binding of the two HIF-alpha isoforms there were striking differences in gene regulation with HIF-2alpha contributing very little to the overall HIF response.
###end p 5

###begin title 11
Chromatin Immunoprecipitation
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 307 309 299 301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 311 313 303 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 472 474 456 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 237 243 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
Three independent chromatin immunoprecipitation assays were performed using the Upstate protocol (Millipore). Cells were sonicated in 30-s pulses for a total of 3 min (Sonics & Materials, VCX 500). Chromatin was immunoprecipitated using rabbit polyclonal antisera to HIF-1alpha (PM14) and HIF-2alpha (PM9) (36, 38). These antibodies have previously been shown to perform well in chromatin immunoprecipitation assays and to be highly specific for HIF-1alpha or HIF-2alpha (36). Preimmune serum was used as a negative control.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
###xml 68 73 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)-ChIP Analysis Using Affymetrix Human Promoter 1.0R Microarray
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 220 222 220 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 469 474 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Immunoprecipitated chromatin was amplified using the Sigma Whole Genome Amplification kit according to the manufacturer's instructions. After each stage of amplification, preservation of enrichment of the PHD3 enhancer (36) was demonstrated by quantitative-PCR. 7.5 mg of amplified chromatin was then digested with 150 miliunits of DNase I (Invitrogen) for 35 min at 37 degreesC, to generate a fragment size of approximately70 bp, labeled, and hybridized to Affymetrix Human promoter 1.0R arrays, and detected according to the Affymetrix user manual.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 45 47 45 47 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 47 47 47 47 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40"/>
###xml 48 50 48 50 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 813 815 805 807 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 815 815 807 807 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40"/>
###xml 816 818 808 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
The ChIP-ChIP peak detection tool CisGenome (39-41) was used to define potential protein-binding regions. Three independent HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha chromatin immunoprecipitations were compared with three independent control chromatin immunoprecipitations performed using preimmune sera. Quantile normalization was applied prior to analysis. A moving average (MA) statistic was computed for each probe based on a half-window size of 300 bp or 5 probes. Probes with the MA statistic 4 S.D. away from the global mean were used to define protein-binding regions. Peaks were discarded if they contained less than five probes or were less than 100 base pairs in width. Peaks that were separated by less than 300 base pairs or 5 probes were merged. Peaks that had a left-tail false discovery rate >5% were discounted (39-41). The genomic coordinates of all regions were converted into coordinates based on NCBI build 36 (hg18) and mapped to the nearest transcriptional start site.
###end p 15

###begin p 23
###xml 677 685 663 671 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T1">Tables 1</xref>
###xml 690 691 676 677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 1179 1181 1149 1151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 1183 1185 1153 1155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 1242 1248 1212 1218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
###xml 1545 1551 1515 1521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Fig. 1</xref>
ChIP analyses from three independent experiments identified 546 HIF-1alpha-binding regions (length 128-1738 bp) and 143 HIF-2alpha-binding regions (length 142-1866 bp) that met stringent criteria of an MA score more than 4 mean +/- S.D., a width of 100 bp, or 5 probes or more and a false discovery rate of <5%. Binding regions were annotated using the nearest gene locus, as identified by the shortest distance to a transcriptional start site. The 546 HIF-1alpha binding sequences mapped to 394 different gene loci and the 143 HIF-2 binding sequences mapped to 134 different gene loci. The results for the top 25 sequences (as ranked by statistical significance) are given in Tables 1 and 2 and a complete list of all binding regions identified in HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha chromatin immunoprecipitations is provided in supplemental materials Tables  and . Immunoprecipitation of all of these top 25 sequences for both HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha was confirmed by quantitative-PCR. Genes at the immunoprecipitated loci included both known and previously unknown HIF-target genes. When stratified by gene ontology using the DAVID Bioinformatics Resources (david.abcc.ncifcrt.gov) (42, 43) a trend toward a higher proportion of binding to HIF-1 versus HIF-2 was observed in genes encoding glycolytic enzymes and oxidoreductase enzymes, as compared with genes encoding molecules involved in angiogenic and hematopoietic pathways. However, the numbers in some functional groups were small, and overall differences were not statistically significant (Fig. 1).

###begin title 31
Relationship of Immunoprecipitated DNA Sequences to the HRE Consensus Sequence
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 187 188 175 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
Previous studies have identified HIF binding sequences at approximately70 loci, proposed to encode direct HIF transcriptional targets, defining a consensus core HIF-binding motif, RCGTG (8).
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 550 551 550 551 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 556 558 556 558 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;9</sup>
###xml 740 741 740 741 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 746 748 746 748 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;3</sup>
As a first step in analyzing these sequences we sought to determine what proportion of the captured DNA sequences contained the RCGTG core motif. Using an MA score of >4 to define the boundaries of immunoprecipitated sequences, we found that 235 (43%) HIF-1-binding sequences and 92 (66%) of the HIF-2-binding sequences contained an RCGTG consensus, many containing multiple motifs; as expected, in each case, this frequency was very much higher than in a control set of sequences taken from the promoter regions of a randomly selected set of genes (p < 10-9). We also observed an increased frequency of the RCGTG motif in the 300 bp (the limit of resolution derived from the DNA fragmentation procedure) flanking each identified sequence (p < 10-3), indicating that many loci are associated with RCGTG motifs just outside the defined sequence, most probably reflecting the use of a very stringent boundary definition. When these flanking sequences were included, the number of sequences that contain an RCGTG motif rose to 382 (70%) for HIF-1alpha and 121 (85%) for HIF-2alpha. Thus, whereas the large majority of HIF-binding sequences contain an RCGTG motif, a significant minority apparently do not. Whether these sequences are captured through higher order DNA/protein interactions with HIF bound at HREs, or represent other modes of DNA binding by HIF proteins is unclear.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
Overall, however, within the promoter array, only a small proportion (<1%) of DNA sequences containing the core RCGTG motif bound HIF-1alpha or HIF-2alpha. We next sought to define features of the DNA sequence that might be associated with immunoprecipitation either by anti-HIF-1alpha or anti-HIF-2alpha antibodies. First, we examined the sequences in the immediate vicinity of the RCGTG motifs. All immunoprecipitated RCGTG motifs were extended by 15 base pairs in both directions. However, analysis did not reveal any significant overrepresentation of particular bases beyond the core 5-base pair motif for either HIF-1- or HIF-2-binding sites (supplemental materials Fig. ).
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 397 398 389 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
Second, we tested the predictive value of conservation in determining HIF-binding to the consensus motif. Altogether 82 of the 464 RCGTG consensus motifs that bound HIF-1alpha and 38 of 188 that bound HIF-2alpha were within the top 10% most conserved regions of the genome (as defined by PhastCons score). Although this proportion was significantly greater than would be expected by chance alone (p < 0.005), when the same analysis was performed on a set of control sequences from promoters that did not bind HIF, comparable results were obtained, suggesting that, within promoter regions, whether a putative HRE lies within a region of apparent conservation is not a good guide to its ability to bind HIF.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 1121 1123 1113 1115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 1400 1406 1392 1398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">Fig. 2</xref>
Third, we analyzed the distribution of the captured sequences and associated RCGTG motifs in relation to the transcriptional start site of the closest gene. Clearly the design of the array (intended to represent sequences from -7.5 to +2.5 kb of the transcription start site) places limits on this analysis, although a proportion of the immunoprecipitated sequences lay outside these regions, most likely representing changes in gene annotation between that on which the array was designed and the (latest) annotation that we used for locus assignment. Several interesting features were observed irrespective of whether the precise position of the peak of enrichment profile or the associated HRE was used in the analysis. The distribution of immunoprecipitated sequences was closely similar for both HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha with the greatest density of HIF-binding sites being observed within 2 kb of the transcriptional start. This region comprised two distinct frequency peaks. One is a sharp peak centered 500 bp upstream of the transcriptional start site, similar to that observed for other transcription factors (44). The other is a broader peak centered 1000 bp downstream of the transcriptional start site. This distribution contrasted markedly with the distribution of all RCGTG motifs at the same set of promoters, which revealed a single peak centered at the transcriptional start site (Fig. 2).
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 0 57 0 49 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Distribution of HIF-1&#945;- and HIF-2&#945;-binding sites.</bold>
###xml 57 58 49 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">A</italic>
###xml 262 263 246 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">B</italic>
Distribution of HIF-1alpha- and HIF-2alpha-binding sites.A, distribution of high stringency (MA score >4) HIF-1alpha- and HIF-2alpha-binding sites referred to the nearest transcriptional start site. Number of HIF-binding peaks expressed per 500-bp bin is shown. B, frequency distribution of RCGTG motifs at gene loci that bind HIF-1alpha or HIF-2alpha. The number of RCGTG motifs expressed per 500-bp bin is shown.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 429 431 429 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 549 551 549 551 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 943 944 943 944 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 953 955 953 955 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;5</sup>
###xml 957 958 957 958 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
Finally, to search for overrepresented transcription factor-binding sites that might bind factors cooperating with HIF we identified HIF-binding regions falling within 1 kb of the gene transcriptional start sites (any transcript from the gene). We then extracted the regions 1 kb up- and downstream of the transcriptional start site, omitting known exons (all annotation was taken from the ENSEMBL genome data base (release 51) (45)). We searched these regions using the vertebrate matrices of the TRANSFAC data base (release 2008.4, 621 matrices) (46), using the transcription factor-binding sites perl modules and a 90% scoring threshold cut off, recording, for each factor, and each potential target gene, the number of matches per 1000 non-exonic bases. We compared these numbers to the corresponding numbers for all non-HIF binding gene loci in the genome using a Wilcoxon test implemented in the R software package, using a threshold of p < 8 x 10-5 (p < 0.05 with Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons).
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 149 155 141 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">Fig. 5</xref>
###xml 307 309 299 301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 339 341 331 333 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 375 377 367 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 684 685 676 677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
For all matched HIF regions (combining the HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha sets), six matrices were identified as overrepresented in the HIF-binding loci (Fig. 5). Three are HIF-binding motifs, further validating the currently held HRE consensus; the others being binding motifs for nuclear respiratory factor-1 (47), Myc intron factor (MIF-1) (48), and E2F transcription factors (49). Given the strict definition of statistical significance, it is also likely that a number of other overrepresented matrices are also valid. Indeed, a number of other E2F matrices, as well as those for an array of previously described HIF-interacting transcriptional cofactors, such as STAT, ETS, and MYC (8), were enriched, but failed to reach the statistical threshold (supplemental materials Table ). When subgroups of HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha interacting gene loci were analyzed no significant difference was identified between the two sets of genes, although the numbers in this subgroup analysis were smaller.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
DNA Sequences Immunoprecipitated by Anti-HIF-1alpha and Anti-HIF-2alpha Overlap
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 677 683 645 651 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">Fig. 3</xref>
Overall, there was a substantial overlap between the loci immunoprecipitated with the two HIF isoforms. Thus, of the 394 loci that bound HIF-1alpha and the 134 loci that bound HIF-2alpha, 90 bound both isoforms. When the stringency of binding for the second isoform was relaxed slightly (MA score >3), the overlap increased, with 250/394 HIF-1alpha loci binding HIF-2alpha and 130/134 HIF-2alpha loci binding HIF-1alpha. In addition, when both HIF isoforms bind to the same gene locus, they do so at a common site. Thus, of the 90 gene loci that bound both HIF isoforms at the highest level of stringency, all apart from 3 bound HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha at overlapping sites (Fig. 3).
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 0 66 0 58 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Intersection between HIF-1&#945;- and HIF-2&#945;-binding gene loci.</bold>

###xml 146 147 130 131 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Z</italic>
###xml 158 159 142 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">B</italic>
###xml 196 197 176 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Z</italic>
###xml 257 258 233 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</italic>

C, defining HIF-2alpha capture with a Z-score of 3 excludes almost one-third of HIF-1alpha binding loci.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
Nevertheless, a substantial number of gene loci apparently bound HIF-1alpha in isolation, despite the similar consensus. We hypothesized that this could be due to higher levels of HIF-1alpha in the cells, higher affinity of HIF-1alpha capture by the immunoprecipitating antibody, a generalized higher affinity binding of HIF-1alpha to the DNA across all loci, or specific differences in affinity for the HIF-alpha isoforms at particular loci despite a similar consensus.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 1301 1307 1253 1259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">Fig. 4</xref>
Although we had selected MCF-7 cells for this study on the basis of apparently similar expression of both HIF-alpha isoforms, we wished to check the relative levels of each isoform using quantitative immunoblotting related to a common standard (supplemental materials Fig. ). These studies revealed that HIF-2alpha levels were at least as high as HIF-1alpha (probably in the region of 3-5-fold higher) indicating that the relative abundance of the two HIF isoforms cannot explain the increased capture by anti-HIF-1alpha. If the reason for the excess of sites captured by anti-HIF was a greater affinity of the anti-HIF-1alpha antibody, or a globally higher affinity of HIF-1alpha for the consensus binding motif, then it would be predicted that enrichment with anti-HIF-1alpha immunoprecipitation would generally be greater than enrichment by HIF-2alpha immunoprecipitation across sequences that were immunoprecipitated by both antibodies. We therefore examined all overlapping HIF-1alpha- and HIF-2alpha-binding sites for which the MA score was greater than 2. For each of these overlapping binding sites, the maximum fold-enrichment by the two HIF isoforms was compared. In contrast to prediction, across these common sites, HIF-2alpha showed a trend to greater maximum enrichment than HIF-1alpha (Fig. 4). Therefore, we conclude that excess HIF-1alpha-binding sites is not due to better performance of the anti-HIF-1alpha antibody or higher affinity of HIF-1alpha for its consensus, and is likely to be due to specific cooperative interactions that regulate (positively or negatively) preferential binding of HIF-alpha isoforms at certain loci. Interestingly, a weak, but significant positive correlation was also seen between the maximum fold-enrichment by each of the two antibodies suggesting that binding of one isoform to a given sequence is not a result of decreased binding of the other isoform.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 0 61 0 53 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Capture by HIF-1&#945; and HIF-2&#945; at common binding sites.</bold>
###xml 70 71 62 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Z</italic>
Capture by HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha at common binding sites. Using a Z-score of 2 for each immunoprecipitation identified 992 genomic regions that bound both HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha. For each of these sequences the maximum enrichment by HIF-2alpha immunoprecipitation was plotted against that for the HIF-1alpha immunoprecipitation. More points lie above than below the line of identity indicating greater average enrichment by HIF-2alpha immunoprecipitation at these common binding loci.

###begin title 46
Relationship of Chromatin Immunoprecipitation to the Functional Effects of the HIF-alpha Isoforms
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 251 252 243 244 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</sc>
###xml 321 322 309 310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 565 566 549 550 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 874 880 846 852 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
###xml 919 920 891 892 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 925 928 897 900 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;10</sup>
###xml 1019 1025 987 993 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
###xml 1032 1033 1000 1001 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 1038 1041 1006 1009 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;10</sup>
To better understand the functional consequences of promoter binding by HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha we next compared chromatin immunoprecipitation data with functional effects on gene expression across the genome, assayed in the same cell line using 2 mm DMOG or 1% hypoxia with or without siRNA to the HIF-alpha isoforms (9). Overall, 4% of Affymetrix transcripts were significantly up- or down-regulated by one or another of the stimuli with very strong correlations being observed between up-regulation by DMOG and hypoxia and down-regulation by HIF-1alpha siRNA (9). Genes at loci that were immunoprecipitated by anti-HIF-1alpha or anti-HIF-2alpha antibodies were very significantly more likely to be up-regulated by the hypoxia/HIF-response. Thus 20.8% of the loci immunoprecipitated by anti-HIF-1alpha manifest significant up-regulation by at least one of these stimuli versus 3.6% of those not immunoprecipitated (p < 10-10), whereas for loci immunoprecipitated by anti-HIF-2alpha the equivalent figures were 32.8 versus 3.7% (p < 10-10). Although, as expected, these correlations are highly significant, the proportion of HIF-binding loci associated with HIF-inducible transcripts was still well below 50%. To explore this further we examined other genes in the vicinity of the immunoprecipitated loci. When all genes within 100 kb of the immunoprecipitated sequences were analyzed, only a few additional regulated transcripts were identified. For instance, at HIF-1alpha binding loci only 18 further regulated genes were identified in the expression array. Hence, the majority of HIF immunoprecipitating sequences do not regulate expression of a gene within 100 kb of the binding site.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 343 344 327 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 353 355 337 339 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;5</sup>
###xml 764 765 728 729 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">r</italic>
###xml 776 777 740 741 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 807 808 767 768 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">r</italic>
###xml 819 820 779 780 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 1134 1135 1078 1079 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 1151 1152 1095 1096 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 1197 1198 1137 1138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
When HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha binding was separately correlated with functional effects of siRNA suppression a very striking difference was observed. Whereas 15.6% of gene loci that were immunoprecipitated by the HIF-1alpha antibody were down-regulated by HIF-1alpha siRNA as compared with 2.3% of gene loci that were not immunoprecipitated (p < 2 x 10-5), only 1.5% of the gene loci immunoprecipitated by the HIF-2alpha antibody were reduced by HIF-2alpha siRNA, compared with 0.3% of gene loci that did not immunoprecipitate. Furthermore, when quantitative comparisons were made between maximum fold-enrichment by anti-HIF-alpha chromatin immunoprecipitation and reduction in gene expression by HIF-alpha siRNA, a clear correlation was observed for HIF-1alpha (r = -0.367, p < 0.001) but not HIF-2alpha (r = -0.183, p = 0.04) where the gradient of any correlation was close to zero (supplemental materials Fig. ). Similarly no correlation was found between the ratio of combined HIF-1 and -2alpha siRNA to HIF-1alpha siRNA (as a measure of HIF-2alpha activity) and enrichment by anti-HIF-2alpha ChIP, whereas responses to hypoxia (p = 0.02), DMOG (p = 0.015), combined HIF-1 and -2alpha siRNA (p < 0.001), and the ratio of combined HIF-1 and -2alpha to HIF-2alpha siRNA (as a measure of HIF-1alpha activity) all correlated with enrichment by HIF-1alpha ChIP.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
Despite the increased binding of HIF-2alpha compared with HIF-1alpha at gene loci that bound both isoforms, only one of 90 (TMTC2) was significantly down-regulated by HIF-2alpha siRNA compared with 25 that were down-regulated by HIF-1alpha siRNA. Furthermore, TMTC2 was down-regulated by both siRNAs.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
In addition, when the top 500 HIF-2alpha responsive genes (defined as those manifesting the greatest reduction by HIF-2alpha siRNA) were compared with the 500 least responsive HIF-2alpha genes no differences were observed either in the numbers reaching an MA score of at least 4 or in the average MA score or fold-enrichment by anti-HIF-2alpha ChIP. Thus, even the modest effects of HIF-2alpha on gene regulation that were observed do not appear to relate to HIF-2alpha binding at these loci, suggesting that these effects of HIF may be indirect. This suggests that although HIF-2alpha is (on average) binding more strongly than HIF-1alpha at these loci, HIF-1alpha is largely or even entirely responsible for transcriptional responses over the time course of the experiments.
###end p 50
###begin title 51
Direction of Regulation by Hypoxic Stimuli and HIF Binding
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 419 426 419 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">Figs. 5</xref>
###xml 1075 1081 1067 1073 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
###xml 1154 1155 1146 1147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 1160 1163 1152 1155 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;12</sup>
###xml 1166 1172 1158 1164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">Fig. 5</xref>
Finally, to pursue the question of the extent to which hypoxia/DMOG inducible and HIF-dependent changes in gene expression are directly or indirectly dependent on HIF binding at the relevant locus, we categorized genes based on the amplitude and direction of changes in expression in response to these stimuli, and determined the proportion of genes in each category that were associated with sequences binding to HIF (Figs. 5 and supplemental ). This analysis revealed a striking difference between genes that were up-regulated by hypoxic stimuli and those that were down-regulated by these stimuli. Thus for genes that were significantly up-regulated by DMOG, 28% of all loci bound HIF-1alpha, HIF-2alpha, or both, whereas for genes that were down-regulated the figure was not significantly different from the 1.4% of genes defined as unregulated (on the basis of fold-regulation by DMOG between 0.99 and 1.01). Grouping of genes by the magnitude of regulation by DMOG revealed that more of the highly up-regulated genes bound HIF (27.5% of those regulated >4-fold by DMOG versus 12.7% of those regulated 2-4-fold and 0% of those regulated 1.5-2 fold; p < 10-12) (Fig. 5). However, no such association was observed among the down-regulated genes where even the most strongly down-regulated genes were no more likely than unregulated genes to bind HIF. Similar results were obtained whether functional responses were stratified on the basis of the response to hypoxia, or suppression of that response by siRNA directed against HIF-alpha (supplemental materials Fig. ).

###begin title 54
DISCUSSION
###end title 54
###begin p 55
Genome-wide analysis of DNA binding to the HIF transcription factors defined more than 600 sites that bound HIF-1alpha, HIF-2alpha, or both proteins, although, given the highly stringent criteria we used to define binding sites and the restriction of the tiled array to known or predicted gene promoter regions, the actual number of HIF-binding sites is likely to be substantially greater.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 57 58 57 58 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 947 949 935 937 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 951 953 939 941 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 956 965 944 953 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In silico</italic>
###xml 1066 1072 1054 1060 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
###xml 1379 1381 1367 1369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
Our results confirmed the core HIF-binding motif, RCGTG (8), and did not define other bases in the immediate vicinity that were significantly overrepresented at bound regions. Nevertheless, HIF binding to this motif was highly selective, indicating that its epigenetic context must in some way define binding. Less than 1% of such motifs within the tiled array bound HIF, and bound sites showed a highly selective distribution in relation to the transcriptional start site. Unexpectedly, binding sites were found to cluster in two distinct regions approximately0.5 kb upstream and 1 kb downstream of the start site, presumably representing the operation of other factors that promote HIF binding within these regions, or restrict accessibility or binding of HIF in the immediate vicinity of the start sites. Indeed, such binding patterns have been previously described for several other transcription factors as well as for histone modifications (50, 51). In silico searches for transcription factor binding motifs that were overrepresented at HIF binding promoters versus equivalent regions at other promoters revealed a number of sites. Interestingly, after the HIF binding consensus itself, the most strongly enriched site was the core binding motif for nuclear respiratory factor-1, a key transcriptional regulator of genes involved in mitochondrial biogenesis and function (47). Whether this reflects specific cooperation in the response to hypoxia, or the operation of physiologically distinct pathways on a group of common genes will require further study.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 92 93 92 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 638 640 638 640 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 741 743 741 743 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
Pan-genomic comparison of the chromatin immunoprecipitation data with functional responses (9) enabled HIF-binding and transcriptional responses to be compared across the genome. Surprisingly, even though analysis of HIF-binding sequences was restricted to high stringency binding at known or predicted promoter regions, the region of 70% of bound loci were not associated with genes that manifest significant responses to either hypoxia or HIF, within 100 kb of the bound locus. The proportion of HIF-binding loci responding to HIF is comparable with the ratio of Myc binding genes that were differentially modulated in response to Myc (52), where additional transcription factor binding is necessary so that targets are poised to respond (50). Furthermore, although it is also possible that these sites affect gene expression over even longer distances, or are entirely non-functional, the findings also raise the possibility that HIF has as yet unknown effects on genome integrity or function that are mediated by binding at such sites.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 114 120 114 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">versus</italic>
Comparison of chromatin immunoprecipitation and functional responses to HIF also allowed the proportion of direct versus indirect HIF-dependent responses to estimated. Across the genome, in the region of 20% of genes reported to demonstrate positive HIF-1alpha-dependent responses bound HIF at their promoters. Although we cannot be certain that all these HIF-binding sites are functional, the use of highly stringent criteria to define HIF-binding and limited genome coverage by the promoter array would suggest that 20% is more likely to be a lower estimate of the proportion of positive transcriptional responses that are directly dependent on HIF.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 520 522 520 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 522 522 522 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54"/>
###xml 523 525 523 525 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 969 974 969 974 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">trans</italic>
###xml 1188 1190 1188 1190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 1192 1194 1192 1194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 1370 1372 1370 1372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 1374 1376 1374 1376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
These results are in striking contrast with the analysis of genes manifesting down-regulation of expression in response to HIF. Expression array studies have defined similar numbers of genes that are positively and negatively regulated by HIF. In some cases promoter analyses of negatively regulated genes have defined HIF-binding sites and it has been proposed that displacement of more powerful transcriptional activators, or recruitment of corepressors to HIF, accounts for down-regulation of gene expression by HIF (53-55). Our results suggest that these particular mechanisms of HIF-dependent down-regulation of genes are unusual. When promoters of negatively regulated genes were surveyed for HIF binding we did not observe an excess of binding over that in the promoters of genes that were entirely unresponsive to HIF. Thus it appears that for the large majority of genes, HIF-dependent down-regulation of expression is likely to be due to indirect effects "in trans," rather than direct effects of HIF on the promoter. In keeping with this, a number of genes encoding transcriptional repressors have been identified as positively regulated HIF targets in this and other studies (56, 57). In addition, recent studies have established a role of hypoxia and HIF in the regulation of specific microRNAs, notably miR-210, which act to down-regulate gene expression (58, 59).
###end p 59
###begin p 60
Comparison of HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha DNA binding revealed that whereas many loci bound both HIF-alpha isoforms, substantially more bound HIF-1alpha than HIF-2alpha. Further analysis indicated that this was not due to greater abundance, better antibody capture, or a generally higher affinity of HIF-1alpha for DNA. Rather, distinct patterns of binding were observed. At common binding sites there was a tendency to greater enrichment of DNA in the anti-HIF2alpha chromatin immunoprecipitations, suggesting that affinity for HIF-2alpha was at least as high as for HIF-1alpha, whereas at other sites, binding was essentially restricted to HIF-1alpha. How this relates to functional transcriptional selectively is currently unclear.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 204 206 200 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 331 333 323 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 335 337 327 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
###xml 539 541 527 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 543 545 531 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 547 549 535 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 551 553 539 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 990 992 966 968 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 994 996 970 972 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
###xml 1068 1070 1036 1038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 50 55 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Recently, studies of selected HIF target genes in mouse embryonic stem cells demonstrated that bound HIF-2alpha is transcriptionally inactive at the loci analyzed, possibly due to a titratable repressor (12). Although studies in other cells also reported that HIF-2alpha is transcriptionally inactive on specific HIF target genes (60, 61), we had assumed that this phenomenon would be highly locus specific, because analyses conducted have demonstrated a range of HIF-2alpha-dependent transcriptional effects under specific circumstances (11, 32, 34, 35). Our genome-wide studies of both chromatin immunoprecipitation and HIF-alpha-dependent gene expression indicate that this is not the case. Rather, under the conditions of these experiments, HIF-2alpha appears to exert little or no direct transcriptional activity at HRE-associated targets across the genome despite high affinity binding to these sites. Whether this is due to interaction of HIF-2alpha with other pathways such as Myc (22, 62) or temporal differences between HIF-1alpha and HIF-2alpha activation (34) remains to be determined. Overall, our results provide a framework for a better understanding of the HIF transcriptional cascade that should be of importance in understanding the biology of hypoxia and opportunities for therapeutic manipulation of the response.
