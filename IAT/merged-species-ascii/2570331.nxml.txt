###begin title 5
Background
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 6 7 6 7 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 49 51 46 48 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 124 125 117 118 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 141 142 134 135 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 152 153 138 139 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
The Cavbeta subunits of high voltage-activated Ca2+ channels control the trafficking and biophysical properties of the alpha1 subunit. The Cavbeta-alpha1 interaction site has been mapped by crystallographic studies. Nevertheless, how this interaction leads to channel regulation has not been determined. One hypothesis is that betas regulate channel gating by modulating movements of IS6. A key requirement for this direct-coupling model is that the linker connecting IS6 to the alpha-interaction domain (AID) be a rigid structure.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 107 108 103 104 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 132 133 128 129 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 511 512 495 496 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 538 539 515 516 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 678 679 655 656 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 816 817 790 791 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
The present study tests this hypothesis by altering the flexibility and orientation of this region in alpha12.2, then testing for Cavbeta regulation using whole cell patch clamp electrophysiology. Flexibility was induced by replacement of the middle six amino acids of the IS6-AID linker with glycine (PG6). This mutation abolished beta2a and beta3 subunits ability to shift the voltage dependence of activation and inactivation, and the ability of beta2a to produce non-inactivating currents. Orientation of Cavbeta with respect to alpha12.2 was altered by deletion of 1, 2, or 3 amino acids from the IS6-AID linker (Bdel1, Bdel2, Bdel3, respectively). Again, the ability of Cavbeta subunits to regulate these biophysical properties were totally abolished in the Bdel1 and Bdel3 mutants. Functional regulation by Cavbeta subunits was rescued in the Bdel2 mutant, indicating that this part of the linker forms beta-sheet. The orientation of beta with respect to alpha was confirmed by the bimolecular fluorescence complementation assay.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 49 50 49 50 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 84 85 77 78 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 194 195 187 188 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
These results show that the orientation of the Cavbeta subunit relative to the alpha12.2 subunit is critical, and suggests additional points of contact between these subunits are required for Cavbeta to regulate channel activity.

###begin title 11
Introduction
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 35 37 35 37 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>

###xml 176 178 176 178 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 292 294 292 294 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>

###xml 371 372 363 364 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 457 458 438 439 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 476 477 453 454 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 559 561 529 531 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 620 621 590 591 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 786 787 749 750 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 824 825 787 788 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 839 842 799 802 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Lacerda1">[2]</xref>
###xml 843 846 803 806 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Singer1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1132 1135 1088 1091 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Dolphin1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1137 1140 1093 1096 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Richards1">[6]</xref>
Calcium influx via voltage-gated Ca2+ channels (Cav) play vital roles in cell physiology, such as triggering muscle contraction and hormone secretion [1]. Both the amount of Ca2+ that enters a cell, and where in the cell it enters, are highly regulated. To fulfill these specialized roles, Ca2+ channels have evolved into multimeric complexes composed of an alpha1, alpha2delta, and beta, and each of these subunits has evolved such that there are ten alpha1 genes, four alpha2delta genes, and four beta genes. Other mechanisms by which cells can fine tune Ca2+ channel activity include: alternative splicing of these Cav genes, regulation by calmodulin and G protein betagamma subunits, and phosphorylation by protein kinases. One of the first findings from studies with recombinant Cav channels was the dominant role of Cavbeta subunits [2]-[4]. Although the alpha1 subunit contains the channel pore, the voltage sensors, and most of the drug binding sites, the auxiliary subunits regulate all of these structures to increase channel opening, shift the voltage and time dependence of channel gating, and to increase drug affinity [5], [6].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 2 3 2 3 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 94 97 87 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Pragnell1">[7]</xref>
###xml 334 335 327 328 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 418 421 408 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-VanPetegem1">[8]</xref>
###xml 422 426 412 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Opatowsky1">[10]</xref>
###xml 474 475 464 465 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 527 531 514 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Hanlon1">[11]</xref>
###xml 623 624 606 607 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 777 778 753 754 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 892 893 865 866 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">o</sub>
###xml 893 897 866 870 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Harry1">[12]</xref>
###xml 899 903 872 876 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Cohen1">[13]</xref>
Cavbeta subunits are known to bind with high affinity to the I-II loop of HVA alpha1 subunits [7]. This site has been termed the alpha-interacting domain (AID), and is located 22 amino acids (a.a.) away from the C-terminus of the last transmembrane segment of repeat I (IS6). Recently three groups reported the crystal structure of Cavbeta, either alone or in complex with a synthetic peptide corresponding to the AID [8]-[10]. These results confirmed the hypothesis that Cavbeta subunits were part of the MAGUK protein family [11], and showed how the alpha-helical AID is embedded in the guanylate kinase (GK) domain of Cavbeta. Despite such a clear picture of where it binds to alpha1, it is unclear how this translates into channel regulation. In fact, splice variants of Cavbeta have been found that lack the GK domain, yet are still able to regulate the probability of channel opening, Po[12], [13].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 48 49 48 49 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 125 126 114 115 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>

###xml 229 230 214 215 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 566 570 545 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Hering1">[15]</xref>
###xml 596 597 575 576 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 694 695 666 667 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 717 718 689 690 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 748 749 717 718 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">o</sub>
###xml 836 840 805 809 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Wakamori1">[16]</xref>
###xml 842 846 811 815 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Yasuda1">[17]</xref>
###xml 941 942 906 907 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 1066 1067 1031 1032 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 1267 1268 1222 1223 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
Previously we have shown that some aspects of Cavbeta regulation could be conferred on a T-type channel alpha1 subunit (alpha13.1) by transfer of the AID region from alpha12.2 [14]. Similar to their regulation of HVA channels, Cavbeta shifted the voltage dependence of activation to more hyperpolarized potentials, and increased the amount of current observed at the end of a sustained pulse. These studies provided the first evidence that beta regulation required a rigid linker between IS6 and the AID, thereby providing support for the direct coupling hypothesis [15], which postulates that Cavbeta alters movements of the IS6 segment that occur during gating. Notably missing from the alpha13.1-2.2 chimera was Cavbeta's regulation of channel Po and closed state inactivation, which has been observed with wild-type N-type channels [16], [17]. Due to these limitations, we have now tested the direct coupling hypothesis by mutating alpha12.2 directly. We show that deletion of a single amino acid in the IS6-AID linker is sufficient to abolish most aspects of Cavbeta regulation (except trafficking to the plasma membrane). This result seemingly contradicts the direct-coupling hypothesis, and highlights the importance of beta's orientation with respect to alpha1 in allowing interaction with its gating machinery.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Results
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 32 33 32 33 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 394 400 384 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003560-g001">Fig. 1</xref>
###xml 616 620 606 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Arias1">[14]</xref>
###xml 817 818 800 801 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 874 875 853 854 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 859 862 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
The direct coupling model for Cavbeta regulation predicts that the linker separating the AID from IS6 is a rigid alpha helix or beta sheet. To test this hypothesis, we replaced six consecutive amino acids in the middle of the linker with either glycine (PG6) residues to introduce flexibility, or as a positive control for charge disruptions, with alanines (PA6) to conserve a rigid structure (Fig. 1). Previous circular dichroism studies using peptides designed against wild-type, PG6, and PA6, confirmed the PG6 mutation increased the random coil content from 38.5 to 51%, and the PA6 mutation decreased it to 28% [14]. As a second test of the direct coupling hypothesis, we deleted 1, 2, or 3 amino acids in the middle of the linker in order to alter the orientation of AID-bound beta subunit with respect to alpha1. These mutations were introduced into a rat brain alpha12.2, then studied in HEK-293 cells by whole cell patch clamp electrophysiology.
###end p 16

Hallmarks of Cavbeta regulation of high voltage-activated alpha1 subunits include: their ability to increase the number of functional channels at the plasma membrane (due to both an increase in surface expression of alpha1 and an increased probability that these channels will open (Po) in response to a test depolarization); and to shift the voltage dependence of channel activation [18]. This regulation was reliably detected under our experimental conditions (Fig. 2). Notably, Cavbeta regulation of inactivation was isoform-specific. Various beta2 splice variants, such as beta2a, have the ability to dramatically slow inactivation of alpha12.x currents [19], [20], which can be quantitated by measuring the residual current after 350 ms of depolarization and normalizing it to the peak current amplitude (R350; Fig. 2C,D). The ability of beta2a to increase R350 was relatively voltage independent, therefore the value at +20 mV is representative and is the value reported in the Tables. beta3 did not have a statistically significant effect on the R350 of WT channels (Fig. 2D, Table 1). Isoform-specific effects on the steady-state inactivation curve (hinfinity) were also observed, with beta2a shifting the mid-point (V50) +6 mV, while beta3 produced a large -30 mV shift in the V50 (Fig. 2F). As noted previously, this beta3 effect is due to acceleration of closed state inactivation [17]. Preliminary results with beta1a and beta4 were similar to those obtained with beta3 (data not shown), therefore we selected beta2a and beta3 for further study. In addition, native N-type channels are formed by alpha12.2 and either beta2a or beta3 [21], [22]. These electrophysiological signatures provide assays of Cavbeta regulation that can be used to test alpha12.2 mutants for loss of regulation (Table 1). Specifically, these betas increase current density over 20-fold and shift the activation curve -10 mV, beta2a increase the R350 current 10-fold, and beta3 shifts steady-state inactivation -34 mV (Table 1).

The IS6-AID linker sequence is more highly conserved than the AID itself. For example, in the alpha12.x family there is only 1 substitution in 20 residues of the linker (a leucine in alpha12.1 and alpha12.2 is substituted by methionine in alpha12.3), but 3 substitutions in the 20 AID residues. As predicted, almost all the hallmarks of beta regulation were observed with PA6 channels: betas increased peak currents >15-fold, shifted activation approximately-10 mV, beta2a increased R350, and beta3 shifted the hinfinity curve (Fig. 3E-H, Table 1). In contrast, beta3 regulation of PA6 was similar to its regulation of WT channels in terms of current density and ability to shift the hinfinity curve. The results with PG6 and PA6 are entirely consistent with the IS6-AID linker being a ordered structure as observed in circular dichroism studies of isolated peptides [14].

Using the same voltage protocols as Alger et al., (2005) we were able to reliably measure gating currents in cells transfected with beta2a, alpha2delta1, and either WT or Bdel1 (Fig. 5). Representative traces from the same cells clearly show that WT channels generate large currents from a small number of channels, while Bdel1 generates smaller currents from a larger number of channels. The slope of the line correlating Gmax to Qmax was 19-fold lower for Bdel1 (0.074+/-0.004, n = 7) than for WT channels (1.32+/-0.13, n = 8, P<0.01). This results shows the Po of Bdel1 channels is extremely low, explaining why current density was so low. As noted in the Methods, the transfection protocol was different between WT and Bdel1, thereby precluding direct comparison of Gmax and Qmax in this assay. We conclude that deletion of a single amino acid in the IS6-AID linker abolishes almost all beta regulation of the biophysical properties of alpha12.2, including its ability to increase Po, leaving only the beta regulation of trafficking.

###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 111 112 104 105 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>

###xml 1457 1461 1420 1424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Shyu1">[31]</xref>
###xml 1563 1569 1526 1532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003560-g006">Fig. 6</xref>
###xml 1701 1707 1661 1667 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003560-g006">Fig. 6</xref>
###xml 2190 2191 2134 2135 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
To address the question of whether the deletions in the IS6-AID linker altered the orientation of beta to alpha12.2 more directly, we utilized bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) analysis [29]. In this method a fluorescent protein such as CFP is split into two fragments, and then fused to proteins of interest. If the proteins of interest interact, and the two CFP fragments are brought into the proper orientation, then they will reassemble and restore fluorescence. In the present study we relied on the high affinity binding of beta to the AID region on alpha12.2 [30], and fused the fragments of the cyan fluorescent protein (CFP) to the N-terminus of alpha12.2 and either the N- or C-termini of beta3. Preliminary experiments with a full-length beta3 tested which combination of tagged proteins could restore the proper orientation (see Methods for all combinations tested), and found the strongest BiFC signal when the big N-terminal fragment of CFP (a.a. 1-158) was fused to the N-terminus of alpha12.2, and the small C-terminal fragment of CFP was fused to the C-terminus of beta3. To restrict the movement of the C-terminal CFP fragment, we truncated the C-terminus of beta3 to the same length (beta3-core) used in the crystallographic studies [9]. HEK-293 cells were also cotransfected with the RFP, mCherry, which allowed for both selection of transfected cells and calculation of the cyan BiFC to red ratio. As described previously [31], the specificity of the BiFC signal can be calculated from the median ratio of the cyan/red signals (Fig. 6). In our experiments the strongest BiFC signal was observed with tagged Bdel1+beta3core constructs, being 3.4-fold higher than WT (Fig. 6). In contrast, the tagged Bdel2+beta3core combination produced a lower BiFC signal than Bdel1+beta3core, but still higher than WT, a difference that was statistically significant. These results are consistent with the electrophysiology results, where both WT and Bdel2 channels are regulated by beta subunits, while Bdel1 is not. We affirm that these results strongly support our hypothesis that the Bdel mutations alter the orientation of the beta subunit with respect to the alpha1 subunit.

###xml 1241 1245 1188 1192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Opatowsky1">[10]</xref>
###xml 1366 1367 1306 1307 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 1395 1397 1335 1337 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 1474 1478 1414 1418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Soldatov1">[37]</xref>

###xml 1536 1540 1476 1480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Long1">[38]</xref>
###xml 1640 1644 1577 1581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Hering1">[15]</xref>
###xml 1646 1650 1583 1587 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Striessnig1">[39]</xref>
###xml 1754 1755 1684 1685 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 1800 1803 1730 1733 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-VanPetegem1">[8]</xref>
###xml 1805 1809 1735 1739 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Hering1">[15]</xref>
Since the seminal experiments of Ringer on cardiac muscle contraction [32], it has been recognized that Ca2+ entry into cells provides a crucial trigger for life and death processes. Key pathways for Ca2+ entry are voltage-gated Ca2+ channels, and to fulfill specialized roles these channels have evolved into ten alpha1 subunit genes that are extensively spliced to generate unique channels. Biochemical purification of high voltage-activated channels revealed their multi-subunit structure, being composed of alpha1, alpha2delta, and beta subunits [33], [34]. Studies with the cloned subunits clearly established the critical roles of the alpha2delta and beta subunits in trafficking and regulating the biophysical properties of the alpha1 subunit. For these reasons, the mechanism of action of these subunits has been extensively investigated. Arguably the greatest progress has been made in understanding the roles and mechanism of action of beta subunits [18], [35], [36]. Campbell and coworkers provided a major breakthrough by mapping the site on alpha1 that binds beta, termed the AID [7]. The precise details of this interaction were elucidated by X-ray crystallographic studies of AID peptides bound to beta2, beta3, and beta4 [8]-[10]. The AID anchors the beta subunit 22 amino acids away from the end of IS6, a distance that is invariant in all HVA alpha1 subunits. S6 segments of Ca2+ channels are thought to form an inner gate that opens during channel gating [37], as so clearly observed in K+ channel crystal structures [38]. S6 segments are also involved in drug binding, and this binding can be regulated by beta subunits [15], [39]. Taken together, these observations led to the direct coupling hypothesis, whereby beta modulates alpha1 gating by direct modulation of IS6 movement [8], [15].
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 62 63 62 63 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 70 71 66 67 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 174 178 170 174 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-PerezReyes1">[40]</xref>
###xml 323 324 315 316 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 338 339 326 327 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 343 347 331 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Arias1">[14]</xref>
###xml 796 800 778 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Arias1">[14]</xref>
###xml 1040 1041 1022 1023 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 1131 1135 1113 1117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-AriasOlgun1">[41]</xref>
###xml 1169 1170 1151 1152 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 1181 1185 1163 1167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Baumgart1">[42]</xref>
###xml 1256 1257 1235 1236 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">o</sub>
###xml 1496 1497 1468 1469 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
In sharp contrast to HVA channels, expression of cloned LVA Cav3 alpha1 subunits produces robust currents with properties that are nearly identical to native T-type currents [40]. In a previous study we exploited this difference to make chimeras that would test the direct coupling hypothesis, moving the I-II loop of alpha12.2 into alpha13.1 [14]. Four key results from this study were: one, that some aspects of beta regulation could be conferred (beta induced a shift in activation curve and slowed open channel inactivation); two, that this regulation was completely dependent on the IS6-AID linker; 3) circular dichroism of peptides corresponding to WT, PA6, and PG6 confirmed the ability of these mutations to disrupt structure; and 4) that this linker was likely to form a rigid structure [14]. Two other interesting conclusions from this previous study were: 1) that the distal portion of the S6 segment is part of an inner gating ring that controls channel inactivation; and 2) that the post-IS6 linker acts as a gating brake in Cav3 channels. The structure of this gating brake is likely an antiparellel helix-loop-helix [41], and is conserved in all three Cav3 channels [42]. A limitation to this previous study was that betas had no effect on Po, and beta isoform specific regulation of inactivation from open and closed states were not conferred to the chimera. Therefore, in the present study we have explored the importance of the IS6-AID structure by mutating this region in alpha12.2.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 242 250 226 227 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8734;</sub>
We show that mutations that induce flexibility and destabilize alpha-helices and beta-sheets, as with PG6, totally abolish beta subunit regulation. Specifically, beta subunits no longer shift the activation curve, beta3 no longer shifts the hinfinity curve, and beta2a no longer increases plateau currents. As a control, we show that replacement of these same amino acids with alanines largely retain beta regulation of gating. Interestingly, beta3 accelerated inactivation kinetics of PA6, and beta2a's ability to affect inactivation was altered. Two possible explanations for the loss of beta regulation of PA6 are: one, particular amino acid residues may play a role (e.g. charged residues stabilize interactions with other channel regions) or two, the structure of PA6 is not identical to WT.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 354 355 347 348 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 765 766 736 737 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">v</sub>
###xml 872 878 830 836 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003560-g007">Fig. 7</xref>
###xml 1287 1291 1235 1239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Opatowsky1">[10]</xref>
###xml 1333 1337 1281 1285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Geourjon1">[43]</xref>
###xml 1697 1698 1628 1629 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 1824 1825 1745 1746 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 1876 1877 1797 1798 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">o</sub>
As a second test of the direct coupling hypothesis, we deleted 1, 2, or 3 amino acids from the middle of the IS6-AID linker. The key results were loss of beta regulation in Bdel1 and Bdel3, and retention of regulation in Bdel2. A limitation to these studies is that little or no currents could be recorded from Bdel2 or Bdel3 when expressed alone (+alpha2delta1), so the presence or absence of beta regulation could only be inferred by comparisons between beta2a and beta3. Nevertheless, the ability of betas to increase functional channels at the plasma membrane was retained in all 3 Bdel mutants. We conclude that the central region of the IS6-AID adopts a beta-sheet structure. Deletion of 1 or 3 amino acids in this beta-sheet would alter the orientation of Cavbeta with respect to alpha1 by 180degrees, while deletion of two would return the orientation back to WT (Fig. 7). Although beta subunits were crystallized with peptides corresponding to the AID sequence, these peptides did not include the 20 amino acids of the IS6-AID linker, consequently the exact structure of this region was not determined. Opatowsky et al., (2004) proposed an interesting hypothesis whereby beta binds to a disordered AID and induces its folding into an alpha helix that extends all the way to IS6 [10]. Secondary structure prediction programs [43] suggest that this linker has a high tendency to form an alpha helix, and near the middle of the linker an equal tendency to form a beta sheet. Deletions in this region may preferentially stabilize the beta-sheet structure. Regardless of the structure, the orientation of beta subunit is critical for its ability to regulate the biophysical properties of alpha12.2 channels. We hypothesize that beta is precisely anchored at the AID to orient other parts of beta towards regions of alpha1 that control channel opening (thereby modulating Po and the activation curve) and channel inactivation.
###end p 55

###xml 1792 1796 1731 1735 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Geib1">[49]</xref>
###xml 1798 1802 1737 1741 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003560-Walker1">[50]</xref>
###xml 1884 1885 1819 1820 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 2102 2103 2028 2029 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">o</sub>
###xml 2222 2223 2141 2142 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
Mutations in the AID that weaken its interaction with beta subunits may allow a new orientation, which explains why the W391A mutation in alpha12.2 abolishes beta1 but not beta2a regulation [30]. Alternative splicing of either beta subunits [44]-[46] or alpha1 subunits may provide additional isoform specific regulation [47]. The crystal structures of betas provided clues for additional binding sites: one, the SH3 domain, which is a common structural motif at protein interaction sites, and two, the large groove between the GK and SH3 domain [8]-[10]. Notably, a rigid IS6-AID linker would orient this groove directly below the cytoplasmic face of alpha1. The other interaction sites on alpha1 are likely to be of much lower affinity, allowing regulatory proteins such as G protein betagamma to disrupt these interactions. In this scenario, Gbetagamma could shift channels into reluctant states without complete dissociation of Cavbeta from the AID, which reconciles conflicting observations [6], [8], [48]. Mutational studies support the hypothesis that all voltage-gated channels have a similar inverted teepee structure where the intracellular gate is formed by a bundle crossing of S6 segments as observed in the crystal structure of Shaker K+ channels [15], [37], [38]. The precise positioning of Cavbeta near this bundle crossing would allow it to interact with any of the post-S6 segments. Inappropriate interactions appear to severely accelerate channel inactivation, as observed with the needle-like kinetics of PG6 and Bdel2 induced by beta3, which may be due to novel interactions with other post-S6 segments. Accordingly, De Waard and colleagues have provided evidence that post-IIIS6, as well as the carboxyl terminus, play important roles in Cavbeta regulation of alpha12.1 [49], [50]. An alternative version of the direct coupling hypothesis is that the other alpha1-beta interactions serve mainly as a fulcrum, allowing beta to restrain movement of IS6. The gating current studies show that deletion of one amino acid from IS6-AID linker largely disrupts betas ability to increase Po, therefore, we conclude that orientation of beta is critical for its regulation of the biophysical properties of alpha12.2, and confirm the importance of additional points of contact between the two subunits.

###begin title 67
Electrophysiology
###end title 67
###begin p 68
###xml 465 466 465 466 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 643 644 643 644 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>

Electrophysiological experiments were carried out using the whole cell configuration of the patch clamp technique. Recordings were obtained using an Axopatch 200A amplifier equipped with a CV201A headstage. The amplifier was connected to a computer (Dell, Round Rock, TX) through a Digidata 1200 A/D converter, and controlled using pCLAMP 9.2 software (Axon Instruments, Union City, CA). Currents were recorded using the following external solution (in mM): 10 BaCl2, 156 tetraethyl ammonium (TEA) chloride, and 10 HEPES, pH adjusted to 7.4 with TEA-OH. The internal pipette solution contained the following (in mM): 125 CsCl, 10 BAPTA, 2 CaCl2, 1 MgCl2, 4 Mg-ATP, 0.3 Li-GDPbetaS, and 10 HEPES, pH adjusted to 7.2 with CsOH.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
Pipettes were made from TW-150-3 capillary tubing (World Precision Instruments, Inc., Sarasota, FL). Initial pipette resistance was typically 2-3 MOmega. Access resistance and cell capacitance were measured using on-line exponential fits to a capacitance transient (Membrane Test, Clampex). Cell capacitance ranged between 8-30 pF. Access resistance averaged 4.2 MOmega. Data from cells where the access resistance exceeded 5.5 MOmega were discarded. Series resistance was compensated between protocols to 70% (prediction and correction; 10 micros lag), resulting in maximal residual voltage error below 1.6 mV during measurement of the current-voltage relationship. Data were collected at room temperature.

To balance the effects of inactivation, slow recovery, and rundown, we selected 350 ms pulses for the current-voltage protocol, and an inter-sweep interval of 20 s. Peak currents at each voltage step were used to calculate the voltage dependence of activation (V0.5, and k), and the conductance (G) as described previously [27]. The current at the end of the depolarizing pulse was also measured, and divided by the peak current in that pulse to derive the R350 value. The voltage dependence of steady-state inactivation was estimated using 15 s prepulses to varying potentials followed by a test pulse to +40 mV to measure channel availability (h). The current elicited during each test pulse was normalized to that observed when the holding potential was -90 mV (I/Imax), and the data from each cell were fit with a Boltzmann equation using Prism(R) software (version 5, Graphpad Software, San Diego, CA). Results are presented as mean+/-SEM. Significant differences in the average data were analyzed using one-way ANOVA followed by Bonferroni's multiple comparison test (GraphPad Prism).

The CFP fragments and beta3 coding region were PCR amplified with primers that added unique restriction sites in the correct reading frame. The CFP fragments were ligated to full-length alpha12.2 cDNA using KpnI (polylinker) and AscI (-63), thereby creating a flexible 21 a.a. linker (5 glycines) from the 5' untranslated region. The CFP-beta3 linker included an SbfI site that encoded PAGAT, while the beta3-CFP linker included a BspEI site that encoded SGAT. Of the four combinations that could produce a BiFC signal, the largest signal was detected with a1B-B+SCB3. The crystal structure of beta3 was obtained with a construct called beta3core (B3c). In agreement with previous reports [9], [55], we found that B3c remains functional despite having the non-conserved amino and carboxy termini removed (results not shown). Since the 121 a.a. of the C-terminus might allow flexibility that would obscure the orientation of beta, we prepared a C-terminal CFP (a.a. 159-238; SCB3c) fused to the beta3core. The Bdel1-B and Bdel2-B were prepared using the same KpnI/AscI cloning strategy as for WT. These constructs (88 ng each) were transiently transfected into HEK-293 cells along with alpha2delta1 and the red fluorescent protein (RFP), mCherry [56]. Similar results were obtained using the RFP, DsRed-Monomer (Clontech), so the results were pooled. After 18 hrs the cells were plated onto polylysine-treated glass bottom dishes (Fluorodish, World Precision Instruments, Saratoga, FL). Transfected cells were identified by their red fluorescence, and their red and cyan fluorescence signals were collected with IPLab software and the Olympus microscope described above (40x objective, 2x2 binning, confocal off). Data were background subtracted using a region devoid of cells, and the ratio of green to red signal for each cell was calculated. Following the method of Shyu et al. [31], BiFC specificity was determined using the median of the CFP/RFP signal for each condition. Statistical analysis was performed using one-way ANOVA with the Kruskal-Wallis post test using GraphPad Prism.
###end p 74
