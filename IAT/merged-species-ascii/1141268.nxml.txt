Processing of Pre-microRNAs by the Dicer-1-Loquacious Complex in Drosophila Cells
###end article-title 0
###begin article-title 1
Normal microRNA Maturation and Germ-Line Stem Cell Maintenance Requires Loquacious, a Double-Stranded RNA-Binding Domain Protein
###end article-title 1
###begin article-title 2
miRNA processing: dicer-1 meets its match
###end article-title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 512 522 512 522 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 665 675 665 675 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
microRNAs (miRNAs) are a large family of 21- to 22-nucleotide non-coding RNAs that interact with target mRNAs at specific sites to induce cleavage of the message or inhibit translation. miRNAs are excised in a stepwise process from primary miRNA (pri-miRNA) transcripts. The Drosha-Pasha/DGCR8 complex in the nucleus cleaves pri-miRNAs to release hairpin-shaped precursor miRNAs (pre-miRNAs). These pre-miRNAs are then exported to the cytoplasm and further processed by Dicer to mature miRNAs. Here we show that Drosophila Dicer-1 interacts with Loquacious, a double-stranded RNA-binding domain protein. Depletion of Loquacious results in pre-miRNA accumulation in Drosophila S2 cells, as is the case for depletion of Dicer-1. Immuno-affinity purification experiments revealed that along with Dicer-1, Loquacious resides in a functional pre-miRNA processing complex, and stimulates and directs the specific pre-miRNA processing activity. These results support a model in which Loquacious mediates miRNA biogenesis and, thereby, the expression of genes regulated by miRNAs.
###end p 3
###begin p 4
This and an accompanying paper by Forstemann et al. identify Loquacious, which encodes a double-stranded RNA binding domain protein, and partners with Dicer-1 in the processing of microRNAs.

###begin title 5
Introduction
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 172 176 172 176 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b01">1&#8722;12</xref>
###xml 262 267 262 267 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b13">13&#8722;15</xref>
###xml 448 453 448 453 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b16">16&#8211;30</xref>
###xml 619 621 619 621 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b31">31</xref>
###xml 733 738 733 738 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b32">32&#8211;34</xref>
###xml 605 610 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
microRNAs (miRNAs) act as RNA guides by binding to complementary sites on target mRNAs to regulate gene expression at the post-transcriptional level in plants and animals [1-12], much as small interfering RNAs (siRNAs) do in the RNA interference (RNAi) pathway [13-15]. The expression of miRNAs is often developmentally regulated in a tissue-specific manner, suggesting an important role for miRNAs in the regulation of endogenous gene expression [16-30]. The importance of miRNAs for development is also highlighted by a recent computer-based analysis that predicted nearly a thousand miRNA genes in the human genome [31]. Furthermore, recent studies have revealed that miRNAs regulate a large fraction of the protein-coding genes [32-34].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 91 93 91 93 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b35">35</xref>
###xml 185 190 185 190 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b36">36&#8211;38</xref>
###xml 469 474 469 474 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b39">39&#8211;41</xref>
###xml 677 682 677 682 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b42">42&#8211;44</xref>
###xml 787 789 787 789 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b45">45</xref>
###xml 916 918 916 918 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b46">46</xref>
###xml 957 959 957 959 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b47">47</xref>
miRNAs are transcribed as long primary miRNA (pri-miRNA) transcripts by RNA polymerase II [35]. miRNA maturation begins with cleavage of the pri-miRNAs by the nuclear RNase III Drosha [36-38] to release approximately 70-nucleotide hairpin-shaped structures, called precursor miRNAs (pre-miRNAs). Pre-miRNAs are then exported to the cytoplasm by the protein Exportin 5, which recognizes the two-nucleotide 3' overhang that is a signature of RNase III-mediated cleavage [39-41]. In the cytoplasm, pre-miRNAs are subsequently cleaved by a second RNase III enzyme, Dicer, into approximately 22-nucleotide miRNA duplexes, with an end structure characteristic of RNase III cleavage [42-44]. Only one of the two strands is predominantly transferred to the RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC) [45], which mediates either cleavage of the target mRNA or translation silencing, depending on the complementarity of the target [46] by a mechanism that remains unclear [47].
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 132 134 132 134 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b48">48</xref>
###xml 275 298 275 298 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caenorhabditis elegans,</italic>
###xml 370 375 370 375 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b49">49&#8211;52</xref>
###xml 519 521 519 521 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b53">53</xref>
###xml 522 524 522 524 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b54">54</xref>
###xml 696 698 696 698 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b55">55</xref>
###xml 699 701 699 701 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b56">56</xref>
###xml 707 718 707 718 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila,</italic>
###xml 765 767 765 767 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b57">57</xref>
###xml 768 770 768 770 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b58">58</xref>
###xml 964 966 964 966 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b59">59</xref>
###xml 969 979 969 979 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1037 1039 1037 1039 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b58">58</xref>
###xml 1040 1042 1040 1042 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b60">60</xref>
###xml 275 297 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
There is a growing list of double-stranded RNA (dsRNA)-binding proteins that play important yet distinct roles in the RNAi pathway [48]. Both Drosha and Dicer contain dsRNA-binding domains (dsRBDs). Drosha requires a dsRNA-binding protein partner known as Pasha in flies and Caenorhabditis elegans, and its ortholog DGCR8 in mammals to convert pri-miRNAs to pre-miRNAs [49-52]. In plants, the predominantly nuclear Dicer-like-1, equipped with two dsRBDs, is thought to catalyze both pri-miRNA and pre-miRNA processing [53,54]. The HYL1 protein, which also contains a tandem dsRBD, is required for miRNA accumulation and may play the same molecular role as Pasha/DGCR8 for Dicer-like-1 in plants [55,56]. In Drosophila, Dicer-2 is required for production of siRNAs [57,58], and forms a heterodimeric complex with the dsRNA-binding protein R2D2, which is required for its function in RISC assembly, although Dicer-2 alone suffices to convert long dsRNA into siRNAs [59]. Drosophila Dicer-1 is associated with the processing of pre-miRNAs [58,60]. However, if there is a dsRNA-binding protein partner for Dicer-1, it has not been identified.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 19 29 19 29 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 149 159 149 159 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
Here, we show that Drosophila Dicer-1 interacts with the dsRBD protein Loquacious (Loqs). Depletion of Loqs results in accumulation of pre-miRNAs in Drosophila S2 cells. Loqs is predominantly cytoplasmic and is conserved in mammals. Immuno-affinity purification experiments, together with the use of recombinant Loqs, reveal that along with Dicer-1, Loqs resides in a functional pre-miRNA processing complex, and stimulates and directs specific pre-miRNA processing activity. These results support a model in which Loqs mediates miRNA biogenesis and, thereby, the expression of genes regulated by miRNAs.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Results
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 49 51 49 51 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b61">61</xref>
###xml 73 83 73 83 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 100 102 100 102 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b62">62</xref>
###xml 163 173 163 173 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 497 507 497 507 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 528 536 528 536 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 593 595 593 595 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b59">59</xref>
###xml 596 598 596 598 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b63">63</xref>
###xml 497 507 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
We have used RNAi-based reverse-genetic methods [61] to screen a list of Drosophila dsRBD proteins [62] for a protein(s) that has an effect on miRNA biogenesis in Drosophila S2 cells and found a novel protein equipped with three dsRBDs (two canonical dsRBDs at the N-terminal half, and one non-canonical dsRBD at the C-terminal), originally dubbed CG6866 (candidate gene 6866), which has a role in pre-miRNA processing (data presented below). This protein bears high similarity to R2D2 and to the C. elegans RNAi protein RDE-4 (Figure 1), both of which contain dsRBDs and interact with Dicer [59,63]. Thus the sequence data show that CG6866 is a paralog of R2D2. A parallel study presents genetic evidence that several types of silencing are lost in CG6866 mutant flies (Forstemann K, et al. DOI: 10.1371/journal.pbio.0030236). Therefore, CG6866 was designated as Loquacious ("very talkative").
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Depletion of Loqs and Dicer-1 by RNAi Resultsin Pre-miRNA Accumulation
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 64 74 64 74 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 76 78 76 78 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b58">58</xref>
###xml 181 191 181 191 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">miR-bantam</italic>
###xml 197 204 197 204 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">miR-ban</italic>
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b60">60</xref>
###xml 292 299 292 299 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">miR-ban</italic>
###xml 301 309 301 309 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 362 370 362 370 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 457 465 457 465 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 607 612 607 612 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">miR-8</italic>
###xml 663 671 663 671 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 801 803 801 803 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b59">59</xref>
###xml 857 865 857 865 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 871 872 871 872 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g002">2</xref>
###xml 185 191 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">bantam</span>
Dicer-1 has been shown to be the pre-miRNA processing factor in Drosophila [58]. We have previously shown that depletion of Dicer-1 by RNAi resulted in a marked accumulation of pre-miR-bantam (pre-miR-ban) [60]. Depletion of Loqs by RNAi resulted in a similar effect to Dicer-1 depletion for miR-ban (Figure 2A). Loqs dsRNAs caused the suppression of Loqs mRNA (Figure 2B). RNAi against Loqs does not appear to affect Dicer-1 protein levels (lower panel in Figure 2B), suggesting that the observed pre-miRNA accumulation in Loqs-depleted cells is not simply due to destabilizing Dicer-1. Similar effects on miR-8 were seen in Dicer-1- and Loqs-depleted S2 cells (Figure 2C). Depletion of Dicer-2 and R2D2, which form the enzyme complex predominantly responsible for generating siRNAs from long dsRNA [59], had no significant effect on pre-miRNA processing (Figure 2A and 2C). These results show that along with Dicer-1, Loqs is essential for efficient pre-miRNA processing in vivo.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Loqs Associates with Dicer-1 In Vivo and In Vitro
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 364 372 364 372 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 594 602 594 602 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g003">Figure 3</xref>
This observation prompted us to ask if Loqs forms a complex in vivo with Dicer-1. For these studies, we simultaneously expressed Dicer-1 tagged with the Flag epitope and Loqs tagged with the myc epitope in S2 cells. We then immunoprecipitated Dicer-1 with anti-Flag antibodies, and Loqs with anti-myc antibody and then analyzed the precipitates by immunoblotting (Figure 3A). In reciprocal assays, Dicer-1 and Loqs were found to co-precipitate. Consistent with these findings that Dicer-1 and Loqs form a complex in vivo, both proteins are localized predominantly in the cytoplasm of S2 cells (Figure 3B).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 328 336 328 336 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g003">Figure 3</xref>
We further investigated whether Loqs can bind to Dicer-1 in vitro. Dicer-1 was produced by an in vitro translation system and used in binding assays with recombinant Loqs fused to glutathione S-transferase (GST). GST-Loqs interacted with Dicer-1 even in the presence of RNase A, whereas GST itself showed no detectable binding (Figure 3C). These results demonstrate that the association of Loqs with Dicer-1 occurs both in vivo and in vitro, and that RNA molecules do not appear to mediate the association.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Dicer-1 and Loqs Are Present in a Functional Complex That Mediates Pre-miRNA Processing
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 301 324 301 324 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 324 329 324 329 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">let-7</italic>
###xml 367 372 367 372 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">let-7</italic>
###xml 408 413 408 413 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">let-7</italic>
###xml 452 457 452 457 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">let-7</italic>
###xml 485 493 485 493 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g004">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 531 533 531 533 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b60">60</xref>
###xml 626 631 626 631 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">let-7</italic>
###xml 667 672 667 672 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">let-7</italic>
###xml 697 705 697 705 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g004">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 747 752 747 752 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">let-7</italic>
###xml 861 866 861 866 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">let-7</italic>
###xml 945 950 945 950 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">let-7</italic>
###xml 975 983 975 983 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g004">Figure 4</xref>
To examine the functional connection between the Dicer-1-Loqs complex and pre-miRNA processing, we investigated if depletion of Dicer-1 or Loqs had any effect on the production of mature miRNA from the precursor. We first tested if cytoplasmic lysates of S2 cells were capable of processing synthetic Drosophila melanogasterlet-7 precursor RNA into functional mature let-7. In this experiment, the synthetic let-7 precursor RNA was converted to mature let-7 in S2 cytoplasmic lysates (Figure 4A), as is the case in embryo lysates [60]. In in vitro RNAi assay, target RNA harboring a sequence perfectly complementary to mature let-7 was cleaved efficiently within the let-7 complementary sequence (Figure 4B), thus showing production of functional let-7 in S2 cell lysates. Cytoplasmic lysates from Dicer-1- or Loqs-depleted cells were then subjected to the pre-let-7 processing assay. Both Dicer-1 and Loqs depletion led to reductions of mature let-7 compared with controls (Figure 4C), showing that both Dicer-1 and Loqs function in pre-miRNA processing.

###xml 867 875 867 875 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 976 984 976 984 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 1268 1275 1268 1275 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">miR-ban</italic>
###xml 1285 1292 1285 1292 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">miR-ban</italic>
###xml 1294 1302 1294 1302 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 1394 1396 1394 1396 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b64">64</xref>
###xml 1495 1497 1495 1497 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b65">65</xref>
###xml 1634 1642 1634 1642 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g005">Figure 5</xref>
We next used pre-miR-ban as a substrate for pre-miRNA processing assays. It was shown recently that S2 cell extracts contained pri-miRNA processing activity that cleaved pri-miRNA into an approximately 60- to 70-bp pre-miRNA precursor [49]. This processing is known to occur in the nucleus; thus pre-miR-ban was prepared by in vitro processing of pri-miR-ban incubated with S2 nuclear extracts (Figure 5A). Uniformly labeled pre-miR-ban was then gel-purified and used as a substrate for analysis of pre-miRNA processing. Incubation of the pre-miRNA with S2 cytoplasmic extracts resulted in the appearance of a mature 21-nucleotide miR-ban (Figure 5B). We then examined the requirement of Dicer-1 and Loqs in pre-miR-ban processing. Incubation of pre-miRNA with Dicer-1- and Loqs-depleted S2 cytoplasmic extracts resulted in a marked reduction in mature miRNA levels (Figure 5B). In contrast, depletion of Dicer-2 or R2D2 showed no measurable reduction of mature miRNA levels (Figure 5B). We then assayed the pre-miRNA processing activity of the purified complexes (both Flag-Dicer-1 and Flag-Loqs complexes). That the Flag-Loqs complex contains Dicer-1 was confirmed by immunoblotting (data not shown). Both Dicer-1 and Loqs complexes were capable of generating maturemiR-ban from pre-miR-ban (Figure 5C). Several steps in the RNAi and miRNA pathways are known to require a divalent metal ion [64]. In addition, it is well known that RNase III-type enzymes require divalent metals for cleavage [65]. Flag-Dicer-1 complex was employed and the processing was performed in the presence of magnesium ions or EDTA in a buffer. As shown in Figure 5D, no pre-miRNA processing activity was detected at 10 mM EDTA. These results demonstrated that the Dicer-1-Loqs complex converts pre-miRNAs into mature miRNAs in a divalent metal ion-dependent manner.

###begin title 20
Loqs Stimulates and Confers upon Dicer-1 the Specific Processing of Pre-miRNAs
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 190 198 190 198 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 333 341 333 341 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g003">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 482 490 482 490 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 627 635 627 635 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 714 722 714 722 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 984 992 984 992 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 1092 1094 1092 1094 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b58">58</xref>
###xml 1145 1153 1145 1153 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 1275 1283 1275 1283 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 1752 1760 1752 1760 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g006">Figure 6</xref>
To further examine the requirement for Loqs in pre-miRNA processing, we purified Flag-Dicer-1 complex under a harsher condition (high salt), where Dicer-1 was stripped of most Loqs protein (Figure 6A), and used this Dicer-1 complex in pre-miRNA processing assays with or without supplement of recombinant GST-Loqs (see left panel in Figure 3C). Without any supplement, the Flag-Dicer-1 complex purified under the harsh condition showed less activity than that under mild condition (Figure 6B). Then we added GST-Loqs in the assay mixture. The addition of GST-Loqs to the Dicer-1 complex stimulated the processing of pre-miRNA (Figure 6C). GST-Loqs alone did not show any significant pre-miRNA processing activity (Figure 6C). These results show that Loqs is required for stimulating the processing of pre-miRNAs. Interestingly, we found that the Dicer-1 complex purified under the harsh condition displayed considerable siRNA-generating activity on the long dsRNA substrate in vitro (Figure 6D), although previous genetic studies have shown that Dicer-1 is not required for siRNA production [58]. The addition of GST-Loqs inhibited this effect (Figure 6D). Western blot analysis failed to show that the Dicer-1 complex used in this experiment contains Dicer-2 (right panel inFigure 6D). GST-Loqs alone showed no activity for generating siRNAs from long dsRNAs. These results suggested that Dicer-1 stripped of much of its bound Loqs processes both dsRNA and pre-miRNA substrates, but re-addition of recombinant Loqs suppresses dsRNA processing activity and enhances pre-miRNA processing activity. Our findings thus imply that much of the apparent substrate specificity of Dicer-1 in vivo results from its association with Loqs. Although very unlikely (Figure 6D), it is, however, formally possible that the Dicer-1 immunoprecipitates may contain very small amounts of Dicer-2 protein that can catalyze long dsRNA cleavage, and that addition of a large amount of dsRBD-containing Loqs may block the activity of Dicer-2 in this experiment.

We examined the presence of endogenous miRNA in RNA preparations from Flag-Dicer-1 and Flag-Loqs complexes obtained from S2 cells using anti-Flag antibodies. Complexes were prepared as in Figure 3A, and RNA preparations from each complex were subjected to Northern blotting using an oligo probe recognizing both pre-miR-ban and mature miR-ban. The Dicer-1 complex contained both the pre- and mature form of miR-ban, and the complex seems to preferentially bind the precursor form of miR-ban (Figure 7A). In contrast, the precursor form of miR-ban was barely detectable in the Loqs complex, though it contained mature miR-ban. ###begin title 24
An AGO1-Associated Complex Contains Dicer-1 and Loqs, and Is Capable of Pre-miRNA Processing
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 133 135 133 135 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b60">60</xref>
###xml 654 662 654 662 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 1007 1015 1007 1015 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g005">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 1021 1028 1021 1028 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">miR-ban</italic>
###xml 1111 1118 1111 1118 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">miR-ban</italic>
###xml 1141 1149 1141 1149 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 1346 1348 1346 1348 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b60">60</xref>
###xml 1633 1635 1633 1635 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b60">60</xref>
We have previously shown that Argonaute protein AGO1 is required for stable production of mature miRNAs and associates with Dicer-1 [60]. Thus, we sought to ascertain if Loqs was also present in an AGO1-associated complex, and if so, if the AGO1 complex was capable of processing pre-miRNA in vitro. We simultaneously expressed Flag-Loqs and AGO1 tagged with TAP in S2 cells and purified the AGO1-TAP complex through immunoglobulin G (IgG) bead-binding. The IgG bound was then subjected to Western blot analysis using anti-Dicer-1, anti-AGO1, or anti-Flag (for Loqs detection) antibodies. Not only Dicer-1 but also Loqs was detected in the AGO1 complex (Figure 7B). These results indicated that all three proteins are present in the same complex, although they cannot exclude the possibility that there is one complex that contains AGO1 and Dicer-1 but not Loqs, and another complex that contains AGO1 and Loqs but not Dicer-1. The pre-miRNA processing activity of the AGO1 complex was then examined. As in Figure 5, pre-miR-ban was utilized as a substrate. The AGO1 complex was able to efficiently process pre-miR-ban into the mature form (Figure 7C). In contrast, another Argonaute protein AGO2-associated complex showed no such activity, which is consistent with our previous finding that the AGO2-associated complex does not contain Dicer-1 [60]. Considered together, these results showed that Dicer-1 and Loqs form a functional complex that mediates the genesis of mature miRNAs from pre-miRNAs, and suggested that the resultant mature miRNAs are loaded onto an AGO1-associated complex, which probably is miRNA-associated RISC [60], through specific interaction of AGO1 with Dicer-1 and Loqs.

###begin title 26
Discussion
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 220 228 220 228 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 409 411 409 411 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b59">59</xref>
###xml 835 837 835 837 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b59">59</xref>
###xml 838 840 838 840 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b66">66</xref>
###xml 1156 1165 1156 1165 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g004">Figures 4</xref>
###xml 1171 1172 1171 1172 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g005">5</xref>
###xml 1255 1263 1255 1263 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g002">Figure 2</xref>
Our results indicate that Loqs and Dicer-1 form a complex that converts pre-miRNAs into mature miRNAs; so how do they act together in pre-miRNA processing? Sequence comparison reveals that Loqs is a paralog of R2D2 (see Figure 1). Therefore, Loqs may play the molecular role of R2D2 for Dicer-1. R2D2 forms a stable heterodimeric complex with Dicer-2, while either protein alone seems to be unstable in vivo [59]. In the absence of R2D2, Dicer-2 is still capable of efficiently processing long dsRNA into siRNAs. Therefore, the siRNA generating activity of Dicer-2 is not dependent upon R2D2. However, the resultant siRNAs are not effectively channeled into RISC in the absence of R2D2. The Dicer-2-R2D2 complex, but not Dicer-2 alone, binds to siRNA, which indicates that siRNA binding by the heterodimer is important for RISC entry [59,66]. In the case of Loqs, this protein alone is not capable of converting pre-miRNAs into mature miRNAs, but it clearly stimulates and directs the specific pre-miRNA processing activity of Dicer-1. Furthermore, knocking down Loqs markedly reduced the pre-miRNA processing activity in cytoplasmic lysates in vitro (see Figures 4C and 5B), but did not cause a significant reduction of the level of Dicer-1 protein (see Figure 2B); implying that Dicer-1 may largely depend on Loqs for its pre-miRNA processing activity. Thus, the molecular role of Loqs for Dicer-1 is not simply similar to that of R2D2 for Dicer-2.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 235 243 235 243 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g006">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 249 250 249 250 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g006">6</xref>
###xml 731 733 731 733 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b67">67</xref>
###xml 962 964 962 964 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b50">50</xref>
###xml 965 967 965 967 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b68">68</xref>
It can be envisioned that Loqs may have one of several roles in pre-miRNA processing. Dicer-1 contains only one dsRBD, which may not be sufficient for strong interaction with and/or specific recognition of the pre-miRNA substrate (see Figure 6C and 6D). Loqs, containing three dsRBDs with no other identifiable domains being apparent, could provide the additional RNA-binding modules required for specific recognition of the pre-miRNA, and thereby stabilize pre-miRNA binding for Dicer-1. Loqs could also organize binding of Dicer-1 on the pre-miRNA, contributing to the specific positioning of the Dicer-1 cleavage site. Alternatively, since dsRBDs are known to not only bind dsRNAs but also mediate protein-protein interactions [67], Loqs may directly bind Dicer-1 through its dsRBDs. This protein-protein interaction may trigger a conformational change of Dicer-1 that facilitates either the formation of an intramolecular dimer of its two RNase III domains [50,68], which creates a pair of catalytic sites, or the handover of the Dicer-1 cleaved mature miRNAs to the RISC.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 98 100 98 100 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b69">69</xref>
###xml 142 144 142 144 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b70">70</xref>
###xml 237 245 237 245 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0030235-g008">Figure 8</xref>
###xml 381 386 381 386 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b71">71&#8211;73</xref>
###xml 458 460 458 460 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b67">67</xref>
###xml 749 751 741 743 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b74">74</xref>
###xml 921 931 913 923 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 106 109 <span type="species:ncbi:12721?0.9873544761140104">HIV</span>
Sequence analysis revealed that protein activator of protein kinase dsRNA dependent (PKR) (PACT) [69] and HIV TAR RNA binding protein (TRBP) [70] in mammals bear 34% identity to Loqs, and share a highly similar domain structure with it (Figure 8). Both PACT and TRBP are thought to play a role in the regulation of translation through modulating PKR that also contains two dsRBDs [71-73]. PACT interacts with PKR and enhances the autophosphorylation of PKR [67], which in turn, phosphorylates the alpha subunit of eukaryotic translation initiation factor 2 (eIF2alpha) and leads to an inhibition of mRNA translation in response to viral infection and other stimuli. TRBP prevents PKR-mediated inhibition of protein synthesis through binding to PKR [74]. Considered together, it will be important to find out Loqs' partners other than Dicer-1 for possible involvement of Loqs in miRNA-mediated translational regulation in Drosophila.

RNAi

###begin p 32
###xml 72 74 72 74 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0030235-b75">75</xref>

dsRNAs were introduced to S2 cells by soaking essentially as described [75]. Briefly, approximately 5 x 106 cells were soaked in 1 ml of serum-free medium containing 15 mug of dsRNA for 30 min at room temperature followed by addition of 2 ml of the medium containing 15% serum, 3 mM glutamine, and penicillin-streptomycin. Northern blot analysis

Immunofluorescence analysis

After incubation with TNT products and extensive washing, the bound proteins were separated by SDS-PAGE. RNaseA treatment was carried out by adding the enzyme to the binding mixture. ###begin title 43
Preparation of nuclear and cytoplasmic lysate for in vitro processing assays
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 47 48 47 48 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
S2 cells were suspended at approximately 1 x 108 cells/ml into Hypotonic buffer (Buffer A without KOAc) and lysed by passing through a 30G needle. After centrifugation at 500 x g for 20 min, the supernatant and the precipitate were separated. The supernatant was centrifuged to obtain the supernatant as a cytoplasmic lysate. The pellet was washed twice with Hypotonic buffer and lysed by sonication in Buffer A containing 100 mM KOAc and 20% glycerol, followed by centrifugation to obtain the supernatant as a nuclear lysate. Total protein concentration in each lysate was determined with Protein assay (Bio-Rad, Hercules, California, United States) and adjusted to be equal.

###begin p 46
###xml 26 33 26 33 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">miR-ban</italic>

Briefly, in 1 ml reaction, 10 mM creatine phosphate, 0.5 mM ATP, 30 mug/ml creatine kinase, 0.1 U/ul RNasin, 0.1 mug yeast RNA, and 500 mul nuclear lysate were added, and pri-miR-bantam in 0.5x Buffer A with 100 mM KOAc was further added to the mixture.
