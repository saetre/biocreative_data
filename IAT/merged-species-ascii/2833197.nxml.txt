###begin article-title 0
Transcriptional Regulation of VEGF-A by the Unfolded Protein Response Pathway
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: RG KLL DR FU. Performed the experiments: RG KLL KES FU. Analyzed the data: RG KLL KES DR FU. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: AMM JH DR FU. Wrote the paper: RG KLL FU.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
Angiogenesis is crucial to many physiological and pathological processes including development and cancer cell survival. Vascular endothelial growth factor-A (VEGFA) is the predominant mediator of angiogenesis in the VEGF family. During development, adverse environmental conditions like nutrient deprivation, hypoxia and increased protein secretion occur. IRE1alpha, PERK, and ATF6alpha, master regulators of the unfolded protein response (UPR), are activated under these conditions and are proposed to have a role in mediating angiogenesis.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 136 139 124 127 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 127 139 119 127 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Ire1&#945;<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 148 151 136 139 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 144 151 132 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Perk<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 340 352 324 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Ire1&#945;&#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 499 502 471 474 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 490 502 466 474 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Ire1&#945;<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 511 514 483 486 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 507 514 479 486 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Perk<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 152 157 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 353 357 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Here we show that IRE1alpha, PERK, and ATF6alpha powerfully regulate VEGFA mRNA expression under various stress conditions. In Ire1alpha-/- and Perk-/- mouse embryonic fibroblasts and ATF6alpha-knockdown HepG2 cells, induction of VEGFA mRNA by endoplasmic reticulum stress is attenuated as compared to control cells. Embryonic lethality of Ire1alpha-/- mice is due to the lack of VEGFA induction in labyrinthine trophoblast cells of the developing placenta. Rescue of IRE1alpha and PERK in Ire1alpha-/- and Perk-/- cells respectively, prevents VEGFA mRNA attenuation. We further report that the induction of VEGFA by IRE1alpha, PERK and ATF6 involves activation of transcription factors, spliced-XBP-1, ATF4 and cleaved ATF6 respectively.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Our results reveal that the IRE1alpha-XBP-1, PERK-ATF4, and ATF6alpha pathways constitute novel upstream regulatory pathways of angiogenesis by modulating VEGF transcription. Activation of these pathways helps the rapidly growing cells to obtain sufficient nutrients and growth factors for their survival under the prevailing hostile environmental conditions. These results establish an important role of the UPR in angiogenesis.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 783 786 783 786 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Ron1">[1]</xref>
###xml 788 791 788 791 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Rutkowski1">[2]</xref>
###xml 1777 1780 1777 1780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Ron1">[1]</xref>
Productive folding of secretory proteins in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) is essential to ensure normal cell function. In order for secretory proteins to fold properly, ER homeostasis must be maintained. ER homeostasis is defined by the dynamic balance between the ER protein load and the ER capacity to process this load. ER homeostasis can be perturbed by pathological processes such as hypoxia, glucose deprivation, viral infections, environmental toxins, inflammatory cytokines, and mutant protein expression, as well as by physiological processes such as aging. Disruption of ER homeostasis causes accumulation of unfolded and misfolded proteins in the ER. This condition is referred to as ER stress. Cells cope with ER stress by activating the Unfolded Protein Response (UPR) [1], [2]. The UPR is initiated by three ER transmembrane proteins: Inositol Requiring 1 (IRE1), PKR-like ER kinase (PERK), and Activating Transcription Factor 6 (ATF6). These three master regulators sense and interpret protein folding conditions in the ER and translate this information across the ER membrane to regulate downstream effectors. These effectors have two distinct outputs, homeostatic and apoptotic. Homeostatic outputs are adaptive responses that function to attenuate ER stress and restore ER homeostasis. These responses include the attenuation of protein translation to reduce ER workload and prevent further accumulation of unfolded proteins, upregulation of molecular chaperones and protein processing enzymes to enhance the ER folding activity, and the increase in ER-associated degradation (ERAD) components to promote clearance of unfolded proteins. When ER stress reaches a point where the cells cannot tolerate the load of unfolded proteins any more, apoptosis sets in [1].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 374 377 374 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-RomeroRamirez1">[3]</xref>
###xml 379 382 379 382 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Koumenis1">[4]</xref>
###xml 384 387 384 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Drogat1">[5]</xref>
###xml 451 454 451 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Rossant1">[6]</xref>
###xml 456 459 456 459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Adelman1">[7]</xref>
###xml 461 464 461 464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Genbacev1">[8]</xref>
###xml 651 654 651 654 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Shweiki1">[9]</xref>
###xml 656 660 656 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Shweiki2">[10]</xref>
###xml 662 666 662 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Stein1">[11]</xref>
Recent studies have indicated that cells suffering from insufficient blood supplies experience ER stress. The ER needs energy and oxygen for the folding process, thus nutrient deprivation (low ATP production) and hypoxia caused by insufficient blood supply leads to inefficient protein folding and ER stress in cells, especially in cancer cells that grow and spread rapidly [3], [4], [5]. This condition also occurs in the development of the placenta [6], [7], [8]. Both nutrient deprivation and hypoxia stimulate the production of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and other angiogenic factors, leading to protection against ischaemic injury [9], [10], [11]. Here we report that the three master regulators of the UPR, IRE1alpha, PERK and ATF6alpha, mediate transcriptional regulation of VEGFA under ER stress which occurs during normal development of labyrinthine trophoblast cells in the placenta as well as in cancer cells.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
Results
###end title 11
###begin title 12
VEGFA Expression Is Increased by ER Stress
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 238 242 238 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Abcouwer1">[12]</xref>
###xml 411 420 411 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g001">Figure 1A</xref>
###xml 454 463 454 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g001">Figure 1B</xref>
###xml 506 515 506 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g001">Figure 1C</xref>
###xml 159 164 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Vascular endothelial growth factor isoform A (VEGFA) is the most abundant variant of the VEGF family. Previous studies have shown that VEGFA is upregulated in human retinal ARPE-1 cells when treated with tunicamycin, an ER stress inducer [12]. To determine whether VEGFA expression is increased by other ER stress inducers in other cell types including cancer cells, we treated prostate cancer cell line, PC-3 (Figure 1A), liver cancer cell line, HepG2 (Figure 1B), and the insulinoma cells, INS-1 832/13 (Figure 1C), with two ER stress inducers, thapsigargin and tunicamycin for 4 hr. We found that VEGFA mRNA expression was increased 2-5 fold by ER stress. We confirmed that these cells were under ER stress by measuring expression levels of typical ER stress markers, spliced XBP-1 and BiP.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
VEGFA mRNA expression is induced by ER stress.
###end title 14

###begin title 16
HIF1alpha Is Not Involved in ER Stress-Mediated VEGFA Induction
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 70 74 66 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Forsythe1">[13]</xref>
###xml 379 388 363 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g002">Figure 2A</xref>
###xml 775 784 743 752 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g002">Figure 2B</xref>
HIF1alpha is a major regulator of VEGFA mRNA expression under hypoxia [13]. To test if HIF1alpha is also involved in ER stress-mediated VEGF mRNA induction, we treated HepG2 cells with either the ER stress inducer thapsigargin or hypoxia, and then measured HIF1alpha protein expression. HIF1alpha protein expression by thapsigargin treatment was much lower than that by hypoxia (Figure 2A), raising the possibility that HIF1alpha is not involved in ER stress-mediated VEGFA induction. To test this possibility, we knocked down HIF1alpha expression using siRNA directed against HIF1alpha in HepG2 cells, stimulated them with thapsigargin, and then measure VEGFA induction. As we predicted, siRNA-mediated knockdown of HIF1alpha did not affect VEGFA induction by thapsigargin (Figure 2B). These results strongly suggest that HIF1alpha is not a major regulator of VEGFA mRNA expression under ER stress conditions.

###xml 1775 1784 1707 1716 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g003">Figure 3E</xref>
###xml 1897 1906 1825 1834 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g003">Figure 3F</xref>
###xml 489 494 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1493 1498 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1542 1547 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1695 1700 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
IRE1alpha, a key regulator of the UPR, is involved in transcriptional regulation of genes upregulated by ER stress. It has been shown that IRE1alpha signaling is involved in VEGFA mRNA expression by glucose deprivation and hypoxia [5]. Glucose deprivation is known to activate the UPR. We were therefore interested in determining whether IRE1alpha was required for VEGFA mRNA expression under ER stress conditions. VEGFA mRNA expression levels were significantly decreased in Ire1alpha-/- mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs) as compared to those in control wild-type MEFs under various ER stress conditions induced by thapsigargin (Figure 3A), tunicamycin (Figure 3B), and hypoxia (0.5% O2) (Figure 3C). ER stress during hypoxia was confirmed in wild-type MEFs by measuring upregulation of spliced Xbp-1 which was absent in Ire1alpha-/- cells (Figure 3C, right panel). It has been shown that IRE1alpha is involved in the degradation of mRNAs encoding secretory proteins [14], [15][16][17]. To determine whether IRE1alpha is involved in VEGFA mRNA stability under ER stress conditions, we used actinomycin D to attenuate mRNA transcription, then challenged Ire1alpha-/- and control cells with thapsigargin to induce VEGFA mRNA degradation. We found that IRE1alpha was not involved in ER stress-mediated VEGFA mRNA decay (Figure S1). To further confirm the relationship between IRE1alpha signaling and VEGFA mRNA induction, we established Ire1alpha-/- cells transduced with lentivirus expressing human IRE1alpha. Ire1alpha-/- cells rescued with human IRE1alpha could activate XBP-1 splicing, indicating that ectopically expressed IRE1alpha was functional (Figure 3D). In Ire1alpha-/- cells rescued human IRE1alpha, VEGFA mRNA expression was restored under ER stress conditions (Figure 3E). IRE1alpha-mediated induction of VEGFA mRNA also enhanced VEGFA protein production as well as VEGFA secretion (Figure 3F).

IRE1alpha mediates XBP-1 mRNA splicing, which leads to production of an active transcription factor XBP-1-p, raising the possibility that XBP-1-p was involved in the regulation of VEGFA mRNA expression under ER stress conditions. To test this idea, we measured expression levels of VEGFA in Ire1alpha-/- MEFs transfected with mouse processed-XBP1 plasmid. Ectopic expression of XBP-1-p restored VEGFA induction in Ire1alpha-/- MEFs under ER stress conditions (Figure 3G, left panel). Although expression levels of VEGFA and spliced XBP-1 were lower in Ire1alpha-/- MEFs ectopically expressing XBP-1p and IRE1alpha as compared to control wild-type MEFs, the degree of VEGFA mRNA induction was strongly correlated with an increase in spliced Xbp1 mRNA expression (Figure 3G), suggesting that XBP-1-p-mediated induction of VEGFA is dose-dependent. To further confirm this idea, we compared expression levels of VEGFA in HepG2 cells transfected with siRNA directed against IRE1alpha, XBP-1, or control scramble siRNA under ER stress induced by thapsigargin. As we predicted, VEGFA mRNA induction was attenuated in the cells transfected with IRE1alpha siRNA and Xbp1 siRNA as compared to the cells transfected with control scramble siRNA (Figure 3H). Suppression of IRE1 and XBP1 signaling was confirmed by the downregulation of EDEM, which is a known downstream effector of XBP-1 [18].

The VEGFA promoter region contains one putative XBP-1-p binding site, the ACGT core [19], [20], 972 bp upstream of the mouse VEGFA start site, raising the possibility that XBP-1-p binds to VEGFA promoter. To test this idea, we derived a reporter construct by cloning 1 kb upstream sequences of the mouse VEGFA transcription start site upstream of the luciferase gene. Ectopic XBP-1-p expression increased activity of VEGFA promoter-luciferase (Figure 3I). In order to determine direct interaction of XBP1 with VEGFA, we did a chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) analysis of the mouse VEGFA promoter. Mouse neuro2A cells either mock transfected or transfected with XBP-1-p, were treated with thapsigargin. ChIP analysis indicates that XBP-1-p was recruited to the mouse VEGFA promoter under ER stress conditions in vivo (Figure 3J). XBP-1-p overexpression improves binding efficiency which is further increased by ER stress thus supporting the idea of dose-dependent upregulation of VEGFA transcription under ER stress. Collectively, the results of Figure 3 show that VEGFA is a direct target of the IRE1alpha-XBP-1 pathway.

Our group and others have previously reported that Ire1alpha-/- mice are embryonic lethal by embryonic day 12.5 [21], [22], [23]. It has been reported that the embryonic lethality of Ire1alpha-/- mice is due to the defect in the development of the labyrinthine trophoblast cells which are critical for integration and exchange between fetal and maternal blood vessels in the placenta [23]. It has previously been shown that loss of a single Vegfa allele in mice can result in embryonic lethality by embryonic day 11.5 due to a defect in the development of labyrinthine trophoblast cells [24]. We therefore considered the possibility that IRE1alpha has a role in the survival of labyrinthine trophoblast cells through the activation of VEGFA expression. As reported previously, we found that Ire1alpha-/- placentas failed to develop proper labyrinthine trophoblast on embryonic day 10.5 (Figure 4A). Because of the progressive loss of blood vessels in the labyrinthine layer, Ire1alpha-/-embryos were comparatively smaller in size than wild-type embryos and did not survive more than embryonic day 12.5 (Figure 4B and Table 1). To confirm the role of IRE1alpha signaling in mouse labyrinthine trophoblast cells, we also analyzed the mouse labyrinthine trophoblast cell line, SM10 [25]. These cells responded robustly to ER stress inducers, tunicamycin and thapsigargin as well as 2-deoxy glucose which causes glucose deprivation, as indicated by upregulation of spliced Xbp1 and Chop protein expression levels (Figure 4C, left panel). VEGFA mRNA was also induced significantly upon ER stress (Figure 4C, right panel). During embryonic development, HIF1alpha is activated in the growing placenta [8]. To confirm that HIF1alpha was not involved in ER stress-mediated VEGFA mRNA induction in SM10 cells, we knocked down HIF1alpha expression by siRNA and measured VEGFA mRNA expression. As expected, RNAi-mediated knockdown of HIF1alpha did not affect VEGFA mRNA induction by thapsigargin treatment in SM10 cells (Figure 4D).

We next sought to verify that ER stress-mediated VEGFA mRNA induction required IRE1alpha in SM10 cells. ###begin title 33

###end title 33
###begin p 34

Previous studies have shown ATF4 translation is dependent on PERK and suggested that ATF4 plays a role in VEGF mRNA expression under oxidative stress [28], [29]. These considerations prompted us to investigate the role of ATF4 in VEGFA mRNA expression under ER stress conditions. We measured VEGFA mRNA expression in Atf4-/- MEFs and wild-type MEFs. ###begin p 39
###xml 332 347 332 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g005">Figure 5F and G</xref>
To further confirm the role of the PERK-ATF4 pathway in VEGFA mRNA induction, we transfected HepG2 cells with siRNAs directed against PERK and ATF4 and then measure VEGFA mRNA expression under ER stress conditions. ###begin p 40
###xml 148 152 148 152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Roybal1">[28]</xref>

ATF4 has been previously shown to interact with first intron of the human VEGFA gene under oxidative stress conditions caused by arsenite treatment [28]. ATF4 interacts with an amino-acid response element (AARE) present in the intron. We therefore considered the possibility that ATF4 binds to the first intron of VEGFA gene under ER stress conditions.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 221 230 221 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g005">Figure 5H</xref>
###xml 524 533 524 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g005">Figure 5I</xref>
###xml 84 89 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 391 396 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
To test this idea, a reporter plasmid carrying a 3.3 Kb fragment of intron 1 of the mouse VEGFA gene was cloned into pGL3 luciferase vector. Ectopic ATF4 expression, but not XBP-1, increased the activity of the reporter (Figure 5H). ChIP analysis was performed on neuro2A cells in order to confirm the interaction. The ChIP PCR product comprised of 109 bp starting from +1581 of the 2891 bp mouse VEGFA intron1.The ChIP experiments revealed that following addition of thapsigargin, ATF4 was recruited to the VEGFA intron 1 (Figure 5I). Collectively, these results indicate that the PERK-ATF4 pathway directly regulates VEGFA mRNA expression through intron 1 of VEGFA gene under ER stress condtions.

###begin title 42
ATF6alpha Also Plays a Role in VEGFA mRNA Upregulation under ER Stress
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 280 284 272 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Schroder1">[30]</xref>
###xml 335 339 323 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Yoshida1">[31]</xref>
###xml 607 616 587 596 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g006">Figure 6A</xref>
###xml 890 899 858 867 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g006">Figure 6B</xref>
###xml 933 942 901 910 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g006">Figure 6C</xref>
ATF6alpha, an ER transmembrane protein, is another upstream component of the UPR. Under ER stress conditions ATF6alpha translocates from ER to the Golgi where its cytoplasmic domain is cleaved from the membrane to produce a potent transcription factor regulating UPR target genes [30]. XBP-1 is one of the targets of cleaved-ATF6alpha [31]. Hence, we tested the possibility if ATF6alpha was involved in VEGFA mRNA expression under ER stress conditions. As predicted, RNAi-mediated knockdown of ATF6alpha in HepG2 cells resulted in a significant decrease of VEGFA mRNA expression under ER stress conditions (Figure 6A). To further establish a relationship between ATF6alpha and VEGFA mRNA induction, we tested if ATF6alpha could increase the activity of gene regulatory regions of VEGFA gene. Ectopic expression of cleaved active ATF6alpha increased the activity of VEGFA-promoter reporter (Figure 6B), but not VEGFA-intron reporter (Figure 6C). Collectively, these results strongly suggest that ATF6alpha also plays role in VEGFA mRNA induction by binding its promoter under ER stress conditions.
###end p 43

###begin title 46
Discussion
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 353 361 345 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0009575-g007">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 538 542 530 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Forsythe1">[13]</xref>
Our results indicate that the UPR is activated not only by ER stress but also deprivation of nutrients and oxygen, and robustly regulates VEGFA mRNA expression. The regulation of VEGFA by ER stress is mediated by UPR master regulators, IRE1alpha, PERK and ATF6alpha via their respective transcription factors, XBP-1, ATF4 and cleaved-ATF6 respectively (Figure 7). Our results show that VEGFA induction by ER stress is independent of the hypoxia inducible factor 1 (HIF-1) pathway, a major signaling pathway for VEGFA induction by hypoxia [13] or there is a cross-talk between the XBP-1, ATF4, ATF6alpha, and HIF-1 pathways. The robust induction of VEGFA by IRE1alpha signaling is also important for the development and survival of labyrinthine trophoblast cells in the placenta.
###end p 47
###begin title 48
VEGFA expression is regulated by ER stress.
###end title 48
###begin p 49
ER stress signaling regulates VEGFA expression via its principal regulators Ire1alpha, Perk and ATF6alpha. Proposed pathway shows how ER stress signaling might affect growth and survival of both developing placenta as well as tumor through angiogenesis. This is accomplished by activating VEGFA transcription via different regulatory regions on the VEGFA gene.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 96 100 96 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Ferrara2">[32]</xref>
###xml 247 251 247 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Ferrara1">[24]</xref>
###xml 253 257 253 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Rinkenberger1">[33]</xref>
###xml 259 263 259 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Rossant2">[34]</xref>
###xml 930 934 930 934 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Rossant2">[34]</xref>
###xml 1046 1049 1046 1049 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Rossant1">[6]</xref>
###xml 1051 1055 1051 1055 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Rinkenberger1">[33]</xref>
###xml 1293 1296 1289 1292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Rossant1">[6]</xref>
###xml 1298 1301 1294 1297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Adelman1">[7]</xref>
###xml 1303 1306 1299 1302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Genbacev1">[8]</xref>
###xml 474 478 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
VEGFA is the major vascular endothelial growth factor and plays prominent roles in angiogenesis [32]. Angiogenesis is crucial to many physiological and pathological processes including development of labyrinthine trophoblast cells in the placenta [24], [33], [34]. Mammalian placenta is a vital secretory organ characterized by secretion of large amounts of diverse proteins like placental lactogens, proliferins and other growth factors required for the growing embryo. In mice, the placenta is formed by fusion of the embryo-derived allantois (i.e., umbilical cord) and the extra-embryonic chorion which derives from the polar trophectoderm overlying the embryo. Chorioallantoic attachment at embryonic day 8.5 causes chorionic trophoblast to differentiate into various layers of labyrinthine trophoblast cells. These labyrinthine trophoblast cells undergo extensive villus branching to form the labyrinth by embryonic day 10.5 [34]. The formation of this layer is critical for integration and exchange between fetal and maternal blood vessels [6], [33]. It has been reported that hypoxia and nutrient deprivation, which have been shown to activate both IRE1alpha and PERK in this study, are involved in various stages of placental development, one of them being trophoblast differentiation [6], [7], [8]. Our results suggest that the generation of a fully functional labyrinthine trophoblast requires a coordinated action of downstream effectors regulated by IRE1alpha including VEGFA which is independent of HIF1alpha signaling.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 159 162 155 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-RomeroRamirez1">[3]</xref>
###xml 164 167 160 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Drogat1">[5]</xref>
###xml 248 252 244 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0009575-Blais1">[35]</xref>
Angiogenesis is crucial for sustaining tumors. IRE1alpha signaling is known to be essential for angiogenesis and growth of tumor cells through VEGFA induction [3], [5]. PERK signaling also has been shown to be important for survival of tumor cells [35]. Our data show that the IRE1alpha-XBP-1, PERK-ATF4, and ATF6alpha pathways can independently regulate VEGFA mRNA expression under ER stress conditions via different regulatory regions of the VEGFA gene. Our results show that both VEGFA promoter as well as the intron is engaged simultaneously by UPR effectors under ER stress conditions. This tells us that coordinated activation of all three regulators is essential for proper upregulation of VEGFA mRNA expression. Inactivation or knockdown of any of these three master regulators impairs VEGFA upregulation under ER stress.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
Many downstream target genes of the UPR are co-regulated by IRE1alpha, PERK, and ATF6alpha, ensuring the redundancy and robustness of this adaptive response. However, this biological design that mediates robustness also has drawbacks. In cancer cells, VEGFA induction mediated by the UPR is used to maintain abnormal ER homeostasis and help these cells to survive and proliferate under chronic ER stress conditions caused by nutrient deprivation and hypoxia. Thus, inhibiting IRE1alpha, PERK, and ATF6alpha is a promising novel therapeutic modality for cancer treatment.
###end p 52

Total RNA was isolated from the cells by using the RNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen, Valencia, CA). 1 microg of total RNA from cells was reverse transcribed with Oligo-dT primer (Promega, Madison, WI). For the thermal cycle reaction, the iQ5 system (BioRad, Hercules, CA) was used at 95degreesC for 10 min, then 40 cycles at 95degreesC for 10 sec, and at 55degreesC for 30 sec. The relative amount for each transcript was calculated by a standard curve of cycle thresholds for serial dilutions of cDNA sample and normalized to the amount of the house-keeping beta-actin levels. The polymerase chain reaction (PCR) was performed in triplicate for each sample; all experiments were repeated three times. Power SYBR Green PCR Master Mix (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA) was used for the quantitative PCR. PCR primer sequences are listed in Table S1.

###begin title 66
mRNA Stability Assay
###end title 66
###begin p 67
Cellular mRNA transcription was attenuated by treating cells with 5 microg/ml Actinomycin D (Sigma A-4262) for 1 hr. followed by treatment with thapsigargin (0.2 microM) for different times. Total RNA was collected and quantitative PCR method described above was employed to measure levels of VEGFA gene transcripts. Time point zero for each condition was standardized to 1 and the subsequent rate of degradation of mRNA was measured.
