###begin article-title 0
Meltrin beta/ADAM19 Interacting with EphA4 in Developing Neural Cells Participates in Formation of the Neuromuscular Junction
###end article-title 0

###begin title 3
Background
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 337 340 337 340 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">A D</underline>
###xml 351 352 351 352 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">A</underline>
###xml 355 356 355 356 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M</underline>
Development of the neuromuscular junction (NMJ) is initiated by the formation of postsynaptic specializations in the central zones of muscles, followed by the arrival of motor nerve terminals opposite the postsynaptic regions. The post- and presynaptic components are then stabilized and modified to form mature synapses. Roles of ADAM (A Disintegrin And Metalloprotease) family proteins in the formation of the NMJ have not been reported previously.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Principal Findings
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 223 235 216 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;</italic>
###xml 355 364 345 354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ephrin-A5</italic>
###xml 424 436 414 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;</italic>
###xml 535 539 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We report here that Meltrin beta, ADAM19, participates in the formation of the NMJ. The zone of acetylcholine receptor alpha mRNA distribution was broader and excess sprouting of motor nerve terminals was more prominent in meltrin beta-deficient than in wild-type embryonic diaphragms. A microarray analysis revealed that the preferential distribution of ephrin-A5 mRNA in the synaptic region of muscles was aberrant in the meltrin beta-deficient muscles. Excess sprouting of motor nerve terminals was also found in ephrin-A5 knockout mice, which lead us to investigate a possible link between Meltrin beta and ephrin-A5-Eph signaling in the development of the NMJ. Meltrin beta and EphA4 interacted with each other in developing motor neurons, and both of these proteins localized in the NMJ. Coexpression of Meltrin beta and EphA4 strongly blocked vesicular internalization of ephrin-A5-EphA4 complexes without requiring the protease activity of Meltrin beta, suggesting a regulatory role of Meltrin beta in ephrin-A5-Eph signaling.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Conclusion
###end title 7
###begin p 8
Meltrin beta plays a regulatory role in formation of the NMJ. The endocytosis of ephrin-Eph complexes is required for efficient contact-dependent repulsion between ephrin and Eph. We propose that Meltrin beta stabilizes the interaction between ephrin-A5 and EphA4 by regulating endocytosis of the ephrinA5-EphA complex negatively, which would contribute to the fine-tuning of the NMJ during development.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Introduction
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 244 247 244 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Burden1">[1]</xref>
###xml 248 251 248 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Sanes1">[3]</xref>
###xml 520 523 520 523 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-FlanaganSteet1">[4]</xref>
###xml 524 527 524 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Yang2">[6]</xref>
###xml 667 670 667 670 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Kim1">[7]</xref>
###xml 671 674 671 674 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Okada1">[9]</xref>
A neuromuscular junction (NMJ) is the synapse between an axon terminal of a motor neuron on the presynaptic side and a specialized region of a muscle fiber on the postsynaptic side. Formation of the NMJ during development is a multistep process[1]-[3] . In the initial phase of NMJ formation, nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (AChRs) cluster with other postsynaptic proteins in the central regions of the myofibers after myogenesis independently of innervations by motor neurons; this process is called "prepatterning" [4]-[6]. Some proteins expressed in the muscle, MuSK (a muscle-specific receptor tyrosine kinase), LRP4, and Dok-7, are essential for this process [7]-[9]. The axons of motor neurons are guided by chemoattractants and chemorepellents to elongate toward target muscles so that the axon terminals can contact the prepatterned postsynaptic regions. The pre- and postsynaptic regions then differentiate and are stabilized and modified by the elimination of redundant synapses to form structurally and functionally mature NMJs. However, precisely how these processes occur remains an important unanswered question.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 10 13 10 13 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">a d</underline>
###xml 24 25 24 25 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">a</underline>
###xml 28 29 28 29 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">m</underline>
###xml 533 537 529 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Black1">[10]</xref>
###xml 670 674 666 670 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Hattori1">[11]</xref>
###xml 675 679 671 675 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Reiss1">[15]</xref>
The ADAM (a disintegrin and metalloprotease) family proteins play critical roles in morphogenesis, fertilization, and diseases. Some ADAM proteins contain an active metalloprotease domain, and ADAM proteases modulate the ectodomain shedding of various membrane proteins, such as membrane-anchored growth factors, receptors, and adhesion molecules. For example, tumor necrosis factor-alpha converting enzyme (TACE/ADAM17) is essential for the phorbol ester-stimulated shedding of the ectodomains of various membrane-anchored proteins [10], and Kuzbanian/ADAM10 is involved in the ectodomain shedding of multiple substrates, including ephrin, Notch ligands, and cadherins [11]-[15].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 162 166 159 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Inoue1">[16]</xref>
###xml 168 172 165 169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Kurisaki1">[17]</xref>
###xml 292 296 283 287 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Shirakabe1">[18]</xref>
###xml 297 301 288 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Yokozeki1">[20]</xref>
Meltrin beta, an ADAM protein that contains an active metalloprotease domain, is strongly expressed in the heart and peripheral nervous system during development [16], [17]. Meltrin beta mediates the ectodomain shedding of neuregulin beta1, a membrane-anchored ErbB ligand, in cultured cells [18]-[20].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 122 126 122 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Blobel1">[21]</xref>
###xml 315 318 312 315 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 303 318 303 315 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 474 478 465 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Kurohara1">[22]</xref>
###xml 479 483 470 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Komatsu1">[24]</xref>
###xml 512 524 503 512 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;</italic>
###xml 524 527 512 515 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 845 849 830 834 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Kurisaki1">[17]</xref>
###xml 319 323 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 385 389 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 528 532 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Although the protease functions of ADAM proteins have been studied most intensively, these proteins contain other domains [21] such as the disintegrin domain, and it is important to investigate the functions that ADAM proteins may possess in addition to their protease activity. We previously generated meltrin beta-/- mice to further study the properties of Meltrin beta. Using these mice, we and another group showed that Meltrin beta is involved in the heart development [22]-[24]. In the C57BL/6 background, meltrin beta-/- mice are born, but die immediately after birth. Neonatal death is often caused by respiratory failure, including defective regulation of the contractions of respiratory muscles, such as the diaphragm and intercostal muscles. This finding, combined with the expression of Meltrin beta in the peripheral nervous system [17], led us to further investigate the roles of this gene in the development of the NMJ.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 231 243 224 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;</italic>
###xml 431 434 414 417 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 419 434 405 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 504 507 487 490 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 435 439 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 508 512 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In this study, we found that Meltrin beta plays a regulatory role in the formation of NMJs. The zone of acetylcholine receptor alpha mRNA distribution was broader and excess sprouting of motor nerve terminals was more prominent in meltrin beta-deficient than in wild-type embryonic diaphragms. A microarray analysis revealed aberrant distribution of ephrin-A5 in addition to acetylcholine receptor alpha transcripts in meltrin beta-/- mice. Excess sprouting of axon terminals were also found in ephrin-A5-/- mice, which lead us to examine whether Meltrin beta regulates ephrin-A5-Eph signaling in the formation of the NMJ. Meltrin beta interacted with EphA4 in motor neurons during development, and the expression of Meltrin beta in EphA4-expressing cells blocked the vesicular internalization of ephrin-A5 bound to EphA4, independently of the Meltrin beta metalloprotease function. Previous studies indicated the requirement of endocytosis in efficient contact-dependent repulsion induced by ephrin-Eph signaling. From these results and previous findings, we propose that Meltrin beta negatively regulates ephrin-A5-EphA vesicular internalization, which could be one of mechanisms to stabilize the developing NMJ.
###end p 14

To examine the role of Meltrin beta in the development of the NMJ, we first investigated the expression of Meltrin beta at the NMJ in muscles (Fig. 1A-C). Anti-Meltrin beta antibody recognizes the C-terminus of Meltrin beta, and show little affinity to other proteins in meltrin beta-/- mice ([22], also see Figure S1S). Acetylcholine receptors (AChRs) were visualized with Alexa-594-alpha-bungarotoxin (BTX). In the adult, Meltrin beta was expressed at NMJs (Fig. 1C, arrowheads). During development, expression of Meltrin beta gradually clustered at the NMJ during the late stage of embryogenesis (Figure S1).

To investigate whether Meltrin beta is involved in the formation of the NMJ, we performed whole-mount immunostaining of wild-type and meltrin beta-/- diaphragms to examine the distribution of presynaptic axon terminals and postsynaptic AChR clustering (Fig. 1D-I). In wild-type embryos at embryonic day (E) 18.5, AChR clusters were confined to the central endplate zone, and axon terminals of motor neurons detected with synaptophysin were apposed to the AChR clusters (Fig. 1D-F). In contrast, in meltrin beta-/- embryos AChR clusters were more broadly distributed (Fig. 1H, I, also compare the bars in E and H), and excess sprouting of the axon terminals was observed prominently (Fig. 1G, I, arrowheads). Consistent with these data, the zone of AChRalpha mRNA distribution was broader in meltrin beta-/- than in wild-type diaphragms at E18.5 (Fig. 1J, K; see the bars). Furthermore, distribution of AChR clusters remained sparse in diaphragms of meltrin beta-/- newborns, and the average size of AChR clusters was smaller in meltrin beta-/- than in wild-type muscles (Fig. 1L). The primary phrenic nerve trunk projected onto the muscles normally (data not shown). Together, these results indicate that Meltrin beta is expressed at the NMJ and contributes to fine-tuning of the formation and/or maintenance of the NMJ.

We hypothesized that muscles of meltrin beta-/- mice would exhibit aberrant gene expression patterns related to their defects in NMJ formation. We microdissected AChR-positive "synaptic regions" (Fig. 2A, B) and AChR-negative "extrasynaptic regions" (Fig. 2A, C) from wild-type and meltrin beta-/- diaphragms, as described previously [25]. The genes were categorized into 3 groups: genes expressed more highly in synaptic regions than in extrasynaptic regions of wild-type muscle (Fig. 2D, green), genes expressed in synaptic regions more highly in wild-type than in meltrin beta-/- muscle (Fig. 2D, red), and genes whose expression patterns differed between wild-type and meltrin beta-/- muscles (Fig. 2D, blue). Among 110 genes that fulfilled all the criteria (Table S1), secreted or membrane-bound proteins were selected. Inconsistent with the array data, expression of ephrin-A5 was even higher in meltrin beta-/- muscle than in wild type muscle. However, we were interested in this gene because of its roles in morphogenesis and synaptogenesis. A result representative of the RT-PCR analyses is shown in Fig. 2E.

Ephrin-A5 is a glycosylphosphatidylinositol-anchored protein expressed in muscles and is a ligand for a group of membrane-bound receptor tyrosine kinases, the Ephs. Motor neurons express EphA4 receptors [27]-[30], and EphA4 is localized at the NMJ [31]. Islet 1/2-positive motor neurons of E12.5 spinal cords expressed Meltrin beta as well as EphA4 (Fig. 4A-D). We asked whether Meltrin beta interacts with EphA4 in motor neurons to regulate ephrin-Eph signaling. As shown in Fig. 4E-G, immunoprecipitation analyses were performed. When myc-tagged Meltrin beta and HA-tagged EphA4 were co-expressed in HEK293T cells, these two proteins interacted with each other (Fig. 4E). When HA-tagged Meltrin beta lacking its ectodomain was co-expressed with EphA4, the mutant Meltrin beta did not precipitate EphA4; in contrast, EphA4 did co-precipitate with Meltrin beta lacking the cytoplasmic domain (Fig. 4F). This suggested that Meltrin beta, through its ectodomain, interacts with EphA4. We tested whether Meltrin beta interacts with EphA4 in developing neural cells. We dissected spinal cords and dorsal root ganglia (DRG) from embryos at E13.5 and lysed them with Triton-X100 lysis buffer. Proteins were immunoprecipitated with the anti-EphA4 antibody and immunoblotted with the anti-Meltrin beta antibody. Meltrin beta co-immunoprecipitated with EphA4 (Fig. 4G, open arrow), suggesting that Meltrin beta interacts with EphA4 in neural cells during development.

Meltrin beta reaches lipid rafts to cleave the neuregulin-beta1 extracellular region [19] , and is expressed mainly in the Golgi apparatus and the intracellular vesicles around it [20]. The molecular interaction between Meltrin beta and EphA4 in neural cells led us to investigate intracellular localization of Meltrin beta in the existence of EphA4. When Meltrin beta was expressed together with EphA4, some of the Meltrin beta proteins were translocated to the cell surface (Figure S2). Then, we examined whether Meltrin beta affects the membrane localization of EphA4 on the cellular membrane. We compared the amount of EphA4 expressed on the cell surface; this was estimated by cell surface biotinylation, in NIH3T3 cells transformed with EphA4-HA, with or without the exogenous expression of Meltrin beta (Fig. 5A). Meltrin beta expression did not affect the expression of EphA4 on the cell surface (Fig. 5A, arrowhead). Next, we investigated the distribution of EphA4 in lipid raft and non-raft fractions (Fig. 5B). EphA4-HA was expressed together with wild-type Meltrin beta (Meltrin beta WT); Meltrin beta EQ, which contains a point mutation in the active site of its metalloprotease domain; or the empty plasmid as a control, and total cell lysates were prepared from each transfectant with Triton-X100 lysis buffer. We divided the cellular lysates into two fractions, a Triton-X100-soluble fraction and a Triton-X100-resistant insoluble fraction, and analyzed them with anti-HA antibodies. The distribution pattern of EphA4 in the soluble and insoluble fractions was not altered by the expression of Meltrin beta WT or the EQ mutant, regardless of stimulation with ephrin-A5-Fc, a chimeric protein of a soluble form of ephrin A5 and a human Fc fragment. However, the amount of EphA4 protein in the soluble fraction that was precipitated with an anti-HA antibody (clone HA-7) was reduced significantly when Meltrin beta WT or the EQ mutant was expressed in the cells (Fig. 5B, bottom). Similar results were obtained with an anti-EphA4 antibody that recognizes the SAM domain in the cytoplasmic region of EphA4 (Fig. 5C). These results suggest that the C-terminus, especially SAM domain, of EphA4 becomes less accessible to these antibodies when Eph4A interacts with Meltrin beta, independent of ephrin-A5 stimulation, and that the interaction of these two proteins does not require the protease activity of Meltrin beta.

###begin title 43
Discussion
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 161 165 158 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Inoue1">[16]</xref>
###xml 167 171 164 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Kurisaki1">[17]</xref>
###xml 321 324 312 315 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 309 324 303 315 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 325 329 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Meltrin beta is a metalloprotease-containing member of the ADAM family of proteins that is expressed in the developing heart and peripheral nervous system (PNS) [16], [17]. We previously showed that Meltrin beta played a role in the endocardial cushion development. In this study, we extended the analyses of meltrin beta-/- mice in the development of PNS and explored molecular mechanisms for it. Our study revealed a novel role of Meltrin beta in the formation of the NMJ, and suggested a link between Meltrin beta and ephrin-Eph signaling. Based on these data and previous reports, we discuss possible mechanisms through which Meltrin beta contributes to the formation of NMJs together with ephrin-A5-EphA.
###end p 44
###begin title 45
Role of Meltrin beta in the development of the NMJ
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 85 88 85 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-FlanaganSteet1">[4]</xref>
###xml 90 93 90 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Yang2">[6]</xref>
###xml 241 245 241 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Misgeld1">[35]</xref>
###xml 247 251 247 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Lin1">[36]</xref>
###xml 268 271 265 268 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 256 271 256 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 530 533 516 519 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 518 533 507 519 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 272 276 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 534 538 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 662 666 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Muscles develop specialized postsynaptic apparatuses before motor neurons reach them [4], [6]. These prepatterned postsynaptic structures are stabilized and modified by secreted factors, such as Agrin and ACh, that emanate from motor neurons[35], [36]. In meltrin beta-/- mice, AChRalpha mRNA was distributed in a broader zone in the muscles and excess sprouting of nerve terminals was evident. Moreover, preferential expression of the AChR alpha and ephrin-A5 genes in the synaptic region of muscles was disrupted in meltrin beta-/- mice, whereas the expression pattern of the MuSK gene, a key player for the prepatterning of the NMJ, was not affected in these mice. These phenotypes suggest that Meltrin beta does not participate in the prepatterning of the NMJ but mediates the stabilization of the NMJ through the fine-tuning of terminal axon branching and/or the transcriptional confinement of some post-synaptic genes. Consistent with this idea, Meltrin beta gradually localizes to the NMJ during the late stage of embryogenesis, after the establishment of prepatterning in muscles and initial axon pathfinding although Meltrin beta is expressed in the ventral horn of the spinal cord already at E12.5.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
Role of ephrin-A5 in NMJ Formation
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 173 177 173 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Feng1">[26]</xref>
###xml 179 183 179 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Kania1">[29]</xref>
###xml 185 189 185 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Frisen1">[37]</xref>
###xml 266 270 266 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Pasquale1">[38]</xref>
###xml 321 325 321 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Feng1">[26]</xref>
###xml 403 409 403 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003322-g003">Fig. 3</xref>
###xml 533 537 533 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Lai1">[31]</xref>
Both Eph receptor tyrosine kinases and ephrins, high-affinity ligands of Ephs, are membrane proteins. Ephrin-Eph signals function as topographical guidance cues for neurons [26], [29], [37] and synaptogenesis and plasticity of synapses in the central nervous system [38]. Ephrin-A5 is expressed in the developing muscles [26] and our study revealed that ephrin-A5 proteins were concentrated at the NMJ (Fig. 3). Other ephrin-As and EphAs, including ephrin-A2, EphA4, and EphA7, also localize at the NMJ in adult or postnatal muscles [31]. The localized expression of these ephrins and Ephs at the NMJ suggests that they also function at the interface between motor neurons and muscles during late stages of development and/or in adult for the formation or maintenance of the NMJ.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 114 118 114 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Feng1">[26]</xref>
###xml 216 220 216 220 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Eberhart2">[28]</xref>
###xml 222 226 222 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Kramer1">[30]</xref>
###xml 245 254 245 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ephrin-A5</italic>
###xml 340 349 340 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ephrin-A5</italic>
###xml 349 352 349 352 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 494 503 494 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ephrin-A5</italic>
###xml 503 506 503 506 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 590 593 587 590 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 578 593 578 590 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 724 733 721 730 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Ephrin-A5</italic>
###xml 733 736 730 733 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 507 511 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 594 598 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 737 741 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 909 913 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Previous studies showed that Ephrin-A5 is a rostrocaudal positioning cue for the projection of some motor neurons [26] and a guidance cue for another group of motor neurons innervating the muscles of the dorsal limb [28], [30]. We asked whether ephrin-A5 was also involved in the fine- tuning of motor axon terminals during development. In ephrin-A5-/- embryos, axon arbors failed to be stabilized at the postsynaptic sites and excess sprouting of the motor terminals was prominent at E18.5 in ephrin-A5-/- mice. This phenotype was similar to, but more pronounced than, that of meltrin beta-/- mice. These results indicate that ephrin-A5 is required for the axon terminals to contact with postsynaptic apparatuses properly. Ephrin-A5-/- mice survive to adulthood. The NMJ of their muscles after birth would be also worth studying in detail because it is possible that signs of abnormality in the NMJ of these mice have been unnoticed. Alternatively, because structurally similar ephrins are expressed in muscles, they might compensate the phenotype after birth.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 273 277 273 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Egea1">[39]</xref>
###xml 279 283 279 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Holmberg1">[40]</xref>
###xml 390 394 390 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Holmberg1">[40]</xref>
###xml 395 399 395 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Poliakov1">[42]</xref>
###xml 509 513 509 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Egea1">[39]</xref>
###xml 515 519 515 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Holmberg1">[40]</xref>
###xml 608 612 605 609 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Davy1">[43]</xref>
###xml 712 716 706 710 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Schwander1">[44]</xref>
###xml 891 895 882 886 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Weinl1">[45]</xref>
###xml 943 947 931 935 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Martin1">[46]</xref>
###xml 949 953 937 941 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Noakes1">[47]</xref>
###xml 804 811 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus</span>
In contrast to the molecular mechanisms of prepatterning of the NMJ postsynaptic regions, the factors produced by muscles to differentiate presynaptic specializations have been poorly understood. The interaction of ephrins with Ephs leads to bidirectional signaling events [39], [40], and ephrin-Eph interactions can induce attractive or adhesive responses in addition to repulsive effects [40]-[42]. Whereas the forward signaling from ephrin-A5 to EphA4 causes cytoskeletal rearrangements in some conditions [39], [40], the reverse signal from EphA to ephrin-A5 upregulates the expression of integrin-beta1 [43] to modify cell morphology. Integrin-beta1 in muscles is essential for the formation of stable NMJs [44], supporting a molecular link between ephrin-A5 and integrin-beta1 in NMJ formation. In Xenopus, ephrin-A5 acts as an attractive signal to neurons in cooperation with laminin [45]. At the NMJ, a specific laminin, laminin-beta2 [46], [47], may cooperate with ephrin-A5 to attract and stabilize motor terminals. Thus, the adhesive ephrin-Eph interaction at the NMJ could be a noticeable mechanism through which contact-dependent bidirectional signaling contributes to development of the pre- and postsynaptic specializations.
###end p 50
###begin title 51
A regulatory role for Meltrin beta in ephrin-A5-EphA signaling
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 94 103 91 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ephrin-A5</italic>
###xml 103 106 100 103 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 123 126 117 120 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 111 126 108 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 202 205 193 196 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 190 205 184 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">meltrin &#946;<sup>&#8722;/&#8722;</sup></italic>
###xml 487 496 466 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0003322.s002">Figure S2</xref>
###xml 127 131 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 206 210 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The molecular interaction between Meltrin beta and EphA4, the similarity of the phenotypes of ephrin-A5-/- and meltrin beta-/- mice at the NMJ, and the aberrant ephrin-A5 mRNA expression in meltrin beta-/- mice suggest a relationship between Meltrin beta and ephrin-A5-EphA signaling. Although Meltrin beta was not found at the cell surface without coexpressing with EphA4, some Meltrin beta molecules were translocated to the cell surface when Meltrin beta was co-expressed with EphA4 (Figure S2). Because Meltrin beta is not usually found at the cell surface, we interpret the result as indicating that the molecular interaction with EphA4 likely promoted the translocation of Meltrin beta to the cell surface.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 207 211 204 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Hattori1">[11]</xref>
###xml 213 217 210 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Egea1">[39]</xref>
###xml 341 347 338 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in cis</italic>
###xml 450 454 447 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Hattori1">[11]</xref>
###xml 555 563 552 560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in trans</italic>
###xml 565 569 562 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Janes1">[12]</xref>
###xml 913 917 910 914 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Cowan1">[34]</xref>
###xml 919 923 916 920 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Marston1">[48]</xref>
###xml 924 928 921 925 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Irie1">[50]</xref>
###xml 1096 1100 1093 1097 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Cowan1">[34]</xref>
The most important question was whether Meltrin beta regulates the ephrin-A5-EphA4 interaction. An ephrin-Eph interaction delivers a contact-triggered repulsive signal to the ephrin- and Eph-expressing cell [11], [39]. Mechanisms to convert the signal from contact to repulsion include cleavage of the ephrin. Ephrin-A2 is cleaved by ADAM10 in cis upon binding to EphA receptor, and a cleavage-inhibiting mutation in ephrin-A2 delays axon withdrawal [11]. In contrast, Janes et al. showed that ADAM10 cleaves ephrin-A5 on the membranes of opposing cells (in trans; [12]). Either result of these experiments with cultured cells can explain contact-triggered repulsion between ephrin- and Eph-expressing cells. Another mechanism is endocytosis. Ephrins or Ephs on opposing cells are endocytosed into the cells expressing Ephs or ephrin, respectively, without ectodomain shedding, a process called trans-endocytosis [34], [48]-[50]. With some evidences, it is also suggested that endocytosis is a critical step for turning the interaction of ephrins and Ephs from contact into repulsion efficiently [34].
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 283 289 277 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003322-g006">Fig. 6</xref>
In contrast to ADAM10, Meltrin beta did not cleave ephrin-A5 even when ephrin-A5-expressing cells were mixed with EphA4-expressing cells (data not shown). Instead, the expression of Meltrin beta in EphA4-expressing cells interfered with the endocytosis of ephrin-A5-EphA4 complexes (Fig. 6). This interference might diminish the ability of ephrin- and Eph-expressing cells to repel with each other. Alternatively, cell-cell adhesion would overcome the repulsion when EphA4 interactes with Meltrin beta.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 235 239 232 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Marignani1">[51]</xref>
###xml 241 245 238 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Schuebel1">[52]</xref>
###xml 337 341 334 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Cowan1">[34]</xref>
How does Meltrin beta regulate the endocytosis of ephrin-A5-EphA4 complexes? Endocytosis is triggered by Vav family GEFs, which bind to phosphorylated Ephs and are themselves phosphorylated by other kinases, possibly of the Src family [51], [52]. This results in the activation of Rac1 GTPase and the endocytosis of ephrin-Eph complexes [34]. In the current study, when EphA4 interacted with Meltrin beta, EphA4 became less accessible to an antibody against its SAM domain significantly. We speculate that the association of EphA4 with Meltrin beta may alter the accessibility of endocytosis regulators to EphA4 or change their association and may prevent Vav family GEFs from activating ephrin-triggered endocytosis.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 224 230 221 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003322-g007">Fig. 7</xref>
On the basis of our findings and previous reports, we present a working hypothesis of how Meltrin beta and ephrin-A5-EphA signaling cooperate to stabilize the association of the pre- and postsynaptic apparatuses of the NMJ (Fig. 7). In short, Meltrin beta associated with EphA4 would convert a state of an axon terminal from a mobile to a static one through modulating the ephrinA5-EphA4 signaling at the NMJ by interfering with the internalization of the ephrin-Eph complexes there. This conversion might contribute to formation of the stabilized NMJ. The sustained expression of EphA4-Meltrin beta at the presynapses would cause localized expression of ephrin-A5 at the postsynapses, which would increase the stability of the ephrin-A5-EphA4 interaction.
###end p 56
###begin title 57
Hypothetic model of how Meltrin beta and ephrin-A5-EphA4 signaling stabilize the NMJ.
###end title 57
###begin p 58
When axon terminals touch the ephrin-A5-expressing postsynaptic region, EphA4 associated with Meltrin beta is localized preferentially at the axon terminal opposite the postsynaptic cluster on the muscle fiber; Meltrin beta interferes with the internalization of the ephrin-A5-EphA4 complexes so that the axon terminal and muscle could avoid receiving a repulsive signal at the NMJ, and many molecules, such as agrin, MuSK and adhesion proteins, cooperate in stabilization of the NMJ. In the absence of Meltrin beta, axon terminals of motor neurons cannot be stabilized because impaired suppression of endocytosis would result in a failure to block the ephrin-A5-EphA4 repulsive signal. As a result, the motor terminals remain mobile at the NMJ, and the reverse signal from EphA4 to ephrin-A5 is unstable, leading to the diffuse distribution of ephrin-A5 and AChR transcripts in muscles. (see also text).
###end p 58
###begin p 59
In summary, this study revealed that Meltrin beta plays a role in the fine tuning of the NMJ during development. Although we discussed a non-proteolytic role of Meltrin beta in the regulation of ephrin-A5-EphA4 signaling here, functional significance of Meltrin beta as a protease is not excluded in the NMJ formation. Further studies are necessary to determine whether Meltrin beta presented together with EphA4 on the cell surface also functions as a protease to cleave substrates, which contribute to the formation of the NMJ including the confinement of AChR mRNA in the central endplate zone.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 402 406 390 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003322-Lai1">[31]</xref>
###xml 742 746 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In addition to the question on the proteolytic function of Meltrin beta, many questions remain to be solved: the molecular mechanisms by which Meltrin beta blocks the ephrin-dependent endocytosis and the mechanisms that localize the Meltrin beta-EphA4 complex in axon terminals apposed to the NMJ. Meltrin beta and EphA4 are not only expressed in neurons but also in developing muscles (this study and [31]), and ephrin-A5 could be also expressed in terminal Schwann cells. Cell type-specific ablation of Meltrin beta and ephrin-A5 would help understanding more precise mechanisms of the NMJ stabilization which these molecules mediate. It is noteworthy that the expression of ephrins, Ephs, and Meltrin beta in the NMJ is sustained in adult mice, implying that these molecules are also important in the maintenance of the NMJ.
###end p 60

EphA4-HA transformant cells were transfected with pBOS-meltrin beta WT or EQ and incubated for 36 h, followed by replacement of the growth medium with serum-free medium for 2 h. The cells were washed once with Hanks balanced salt solution (HBSS, Invitrogen) and incubated in HBSS at 12degreesC or 37degreesC for 10 min. After this treatment, 3 microg/ml human Fc or ephrin-A5-Fc (R&D systems, nonclustered) proteins were added, and the mixture was incubated for 15 min at 12degreesC or 37degreesC respectively. The cells were washed twice with HBSS and fixed with 4% PFA, followed by permeabilization with 0.2% Triton-X 100/PBS solution. To label human Fc or ephrin-A5-Fc proteins, goat anti-human Fc fragment antibodies were added before the permeabilization for the cells incubated at 12degreesC and after the permeabilization for the cells incubated at 37degreesC. Immunocytochemistry was performed. Single sections of confocal images were captured on a Leica TCS SP5 confocal microscope. Several fields were chosen randomly and the number of cells without endosomes containing ephrin-Eph complexes was counted. The experiment was performed in three 2-well glass culture slides on two independent occasions. Student's t-test was used for statistical comparisons.
