Loss of p24 function in Drosophila melanogaster causes a stress response and increased levels of NF-kappaB-regulated gene products

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 574 598 574 598 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster </italic>
###xml 337 342 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 366 371 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
###xml 574 597 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
Secretory and transmembrane proteins traverse the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and Golgi compartments for final maturation prior to reaching their functional destinations. Members of the p24 protein family, which are transmembrane constituents of ER and Golgi-derived transport vesicles, function in trafficking some secretory proteins in yeast and higher eukaryotes. Yeast p24 mutants have minor secretory defects and induce an ER stress response that likely results from accumulation of proteins in the ER due to disrupted trafficking. We tested the hypothesis that loss of Drosophila melanogaster p24 protein function causes a transcriptional response characteristic of ER stress activation.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 63 74 63 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 105 109 105 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p24 </italic>
###xml 114 121 114 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">logjam </italic>
###xml 122 125 122 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj</italic>
###xml 197 201 197 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 371 375 371 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 387 398 387 398 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
We performed genome-wide profiling experiments on tissues from Drosophila females with a mutation in the p24 gene logjam (loj) and identified changes in message levels for 641 genes. We found that loj mutants have expression profiles consistent with activation of stress responses. Of particular note is our observation that approximately 20% of the loci up regulated in loj mutants are Drosophila immune-regulated genes (DIRGs), many of which are transcriptional targets of NF-kappaB or JNK signaling pathways.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 4 8 4 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 276 280 276 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 371 375 371 375 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 413 424 413 424 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 624 635 616 627 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 777 787 769 779 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
The loj mutant expression profiling data support the hypothesis that loss of p24 function causes a stress response. Genes involved in ameliorating stress, such as those encoding products involved in proteolysis, metabolism and protein folding, are differentially expressed in loj mutants compared to controls. Nearly 20% of the genes with increased message levels in the loj mutant are transcriptional targets of Drosophila NF-kappaB proteins. Activation of NF-kappaB transcription factors is the hallmark of an ER stress response called the ER overload response. Therefore, our data are consistent with the hypothesis that Drosophila p24 mutations induce stress, possibly via activation of ER stress response pathways. Because of the molecular and genetic tools available for Drosophila, the fly will be a useful system for investigating the tissue-specific functions of p24 proteins and for determining the how disrupting these molecules causes stress responses in vivo.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 323 324 323 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 325 326 325 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 849 850 849 850 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 851 852 851 852 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 853 855 853 855 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 194 199 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Proper functioning of the ER is critical for cell survival and function. Perturbations in protein folding in the ER or in trafficking of secretory proteins are associated with a large number of human maladies, including diabetes and neurodegenerative diseases, and can lead to chronic inflammation and cancer [Reviewed in [1-5]]. The ER is the first cellular compartment in which secretory and membrane proteins undergo post-translational modification as they progress through the cellular membrane systems to their final functional location. When ER homeostasis is disrupted or trafficking is blocked, cells attempt to correct the problem via induction of stress response pathways such as the unfolded protein response (UPR) and the ER overload response (EOR), phylogenetically conserved mechanisms for dealing with cellular assaults [Reviewed in [4,6-12]].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 521 523 517 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
The UPR and EOR signaling programs can be activated by the presence of unusually large amounts of proteins within the ER that are not trafficked to the Golgi. For instance, the UPR is induced when mutant proteins cannot be properly folded and accumulate within the ER lumen. The EOR, which is characterized by activation of the Rel transcription factor NF-kappaB and its downstream signaling pathways, is stimulated by events such as viral infection that produce overwhelming amounts of non-mutant proteins [Reviewed in [13]].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 534 535 534 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 536 537 536 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 538 540 538 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
The UPR attenuates translation to decrease overall protein load in the ER and activates transcription of genes encoding chaperones, oxidoreductases, and other proteins necessary for folding and post-translational modification. Genes involved in protein metabolism are up regulated as well. The activated UPR also targets improperly folded proteins to the ER-associated degradation (ERAD) pathway and up regulates genes necessary for protein metabolism. Cells unable to overcome ER stress may ultimately die by apoptosis [Reviewed in [4,6-10]].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 95 96 95 96 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 97 98 97 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 99 101 99 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
Three cellular signal transduction pathways mediate downstream UPR or EOR events [Reviewed in [4,6-10]]. In mammals PERK, Atf6 and Ire-1 are ER transmembrane proteins, with N-termini in the ER lumen and C-termini in the cytoplasm, which serve as sensors for changes within the ER, including alterations in calcium levels, redox environment or levels of nascent proteins. Translational attenuation, which decreases the overall amount of nascent protein requiring ER modification and folding, is mediated by PERK kinase activation by autophosphorylation. Subsequent PERK inactivation of the eIF2alpha translation initiation factor via phosphorylation is responsible for the resulting translational attenuation.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 92 97 92 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xbp1 </italic>
###xml 326 328 326 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 558 562 558 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xbp1</italic>
###xml 574 585 574 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 614 624 614 624 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 650 652 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 653 655 653 655 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 671 673 671 673 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
Upon UPR induction, the Ire-1 endonuclease is also phosphorylated, allowing cleavage of the Xbp1 mRNA to produce a functional bZIP transcription factor that positively regulates expression of downstream genes such as ER chaperones. Ire-1 also targets a subset of mRNAs encoding membrane and secreted proteins for degradation [14]. The third UPR signal, Atf6 is a transcription factor that is released from the ER during cellular stress, moves to the Golgi for final modification, and then activates transcription of downstream genes including chaperones and Xbp1. There are Drosophila counterparts to PERK (PEK in Drosophila), Atf6, Ire-1 and Xbp1 [[15-17], Reviewed in [11]], although the genes and their targets are largely unstudied in this model system.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 260 262 244 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 263 265 247 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 453 455 429 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 458 463 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
###xml 600 605 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
One or more of the signaling cascades involved in the UPR also activate the EOR and NF-kappaB. This NF-kappaB activation occurs post-translationally, possibly as a result of PERK-mediated translational attenuation of the NF-kappaB inhibitory partner I-kappaB [18,19]. Other data suggest a model in which NF-kappaB is a downstream target of Ire-1 through an interaction between Ire-1 and the E3 ubiquitin ligase Traf2 that leads to NF-kappaB activation [20]. Yeast do not have NF-kappaB homologs and lack two of the UPR signaling pathways found in multi-cellular organisms; all known UPR signaling in yeast is regulated by Ire-1.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 173 175 173 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 305 307 305 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 430 432 430 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 649 651 649 651 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
It is not clear that activation of the UPR and EOR are entirely separable events since both responses require components of the same ER stress-activated signaling cascades [21]. Several chemical agents that induce the UPR also trigger the EOR, although some inducers may be pathway specific [Reviewed in [13]]. One possibility is that the EOR is a later response to stresses that cannot be overcome by the initial UPR activation [21]. Early UPR activation may induce "adaptation genes", and, if the stress cannot be overcome, the "alarm genes" characteristic of the EOR may be induced in a final effort to save cells before they initiate apoptosis [21].
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 189 191 189 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 264 266 264 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 267 269 267 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 301 303 301 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 349 360 349 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 560 571 560 571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 294 299 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 613 618 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Members of the p24 protein family are constituents of ER and Golgi-derived vesicles and are hypothesized to function in a variety of ways in intracellular protein trafficking [Reviewed in [22]]. Since loss of p24 function reduces trafficking of specific proteins [23-25] and induces the UPR in yeast [26], we reasoned that loss of p24 expression in Drosophila should similarly reduce protein trafficking and induce an ER stress response. Because we expected trafficking of otherwise normal proteins to be affected in p24 mutants, we further predicted that the Drosophila stress response would differ from that in yeast by more closely resembling the EOR rather than the UPR and that the response would be activated by the PEK or Ire-1 pathways. Our genome-wide expression profiling and genetic interaction studies are consistent with the hypotheses that loss of p24 function leads to activation of stress responses in metazoans and that both the PEK and Ire-1 pathways may be involved in these responses through activation of the NF-kappaB proteins Dif and Rel.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Results
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 69 71 69 71 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 102 113 102 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 642 645 642 645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj</italic>
###xml 645 650 645 650 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">00898</sup>
###xml 651 654 651 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj</italic>
###xml 654 660 654 660 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">04026 </sup>
###xml 695 699 695 699 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 836 838 836 838 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 20 25 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 255 260 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Since deletion of a yeast p24 protein induces an ER stress response [26], we predicted that loss of a Drosophila p24 protein, Loj, would elicit a similar response in flies. Because flies contain all three stress response pathways (PEK, Atf6, Ire-1) while yeast only have the Ire-1-mediated response, we expected that loss of trafficking due to a p24 mutation might induce multiple ER stress-activated signaling pathways. Since one characteristic of ER stress responses is transcriptional activation of numerous gene products to ameliorate the stress, we utilized a genomic profiling approach to determine the genes with altered expression in loj00898/loj04026 mutants (henceforth referred to as loj mutants) compared to controls. We showed previously that this allelic combination is strongly hypomorphic and probably genetically null [27].
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 132 134 132 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 135 137 135 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 633 644 633 644 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 1031 1032 1031 1032 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
loj transcripts and proteins are highly expressed in the follicle cells of developing eggs and in the central nervous system (CNS) [27,28] as well as in other tissues such as the gut and fat body (K.A. Boltz, S. Grady, and G.E. Carney, unpublished results). For our analyses, female abdomens containing the ovaries, genital tract, fat, and gut tissue were dissected away from the remainder of the body (head/thorax), which contains the entire CNS as well as the fat surrounding the head. Mutant and control samples from either the abdomen or head/thorax were used to generate labeled samples for microarray analyses using Affymetrix Drosophila Genome 2.0 arrays. We identified a similar number of expression changes in each of the two samples using a 1.5-fold cut-off (p < 0.001 for all data extraction methods for each sample). In the abdominal preparations 366 genes showed transcriptional changes, and 372 genes were identified in the head/thorax samples. Ninety-seven of the genes are common to both tissue preparations (Table 1).
###end p 19
###begin p 20
###xml 56 60 56 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
Total number of genes with altered expression levels in loj mutant tissues
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 171 172 171 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 231 233 231 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 234 236 234 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 259 270 259 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 398 400 394 396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 401 403 397 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 548 550 544 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 604 615 596 607 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 655 657 647 649 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 878 882 862 866 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
Of the 641 genes showing transcriptional changes in one or both tissues, approximately one fifth (131 genes) are up regulated by various pathogens or septic injury (Table 2); many are targets of the Toll or Imd signaling cascades [29-37]. The Toll pathway in Drosophila regulates immune response to pathogens and septic injury by activating the NF-kappaB transcription factors Dorsal (Dl) and Dif [38-41], while the Imd pathway branches downstream of TAK1 to activate Jun kinase (JNK) and Relish (Rel) signaling in response to pathogens or injury [31]. Rel is the third NF-kappaB transcription factor in Drosophila that controls humoral immune responses [42]. Since activation of NF-kappaB is the hallmark of the EOR, our data showing increased levels of numerous immune responsive and NF-kappaB target genes suggest that a stress response that includes the EOR is activated in loj mutant females.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 46 50 46 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>

Expression changes common to both tissues
###end title 23

###xml 251 252 251 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>

###xml 392 393 392 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 526 528 526 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 671 672 671 672 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 677 678 677 678 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 681 688 681 688 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tequila</italic>
###xml 717 721 717 721 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 750 751 750 751 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 911 913 911 913 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
While the majority of the genes identified in our analyses were specific to either the abdomen or the head/thorax samples, numerous changes were common to both tissue preparations. Twenty genes had decreased levels (including loj, see Additional file 1) in both mutant tissue samples, while 77 genes showed increased expression in loj mutants compared to controls (see Additional files 1 and 2). Seventeen of these 97 genes were found previously to be transcriptionally responsive to the ER stress inducing agent tunicamycin [43]. Fourteen of our genes show a similar response to tunicamycin-induced changes, while 3 genes show the opposite pattern (see Additional files 1 and 2). Tequila, which is down regulated in loj mutants (see Additional file 1), is also repressed in tissue culture cells in which the UPR has been induced by dithiothreitol treatment; this decrease is dependent upon Ire-1 but not Xbp1 [14].
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 73 84 73 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 196 197 196 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 200 211 200 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GADD45&#946; </italic>
###xml 342 344 335 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 522 530 515 523 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PGRP-SA </italic>
###xml 551 552 544 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 574 578 563 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rel </italic>
###xml 622 626 611 615 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
Of the genes identified in both tissue data sets, 36 are known or likely Drosophila immune-regulated genes (DIRGs), the majority of which are targets of Toll or Imd signaling (see Additional file 2). GADD45beta is an ER stress response gene that is regulated by NF-kappaB and exerts an anti-apoptotic effect by down regulating JNK signaling [44]. Also up regulated in this common data set are genes encoding small immune peptides such as Cecropins and Attacins as well as the gram-positive bacteria peptidoglycan receptor PGRP-SA (see Additional file 2). The NF-kappaB gene Rel also has increased transcript levels in the loj mutant tissues.
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 244 245 244 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 250 251 250 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 347 351 347 351 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 422 427 422 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GstD5</italic>
To validate our microarray data, we used qPCR to test a subset of genes with altered expression profiles in both tissue samples. Each tissue was assayed independently and showed the expected directional change (see Methods and Additional files 1 and 2). For most of the genes tested, the gene expression levels are significantly different between loj mutants and controls in both tissues (p < 0.05). In only one instance (GstD5) was the difference non significant for either tissue.
###end p 26
###begin title 27
Mutations in the p24 gene eclair also have elevated levels of immune-regulated products
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 120 124 116 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p24 </italic>
###xml 169 179 165 175 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 204 208 200 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p24 </italic>
###xml 215 222 211 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eclair </italic>
###xml 223 226 219 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eca</italic>
###xml 232 239 228 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">baiser </italic>
###xml 240 243 236 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bai</italic>
###xml 302 306 298 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 315 317 311 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 318 320 314 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 328 332 324 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eca </italic>
###xml 336 340 332 336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bai </italic>
###xml 362 366 358 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 422 424 418 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
We anticipated that increased expression of immune responsive and NF-kappaB target genes would be a general response to p24 gene mutations in multi-cellular animals. In Drosophila, mutations in two other p24 genes, eclair (eca) and baiser (bai), cause oviposition defects similar to those observed for loj mutants [27,45]. Both eca and bai are co-expressed with loj in ovarian follicle cells and are expressed in the CNS [28].
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 20 24 20 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eca </italic>
###xml 143 147 143 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 184 190 184 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CG6687</italic>
###xml 192 200 192 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tsp42Ed </italic>
###xml 204 210 204 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Frost </italic>
###xml 240 244 240 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eca </italic>
###xml 280 288 280 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Socs36E </italic>
###xml 322 326 322 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eca </italic>
###xml 460 464 456 460 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p24 </italic>
We generated viable eca mutant females and tested them for increased expression of four of the immune regulated genes that are up regulated in loj mutants (See Methods). Expression of CG6687, Tsp42Ed and Frost is significantly increased in eca mutants relative to controls, while Socs36E is not significantly increased in eca mutants (data not shown). These results indicate that activation of NF-kappaB and immune-regulated genes may be a general response to p24 loss-of-function mutations.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
Expression changes in the abdomen
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 22 33 22 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 198 200 198 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 201 203 201 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 343 345 343 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 346 348 346 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
The egg tissue of the Drosophila abdomen expresses Loj throughout all stages of development, with particularly strong expression observed at stage 10 in the somatically-derived follicle cell layer [27,28], whose primary function is secreting factors needed for eggshell formation. Low-level Loj expression is also observed in the nurse cells [27,28] that produce mRNAs and other factors necessary for embryonic development. Our recent analysis indicates that the Loj protein is highly expressed in other adult abdominal tissues as well, including the fat body and the gut (K.A. Boltz, S. Grady, and G.E. Carney, unpublished results).
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 69 73 69 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 224 225 224 225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 248 249 248 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S3">3</xref>
###xml 254 255 254 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
###xml 489 490 489 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S3">3</xref>
###xml 577 580 577 580 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-17</sup>
###xml 653 655 653 655 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-5</sup>
We identified a set of 366 genes with altered expression patterns in loj mutant abdominal tissue. Of these loci, 269 were noted only in the abdomen while the remaining 97 were also detected in the head/thorax samples (Table 1; see Additional files 3 and 4). Forty-seven of the 269 abdomen-specific genes were previously determined to be immune responsive. Most are transcriptional targets of Toll or Imd signaling while others are components of JNK signaling pathways (see Additional file 3). General immune-responsive genes are overrepresented in this data set (p = 4.915 x 10-17, Fisher's Exact Test) as are components of JNK signaling (p = 9.192 x 10-5, Fisher's Exact Test).
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 117 118 117 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
###xml 241 252 241 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 266 268 266 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 269 271 269 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 272 274 272 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 329 340 329 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 425 426 425 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
The abdominal tissue also differentially expressed 222 genes with no known function in immunity (see Additional file 4). Most of the published genome profiling experiments that identified immune response genes used an earlier version of the Drosophila genome array [29-32,35] that lacked many of the transcriptional units on the Drosophila Genome 2.0 chips used in this study. Therefore, some of the genes in Additional file 4 likely have previously unrecognized roles in the immune response.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 114 115 114 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
###xml 118 126 118 126 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CG14207 </italic>
###xml 164 172 164 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CG33486 </italic>
###xml 275 277 275 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 306 313 306 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CG4415 </italic>
Genes in the up-regulated class have functions consistent with involvement in a UPR response (see Additional file 4). CG14207 encodes a small Hsp20-like chaperone. CG33486 encodes an asparagine synthetase, a protein previously implicated in the mammalian ER stress response [46]. The predicted function of CG4415 is unfolded protein binding, suggesting it functions in the UPR as well. Many other up-regulated genes encode proteins with predicted functions such as proteolysis and peptidolysis, metabolism and oxidoreductase activity.
###end p 34
###begin title 35
Expression changes in the head/thorax
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 87 91 87 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 94 98 94 98 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 140 142 140 142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 162 166 162 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
A subset of cells in the CNS also has high-level Loj expression, and CNS expression of loj in loj mutants rescues egg laying and fertility [28]. We separated the loj CNS effects from those occurring in developing eggs and gut by dissecting the abdomen (containing the ovaries and gut but not CNS tissue) away from the remainder of the fly.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 114 115 114 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 138 139 138 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S5">5</xref>
###xml 144 145 144 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S6">6</xref>
###xml 318 319 318 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 341 342 341 342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S5">5</xref>

###xml 504 506 504 506 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-3</sup>
We identified a set of 275 genes differentially regulated in these samples compared to those from abdomens (Table 1; see Additional files 5 and 6). Many up-regulated genes function in processes such as lipid and protein metabolism. Again, approximately 20% of the genes have been implicated in immune signaling (Table 2; see Additional file 5). Immune responsive as well as JNK pathway components are also overrepresented in the head/thorax preparations (Immune gene p = 1.594 x 10-19; JNK p = 3.110 x 10-3; Fisher's Exact Test).

###begin title 38
loj interacts genetically with members of the NF-kappaB genetic signaling pathways
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 129 131 129 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 368 370 364 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 577 581 569 573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 643 647 635 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 661 672 653 664 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
The Toll and Imd pathways activate different, but sometimes overlapping, immune-responsive genes depending upon the pathogen [32,47]. In adult flies, the NF-kappaB protein Dif is the Toll signaling effecter molecule in response to immune challenge, while the adult function of Dl is not clear. Rel functions downstream of Imd signaling to activate DIRGs [Reviewed in [48]]. The fact that we observed increased message levels of signaling molecules that lie downstream of each pathway begs the question as to whether the three Drosophila NF-kappaB proteins are activated in the loj mutant. Therefore, we tested for genetic interactions between loj and the three Drosophila NF-kappaB genes to identify which may be involved in the stress response.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 6 10 6 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 47 49 47 49 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 112 116 112 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 330 334 322 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 434 438 426 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 443 446 435 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dif</italic>
###xml 448 451 440 443 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dl </italic>
###xml 454 457 446 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rel</italic>
###xml 507 516 499 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dif; loj </italic>
###xml 519 528 511 520 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj, Rel </italic>
###xml 580 581 572 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T3">3</xref>
###xml 650 654 642 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
Since loj is expressed throughout development [27], we anticipated that activation of the ER stress response in loj mutants would be important for survival to adult eclosion from the pupal case. If NF-kappaB proteins are needed for stress response activation to survive pupation, we expected that reducing NF-kappaB levels in the loj mutant would affect adult survival. When we assayed eclosion rates in double mutant combinations of loj with Dif, dl or Rel, we found that fewer than the expected number of Dif; loj or loj, Rel mutant offspring emerged from the pupal case (Table 3). These results suggest that the Dif and Rel proteins are needed in loj mutants to ameliorate the stress response so that animals can survive development.

###begin title 43
Discussion
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 435 439 435 439 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 546 557 546 557 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 641 645 637 641 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
When the ER has an unusually high burden of protein products due to viral infection, mutation, or other assaults, mechanisms are activated to reduce the ER protein load. Our results that stress response genes are activated in p24 mutants suggest that loss of p24 protein function causes stress, possibly by disrupting ER homeostasis. Furthermore, we show that a large proportion of the genes with altered transcriptional profiles in a loj p24 mutant genetic background are known or suspected targets of immune signaling pathways regulated by the Drosophila NF-kappaB proteins Dif and Relish; function of each of these proteins is needed for loj mutants to survive to adulthood. Activation of NF-kappaB is the signature event in the EOR response to increased ER protein load.
###end p 44
###begin title 45
The loj p24 mutant activates stress response genes
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 263 274 263 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 275 277 275 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 475 477 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
We discovered transcript-level changes in genes encoding factors involved in protein metabolism and folding that are consistent with induction of the UPR. However, we did not identify all of the genes found in a previous study on tunicamycin-induced ER stress in Drosophila [43], which identified approximately 600 transcriptional changes in untreated compared to tunicamycin-treated flies using a 1.5-fold change threshold. Tunicamycin activates the UPR as well as the EOR [13].
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 81 86 81 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xbp1 </italic>
###xml 87 89 87 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 148 153 148 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xbp1 </italic>
###xml 232 237 232 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 297 302 297 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xbp1 </italic>
###xml 430 435 430 435 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xbp1 </italic>
###xml 438 442 438 442 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 520 530 520 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xbp1-EGFP </italic>
###xml 531 533 531 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 549 553 549 553 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 586 591 586 591 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xbp1 </italic>
###xml 814 819 814 819 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xbp1 </italic>
###xml 869 874 869 874 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xbp1 </italic>
###xml 892 894 892 894 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 3 8 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In yeast, loss of p24 function results in increased splicing of the Ire-1 target Xbp1 [26]. We did not find evidence for increased transcription of Xbp1 in our p24 mutant microarray experiment, which is consistent with the Girardot et al. report (2004) showing that the UPR transcriptional target Xbp1 is not up regulated in tunicamycin-treated flies. Furthermore, a second RT-PCR-based assay did not reveal increased splicing of Xbp1 in loj compared to control animals (data not shown) nor did we observe activation of Xbp1-EGFP [16] expression in loj mutants. The fact that increased Xbp1 transcription and splicing are not detected could be because these responses occur prior to the time point of our assays. Alternatively, some aspects of the tunicamycin and p24 mutant stress responses may be independent of Xbp1 signaling. Activated Atf6 increases expression of Xbp1 during ER stress [49], but Atf6 signaling has not been implicated in NF-kappaB activation during the EOR.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 181 186 181 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 196 198 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 269 270 269 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 272 273 272 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 275 276 275 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S3">3</xref>
###xml 278 279 278 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
###xml 281 282 281 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S5">5</xref>
###xml 284 285 284 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S6">6</xref>
###xml 373 384 373 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 439 450 439 450 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 624 628 624 628 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 878 880 878 880 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
Our study identified 641 expression changes, 92 of which were demonstrated previously to be tunicamycin sensitive. Sixty-five transcripts had the same directionality as in Girardot et al. (2004) [43], while the remaining 27 show opposite patterns (see Additional files 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6). The tunicamycin experiment differed from ours in that the researchers used wild-type Drosophila adult male bodies and probed an earlier version of the Drosophila genome array with lower transcriptional coverage. Some of the differences in the two studies may be due to sex, genetic background and assayed transcripts. Additionally, the loj mutants should be chronically stressed due to loss of p24 function throughout development; the tunicamycin-treated animals experienced an acute stress response since they were assayed 24 hrs after being placed on sugar medium containing tunicamycin [43]. Therefore, we expect that many differences in the two studies are due to differential effects from chronic compared to acute stress responses.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 121 125 121 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 190 201 190 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 255 266 255 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 474 479 474 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cht4 </italic>
###xml 500 501 500 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 504 510 504 510 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ImpL2 </italic>
###xml 514 522 514 522 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CG33093 </italic>
###xml 543 544 543 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
Our most interesting finding is the large number of immune-responsive genes with altered transcriptional profiles in the loj p24 mutant. Since the majority of currently published studies on Drosophila immune-responsive genes used the Affymetrix version 1 Drosophila Genome Arrays (based upon Berkeley Drosophila Genome Project v4.0), they could not identify all immune-regulated loci. Based upon their predicted functions, immune-regulated candidates from our study include Cht4 (see Additional file 1), ImpL2 and CG33093 (see Additional file 4).
###end p 49
###begin title 50
loj interacts genetically with Dif and Rel
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 169 173 165 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 177 180 173 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dif</italic>
###xml 182 185 178 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dl </italic>
###xml 188 191 184 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rel</italic>
###xml 207 210 203 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dif</italic>
###xml 210 211 206 207 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 211 217 207 213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">; loj </italic>
###xml 221 224 217 220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj</italic>
###xml 224 229 220 225 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">00898</sup>
###xml 231 234 227 230 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rel</italic>
###xml 234 235 230 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E</italic>
###xml 234 238 230 234 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>E</italic>20 </sup>
###xml 316 318 312 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dl</italic>
###xml 318 319 314 315 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sup>
###xml 319 324 315 320 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">; loj</italic>
###xml 324 330 320 326 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">00898 </sup>
###xml 376 378 372 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dl</italic>
###xml 378 379 374 375 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sup>
###xml 379 384 375 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">; loj</italic>
###xml 384 389 380 385 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">00898</sup>
###xml 395 397 391 393 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dl</italic>
###xml 397 398 393 394 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sup>
###xml 398 405 394 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">/+; loj</italic>
###xml 405 410 401 406 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">00898</sup>
###xml 454 458 446 450 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 524 528 516 520 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 565 569 557 561 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 758 762 750 754 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dif </italic>
###xml 766 770 758 762 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rel </italic>
###xml 814 817 806 809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dl </italic>
###xml 821 825 813 817 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">imd </italic>
###xml 847 851 839 843 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 909 915 901 907 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj/+ </italic>
Recognizing that increased DIRG message levels could be explained by activation of one or more Drosophila NF-kappaB proteins, we tested for genetic interactions between loj and Dif, dl or Rel. We found that Dif2; loj and loj00898, RelE20 mutant combinations dramatically reduced adult eclosion, while effects of the dl1; loj00898 double mutant combination did not differ from dl1; loj00898/+ or dl1/+; loj00898. These results indicate that NF-kappaB and loj mutations interact with one another. The results suggest that the loj mutant stress response is needed for loj mutant animals to survive development and that this response is partially mediated by Dif and Rel. It is possible that the two mutations independently make a "sick" fly worse off. However, Dif and Rel are not required for viability, and neither dl nor imd mutations enhance the loj mutant phenotype, although both genes affect viability in loj/+ animals.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 137 141 137 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 407 409 395 397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 465 469 453 457 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
In our study we did not identify expression changes in all of the known immune-regulated genes. If both Dif and Rel are activated in the loj mutant, one might expect that all potential NF-kappaB target genes should be affected. However, NF-kappaB target sites in immune-responsive promoters are variably responsive to NF-kappaB and can be modulated by other factors to provide specialized immune responses [50], possibly in a tissue-specific manner. Therefore, the loj mutant may not activate all possible NF-kappaB targets because other cellular factors are needed for a strong response.
###end p 52
###begin title 53
Mechanisms for stress-induced activation of NF-kappaB
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 202 204 190 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 205 207 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 213 224 201 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 362 373 346 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 397 399 377 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 528 539 508 519 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
In mammals two potential mechanisms for ER stress-induced NF-kappaB activation have been described. The first involves PERK-mediated translational inhibition of the NF-kappaB inhibitor protein IkappaB [18,19]. In Drosophila loss of IkappaB could only affect Dif signaling since Dif, but not Rel, is maintained in an inactive form through an association with the Drosophila IkappaB protein Cactus [48]. Degradation of Cactus and release of its inhibitory effect on Dif allows downstream transcriptional changes. In contrast, the Drosophila NF-kappaB transcription factor Rel is a bi-partite protein containing inhibitory as well as activating domains. Similarly to the mammalian p105 and p100 NF-kappaB proteins, Rel activation involves a proteosome-mediated cleavage event that releases the inhibitory domain from the activating domain. Therefore, it is unlikely that PEK-mediated translational attenuation of an inhibitor molecule functions in Rel activation.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 173 175 169 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 255 257 247 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 273 275 265 267 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 279 290 271 282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 418 420 410 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 421 423 413 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 493 504 481 492 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
The second proposed mechanism for mammalian NF-kappaB activation due to ER stress is mediated by Ire-1 via an interaction with the TNF-receptor-associated factor 2 (Traf2) [20]. Traf2 is an E3 ubiquitin ligase that activates NF-kappaB and JNK signaling [[51], Reviewed in [52]]. Drosophila encodes a Traf2-like molecule, and there is evidence that Drosophila Traf proteins are involved in Toll, Imd and JNK signaling [53-56]. One possible scenario for NF-kappaB activation due to ER stress in Drosophila is that Ire-1 modulates the Rel-induced ER stress pathways while Pek translational attenuation regulates Dif signaling.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 159 161 159 161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 304 306 304 306 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 307 309 307 309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 422 432 418 428 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 625 628 617 620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rel</italic>
###xml 714 717 706 709 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj</italic>
###xml 725 729 717 721 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">imd </italic>
###xml 739 743 731 735 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rel </italic>
###xml 782 785 774 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj</italic>
###xml 990 994 982 986 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 1024 1025 1016 1017 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 1027 1028 1019 1020 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S3">3</xref>
###xml 1033 1034 1025 1026 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S5">5</xref>
###xml 3 8 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In yeast, loss of p24 protein function causes an ER stress response, one consequence of which is secretion of the heat shock protein and ER stress sensor BiP [26]. Interestingly, there is evidence in mammals that BiP and related heat shock proteins bind to and activate Toll-like receptors [Reviewed in [57-59]]. Therefore, it may be possible to induce NF-kappaB independently of PEK, Atf6 or Ire-1 if BiP is secreted. In Drosophila, the Toll receptor regulates Dif and Dorsal signaling. Even if BiP activation of Toll receptors plays a role in stress-induced NF-kappaB activation, it cannot account for the observation that Rel, which is not regulated by Toll signaling, is induced and interacts genetically with loj. Since imd regulates Rel but does not interact genetically with loj, Rel signaling in this p24 mutant context could be mediated by the Ire-1/Traf2 pathway rather than via Imd. Similarly, the Ire-1/Traf2 pathway may modulate the JNK response genes that are up regulated in loj mutants (see Additional files 2, 3 and 5). Because Rel activation requires Rel cleavage rather than disruption of an inhibitory protein interaction, it is unlikely that Rel activation is immediately downstream of Pek.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 154 156 154 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 175 182 175 182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tequila</italic>
Our analysis did not identify the same set of stress-responsive genes previously shown to require Ire-1 but not other components of the UPR/EOR response [14]. Only one locus, Tequila, is down regulated in both experiments. However this difference in affected genes may be due to the stressors or the time periods examined.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 90 94 86 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 183 187 179 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 601 605 581 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
There are other possibilities for the observed increases in NF-kappaB target genes in the loj mutant. One is that the observed stress response results from tissue damage occurring in loj mutants. Tissue damage could activate NF-kappaB and stress response genes in the fat body. Another possibility is that increased levels are due to increased message stability rather than activation of NF-kappaB in the adult. We cannot rule out the possibility that changes in stability of NF-kappaB target gene transcripts, rather than NF-kappaB activation, account for the observed expression differences between loj and wild-type females.
###end p 58
###begin title 59
Do loj p24 mutants have a trafficking defect?
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 6 10 6 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 96 100 96 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 228 232 224 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 287 292 283 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dec-1</italic>
###xml 294 300 290 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vm32E </italic>
###xml 304 308 300 304 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cp15</italic>
###xml 370 371 366 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
###xml 440 444 436 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 557 561 553 557 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 670 674 666 670 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
Since loj mutants have increased expression of many genes implicated in ER stress responses and loj mutations interact genetically with mutations in two NF-kappaB genes, it is likely that intracellular trafficking is reduced in loj mutants. Genes that encode components of the eggshell (dec-1, Vm32E and Cp15) are down regulated in abdominal tissue (see Additional file 4). It is possible that trafficking of these proteins or is slowed in loj mutants and provides feedback inhibition of transcription. Alternatively, these products may be decreased in the loj mutant because egg production is slowed and fewer eggs of the stages that express these genes are present in loj females.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 112 116 112 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 144 145 144 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
###xml 224 226 224 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 227 229 227 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
###xml 294 296 294 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B63">63</xref>
###xml 468 472 468 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
Another interesting finding is that transcript levels of the octopamine receptor Oamb are also decreased in the loj mutant (see Additional file 4). Octopamine signaling is required for egg laying in flies and other insects [60-62], and reduced Oamb protein expression impairs female ovulation [63]. Loss of octopamine signaling via decreased trafficking of the receptor or molecules involved in octopamine or Oamb production could account for the egg-laying defect in loj mutants.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 8 12 8 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 38 44 38 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p24-2 </italic>
###xml 50 57 50 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CG33105</italic>
###xml 96 97 96 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 100 106 100 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p24-2 </italic>
###xml 163 167 163 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 210 212 210 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 235 241 235 241 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">p24-2 </italic>
###xml 265 269 265 269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
In both loj mutant tissue samples the p24-2 gene (CG33105) is up regulated (see Additional file 1). p24-2 is a member of the Drosophila p24-alpha subfamily, while loj is a member of the p24-gamma subfamily [22,28]. It is possible that p24-2 can substitute for some loj functions during development or in the adult. We plan to test these hypotheses during future investigations into p24 protein function in flies.
###end p 62
###begin title 63
Conclusion
###end title 63
###begin p 64
###xml 257 261 253 257 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">loj </italic>
###xml 265 269 261 265 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dif </italic>
###xml 272 276 268 272 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rel </italic>
The genetic profiling data support our assertion that loss of p24 function induces a stress response that includes increasing the levels of NF-kappaB target gene transcripts, a hallmark of the EOR. Furthermore, the decreased viability of double mutants for loj and Dif or Rel suggests that these NF-kB proteins and their downstream signaling pathways play important roles in ameliorating the stress response.

Females of the genotypes loj00898/loj04026, loj00898/+, and loj04026/+ were collected as virgins within 1-2 h of the beginning of the 12 h lighted portion of the light-dark cycle, kept in glass vials in groups of 20 or fewer flies, and aspirated singly into new vials on day 3 after collection. On day 4, a single wild-type male was aspirated into a vial containing a single female and observed for mating. Only females that mated within 30 min for 18-30 min were collected for subsequent RNA extractions. Female abdomens were dissected away from the head/thorax 3 hours after the end of the mating period and each of the two tissue types (abdomen or head with thorax) was quick-frozen in Trizol reagent (Invitrogen). Total RNA from abdomen or head/thorax tissue was extracted in Trizol using the manufacturer's protocol. Twelve female tissue samples were collected for each of 6 independent RNA extractions for loj00898/loj04026. Our control samples consisted of 6 loj00898/+ and 6 loj04026/+ samples combined together for each of 6 independent RNA extractions. Cyber-T was used to perform Bayesian statistical analyses on expression values derived from dChipPM-MM and dChipPM [64] and GCOS (Affymetrix) essentially as described previously [65]. ###begin p 72

###xml 580 588 580 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TM3, Sb </italic>
From the crosses of loj00898, RelE20/TM3, Sb to either loj00898/TM3, Sb or RelE20/TM3, Sb there are three classes of expected progeny (e.g., (1) loj00898, RelE20/TM3, Sb (2) loj00898/TM3, Sb and (3) loj00898, RelE20/loj00898) that should each account for one-third of the total number of offspring. We are not able to distinguish the two classes of progeny that contain the TM3, Sb chromosome. Therefore, we calculate the percentage of expected loj00898, RelE20/loj00898 progeny by dividing the total number of loj00898, RelE20/loj00898 progeny by one-half of the total number of TM3, Sb progeny.
