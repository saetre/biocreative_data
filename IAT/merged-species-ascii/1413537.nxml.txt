Stationary phase expression of the arginine biosynthetic operon argCBH in Escherichia coli

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 25 42 25 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 25 41 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Arginine biosynthesis in Escherichia coli is elevated in response to nutrient limitation, stress or arginine restriction. Though control of the pathway in response to arginine limitation is largely modulated by the ArgR repressor, other factors may be involved in increased stationary phase and stress expression.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 48 55 48 55 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 153 157 153 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoS</italic>
###xml 233 237 233 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR</italic>
###xml 243 248 243 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoS </italic>
###xml 344 349 344 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH </italic>
###xml 362 367 362 367 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 448 454 448 454 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH</italic>
###xml 668 675 667 674 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 792 797 787 792 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 826 833 821 828 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 917 924 912 919 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 962 967 953 958 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
In this study, we report that expression of the argCBH operon is induced in stationary phase cultures and is reduced in strains possessing a mutation in rpoS, which encodes an alternative sigma factor. Using strains carrying defined argR, and rpoS mutations, we evaluated the relative contributions of these two regulators to the expression of argH using operon-lacZ fusions. While ArgR was the main factor responsible for modulating expression of argCBH, RpoS was also required for full expression of this biosynthetic operon at low arginine concentrations (below 60 muM L-arginine), a level at which growth of an arginine auxotroph was limited by arginine. When the argCBH operon was fully de-repressed (arginine limited), levels of expression were only one third of those observed in DeltaargR mutants, indicating that the argCBH operon is partially repressed by ArgR even in the absence of arginine. In addition, argCBH expression was 30-fold higher in DeltaargR mutants relative to levels found in wild type, fully-repressed strains, and this expression was independent of RpoS.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 174 181 174 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 174 181 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The results of this study indicate that both derepression and positive control by RpoS are required for full control of arginine biosynthesis in stationary phase cultures of E. coli.

###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 103 119 103 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 149 157 149 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 157 164 157 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 175 179 175 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">artJ</italic>
###xml 246 247 246 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 262 263 262 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 439 447 439 447 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 448 449 448 449 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 523 531 523 531 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 565 570 565 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argC </italic>
###xml 624 625 624 625 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 648 656 648 656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 657 658 657 658 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 743 747 743 747 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">adiA</italic>
###xml 817 818 817 818 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 819 820 819 820 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 103 119 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 149 156 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 439 446 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 505 511 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 523 530 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 648 655 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The biosynthesis and/or scavenging of arginine are important during host colonization by uropathogenic Escherichia coli. In urine, expression of the E. coli argCBH operon and artJ, encoding a periplasmic transporter, increases more than 10 fold [1] and 18 fold [2], respectively. Synthesis of arginine is likely required during infection as the concentration of arginine found in urine is below that necessary to support maximal growth of E. coli [1]. Consistent with these data, infection challenge in a murine model with E. coli strains carrying mutations in the argC gene results in impaired proliferation in the kidney [1]. In enteropathogenic E. coli [3] arginine synthesis and transport, together with arginine decarboxylase (encoded by adiA), are important components of the cell's acid resistance repertoire [3-5].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 36 44 36 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 117 118 117 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 260 261 260 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 262 263 262 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 433 435 433 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 510 512 510 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 36 43 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Under nutrient-limiting conditions, E. coli can potentially utilize arginine as both a carbon and a nitrogen source [6]. Arginine is a precursor for the synthesis of polyamines, putrescine and spermidine, which may reduce oxidative damage to proteins and DNA [7,9]. In addition, in phosphate-starved cells, aerobic metabolism of arginine may be an important physiological adaptation that is intimately associated with cell survival [10]. Since arginine contains 11% of the cell's nitrogen in stationary phase [11], biosynthesis of this amino acid is likely important under sub-optimal conditions.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 136 138 136 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 191 193 191 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 194 196 194 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 300 302 300 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 378 382 378 382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arg </italic>
###xml 449 451 449 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 737 739 737 739 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 769 774 769 774 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">astD </italic>
###xml 775 777 775 777 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 778 780 778 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 787 792 787 792 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cstC </italic>
###xml 793 797 793 797 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">astC</italic>
###xml 800 802 800 802 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 803 805 803 805 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 931 933 931 933 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 934 936 934 936 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 982 993 982 993 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella </italic>
###xml 994 996 994 996 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 1039 1047 1039 1047 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1152 1154 1152 1154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 1176 1177 1176 1177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 1183 1190 1183 1190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 1269 1271 1269 1271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 1300 1302 1300 1302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 1398 1406 1398 1406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1407 1409 1407 1409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 1039 1046 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1183 1190 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1398 1405 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Arginine is synthesized by a complex biosynthetic pathway consisting of several operons and unlinked genes that are controlled by ArgR [12] which represses by binding to a conserved ARG box [13,14] to overlap with RNA polymerase binding sites. Maximum derepression occurs in the absence of arginine [12]. In contrast to most amino acid biosynthetic genes, the expression of the arg biosynthetic genes increases briefly during diauxic growth arrest [15]. As this stress is similar to that imposed by nutrient limitation, it is plausible that stationary phase regulators participate in control of arginine biosynthesis. Several enzymes required for arginine catabolism are controlled by RpoS, an alternative stationary phase sigma factor [16], including those encoded by astD [11,17], and cstC (astC) [11,18]. Many of the members of the large RpoS regulon are specifically expressed during the transition to stationary phase growth [19,20]. Although clearly required for virulence in Salmonella [21], the role of RpoS in the pathogenesis of E. coli is equivocal. RpoS controls many functions that contribute to host adaptation, including osmotic stress [22] and acid challenge [3]. In E. coli, however, RpoS mutants are not impaired in colonization of the urinary tract [23] or gastrointestinal tract [24] in animal models. There are several different alleles of RpoS found in natural populations of E. coli [25], and it is thus possible that RpoS regulation may be strain specific.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 83 85 83 85 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 115 122 115 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 397 404 397 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
In a previous study, we identified many independent RpoS-dependent operon fusions [26]. Two of these mapped to the argCBH operon and were of particular interest because, unlike other RpoS-regulated functions that we identified, this operon, when mutated, rendered the cell auxotrophic. In this study, we have employed these fusions as probes to examine regulatory controls on transcription of the argCBH operon to identify how this key biosynthetic pathway is activated.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Results
###end title 13
###begin title 14
###xml 20 27 20 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
Characterization of argCBH operon fusions
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 73 78 73 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 87 89 87 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 90 92 90 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 207 214 207 214 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 227 228 227 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 288 289 288 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 319 323 319 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH</italic>
###xml 406 409 406 409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rsd</italic>
###xml 437 440 437 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rsd</italic>
###xml 470 475 470 475 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH </italic>
###xml 540 541 540 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 565 568 565 568 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rsd</italic>
In a large mutational screen, we isolated over 100 RpoS dependent operon-lacZ fusions [26,27], many of which were not known to require RpoS for expression. Two of the isolated fusion mutations mapped to the argCBH operon (Fig. 1) and were clearly RpoS-dependent on indicator plates (Fig. 2). While both fusions were in argH; one was intragenic and, as expected, rendered the cell auxotrophic for arginine (rsd1072) and the other fusion (rsd1066) was located between the argH coding sequence and a predicted transcriptional terminator (Fig. 1). Strains carrying the rsd1066 fusion do not have an arginine auxotrophic phenotype (data not shown).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 35 42 35 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 43 45 43 45 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 77 82 77 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 86 91 86 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoS </italic>
###xml 220 225 220 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 247 259 247 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH-lacZ </italic>
###xml 321 322 321 322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 372 379 372 379 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 391 396 387 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
Since ArgR is a known regulator of argCBH [12], we constructed combinatorial argR and rpoS mutants to determine the relative contributions of each regulator to the expression of the operon. Introduction of a deletion of argR into a strain with an argCBH-lacZ fusion resulted in high expression with or without RpoS (Fig. 2). It is likely that RpoS-dependent expression of argCBH in the DeltaargR strains may be masked by strong derepression as a consequence of loss of ArgR.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
###xml 27 34 27 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
Growth phase dependence of argCBH expression
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 33 40 33 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 194 201 194 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 288 293 284 289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoS </italic>
###xml 307 308 303 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 331 338 327 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 436 443 432 439 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 481 486 473 478 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 573 574 565 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
To test if the expression of the argCBH operon is induced upon entry into stationary phase, expression of the operon was assayed in rich media. During entry into stationary phase, expression of argCBH increased 7-fold in wild type cultures, but only exhibited 3-fold increase in the DeltarpoS mutant (Table 1). This indicates that argCBH expression is not only growth phase-dependent but also is affected by loss of RpoS. Expression of argCBH expression was 14-fold higher in DeltaargR mutants than in wild type strains and in these mutants, was independent of RpoS (Table 1).
###end p 18
###begin title 19
###xml 32 39 32 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
Effect of exogenous arginine on argCBH expression
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 81 83 81 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 199 206 199 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 496 501 496 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoS </italic>
###xml 529 536 529 536 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 662 663 661 662 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 705 711 704 710 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH</italic>
###xml 770 772 769 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 815 822 814 821 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 903 904 901 902 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 928 935 926 933 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
RpoS is likely an important factor in amino acid scavenging in stationary phase [28]. Amino acid biosynthesis offers an alternative to the scavenging strategy. It is possible that the RpoS effect on argCBH expression is more pronounced when arginine becomes limiting, as might be the case in stationary phase. To test this, expression of the operon was assessed in exponential phase cultures grown in minimal media supplemented with various concentrations of L-arginine. In strain HS1066 and its rpoS mutant derivative, HS1066p, argCBH expression was found to be inversely proportional to arginine concentration at arginine concentrations less than 60 muM (Fig. 3). Arginine biosynthetic genes, including argCBH, are normally induced in response to arginine limitation [29]. However, in exponential phase cultures, argCBH expression was RpoS dependent only at arginine concentrations below 30 muM (Fig. 3). In stationary phase, argCBH expression was 2-fold RpoS-dependent at all arginine concentrations tested (data not shown).
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 19 24 15 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 416 421 412 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">carAB</italic>
###xml 509 511 505 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>

###xml 2051 2056 2015 2020 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 2203 2208 2163 2168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 2588 2593 2548 2553 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 2638 2640 2598 2600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
Surprisingly, DeltaargR mutants were impaired in growth on minimal media even when supplemented with arginine, strongly suggesting that these strains possessed an additional unidentified nutritional requirement. Intermediates in the arginine biosynthetic pathway are also precursors in other pathways. For example, carbamoylphosphate, the product of carbamoylphosphate synthetase (encoded by the bi-cistronic operon carAB) is not only a precursor of arginine but is also required for pyrimidine biosynthesis [30]. Therefore, it is possible that the derepression of arginine biosynthesis may deplete carbamoylphosphate required for synthesis of pyrimidine thus rendering DeltaargR mutants auxotrophic for pyrimidine. To test this, we examined the growth of DeltaargR mutants on minimal media supplemented with pyrimidines relative to argR+ strains. As shown in Table 2, the growth deficiency of DeltaargR mutants could be completely suppressed by the addition of pyrimidines (growth of DeltaargR and wild type strains were equivalent in the presence of added pyrimidines). We also compared the generation time of argR+ and DeltaargR strains on minimal media with or without pyrimidines, and examined the average colony size by plating the cells on minimal media plates. As shown in Table 3, the generation time of DeltaargR was much greater than that of the argR+ strain, and this growth impairment could be remedied by addition of pyrimidines into the media. The growth requirement was not absolute as some residual growth was observed in the DeltaargR strain. Reduced growth could be due to either a uniform slow growth among cells or by the selection of suppressor mutants which could overcome impairment by acquiring advantageous mutations. The diameters of DeltaargR deletion colonies were only one half that of argR+ colonies indicating ArgR is required for robust growth in minimal media. Furthermore, the colony morphology and size of all colonies were uniform, consistent with the idea that growth results were a consequence of poor growth of argR deletion mutants in general and were not due to suppression by selected mutants. To ensure that this was not a strain-specific phenotype, the DeltaargR mutation was transduced and tested in another common laboratory strain, MG1655 and, as expected, the resultant transductants were similarly found to be pyrimidine limited (data not shown). To exclude the possibility that an uncharacterized mutation linked to the original operon fusion mutation was responsible for the pyrimidine phenotype, we tested an independently constructed argR deletion mutant from the Nara KO collection [31]. This strain was similarly impaired in pyrimidine synthesis (data not shown).
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 66 71 66 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 95 99 95 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR</italic>
###xml 151 153 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 193 198 193 198 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
We further examined the pyrimidine requirement by transforming an argR deletion mutant with an argR-containing plasmid clone from the ASKA collection [31]. Colony size of the ArgR-complemented argR deletion mutant was more than twice that of the control strain after two days growth on minimal media (0.76 +/- 0.03 mm vs. 0.34 +/- 0.03 mm).
###end p 22
###begin title 23
###xml 13 22 13 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">astCADBE </italic>
###xml 55 61 55 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH</italic>
Effect of an astCADBE operon deletion on expression of argCBH
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 4 13 4 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">astCADBE </italic>
###xml 216 218 216 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 254 261 254 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 403 412 403 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">astCADBE </italic>
###xml 470 482 470 482 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH-lacZ </italic>
###xml 673 680 673 680 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 683 692 683 688 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#916;ast </italic>
###xml 772 791 768 779 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#916;rpoS &#916;ast </italic>
###xml 821 822 809 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 854 861 842 849 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 864 873 852 857 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#916;ast </italic>
###xml 938 940 922 924 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 988 995 972 979 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 1103 1110 1087 1094 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 1113 1122 1097 1102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#916;ast </italic>
###xml 1180 1182 1160 1162 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+ </sup>
###xml 1289 1296 1269 1276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
The astCADBE operon, encoding a set of enzymes responsible in the arginine succinyltransferase (AST) pathway, is RpoS-dependent and can be induced in nitrogen limited environment especially when arginine is present [11]. It is possible that RpoS affects argCBH expression through AST-mediated depletion of intracellular arginine resulting in increased derepression by ArgR. To test this possibility, an astCADBE deletion mutant was constructed in strains containing the argCBH-lacZ fusion, and the resultant mutant was assayed in rich media. In exponential phase there is not much expression difference in all these strains. However, in stationary phase, the expression of argCBH in Deltaast mutation strain was nearly 2-fold (an increase of 18 units) higher than that in DeltarpoS Deltaast double mutation strains (Fig. 4). Thus, the RpoS-dependence of argCBH in Deltaast deletion background is consistent with results obtained using AST+ strains, and it is unlikely that RpoS-modulated argCBH expression is indirectly induced by arginine catabolism through the AST pathway. However, the expression of argCBH in Deltaast mutant was slightly lower (10% difference) than in the AST+ strain, indicating that metabolism of arginine through the AST pathway may have a slight overall effect on argCBH expression, probably through modulation of ArgR.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
The effect of exogenous arginine on growth
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 37 42 37 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH </italic>
###xml 465 473 465 473 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 692 699 691 698 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 832 833 830 831 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 1035 1039 1033 1037 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH</italic>
###xml 1076 1083 1074 1081 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 1239 1246 1236 1243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 1346 1353 1343 1350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 465 472 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Since strains carrying a mutation in argH are auxotrophic for arginine (HS1072 this study), these strains could be used to establish the concentration at which this key amino acid becomes growth limiting. Similarly, because the operon fusion in strain HS1066 does not render the cell arginine auxotrophic but is nonetheless ArgR dependent, this strain can be used in parallel to assess ArgR-dependent activation of the operon during arginine restriction. Growth of E. coli was impaired at arginine concentrations less than 60 muM: below this level growth rate was proportional to concentration of the limiting amino acid. Growth yield was similarly affected (data not shown). Derepression of argCBH expression was found to occur at concentrations below 60 muM and, as expected, was inversely related to arginine concentration (Fig. 5). Limitation by the availability of intracellular arginine may account for the reduced growth observed and, through ArgR derepression, may also be partially responsible for the increased expression of argH. Together, these data indicate that argCBH regulation is tightly coupled to arginine availability. At 60 muM, even though the cultures were not arginine-limited with respect to growth, the levels of argCBH were five fold higher than in fully repressed cells. This suggests that, in nutrient-starved cells, argCBH derepression would occur prior to potential growth restriction by arginine limitation ensuring maximum growth yield.
###end p 26
###begin title 27
###xml 21 26 21 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH </italic>
Northern analysis of argH expression
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 47 52 47 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH </italic>
###xml 95 96 95 96 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 253 255 253 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 327 332 327 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH </italic>
###xml 349 354 349 354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoS </italic>
###xml 397 398 397 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 464 469 464 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH </italic>
###xml 559 564 559 564 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoS </italic>
###xml 653 658 653 658 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH </italic>
###xml 733 734 733 734 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 829 834 829 834 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH </italic>
###xml 956 958 956 958 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
Although plate expression assays indicate that argH expression is clearly RpoS-dependent (Fig. 2), the results of reporter gene expression studies showed that RpoS dependence was only 2-fold, somewhat less than that found for most RpoS regulated genes [26]. To resolve this discrepancy, we independently assessed expression of argH in wild type and rpoS mutant cultures by Northern analysis (Fig. 6). Consistent with reporter gene studies on plates, expression of argH was much higher in stationary phase cultures of the wild type strain than in the isogenic rpoS mutant and was much lower in exponential phase samples of both cultures. The hybridizing argH transcript co-migrated with the 16S RNA leading to some interference (Fig. 6). These results, in conjunction with the reporter fusion studies, indicate that expression of argH is both RpoS and stationary phase dependent in a wild type strain and is thus similar to many other RpoS-dependent genes [26].
###end p 28

To determine if RpoS and ArgR modulation of argH expression results in changes in the level of the encoded enzyme, we assayed exponential phase cultures of strains deficient in the expression of these regulators for argininosuccinate lyase activity. ###begin title 32
Discussion
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 53 60 53 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 124 140 124 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 237 244 237 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 363 368 363 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argH </italic>
###xml 420 425 420 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoS </italic>
###xml 493 495 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 124 140 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
In this study, we examined RpoS-dependent control of argCBH expression, and its modulation by ArgR and external arginine in Escherichia coli. Using independently-isolated mutants carrying operon fusions in different positions within the argCBH region, we conducted expression studies of this biosynthetic operon by assaying reporter gene fusions and by examining argH transcript levels in both a wild type strain and an rpoS mutant by Northern analysis. Since ArgR also regulates this operon [12], we evaluated the relative contributions of both ArgR and RpoS to its expression by constructing appropriate double and single null mutants.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 96 98 96 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 154 159 154 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gabP </italic>
###xml 160 162 160 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 165 170 165 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">proP </italic>
###xml 171 173 171 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 176 181 176 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">proU </italic>
###xml 182 184 182 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 187 193 187 193 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gadAB </italic>
###xml 194 196 194 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 202 207 202 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ldcC </italic>
###xml 208 210 208 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 312 314 312 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 446 453 446 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
RpoS regulates many genes that play important roles in stress resistance and energy metabolism [16], but a subset of these RpoS-dependent genes including gabP [26], proP [32], proU [33], gadAB [34] and ldcC [35] aid in amino acid transport and utilization. In a previous genetic screen for RpoS-dependent genes [26], we identified one mutant that was auxotrophic for arginine and carried a mutation that mapped to the terminal gene member of the argCBH operon. To the best of our knowledge, this is the only RpoS-modulated gene known which, when mutated, renders the cell auxotrophic. As such, examining the regulation of this operon may offer unique insight into RpoS-controlled stationary phase physiology.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 57 64 57 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 318 323 318 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gadA </italic>
###xml 327 331 327 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gadB</italic>
###xml 444 446 444 446 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 572 574 572 574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
Our data showing that stationary phase expression of the argCBH operon is affected by RpoS does not reveal whether this effect is direct or indirect. In fact for many members of the RpoS regulon such information has not been established. Indirect regulation is known to be operant for at least some members, including gadA and gadB, two glutamate decarboxylases that are among the most highly RpoS-dependent genes based on microarray analysis [27]. The expression of these genes depends on GadX, a regulator whose growth phase dependent increase expression requires RpoS [36].
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 52 57 52 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">osmY </italic>
###xml 58 60 58 60 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 83 88 83 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gadW </italic>
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 154 161 154 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 186 191 182 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 507 515 503 511 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo </italic>
###xml 637 644 633 640 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 906 913 902 909 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
RpoS can regulate its operon members directly (e.g. osmY [37]) or indirectly (e.g. gadW by the RpoS-dependent GadX regulator [38]). As RpoS dependence of argCBH was not observed in DeltaargR mutants, ArgR appears to be necessary for RpoS-modulated expression of the operon. There are at least two mechanisms that could explain the increase in expression in stationary phase. In the first, as RpoS activates expression of its large regulon, depletion of intracellular arginine may result as a consequence of de novo synthesis of stationary phase proteins. This may derepress the arginine biosynthetic pathway, resulting in an increase in argCBH expression. Alternatively, increased catabolism through the RpoS-dependent AST pathway lower intracellular arginine and might also result in de-repression of the operon. The latter explanation, however, seems unlikely in view of the fact that RpoS modulation of argCBH is not affected by deletion of the AST operon (this study).
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 53 61 53 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 366 367 364 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 607 614 605 612 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 53 60 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Using the arginine auxotroph (HS1072), we found that E. coli becomes growth limited at arginine concentrations below 60 muM. As derepression also occurs at these concentrations (this study), it appears that expression of the biosynthetic pathway is closely coupled to the biosynthetic need for arginine. As the concentration of arginine in LB media is about 60 muM [9], it is possible that our results could be partially explained by depletion of arginine during growth in late exponential phase. However, as supplementation with exogenous arginine did not markedly reduce stationary phase induction of the argCBH operon, it is likely that other mechanisms, including control by RpoS, ensure that this operon continues to be expressed under nutrient limited conditions.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 32 39 32 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 129 134 125 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR </italic>
###xml 722 726 718 722 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR</italic>
###xml 826 834 822 830 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo </italic>
###xml 963 968 959 964 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">carAB</italic>
###xml 1035 1037 1031 1033 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
Surprisingly, maximum levels of argCBH expression in the wild type strain were only one third of those found in an isogenic DeltaargR mutant suggesting that ArgR can be an active repressor even in the absence of exogenous arginine. This may be due to the fact that, even under starvation conditions, synthesized endogenous arginine can be an effective co-repressor. Such control may be physiologically necessary as carbamoylphosphate, an arginine precursor, is also required for pyrimidine biosynthesis. Balancing these two pathways, arginine and pyrimidine biosynthesis, under nutrient-limited conditions, is likely an important physiological imperative, as complete derepression of arginine biosynthesis, by deletion of argR, causes cells to develop a partial requirement for exogenous pyrimidine (this study). The need for de novo arginine synthesis therefore appears to be balanced against other biosynthetic requirements of the cell. he multiple controls on carAB, including availability of arginine, pyrimidines as well purines [39] ensure that the synthesis of these macromolecule precursors is balanced in actively growing cells.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 167 169 167 169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 348 350 348 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 555 557 555 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 600 601 600 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 602 603 602 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 604 606 604 606 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 621 629 621 629 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo </italic>
###xml 876 878 876 878 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 1027 1029 1027 1029 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 1183 1185 1183 1185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 1313 1321 1313 1321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo </italic>
###xml 1401 1408 1401 1408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 1619 1621 1619 1621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
Why might arginine biosynthesis be stationary phase dependent? It is well established that nutrient scavenging is an important survival mechanism in starved cultures [40]. Arginine in particular is likely to be an important metabolite in stationary phase cultures for several reasons. As arginine represents 11 percent of the cells total nitrogen [11], it is potentially an important nitrogen reservoir for starving populations. Arginine is also a potential precursor for the biosynthesis of polyamines which stabilize and condense DNA during senescence [41] and protect it against oxidative damage [7,8,42]. Finally, as de novo protein synthesis in non-growing stationary phase cells is required for the expression of stationary phase adaptive proteins, this may impose a significant biosynthetic demand upon the cell both because there are many such proteins produced (see [16] for review) and because some of these are expressed to extraordinarily high levels. For example, Dps, a highly RpoS-dependent DNA binding protein [43], is almost undetectable in exponential phase, but accumulates to 200,000 molecules per cell in stationary phase (approx. 5% of total cellular protein) [44]. Many genes are induced upon entry into stationary phase and it is likely that this creates a high demand for amino acids for de novo protein synthesis. The up-regulation of amino acid biosynthetic operons such as argCBH may provide a means to satisfy this demand in addition to nutrient scavenging mechanisms including arginine transport which is also a key factor in maintaining high arginine levels in stationary phase cultures [45].
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 161 168 161 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 358 361 358 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">stC</italic>
###xml 379 388 379 388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">astCADBE </italic>
###xml 435 437 435 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 908 910 908 910 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 1027 1028 1027 1028 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>
Biosynthetic regulons, particularly those required for amino acid biosynthesis, are often controlled by a transcriptional repressor (e.g. TrpR and ArgR). Though argCBH operon is modulated by RpoS in stationary phase cultures the dynamic range of this control was small in relation to that exerted by ArgR. Interestingly, another member of the ArgR regulon, astC, a member of the astCADBE operon, is RpoS-dependent to a similar degree [17]. These observations, in conjunction with the results of this study, suggest that RpoS plays an important role in coordinately regulating arginine metabolism in stationary phase. Such control is likely effective because of the nature of the pathway. ArgA-mediated synthesis of N-acetylglutamate is the first committed step in the arginine biosynthetic pathway and is controlled by 1) cumulative feedback inhibition by arginine and 2) ArgR at the level of transcription [12]. Since ArgC and ArgB catalyze early steps in this pathway and ArgH catalyzes the final biosynthetic reaction (Fig. 7), it is likely that control of these key steps by ArgR/RpoS modulates the entire arginine biosynthetic pathway.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 44 52 44 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 114 122 114 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 203 211 203 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 212 214 212 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 281 283 281 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 295 296 295 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 335 336 335 336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 356 357 356 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 682 684 682 684 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 44 56 <span type="species:ncbi:83333">E. coli K-12</span>
###xml 114 121 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 203 210 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
While our studies employed a non-pathogenic E. coli K-12 strain, the results of this study may have relevance for E. coli pathogenesis. For example, though urine is a good growth media for uropathogenic E. coli [46], low concentration of several key nutrients, including arginine [46] and iron [2], can be limiting. As both transport [2] and biosynthesis [1] of arginine are required for maximum growth in urine and in minimal media containing restrictive levels of arginine, control of the functions for the metabolism of this amino acid are likely critical for urovirulence. As RpoS has now been implicated in both control of biosynthesis (this study) and catabolism of arginine [18], it will be useful, in future studies, to establish the relative importance of these metabolic functions in pathogenesis.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
Conclusion
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 12 19 12 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
In summary, argCBH expression is clearly controlled by a finely balanced mechanism mediated by two signals: 1) a general nutrient stress signal mediated, in part, by RpoS and, 2) well known specific control through arginine-dependent modulation of the ArgR repressor.

###begin title 49
Growth conditions
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 134 138 134 136 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174; </sup>
###xml 251 254 249 252 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600</sub>
###xml 361 365 359 363 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>
###xml 500 504 498 502 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>
All cultures were grown in triplicate from independently isolated colonies. Cell growth was monitored spectrophotometrically (Novaspec(R) II spectrophotometer, Pharmacia LKB Biochrom Ltd., Cambridge, England) by measuring optical density at 600 nm (OD600). Expression studies in rich media were conducted using cultures maintained in early exponential phase (OD600 of < 0.3) in antibiotic-free LB media for at least 8 generations, prior to the start of the experiment. Sub-cultures with a starting OD600 of 0.01 were grown in LB at 37degreesC and agitated at 200 rpm.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 35 42 35 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argCBH </italic>
###xml 626 630 624 628 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>
###xml 1038 1042 1036 1040 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>
###xml 1301 1305 1293 1297 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>
To quantify the RpoS dependence of argCBH expression in relation to exogenous arginine concentration in minimal media, overnight minimal media cultures (0.4% glucose) were inoculated from well-isolated colonies on minimal media plates (0.2% glucose) and grown with appropriate antibiotics. To ensure complete repression of arginine biosynthesis, the overnight cultures were supplemented with 230 muM L-arginine. The cultures were diluted (1 in 1000) and maintained in early exponential phase (as described above) prior to the start of experiments in antibiotic-free minimal media supplemented with 230 muM L-arginine. At an OD600 of 0.3, cultures were placed on ice for 2 min and then centrifuged for 10 min at 4000 x g at room temperature. The supernatant was decanted and the resulting cell pellets washed twice with arginine-free minimal media to remove remaining exogenous arginine. The arginine-free cell pellets were re-suspended in minimal media to the same optical density as collected. A series of sub-cultures with a starting OD600 of 0.05 were made into minimal media supplemented with various concentrations of exogenous L-arginine. The sub-cultures were incubated at 37degreesC and agitated at 200 rpm. For each concentration of L-arginine, samples were taken in mid-exponential phase (OD600 of 0.3) and assayed for beta-galactosidase activity
###end p 51
###begin title 52
Enzyme Assays
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 75 77 72 74 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 156 158 153 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
beta-galactosidase activity was assayed as previously described by Miller [47]. ONPG was used as the substrate, and activity was expressed in Miller units [47]. All cultures were grown in triplicate from independent colony isolates (biological replicates) and all assays were performed in duplicate.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 133 140 133 140 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">240 nm </sub>
###xml 141 143 141 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 189 191 189 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 350 356 350 356 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">240 nm</sub>

The reaction mixtures containing 10 ug/ml protein, 1.0 mM argininosuccinate in 50 mM potassium phosphate buffer (pH7.5) were monitored photometrically at OD240 nm. One unit of ASL activity corresponds to 1 nmole L-argininosuccinate hydrolysized per min at pH 7.5 and 37degreesC.
