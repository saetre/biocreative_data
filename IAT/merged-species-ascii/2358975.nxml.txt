5'HS5 of the Human beta-globin Locus Control Region Is Dispensable for the Formation of the beta-globin Active Chromatin Hub
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 37 48 37 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>
###xml 227 236 224 230 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-gene</italic>
###xml 626 637 620 628 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>
###xml 1155 1161 1146 1152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">per se</italic>
Hypersensitive site 5 (5'HS5) of the beta-globin Locus Control Region functions as a developmental stage-specific border in erythroid cells. Here, we have analyzed the role of 5'HS5 in the three dimensional organization of the beta-gene locus using the Chromatin Conformation Capture (3C) technique. The results show that when 5'HS5 is deleted from the locus, both remote and internal regulatory elements are still able to interact with each other in a three-dimensional configuration termed the Active Chromatin Hub. Thus, the absence of 5'HS5 does not have an appreciable effect on the three dimensional organization of the beta-globin locus. This rules out models in which 5'HS5 nucleates interactions with remote and/or internal regulatory elements. We also determined the binding of CTCF, the only defined insulator protein in mammalian cells, to 5'HS5 by using chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) assays. We detect low levels of CTCF binding to 5'HS5 in primitive erythroid cells, in which it functions as a border element. Surprisingly, we also observe binding levels of CTCF to 5'HS5 in definitive erythroid cells. Thus, binding of CTCF to 5'HS5 per se does not render it a functional border element. This is consistent with the previous data suggesting that CTCF has dual functionality.

Introduction

###xml 1454 1457 1418 1421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Tolhuis1">[6]</xref>
###xml 1459 1462 1423 1426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Palstra1">[7]</xref>
###xml 1688 1691 1652 1655 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Hanscombe1">[8]</xref>
###xml 1693 1696 1657 1660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Dillon1">[9]</xref>
###xml 1735 1746 1699 1707 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>
###xml 1949 1953 1904 1908 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Tanimoto1">[10]</xref>
###xml 2152 2156 2107 2111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Wai1">[11]</xref>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 356 361 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 507 511 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 778 783 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The human beta-globin locus contains five genes that are arranged in the same order as their developmental expression pattern: 5'-epsilon (embryonic) Ggamma Agamma (foetal) delta beta (adult)-3'. The major activating region, termed the Locus Control Region (LCR), is located 6 to 22 kb 5' to the epsilon-globin gene. The most prominent charactistic of the human beta-globin LCR is that it drives high level, tissue-specific, copy number-dependent and position-independent expression of linked transgenes in mice [1]. The core components of the LCR are the DNase I hypersensitive sites (5'HS1-5) [2]. Each of the hypersensitive sites holds a unique array of transcription factor binding sites, including those for GATA1, NF-E2 and EKLF proteins. This suggests that the HS in the human LCR may have different stage-specific activities and cannot be replaced functionally by each other [3], [4]. Transcriptional activation of the beta-like globin genes is thought to be a multi-step system. This model includes the active involvement of erythroid transcription factors in the spatial organization of the locus, and increased accessibility to trans-acting factors by recruitment of chromatin remodeling complexes [5]. The resultant transcriptionally active locus is arranged in a three dimensional structure termed the active chromatin hub (ACH), in which the LCR hypersensitive sites interact directly with the transcribed genes through a looping mechanism [6], [7]. The deduced spatial arrangement of the regulatory elements and the genes in the ACH offers an explanation for the observation that a gene proximal to the LCR has a transcriptional advantage over a more distally located gene [8], [9]. The mode of action of the LCR to the beta-globin genes appears to be orientation dependent as an inverted LCR is incapable of activating downstream globin genes at high levels, and the epsilon-gene can not be activated when placed upstream of the LCR [10]. This property of the LCR may be due to the spatial organization of its hypersensitive sites, but it is also possible that the locus contains elements that block LCR action in one of the directions [11].
###end p 4

###xml 149 160 149 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>

###xml 294 298 288 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Wai1">[11]</xref>
###xml 29 34 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 229 234 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Recently, we have shown that human 5'HS5 acts as a developmental stage-specific border element in erythroid cells. In that study, we placed a marked beta-globin gene upstream of the LCR and the genes, using PAC constructs of the human beta-globin locus in which 5'HS5 was flanked by loxP sites [11].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 139 146 139 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 246 257 246 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>
###xml 314 325 311 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>

###xml 536 547 527 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>
###xml 308 313 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 340 344 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 486 491 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 530 535 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In the present study, we applied Chromosome Conformation Capture (3C) technology to further investigate the functional properties of 5'HS5 in vivo. The rationale behind the use of the 3C technique was to delineate the spatial organization of the beta-globin locus upon Cre/loxP-mediated deletion of 5'HS5 in human beta-globin transgenes in mice. To date, directionality of the LCR has not yet been studied using the 3C technique in the context of the three-dimensional structure of the human beta-globin locus. 3C analysis of the human beta-globin transgenes was performed at the primitive and definitive stages of erythropoiesis. Since 5'HS5 functions as a border in primitive erythroid cells, any major alterations in the 3D organization of the locus related to this property should be revealed. We also performed 3C analysis in the same transgenic lines in which 5'HS5 had been removed by Cre-mediated excision.
###end p 6

###xml 83 94 83 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>

###xml 245 249 242 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Bulger1">[14]</xref>
###xml 344 351 341 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 75 82 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 332 337 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The first insulator element reported in vertebrates is 5'HS4 (cHS4) in the chicken beta-globin locus [12]. Binding of the 11 Zn-finger protein CTCF to the FII region of the cHS4 is thought to play an important role in the activity of cHS4 [13], [14]. To investigate the potential activity of CTCF as an enhancer blocking protein on human 5'HS5 in vivo, we performed chromatin immunoprecipitation assays using antibodies directed against CTCF.

To examine the potential conformational changes of the ACH in the presence or absence of 5'HS5 in the context of the human beta-globin locus, we employed transgenic mice carrying modified human beta-globin PAC transgenes (Fig. 1, [11]). The transgenes were derived from PAC185 which contains the entire human beta-globin gene cluster. Homologous recombination in E. coli DH10B cells [15] was used for generation of the constructs. To determine the effect of deletion of 5'HS5 on the three dimensional (3D) organization of the locus, 5'HS5 was flanked by loxP sites to enable Cre-mediated deletion of 5'HS5 (PACDelta1B). The basal promoter and 5' untranslated regions of the beta-globin gene, from -139 to +49 relative to the cap site of the gene, has been removed to generate the beta-globin promoter deletion (PAC3K; Fig. 1). Intactness of the transgenes was carefully mapped by Southern blot analysis using a total of 11 different restriction enzymes and hybridization with cosmid LCRepsilon, -gammagammadeltabeta probes [16], and smaller probes along the beta-globin locus [11].

###xml 1318 1321 1287 1290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Tolhuis1">[6]</xref>

###xml 52 56 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Previous studies on human beta-globin transgenes in mice have shown that the beta-globin gene interacts with the ACH when it is transcribed in definitive erythroid cells [7]. In these studies, the Chromosome Conformation Capture (3C) technique [6], [7], [17] was used to examine the three dimensional organization of the beta-globin locus. First, we determined whether introduction of the beta-marked (betam) gene, which is an artificially modified beta-globin gene with part of its exon 2 replaced by equivalent Agamma-globin gene [9] (Fig. 1B), and flanking 5'HS5 by loxP sites per se caused conformational changes of the beta-globin locus. We performed 3C analysis on the PAC transgenes and compared this directly to the results obtained with the wild type locus (PAC8.1; Fig. 2). In the wild type locus, 5'HS1-5 of the LCR and the HS in the 3' flanking region (3'HS1) interact with each other to form the ACH of the beta-globin locus. The results of 3C analysis on the PAC1B transgene show that the spatial structure of the locus is basically unaltered when compared with that of wild type PAC8.1. In brain, a non-erythroid tissue, the PAC transgene shows an essentially linear conformation of the beta-globin locus (data not shown). This is consistent with the notion that the formation of ACH is tissue specific [6].

###xml 64 75 64 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>
###xml 201 205 194 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Imam1">[15]</xref>
To study the effect of 5'HS5 on the spatial organization of the beta-globin locus, we compared the PAC1B line with the PACDelta1B line, in which 5'HS5 had been deleted by the action of Cre recombinase [15].
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 56 72 56 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-like globin</italic>
###xml 132 135 129 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Levings1">[5]</xref>
###xml 495 498 492 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Tolhuis1">[6]</xref>
###xml 500 503 497 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Palstra1">[7]</xref>
###xml 505 509 502 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Wijgerde1">[18]</xref>
It has been proposed that transcriptional activation of beta-like globin genes at each stage of development is a multi-step process [5]. An LCR holocomplex is formed to allow the accessibility of transcription and chromatin remodeling factors to the locus, thus providing a high local concentration of the relevant trans-acting factors for efficient transcription. The resultant functional active chromatin hub (ACH) comprises the LCR holocomplex interacting directly with the transcribed genes [6], [7], [18]. This model implies that maintaining the integrity of the ACH is the key to create a chromatin domain permissive for transcriptional regulation.
###end p 19
###begin p 20
###xml 234 240 230 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002134-g003">Fig. 3</xref>
###xml 287 298 283 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>
###xml 495 499 484 488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Bulger2">[19]</xref>
###xml 510 521 499 507 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>
###xml 570 577 556 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002134-g003">Fig. 3B</xref>
To examine the possible consequences of deletion of 5'HS5 on ACH formation, we compared the 3D structures of the PAC1B and PACDelta1B transgenes in definitive erythroid cells isolated from 14.5 dpc fetal livers with the 3C technique (Fig. 3). The results show that the overall 3D of the beta-globin locus has remained the same when 5'HS5 is deleted (PACDelta1B). However, the relative crosslinking frequency of the 5'HS6, the distal hypersensitive site located about 6 kb 5' upstream to the HS5 [19], with the beta-globin gene is increased by upon the removal of 5'HS5 (Fig. 3B, primer 418). This increase in association frequency with the ACH is presumably due to the closer proximity of 5'HS6 to the LCR after Cre-mediated excision of 5'HS5. 5'HS6 is located 800bp closer to the LCR in the PACDelta1B line. Besides, in the absent of HS5, the beta-gene promoter interacts strongly with the A-gamma promoter.
###end p 20

###begin p 23
###xml 245 249 232 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Strouboulis1">[16]</xref>
###xml 620 623 590 593 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Hanscombe1">[8]</xref>
###xml 625 628 595 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Dillon1">[9]</xref>
###xml 630 634 600 604 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Grosveld2">[20]</xref>
###xml 801 804 771 774 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Tolhuis1">[6]</xref>
###xml 806 809 776 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Palstra1">[7]</xref>
###xml 811 815 781 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Gribnau1">[21]</xref>
###xml 966 970 933 937 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Wai1">[11]</xref>
###xml 82 97 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
###xml 111 116 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 191 196 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 846 851 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In mammals, two developmental switches of hematopoiesis occur during ontogeny. In transgenic mice carrying the human beta-globin locus, yolk sac-derived primitive erythroid cells express the human embryonic epsilon- and fetal gamma-globin genes [16]. beta-like globin gene expression switches to the fetal gamma- and adult delta- and beta-globin genes during early definitive erythropoiesis between E12.5 and E14.5. The LCR plays an important role in the regulation of expression of the beta-like globins, and the order and distance of the genes relative to the LCR are important parameters for the developmental switch [8], [9], [20]. These developmental switches of hematopoiesis are accompanied by changes in chromatin structure and spatial organization of regulatory elements throughout the locus [6], [7], [21]. In a previous study with the human beta-globin PAC transgenes we have shown that 5'HS5 functions as an enhancer blocker in embryonic erythroid cells [11]. Therefore, it is interesting to determine the possible conformational changes of the locus without 5'HS5 in embryonic erythroid cells.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 261 266 258 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;m</italic>
###xml 959 962 935 938 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Palstra1">[7]</xref>
###xml 964 968 940 944 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Wijgerde1">[18]</xref>
###xml 970 974 946 950 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Gribnau2">[22]</xref>
###xml 1017 1022 993 998 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1024 1028 1000 1004 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Patrinos1">[23]</xref>
PAC3K transgenic lines, in which the promoter of the beta-globin gene in the normal position is deleted, have been used for the 3C analysis. The rationale behind the usage of the PAC3K construct for performing the 3C analysis is that in this configuration, the betam gene located upstream of the LCR is maximally activated in definitive cells, while expression in primitive cells is only detectable after deletion of 5'HS5. We therefore compared the locus-wide crosslinking frequencies of the PAC3K and PACDelta3K (PAC construct with deletion of 5'HS5) transgenes at different developmental stages. In the fetal liver, we observed that the 3D conformation of the PAC3K and PACDelta3K transgenes is virtually identical to that observed for the PAC1B and PACDelta1B transgenes (data not shown). This implies that neither 5'HS5 nor the beta-globin promoter is required for structural changes in the ACH occurring during the process of beta-globin gene switching [7], [18], [22]. The latter is in agreement with Patrinos et al. [23] who have shown that deletion of the beta-globin promoter alone is not sufficient to alter the 3D configuration of the ACH.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 57 61 57 61 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Wai1">[11]</xref>
###xml 214 218 210 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Tuan1">[24]</xref>
###xml 325 331 321 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002134-g004">Fig. 4</xref>
###xml 651 663 639 647 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#947;-globin</italic>
###xml 685 696 669 677 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>
###xml 721 727 702 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002134-g004">Fig. 4</xref>
Since 5'HS5 functions as a border at the primitive stage [11], we next examined the spatial organization of the PAC3K and PACDelta3K transgenes in embryonic blood samples. The restriction fragment containing 5'HS2 [24], a classical enhancer element within the LCR, was used as the fixed fragment for the results presented in Fig. 4. The analysis shows that the spatial organization of the locus is basically unchanged upon deletion of 5'HS5 (PACDelta3K) when compared with the PAC3K transgenic line. In agreement with the notion that the gamma-globin gene is predominantly expressed in primitive cells, higher relative crosslinking frequencies of the gamma-globin gene compared to the beta-globin gene could be observed (Fig. 4). The results imply that in primitive erythroid cells association of the actively transcribed gamma-globin genes with the ACH principally remains the same in the absence of 5'HS5.

For investigating the spatial organization of the chromatin hub (CH), which can be observed in the erythroid progenitor cells without beta-like globin gene expression [7], we used the beta-globin gene as the fixed primer in 3C analysis (Fig. 5). ###xml 570 574 567 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Chung1">[28]</xref>
###xml 576 580 573 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Chung2">[29]</xref>
###xml 30 35 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 539 546 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
We have shown previously that human 5'HS5 has an enhancer blocking function at the primitive stage [11]. However, the mechanism by which this function is achieved remains unknown. Although a number of enhancer blocking proteins have been identified in Drosophila[25]-[27], CTCF is the only protein known so far to mediate enhancer blocking activity in vertebrates [28], [29]. Previous studies conducted by Felsenfeld and his colleagues have described a 1.2kb DNA element with strong enhancer blocking activity, located at the 5'HS4 of the chicken beta-globin gene locus [28], [29].
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 209 213 209 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Bell1">[30]</xref>
###xml 285 290 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 295 300 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
A 250bp core fragment of the cHS4 is fully functional and a single binding site for the protein CTCF, FII, is necessary and sufficient to block the action of an enhancer on a promoter when placed between them [30]. Conserved CTCF binding sites are found at 5'HS5 and 3'HS1 of both the mouse and human loci.
###end p 33

To investigate whether CTCF binds to 5'HS5, ChIP analysis using an antibody to CTCF was performed (Fig. 6). ###begin title 38
Discussion
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 243 246 237 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Palstra1">[7]</xref>
###xml 379 383 365 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Imam1">[15]</xref>
###xml 893 897 863 867 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Patrinos1">[23]</xref>
###xml 109 114 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 221 226 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 559 564 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In this study, we have analyzed the role of 5'HS5 with respect to the three dimensional configuration of the human beta-globin locus. The transgenic lines carried PAC constructs that were derived from the original 185 kb human beta-globin PAC [7]. Transgenic lines carrying single copies of four modified PAC constructs, namely PAC1B, PACDelta1B, PAC3K and PACDelta3K, were used [15] to determine possible conformational changes of the locus after deletion of the 5'HS5 core region. The 3D conformation of the transgenic loci was determined by 3C analysis. A human beta-globin promoter deletion in the context of the full LCR was used to determine whether or not the interaction between the beta-globin gene and the LCR would be perturbed when both 5'HS5 and the beta-globin promoter (PACDelta3K) were deleted, as was previously found when both 5'HS3 and the beta-globin promoter were deleted [23].
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 526 529 513 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Dillon1">[9]</xref>
###xml 900 904 881 885 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Wai1">[11]</xref>
###xml 94 99 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 691 696 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 709 724 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">transgenic mice</span>
We analyzed the conformation of the globin transgenes from two important genomic sites in the human beta-globin locus, namely the beta-globin gene and 5'HS2. We first examined the integrity of the ACH upon the removal of 5'HS5 in the PACDelta1B transgenic line at the definitive stage. The results show that the three dimensional structure of the beta-globin locus has essentially remained the same when 5'HS5 is deleted. Mild increments of the association frequency of 5'HS6 to the LCR could be explained by distance effects [9]: 5'HS6 interacts more frequently with the ACH once it moves closer to the LCR after Cre-mediated excision of 5'HS5. This is consistent with our previous data on human beta-globin transgenic mice which showed that the increase in expression of the betam gene, which is located 5' upstream of the LCR, is likely due to the shorter distance of the gene relative to the LCR [11].
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 182 186 179 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Wai1">[11]</xref>
###xml 26 31 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our previous study on the human beta-globin PAC transgenes has shown that 5'HS5 is a stage-specific enhancer blocking element that functions at the primitive stage of erythropoiesis [11]. Surprisingly, we did not observe any significant alterations in the structure of the beta-globin locus by 3C analysis primitive embryonic blood when 5'HS5 was deleted. The results obtained from both the definitive and primitive stages imply that spatial organization of the locus is essentially unchanged upon deletion of 5'HS5 (PACDelta1B) or in the double deletion of HS5 and the beta-globin promoter (PACDelta3K). We can not completely discount the possible influence of 5'HS5 on beta-globin locus configuration as this might not be detectable due to limitations of the resolution achieved with the 3C technique. However, we can conclude that the enhancer blocking properties of 5'HS5 in primitive erythroid cells is not accompanied by major effects on the spatial organization of the beta-globin locus.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 107 111 107 111 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Chung1">[28]</xref>
###xml 143 147 143 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Farrell1">[13]</xref>
###xml 149 153 149 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Bulger1">[14]</xref>
###xml 268 275 268 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 566 572 566 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 572 576 572 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Filippova1">[31]</xref>
###xml 640 645 640 645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c-Myc</italic>
###xml 791 795 788 792 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Vostrov1">[32]</xref>
###xml 1157 1166 1148 1157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0002134.s001">Figure S1</xref>
###xml 1324 1329 1312 1317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1338 1342 1326 1330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Splinter1">[33]</xref>
###xml 1631 1635 1616 1620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Splinter1">[33]</xref>
###xml 1926 1930 1908 1912 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Yusufzai1">[34]</xref>
###xml 1488 1493 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1738 1743 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chick</span>
Interestingly this study shows that CTCF, the first transcription factor found to have insulating activity [28] and enhancer blocking function [13], [14] in mammalian cells, binds to the 5'HS5 core region at both the primitive and definitive stages in erythroid cells in vivo. This indicates that CTCF could be involved in the border function of 5'HS5 in the primitive cells. Since it also binds to the 5'HS5 region in definitive cells, this suggests that CTCF, most likely in conjunction with other proteins, can have opposing effects on gene expression. Filippova et al.[31] proposed that CTCF could be a transcriptional repressor of the c-Myc oncogene in vertebrates. In contrast, the CTCF protein can bind to the promoter of the amyloid beta protein precursor and activate transcription [32]. Thus, CTCF could be a "dual functionality" protein that has divergent effects on different gene regulation systems. In addition, we show that CTCF binds to the betam gene in both primitive and definitive stage. Three putative CTCF binding sites can be identified at -133 to -149, -1005 to -990 and -1396 to -1412 bp relative to the cap site of the betam gene (Figure S1). Collectively, our results indicate that CTCF may have a more general role in the regulation of the beta-globin locus than previously anticipated. Splinter et al. (2006) [33] have recently shown that in CTCF null cells, there were reduced DNA-DNA interaction frequencies between the sites that normally bind CTCF in the mouse beta-globin gene locus. Interestingly, no changes were seen in the interaction of the surrounding olfactory genes (MOR5B1-3 and MOR3B1-4 [33]), or the expression level of the beta-globin genes. It has been proposed previously that CTCF and the chick HS4 insulator sequence are highly concentrated in Matrix Attachment Regions (MARs) which create separate "loop" domains that prevent the interaction between an enhancer and promoter [34]. Collectively, these data suggested that CTCF participates in more than one pathway in the regulation of the globin genes.

###begin title 48
Preparation of 3C templates
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 41 47 41 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 55 58 55 58 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Tolhuis1">[6]</xref>
###xml 64 65 64 65 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sup>
The 3C protocol was adapted from Tolhuis et al. (2002) [6]. 1x107 cells was defined as 1 unit of sample in a 10ml crosslinking reaction (2% formaldehyde in DMEM with 10% FCS). The samples were cross-linked for 10 minutes at room temperature with gentle mixing. The reaction was quenched by the addition of glycine to 0.125M. Nuclei were harvested by lysis of the cells in 5ml ice-cold lysis buffer (10mM Tris-Cl, 10mM NaCl, 0.2% NP-40 pH8.0) containing protease inhibitors (pefabloc, Roche) for 10 minutes on ice. Nuclei were resuspended in 0.5ml 1x HindIII restriction buffer (Roche) containing 0.3% SDS and incubated for 1 hour at 37degreesC with agitation. Triton X-100 was added to 2% (v/v), and the nuclei were further incubated for 1 hour at 37degreesC to sequester the SDS. The crosslinked DNA was digested overnight at 37degreesC with 400U HindIII. The restriction enzyme was inactivated by the addition of SDS to 1.6% and incubated at 65degreesC for 20 minutes with agitation. The reaction was diluted with 7ml 1x ligation buffer (Promega) and incubated for 1 hour at 37degreesC. The chromatin was ligated with 100U T4 DNA ligase (Promega) for 4 hours at 16degreesC followed by 30 minutes at room temperature. 600 microg of Proteinase K was added, and samples were incubated overnight at 65degreesC to reverse the cross-links. Next, the samples were incubated for 30 minutes at 37degreesC with 1500 microg of DNase-free RNaseA, and the DNA was purified by phenol extraction and ethanol precipitation. The DNA pellet was resuspended in 150 microl 10 mM Tris-Cl (pH7.5).
###end p 49

###xml 88 92 88 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Imam1">[15]</xref>

###xml 228 231 225 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0002134-Tolhuis1">[6]</xref>

###xml 531 538 528 535 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0002134-t001">Table 1</xref>

PCR products were run on a 2% agarose gel and quantified with a Typhoon 9200 imager (Amersham). Typically 300ng of DNA was subsequently used for each 3C-PCR reaction. Each PCR reaction was performed in triplicate and repeated at least three times. Sequences of the 3C PCR primers are shown in Table 1.

###begin title 54
Anti-CTCF ChIP assays
###end title 54
###begin p 55
###xml 4 5 4 5 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sup>
###xml 1586 1597 1561 1569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-globin</italic>
###xml 1652 1658 1624 1630 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Necdin</italic>
###xml 1766 1772 1738 1744 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Necdin</italic>
###xml 1892 1899 1864 1871 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0002134-t002">Table 2</xref>
###xml 1954 1961 1923 1930 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0002134-g001">Fig. 1B</xref>
###xml 1612 1617 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1646 1651 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
1x107 cells were defined as 1 unit of sample for 10ml ChIP crosslinking reaction (2% formaldehyde and 40mM HEPES pH7.9 in DMEM with 10% FCS). The samples were crosslinked for 20 minutes at room temperature with gentle mixing. The reaction was quenched by the addition of glycine to 0.125M. The cells were harvested by centrifugation at 1500rpm for 5 minutes at 4degreesC. The cells were first washed with PBS buffer containing protease inhibitors (1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF), 1 microg/ml aprotinin and 1 microg/ml pepstatin A) and centrifuged at 1500rpm for 5 minutes. 10 ml of Triton-wash buffer (0.25% Triton-X 100, 10mM Na-EDTA, 0.5 mM Na-EGTA and 10 mM Tris-Cl pH8.0) was added and the samples were incubated at room temperature for 10 minutes. The cells were pelleted by centrifugation at 1500rpm for 5 minutes and they were further washed with 10ml NaCl-wash (200mM NaCl, 1 mM Na-EDTA, 0.5mM Na-EGTA and 10mM Tris-Cl pH8.0) buffer at room temperature for 10 minutes. After centrifugation at 1500rpm for 5 minutes, the pellet was resuspended in 500 microl sonication buffer (1mM Na-EDTA, 0.5mM Na-EGTA and 10mM Tris-Cl pH8.0) and kept on ice for 5 minutes. DNA was sheared by sonication to lengths between 200 and 1000bp, keeping the sample cold on ice. Cell debris was removed by centrifugation. ChIP reactions were performed according to the Upstate Biotechnology chromatin immunoprecipitation protocol with 1 microg of anti-CTCF antibody (Upstate Biotechnology) per IP reaction. Enrichment of specific immunoprecipitated sequences, including 5'HS5, betam (marked beta-globin gene), mHS62 (mouse HS62) and a fragment of the mouse Necdin gene (control), were determined by real-time PCR. Fold enrichment was calculated by comparing the ratio to Necdin in pull-down-eluate against the value from input-starting material. The sequences of the ChIP PCR primers are shown in Table 2. The location of the betam gene primers were shown in Fig. 1B.
