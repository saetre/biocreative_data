Mutations in genes involved in nonsense mediated decay ameliorate the phenotype of sel-12 mutants with amber stop mutations in Caenorhabditis elegans

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 326 356 326 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caenorhabditis elegans sel-12 </italic>
###xml 458 464 458 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 498 502 498 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
###xml 513 514 513 514 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</underline>
###xml 527 529 527 529 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pr</underline>
###xml 570 576 570 576 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12</italic>
###xml 603 607 603 607 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
###xml 622 629 622 629 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 703 709 703 709 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hop-1 </italic>
###xml 756 762 756 762 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 776 783 776 783 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 236 241 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 326 348 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
Presenilin proteins are part of a complex of proteins that can cleave many type I transmembrane proteins, including Notch Receptors and the Amyloid Precursor Protein, in the middle of the transmembrane domain. Dominant mutations in the human presenilin genes PS1 and PS2 lead to Familial Alzheimer's disease. Mutations in the Caenorhabditis elegans sel-12 presenilin gene cause a highly penetrant egg-laying defect due to reduction of signalling through the lin-12/Notch receptor. Mutations in six spr genes (for suppressor of presenilin) are known to strongly suppress sel-12. Mutations in most strong spr genes suppress sel-12 by de-repressing the transcription of the largely functionally equivalent hop-1 presenilin gene. However, how mutations in the spr-2 gene suppress sel-12 is unknown.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 13 19 13 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 52 59 52 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 172 179 172 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 279 285 279 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 341 345 341 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 392 404 392 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 454 461 454 461 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 525 532 525 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 535 539 535 539 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 613 617 613 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
###xml 637 642 637 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2</italic>
###xml 707 711 707 711 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 741 745 741 745 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
We show that spr-2 mutations increase the levels of sel-12 transcripts with Premature translation Termination Codons (PTCs) in embryos and L1 larvae. mRNA transcripts from sel-12 alleles with PTCs undergo degradation by a process known as Nonsense Mediated Decay (NMD). However, spr-2 mutations do not appear to affect NMD. Mutations in the smg genes, which are required for NMD, can restore sel-12(PTC) transcript levels and ameliorate the phenotype of sel-12 mutants with amber PTCs. However, the phenotypic suppression of sel-12 by smg genes is nowhere near as strong as the effect of previously characterized spr mutations including spr-2. Consistent with this, we have identified only two mutations in smg genes among the more than 100 spr mutations recovered in genetic screens.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 0 6 0 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 32 39 32 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 59 71 59 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 163 167 163 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 205 212 205 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 271 285 271 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(amber) </italic>
###xml 303 307 303 307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
spr-2 mutations do not suppress sel-12 by affecting NMD of sel-12(PTC) transcripts and appear to have a novel mechanism of suppression. The fact that mutations in smg genes can ameliorate the phenotype of sel-12 alleles with amber PTCs suggests that some read-through of sel-12(amber) alleles occurs in smg backgrounds.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 140 141 140 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 477 478 474 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 425 433 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 514 519 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Presenilin proteins are part of a complex of proteins that can cleave many type I transmembrane proteins with short extra-cellular domains [1]. Two of the best characterized substrates of the presenilin complex are the amyloid precursor protein (APP) and Notch-type receptors. Presenilin activity is necessary for the generation of beta-amyloid, the major constituent of the senile plaques found post-mortem in the brains of patients who had suffered from Alzheimer's disease [1]. Mutations in APP, and in the two human presenilin genes PS1 and PS2, dominantly cause early onset familial Alzheimer's disease. Presenilins are also absolutely required for the signalling through Notch receptors and in all animals examined, the complete loss of presenilin activity is lethal due to developmental defects arising from the absence of Notch signalling.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 3 26 3 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caenorhabditis elegans </italic>
###xml 73 79 73 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spe-4 </italic>
###xml 168 169 168 169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 170 171 170 171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 206 213 206 213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 217 222 217 222 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hop-1</italic>
###xml 348 349 348 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 350 351 350 351 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 372 377 372 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hop-1</italic>
###xml 411 412 411 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 413 414 413 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 422 429 422 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 476 477 476 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 478 479 478 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 480 481 480 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 484 498 484 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hop-1; sel-12 </italic>
###xml 606 607 606 607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 608 609 608 609 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 629 636 629 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 669 675 669 675 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12</italic>
###xml 761 768 761 768 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 912 913 911 912 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 1303 1304 1302 1303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 1307 1314 1306 1313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 1504 1511 1503 1510 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 1614 1621 1613 1620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 1685 1686 1684 1685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 3 25 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
In Caenorhabditis elegans there are three presenilin genes. Mutations in spe-4 lead to sterility and this gene appears to have a very specific role in spermatogenesis [2,3]. The two other presenilin genes, sel-12 and hop-1, each have broader, and partially redundant roles throughout the organism and are both required for proper Notch signalling [4-8]. In the absence of hop-1, animals have reduced fertility [5,6] while sel-12 mutants show a defect in egg-laying behaviour [4,7,8]. hop-1; sel-12 double mutants have a lethal phenotype and show defects in all known LIN-12 and GLP-1 signalling decisions [5,6]. The phenotype of sel-12 mutants is due to defects in two lin-12/Notch-dependent developmental decisions that occur in the mid/late larval stages. In sel-12 animals there is often a defect in determination of the pi cell fate and in the development of the connection between the vulva and the uterus [7]. In these animals the passage way between the uterus and the vulva remains blocked by thick tissue, which everts in the early adult stage giving rise in the adult stage to a protruding vulva phenotype (Pvl). However, this Pvl phenotype, which can easily be seen in a dissecting microscope, is only partially penetrant and even in the strongest alleles only 3/4 of all animals display it [8]. sel-12 animals also display a highly penetrant defect in the orientation of the vulval muscles that open the vulva to allow egg-laying. The muscle defects vary from animal to animal, but in strong sel-12 alleles no animals show a completely normal pattern of sex muscle orientation and consequently, strong sel-12 alleles display a completely penetrant egg-laying (Egl) defect [8].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 153 159 153 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12</italic>
###xml 172 184 172 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-1-spr-5 </italic>
###xml 188 194 188 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-10</italic>
###xml 244 251 244 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 260 261 260 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 262 264 262 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 324 331 324 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 378 384 378 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-3 </italic>
###xml 403 405 403 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 422 426 422 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
###xml 472 479 472 479 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
In order to find out more about the biological roles of presenilins, we and others have done several screens for suppressors of the egg-laying defect of sel-12. Six genes (spr-1-spr-5 and sel-10) have been identified that strongly suppress the sel-12 defects [9-14]. In the strongest suppressor mutations, the penetrance of sel-12 defects is reduced to less than 10% and in the spr-3 gene to almost 0% [13]. However, many spr mutations, especially those that suppress the sel-12 phenotype more weakly, remain uncharacterized.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 24 31 24 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 48 61 48 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-3, spr-4 </italic>
###xml 65 71 65 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-5 </italic>
###xml 126 132 126 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hop-1 </italic>
###xml 170 177 170 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 235 237 235 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 238 240 238 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 357 359 357 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 360 362 360 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 470 472 470 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 568 574 568 574 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hop-1 </italic>
###xml 589 591 589 591 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 645 651 645 651 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-10</italic>
###xml 728 730 728 730 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 802 809 802 809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-10 </italic>
###xml 939 940 939 940 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 941 943 941 943 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 944 946 944 946 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 954 961 954 961 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-10 </italic>
###xml 981 988 981 988 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 440 445 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Mutations in the strong sel-12 suppressor genes spr-3, spr-4 and spr-5 have been shown to de-repress the transcription of the hop-1 presenilin gene and probably suppress sel-12 by replacing the activity of one presenilin with another [11,13]. SPR-1, SPR-3, SPR-4 and SPR-5 resemble components of the mammalian REST-CoREST transcriptional repressor complex [11-13]. Furthermore, SPR-1 physically interacts with SPR-5 in GST pull down and in yeast two hybrid experiments [11]. This suggests that SPR-1 probably functions together with SPR-3, SPR-4 and SPR-5 to regulate hop-1 transcription [14]. An additional suppression mechanism is provided by sel-10. SEL-10 is an F-box protein that is part of an E3 ubiquitin ligase complex [15] that targets proteins for degradation by the proteosome. Mutations in sel-10 result in the stabilization of its target proteins which include the presenilins and the intracellular domain of Notch receptors [9,16-20]. Thus sel-10 probably suppresses sel-12 by either increasing HOP-1 protein levels, by increasing the half-life of LIN-12 intracellular fragment, or by both.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 9 15 9 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 29 33 29 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
###xml 70 77 70 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 115 117 115 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 127 134 127 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-10 </italic>
###xml 158 164 158 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 191 198 191 198 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 209 211 209 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 261 281 261 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-1, spr-3, spr-4 </italic>
###xml 285 290 285 290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-5</italic>
###xml 292 298 292 298 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 365 384 365 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hop-1;spr-2;sel-12 </italic>
###xml 421 434 421 434 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hop-1;sel-12 </italic>
###xml 449 451 449 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 554 556 554 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 805 807 805 807 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 808 810 808 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 847 853 847 853 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 876 882 876 882 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12</italic>
###xml 892 898 892 898 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 964 966 964 966 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 518 523 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Although spr-2 was the first spr gene to be cloned, how it suppresses sel-12 on a molecular level remains unknown [10]. Unlike sel-10 mutations, mutations in spr-2 have no obvious effects on lin-12 signaling [10]. However, genetic evidence indicates that, like spr-1, spr-3, spr-4 and spr-5, spr-2 does not bypass the requirement for presenilin activity, because a hop-1;spr-2;sel-12 triple mutant is as unviable as is a hop-1;sel-12 double mutant [10]. SPR-2 is a Nucleosome Assembly Protein (NAP) orthologous to the human SET/Taf-1beta/INHAT oncogene [10]. SET has been implicated in a wide range of processes including facilitating transcription, transcriptional repression, inhibition of Protein Phosphatase 2A, regulation of the cell cycle and the membrane recruitment of Rac1 during cell migration [21-26]. However, apart from the fact that spr-2 mutations can suppress sel-12, all the spr-2 mutants isolated so far are viable and have no strong phenotypes [10].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 0 1 0 1 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">N</underline>
###xml 9 10 9 10 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</underline>
###xml 18 19 18 19 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</underline>
###xml 186 188 186 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 189 191 189 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 197 208 197 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans </italic>
###xml 214 218 214 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 219 220 219 220 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</underline>
###xml 245 246 245 246 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</underline>
###xml 270 271 270 271 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g</underline>
###xml 338 340 338 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 341 343 341 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 502 509 502 509 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smgl-1 </italic>
###xml 513 520 513 520 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smgl-2 </italic>
###xml 525 529 525 529 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 542 544 542 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 197 207 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
Nonsense mediated decay (NMD) is a normal cellular process in eukaryotes in which messenger RNAs containing Premature translation Termination Codons (PTCs) are targeted for degradation [27-29]. In C. elegans seven smg (suppressor mutations with morphological effects on genitalia) genes were identified and found to be essential for NMD [30-32]. More recently, two additional genes that play a role in NMD were identified but reduction of function of these genes is lethal so they were given the names smgl-1 and smgl-2 (for smg and lethal) [33].
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 99 101 99 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 102 104 102 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 151 153 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 232 245 232 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-5, smg-6 </italic>
###xml 249 255 249 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-7 </italic>
###xml 297 303 297 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-2 </italic>
###xml 472 474 472 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 535 537 535 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 538 540 538 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 614 616 614 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>

###xml 1803 1809 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Cycles of phosphorylation and de-phosphorylation of the protein SMG-2, the ortholog of yeast UPF1 [34,35], plays a central role in the process of NMD [36]. In the nematode, SMG-1, SMG-3 and SMG-4 act to phosphorylate SMG-2, whereas smg-5, smg-6 and smg-7 are required for SMG-2 dephosphorylation. smg-2 encodes an ATPase/helicase that can bind to mRNAs containing PTCs with greater affinity than those without. This process is enhanced by the presence of SMG-3 and SMG-4 [37], which are known as UPF2 and UPF3, respectively, in yeast [38,39]. SMG-1, a conserved Phosphatidylinositol Kinase-Related Protein Kinase [36], then phosphorylates SMG-2 in a SMG-3- and SMG-4-dependent manner [40]. smg-5, smg-6 and smg-7 encode related proteins all containing an N-terminal 14-3-3 like domain [41], and are thought to recognize phosphorylated SMG-2 and trigger its dephosphorylation by recruiting protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A) into a SMG-5-PP2A sub-complex [42-44]. SMG5 and SMG6 proteins further share a C-terminal PINc domain (an RNAse H-related domain), although only SMG6 has conserved the canonical triad of acidic residues that are crucial in RNAse H for nuclease activity [45]. These three smg genes were initially thought to be absent from yeast, however, it has been recently determined that the metazoan SMG6 proteins are orthologous to yeast Ever shorter telomeres 1 (Est1a) [46-48]. Also, Ebs1, a second yeast gene with sequence similarity to Est1, shares functional and molecular similarities to hSMG5/7 (although similarities are greater with hSMG7) [49]. While both yeast Est1 and Ebs1 are involved in telomere maintenance, a role for smg genes in telomere maintenance has not yet been demonstrated in C. elegans. However, other NMD genes do play a role in telomere function in yeast [50] and in humans, where they play a negative role by regulating the association with chromatin of telomeric repeat-containing RNA and by protecting chromosome ends from telomere loss [51].

Here, we present experiments in which we tried to determine better how the strong suppressor gene spr-2 suppresses sel-12. We show that, although spr-2 does not affect hop-1 transcript levels, spr-2 mutations do increase sel-12(PTC) transcript levels in the embryo and in the L1 larvae. This study led us to examine the effects of smg mutations on sel-12 transcript levels and in modulating the sel-12 phenotype. We find that the transcripts of three sel-12 alleles with PTC mutations are subject to NMD and that smg mutations restore nearly normal sel-12 transcript levels. Furthermore, we show that a new weak suppressor mutation we had identified, spr-8(pf52), is an allele of smg-6 and that another mutation identified in a putative weak spr strain, by146, is an allele of smg-1. smg mutations can weakly ameliorate the phenotype of sel-12 alleles with amber stop mutations but do not affect the phenotypes of missense, deletion or opal stop alleles. This suggests that some read through of sel-12(amber) alleles occurs in smg backgrounds. Furthermore, we show that spr-2 does not appear to be affecting NMD and that the suppression of sel-12 by spr-2 can not be explained by the effect of spr-2 mutations on sel-12(amber) transcript levels. Our results suggest that spr-2 suppresses sel-12 by a novel mechanism.

In order to determine if spr-2 functions through a similar mechanism to other spr genes to suppress sel-12, we looked at the effect of mutations in spr-2 on presenilin transcript levels. We find that spr-2 mutations do not increase the amounts of hop-1 or spe-4 transcripts (Gontijo and Lakowski unpublished data). The sel-12 allele tested, ar171, contains a Premature translation Termination Codon (PTC) that is predicted to truncate the SEL-12 protein before the large cytoplasmic loop region (See Figure 1B for scheme). ###begin p 29

###xml 548 554 548 554 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 563 567 563 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
To see if spr-2 mutations had a more general effect on RNA levels, we tested whether spr-2 could influence NMD in several ways. Firstly, we made an unc-54(r293); spr-2(ar199) double-mutant strain. unc-54 codes for a major muscle myosin. In strains containing the r293 allele, the unc-54 transcript is unstable and subject to NMD, yielding paralyzed adult animals. Mutations in any smg gene stabilize unc-54(r293) transcripts so that the animals can move more normally [30]. unc-54;spr-2 double mutants are still strongly paralyzed, suggesting that spr-2 is not a smg gene.
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 28 34 28 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl-12</italic>
###xml 95 97 95 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 308 310 308 310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 326 333 326 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl-12 </italic>
###xml 504 508 504 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 527 534 527 534 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpl-12 </italic>
###xml 669 671 669 671 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
The ribosomal protein gene, rpl-12, is normally alternatively spliced to produce two isoforms [53]. One form is unproductively spliced and contains a piece of an intron with a Premature translation Termination Codon (PTC) and another productively spliced, containing no intronic sequences and no PTC (Figure 2B). Although the rpl-12 PTC transcript is constitutively produced, it is actively degraded by NMD, so that its levels are normally very low with respect to the other isoform. In the absence of a smg gene, however, the rpl-12 PTC transcript accumulates and can be readily detected by reverse transcriptase PCR (RT-PCR), yielding a robust test for Smg activity [53].
###end p 30

So we prepared mixed stage RNA from a spr-2 homozygous strain and tested whether there would be any detectable enrichment of the rpl-12 PTC isoform, but found none (Figure 2C), confirming that spr-2 mutants are proficient in NMD. As the effect of spr-2 on sel-12 transcripts was found only in early stages, it could be that spr-2 affects NMD only in these particular stages of development. However, NMD of rpl-12 happens normally in a spr-2(ar199) strain at all the stages in which we have looked, even in embryos and L1s (data not shown), suggesting no general role for spr-2 in NMD at any developmental stage.

In the process of doing these experiments we discovered that a smg-2 mutation partially ameliorates the phenotype of sel-12(ty11). To see if other smg genes could also ameliorate the sel-12(ty11) phenotype, we made additional smg; sel-12(ty11) double mutants. Indeed, mutations in all smg genes that we tested greatly increased the levels of sel-12(ty11) transcripts (Figure 3A). Most smg; sel-12(ty11) double mutants also appeared to have some amelioration of the sel-12 phenotype. The ability of sel-12 animals to lay eggs can be scored in different ways which we quantified in both a smg-1 and a smg-2 background. More smg; sel-12(ty11) double mutants can lay some eggs than sel-12(ty11) animals (Figure 3B). Furthermore, fewer smg; sel-12(ty11) animals display a strong protruding vulva phenotype (Figure 3C), even though smg mutations often affect vulval morphology. Finally, most sel-12 animals die during the reproductive period due to internal hatching of progeny. These progeny develop further in the mother, feeding off her tissue, leading to a terminal 'bag-of-worms' phenotype where the developing larvae are encased by the empty cuticle of the mother. The internal hatching of progeny severely limits the lifespan of animals. However, in smg; sel-12(ty11) animals, many animals survive the reproductive period (Figure 3D). In smg; sel-12(ty11) animals, the restoration of egg-laying behavior is variable and animals can display severe side-effects seldom seen in spr; sel-12(ty11) or sel-12(ty11) mutants alone, such as exploding during the L4 to adult molt and increased sterility (data not shown). Although animals with these problems were excluded from our analysis, we found that these smg mutations do not obviously increase brood size (Figure 3E). The brood size remained very similar to that of sel-12 with only very modest increases in a few of the smg; sel-12(ty11) double mutants that were not statistically significant (Additional file 2, Expt.1). The phenotypes of smg; sel-12(ty11) strains clearly contrasts with that of the LA259 spr-2(ar199) IV; sel-12(ty11) strain. For all phenotypes examined, smg mutations suppress sel-12(ty11) much more weakly than spr-2(ar199) mutation (Figures 3B, C and 3E).

In our spr screens we have recovered many weaker mutations that allow some sel-12 animals to start laying eggs as young adults. However, most of these animals eventually become Egl and die from internal hatching of progeny. In the process of doing the studies on the effect of smg genes on sel-12 phenotype, we realized that one of the weak sel-12 suppressors that we had been trying to positionally clone mapped near smg-6. The pf52 allele complemented all of the known sel-12 suppressor genes, so we tried to map it genetically. pf52 is linked to the left arm of LGIII, where no other spr genes have been mapped. So we gave pf52 the gene name spr-8. The spr-8(pf52) mutation was mapped by a combination of classical and Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP) mapping (see Methods) about mid way between dpy-1 and daf-2. The SNP mapping data was most consistent with a position between the SNPs pkP3086 and SNP_Y71H2B[2] (Figure 4A). The gene smg-6, which is involved in nonsense mediated decay (NMD), maps to the same genetic interval as spr-8 (Figure 4A). The pf52 mutation was recovered as a suppressor of the amber stop allele ty11. So we tested whether pf52 could be allelic to smg-6. We tested complementation of pf52 to smg-6(r896) for sel-12(ty11) suppression and found that some spr-8(pf52)/smg-6(r896); sel-12(ty11) animals were clearly suppressed in the first heterozygous generation and their progeny appeared to lay more eggs than sel-12(ty11). Moreover, pf52 strongly suppresses the movement defects of unc-54(r293) animals and displays a partial maternal effect for unc-54 suppression (See Methods). Consistent with this, smg-6 is known to have a maternal effect [30,54]. Finally, the PTC form of rpl-12 clearly accumulates in pf52 strains but that pf52 has a weaker defect in NMD than most other smg genes (Figure 4B and data not shown). Interestingly, smg-6(r896) is known not to completely abolish NMD [54], and also shows this same weak effect on rpl-12 PTC transcript accumulation (data not shown).

###begin title 50
Discussion
###end title 50
###begin title 51
###xml 4 8 4 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 21 28 21 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
All smg genes affect sel-12 similarly
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 47 54 47 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 168 180 168 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 232 236 232 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 296 309 296 309 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1, smg-3 </italic>
###xml 313 319 313 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-4 </italic>
###xml 364 377 364 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-5, smg-6 </italic>
###xml 381 387 381 387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-7 </italic>
###xml 434 447 434 447 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-2, smg-5 </italic>
###xml 451 457 451 457 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-6 </italic>
###xml 511 517 511 517 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1 </italic>
###xml 525 527 525 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 568 569 568 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 635 639 635 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 648 660 648 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 682 689 682 689 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 721 725 721 725 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 750 763 750 763 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ty11) </italic>
###xml 795 808 795 808 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ty11) </italic>
In the course of this study we have shown that sel-12 transcripts with premature termination codons are subject to NMD. The loss of nonsense mediated decay can restore sel-12(PTC) transcripts to near wild type levels. The different smg genes have different roles in mediating NMD. In particular, smg-1, smg-3 and smg-4 help to promote SMG-2 phosphorylation, while smg-5, smg-6 and smg-7 promote SMG-2 de-phosphorylation. Furthermore, smg-2, smg-5 and smg-6 have been implicated in the maintenance of RNAi while smg-1 is not [55]. However, in our initial study (Figure 3), we found no strong differences in the effects of the different smg genes on sel-12(PTC) transcript levels and sel-12 suppression. This suggests that smg mutations ameliorate the sel-12(ty11) phenotype simply by stabilizing sel-12(ty11) transcripts.
###end p 52
###begin title 53
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 30 36 30 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12</italic>
smg mutations poorly suppress sel-12
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 57 71 57 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(amber) </italic>
###xml 157 161 157 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
###xml 175 180 175 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2</italic>
###xml 189 193 189 193 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
###xml 230 234 230 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 267 274 267 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 286 290 286 290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
###xml 328 335 328 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 380 384 380 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 402 406 402 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 432 438 432 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12</italic>
###xml 445 462 445 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg; sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 567 579 567 579 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg; sel-12 </italic>
###xml 677 681 677 681 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 729 741 729 741 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ty11)</italic>
###xml 788 801 788 801 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1(by146) </italic>
###xml 821 835 821 835 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ar171) </italic>
###xml 849 853 849 853 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 896 902 896 902 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ar171 </italic>
Genes involved in NMD weakly ameliorate the phenotype of sel-12(amber) mutations. However, this effect is much weaker than the effect of mutations in strong spr genes such as spr-2. In the spr screens, we have identified only two smg mutations as weak suppressors of sel-12 in over 100 spr alleles indicating that screening for sel-12 suppression is an inefficient way to recover smg alleles. Although smg mutations weakly suppress sel-12, most smg; sel-12(PTC) animals still become Egl and die of internal hatching of progeny. Thus at any time, only a proportion of smg; sel-12 animals lay eggs and can be isolated for this phenotype. This may explain the relative paucity of smg alleles recovered in screens for suppressors of sel-12(ty11). Furthermore, the absence of clear effects of smg-1(by146) on the phenotype of sel-12(ar171) suggests that smg should only be very poorly selected for in ar171 suppressor screens.
###end p 54
###begin title 55
###xml 4 10 4 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">by146 </italic>
The by146 mutation was recovered serendipitously
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 15 21 15 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">by146 </italic>
###xml 55 88 55 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">by146; sel-12(ar171) unc-1(e538) </italic>

###xml 551 558 551 558 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 581 587 581 587 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ar171 </italic>
###xml 596 598 596 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6A</xref>
###xml 619 620 619 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 732 738 732 738 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ar171 </italic>
###xml 754 756 754 756 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6B</xref>
###xml 777 778 777 778 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 829 841 829 841 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">unc-1(e538) </italic>
###xml 972 986 972 986 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ar171) </italic>
###xml 1015 1028 1015 1028 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1(by146) </italic>
###xml 1107 1113 1107 1113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">by146 </italic>
###xml 1176 1182 1176 1182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1 </italic>
###xml 1266 1279 1266 1279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ar171)</italic>
###xml 1327 1340 1327 1340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1(by146) </italic>
###xml 1372 1385 1372 1385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ty11) </italic>
###xml 1389 1402 1389 1402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(by125)</italic>
###xml 1448 1461 1448 1461 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1(by146) </italic>
###xml 1510 1516 1510 1516 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12</italic>
###xml 1574 1581 1574 1581 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al. </italic>
###xml 1594 1608 1594 1608 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ar171) </italic>
###xml 1634 1646 1634 1646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1(r861) </italic>
###xml 1647 1649 1647 1649 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 1789 1793 1789 1793 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
Curiously, the by146 allele was isolated in the BR2097 by146; sel-12(ar171) unc-1(e538) strain. However, our results show that by146 does not obviously suppress sel-12(ar171), so it is unclear why it was recovered in a sel-12(ar171) background. The BR2097 strain was frozen soon after it was isolated and was not examined further until this study. Not all of our putative weak suppressor alleles have been characterised carefully, so we re-examined the phenotype of BR2097. BR2097 animals have a similar proportion of egg laying animals to all of the sel-12 null alleles including ar171 (Figures 6A and Additional file 2 experiment 6). Furthermore, the brood size of BR2097 is not elevated and is in fact significantly smaller than ar171 itself (Figures 6B and Additional file 2 experiment 6), an effect that might be due to the unc-1(e538) background mutation. These results indicate that the BR2097 strain does not appear to have a suppressor mutation that can suppress sel-12(ar171) and that the presence of the smg-1(by146) mutation could not have been selected for. This suggests that the isolation of by146 could have been due to the fortuitous presence of a background smg-1 mutation in the BR2097 strain that did not strongly affect the Egg-laying defect of sel-12(ar171). However, our subsequent studies indicate that smg-1(by146) can ameliorate the phenotype of sel-12(ty11) and sel-12(by125). Thus all of our results are consistent with smg-1(by146) being a weak amber allele specific suppressor of sel-12. We note that our results conflict with those of Levitan et al. who saw that sel-12(ar171) is somewhat suppressed by smg-1(r861) [56]. However, no data was provided to support this claim and this may highlight the necessity of quantifying the suppressor phenotype of weak spr mutations.

###begin title 57
###xml 4 9 4 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pf52 </italic>
The pf52 mutation suggests an important function for the PINc domain of SMG-6
###end title 57
###begin p 58
###xml 14 19 14 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pf52 </italic>
###xml 203 205 203 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 361 363 361 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 364 366 364 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 487 489 487 489 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 490 492 490 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 575 577 575 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 668 673 668 673 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pf52 </italic>
###xml 822 827 822 827 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pf52 </italic>
###xml 895 908 895 908 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1(by146) </italic>
###xml 920 922 920 922 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4B</xref>
###xml 945 951 945 951 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-6 </italic>
###xml 989 999 989 999 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 1026 1031 1026 1031 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pf52 </italic>
###xml 1127 1133 1127 1133 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-6 </italic>
###xml 1217 1219 1217 1219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 1263 1269 1263 1269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-6 </italic>
###xml 1292 1297 1292 1297 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pf52 </italic>
###xml 1327 1333 1327 1333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-6 </italic>
###xml 256 266 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 271 276 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 989 999 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
The fact that pf52 affects the PINc domain of SMG-6 indicates that this domain has an important function in NMD. The highly conserved PINc domain of SMG-6 proteins was predicted to function as an RNAse [45]. Recently, it has been demonstrated that both in Drosophila and Human cell culture, SMG6 proteins act as RNAses that cleave mRNAs with PTCs near the PTC [57,58]. Furthermore, this activity is dependent on the PINc domain and the three catalytic aspartate residues in this domain [57,58]. There are also several basic residues in the PINc domain that may bind to RNAs [45]. The addition of an acidic amino acid in this region may destabilize RNA binding. So the pf52 mutation could affect the putative RNAse activity of SMG-6 by either disrupting the catalytic core, by affecting RNA binding, or both. The fact that pf52 does not eliminate NMD as well as alleles of other genes, including smg-1(by146) (see Figure 4B) suggests that either smg-6 is not completely required for NMD in C. elegans, or that alternately, the pf52 allele may be a partial loss of function mutation. However, in a non-complementation screen for smg-6 alleles several alleles were recovered including three homozygous lethal mutations [54]. This suggests that the null phenotype of smg-6 may be lethal and that pf52 does not completely eliminate smg-6 function.

###xml 339 357 339 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg; sel-12(ty11) </italic>

###xml 1169 1182 1169 1182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1(by146) </italic>
###xml 1241 1243 1241 1243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6B</xref>

###xml 1297 1310 1297 1310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg-1(by146) </italic>
###xml 1584 1596 1578 1590 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg; sel-12 </italic>

Although they were not directly compared, the suppression of sel-12(amber) mutations by our alleles, smg-1(by146) and smg-6(pf52) was clearer, especially for brood size (see Figure 6B and Additional file 2 experiments 5 and 6), than with reference alleles ordered from the Caenorhabditis Genetics Center (CGC) (Figure 3E). Indeed, in many smg; sel-12(ty11) strains with smg alleles from the CGC collection we saw a high degree of sterility. The sterility in the smg-2(e2008); sel-12(ty11) strain was so high that we were not able to continue propagating the strain and it was subsequently lost. Our alleles have been propagated for a limited number of generations while the reference alleles of the smg genes were isolated over 20 years ago [30]. Our data also suggest that pf52, the allele with a weak effect on NMD (Figure 4B and Additional file 1), may suppress sel-12 better than the by146 allele which has completely lost NMD (Figures 4B, 6B and Additional file 2). All of this suggests that there might be negative effects on fertility of eliminating NMD and maintaining strains without NMD for many generations. Consistent with this, our results show that LA920 smg-1(by146) has a clearly reduced brood size as compared to N2 (Figure 6B and Additional file 2). Additionally, when the LA920 smg-1(by146) strain was cultured at the stressful temperature of 25degreesC for several generations, we saw that some animals had greatly reduced number of progeny (Lakowski data not shown), suggesting that some epigenetic effect may underlie the severely reduced fertility seen in some smg; sel-12 animals. As genes involved in NMD have been implicated in telomere maintenance in other systems, it would be interesting to investigate whether telomere length or sub-telomeric silencing is affected by maintaining strains without NMD for many generations in C. elegans.

###begin title 61
###xml 19 26 19 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 29 33 29 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
The suppression of sel-12 by smg mutations may involve translational read through
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 22 26 22 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 61 73 61 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 162 169 162 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 362 364 362 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 479 480 475 476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 732 733 720 721 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 930 937 914 921 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 940 944 924 928 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 1233 1240 1217 1224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 1255 1259 1239 1243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 1333 1340 1317 1324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 1362 1368 1346 1352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">by146 </italic>
###xml 1441 1447 1425 1431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ar171 </italic>
###xml 1520 1534 1504 1518 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(amber) </italic>
###xml 1657 1659 1641 1643 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 1971 1981 1955 1965 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 2093 2095 2077 2079 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 2107 2117 2091 2101 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 2252 2264 2236 2248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg; sel-12 </italic>
###xml 1650 1655 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1961 1966 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1971 1981 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 2107 2117 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
It is surprising that smg mutations can sometimes ameliorate sel-12(PTC) phenotypes as these transcripts still retain a stop codon and can not encode full length sel-12 proteins. Presenilin proteins have eight predicted transmembrane (TM) domains and are normally cleaved in the large cytoplasmic loop between TMs 6 and 7 (for a scheme of the protein see Figure 1B) to generate N and C terminal fragments that remain associated in a protein complex known as the gamma-secretase [1]. The gamma-secretase also contains three other proteins known as APH-2/Nicastrin, APH-1 and PEN-1. The gamma-secretase is an unusual protease complex and all four proteins are required for activity and trafficking to the cell surface of the complex [1]. Partial protein fragments of presenilin proteins are not known to retain protease activity, or to be efficiently assembled in to gamma-secretase complexes. This suggests that the suppression of sel-12 by smg mutations could involve translational read through. We did not directly determine SEL-12 protein levels as we had no antibodies that could detect SEL-12 either in Immunoflorescence or on Western blots. However, our results strongly implicate translational read-through in the mechanism of sel-12 suppression by smg mutations. The efficiency of read through may be dependent on the type of sel-12 allele. The fact that by146 ameliorates of the phenotype of both amber stop alleles but not that the ar171 opal stop allele is consistent with a read through mechanism specific to sel-12(amber) alleles. Proteins involved in NMD have been implicated in modulating read-through of transcripts containing PTCs in yeast [53]. Although all three types of stop codons could be read through in these experiments, the levels of read through of ochre stop mutations was consistently lower than those of amber or opal stop codons. This indicates that the efficiency of read through can be dependent on the type of stop mutation in yeast. In C. elegans, amber suppressor tRNA mutations are readily recovered, while opal and ochre specific suppressors are unknown [59]. Thus, in C. elegans, informational suppression of amber mutations is more common than that of opal or ochre mutations, consistent with what we see in the smg; sel-12 strains.
###end p 62
###begin title 63
###xml 15 21 15 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 24 31 24 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 68 74 68 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
The effects of spr-2 on sel-12 transcript levels do not explain the spr-2 phenotype
###end title 63
###begin p 64
###xml 43 49 43 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 83 97 83 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ar171) </italic>

###xml 712 726 712 726 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ar131) </italic>
###xml 743 757 743 757 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ar171) </italic>
###xml 778 780 778 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 796 802 796 802 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 839 852 839 852 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ty11) </italic>

###xml 914 926 914 926 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 961 968 961 968 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 993 999 993 999 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 1042 1054 1042 1054 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 1110 1117 1110 1117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 1155 1161 1155 1161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 1174 1181 1174 1181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 1252 1259 1252 1259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 1319 1325 1319 1325 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 1350 1356 1350 1356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 1380 1387 1380 1387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lin-12 </italic>
###xml 1398 1400 1398 1400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 1405 1411 1405 1411 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hop-1 </italic>
###xml 1464 1470 1464 1470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 1489 1496 1489 1496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 1614 1618 1614 1618 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
Curiously, we also found that mutations in spr-2 increase the transcript levels of sel-12(ar171) and sel-12(ty11) but only in the embryo and the L1 larva. The increase in sel-12(ar171) or sel-12(ty11) transcript levels in the embryo and the L1 stage in a spr-2 background is robust, but much weaker than the near restoration of sel-12 levels seen in the smg-2 background at almost all developmental stages. However, the effects of spr-2 on sel-12 transcript levels can not explain the suppression of sel-12 by spr-2 mutations. Firstly, mutations in strong spr genes suppress all sel-12 alleles that have been tested. For spr-2, it was initially shown that spr-2 mutations strongly suppress the phenotypes of the sel-12(ar131) missense and the sel-12(ar171) opal stop mutations [10]. We show that spr-2 mutations also strongly suppress the sel-12(ty11) amber allele. Secondly, smg mutations, which greatly increase sel-12(PTC) transcript levels, do not suppress sel-12 anywhere near as well as spr-2 mutations. This indicates that stabilizing sel-12(PTC) transcripts can only weakly ameliorate the phenotype of sel-12 mutants. Thus, although the effect of spr-2 mutations on sel-12 transcript levels might in some cases contribute to the suppression of sel-12 phenotypes, it is wholly insufficient to explain the strong spr-2 suppressor phenotype. As spr-2 mutations do not affect lin-12 signaling [10] or hop-1 transcript levels (Gontijo and Lakowski unpublished) spr-2 mutations suppress sel-12 through another, as yet uncharacterized, mechanism that is distinct from both that of NMD and that of the other known spr genes.

###begin title 65
###xml 4 10 4 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 33 45 33 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
How spr-2 mutations might affect sel-12(PTC) transcript levels
###end title 65
###begin p 66
###xml 14 20 14 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 33 45 33 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 85 91 85 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 176 183 176 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 355 376 355 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aspergillus nidulans </italic>
###xml 420 432 420 432 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. nidulans </italic>
###xml 457 459 453 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 534 546 530 542 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 573 579 569 575 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 711 718 707 714 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 892 898 888 894 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 929 936 925 932 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 355 375 <span type="species:ncbi:162425">Aspergillus nidulans</span>
###xml 420 431 <span type="species:ncbi:162425">A. nidulans</span>
The effect of spr-2 mutations on sel-12(PTC) transcript levels could be explained if spr-2 mutations directly, or indirectly, affect transport out of the nucleus of the mutant sel-12 transcripts. Proteins with Nucleosome Assembly Protein (NAP) domains are known to shuttle between the nucleus and the cytoplasm and can function as histone chaperones. The Aspergillus nidulans protein most similar to SPR-2, binds to the A. nidulans importin alpha ortholog [60]. Most, if not all, NMD takes place in the cytoplasm and if the export of sel-12(PTC) transcripts is affected by spr-2 mutations, more of the transcript could be retained in the nucleus and be protected from degradation. In this mechanism, the excess sel-12 transcript should have no effect on SEL-12 protein levels as translation also takes place in the cytoplasm. Alternatively, since NMD requires a primary round of translation, spr-2 could be necessary for optimal sel-12 translation after nuclear export. Reducing translation could thus reduce NMD.
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 39 45 39 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 64 71 64 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 216 218 216 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
###xml 238 244 238 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 257 264 257 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 350 357 350 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 625 627 625 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 717 723 717 723 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 767 774 767 774 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 798 811 798 811 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2(ar199) </italic>
###xml 837 847 837 847 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(+) </italic>
###xml 947 953 947 953 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 961 968 961 968 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>

Another possible mechanism could be if spr-2 mutations increase sel-12 transcription in the embryo and early larva. The human homolog of SPR-2 is known as SET/TAF1beta and has been shown to facilitate transcription [61]. So the effect of spr-2 mutations on sel-12 transcript levels could be at the level of decreasing transcription of a repressor of sel-12 transcription. Paradoxically, SET is also a component of the INHAT (Inhibitor of Histone Acetyltransferase) transcriptional repressor complex, which represses transcription of some loci by blocking the acetylation of histones and thus the activation of transcription [23]. In the absence of this activity, the transcription of a gene could be de-repressed. So spr-2 could also be a stage specific repressor of sel-12 transcription. However, spr-2(ar199) does not obviously affect sel-12(+) transcript levels in the embryo and L1 stages (data not shown), suggesting that it is unlikely that spr-2 affects sel-12 transcription.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 4 10 4 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 52 64 52 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 113 115 113 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 171 177 171 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 286 292 286 292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 305 317 305 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 364 370 364 370 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 392 404 392 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 589 595 589 595 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 630 636 630 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 672 684 672 684 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
Why spr-2 mutations have a stage specific effect on sel-12(PTC) transcript levels is also unclear. All evidence [10] (and Gontijo and Lakowski unpublished) indicates that spr-2 is broadly expressed at all developmental stages. Thus our results suggest that the stage specific effect of spr-2 mutations on sel-12(PTC) transcript levels might be due to an effect of spr-2 on the interaction of sel-12(PTC) transcripts and some unknown factor with a stage specific expression pattern. Alternatively, the effect could be due to developmental, or tissue-specific, differences in the effects of spr-2 mutations. Determining the role of spr-2 in the stage specific regulation of sel-12(PTC) transcript levels will require further study.

###begin title 69
Conclusion
###end title 69
###begin p 70
###xml 13 19 13 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 28 40 28 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(PTC) </italic>
###xml 126 132 126 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 157 163 157 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 233 239 233 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-2 </italic>
###xml 250 257 250 257 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 312 316 312 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 355 362 355 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 412 419 412 419 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 489 503 489 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(amber) </italic>
###xml 528 532 528 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 554 558 554 558 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr </italic>
###xml 629 643 629 643 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ar171) </italic>
###xml 664 677 664 677 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ty11) </italic>
###xml 715 719 715 719 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 784 791 784 791 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 865 878 865 878 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ty11) </italic>
###xml 928 942 928 942 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12(ar171) </italic>
Mutations in spr-2 increase sel-12(PTC) transcript levels in embryos and L1 larvae. However, this effect does not explain the spr-2 suppressor phenotype and spr-2 does not affect nonsense mediated decay (NMD). The mechanism by which spr-2 suppresses sel-12 is still unclear but appears to be novel. Mutations in smg genes weakly suppress the phenotype of sel-12 animals with amber PTC alleles by stabilizing the sel-12 transcripts which are otherwise subject to NMD. Some read thorough of sel-12(amber) PTCs appears to occur in smg backgrounds. Over 100 spr mutations have been recovered in screens for suppressors of either the sel-12(ar171) opal mutation or the sel-12(ty11) amber mutation. However, mutations in smg genes are only very infrequently recovered in direct screens for sel-12 suppressors. This is probably due to the weakness of their effects on the sel-12(ty11) phenotype and the absence of clear effects on the sel-12(ar171) phenotype.

###begin title 85
Staging of worms
###end title 85
###begin p 86
###xml 136 138 136 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
To stage worms, plates full of gravid adult hermaphrodites were treated with alkaline hypochlorite solution to kill and dissolve worms [62]. The more resistant eggs were washed three times with M9 buffer and then allowed to hatch overnight in M9 buffer with aeration. Synchronized L1s were then placed onto 9 cm Petri plates to feed and allowed to develop until the desired stage. Staged worms were then washed off the plates, and washed 3 times with M9 before freezing the worm pellet at -80degreesC until RNA preparation. Where not otherwise specified, L1 corresponds to 5 hours, L2 to 22 hours, L3 to 28 hours, L4 to 44 hours, L4/YA to 50 hours and YA (young adult) to 58 hours of growth. Eggs were not synchronized.
###end p 86
###begin title 87
Isolation of RNA, PCR and Quantitative-PCR
###end title 87
###begin p 88
To examine the effect of mutations on transcripts, we extracted total RNA from either mixed stage, or staged worms, depending on the experiment, using TRIZOL reagent following the manufacturer's recommendations (Invitrogen). RNA was treated with Turbo DNAse (Ambion) following their protocol. Reverse transcriptase reactions and quantitative PCR were done using the two-step RT-qPCR Sybr Green low ROX kit by ABgene (reference: AB-1381/a, which is called AB-4116/b since 14/09/2007) according to the manufacturer's recommendations. To synthesize cDNAs we used a 3:1 ratio of random primers and oligo dT primers. Real time PCR reactions were done in 20 mul volumes and run on an Applied Biosystems AB7500 machine in 96 well plates.

For most Q-PCR experiments, the Ct levels of the control genes ama-1, nhx-4 and eft-2 were averaged and this average served as the internal control for the DeltaDeltaCt method to estimate relative fold differences in the target gene RNA levels. The RNA polymerase II large subunit, encoded by the ama-1 gene, has often been used as a control in Northern blots including by us [11,13]. ama-1 is expressed strongly in all developmental stages, however, we found that it does seem to be even more strongly expressed in embryos than the larval stages (data not shown) consistent with the high level of transcription in embryos and with the fact that ama-1 has a strong maternal component. In order to normalize better our studies, we sought additional controls that were broadly expressed housekeeping genes with expression levels both higher and lower than ama-1. As a control gene with expression levels lower than ama-1 we chose the gene encoding the presumptive housekeeping Na+-H+ exchanger, nhx-4, which has been shown to be expressed in all cells at all times examined [64]. As a very highly expressed gene, we chose eft-2 [65], a gene that encodes a homolog of translation elongation factor 2 (EF-2), a GTP-binding protein essential for the elongation phase of protein synthesis that is broadly expressed in all stages of development [65]. We determined the absolute transcriptional profiles of these genes (eft-2, ama-1 and nhx-4) under various conditions. The relative expression levels between these three control genes are highly stereotyped during development and reproducible between the strains we are working with (data not shown). Importantly, their expression levels cover 3 log scales and can be very useful for the validation of expression data of genes whose expression vary considerably during development. ###xml 1175 1180 1175 1180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pf52 </italic>
###xml 1205 1213 1205 1213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pkP3086 </italic>
To refine the position of pf52, we crossed +/pf52; sel-12(ty11) males to daf-7(e1372) dpy-1(e1); sel-12(ty11) hermaphrodites and isolated cross progeny. We singled all non-Egl F2 progeny from 8 plates and scored their progeny for the various phenotypes. The results of this cross was consistent with pf52 being to the right of dpy-1 (data not shown). So we crossed +/pf52 III; sel-12(ty11) X males to dpy-1(e1) daf-2(e1370) unc-32(e189)III hermaphrodites, isolated cross progeny and then isolated Egl F2 progeny. In the F3 we singled Unc non Dpy and Dpy non Unc recombinant progeny from those plates that had some eggs. The final scoring of these recombinants was dpy-1 31 spr-8 20 daf-2 41 unc-32, placing spr-8 about midway between dpy-1 and daf-2. We constructed a dpy-1(e1) spr-8(pf52) unc-32(e189) III; sel-12(ty11) X strain for further SNP mapping with polymorphisms from the CB4856 strain. However, the results of these experiments were very difficult to interpret due to the weak phenotype of pf52 and the presence of polymorphisms that modify the sel-12 phenotype in the CB4856 background. However, the results of these mapping experiments were most consistent with pf52 mapping between the SNPs pkP3086 and SNP_Y71H2B[2] (data not shown).

Testing the ability of pf52 to suppress unc-54(r193)
###end title 95
###begin p 96
###xml 10 15 10 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pf52 </italic>
###xml 62 67 62 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pf52 </italic>
###xml 86 90 86 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 103 116 103 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">unc-54(r293) </italic>
###xml 135 150 135 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">unc-54(r293)/+ </italic>
###xml 159 201 159 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">spr-8(pf52) III; sel-12(ty11) unc-1(e719) </italic>
###xml 304 311 304 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Unc-54 </italic>
###xml 578 582 578 582 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 676 683 676 683 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">unc-54 </italic>
###xml 1068 1073 1068 1073 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pf52 </italic>
###xml 1094 1107 1094 1107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">unc-54(r293) </italic>
To see if pf52 affected NMD more generally, we tested whether pf52 could suppress the smg suppressible unc-54(r293) allele. We crossed unc-54(r293)/+ males to spr-8(pf52) III; sel-12(ty11) unc-1(e719) hermaphrodites and isolated wild type moving cross progeny. In the F2 generation we randomly picked 19 Unc-54 animals individually to plates and then examined the F3 and F4 progeny. All of the progeny in three of 19 F3 broods were nearly wild type, although slightly slow moving and displayed a mild protruding vulva phenotype similar to that seen in all previously identified smg mutations. Eight of the 16 remaining broods had some eggs and some animals moving better than unc-54 animals usually move. In the F4 generation these eight plates had many wild type moving progeny and 6/8 had some Unc-1 progeny. In the F4 two of the remaining eight broods had no wild type progeny but many animals suppressed for Unc-54 but with Unc-1 movement defects. The remaining six broods were phenotypically Unc-54 in the F3 and F4 generations. These results are consistent with pf52 strongly suppressing unc-54(r293) but displaying a partial maternal effect.

Linkage analysis of smg-1(by146)
###end title 99
###begin p 100
###xml 68 75 68 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sel-12 </italic>
###xml 193 206 193 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">unc-54(r293) </italic>
###xml 508 514 508 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">by146 </italic>
###xml 576 582 576 582 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">by146 </italic>
###xml 794 798 794 798 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 1053 1057 1052 1056 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smg </italic>
###xml 1061 1066 1060 1065 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">smgl </italic>
As we do not know the reason why the CB4856 background modifies the sel-12 phenotype, we examined if there were polymorphisms in this strain that could modulate NMD. We crossed CB4856 males to unc-54(r293) hermaphrodites, isolated cross progeny and then placed 30 Unc-54 F2 progeny singly on plates. All 30 animals gave rise to strains in which all animals were strongly paralyzed indicating that the CB4856 strain does not contain any polymorphisms that strongly affect NMD. We then crossed CB4856 males to by146 hermaphrodites. Using the protruding vulva (Pvl) phenotype of by146 we isolated cross progeny and then placed 60 F2 animals with Pvls individually on plates. We examined the F3 generation for a high penetrance of Pvl defect and found 45 strains that we were fairly confident were smg based on their highly penetrant mild Pvl phenotype. We made lysates from each of these strains and then pooled 2 mul of each lysate. We then tested these pooled lysates, along with N2 and CB4856 genomic DNA for the presence of SNPs near each of the known smg and smgl genes (data not shown).
