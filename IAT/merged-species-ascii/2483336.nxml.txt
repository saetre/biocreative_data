We examined the concentration dependence of currents through CaV3.1 T-type calcium channels, varying Ca2+ and Ba2+ over a wide concentration range (100 nM to 110 mM) while recording whole-cell currents over a wide voltage range from channels stably expressed in HEK 293 cells. To isolate effects on permeation, instantaneous current-voltage relationships (IIV) were obtained following strong, brief depolarizations to activate channels with minimal inactivation. Reversal potentials were described by PCa/PNa = 87 and PCa/PBa = 2, based on Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz theory. However, analysis of chord conductances found that apparent Kd values were similar for Ca2+ and Ba2+, both for block of currents carried by Na+ (3 muM for Ca2+ vs. 4 muM for Ba2+, at -30 mV; weaker at more positive or negative voltages) and for permeation (3.3 mM for Ca2+ vs. 2.5 mM for Ba2+; nearly voltage independent). Block by 3-10 muM Ca2+ was time dependent, described by bimolecular kinetics with binding at approximately3 x 108 M-1s-1 and voltage-dependent exit. Ca2+o, Ba2+o, and Mg2+o also affected channel gating, primarily by shifting channel activation, consistent with screening a surface charge of 1 e- per 98 A2 from Gouy-Chapman theory. Additionally, inward currents inactivated approximately35% faster in Ba2+o (vs. Ca2+o or Na+o). The accelerated inactivation in Ba2+o correlated with the transition from Na+ to Ba2+ permeation, suggesting that Ba2+o speeds inactivation by occupying the pore. We conclude that the selectivity of the "surface charge" among divalent cations differs between calcium channel families, implying that the surface charge is channel specific. Voltage strongly affects the concentration dependence of block, but not of permeation, for Ca2+ or Ba2+.

###begin title 5
INTRODUCTION
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 115 117 115 117 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 160 162 160 162 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 421 453 421 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Frankenhaeuser and Hodgkin, 1957</xref>
###xml 493 520 493 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib51">Swenson and Armstrong, 1981</xref>
###xml 562 587 562 587 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Gilly and Armstrong, 1982</xref>
It seems like a simple experiment: determine the concentration dependence of current through a voltage-dependent Ca2+ channel by varying the concentration of Ca2+. But it has long been recognized that interpretation of such results is complicated. In particular, the whole-cell current is affected by gating as well as permeation. Ions can modify gating by a variety of mechanisms, including screening of surface charge (Frankenhaeuser and Hodgkin, 1957), effects of pore occupancy on gating (Swenson and Armstrong, 1981), and binding to sites outside the pore (Gilly and Armstrong, 1982).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 116 140 116 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">Hodgkin and Huxley, 1952</xref>
###xml 414 420 414 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1</xref>
The classical method for separating gating from permeation is the instantaneous current-voltage relationship (IIV) (Hodgkin and Huxley, 1952). Channels are first activated by a fixed depolarizing pulse, followed by repolarization to a wide range of voltages. The current measured "instantaneously" at each new voltage reflects the current through the population of channels opened by the prior depolarizing pulse (Fig. 1). Thus, the number of channels open is constant, so any variation in current must reflect the effect of voltage on permeation through those open channels. The IIV method makes several assumptions, most critically that currents can be accurately measured before gating processes affect the number of channels open (see Materials and methods).
###end p 7

###begin p 9
###xml 12 13 12 13 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 92 112 92 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib46">Serrano et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 114 118 114 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib47">2000</xref>
###xml 211 213 211 213 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 220 222 220 222 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 422 424 422 424 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 431 433 431 433 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 528 530 528 530 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 550 570 550 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib47">Serrano et al., 2000</xref>
Gating of CaV3.1 is sufficiently slow for the IIV to be measured over a wide voltage range (Serrano et al., 1999, 2000). In this study, we examine the concentration dependence of current from 100 nM to 110 mM Ca2+ (or Ba2+), from +100 to -150 mV. This dataset provides a detailed yet large-scale view of how voltage and ion concentration affect permeation. One major goal was to examine the basis of selectivity between Ca2+ and Ba2+, for a channel that exhibits similar ionic currents with the two ions, but is selective for Ca2+ by other criteria (Serrano et al., 2000).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 88 90 88 90 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 94 96 94 96 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 104 106 104 106 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 249 251 249 251 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 262 281 262 281 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Byerly et al., 1985</xref>
###xml 289 290 289 290 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 301 319 301 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Hille et al., 1975</xref>
###xml 330 332 330 332 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 359 361 359 361 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 367 369 367 369 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 389 391 389 391 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 542 544 542 544 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 601 603 601 603 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 609 610 609 610 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
We also found that divalent cations modify gating, by two distinct mechanisms. First, Ca2+, Ba2+, and Mg2+ shift channel activation, in a manner largely consistent with screening of surface charge. This contrasts with high voltage-activated (HVA) Ca2+ channels (Byerly et al., 1985) and Na+ channels (Hille et al., 1975), where Ca2+ has larger effects than Ba2+ or Mg2+, suggesting that Ca2+ can both bind to and screen surface charges. This implies that some or all of the "surface charge" is specific to a particular ion channel. Second, Ba2+ speeds inactivation of inward currents (compared with Ca2+ or Na+), and the effect correlates with pore occupancy.

###begin title 12
Whole-Cell Recording
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 85 86 85 86 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 91 107 91 107 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib33">Lee et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 134 154 134 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib46">Serrano et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 799 801 786 788 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 801 802 788 789 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 808 810 795 797 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 810 811 797 798 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 79 82 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
Whole-cell recordings were performed from HEK293 cells stably transfected with rat CaV3.1 (Lee et al., 1999) as previously described (Serrano et al., 1999). Data were recorded at room temperature using an Axopatch 200 amplifier driven by pClamp software (pClamp 8.2, Axon Instruments). Electrode resistances were 1.5-2 MOmega, giving series resistances in the whole-cell configuration of 3.8 +/- 1.2 MOmega (mean +/- SD, 57 cells) before compensation, usually with "correction" at 80-90% and "prediction" at 40-60%. Data were sampled at 50 kHz following 10 kHz analogue filtering using the amplifier. Both "raw" and leak-subtracted currents were recorded, and all current records and analyses shown are from leak-subtracted data. The holding potential was -100 mV, except -120 mV in 0.1 or 10 muM Ca2+o or Ba2+o (to avoid possible resting inactivation).

###xml 1231 1233 1213 1215 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
Low Ca2+o concentrations were achieved with 1 mM EGTA (0.1 muM free Ca2+o, with 0.379 mM total Ca2+o) or 1 mM HEDTA (3 or 10 muM free Ca2+o, with 0.334 mM or 0.633 mM total Ca2+o respectively). However, we found that block of inward currents carried by Na+ was significantly faster and more potent when 10 muM Ca2+ was added directly to a nominally Ca2+-free extracellular solution than with our HEDTA-buffered 10 muM Ca2+ solution, as if the actual free Ca2+o was approximately1.6x higher in the unbuffered solution (see Fig. S1, available at ). Extracellular solutions with nominally zero Ca2+ had <3 muM total Ca2+, as measured by atomic absorption flame spectroscopy (Perkin Elmer Atomic Absorption Spectrometer 3100), consistent with the block of current carried by Na+ by nominally Ca2+-free extracellular solution with no added calcium buffer (Fig. S1 B). We attempted to calibrate the Ca2+-HEDTA system, using arsenazo III or Fura 6F as calcium indicators, but we have not obtained results with sufficient reproducibility and precision to measure the Kd for HEDTA (not depicted). Since EGTA-like buffers may bind contaminating cations possibly contained in unbuffered solutions, the data we present here with 3 or 10 muM Ca2+ are with HEDTA-buffered solutions.

Whole-Cell Protocols and Analysis of Permeation
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 100 106 100 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1</xref>
###xml 259 261 259 261 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>

###xml 616 617 616 617 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 655 656 651 652 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n</italic>
In each ionic condition, currents were recorded with two protocols, which we call the IIV protocol (Fig. 1) and the I-V protocol (Fig. S5). Because of channel rundown and other time-dependent changes, we recorded first in a reference solution (usually 2 mM Ca2+ or 2 mM Ba2+), then in a test solution, and finally after return to the reference solution. The effects of one or two different test solutions were examined in each cell. The average of currents in the reference solution (recorded before and after application of the test solution) was used as the control value. For the experiments varying Ca2+o or Ba2+o, rundown was 17 +/- 9% (mean +/- SD, n = 60), comparing the sum of the absolute values of currents at all voltages from the IIV protocols recorded in the reference solution, before vs. after a test solution. Cells with >40% rundown by that criterion were rejected.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 148 172 148 172 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">Obejero-Paz et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 366 368 353 355 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 368 369 355 356 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 375 377 362 364 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 377 378 364 365 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
The leak current tended to be nonlinear in "leaky" cells, producing artifactual time-independent outward currents at strongly depolarized voltages (Obejero-Paz et al., 2004). Most cells with >0.2 nA of inward holding current at the holding potential were excluded from analysis. This criterion was relaxed for approximately15% of the cells recorded in 0.1-100 muM Ca2+o or Ba2+o, since the leak tended to be high in that condition, but currents were also large (giving a high signal-to-leak ratio). In such cells, currents inactivated essentially to zero except at the most positive voltages (well above the reversal potential), where the contribution of leak to the measured current was estimated to be <10%.
###end p 24
###begin p 25

The sum of the sine waves was then subtracted from the entire record. This was done with a program written by S.W. Jones in Microsoft Visual Basic v.6.

Effects of ions on permeation were assessed using IIV relations, measured by fitting single exponentials to the decay of current following a brief (2-ms) step to +60 mV (Serrano et al., 2000). The exponential fit began when the tail currents reached a peak, as an estimate of the current at the time when accurate voltage clamp was actually achieved, and extended 20 ms into the voltage steps. The amplitude of the fitted exponential at the starting point of the fit was used for the IIV measurements shown. In most of our experimental conditions, the time course of block is fast, i.e., pore occupancy has reached steady state by the time the IIV is measured. The time course of block can be resolved by careful analysis in certain conditions (1 mM Mg2+o in 2 mM Ba2+o, Serrano et al., 2000; 10 muM Ca2+o, Figs. 7-9), but the single exponential fit did not include those very fast (tau = 0.1-0.2 ms) components, so we interpret the IIV measurements as representing steady-state block in all experimental conditions in this study.

The concentration dependence of Ca2+ permeation and block was described in terms of chord conductances (G), analyzed as the sum of two components:GNa is the component of conductance that is blocked by Ca2+o, with Kd = KB, and GCa is the component that saturates with increasing Ca2+o, with Kd = KP. ###begin title 30
Analysis of Gating
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 90 94 90 94 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peak</sub>
###xml 102 107 100 105 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Inact</sub>
###xml 134 137 130 133 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IIV</sub>
###xml 218 221 214 217 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O,r</sub>
###xml 224 244 220 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib46">Serrano et al., 1999</xref>
Effects of ions on gating were determined using the peak currents from the I-V protocol, TPeak and tauInact from the I-V protocol, tauIIV from the IIV protocol, and channel activation (from the I-V/IIV current ratio, PO,r) (Serrano et al., 1999).
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 402 422 402 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib6">Burgess et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 476 481 476 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="disp-formula" rid="fd3">Eq. 3</xref>
Peak currents from the I-V protocol were measured following 1 kHz Gaussian filtering, as the average between two cursors placed by eye in Clampfit (Axon Instruments) to delineate the maximal current. Near the reversal potential, cursor placement was based in part on the location of peak inward and outward currents at nearby voltages, with care taken to avoid a fast transient outward gating current (Burgess et al., 2002). Currents and chord conductances were normalized by Eq. 3 to reduce effects of cell-cell variability in expression levels, based on the sum of the absolute value of currents (or conductances, as appropriate) from -90 to +100 mV.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 248 253 246 251 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Inact</sub>
###xml 260 264 258 262 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peak</sub>
###xml 313 315 311 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dI</italic>
###xml 316 318 314 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dt</italic>
Currents from the I-V protocol were fitted by the sum of two exponentials, from the time where activation was approximately half-maximal, to the end of the 100-ms step, following 2 kHz Gaussian filtering. The slower time constant was defined as tauInact, and TPeak was calculated as the time where the calculated dI/dt = 0. For outward currents, the sum of two exponentials did not fit the peak outward current accurately, but did approximate well the time of peak outward current.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 3 6 1 4 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IIV</sub>
tauIIV was measured by fitting currents from the IIV protocol to single exponentials, beginning shortly after the peak, and extending to >95% of decay (often to the end of the 100-ms steps).
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 36 39 36 39 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O,r</sub>

###xml 749 752 749 752 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O,r</sub>
###xml 816 818 816 818 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 818 819 818 819 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 849 850 845 846 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">n</italic>
###xml 968 969 964 965 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O</sub>
###xml 983 986 979 982 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O,r</sub>
###xml 1072 1074 1068 1070 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1074 1075 1070 1071 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 1081 1083 1077 1079 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1083 1084 1079 1080 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 1145 1148 1141 1144 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O,r</sub>
Channel activation was measured as PO,r, the ratio of the peak I-V to the IIV (Serrano et al., 1999). Chord conductances calculated from the I-V protocol are often used as a measure of channel activation, but this assumes that the open-channel IIV relationship is linear, which is far from true for T-channels (Fig. 2). On the other hand, the PO,r is undefined at the reversal potential, and subject to large error nearby. In principle, this singularity can be eliminated by calculating PO,r from chord conductances rather than currents. However, the additional calculation (dividing by driving force) can increase error as well, so we present PO,r values from the method that we judge to be most accurate (as noted in figure legends). The maximal PO,r at strongly depolarized voltages often exceeded 1.0 (in 2 mM Ca2+o, 1.10 +/- 0.08, mean +/- SD, n = 52), probably because the 2-ms prepulse to +60 mV in the IIV protocol did not precisely match the time of maximal PO. Therefore, PO,r values were normalized to the average values from +70 to +100 mV (except in 110 mM Ca2+o or Ba2+o, where those voltages were near the reversal potential and PO,r was not accurately measurable, so unnormalized values were used).

###begin title 38
Series Resistance Error
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 388 408 388 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib46">Serrano et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 825 827 821 823 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 833 835 829 831 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1022 1024 1006 1008 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1030 1032 1014 1016 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1123 1125 1095 1097 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
Whole-cell experiments required accurate voltage clamp to control the large currents observed over a wide voltage range. Cells were selected primarily based on the time course and shape of the tail current near -100 mV with the IIV protocol. In some cells, series resistance error was also evaluated from the effect of partial inactivation on the time course of tail currents at -100 mV (Serrano et al., 1999). The average series resistance was 0.75 MOmega (assuming 80% compensation), which would give 16 mV of error for a 21-nA current. This is significant for the largest currents observed in this study, but we note that use of the IIV protocol eliminates the negative slope conductance region normally observed for calcium currents. Series resistance error would selectively underestimate the chord conductance in low Ca2+ or Ba2+ by approximately13% (calculated for a 16-mV error with 120-mV driving force). The error will be proportionally less for the smaller currents observed with millimolar concentrations of Ca2+ or Ba2+ (e.g., approximately5% error for the conductance at the most negative voltage in 110 mM Ca2+). Put another way, the estimated error was 1% for each 13 nS of conductance. No correction was made for this effect.

For measurement of I-V relations, peak inward currents were <2.5 nA in all conditions (except 100 nM Ca2+o or Ba2+o), so errors should be <2 mV (with 0.75 MOmega of uncompensated series resistance). Errors in TPeak should also be small, since that value was also measured from inward currents with the I-V protocol. Activation curves were calculated from the I-V/IIV ratio, and the error can be significant for the IIV. In the worst case (100 nM Ca2+o or Ba2+o), at the measured 50% activation voltage (-50 mV), there would be approximately7 mV of error for the IIV. The error would be less than half that value at 10 muM Ca2+o or Ba2+o, and even smaller in all other conditions (e.g., for 110 mM Ca2+o, 2 mV error near half activation). Effects of ions on PO,r were analyzed by Gouy-Chapman theory, i.e., assuming that ions screen but do not bind to a uniform planar surface charge. The measured shift in PO,r was interpreted as a change in surface potential (DeltaPsi). ###begin p 48

Our approach was to examine the effect of ion concentration on the IIV, using near normal ionic conditions as the reference. Fig. 1 illustrates this for changes to the extremes, 100 nM Ca2+o and 110 mM Ca2+o. It is immediately apparent that inward currents are substantially increased in both test solutions. With 100 nM Ca2+o, outward currents are also increased. This illustrates several well-known features of calcium channel permeation. First, in the near absence of Ca2+o, channels are highly permeable to monovalents (in our conditions, Na+). Second, the inward current through calcium channels is not saturated at the physiological concentration (2 mM Ca2+o). Third, Na+i can carry large outward currents, but these outward currents are reduced by 2 or 110 mM Ca2+o. This is consistent with the view that T-type calcium channels, like other calcium channels, are essentially nonselective cation channels in the absence of Ca2+o (Fukushima and Hagiwara, 1985; Carbone and Lux, 1987). Furthermore, Ca2+ can either block current carried by monovalent cations, or permeate, depending on voltage and concentration.

The effects of Ca2+o and Ba2+o on permeation are illustrated more clearly by chord conductance-voltage relationships (Fig. 4), which eliminate the discontinuity in the IIV at VR (Fig. 2). Consider the effect of increasing Ca2+o from 100 nM to 10 muM (Fig. 4 A). At extreme negative and positive voltages, Ca2+o had little or no effect. However, 10 muM Ca2+o clearly blocked current through calcium channels at intermediate voltages (Fig. 4 C). Qualitatively, this can be explained by a Woodhull (1973) model for voltage-dependent block, where Ca2+ rapidly exits to the extracellular solution at positive voltages, or to the intracellular solution at negative voltages. A U-shaped voltage dependence for block by Ca2+ has been reported previously for T currents in lymphocytes and sensory neurons (Fukushima and Hagiwara, 1985; Lux et al., 1990). Block was stronger at 0.1-0.5 mM Ca2+o (Fig. 4 C), but still little effect was apparent near +100 mV.

Fig. 5 (E and F) shows the parameters (conductance and Kd values) for the fits to the voltage dependence of chord conductance. The component blocked by Ca2+ ("GNa") is the extrapolation of the curves shown in Fig. 5 (A-D) to zero Ca2+, and the component carried by Ca2+ ("GCa") is the extrapolation to saturating Ca2+o. These limiting values are well approximated by the conductances at 100 nM and 110 mM Ca2+o, respectively (see Fig. 5, A-D). GNa and GCa are not true chord conductances for Na+ and Ca2+ in the sense introduced by Hodgkin and Huxley (1952), since they are components of the chord conductance defined by the observed VR, not by the equilibrium potentials of Na+ and Ca2+. Experimentally, that results from the G-[Ca2+]o curves extrapolating to a limiting nonzero value at high Ca2+ (Fig. 5 A).

Close examination of tail currents recorded in 10 muM Ca2+o revealed a rapid component (Fig. 6 A), possibly resulting from time-dependent block by Ca2+o (Lux et al., 1990). Consistent with that interpretation, tail currents were also biphasic in 3 muM Ca2+o (Fig. 6 B), and the fast component was visibly slower in 3 vs. 10 muM Ca2+o (Fig. 6, B and C). Biexponential fits to the tail currents demonstrated a slow component with tau approximately 10 ms, which did not depend on Ca2+o, and a concentration-dependent fast component (Fig. 6 D). The simplest explanation is that much of the block by Ca2+o is relieved by strong depolarization (the 2-ms step to +60 mV, Fig. 6 A), and then Ca2+o reenters the pore upon repolarization to voltages where steady-state block is stronger (see Figs. 4 and 5). The fast component does not decay completely to zero, suggesting a rapidly equilibrating but incomplete block, followed by normal tail current kinetics for channels that remain unblocked (at -20 to -60 mV most channels inactivate rather than close; Serrano et al., 1999). If so, the relative amplitude of the fast tail current component reflects the fraction of channels blocked (Fig. 6 E).

The fast component of closed times observed in 3-10 muM Ca2+o could include both normal closed state(s) (as observed in 0.1 muM Ca2+o) and Ca2+ block events. Since the time constants are not very different (Fig. 8 C), we could not reliably resolve multiple fast components. For a closing rate of 1 s-1 (from the mean open time in 0.1 muM Ca2+o), the blocking rate in 3 muM Ca2+o is 2 s-1 (assuming the mean open time in 3 muM Ca2+o is the sum of closing and blocking rates, Fig. 8 D). That predicts that approximately2/3 of the "closed" times in 3 muM Ca2+o are Ca2+ block events. Correspondingly, nearly all of the closed times in 10 muM Ca2+o should result from Ca2+ block, so those values (Fig. 8 C) should best reflect the true residence time of Ca2+ in the pore. Thus, the apparent increase in the faster closed time with Ca2+ may reflect a transition from brief Ca2+-independent gating events to somewhat slower Ca2+ blocking events, rather than an effect of Ca2+ on fast gating kinetics.

Many studies of calcium channel activation are based on chord conductances, measured from I-V relationships as in Fig. 10. However, the G-V relations are quite complex (Fig. 11, A and B) and do not remotely resemble the Boltzmann relation expected from simple models of channel activation, except in very limited voltage ranges (Fig. S6) (Jones, 2003). The main problem is that G-V is directly related to the extent of channel activation (i.e., open probability) only if the IIV is linear (Hodgkin and Huxley, 1952). For CaV3.1, the IIV is distinctly nonlinear under nearly all conditions (Fig. 2), invalidating that assumption.

A more direct measure of channel activation is provided by the ratio of the peak current evoked by direct depolarization (as in Fig. 10), divided by the instantaneous current through fully activated channels (Fig. 2). Activation curves calculated by this method are shown in Fig. 11 (C and D). These relationships are also not perfectly described by a simple Boltzmann relationship, likely resulting from the complex macroscopic gating kinetics of CaV3.1 channels (Serrano et al., 1999). Fits to Boltzmann relations raised to the fourth power are shown in Fig. 11 (C and D). Although these fits are also not perfect (e.g., they systematically overestimate channel activation at the most negative voltages; Fig. S6, C and D), they are used in this study as an empirical measure of shifts in the voltage dependence of channel activation in different ionic conditions. Qualitatively, activation curves are similar at the lowest Ca2+o (or Ba2+o) concentrations, and shift toward more positive voltages at 2-110 mM. Ca2+o and Ba2+o also shifted other measures of channel gating, including tauIIV and TPeak (Supplemental Results; Figs. S7 and S8).

Differences in inactivation rate could appear to affect the voltage dependence of channel activation. Qualitatively, if channels inactivate more rapidly in Ba2+o, fewer channels would be open when peak current is reached, which would decrease the measured channel activation, especially at more negative voltages (where activation is relatively slow). Faster inactivation could also shorten the time to peak current. And to the extent that tauIIV depends on inactivation, it would be faster in Ba2+o than in Ca2+o. That is, faster inactivation in Ba2+o would produce a downward shift of the activation, tauIIV, and TPeak curves, which would appear to be a rightward shift along the voltage axis for activation and tauIIV, but a leftward shift for time-to-peak (see Fig. 12, D-F). We explored this interpretation by simulating the effect of faster inactivation on these measurements, using the Serrano et al. (1999) model for CaV3.1. The model parameters were varied to optimize the fit to the data of Fig. 12 (D-F), with only the microscopic inactivation rate constant allowed to differ between Ca2+o and Ba2+o (see Materials and methods). The fits to the data were not perfect, but it reproduced the key qualitative features (rightward shifts for activation and tauIIV, leftward shift TPeak), and the amplitudes of the shifts were reasonably close to the experimental values (Fig. 12 C). We conclude that the subtle differences in gating observed between Ca2+o and Ba2+o result primarily from the more rapid inactivation for inward currents with Ba2+o.

###begin title 95
DISCUSSION
###end title 95
###begin p 96
###xml 23 24 23 24 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 65 67 65 67 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 74 76 74 76 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 456 458 456 458 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 465 467 465 467 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 565 567 565 567 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
We report effects on CaV3.1 T-type calcium channels of varying Ca2+ and Ba2+, over a wide range of concentration (100 nM to 110 mM) and voltage (-150 to +100 mV). One key feature of our experimental design was the use of IIV relations, to isolate effects on permeation from effects on channel gating. This study allows better comparison of ion selectivity in T channels to the classical results on L channels and other HVA calcium channels. In addition, Ca2+ and Ba2+ affect channel gating, largely as expected for screening surface charge, but pore occupancy by Ba2+ also speeds inactivation.

Ca2+o vs. Ba2+o: Permeation
###end title 97
###begin p 98
###xml 58 60 58 60 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 68 70 68 70 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 167 173 167 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 264 266 264 266 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 266 267 266 267 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 274 276 274 276 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 276 277 276 277 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 411 413 411 413 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 413 414 413 414 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 421 423 421 423 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 423 424 423 424 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 469 471 469 471 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 471 472 471 472 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 479 481 479 481 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 481 482 481 482 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 557 558 557 558 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 578 586 578 586 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Fig. 5 F</xref>
Classically, T-type calcium channels cannot distinguish Ca2+ from Ba2+, in that inward currents are quite similar for the two ions. We confirm that basic observation (Fig. 2), although careful comparison demonstrates that inward currents are slightly smaller in Ba2+o vs. Ca2+o (Fig. S9). These results dramatically contrast with most HVA calcium channels, where currents are approximately twofold larger for Ba2+o vs. Ca2+o. Furthermore, we find that the pore binds Ca2+o and Ba2+o with similar apparent affinity, either for block of currents carried by Na+ or for permeation (Fig. 5 F).
###end p 98
###begin p 99
###xml 74 76 74 76 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 83 85 83 85 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 95 96 95 96 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sub>

###xml 989 990 989 990 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 990 991 990 991 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sub>
###xml 1015 1017 1015 1017 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1029 1031 1029 1031 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1053 1070 1053 1070 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib24">Hess et al., 1986</xref>
###xml 1083 1100 1083 1100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib55">Wang et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 1124 1125 1124 1125 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 1135 1137 1135 1137 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1144 1146 1144 1146 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1231 1232 1231 1232 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sub>
However, some measures of selectivity report larger differences between Ca2+ and Ba2+. First, VR was more positive with Ca2+o, consistent with PCa/PBa = 2 based on GHK theory (Fig. 3). Second, outward currents are larger with 2 mM Ba2+o vs. Ca2+o near +60 mV, demonstrating weaker block of currents carried by Na+i by Ba2+o, although this effect is reduced at more strongly depolarized voltages (Fig. S9). Third, block by Mg2+o is weaker with Ca2+o, as reported previously (Serrano et al., 2000). This effect is also seen for other pore-blocking ions, including Y3+ (Obejero-Paz et al., 2004) and Ni2+ (Obejero-Paz et al., 2008). We conclude that a T-channel, like an L- or N-channel, can tell the difference between a Ca2+o ion and a Ba2+o ion. However, not all aspects of permeation and block are affected to the same extent by Ca2+o and Ba2+o. This may simply reflect the principle that "affinity" is a poorly defined property for a multiion pore. However, for L channels, the apparent Kd values are lower for Ca2+ than for Ba2+ for both permeation (Hess et al., 1986) and block (Wang et al., 2005). The selectivity of CaV3.1 for Ca2+ vs. Ba2+ seems to be most apparent when competition among ions is the strongest, e.g., near VR, or when di- or trivalent pore blockers are present.

###begin title 103
Chord Conductance vs. Slope Conductance
###end title 103
###begin p 104
###xml 194 195 194 195 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sub>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 272 273 272 273 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 440 460 440 460 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib46">Serrano et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 462 486 462 486 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">Obejero-Paz et al., 2004</xref>
Many features of our analysis exploit chord conductances. Historically, most studies on calcium channel permeation simply measured ionic currents, or used slope conductances, primarily because VR can be difficult to measure for these highly Ca2+-selective channels. For CaV3.1 channels stably expressed in HEK 293 cells, we have previously demonstrated good current isolation (e. g., by the "envelope test") over a very wide voltage range (Serrano et al., 1999; Obejero-Paz et al., 2004).
###end p 104
###begin p 105
###xml 74 75 74 75 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sub>
###xml 203 227 203 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib1">Adelman and French, 1978</xref>
###xml 281 282 281 282 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 470 471 470 471 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 587 589 587 589 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 595 597 595 597 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 621 623 621 623 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
Chord conductances are directly related to ion permeation, by I = G (V - VR). Chord conductances are always positive, while slope conductances can be negative, e.g. for strongly voltage-dependent block (Adelman and French, 1978). For the nonlinear IIV relationships observed for CaV3.1, slope conductances will be systematically larger, and more voltage-dependent, than chord conductances. This effect is small for the relatively linear IIV relationship observed with Na+ as charge carrier, but slope conductances are approximately twofold larger than chord conductance at 2 or 110 mM Ca2+ or Ba2+ (Fig. S21). Block by Mg2+ reduces the slope conductance to near zero at voltages and concentrations where block is far from complete (Fig. S21, E and F; compare to Fig. S12, C and D). We do not mean that it is incorrect to use slope conductances as a measure of ion permeation, but it is worth noting that chord and slope conductances can give substantially different numerical values.
###end p 105
###begin title 106
###xml 26 28 26 28 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 28 29 28 29 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
Time-dependent Block by Ca2+o
###end title 106
###begin p 107
###xml 149 151 148 150 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 313 315 312 314 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 326 327 325 326 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 371 372 370 371 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
###xml 374 376 373 375 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 377 379 376 378 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 432 438 431 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig9" ref-type="fig">Fig. 9</xref>
###xml 443 445 442 444 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 487 488 486 487 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 538 539 537 538 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
###xml 541 543 540 542 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 544 546 543 545 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 548 564 547 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib36">Lux et al., 1990</xref>
###xml 595 596 594 595 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
###xml 598 600 597 599 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 601 603 600 602 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 605 625 604 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Carbone et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 688 690 687 689 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 707 728 706 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib32">Lansman et al. (1986)</xref>
###xml 781 783 780 782 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 795 796 794 795 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
###xml 798 800 797 799 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 801 803 800 802 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 830 831 829 830 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 888 890 887 889 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
For most conditions in this study ion entry and exit from the pore are rapid with respect to the time course of gating. The exception was 3-10 muM Ca2+. From three different experimental approaches (single channel gating, noise analysis of macroscopic currents, and macroscopic tail currents), we estimate that Ca2+ blocked CaV3.1 channels at a bimolecular rate of 3 x 108 M-1s-1, with little voltage dependence from -20 to -80 mV (Fig. 9). Ca2+ blocked whole-cell currents carried by Na+ through T channels in sensory neurons at 1.9 x 108 M-1s-1 (Lux et al., 1990), and HVA channels at 1.4 x 108 M-1s-1 (Carbone et al., 1997), although block deviated from bimolecular kinetics at high Ca2+ concentrations. Lansman et al. (1986) reported essentially voltage-independent block by Ca2+ at 4.5 x 108 M-1s-1 for currents carried by Li+ through single L channels, which led to the idea that Ca2+ enters open channels at a diffusion-limited rate.
###end p 107
###begin p 108
###xml 26 28 26 28 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 143 145 143 145 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 170 172 170 172 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 185 187 185 187 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 187 188 187 188 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 261 263 261 263 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 276 278 276 278 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 278 279 278 279 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 504 506 503 505 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 573 575 572 574 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 608 610 607 609 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
Time-dependent block by Ca2+ is visible only in a relatively narrow window of concentration and voltage. For example, the pseudo first-order Ca2+ entry rate would be 30 s-1 at 100 nM Ca2+o (slow with respect to channel gating, including inactivation) or 3,000 s-1 at 0.1 mM Ca2+o (not fully resolved by our voltage clamp). Detection of blocking kinetics also depends on the extent of block, which is weak at extreme voltages. We did not see evidence for time-dependent block in tail currents at 10 muM Ba2+; possible contributing factors include slightly weaker block by Ba2+, and a possible difference in Ba2+ entry rate.
###end p 108
###begin p 109
###xml 2 4 2 4 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 125 140 125 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Fig. 4, C and F</xref>
###xml 142 150 142 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Fig. 5 F</xref>
###xml 217 245 217 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Fukushima and Hagiwara, 1985</xref>
###xml 247 263 247 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib36">Lux et al., 1990</xref>
###xml 265 291 265 291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib48">Shcheglovitov et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 311 331 311 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Carbone et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 418 424 418 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig9" ref-type="fig">Fig. 9</xref>
###xml 524 526 524 526 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 543 545 543 545 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3+</sup>
###xml 615 639 615 639 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">Obejero-Paz et al., 2004</xref>
Ca2+ block has a biphasic voltage dependence, becoming weaker either with strong depolarization or strong hyperpolarization (Fig. 4, C and F; Fig. 5 F). Qualitatively, this agrees with previous results on T channels (Fukushima and Hagiwara, 1985; Lux et al., 1990; Shcheglovitov et al., 2007) and HVA channels (Carbone et al., 1997). From -90 to -20 mV, voltage dependence results primarily from changes in exit rate (Fig. 9). At more depolarized voltages, we do not have separate measurements of entry vs. exit rates for Ca2+, but relief of Y3+ block by strong depolarization primarily reflected accelerated exit (Obejero-Paz et al., 2004).
###end p 109
###begin title 110
Permeation Models and Mechanisms
###end title 110
###begin p 111
###xml 142 168 142 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Almers and McCleskey, 1984</xref>
###xml 170 190 170 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Hess and Tsien, 1984</xref>
###xml 192 209 192 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib58">Yang et al., 1993</xref>
###xml 287 313 287 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib38">Nonner and Eisenberg, 1998</xref>
###xml 315 332 315 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">Boda et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 379 380 379 380 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sub>
###xml 403 409 403 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3</xref>
###xml 522 528 522 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Fig. 5</xref>
Our results are consistent with the traditional view that calcium channel selectivity results from ion-ion competition in a single-file pore (Almers and McCleskey, 1984; Hess and Tsien, 1984; Yang et al., 1993), but also with alternative approaches such as Poisson-Nernst-Planck theory (Nonner and Eisenberg, 1998; Boda et al., 2006). The simple models used here, e.g., fitting VR shifts to GHK theory (Fig. 3), and fitting the concentration dependence of chord conductances to the sum of block and permeation components (Fig. 5), are essentially empirical approaches to data analysis, as opposed to full theories for ion selectivity and permeation. Such analysis can also be applied to current-voltage relationships predicted by a particular quantitative model. We expect that the dataset reported here (Table S2) will be useful to constrain and test future quantitative descriptions of calcium channel permeation.
###end p 111
###begin title 112
Measurement of Channel Activation
###end title 112
###begin p 113
###xml 30 32 30 32 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 39 41 39 41 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 125 128 125 128 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O,r</sub>
###xml 450 451 450 451 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 456 462 456 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 473 493 473 493 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib46">Serrano et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 495 499 495 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib47">2000</xref>
###xml 637 653 637 653 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig11" ref-type="fig">Fig. 11, A and B</xref>
###xml 696 698 696 698 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 804 807 804 807 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O,r</sub>
###xml 823 826 823 826 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1/2</sub>
We also examined effects of Ca2+ and Ba2+ on channel gating, measuring channel activation as the relative open probability (PO,r), the ratio of the peak current evoked by the I-V protocol to the maximally activated current (from the IIV protocol). Many studies on calcium channels use chord conductances as a measure of channel activation, but that is valid only if the instantaneous I-V for an open channel is linear. That is clearly not true for CaV3.1 (Fig. 2; see also Serrano et al., 1999, 2000). The voltage dependence of chord conductances from the I-V protocol differs dramatically from the expected Boltzmann-like relationship (Fig. 11, A and B). However, the G-V relationship in 2 mM Ca2+ is well described by a Boltzmann relationship, if data above -20 mV are not included. Compared with our PO,r analysis, the V1/2 is too negative by approximately13 mV, and the steepness of the activation curve is greatly exaggerated (Fig. S6). The "maximal" conductance in that analysis occurs at -20 mV, where a comparison of I-V and IIV protocols clearly demonstrates that channels are not maximally activated (Fig. S22).
###end p 113
###begin p 114
###xml 174 200 174 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib48">Shcheglovitov et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 584 586 584 586 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 586 587 586 587 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 678 680 678 680 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 680 681 680 681 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 811 814 811 814 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O,r</sub>
The voltage dependence of block can affect voltage shifts measured from the position of peak current with the I-V protocol, or from chord conductances, so such measurements (Shcheglovitov et al., 2007) may not accurately measure shifts in channel gating. Conversely, measurement of fractional inhibition from the I-V protocol (using either currents or conductances) would not accurately estimate the extent of pore block, since that measurement would be affected by both block and by surface charge shifts. For example, measurements from the I-V protocol overestimate the extent of Mg2+o block (and its voltage dependence) at voltages where the activation curve is shifted by Mg2+o (analysis not shown), and the shift in peak inward current (Fig. S14, C and D) greatly exceeds the voltage shift measured using PO,r (Fig. S16 A).
###end p 114
###begin p 115
###xml 80 81 80 81 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 81 82 81 82 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 115 137 115 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib52">Talavera et al., 2003a</xref>
###xml 275 277 275 277 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 277 278 277 278 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 306 307 306 307 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 307 308 307 308 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 359 361 359 361 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 361 362 361 362 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
Channel activation is also shifted by extracellular protons, and the effect of H+o is reduced by divalent cations (Talavera et al., 2003a). That is, reducing the pH from 7.2 (as used in the present study) might shift activation to more negative voltages, especially in low Ca2+o. A differential effect of H+o ions would tend to underestimate the effects of Ca2+o, as measured at pH 7.2. This possibility remains to be evaluated.
###end p 115
###begin title 116
###xml 2 4 2 4 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 4 5 4 5 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 12 14 12 14 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 14 15 14 15 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
Ca2+o vs. Ba2+o: Gating
###end title 116
###begin p 117
###xml 11 13 11 13 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 13 14 13 14 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 21 23 21 23 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 23 24 23 24 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 119 122 119 122 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">O,r</sub>
###xml 127 130 125 128 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IIV</sub>
###xml 133 137 131 135 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peak</sub>
###xml 149 151 147 149 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 151 152 149 150 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 159 161 157 159 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 161 162 159 160 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 190 202 188 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig12" ref-type="fig">Fig. 12, C&#8211;F</xref>
###xml 313 315 311 313 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 315 316 313 314 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 460 485 458 483 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib54">Talavera and Nilius, 2006</xref>
###xml 631 651 629 649 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib50">Stimers et al., 1985</xref>
###xml 700 704 698 702 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Peak</sub>
###xml 740 761 738 759 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib19">Gonoi and Hille, 1987</xref>
Although Ca2+o and Ba2+o shifted activation similarly, direct comparison of our three measures of voltage dependence (PO,r, tauIIV, TPeak) in 2 mM Ca2+o vs. Ba2+o showed subtle differences (Fig. 12, C-F). Simulations indicate that these differences can be explained by faster inactivation of inward currents in Ba2+o. Kinetic coupling of activation to inactivation means that changes in either process can appear to affect the other, at the macroscopic level (Talavera and Nilius, 2006). It has long been appreciated that channel "activation curves" do not solely reflect the activation process for a rapidly inactivating channel (Stimers et al., 1985), and the time course of activation (including TPeak) is also affected by inactivation (Gonoi and Hille, 1987).
###end p 117
###begin p 118
###xml 39 41 39 41 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 41 42 41 42 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 44 53 44 53 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig12" ref-type="fig">Fig. 12 A</xref>
###xml 107 109 107 109 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 109 110 109 110 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 122 123 122 123 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 133 148 133 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Fig. 5, C and D</xref>
###xml 180 182 180 182 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 260 261 260 261 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 267 269 267 269 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 385 387 385 387 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
The inactivation rate increased with Ba2+o (Fig. 12 A), correlating with the concentration dependence of Ba2+o block of Na+ current (Fig. 5, C and D). Therefore, we propose that Ba2+ occupancy of the pore speeds inactivation, compared with pores occupied by Na+ or Ca2+. Although there is ample precedent for effects on gating of pore occupancy by specific ions, it is not clear why Ba2+ would have this particular effect.
###end p 118
###begin title 119
###xml 38 40 38 40 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 47 48 47 48 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
Surface Charge Effects on Gating of Ca2+ and Na+ Channels
###end title 119
###begin p 120
###xml 49 51 49 51 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 51 52 51 52 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 59 61 59 61 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 61 62 61 62 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 75 76 75 76 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 87 105 87 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Hille et al., 1975</xref>
###xml 107 130 107 130 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib42">Ohmori and Yoshii, 1977</xref>
###xml 132 154 132 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">Hanck and Sheets, 1992</xref>
###xml 166 168 166 168 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 168 169 168 169 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 180 203 180 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib42">Ohmori and Yoshii, 1977</xref>
###xml 205 224 205 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib56">Wilson et al., 1983</xref>
###xml 226 248 226 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Cota and Stefani, 1984</xref>
###xml 250 269 250 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Byerly et al., 1985</xref>
###xml 271 292 271 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib30">Kass and Krafte, 1987</xref>
###xml 294 318 294 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">Ganitkevich et al., 1988</xref>
###xml 320 344 320 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib59">Zamponi and Snutch, 1996</xref>
###xml 346 364 346 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib49">Smith et al., 1993</xref>
###xml 369 370 369 370 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 427 429 427 429 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 429 430 429 430 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 437 439 437 439 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 439 440 439 440 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 442 474 442 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Frankenhaeuser and Hodgkin, 1957</xref>
###xml 476 494 476 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Hille et al., 1975</xref>
###xml 496 518 496 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">Hanck and Sheets, 1992</xref>
###xml 568 569 568 569 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 628 630 628 630 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 630 631 630 631 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 635 637 635 637 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 637 638 637 638 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 646 648 646 648 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 648 649 648 649 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 651 667 651 667 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig11" ref-type="fig">Fig. 11, E and F</xref>
###xml 721 722 721 722 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 767 769 767 769 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 769 770 769 770 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 777 779 777 779 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 779 780 779 780 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 782 804 782 804 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib31">Klugbauer et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 806 823 806 823 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib29">Kaku et al., 2003</xref>
Activation occurs at more positive voltages in Ca2+o vs. Ba2+o, for both Na+ channels (Hille et al., 1975; Ohmori and Yoshii, 1977; Hanck and Sheets, 1992) and HVA Ca2+o channels (Ohmori and Yoshii, 1977; Wilson et al., 1983; Cota and Stefani, 1984; Byerly et al., 1985; Kass and Krafte, 1987; Ganitkevich et al., 1988; Zamponi and Snutch, 1996; Smith et al., 1993). Na+ channels also activate at more positive voltages with Ca2+o vs. Mg2+o (Frankenhaeuser and Hodgkin, 1957; Hille et al., 1975; Hanck and Sheets, 1992). It is striking that this is not true for the CaV3.1 T-channel, where activation was shifted similarly by Ca2+o, Ba2+o, and Mg2+o (Fig. 11, E and F). Qualitatively, it has been noted previously that CaV3 channel activation is comparable between Ca2+o and Ba2+o (Klugbauer et al., 1999; Kaku et al., 2003), but a quantitative comparison of the concentration dependence does not seem to have been reported, and implications for the molecular basis of the surface charge have not been noted.
###end p 120
###begin p 121
###xml 39 40 39 40 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 70 71 70 71 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 82 84 82 84 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 171 172 171 172 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 216 217 216 217 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 238 239 238 239 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 247 248 247 248 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 367 368 367 368 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 403 404 403 404 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
###xml 618 619 618 619 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 648 649 648 649 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V</sub>
Why might surface charge effects for CaV3 channels differ from both Na+ and HVA Ca2+ channels? Evolutionarily, based on analysis of conservation of amino acid sequence, CaV3 channels are almost equidistant between Na+ channels vs. HVA (CaV1 and CaV2) channels. One known difference is that IQ and EF hand-like domains in the proximal C terminus are conserved among Na+ and HVA channels, but absent in CaV3. It seems highly unlikely that these intracellular domains are causally related to surface charge effects observed for extracellular cations, but this is a precedent for functional characteristics shared among Na+ and HVA channels, but not CaV3.
###end p 121
###begin p 122
###xml 192 194 192 194 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 194 195 194 195 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 230 232 230 232 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 232 233 232 233 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">o</sub>
###xml 304 322 304 322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Hille et al., 1975</xref>
###xml 324 343 324 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Byerly et al., 1985</xref>
###xml 572 595 572 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Elinder and Arhem, 1999</xref>
Our data are generally consistent with a classical Gouy-Chapman model, where ions shift channel activation by screening a uniform surface charge. In contrast, the larger voltage shifts with Ca2+o have led to the suggestion that Ca2+o can also bind to the surface charge, i.e., Gouy-Chapman-Stern theory (Hille et al., 1975; Byerly et al., 1985). It now seems abundantly clear that the "surface charge" is specific to a particular channel. For potassium channels, the surface charge may result primarily from charged groups on extracellular domains of the channel protein (Elinder and Arhem, 1999). This remains to be explored for calcium channels.
