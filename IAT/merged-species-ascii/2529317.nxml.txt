###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 0 26 0 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Phenylobacterium zucineum </italic>
###xml 176 188 176 188 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 0 25 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">Phenylobacterium zucineum</span>
###xml 103 108 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 176 187 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
Phenylobacterium zucineum is a recently identified facultative intracellular species isolated from the human leukemia cell line K562. Unlike the known intracellular pathogens, P. zucineum maintains a stable association with its host cell without affecting the growth and morphology of the latter.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 65 66 65 66 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T</sup>
###xml 318 340 318 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caulobacter crescentus</italic>
###xml 392 404 392 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 522 535 522 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. crescentus</italic>
###xml 318 340 <span type="species:ncbi:155892">Caulobacter crescentus</span>
###xml 392 403 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
###xml 522 535 <span type="species:ncbi:155892">C. crescentus</span>
Here, we report the whole genome sequence of the type strain HLK1T. The genome consists of a circular chromosome (3,996,255 bp) and a circular plasmid (382,976 bp). It encodes 3,861 putative proteins, 42 tRNAs, and a 16S-23S-5S rRNA operon. Comparative genomic analysis revealed that it is phylogenetically closest to Caulobacter crescentus, a model species for cell cycle research. Notably, P. zucineum has a gene that is strikingly similar, both structurally and functionally, to the cell cycle master regulator CtrA of C. crescentus, and most of the genes directly regulated by CtrA in the latter have orthologs in the former.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 68 84 68 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Phenylobacterium</italic>
###xml 173 187 173 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. crescentus </italic>
###xml 191 202 191 202 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum</italic>
###xml 173 186 <span type="species:ncbi:155892">C. crescentus</span>
###xml 191 202 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
This work presents the first complete bacterial genome in the genus Phenylobacterium. Comparative genomic analysis indicated that the CtrA regulon is well conserved between C. crescentus and P. zucineum.
###end p 7

Background

###begin p 9

###xml 0 25 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">Phenylobacterium zucineum</span>
###xml 264 277 <span type="species:ncbi:273793">P. lituiforme</span>
###xml 292 301 <span type="species:ncbi:279827">P. falsum</span>
###xml 315 326 <span type="species:ncbi:21">P. immobile</span>
###xml 337 348 <span type="species:ncbi:266125">P. koreense</span>
###xml 368 379 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
###xml 525 530 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 581 592 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
###xml 835 840 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 910 915 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 994 1000 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 1407 1418 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
Phenylobacterium zucineum strain HLK1T is a facultative intracellular microbe recently identified by us [1]. It is a rod-shaped Gram-negative bacterium 0.3-0.5 x 0.5-2 mum in size. It belongs to the genus Phenylobacterium [2], which presently comprises 5 species, P. lituiforme (FaiI3T) [3], P. falsum (AC49T) [4], P. immobile (ET) [2], P. koreense (Slu-01T) [5], and P. zucineum (HLK1T) [1]. They were isolated from subsurface aquifer, alkaline groundwater, soil, activated sludge from a wastewater treatment plant, and the human leukemia cell line K562, respectively. Except for P. zucineum, they are environmental bacteria, and there is no evidence that these microbes are associated with eukaryotic cells. The HLK1T strain, therefore, represents the only species so far in the genus Phenylobacterium that can infect and survive in human cells. Since most, if not all, of the known microbes that can invade human cells are pathogenic, we proposed that HLK1T may have pathogenic relevance to humans [1]. Unlike the known intracellular pathogens that undergo a cycle involving invasion, overgrowth, and disruption of the host cells, and repeating the cycle by invading new cells, HLK1T is able to establish a stable parasitic association with its host, i.e., the strain does not overgrow intracellularly to kill the host, and the host cells carry them to their progeny. One cell line (SW480) infected with P. zucineum has been stably maintained for nearly three years in our lab (data not shown).

In this report, we present the complete genome sequence of P. zucineum.

###end title 11
###begin title 12
Genome anatomy
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 107 108 107 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 116 117 116 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
The genome is composed of a circular chromosome (3,996,255 bp) and a circular plasmid (382,976 bp) (Figure 1; Table 1). The G + C contents of chromosome and plasmid are 71.35% and 68.5%, respectively. There are 3,861 putative protein-coding genes (3,534 in the chromosome and 327 in the plasmid), of which 3,180 have significant matches in the non-redundant protein database. Of the matches, 585 are conserved hypothetical proteins and 2,595 are proteins with known or predicted functions. Forty-two tRNA genes and one 16S-23S-5S rRNA operon were identified in the chromosome.

###begin p 24
On the basis of COG (Cluster of Orthologous Groups) classification, the chromosome is enriched in genes for basic metabolism, such as categories E (amino acid transport and metabolism) and I (lipid transport and metabolism), accounting for 8.29% and 6.09% of the total genes in the chromosome, respectively. On the other hand, the plasmid is enriched for genes in categories O (posttranslational modification, protein turnover, chaperones) and T (signal transduction mechanisms), constituting 12.96% and 9.72% of the total genes in the plasmid, respectively.

As to genes in the plasmid that cope with environmental stimuli, about half of the genes in category O are molecular chaperones (17/32), including 2 dnaJ-like molecular chaperones, 2 clusters of dnaK and its co-chaperonin grpE (PHZ_p0053-0054 and PHZ_p0121-122), a cluster of groEL and its co-chaperonin groES (PHZ_p0095-0096), and 9 heat shock proteins Hsp20. Of 23 genes in category T, there is one cluster (FixLJ, PHZ_p0187-0188), which is essential for the growth of C. crescentus under hypoxic conditions [6].

###begin title 26
General metabolism
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 274 275 274 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 308 311 308 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kdh</italic>
###xml 351 354 351 354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">kgd</italic>
###xml 727 730 727 730 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">apt</italic>
###xml 767 770 767 770 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ade</italic>
###xml 807 810 807 810 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdd</italic>
###xml 832 836 832 836 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">codA</italic>
###xml 858 861 858 861 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tdk</italic>
###xml 881 885 881 885 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">deoA</italic>
###xml 912 915 912 915 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">upp</italic>
###xml 951 954 951 954 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">udk</italic>
###xml 976 979 976 979 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">udp</italic>
The enzyme sets of glycolysis and the Entner-Doudoroff pathway are complete in the genome. All genes comprising the pentose phosphate pathway except gluconate kinase were identified, consistent with our previous experimental result that the strain cannot utilize gluconate [1]. The genome lacks two enzymes (kdh, alpha ketoglutarate dehydrogenase and kgd, alpha ketoglutarate decarboxylase), making the oxidative and reductive branches of the tricarboxylic acid cycle operate separately. The genome has all the genes for the synthesis of fatty acids, 20 amino acids, and corresponding tRNAs. Although full sets of genes for the biosynthesis of purine and pyrimidine were identified, enzymes for the salvage pathways of purine (apt, adenine phosphoribosyltransferase; ade, adenine deaminase) and pyrimidine (cdd, cytidine deaminase; codA, cytosine deaminase; tdk, thymidine kinase; deoA, thymidine phosphorylase; upp, uracil phosphoribosyltransferase; udk, uridine kinase; and udp, uridine phosphorylase) were absent. The plasmid encodes some metabolic enzymes, such as those participating in glycolysis, the pentose phosphate pathway, and the citric acid cycle. However, it is worth noting that the plasmid has a gene (6-phosphogluconate dehydrogenase) that is the only copy in the genome (PHZ_p0183).
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 37 53 37 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Phenylobacterium</italic>
###xml 147 148 147 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 258 277 258 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas putida </italic>
###xml 280 281 280 281 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 284 296 284 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 528 529 528 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T3">3</xref>
###xml 258 276 <span type="species:ncbi:303">Pseudomonas putida</span>
###xml 284 295 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
Like most other species in the genus Phenylobacterium, the strain is able to use L-phenylalanine as a sole carbon source under aerobic conditions [1]. A recent study revealed that phenylalanine can be completely degraded through the homogentisate pathway in Pseudomonas putida U [7]. P. zucineum may use the same strategy to utilize phenylalanine, because all the enzymes for the conversion of phenylalanine through intermediate homogentisate to the final products fumarate and acetoacetate are present in the chromosome (Table 3).

###begin title 30
Functional elements responding to environmental transition
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 4 6 4 6 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T </sup>
###xml 1003 1004 1003 1004 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T4">4</xref>
###xml 1137 1149 1137 1149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 1335 1336 1335 1336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 1434 1435 1434 1435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T5">5</xref>
###xml 1504 1523 1504 1523 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pseudomonas putida </italic>
###xml 1562 1563 1562 1563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 1566 1578 1566 1578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 1609 1614 1609 1614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rpoH </italic>
###xml 1698 1699 1698 1699 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T6">6</xref>
###xml 1853 1854 1853 1854 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1137 1148 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
###xml 1504 1529 <span type="species:ncbi:160488">Pseudomonas putida KT2440</span>
###xml 1566 1577 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
HLK1T is able to survive intracellularly and extracellularly. Consistently, the genome contains the fundamental elements to support the life cycle in different environments. The genome contains abundant two-component signal transduction proteins, transcriptional regulators, and heat shock response proteins, enabling the strain to respond to extra- and intra-cellular stimuli at transcriptional and post-translational levels. Among the total of 102 two-component signal transduction proteins (91 in the chromosome and 11 in the plasmid), there are 36 histidine kinases, 48 response regulators, and 18 hybrid proteins fused with histidine kinase and response regulator. Sixteen pairs of histidine kinase and response regulator (1 in the plasmid) are adjacently aligned and may act as functional operons. These tightly linked modules make two-component signal transduction systems respond to environmental changes efficiently. The genome encodes 170 transcriptional regulators (16 in the plasmid) (Table 4). Notably, we annotated the proteins of 93 bacteria (see methods - comparative genomics) with the same annotation criteria used for P. zucineum and found that the fraction of two-component signal transduction proteins and transcriptional regulators was positively correlated with the capacity for environmental adaptation (Figure 2). The genome contains 17 extracytoplasmic function (ECF) sigma factors (3 in the plasmid) (Table 5). ECFs are suggested to play a role in environmental adaptation for Pseudomonas putida KT2440, whose genome contains 19 ECFs [8]. P. zucineum has 3 heat shock sigma factors rpoH (2 in the plasmid) and 33 heat shock molecular chaperons (17 in the plasmid) (Table 6), which can cope with a variety of stresses, including cellular energy depletion, extreme concentrations of heavy metals, and various toxic substances. [9].

###xml 229 241 223 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 277 291 271 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. crescentus </italic>
###xml 74 85 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
###xml 167 180 <span type="species:ncbi:155892">C. crescentus</span>
###xml 229 240 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>

Consistent with the results from in silico sequence analysis, the CtrA of P. zucineum can restore the growth of temperature-sensitive strain LC2195 (a CtrA mutant) of C. crescentus [23] at 37degreesC, indicating that the CtrA of P. zucineum can functionally compliment that of C. crescentus in our experimental conditions (data not shown).
###end p 74
###begin p 75
###xml 44 56 44 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 60 74 60 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. crescentus </italic>
###xml 44 55 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
###xml 60 73 <span type="species:ncbi:155892">C. crescentus</span>
Taken together, the comparative genomics of P. zucineum and C. crescentus suggests that the cell cycle of the former is likely to be regulated similarly to that of the latter.
###end p 75
###begin title 76
###xml 34 39 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Presence of ESTs of the strain in human
###end title 76
###begin p 77
###xml 6 18 6 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 29 31 29 31 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T </sup>
###xml 93 94 93 94 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 285 296 285 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum</italic>
###xml 329 331 329 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T14">14</xref>
###xml 528 540 528 540 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>

###xml 183 188 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>

Since P. zucineum strain HLK1T can invade and persistently live in several human cell lines [1], we were curious about whether this microbe can infect humans. By blasting against the human EST database (dbEST release 041307 with 7,974,440 human ESTs) with the whole genome sequence of P. zucineum, we found 9 matched ESTs (Table 14), of which 3 were from a library constructed from tissue adjacent to a breast cancer, and 6 were from a library constructed from a cell line of lymphatic origin. The preliminary data suggest that P. zucineum may invade humans.

###begin title 81
Conclusion
###end title 81
###begin p 82
###xml 68 84 68 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Phenylobacterium</italic>
###xml 199 211 199 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 242 256 242 256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. crescentus </italic>
###xml 186 191 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 199 210 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
###xml 242 255 <span type="species:ncbi:155892">C. crescentus</span>
This work presents the first complete bacterial genome in the genus Phenylobacterium. Genome analysis reveals the fundamental basis for this strain to invade and persistently survive in human cells. P. zucineum is phylogenetically closest to C. crescentus based on comparative genome analysis.
###end p 82
###begin title 83
Methods
###end title 83
###begin title 84
Bacterial growth and genomic library construction
###end title 84
###begin p 85
###xml 0 12 0 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 23 24 23 24 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T</sup>
###xml 130 131 124 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 0 11 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
P. zucineum strain HLK1Twas grown in LB (Luria-Bertani) broth at 37degreesC and then harvested for the preparation of genomic DNA[1]. Genomic DNA was prepared using a bacterial genomic DNA purification kit (V-Gene Biotech., Hangzhou, China) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Sheared DNA samples were fractionated to construct three different genomic libraries, containing average insert sizes of 2.0-2.5 kb, 2.5-3.0 kb and 3.5-4.0 kb. The resulting pUC18-derived library plasmids were extracted using the alkaline lysis method and subjected to direct DNA sequencing with automated capillary DNA sequencers (ABI3730 or MegaBACE1000).
###end p 85
###begin title 86
Sequencing and finishing
###end title 86
###begin p 87
###xml 14 26 14 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 131 133 131 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 134 136 134 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 246 248 246 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 14 25 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
The genome of P. zucineum was sequenced by means of the whole genome shotgun method with the phred/phrap/consed software packages [24-27]. Sequencing and subsequent gene identification was carried out as described in our earlier publications [28-30]. Briefly, during the shotgun sequence phase, clones were picked randomly from three shotgun libraries and then sequenced from both ends. 44,667 successful sequence reads (>100 bp at Phred value Q13), accounting for 5.47x sequence coverage of the genome, were assembled into 563 sequence contigs representing 60 scaffolds connected by end-pairing information.
###end p 87
###begin p 88
###xml 1584 1588 1584 1588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">repA</italic>
###xml 1590 1594 1590 1594 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">repB</italic>
###xml 1596 1601 1596 1601 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">parA </italic>
###xml 1605 1609 1605 1609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">parB</italic>
The finishing phase involved iterative cycles of laboratory work and computational analysis. To reduce the numbers of scaffolds, reads were added into initial contig assembly by using failed universal primers as primers and by using plasmid clones that extended outwards from the scaffolds as sequence reaction templates. To resolve the low-quality regions, resequencing of the involved reads in low quality regions with universal primers and primer walking the plasmid clones were the first choice, otherwise, resequencing with alternate temperature conditions resolved the remaining low-quality regions. New sequence reads obtained from the above laboratory work were assembled into existing contigs, which yielded new contigs and new scaffolds connected by end-pairing information. Then, consed interface helped us to do nest round of laboratory work based on new arisen contig assembly. After about four iterative cycles of the above "finish" procedures to close gaps and to resolve the low-quality regions, the PCR product obtained by using total genomic DNA as template was sequenced from both ends to close the last physical gap. In addition, the overall sequence quality of the genome was further improved by using the following criteria: (1) two independent high-quality reads as minimal coverage, and (2) Phred quality value = Q40 for each given base. Collectively, 3,542 successful reads were incorporated into initial assembles during the finishing phase. The final assembly was composed of two circular "contigs", of which a smaller one with a protein cluster (including repA, repB, parA and parB) related to plasmid replication was assigned as the plasmid, and the larger one was the chromosome.
###end p 88
###begin title 89
Annotation
###end title 89
###begin p 90
###xml 44 46 44 46 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 96 98 96 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 99 101 99 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 442 444 442 444 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 579 581 579 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 1086 1089 1084 1087 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-10</sup>
###xml 1423 1425 1421 1423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 1664 1666 1662 1664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 1725 1727 1723 1725 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
tRNA genes were predicted with tRNAscan-SE [31]. Repetitive sequences were detected by REPuter [32,33], coupled with intensive manual alignment. We identified and annotated the protein profiles of chromosome and plasmid with the same workstream. For the chromosome, the first set of potential CDSs in the chromosome was established with Glimmer 2.0 trained with a set of ORFs longer than 500 bp from its genomic sequence at default settings [34]. The resulting 5,029 predicted CDSs were BLAST searched against the NCBI non-redundant protein database to determine their homology [35]. 1,174 annotated proteins without the word "hypothetical" or "unknown" in their function description, and without frameshifts or in-frame stop codons, were selected as the second training set. The resulting second set of 4,018 predicted CDSs (assigned as "predicted CDSs") were searched against the NCBI non-redundant protein database. Predicted CDSs that accorded with the following BLAST search criteria were considered "true proteins": (1) 80% of the query sequence was aligned and (2) E-value </= 1e-10. Then, the ORFs extracted from the chromosome region among "true proteins" were searched against the NCBI non-redundant protein database. The ORFs satisfying the same criteria as true proteins were considered "true ORFs". Overlapping proteins were manually inspected and resolved, according to the principle we described previously [30]. The final version of the protein profile comprised three parts: true proteins, true ORFs, and predicted CDSs located in the rest of the genome. The translational start codon of each protein was identified by the widely used RBS script [36] and then refined by comparison with homologous proteins [30].
###end p 90
###begin p 91
###xml 130 132 130 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 203 205 203 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 297 299 297 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 300 302 300 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
To further investigate the function of each protein, we used InterProScan to search against the InterPro protein family database [37]. The up-to-date KEGG pathway database was used for pathway analysis [38]. All proteins were searched against the COG database which included 66 completed genomes [39,40]. The final annotation was manually inspected by comprehensively integrating the results from searching against the databases of nr, COG, KEGG, and InterPro.
###end p 91

###begin title 94
Comparative genomics
###end title 94
###begin p 95
###xml 180 192 180 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 288 300 288 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 359 362 359 362 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-10</sup>
###xml 180 191 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
###xml 288 299 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
Sequence data for comparative analyses were obtained from the NCBI database . The database has 520 completely sequenced bacterial genomes (sequences downloaded on 2007/06/05). All P. zucineum ORFs were searched against the ORFs from all other bacterial genomes with BLASTP. The number of P. zucineum ORFs matched to each genome with significance (E value = 1e-10) was calculated.
###end p 95
###begin p 96
###xml 272 274 272 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 1021 1033 1021 1033 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. zucineum </italic>
###xml 1021 1032 <span type="species:ncbi:284016">P. zucineum</span>
To illustrate the contribution of transcriptional regulators and two-component signal transduction proteins to environmental adaptation, we compared the mean fraction of these two types of proteins in bacteria living in 6 different habitats, as described by Merav Parter [42]. These are: (1) obligate bacteria that are necessarily associated with a host, (2) specialized bacteria that live in specific environments, such as marine thermal vents, (3) aquatic bacteria that live in fresh or seawater, (4) facultative bacteria, free-living bacteria that are often associated with a host, (5) multiple bacteria that live in many different environments, and (6) terrestrial bacteria that live in the soil. For bacteria with more than one sequenced strain, we chose only one strain for the comparative study. The numbers of bacterial species in each group were: 26 obligate, 5 specialized, 4 aquatic, 28 facultative, 27 multiple, and 3 terrestrial. We annotated the proteins of these 93 species with the same workflow used for P. zucineum and calculated the mean fraction of transcriptional regulators and two-component signal transduction proteins.
###end p 96
###begin p 97
###xml 118 130 118 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. meliloti </italic>
###xml 139 148 139 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G. oxydan</italic>
###xml 178 186 178 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. suis </italic>
###xml 210 224 210 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. crescentus </italic>
###xml 253 264 253 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R. conorii </italic>
###xml 118 134 <span type="species:ncbi:266834">S. meliloti 1021</span>
###xml 139 154 <span type="species:ncbi:290633">G. oxydans 621H</span>
###xml 178 190 <span type="species:ncbi:204722">B. suis 1330</span>
###xml 210 228 <span type="species:ncbi:190650">C. crescentus CB15</span>
###xml 253 277 <span type="species:ncbi:272944">R. conorii str. Malish 7</span>
In addition, we annotated the ORFs of 5 representative alphaproteobacteria with different habitats (multiple bacteria S. meliloti 1021 and G. oxydans 621H, facultative bacterium B. suis 1330, aquatic bacterium C. crescentus CB15, and obligate bacterium R. conorii str. Malish 7) using the same workflow and computed the distributions of proteins involved in environmental adaptation.
###end p 97
