Defective nucleotide excision repair in yeast hpr1 and tho2 mutants
###end article-title 0

###begin p 2
###xml 773 777 773 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HPR1</italic>
###xml 782 786 782 786 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">THO2</italic>
###xml 967 971 967 971 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RPB2</italic>
###xml 1017 1021 1017 1021 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 1026 1030 1026 1030 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 529 534 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 873 878 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Nucleotide excision repair (NER) and transcription are intimately related. First, TFIIH has a dual role in transcription initiation and NER and, secondly, transcription leads to more efficient repair of damage present in transcribed sequences. It is thought that elongating RNAPII, stalled at a DNA lesion, is used for the loading of the NER machinery in a process termed transcription-coupled repair (TCR). Non-transcribed regions are repaired by the so-called global genome repair (GGR). We have previously defined a number of yeast genes, whose deletions confer transcription-dependent hyper-recombination phenotypes. As these mutations cause impairment of transcription elongation we have assayed whether they also affect DNA repair. We show that null mutations of the HPR1 and THO2 genes, encoding two prominent proteins of the THO complex, increase UV sensitivity of yeast cells lacking GGR. Consistent with this result, molecular analyses of DNA repair of the RPB2 transcribed strand using T4 endo V show that hpr1 and tho2 do indeed impair TCR. However, this effect is not confined to TCR alone because the mutants are slightly affected in GGR. These results indicate that THO affects both transcription and NER. We discuss different alternatives to explain the effect of the THO complex on DNA repair.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 204 205 204 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c1">1</xref>
###xml 206 207 206 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c2">2</xref>
###xml 282 283 282 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c2">2</xref>
###xml 213 218 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Nucleotide excision repair (NER) is an evolutionarily conserved DNA repair pathway that maintains the genome free of a wide variety of lesions, such as those induced by UV light and some chemical agents (1,2). In yeast, more than 25 proteins participate in this multi-step process (2).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 182 198 182 198 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 788 789 788 789 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c3">3</xref>
###xml 790 791 790 791 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c4">4</xref>
###xml 886 887 886 887 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c5">5</xref>
###xml 888 889 888 889 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c7">7</xref>
###xml 1002 1003 1002 1003 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c8">8</xref>
###xml 1004 1005 1004 1005 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c9">9</xref>
###xml 182 198 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 232 237 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 242 248 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 608 613 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
One of the most intriguing aspects of all forms of DNA repair is the mechanism by which a DNA lesion is detected. In NER, even though the proteins that recognize the lesion (UvrA in Escherichia coli and Rad4/Rad23 and XPC/HHR23B in yeast and humans) have been identified, it is not clear how such proteins are targeted to the site of the lesion. According with the mechanism of lesion detection, NER can be divided into two subpathways: global genome repair (GGR) and transcription-coupled repair (TCR). The first one acts in all regions of the genome irrespective of whether or not they are transcribed. In yeast, GGR requires Rad7, a protein carrying leucine-rich repeats and Rad16, a member of the SWI2/SNF2 subfamily of putative helicases. These proteins presumably act in a complex (3,4) that might be required in chromatin remodeling to facilitate damage detection by Rad4/Rad23 (5-7). TCR depends on the product of the Rad26 gene and is confined to the transcribed strands (TS) of active genes (8,9).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 396 404 396 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in&#160;vitro</italic>
###xml 406 408 406 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c10">10</xref>
###xml 574 576 574 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c11">11</xref>
TCR takes advantage of the incapacity of the RNA polymerases (RNAP) to pass particular DNA lesions such as UV-induced cyclobutane-pyrimidine dimers (CPDs). RNAPII, stalled at a DNA lesion, is used for the loading of the NER machinery at the site of the lesion. It has been shown that a stalled bacterial RNAP at sites of base damage in the TS inhibits the repair of CPDs by the UvrABC NER system in vitro (10). This suggests that TCR requires the displacement of RNAP from the damaged site to allow NER, a reaction mediated by a transcription-repair coupling factor (TRCF) (11).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 50 52 50 52 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c12">12</xref>
###xml 53 55 53 55 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c13">13</xref>
###xml 230 235 230 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 262 263 262 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c9">9</xref>
###xml 264 266 264 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c14">14</xref>
###xml 269 274 269 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad28</italic>
###xml 303 305 303 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c15">15</xref>
###xml 452 454 452 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c16">16</xref>
###xml 455 457 455 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c17">17</xref>
###xml 668 670 668 670 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c18">18</xref>
###xml 727 735 727 735 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 737 739 737 739 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c19">19</xref>
###xml 740 742 740 742 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c20">20</xref>
###xml 817 819 817 819 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c21">21</xref>
###xml 1117 1119 1113 1115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c22">22</xref>
###xml 1297 1299 1293 1295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c23">23</xref>
###xml 82 87 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 144 149 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 183 188 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 224 229 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1210 1215 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In eukaryotes, in which TCR was first discovered (12,13), TRCF candidates are the human Cockayne syndrome B and A proteins, or their respective yeast orthologs Rad26 and Rad28. While human cells deficient in CSB and CSA and yeast rad26 mutants show TCR defects (9,14), rad28 mutants are TCR proficient (15). CSB and Rad26 belong to the SWI2/SNF2 subfamily of putative helicases. CSB, as well as bacterial TRCF, have been shown to have ATPase activity (16,17), but no helicase activity has been detected for either protein. However, the function of CSB/Rad26 has not yet been established. The observations that CSB-deficient cells display reduced transcription levels (18), that purified CSB enhances RNAPII-mediated elongation in vitro (19,20) and that CSB resides in a RNA polymerase II (RNAPII)-containing complex (21) have raised the possibility that CSB/Rad26 is a transcription elongation factor. The TCR phenotype of CSB/Rad26-deficient cells could be a consequence of impaired transcription elongation. The observations that alpha-amanitin, an inhibitor of transcription elongation, inhibits TCR in CHO cells (22) and that the absence of the Spt4 elongation factor bypasses the requirement for Rad26 in yeast TCR would be consistent with the idea that CSB/Rad26 is not the eukaryotic TRCF (23).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 31 35 31 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HPR1</italic>
###xml 37 41 37 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">THO2</italic>
###xml 43 47 43 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MFT1</italic>
###xml 52 56 52 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">THP2</italic>
###xml 188 190 188 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c24">24</xref>
###xml 191 193 191 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c26">26</xref>
###xml 277 279 277 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c24">24</xref>
###xml 397 399 397 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c27">27</xref>
###xml 400 402 400 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c28">28</xref>
###xml 483 485 483 485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c29">29</xref>
###xml 638 640 638 640 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c24">24</xref>
###xml 641 643 641 643 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c31">31</xref>
###xml 699 701 699 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c26">26</xref>
###xml 702 704 702 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c27">27</xref>
###xml 705 707 705 707 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c30">30</xref>
###xml 918 920 918 920 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c27">27</xref>
###xml 921 923 921 923 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c28">28</xref>
We have identified four genes, HPR1, THO2, MFT1 and THP2, whose null mutations confer a strong hyper-recombination phenotype between repeats that is dependent on transcription elongation (24-26). These genes encode proteins that belong to the same protein complex, termed THO (24), which connects transcription with mitotic recombination. Mutations in these genes impair transcription elongation (27,28). Such an impairment is particularly strong in long and G+C-rich DNA sequences (29). As a consequence of the transcription elongation defect, mutants of the THO complex show an increased recombination frequency between direct repeats (24-31), and a high frequency of chromosome and plasmid loss (26,27,30). We have hypothesized that THO may have a role in transcription elongation. In the mutants of the THO complex, transcription elongation could be impaired leading to the formation of recombinogenic structures (27,28).
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 197 201 197 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HPR1</italic>
###xml 206 210 206 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">THO2</italic>
###xml 309 313 309 313 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 318 322 318 322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
Considering the existing connection between transcription and repair and the connection between transcription and genetic instability provided by the THO complex, we have asked whether mutation of HPR1 and THO2, the two most prominent components of the THO complex, impair NER. We show that this is the case. hpr1 and tho2 are affected in TCR, but additionally also slightly in GGR. Altogether, our results support a general effect of the THO complex in NER.
###end p 9

###begin title 13
UV survival curves
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 44 46 44 46 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c32">32</xref>
###xml 56 59 56 59 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600</sub>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
Yeast cells were grown in YEPD-rich medium (32) to an OD600 of 1. Different dilutions were plated on YEPD and irradiated with the different UV doses with a 254 nm UV lamp (Philips T UV 30 W). Plates were incubated for 3 days at 30degreesC in the dark before colonies were counted and survival was calculated. All survival curves shown represent the average of three independent experiments.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
UV irradiation and DNA isolation
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 67 70 67 70 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600</sub>
###xml 226 227 226 227 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
Yeast cultures were grown in 500 ml of YEPD overnight to a final OD600 of 1.8. Cells were collected by centrifugation, resuspended in 1.6 l chilled phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and irradiated with a 254 nm UV lamp at 25 J/m2. Subsequently, cells were incubated in YEPD for various time courses in the dark at 30degreesC. After this, cell pellets collected by centrifugation were frozen in liquid nitrogen and maintained at -80degreesC until DNA was isolated.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 85 87 85 87 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c33">33</xref>
###xml 153 154 153 154 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">9</sup>
###xml 662 664 658 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c34">34</xref>
For DNA isolation we followed a modified version of previously published procedures (33). Pellets were resuspended to a final concentration of 1.5-2 x 109/ml in NIB pH 7.2 (17% glycerol, 50 mM MOPS, 150 mM KAc, 2 mM MgCl, 0.5 mM spermidine and 0.15 mM spermine) with 3 mg/ml Zymoliase-20T and incubated at room temperature for 20 min. Cellular suspensions were diluted seven times with chilled water and centrifuged to collect nuclei. Pellets were resuspended in 5.4 ml of TE (50 mM Tris-HCl pH 8, 20 mM EDTA) and mixed with 600 microl of 10% SDS. After phenol extraction and ethanol precipitation, DNA was finally purified on CsCl gradients and dialyzed in TE (34).
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Gene- and strand-specific DNA repair assays
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 50 53 50 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pvu</italic>
###xml 59 62 59 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pvu</italic>

###xml 143 146 143 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hae</italic>

###xml 206 214 202 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HML&#945;</italic>
###xml 233 236 225 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hin</italic>
###xml 267 271 259 263 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MATa</italic>
###xml 285 289 277 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMRa</italic>
###xml 300 302 292 294 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c37">37</xref>
###xml 495 496 481 482 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 582 584 562 564 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>
Five micrograms of genomic DNA were digested with PvuI and PvuII, generating a 5.2 kb RPB2 fragment (35) and a 4.7 kb GAL7 fragment (36), with HaeII, which generates a 3.5 kb MATalpha fragment and a 4.1 kb HMLalpha fragment, or with HindIII, which generates a 4.3 kb MATa and a 5.1 kb HMRa fragment (37). In each case, DNA samples were divided in two halves. One was incubated with T4 endonuclease V (T4 endo V) at 37degreesC for 2 h. After electrophoresis and Southern blot transfer to Hybond N+, samples were hybridized at 65degreesC (7% SDS and 0.5 M phosphate buffer pH 7) with 32P-labeled DNA probes. The same filters were rehybridized up to four times, after removing probes by harsh treatment with boiling 0.1% SDS. Bands were quantified in a Fujix FLA3000. All values of the bands corresponding to the T4-untreated samples were divided by the T4-mock sample of zero time. These values were multiplied by their corresponding values of the T4-treated sample and subtracted from the T4-treated zero time to eliminate the basal background. The final percentage of repair was calculated by dividing these resulting values by the value of the T4-mock-treated zero time.
###end p 19

For determination of RNA levels after UV treatment, yeast cells were grown at 30degreesC to an OD600 of approximately0.5 in 100 ml YEP-3% glycerol-2% lactate, harvested by centrifugation and resuspended in 100 ml of PBS pH 7. Half of the culture was irradiated with a 254 nm UV light lamp at 70 J/m2 and the other half was used as the unirradiated control. Each half was then grown at 30degreesC in 60 ml YEP-2% galactose medium. At the indicated time 10 ml aliquots were taken, collected by centrifugation, frozen in liquid nitrogen and maintained at -80degreesC until RNA extraction. Ten micrograms of each RNA sample were subjected to northern analysis following previously published procedures (27). Filters were first hybridized with a 0.75 kb PvuII/AvaI GAL1 probe and then re-hybridized with a 589 bp DNA internal fragment obtained by PCR as described (27). mRNA levels were quantified in a Fujix FLA3000 analyzer and are given as arbitrary units. All values were normalized with respect to the 28S rRNA. Northern analysis of RPB2 was performed with RNA isolated from mid-log phase cultures according to standard procedures (34).

###xml 1396 1397 1396 1397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf315f2">2</xref>
The hpr1 and tho2 mutations confer a defect in transcription elongation that causes a strong transcription-dependent hyper-recombination phenotype between DNA repeats. As NER consists of two subpathways, one of which, TCR, relies on an elongating RNAPII to detect DNA lesions, we decided to assay if hpr1 and tho2 mutations cause a defect in TCR. These mutations by themselves do not confer a DNA repair defect detectable as a UV-sensitivity phenotype (see Fig. 1) (25). As can be seen in Figure 1, the same UV-sensitivity curves were obtained in wild-type, hpr1, tho2 and rad26 strains, impaired in TCR, as well as in double mutant combination hpr1 rad26 and tho2 rad26. As the TCR defect of rad26 strains is only observed as a UV-sensitivity phenotype when GGR is also abolished, we decided to construct double mutant strains hpr1 rad7, tho2 rad7, hpr1 rad16 and tho2 rad16. As shown in Figure 1 the viability upon UV irradiation of these double mutants was significantly reduced below the levels of the single mutants rad7 and rad16. The survival curves were similar to those of rad7 rad26 and rad16 rad26 double mutants. This result indicates that both hpr1 and tho2 impair NER in the absence of GGR. In addition, triple mutants affected in rad26, hpr1/tho2 and rad7/rad16 display an increased UV sensitivity as compared with the double mutants hpr1/tho2 rad7/rad16 or rad26 rad7/rad16 (Fig. 2). Thus, Rad26 and Hpr1/Tho2 play different roles in TCR.

###xml 3 8 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
In yeast, RNA synthesis of induced genes is initially inhibited after UV irradiation (38), probably a consequence of the inability of the RNAPII to pass through CPDs accumulated in the TS of genes. To determine whether the ability of resuming RNA synthesis after UV irradiation in cells affected in TCR is impaired, we determined the ability of rad26 and tho2 cells to transcribe the endogenous GAL1 gene after irradiation with 70 J/m2 UV. As can be seen in Figure 3, even in the absence of UV irradiation the rad26 and tho2 mutants display reduced transcription rates. Furthermore, after UV irradiation, there was a significant retardation in the kinetics of activation of GAL1 in all strains. Whereas non-irradiated rad26 cells reached 59% of the RNA levels of wild-type cells, this value was only 11% in non-irradiated tho2 cells. Consistent with previous observations (26,39), our results reflect an intrinsic defect of both types of mutants in transcription, regardless of UV damage.

To determine, at the molecular level, whether hpr1 and tho2 impair TCR we analyzed the ability of hpr1 and tho2 cells to repair the TS and NTS of the constitutively expressed RPB2 gene. The kinetics of repair of UV-induced lesions was followed by T4 endo V treatment. This methodology previously established that both wild-type and rad7 cells show the same rates of repair in the TS of RPB2, whereas rad26 cells show reduced rates (9,37). In addition, it is known that wild-type and rad26 cells show the same kinetics of repair in the NTS of RPB2, whereas rad7 cells show no repair (9,37). We have confirmed these results in our W303 isogenic strains (data not shown).

Figure 4 shows that in both the hpr1 and tho2 mutants there is a defect in the repair of the TS, especially observed as a reduction in the initial rate of repair. The defect in the repair of the TS of RPB2 in both the hpr1 and tho2 mutants is as strong as in rad26, although in rad26 cells this effect is persistent and never reaches wild-type levels of DNA repair.

Given that hpr1 and tho2 mutants are affected in DNA repair and transcription we decided to determine the levels of transcription of RPB2 in all mutants analyzed (Fig. 5). Northern analysis shows that in all strains, including rad7, there was a 40-50% reduction in the overall levels of RPB2 RNA. Besides the transcriptional effect of hpr1 and tho2 on transcription elongation (26,27), it is worth noting that the expression levels of RPB2, GAL1 and GAL7 genes in rad26 mutants were below 40% of the wild-type levels (Figs 3 and 5; data not shown). Therefore, DNA lesions might cause the low transcriptional levels observed in untreated rad26 and rad7 cells. If the assumption is correct, it could also imply that Rad26 may not necessarily be directly involved in transcription elongation, in contrast to what has been recently suggested (39).

As the analysis of RPB2, a constitutively expressed gene, indicates that hpr1 and tho2 cells are affected in the kinetics of repair of both the TS and the NTS, we analyzed whether repair of non-transcribed genes was also affected. We first determined the kinetics of repair of both strands of the GAL7 gene under repressed conditions. As expected, the efficiency of TCR proficient rad7 cells to repair the TS of the repressed GAL7 gene was reduced as compared with its ability to repair the TS of the constitutively expressed RPB2 gene (Fig. 6; compare with Fig. 4). We also analyzed GGR in the silenced HMLalpha and HMRa loci that lack transcription due to silencing. As can be seen in Figure 7, repair is completely abolished in rad7 cells, supporting a lack of transcription and associated TCR in these loci and the dependence of GGR on Rad7. ###xml 177 181 177 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 186 190 186 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 249 253 249 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad7</italic>
###xml 257 262 257 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad16</italic>
###xml 345 349 345 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 354 358 354 358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 415 419 415 419 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RPB2</italic>
###xml 433 438 433 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 461 466 461 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 474 478 474 478 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 483 487 483 487 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
We have shown that hpr1 and tho2 cells, impaired in transcription elongation and having a strong transcription-dependent hyper-recombination phenotype, are impaired in NER. The hpr1 and tho2 mutations confer UV sensitivity when combined with either rad7 or rad16, which abolish GGR. Molecular analyses of DNA repair with T4 endo V indicate that hpr1 and tho2 cells are impaired in their ability to repair the TS of RPB2 similarly to rad26 cells. In contrast to rad26 cells, hpr1 and tho2 also show repair defects of non-transcribed DNA, although at levels that are only detected at the molecular level.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 118 122 118 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 127 131 127 131 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 238 242 238 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RPB2</italic>
###xml 311 312 311 312 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf315f4">4</xref>
###xml 400 405 400 405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 417 421 417 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 426 430 426 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 525 529 525 529 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RPB2</italic>
###xml 548 552 548 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GAL7</italic>
###xml 578 586 578 582 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HML&#945;</italic>
###xml 591 595 587 591 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">HMRa</italic>
###xml 607 608 603 604 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf315f4">4</xref>
###xml 610 611 606 607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf315f6">6</xref>
###xml 616 617 612 613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf315f7">7</xref>
###xml 705 715 701 711 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26 hpr1</italic>
###xml 716 720 712 716 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 791 801 787 797 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26 hpr1</italic>
###xml 802 806 798 802 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 1125 1129 1121 1125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 1134 1138 1130 1134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 1146 1151 1142 1147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad26</italic>
###xml 1254 1258 1250 1254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ</italic>
Hpr1 and Tho2 are part of the THO complex, which connects transcription to genomic instability. Our observations that hpr1 and tho2 reduce UV survival only in strains impaired in GGR, and impair the repair of the constitutively expressed RPB2 gene upon UV irradiation, as determined by T4 endo V analysis (Fig. 4) indicate that the THO complex modulates or has a role in TCR. However, in contrast to rad26 mutations, hpr1 and tho2 have a slight effect on GGR. Thus, defective repair is also physically detected in the NTS of RPB2, in the repressed GAL7 gene and in the silenced HMLalpha and HMRa loci (Figs 4, 6 and 7). Such a defect in GGR cannot be observed genetically as a UV-sensitivity phenotype of rad26 hpr1/tho2 double mutants. In addition, in GGR-less strains the double mutations rad26 hpr1/tho2 confer a more severe UV-sensitivity phenotype than each single mutation separately. These results indicate that the putative role that Hpr1 and Tho2 might have in NER is not exclusive of TCR and is different to that of Rad26. Indeed, this is not the only difference between Rad26 and Hpr1/Tho2, because in contrast to hpr1 and tho2 cells, rad26 cells do not show a detectable hyper-recombination phenotype and are able to transcribe the bacterial lacZ sequences (S.Gonzalez-Barrera, A.G.Rondon and A.Aguilera, unpublished results).
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 23 27 23 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad7</italic>
###xml 51 55 51 55 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RPB2</italic>
###xml 67 68 67 68 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf315f5">5</xref>
###xml 229 233 229 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 238 242 238 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 297 301 297 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 306 310 306 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 432 436 432 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
The negative effect of rad7 on the accumulation of RPB2 mRNA (Fig. 5) indicates that defective transcription does not necessarily imply defective TCR. However, it may not be surprising that the transcription elongation defect of hpr1 and tho2 is responsible for the TCR defect. The NER defects of hpr1 and tho2 cells are unlikely to be caused by lack of expression of a particular NER gene, because global transcription analysis of tho2 cells using DNA microarrays shows that all known NER genes are transcribed (M.Gallardo, K.Ohta and A.Aguilera, unpublished results).
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 31 35 31 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 40 44 40 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 187 188 187 188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf315f4">4</xref>
###xml 240 244 240 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 249 253 249 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 304 305 304 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkf315f4">4</xref>
###xml 668 669 668 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c1">1</xref>
###xml 754 758 754 758 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 763 767 763 767 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 824 828 824 828 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 833 837 833 837 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 595 600 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
It is interesting to note that hpr1 and tho2 cells show a low rate of DNA repair, that is, a low amount of UV lesions repaired per minute as inferred by the slope of the curves of Figure 4. However, such slopes are similar in wild-type and hpr1 and tho2 mutants after 60-80 min upon UV irradiation (Fig. 4), suggesting that after this time, mutant cells recover the wild-type capacity of repair. In any case, the overall levels of removed UV lesions are still below wild-type levels at 120 min upon UV irradiation. This might reflect a process of adaptation. It is known that pre-irradiation of yeast cells enhances the removal of lesions induced by a second UV dose (1). Therefore, we cannot exclude the possibility that a slower adaptation response of hpr1 and tho2 cells to UV damage could make the DNA repair defects of hpr1 and tho2 mutants more severe.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 84 88 84 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 93 97 93 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 125 129 125 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 134 138 134 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
###xml 606 610 606 610 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 615 619 615 619 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tho2</italic>
At first, a transient sequestering of NER proteins could explain the NER defects of hpr1 and tho2 mutants. If the defects of hpr1 and tho2 in transcription elongation lead to frequent stalling of the elongating RNAPII, independently of UV lesions, a recruitment of the NER machinery could occur at many sites. As a consequence, NER proteins would be transiently sequestered, leading to a reduction of available NER components, and a concomitant defect in repair. Thus, a stalled RNAPII could recruit TFIIH and other NER factors regardless of the presence of a DNA lesion. Alternatively, the NER defects of hpr1 and tho2 could be explained by a putative unknown role of THO in NER.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 123 133 123 133 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 293 295 293 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c41">41</xref>
###xml 296 298 296 298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c42">42</xref>
###xml 350 352 350 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c43">43</xref>
###xml 487 489 487 489 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c44">44</xref>
###xml 507 531 507 531 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 536 561 536 561 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</italic>
###xml 575 579 575 579 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">XAB2</italic>
###xml 613 615 613 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c45">45</xref>
###xml 616 618 616 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c46">46</xref>
###xml 123 133 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 138 144 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 173 178 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 507 531 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 536 561 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
Proteins connecting DNA repair and transcription could include, besides TFIIH, the transcription terminator factors 2 from Drosophila and humans (DmF2 and HuF2) and XAB2, a human protein identified by its ability to bind the NER protein XPA. DmF2 and HuF2 are members of the SWI2/SNF2 family (41,42) and dissociate RNAPI and RNPAII stalled at a CPD (43). XAB2 interacts with CSB, CSA and RNAPII and anti-XAB2 antibodies inhibit TCR and transcription when micro- injected in fibroblasts (44). Interestingly, Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Schizosaccharomyces pombe orthologs of XAB2 seem to have a role in splicing (45,46), opening the possibility that RNA-processing proteins associated with elongation might influence TCR.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 43 47 43 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 84 86 84 86 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c47">47</xref>
###xml 182 184 182 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c48">48</xref>
###xml 185 187 185 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c49">49</xref>
###xml 238 242 238 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 250 252 250 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c50">50</xref>
###xml 268 272 268 272 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sub2</italic>
###xml 328 332 328 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hpr1</italic>
###xml 334 336 334 336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c50">50</xref>
###xml 519 523 519 523 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rad3</italic>
###xml 568 572 568 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sub2</italic>
###xml 574 576 574 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkf315c51">51</xref>
In this sense it is important to note that hpr1 mutants are affected in RNA export (47) and that overexpression of Sub2, a putative RNA helicase involved in RNA splicing and export (48,49), suppresses the hyper-recombination phenotype of hpr1 cells (50). In addition, sub2 mutants show similar hyper-recombination phenotypes to hpr1 (50). Furthermore, a genetic interaction between Sub2 and Rad3, a component of TFIIH involved in RNAPII-dependent transcription and NER, has recently been inferred from the isolation of rad3 alleles that suppress the growth defects of sub2 (51). Our study, therefore, not only extends the possible functional role of THO to NER, but opens the possibility of a connection between DNA repair, transcription and the processing of the nascent RNA. The identification of the biochemical function of the THO complex will help to understand the mechanisms connecting these processes.
