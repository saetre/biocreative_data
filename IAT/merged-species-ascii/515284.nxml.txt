Site-specific mutagenesis of Drosophila proliferating cell nuclear antigen enhances its effects on calf thymus DNA polymerase delta
###end article-title 0

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 395 399 383 387 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1/2 </sub>
###xml 574 581 556 563 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 630 638 612 620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
We and others have shown four distinct and presumably related effects of mammalian proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA) on DNA synthesis catalyzed by mammalian DNA polymerase delta(pol delta). In the presence of homologous PCNA, pol delta exhibits 1) increased absolute activity; 2) increased processivity of DNA synthesis; 3) stable binding of synthetic oligonucleotide template-primers (t1/2 of the pol delta*PCNA*template-primer complex >/=2.5 h); and 4) enhanced synthesis of DNA opposite and beyond template base lesions. This last effect is potentially mutagenic in vivo. Biochemical studies performed in parallel with in vivo genetic analyses, would represent an extremely powerful approach to investigate further, both DNA replication and repair in eukaryotes.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 0 11 0 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 322 333 318 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 103 108 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 177 181 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 192 197 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 231 235 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
Drosophila PCNA, although highly similar in structure to mammalian PCNA (e.g., it is >70% identical to human PCNA in amino acid sequence), can only substitute poorly for either calf thymus or human PCNA (~10% as well) in affecting calf thymus pol delta. However, by mutating one or only a few amino acids in the region of Drosophila PCNA thought to interact with pol delta, all four effects can be enhanced dramatically.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 142 153 138 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 188 196 184 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 313 322 305 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 355 366 347 358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 432 441 424 433 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 488 493 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 501 505 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
Our results therefore suggest that all four above effects depend at least in part on the PCNA-pol delta interaction. Moreover unlike mammals, Drosophila offers the potential for immediate in vivo genetic analyses. Although it has proven difficult to obtain sufficient amounts of homologous pol delta for parallel in vitro biochemical studies, by altering Drosophila PCNA using site-directed mutagenesis as suggested by our results, in vitro biochemical studies may now be performed using human and/or calf thymus pol delta preparations.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 5 29 5 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster </italic>
###xml 222 223 218 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 224 225 220 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 261 262 249 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 263 264 251 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 297 298 285 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 341 342 329 330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 343 344 331 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 413 415 401 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 416 418 404 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 525 534 513 522 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 568 579 556 567 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 599 600 587 588 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 5 28 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 475 479 <span type="species:ncbi:10633?0.9966199903428296">SV40</span>
###xml 549 553 <span type="species:ncbi:10633?0.9966199903428296">SV40</span>
Many Drosophila melanogaster homologs of the proteins required for both DNA replication and repair have been identified and in several cases purified to apparent homogeneity. These include DNA polymerase alpha holoenzyme [1,2], DNA polymerase delta(pol delta) [2-4], replication protein A (RP-A; [5]), replication factor C (RF-C; e.g., see [6-9]) and various origin recognition complex (ORC) subunits (see e.g., [10,11]). Moreover, complete replication of DNA containing the SV40 origin of replication has been reconstituted in vitro using purified SV40 T-antigen and Drosophila cell-free extracts [7].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 103 114 103 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 305 307 305 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 334 336 334 336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 386 397 386 397 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 440 448 440 448 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 670 684 670 684 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila mus</italic>
###xml 695 697 695 697 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 719 730 719 730 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 832 834 832 834 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 213 218 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 239 243 <span type="species:ncbi:10633?0.9966199903428296">SV40</span>
###xml 440 447 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
A protein about which much information has been obtained is proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA). Drosophila PCNA was first identified both as a highly purified protein able to substitute, albeit poorly, for human PCNA in a cell-free SV40 DNA replication system reconstituted from purified proteins [12] and by Yamaguchi et al. [13] who used an oligonucleotide probe to detect the Drosophila PCNA cDNA and gene, express the protein in E. coli and deduce its complete amino acid sequence. Further results indicated that in flies, PCNA was encoded by a single gene located at position 56F5-15 on the right arm of chromosome 2. This was subsequently identified as the Drosophila mus209 locus [14]. Recently, a second Drosophila PCNA gene of limited homology to the original and of unknown biological function has also been found [15].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 100 102 100 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 103 105 103 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 170 178 170 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 179 181 179 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 189 190 189 190 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 291 293 291 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 404 415 400 411 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 500 502 496 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 62 67 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 129 134 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 170 177 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 237 242 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Protocols have been established for purification of wild-type human PCNA from tissue culture cells [16,17], unmodified wild-type human PCNA after regulated expression in E. coli [18] and NH2-terminally his-tagged but otherwise wild-type human PCNA, also engineered for bacterial expression [19]. All were comparably effective at stimulating mammalian pol delta. Similar protocols have been developed for Drosophila PCNA and strategies for site-directed mutagenesis have been devised and implemented [20].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 24 26 24 26 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 39 41 39 41 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 74 76 74 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 391 393 383 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 506 508 494 496 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 536 547 524 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 726 737 714 725 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 153 158 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 399 404 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 525 530 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 772 777 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Recently, Zhang et al. [21] (see also [22]) as well as others (e.g., see [23]) identified the interdomain connector loop of PCNA (amino acids 119-133 of human PCNA) as crucial for binding pol delta. Of note, relative to wild-type PCNA, mutations of the molecule within this region such as glutamine at position 125 changed to glutamic acid (Q125E) promoted increased pol delta-processivity [21]. In human PCNA, residues 123, 126, 127 and 128 were defined as being essential for interaction with pol delta [21]. Comparison of human with Drosophila PCNA sequences in this region indicated that of these four amino acids, three (residues 126, 127 and 128) are identical. The fourth, residue 123, is glutamine (Q123) in wild-type Drosophila PCNA. The corresponding residue in human PCNA is valine (V).
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 149 160 145 156 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 336 347 332 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 420 422 412 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 423 425 415 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 504 506 492 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 507 509 495 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 590 601 578 589 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 817 828 801 812 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 1002 1013 982 993 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 1378 1386 1350 1358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 1416 1427 1388 1399 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 200 205 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 374 378 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 429 433 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">Calf</span>
###xml 445 450 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 716 720 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 889 894 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 948 952 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 1078 1083 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1119 1123 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
To investigate the role of the interdomain connector loop of PCNA on the effects of PCNA on pol delta, we mutagenized residues within this region of Drosophila PCNA so that they more nearly resembled human amino acids. After bacterial expression and purification, we tested the effects of these site-specifically modified ("humanized") Drosophila PCNA molecules on purified calf thymus pol delta (two-subunit form; see [17,24]). Calf thymus and human pol delta are highly similar in amino acid sequence [25-27] and can, for our purposes, be used interchangeably. "Humanization" of a single Drosophila PCNA residue, conversion of Q123 to V (Q123V), conferred upon it, enhanced ability to affect several properties of calf thymus pol delta. More extensive mutagenesis, in which the entire interdomain connector loop of Drosophila PCNA (amino acids 119-133) was replaced by the corresponding human residues, was still more effective at stimulation of calf thymus pol delta, than either wild-type or Q123V Drosophila PCNA. However, it was considerably less effective than wild-type human PCNA at altering the properties of calf thymus pol delta. These results therefore suggest that in addition to the interdomain connnector loop, other regions of PCNA are also important effectors of pol delta activity. They also provide a means to couple operationally, the considerable power of in vivo genetic analyses performed in Drosophila with the sophistication of mammalian biochemistry.
###end p 13

To study the role of the interdomain connector loop of PCNA (amino acids 119-133), we compared human and Drosophila homologs. Of the 15 interdomain connector loop residues, nine are identical between the two; identical residues are shaded (Fig. 1A). Overall, Drosophila PCNA is >70% identical to that from mammals (e.g., humans; see [13]). Others showed that PCNA residues 123, 126, 127 and 128 were essential for interaction with pol delta [28]. Of these four, only one (residue 123) differs between flies and humans. Also shown is a model constructed from the X-ray crystallographically determined structure of PCNA indicating the locations of the sites to be mutated in Drosophila PCNA (Fig. 1B). Shown (Fig. 1B) is the X-ray crystal structure of human PCNA. The Drosophila homolog is assumed to be similar.

###begin title 20
###xml 15 19 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
Stimulation of calf thymus pol delta activity by highly purified wild-type versus selected mutant PCNA fractions
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 69 71 65 67 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 377 379 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 392 394 384 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>

###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">Calf</span>
###xml 268 272 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 283 288 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 354 358 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 507 512 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 552 556 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 729 733 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 887 892 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1087 1092 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>

Calf thymus pol delta (apparently homogeneous two-subunit form; see [24]) was purified and assayed for polymerase activity in the presence of varying concentrations of both highly purified wild-type and specific mutant PCNA molecules. We showed previously that either calf thymus or human PCNA could be used interchangeably as stimulatory co-factors for calf thymus pol delta [29] (see also [12,18,19]). Assays were performed using poly(dA)-oligo(dT) as described (Experimental Procedures). As can be seen, human PCNA resulted in robust stimulation of calf thymus pol delta; much less stimulation was observed for wild-type Drosophila PCNA (Fig. 3). Mutation of Drosophila PCNA resulted in substantially increased stimulation of calf thymus pol delta; both substitution of a single amino acid (Q123V dPCNA) and replacement of the entire fly interdomain connector loop with corresponding human amino acids (dr119-133h dPCNA) had demonstrable effects. Of note, at relatively high concentrations, Drosophila PCNA but with the entire fly interdomain connector loop replaced by corresponding human amino acids (dr119-133h dPCNA) was similarly effective to wild-type human PCNA at stimulating the activity of calf thymus pol delta; however, it was considerably less effective at lower concentrations (Fig. 3). This suggests an effect on binding of PCNA to pol delta and/or on mutant PCNA multimerization.

###begin title 32
Discussion
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 24 35 24 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 123 134 123 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 291 293 287 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 294 296 290 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 331 342 327 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 459 470 455 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 554 555 546 547 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 577 578 569 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 636 637 624 625 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 702 703 690 691 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 757 768 745 756 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 885 886 873 874 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 887 888 875 876 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 889 890 877 878 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 891 892 879 880 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 935 946 923 934 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 9 14 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 174 179 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 213 217 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 488 492 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 832 837 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1009 1014 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1042 1046 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
Although human PCNA and Drosophila PCNA are more than 70% identical at the level of primary amino acid sequence, wild-type Drosophila PCNA is only a very poor substitute for human PCNA in cell-free reactions with calf thymus pol delta. This is documented both in this report and previously [12,32]. However, mutating only a single Drosophila PCNA amino acid, glutamine at position 123 (Q123) to valine (V), leads to a dramatic enhancement in the abilities of Drosophila PCNA to stimulate calf thymus pol delta. Effects were shown on total activity (Fig. 3), processivity (Fig. 4), pol delta*PCNA*template-primer complex formation (Fig. 5) and extended DNA synthesis beyond a template abasic site (Fig. 6). Replacing the entire interdomain connector loop of Drosophila PCNA (amino acids 119-133) with the corresponding residues from human PCNA resulted in additional enhancement (Figs. 3,4,5,6), but in neither case were the mutants of Drosophila PCNA (Q123V dPCNA or dr119-133h dPCNA) equivalent to wild-type human PCNA in the stimulation of calf thymus pol delta.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 41 52 41 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 263 274 259 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 351 356 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our data indicate that although a single Drosophila PCNA amino acid at position 123 (in addition to conserved residues 126-128) is very important for pol delta-stimulation, the further enhancement of stimulation seen when the entire interdomain connector loop of Drosophila PCNA (amino acids 119-133) was replaced with the corresponding residues from human PCNA suggests that other residues in this loop are also involved directly in binding pol delta. Alternatively, it is possible that loop residues other than 123 and 126-128 play a secondary or indirect (e.g., conformational) role in positioning crucial amino acids so as to optimize their direct binding to pol delta.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 478 487 466 475 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 614 621 602 609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 819 821 807 809 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 966 969 954 957 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eq </sub>
###xml 163 168 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 205 209 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 285 290 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 306 310 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 917 922 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In this context, we would like to call attention to the fact that at relatively low concentrations, dr119-133h dPCNA is considerably less effective than wild-type human PCNA in stimulating the activity of calf thymus pol delta; at higher concentrations, dr119-133h dPCNA and wild-type human PCNA stimulate calf thymus pol delta similarly. This implies complex protein-protein interactions between PCNA and pol delta such that biochemical properties recorded in dilute solutions in vitro may not accurately predict properties manifest at much different and generally much higher intranuclear concentrations present in vivo. Alternatively, PCNA must be present as a trimer (three-subunit ring) in order to function. Since the equilibrium among monomer, dimer and trimer was shown to depend on PCNA protein concentration [33], it is certainly possible that the difference observed between dr119-133h dPCNA and wild-type human PCNA actually reflects differences in the Keq for PCNA multimerization. These two possibilities, concerning both complicated pol delta*PCNA interactions and PCNA multimerization, are not mutually exclusive.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 81 92 81 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 826 828 806 808 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 829 831 809 811 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 832 834 812 814 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 978 989 958 969 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 156 161 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 224 228 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 249 254 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Similarly, the fact that replacement of the entire interdomain connector loop of Drosophila PCNA (amino acids 119-133) with the corresponding residues from human PCNA did not result in a molecule as effective in stimulating calf thymus pol delta as human PCNA suggests that regions other than the interdomain connector loop are important for pol delta-stimulation. Our data do not address the question of whether these putative "other regions" affect pol delta directly (e.g., like the interdomain loop) or indirectly (e.g., through conformational effects on other regions of the molecule that do bind pol delta directly). Additional mutagenesis studies may shed light on this issue. For example, based on experiments of others, it seems likely that the extreme C-terminus of PCNA also interacts directly with pol delta (see [23,34-36]). Hence it may be of interest to perform similar mutagenesis experiments to those reported here, focusing instead on the C-terminal region of Drosophila PCNA, rather than the interdomain connector loop.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 55 57 55 57 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 75 77 75 77 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 122 136 122 136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 173 175 173 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 112 117 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 122 135 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
We think it should also be noted that both Oku et al. [35] and Ola et al. [36] prepared hybrid proteins between human and S. cerevisiae PCNA. As in our studies, Ola et al. [36] found that regions other than the interdomain connector loop of PCNA were important for interaction with pol delta. These authors suggested that additional interacting regions were likely to exist both in the PCNA C-terminus and N-terminus.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 97 108 97 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 141 150 141 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 297 305 293 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 393 396 389 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mus</italic>
###xml 481 483 477 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 570 578 562 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 704 715 696 707 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 173 177 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
It may also be of interest to prepare double-mutants, first in the interdomain connector loop of Drosophila PCNA, thereby allowing efficient in vitro function with purified calf thymus pol delta, and then elsewhere in the PCNA molecule corresponding to interesting sites defined phenotypically by in vivo genetic studies of others. For example, it might be possible to determine if particular mus209 mutations leading to enhanced mutagen sensitivity among affected organisms (see [37] and references therein) alter any functional interactions between PCNA and pol delta in vitro. Results of such studies could lead to novel biochemical insights regarding the mechanism(s) by which point mutations in the Drosophila PCNA gene lead to enhanced mutagen sensitivity among animals bearing these mutations.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 476 478 472 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 1078 1081 1066 1069 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mus</italic>
###xml 1274 1276 1262 1264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 582 587 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The strategy taken here will presumably allow study of interactions between PCNA and other proteins with which it interacts. In this context, we think it important to note that partial effects on pol delta-stimulation have been recorded. This suggests that our methodology will also allow detection of partial rather than complete effects on the binding of other proteins. Interactions between PCNA and many of the molecules with which it interacts have recently been mapped [23] and for example, one might immediately compare interactions between several mammalian proteins (e.g., human RF-C, DNA ligase I, FEN I and/or p21) and both various wild-type and mutant PCNA molecules described in this paper. Functional (e.g., effects on pol delta activity) as well as direct binding measurements may be made. As with PCNA*pol delta interactions, it may ultimately be feasible to correlate interesting PCNA molecules defined phenotypically using genetic analyses performed in living animals and biochemical studies of specific PCNA*protein binding. For example, do mutagen sensitive mus209 animals bear mutations in a region of PCNA responsible for MSH binding? Both MSH3 and MSH6 were reported to possess a consensus motif for binding to the interdomain connector loop of PCNA [38].
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 132 134 128 130 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 135 137 131 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 392 394 388 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 472 474 468 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 627 629 619 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 630 632 622 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 633 635 625 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 648 657 640 649 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. pombe </italic>
###xml 658 660 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 676 690 668 682 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S. cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 691 693 683 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 694 696 686 688 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 234 238 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">calf</span>
###xml 648 656 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S. pombe</span>
###xml 676 689 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
Finally, we think it important to note that pol delta has most recently been reported to contain at least four subunits (see e.g., [39,40]) yet all experiments performed here were with the two-subunit form of the enzyme purified from calf thymus. We and others have shown that the larger subunit, p125, is catalytic while the smaller, p50, does not seem to contact the DNA closely (see e.g, [41]), but instead, is required for processivity-stimulation by PCNA (e.g., see [42]) to which it apparently binds. It is also clear that PCNA binds to what has been termed, the third pol delta subunit, p68 or p66 in mammalian systems [39,43,44], Cdc27p in S. pombe [40] and Pol32p in S. cerevisiae [45,46]. Clearly the physiologically important interaction between PCNA (either mutant or wild-type) and this third pol delta subunit was omitted from our analyses, but could markedly affect any or all of the responses of polymerase to PCNA that we reported here.
###end p 40
###begin title 41
Conclusions
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 40 51 40 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 118 129 118 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila </italic>
###xml 1144 1152 1124 1132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 1182 1191 1162 1171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 141 146 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Through our experiments, we showed that Drosophila PCNA could be "humanized" and that "humanization" (mutation of key Drosophila residues to human ones) increased effects on mammalian pol delta. The highly purified two-subunit form of pol delta was used for all of our studies. It is possible, though we think it unlikely, that different conclusions would be reached if a different form of pol delta (three-or four-subunit) was used. Nevertheless two of the effects we observed could be considered beneficial. They were enhancement of polymerase activity and processivity. A third effect seems likely to be detrimental, at least over the long term, that is increased synthesis opposite and beyond a chemically defined template base lesion (TLS). Our data suggest that all three of these effects result from enhancement of PCNA-dependent stability of the pol delta*PCNA*template-primer complex. In other words, in the range that we have studied, the more tightly pol delta binds to DNA, the greater its activity, the greater its processivity, but also the more likely it is to catalyze TLS. Our results provide an explicit approach to correlate in vivo genetic studies with rigorous in vitro biochemistry.
###end p 42
