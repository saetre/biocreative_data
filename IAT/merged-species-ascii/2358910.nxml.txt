The katG mRNA of Mycobacterium tuberculosis and Mycobacterium smegmatis is processed at its 5' end and is stabilized by both a polypurine sequence and translation initiation

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 3 30 3 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 37 61 37 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium smegmatis </italic>
###xml 65 69 65 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA</italic>
###xml 70 75 70 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 177 203 177 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis furA-katG </italic>
###xml 242 255 242 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 404 409 404 409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA </italic>
###xml 464 469 464 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA </italic>
###xml 501 506 501 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 551 555 551 555 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA</italic>
###xml 3 29 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 37 60 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">Mycobacterium smegmatis</span>
###xml 177 192 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 242 254 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
In Mycobacterium tuberculosis and in Mycobacterium smegmatis the furA-katG loci, encoding the FurA regulatory protein and the KatG catalase-peroxidase, are highly conserved. In M. tuberculosis furA-katG constitute a single operon, whereas in M. smegmatis a single mRNA covering both genes could not be found. In both species, specific 5' ends have been identified: the first one, located upstream of the furA gene, corresponds to transcription initiation from the furA promoter; the second one is the katG mRNA 5' end, located in the terminal part of furA.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 141 154 141 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 291 307 291 307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 311 324 311 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 363 368 363 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sigA </italic>
###xml 385 390 385 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 510 515 510 515 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 597 602 597 602 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 739 752 739 752 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 792 797 792 797 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 952 957 952 957 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 141 153 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 291 306 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 311 323 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 739 751 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
In this work we demonstrate by in vitro transcription and by RNA polymerase Chromatin immunoprecipitation that no promoter is present in the M. smegmatis region covering the latter 5' end, suggesting that it is produced by specific processing of longer transcripts. Several DNA fragments of M. tuberculosis and M. smegmatis were inserted in a plasmid between the sigA promoter and the lacZ reporter gene, and expression of the reporter gene was measured. A polypurine sequence, located four bp upstream of the katG translation start codon, increased beta-galactosidase activity and stabilized the lacZ transcript. Mutagenesis of this sequence led to destabilization of the mRNA. Analysis of constructs, in which the polypurine sequence of M. smegmatis was followed by an increasing number of katG codons, demonstrated that mRNA stability requires translation of at least 20 amino acids. In order to define the requirements for the 5' processing of the katG transcript, we created several mutations in this region and analyzed the 5' ends of the transcripts: the distance from the polypurine sequence does not seem to influence the processing, neither the sequence around the cutting point. Only mutations which create a double stranded region around the processing site prevented RNA processing.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 159 169 159 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA-katG </italic>
###xml 198 203 198 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA </italic>
This is the first reported case in mycobacteria, in which both a polypurine sequence and translation initiation are shown to contribute to mRNA stability. The furA-katG mRNA is transcribed from the furA promoter and immediately processed; this processing is prevented by a double stranded RNA at the cutting site, suggesting that the endoribonuclease responsible for the cleavage cuts single stranded RNA.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 128 145 128 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 329 330 329 330 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 331 332 331 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 448 456 448 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 457 458 457 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 459 460 459 460 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 128 144 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 448 455 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
mRNA decay is known to play an important role in the post-transcriptional regulation of gene expression in bacteria. Studies in Escherichia coli indicated a possible model, in which the endoribonuclease RNase E binds and cuts the mRNA, followed by RNase II and/or PNPase that degrade the fragments generated by RNase E cleavage [1-5]. The accessibility of the 5' end of an mRNA to RNase E has been shown to be an important mediator of stability in E. coli [6,7].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 124 142 124 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacillus subtilis </italic>
###xml 146 168 146 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacillus thuringiensis</italic>
###xml 375 376 375 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 377 379 377 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 697 698 697 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 699 701 699 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 124 141 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">Bacillus subtilis</span>
###xml 146 168 <span type="species:ncbi:1428">Bacillus thuringiensis</span>
Much less is known about mRNA decay in other bacteria, particularly in gram positive species. Several studies, performed in Bacillus subtilis and Bacillus thuringiensis, indicated that the 5' terminal leader sequence has a relevant role in mRNA stabilization, either by containing a polypurine rich sequence or by the presence of a predicted 5'-terminal stem-loop structure [8-12]. Extensive mutagenesis was used to define how these two motifs influence mRNA stability and the presence of an RNase E-like ribonuclease, acting on the 5' end, has been suggested. Its action may be blocked by either the stem-loop structure and/or ribosomes located in proximity of the 5' terminus of the transcript [8,12].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 77 79 77 79 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 80 82 80 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 149 172 149 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium smegmatis</italic>
###xml 353 355 353 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 149 172 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">Mycobacterium smegmatis</span>
In mycobacteria few examples of mRNAs stability control have been described [13-15]. The most interesting is the stability of the DNA gyrase mRNA in Mycobacterium smegmatis, which is 5' protected by a stem-loop structure, followed by a Shine-Dalgarno sequence for translation initiation. Disruption of the stem-loop caused loss of transcript stability [15].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 46 51 46 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 139 144 139 144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA </italic>
###xml 148 152 148 152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG</italic>
###xml 158 163 158 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 337 342 337 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 356 358 356 358 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 359 361 359 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 556 558 556 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 559 561 559 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 751 753 751 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
In this work we report that the mycobacterial katG mRNA originates from specific processing of the longer bicistronic transcript, covering furA and katG. The katG mRNA encodes the catalase-peroxidase, responsible for the degradation of toxic oxygen compounds. In all studied mycobacterial species, the DNA region immediately upstream of katG encodes FurA [16-19]. Fur-like proteins are ubiquitous in bacteria, and act as transcriptional repressors that exhibit an iron-dependent DNA binding activity and regulate several genes involved in iron metabolism [20,21]. In Mycobacteria this role is not achieved by FurA, but by the iron dependent transcription regulator (IdeR) which plays a critical role in maintaining the intracellular iron homeostasis [22].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 192 194 192 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 195 197 195 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
In recent years, genes directly or indirectly controlled by Fur-like proteins have been discovered, leading to the hypothesis that Fur proteins have a wider role in bacterial gene expression [23,24].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 41 51 41 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA-katG </italic>
###xml 123 125 123 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 129 131 129 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 194 204 194 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA-katG </italic>
###xml 218 238 218 238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium bovis </italic>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 246 248 246 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>

###xml 432 437 432 437 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA </italic>
###xml 452 454 452 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>

###xml 463 465 463 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 501 506 501 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 538 543 538 543 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 546 548 546 548 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 566 571 566 571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 613 615 613 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 620 622 620 622 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 218 241 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">Mycobacterium bovis BCG</span>
In mycobacteria, the organization of the furA-katG region is conserved, suggesting that it may constitute a single operon [16,17,19]. This hypothesis was confirmed by identification of a single furA-katG transcript in Mycobacterium bovis BCG [18,25]. In apparent contrast, two different 5' ends were identified by S1 mapping: the first coincides with the initiation codon of furA and is generated by transcription initiation at the furA promoter (Fig. 1A; [18,25,26]); the second is 54 bp upstream of katG translation start codon. Master et al. [25] proposed that a katG promoter was present in this region (Fig. 1A and 1B).
###end p 14

In M. smegmatis specific transcripts for furA and katG were identified by Northern blotting, but a single transcript encompassing both furA and katG genes was not detected [27]. Specific 5' ends were mapped by primer extension analysis [26,27]: the first (coordinate 1 in Fig. 1A) was due to transcription initiation from the furA promoter; two additional 5' ends were mapped upstream of katG, the first one, corresponding to coordinate 443, falls in the terminal part of the furA gene; the second is less intense and corresponds to coordinate 478, located in the furA-katG intergenic region. No specific promoter sequences were identified upstream of either 443 or 478.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 51 56 51 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 257 262 257 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
In this work we addressed the question whether the katG 5' ends were generated by transcription initiation or by mRNA processing. Our results support the latter alternative. Furthermore, a polypurine sequence and translation initiation are both involved in katG mRNA stability.
###end p 17

Results
###end title 18
###begin title 19

The 5' end of katG mRNA is not generated by initiation of transcription

###xml 105 117 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 247 259 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>

In a previous work, Milano et al. [27] reported the presence of a promoter region for katG expression in M. smegmatis, located in the terminal part of the upstream furA gene. In order to demonstrate the presence of this promoter, we amplified the M. smegmatis region that overlaps the 5' ends upstream of katG (coordinate 437-552) and cloned it in plasmid pMV261, substituting the hsp60 promoter region (pMYS648). In vitro transcription was performed in the presence of [alpha32P]CTP and M. smegmatis cell extract, comparing the transcripts synthesized by pMV261, from the constitutive hsp60 promoter, and pMYS648, from the potential katG promoter (Fig. 2). pMV261 produced an RNA of about 300 nt that may represent the RNA starting from the hsp60 promoter and terminating at the transcription terminator downstream. On the contrary, no transcript of the expected size for an mRNA starting at the hypothetical pkatG and terminating at the transcription terminator (about 200 nt) was detected when pMYS648 was used, suggesting that no promoter was present in this region. This data is in agreement with the absence of any promoter consensus sequences upstream of the 5' ends.

In order to exclude that a katG promoter was present in this region, we performed an RNA polymerase Chromatin Immunoprecipitation assay (RNApol-ChIP) on M. smegmatis mc2155. After rifampicin treatment in order to block RNA polymerase on promoter sequences [28], formaldehyde cross-linking, and sonication, the DNA-RNA polymerase complexes were immunoprecipitated with mouse monoclonal antibodies to the beta subunit of E. coli RNA polymerase, as described in the Methods section. As rifampicin inhibits transcript elongation, we expected RNA polymerase to be trapped at any potential promoter. The precipitated DNA was then analyzed by quantitative RT-PCR to determine the amount of recovered DNA.

In Bacillus subtilis and Bacillus thuringiensis polypurine sequences (PPS) in the 5' terminal part of several transcripts were reported to increase mRNA stability [9-11]. By sequence analysis of the region immediately upstream of katG in M. tuberculosis and M. smegmatis we identified a PPS four bases upstream of the katG translation start codon (Fig. 1B; GGAAGGAA at coordinates 470-477 in M. tuberculosis; GAAAGGAAA at coordinates 487-495 in M. smegmatis). We tested whether the PPS in the mycobacterial katG mRNA was also able to stabilize the transcript.

###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 45 50 45 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 263 268 263 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
Both the amount of beta-galactosidase and of lacZ transcript were increased more than 3 fold in pMYS690 compared to pMYS694, whereas in pMYS727 relative expression was even lower than in the control. These results suggest that, beside the PPS, translation of the katG open reading frame is also directly involved in transcript stability.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 59 64 59 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 135 140 135 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 329 334 329 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
Other plasmids were constructed in which the length of the katG open reading frame was varied: in pMYS720 a stop codon substitutes the katG start codon (TTG to TAA); in pMYS723 and pMYS744 the translation stop codon is located after 11 and 19 codons, respectively. We measured beta-galactosidase activity as an indication of the lacZ mRNA stability. pMYS720 expressed beta-galactosidase activity to a level similar to the pMYS694 control, confirming the relevance of translation for transcript stability. An increased expression of beta-galactosidase could be observed in pMYS723 and in pMYS744, in which translation can proceed for 11 and 19 codons, respectively. These data indicate that translation of about twenty or more codons is necessary for achieving full transcript stability.
###end p 38
###begin title 39
###xml 77 82 77 82 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
A single stranded mRNA region is necessary for the correct processing of the katG mRNA
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 59 64 59 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 127 140 127 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 183 188 183 188 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psigA</italic>
###xml 509 510 509 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 570 575 570 575 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sigA </italic>
###xml 595 596 595 596 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 625 626 625 626 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 745 750 745 750 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 876 880 876 880 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sigA</italic>
###xml 127 139 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
To characterize the requirements for the processing of the katG mRNA 5' end, several plasmids were constructed by insertion of M. smegmatis DNA regions from 437 to 552, downstream of psigA: pMYS739 carries the wild type sequence, pMYS740 carries a 30 bp internal deletion (from 450 to 480), in pMYS743 five bp flanking the processing site have been substituted, and in pMYS749 five bp were substituted in order to allow the formation of a double stranded region corresponding to the processing site (see Fig. 6). For each plasmid the predicted structure of the RNA from sigA is reported in Fig. 6. RNAs were extracted from mc2155 strain transformed with the plasmids and primer extension was performed with oligo 809, internal to the downstream lacZ gene. The results indicated that in the wild type pMYS739 two signals were detectable: one corresponding to the 5' end due to sigA, the second corresponding to the processing site at 443. The only small effect of the 30 bp deletion in pMYS740 was the presence of a double signal at 442 and 443. The transcript of pMYS743, which carries a different sequence around the processing site, is normally processed at 443, whereas the transcript of pMYS749, in which a double stranded region is created, was no more correctly processed. In order to confirm that a double stranded RNA around 443 could not be processed, we constructed an other plasmid in which different bases were substituted in order to create a double stranded RNA, and also in this case processing at 443 did not occur (data not shown). These data indicate that the ribonuclease responsible for the processing needs a single stranded RNA region for cutting.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 0 84 0 84 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Structure prediction and primer extension on mRNAs synthesized by different plasmids</bold>
###xml 135 140 135 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sigA </italic>
###xml 333 334 333 334 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 509 514 509 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sigA </italic>
Structure prediction and primer extension on mRNAs synthesized by different plasmids. The structure of the transcripts starting at the sigA promoter in pMYS739, pMYS740, pMYS743 and pMYS749 were obtained with the m-fold structure prediction program of Zuker [41] and primer extension analyses were performed on RNAs extracted from mc2155 transformed with the different plasmids, with oligonucleotide 809. Sequence reactions obtained with the same oligonucleotide were run in the same gel. The position of the sigA promoter, the 443 processing site and the PPS region are indicated on the right. The arrows indicate the position of the cut on the sequence structure. The mutations are circled.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
Discussion
###end title 42
###begin title 43
###xml 18 23 18 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 37 53 37 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 57 70 57 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 37 52 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 57 69 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
The 5' end of the katG transcript in M. tuberculosis and M. smegmatis is a processing site
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 20 22 20 22 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 68 86 68 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis katG </italic>
###xml 125 130 125 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA </italic>
###xml 344 357 344 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 68 80 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 344 356 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
In a previous work [27] we suggested the presence of a promoter for M. smegmatis katG expression in the terminal part of the furA gene and we identified two 5' ends at coordinate 443 and 478. However, in this work we tried without success to use the cloned fragment for in vitro transcription: no RNA could be produced by pMYS648, in which the M. smegmatis 437-552 region has been cloned. Moreover, neither promoter consensus sequences were present upstream of 443 nor this region could be recovered by RNA-polymerase ChIP assays. Thus, we concluded that the 5' end is not due to transcription initiation rather to processing of a longer transcript.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 3 19 3 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 54 59 54 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfurA</italic>
###xml 80 82 80 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 83 85 83 85 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 91 104 91 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 145 149 145 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA</italic>
###xml 150 155 150 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 189 191 189 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 245 249 245 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA</italic>
###xml 250 255 250 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 279 291 279 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis</italic>
###xml 3 18 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 91 103 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 279 291 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
In M. tuberculosis a longer transcript, starting from pfurA, has been observed [18,25]. In M. smegmatis we were unable to detect a polycistronic furA-katG transcript by RT-PCR experiments [27]. This suggests that immediate processing may follow furA-katG transcript synthesis in M. smegmatis.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 31 36 31 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sigA </italic>
###xml 96 109 96 109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 204 209 204 209 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psigA</italic>
###xml 323 346 323 346 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis furA-katG </italic>
###xml 541 542 541 542 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 96 108 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 323 335 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
In the constructs in which the sigA promoter region has been cloned immediately upstream of the M. smegmatis fragment (pMYS690 and pMYS692), we could demonstrate the presence of a longer RNA, starting at psigA. Primer extension analysis also revealed the presence of a 5' end at 443, that is the same position in which the M. smegmatis furA-katG mRNA is cut. However, no primer extension signal was observed at 478. In fact, this latter 5' end was no longer detected in many primer extension experiments performed with RNAs extracted from mc2155 (unpublished data). We suggest that this signal, always less intense than the 5' end at 443, may arise by specific pausing of the reverse transcriptase.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
###xml 33 38 33 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
Role of a polypurine sequence in katG mRNA stability
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 29 34 29 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sigA </italic>
###xml 47 63 47 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 70 83 70 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 106 111 106 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 175 180 175 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 283 288 283 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 47 62 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 70 82 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
By cloning downstream of the sigA promoter the M. tuberculosis or the M. smegmatis regions that cover the katG gene translation start point, beta-galactosidase expression and lacZ mRNA half life were increased more than threefold. These regions contain a PPS four bp upstream of the katG translation start codon. Mutagenesis of the PPS abolished both effects, thus suggesting the central role of this sequence in transcript stability.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 62 64 62 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1C</xref>
###xml 101 106 101 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 212 217 212 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
The complementarity of the PPS to the 16S rRNA sequence (Fig. 1C) indicates that it could act as the katG ribosome binding site. Mutations altering the complementarity to the 16S rRNA decreased the amount of the lacZ transcript, suggesting that ribosome binding protects the RNA from degradation.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 164 169 164 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 173 178 173 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ompA </italic>
###xml 181 189 181 189 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 190 192 190 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 193 195 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 196 198 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 201 205 201 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">aprE</italic>
###xml 207 212 207 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gsiB </italic>
###xml 216 221 216 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ermC </italic>
###xml 224 236 224 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. subtilis </italic>
###xml 237 239 237 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 240 242 240 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 246 248 246 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 251 259 251 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cryIIIA </italic>
###xml 262 279 262 279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. thuringiensis </italic>
###xml 280 282 280 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 181 188 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 224 235 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B. subtilis</span>
###xml 262 278 <span type="species:ncbi:1428">B. thuringiensis</span>
The role of ribosome binding sites in mRNA stabilization has been demonstrated in many gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria. It was shown that many mRNAs, like lacZ and ompA of E. coli [33,34,36], aprE, gsiB and ermC of B. subtilis [31,32,37,38], cryIIIA of B. thuringiensis [11], can be significantly destabilized by mutations in the ribosome binding site that interfere with ribosome binding by reducing its complementarity to 16S rRNA.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
This is the first example in mycobacteria, in which a polypurine rich sequence is able to confer stability to the downstream RNA likely by ribosome binding.
###end p 51
###begin title 52
Translation is required for stabilization
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 139 140 139 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 141 143 141 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 144 146 144 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 147 149 147 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 150 152 150 152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 194 202 194 202 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 245 247 245 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 248 250 248 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 251 253 251 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 254 256 254 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 194 201 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Most examples of mRNA stability mechanisms derived from gram-positive bacteria seem to depend on ribosome binding, but not on translation [9-12,31,32,38]. On the other hand, several examples in E. coli indicate that translation has also a role [33-36,39,40].
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 336 340 336 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG</italic>
We could demonstrate that in our mycobacterial system translation is required for stabilization, and more than 20 codons are needed for conferring full stability to the transcript. Thus, we suggest that the ribonuclease that processes the transcript is impaired both by ribosome bound to the PPS and by translation of the first part of katG.
###end p 54
###begin title 55
###xml 52 57 52 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
Regulation of KatG expression and processing of the katG transcript
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 19 21 19 21 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 22 24 22 24 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 71 76 71 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 286 291 286 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
In previous works [25,27], by means of two different reporter systems, katG expression was suggested to be originated from specific transcription initiation. Considering our new findings, indicating the absence of any promoter and the presence of a stabilizing sequence upstream of the katG start codon, a new explanation should be given to previous results. We hypothesize that the reporter activity could be explained by the presence of an unknown promoter upstream of the cloning site in the vector sequence. Transcription starting from this promoter may produce a transcript that, upon processing at 443, is stabilized by both the polypurine sequence and translation initiation. This led to the expression of the downstream reporter gene. Thus, a reporter gene assay, usually used to identify promoter sequences, led us to detect an RNA stabilizing sequence.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 60 65 60 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 68 70 68 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 76 92 76 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 99 112 99 112 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 133 138 133 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 175 181 175 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfurA </italic>
###xml 222 227 222 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA </italic>
###xml 231 236 231 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 318 322 318 322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA</italic>
###xml 344 348 344 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG</italic>
###xml 425 429 425 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG</italic>
###xml 460 465 460 465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 566 572 566 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pfurA </italic>
###xml 573 575 573 575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 76 91 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 99 111 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
Therefore, in agreement with what has been suggested by Pym et al. [18], in M. tuberculosis and in M. smegmatis transcription of the katG gene may originate from the upstream pfurA promoter. A single transcript containing furA and katG is synthesized and processed to originate two different transcripts, one covering furA, the second covering katG. The latter is stabilized by the PPS and translation of the first codons of katG. In conclusion, expression of katG appears to be under the direct control of the FurA protein that controls transcription initiation at pfurA [26].
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 45 61 45 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 68 80 68 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis</italic>
###xml 183 196 183 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 260 277 260 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis furA</italic>
###xml 278 283 278 283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">katG </italic>
###xml 322 338 322 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 539 552 539 552 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 45 60 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 68 80 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 183 195 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 260 272 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 322 337 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 539 551 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
Although the transcript is processed both in M. tuberculosis and in M. smegmatis, the efficiency of the processing event differs. This may be the consequence of a better substrate in M. smegmatis and/or a more active RNAse. It would be possible to replace the M. smegmatis furA-katG intergenic region by the corresponding M. tuberculosis region and test whether a single mRNA for both genes can be found. If this occurs, one can conclude that the substrate makes the difference. On the contrary, if the transcript is completely processed, M. smegmatis RNAse is more active.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 56 79 56 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis furA-katG </italic>
###xml 148 150 148 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 273 278 273 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA </italic>
###xml 327 333 327 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">psigA </italic>
###xml 339 340 339 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 654 664 654 664 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA-katG </italic>
###xml 56 68 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
The secondary structure of the transcripts covering the M. smegmatis furA-katG region were obtained by mfold-structure prediction program of Zuker [41]. The structure of the region surrounding the processing sites is single stranded both in the transcripts starting at the furA promoter (not shown) and in the RNAs starting at psigA (Fig. 6; pMYS739). Moreover, mutagenesis of this region, either by deletion of about 30 bp or by substitution of the bases surrounding the processing site did not prevent processing at 443. On the contrary, a double stranded RNA region impaired the processing, suggesting that the RNase involved in the processing of the furA-katG transcript requires single stranded RNA.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 50 66 50 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 73 86 73 86 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 161 163 161 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 164 166 164 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 50 65 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 73 85 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
Several endoribonucleases have been identified in M. tuberculosis and in M. smegmatis complete genome sequence by homology with known enzymes in other bacteria [16,42]. Experiments are in progress to identify which mycobacterial ribonuclease is responsible for this processing.
###end p 60
###begin title 61
Conclusion
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 159 169 159 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA-katG </italic>
###xml 198 203 198 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">furA </italic>
This is the first reported case in mycobacteria, in which both a polypurine sequence and translation initiation are shown to contribute to mRNA stability. The furA-katG mRNA is transcribed from the furA promoter and likely processed by a single stranded RNAse.
###end p 62

###begin title 64
Bacterial strains and culture conditions
###end title 64

###begin title 70
Preparation of crude extracts
###end title 70
###begin p 71
###xml 18 31 18 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 79 83 79 83 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>

###xml 255 256 254 255 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 18 30 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
Crude extracts of M. smegmatis were prepared from 100 ml log phase cultures (OD600 = 0.8). The cells were pelleted, washed in 20 mM TrisHCl pH 8, 10 mM MgCl2, 0.1 mM EDTA, 0.1 mM DTT, 50 mM KCl, and resuspended in 500 mul of 20 mM TrisHCl pH 8, 10 mM MgCl2, 0.1 mM EDTA, 0.1 mM DTT, 50 mM KCl, 1 mM PMSF, 10% glycerol. The cells were lysed by sonication (2 x 20 seconds at 40% amplitude). The cell lysate was spun for 10 min at 15000 x g and the supernatant (about 10 mg/ml) was used as the crude extract.

###begin p 83

###begin title 84
RNA pol-ChIP
###end title 84
###begin p 85
###xml 7 20 7 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 46 50 46 50 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>
###xml 1065 1071 1048 1054 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">input </italic>
###xml 1211 1219 1183 1191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1904 1910 1867 1873 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">no ab </italic>
###xml 1936 1941 1899 1904 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">input</italic>
###xml 1950 1956 1913 1919 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">no ab </italic>
###xml 7 19 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 1164 1169 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1211 1218 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Ten ml M. smegmatis culture was grown up to OD600 = 0.8, rifampicin was added to a final concentration of 500 mug/ml and the culture incubated for 5 minutes at 37degreesC with shaking. The culture was then fixed with 1% formaldehyde at room temperature for two minutes under gentle shaking and the reaction stopped by addition of glycine (final concentration 0.125 M) for 10 minutes with gentle shaking. Cross-linked cells were pelleted by centrifugation, washed twice in PBS and once in STET (150 mM NaCl, 10 mM Tris-HCl pH 8, 10 mM EDTA, 0.25% Triton X-100), and finally resuspended in 2 ml of TE (10 mM Tris-HCl pH 8, 1 mM EDTA). The sample was sonicated on ice with 20 pulses of 20 seconds and 40% amplitude. The average DNA fragment size obtained was approximately 0.5 kb. After sonication insoluble debris was removed by centrifugation and the extract precleared with 40 mul 50% protein G-agarose slurry (Sigma, previously blocked with 100 mug/ml lambda DNA, 500 mug/ml tRNA, 1 mg/ml BSA) for 3 h at 4degreesC. 100 mul of the precleared extract were saved as input fraction. 900 mul were incubated under rotation overnight at 4degreesC, with 10 mul of 8RB13 mouse monoclonal antibodies to beta subunit of E. coli RNA polymerase (Neoclone, cross-reacts with RNA polymerases of many others prokaryotes) in 1x RIPA buffer (140 mM NaCl, 10 mM Tris HCl pH 8, 1 mM EDTA, 1% Triton X-100, 0.1% SDS, 0.1% Na deoxycholate) and RNA polymerase-DNA complexes were immunoprecipitated with 50 mul 50% protein G-agarose slurry for 4 h under rotation at 4degreesC. The immunocomplex was recovered by centrifugation, washed four times with 1x RIPA buffer, once with LiCl buffer (250 mM LiCl, 10 mM Tris-HCl pH 8, 1 mM EDTA, 0.5% NP-40, 0.5% Na deoxycholate), twice with TE, and resuspended with 100 mul TE (IP fraction). 900 mul of the precleared extract were treated as above but in the absence of the antibodies (no ab fraction). The DNA of the input, IP and no ab fractions was recovered by treatment for 30 min at 37degreesC with RNase A (20 mug/ml) and overnight digestion with 50 mug/ml proteinase K in 0.5% SDS at 37degreesC. Crosslinking was reversed for 6 h at 65degreesC. DNA was extracted once with phenol/chlorophorm, ethanol precipitated and resuspended in 50 mul TE.
###end p 85
