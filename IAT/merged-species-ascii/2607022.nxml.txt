###begin article-title 0
RIG-I and dsRNA-Induced IFNbeta Activation
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 179 182 179 182 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 625 632 625 632 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 707 715 707 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
Except for viruses that initiate RNA synthesis with a protein primer (e.g., picornaviruses), most RNA viruses initiate RNA synthesis with an NTP, and at least some of their viral pppRNAs remain unblocked during the infection. Consistent with this, most viruses require RIG-I to mount an innate immune response, whereas picornaviruses require mda-5. We have examined a SeV infection whose ability to induce interferon depends on the generation of capped dsRNA (without free 5' tri-phosphate ends), and found that this infection as well requires RIG-I and not mda-5. We also provide evidence that RIG-I interacts with poly-I/C in vivo, and that heteropolymeric dsRNA and poly-I/C interact directly with RIG-I in vitro, but in different ways; i.e., poly-I/C has the unique ability to stimulate the helicase ATPase of RIG-I variants which lack the C-terminal regulatory domain.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 209 212 202 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Stark1">[1]</xref>
###xml 214 217 207 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Samuel1">[2]</xref>
###xml 568 571 554 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Pichlmair1">[3]</xref>
###xml 572 575 558 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Yoneyama1">[5]</xref>
###xml 876 879 862 865 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Saito1">[6]</xref>
###xml 923 926 909 912 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 958 961 944 947 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Pichlmair2">[7]</xref>
###xml 963 966 949 952 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Hornung1">[8]</xref>
###xml 1123 1126 1109 1112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Cui1">[9]</xref>
Virus infection elicits potent cellular responses that contain virus spread before the adaptive immune system can intervene, and the production of type I interferons (IFNalpha/beta) is central to this process [1], [2]. The sensors involved in coupling recognition of virus infection with the induction of IFNalpha/beta have recently been discovered. These sensors, or pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) that recognize pathogen associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), include RIG-I and mda-5, two cytoplasmic, RNA-binding DExD/H box helicases (for recent reviews, see [3]-[5]. Both proteins contain N-terminal CARD domains, followed by a DECH box helicase. Both proteins also contain a C-terminal domain, which in the case of RIG-I acts as an internal repressor or regulatory domain (RD) that prevents the CARDs from interacting with their downstream signaling adaptor, IPS-1 [6]. The binding of 5' tri-phosphorylated RNA (pppRNA, which acts as a viral PAMP [7], [8]) to the RD of RIG-I leads to its dimerization, which is thought to stimulate the helicase ATPase and release the CARDs for homotypic interaction with IPS-1 [9], the mitochondrial adaptor of both RIG-I and mda-5. IPS-1 activation then leads to the recruitment of a series of kinases which in turn leads to the activation of IRF-3/7 and NF-kB and the expression of the early IFN genes, such as IFNbeta.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 70 74 70 74 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Yoneyama2">[10]</xref>
###xml 252 256 252 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Kato1">[11]</xref>
###xml 432 436 432 436 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Kato2">[12]</xref>
###xml 571 575 571 575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Kato2">[12]</xref>
###xml 577 581 577 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Gitlin1">[13]</xref>
###xml 753 756 753 756 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 861 864 861 864 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 881 889 881 889 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 957 960 957 960 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1074 1082 1074 1082 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1095 1099 1095 1099 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Kato2">[12]</xref>
###xml 1101 1105 1101 1105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Gitlin1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1139 1147 1139 1147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1289 1292 1289 1292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Pichlmair2">[7]</xref>
###xml 1294 1297 1294 1297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Hornung1">[8]</xref>
###xml 1355 1358 1355 1358 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 160 164 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 369 373 <span type="species:ncbi:12104?1.0">EMCV</span>
###xml 381 398 <span type="species:ncbi:11320">Influenza A virus</span>
###xml 413 425 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">Sendai virus</span>
###xml 427 430 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 454 458 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 556 560 <span type="species:ncbi:12104?1.0">EMCV</span>
###xml 742 746 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
When RIG-I was first described in the seminal paper of Yoneyama et al [10], poly-I/C was proposed as its ligand based on RIG-I over-expression. RIG-I deficient mice, however, were then found not to be defective in their type I IFN response to poly-I/C [11], whereas they were unable to mount an innate immune response to most RNA viruses other than picornaviruses like EMCV (e.g., Influenza A virus, VSV, JEV and Sendai virus (SeV) [12]. Mda-5 deficient mice, in contrast, were found to be entirely unable to mount a type I IFN response to poly-I/C and to EMCV infection [12], [13]. The role of these two helicases in the innate immune response to virus infection was thus found to be remarkably specific. Using cell lines derived from these mice, mda-5-/- MEFs were found to activate IFN genes in response to various transfected dsRNAs made from complementary pppRNAs transcribed in vitro, whereas these MEFs did not respond to poly-I/C. In contrast, RIG-I-/- MEFs activated IFN genes in response to transfected poly-I/C, but these MEFs did not respond to dsRNAs made from in vitro transcripts [12], [13]. Subsequently, ssRNA transcribed in vitro was also found to be a ligand for RIG-I, and its ability to induce IFN upon transfection depended on the 5' triphosphate moiety of the ssRNA [7], [8]. Thus, RIG-I was thought to act as a PRR exclusively for pppRNA (independent of its single- or double-strandedness), and mda-5 for poly-I/C, or more realistically for the RNA elements of picornavirus infection that are mimicked by poly-I/C.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 69 72 69 72 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 463 466 463 466 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 568 572 568 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Flint1">[14]</xref>
###xml 843 847 840 844 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Strahle1">[15]</xref>
###xml 788 791 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 872 875 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 974 977 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
RIG-I and mda-5 are thus thought to recognize different RNA ligands (pppRNA and poly-I/C or dsRNA, respectively) that act as PAMPs, which presumably accounts for the virus-specific response of these helicases. This is consistent with our view of RNA virus replication. Except for picornaviruses (and caliciviruses) that initiate all RNA synthesis with a protein primer; the other RNA viruses initiate all RNA synthesis with an NTP, and at least some of the viral pppRNAs remain unblocked during the infection (e.g., the minus-strands of plus-strand and dsRNA viruses) [14]. Thus, except for picornaviruses (and possibly caliciviruses), cells require RIG-I (and not mda-5) to activate IFNbeta in response to other RNA virus infections. In order to test this contention, we have designed a SeV infection that generates dsRNA with capped 5' ends [15] to examine whether this SeV infection requires mda-5 rather than RIG-I to activate IFNbeta. Remarkably, this dsRNA-generating SeV co-infection also requires RIG-I [and not mda-5] to activate IFNbeta. This study also provides evidence that RIG-I binds dsRNA devoid of free 5' tri-phosphate ends, and that poly-I/C is not a simple analog of dsRNA; i.e., poly-I/C has the unique ability to stimulate the helicase ATPase of RIG-I variants which lack the C-terminal regulatory domain.
###end p 6

###begin p 16
###xml 554 561 523 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Renilla</italic>

###begin title 19
Recombinant RIG-I cloning and expression
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 250 252 250 252 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 320 322 320 322 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 26 31 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The open reading frame of human RIG-I was amplified by PCR using primers designed to introduce a HindIII site upstream of the start codon and a XhoI site downstream. The PCR products were digested and then inserted between the same sites of pET28-His10Smt3, to fuse the RIG-I proteins in-frame with an amino-terminal His10Smt3 domain. RIG-I (1-796) was constructed with an antisense primer that introduced a stop codon in place of pro797 and a XhoI site. RIG-I (242-796) was constructed with sense primer that introduced a HindIIII site upstream of lys241and the antisense primer used above. The K270A mutation was introduced by a PCR-based two-stage overlap extension method. The plasmid inserts were sequenced completely to ensure that unwanted mutations during amplification and cloning had not occurred.

###begin p 21
###xml 13 15 13 15 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sub>
###xml 56 63 56 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 204 205 194 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">A</italic>
###xml 205 208 195 198 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">600</sub>
###xml 894 896 862 864 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 56 68 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">E. coli BL21</span>
The pET28-His10Smt3-Tgs1 plasmids were transformed into E. coli BL21. Cultures (500 ml) derived from single transformants were grown at 37degreesC in LB medium containing 50 microg/ml kanamycin until the A600 reached 0.6. The cultures were adjusted to 0.2 mM IPTG and 2% ethanol and incubation was continued for 20 h at 17degrees. Cells were harvested by centrifugation and stored at -80degreesC. All subsequent procedures were performed at 4degrees. Thawed bacteria were resuspended in 25 ml of buffer A (50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 200 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol) and one tablet of protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche) and lysozyme (100 microg/ml) were added. After incubation for 30 min, imidazole was added to a final concentration of 5 mM and the lysate was sonicated to reduce viscosity. Insoluble material was removed by centrifugation. The soluble extracts were mixed for 30 min with 1.6 ml of Ni2+-NTA-agarose (Qiagen) that had been equilibrated with buffer A containing 5 mM imidazole. The resins were recovered by centrifugation, resuspended in buffer A containing 5 mM imidazole, and poured into columns. The columns were washed with 8 ml of 10 and 20 mM imidazole in buffer A and then eluted step-wise with 2.5 ml aliquots of buffer A containing 50, 100, 250, and 500 mM imidazole. The elution profiles were monitored by SDS-PAGE. The 250 mM imidazole eluates containing the recombinant RIG-I polypeptides were dialyzed against 50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 200 mM NaCl, 2 mM DTT, 1 mM EDTA, 10% glycerol and then stored at -80degreesC. The protein concentration was determined using the Bio-Rad dye binding method with BSA as the standard.

###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 100 104 100 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Strahle3">[17]</xref>

###xml 319 323 319 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Strahle1">[15]</xref>

###xml 783 787 780 784 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Plumet1">[21]</xref>
###xml 813 816 810 813 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 939 942 936 939 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 1102 1106 1099 1103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Strahle1">[15]</xref>
###xml 1154 1159 1151 1156 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">minus</sup>
###xml 1184 1188 1181 1185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Kato2">[12]</xref>
###xml 1442 1445 1439 1442 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 1622 1626 1619 1623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Strahle3">[17]</xref>
###xml 1690 1693 1687 1690 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 1762 1766 1759 1763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Kolakofsky1">[22]</xref>
###xml 1779 1782 1776 1779 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 1792 1795 1789 1792 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 1914 1919 1908 1913 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">minus</sup>
###xml 10 13 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 62 65 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 129 132 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 373 376 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 990 993 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 1149 1152 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 1194 1197 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 1909 1912 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
Unnatural SeV infection is commonly used to induce IFN, as wt SeV infection induces IFN very poorly [17]. This is because the wt SeV genome expresses C and V proteins which counteract the innate immune response in several ways, most notably by their ability to inhibit IFN activation by transfected pppRNA and poly-I/C [15]. The extent to which IFNbeta is activated during SeV infection presumably depends both on the level of the RNA PAMPs produced and that of the viral products that counteract the innate immune response. For mononegaviruses whose genome and antigenome RNAs are tightly covered with N protein during their synthesis (and thus are unlikely to act as PAMPs), small promoter-proximal (leader and trailer) ppp RNAs are made independent of assembly with N [15], [20], [21]. These promoter-proximal ppp RNAs are essential for the control of genome replication. Viral dsRNA could also be generated by the annealing of trailer pppRNA and L mRNA read-thru transcripts. During wt SeV infection of non-immune cells, the activity of both PAMPs is presumably neutralized by the viral C proteins [15]. Akira and coworkers have previously used SeV-Cminus infection to induce IFN [12]. The SeV which is more commonly used to induce IFN ("Cantell strain") is in fact a mixed virus stock composed mostly of copyback defective-interfering (DI) genomes, and this DI infection both under-produces the C and V proteins and overproduces trailer pppRNA. In addition, because some of the copyback DI genomes are exceedingly small (546 nt vs 15,264 for wt), some of these genomes may also be made without being assembled with N [17]. If so, these unassembled DI genomes would self-anneal to form pppdsRNA panhandle structures because their ends are self-complementary [22]. Thus, both pppssRNA and pppdsRNA (i.e., in which one strand contains a 5' tri-phosphate end) are thought to induce IFNbeta activation during SeV-Cminus and DI infections.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 144 148 141 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Strahle1">[15]</xref>
###xml 42 45 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
More recently, we have also used quasi-wt SeV co-infections that express GFP mRNA and anti-GFP mRNA (from separate genomes) to activate IFNbeta [15]. The ability of this co-infection to induce IFN depended on the presence of both complementary GFP mRNAs in the cytoplasm, and RFP mRNA expression could not substitute for one of the GFP mRNAs. IFNbeta activation induced by the GFP+/- co-infection thus presumably results from the generation of GFP dsRNA in which both 5' ends are capped. Moreover, this IFNbeta activation also appeared to depend on RIG-I, as a dominant-negative form of RIG-I (RIG-I-DeltaCARD) inhibited this response. However, the precise manner in which RIG-I-DeltaCARD acts as a dominant-negative inhibitor of RIG-I is not known, and this mutant helicase may be acting non-specifically when over-expressed.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 79 82 79 82 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 92 95 92 95 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 371 377 368 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g001">fig 1A</xref>
###xml 405 408 402 405 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 684 687 675 678 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 898 904 886 892 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g001">fig 1C</xref>
###xml 243 246 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
To further investigate the helicase requirement for the GFP+/- infection, RIG-I-/- and mda-5-/- MEFs were transfected with plasmids expressing luciferase under the control of the IFNbeta promoter (and control plasmids), and then infected with SeV DI-H4 or GFP+/- stocks. Cells extracts were prepared at 20 hpi and their luciferase activities were determined. As shown in fig 1A, neither infection of RIG-I-/- MEFs activated the IFNbeta promoter above background levels. When RIG-I was re-expressed in these cells by transfection (along with the reporter plasmids), this restored the ability of both infections to activate IFNbeta. In contrast, the DI-H4 and GFP+/- infections of mda-5-/- MEFs activated the IFNbeta promoter in both cases. The re-expression of mda-5 in these cells roughly doubled their response to the GFP+/- infection, but did not stimulate their response to the DI-H4 infection (fig 1C). Thus, the presence of RIG-I is essential for IFNbeta activation during GFP+/- infection that generate capped-dsRNA, as well as during DI-H4 infections. In contrast, the presence of mda-5 is not essential, but can stimulate IFNbeta activation in response to capped dsRNA in mda-5-/- cells.
###end p 34

###xml 35 42 35 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
Interaction of RIG-I with poly-I/C in vivo
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 268 271 265 268 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 283 286 280 283 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 504 512 501 509 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 877 880 874 877 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Hornung1">[8]</xref>
###xml 139 142 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 196 199 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 523 537 <span type="species:ncbi:10245">vaccinia virus</span>
###xml 670 678 <span type="species:ncbi:10891">reovirus</span>
We would of course like to confirm that RIG-I also senses dsRNA without 5' tri-phosphate ends, by examining whether capped GFP dsRNA (from SeV infected cells) activates IFNbeta upon transfection. SeV infected cells, however, will also contain viral leader and trailer pppssRNAs, and pppdsRNAs (made from trailer RNA and L mRNA read-through transcripts), and determining the level of purity of the GFP dsRNA is problematic. We have also tried to prepare such capped dsRNA by capping GFP transcripts (made in vitro) with the vaccinia virus guanylyl transferase, but we were unable to cap more than 70% of each strand. Natural dsRNA that can be obtained in pure form, like reovirus RNA, contains a free 5' tri-phosphate (minus-strand) end. We therefore turned to poly-I/C that contains 5' di-phosphate ends, as model RNAs containing these 5' ends were found not to activate RIG-I [8].
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 382 386 382 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Yoneyama2">[10]</xref>
###xml 550 554 550 554 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Yoneyama2">[10]</xref>
###xml 724 727 724 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Saito1">[6]</xref>
###xml 643 646 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
We examined the possible interaction of poly-I/C and RIG-I in MEFs using helicases that lack their N-terminal CARDs, which appear to act as dominant-negative inhibitors of the helicases. For example, over-expression of the tandem CARDs of RIG-I alone induce IFN independently of the presence of viral RNA, suggesting that the CARDs mediate IPS-1 activation and downstream signaling [10]. Mutation of the RIG-I ATP binding site abolishes RIG-I activity, suggesting that ATP and RNA-dependent conformational changes are essential for sensing viral RNA [10]. Finally, over-expression of the RIG-I RD alone inhibits RIG-I signaling in response to SeV DI infection, by apparently interfering with the oligomerization of wt RIG-I [6]. RIG-I-DeltaCARD could then act as a dominant-negative inhibitor of RIG-I because of its ability to bind viral RNAs and oligomerize with wt RIG-I, but this mixed oligomer would not activate IPS-1. Less is known about the manner in which mda-5 signals to IFNbeta.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 59 62 56 59 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 185 190 178 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g002">fig 2</xref>
###xml 286 289 275 278 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 516 519 497 500 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 796 802 769 775 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g003">fig 3A</xref>
###xml 869 872 842 845 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1139 1145 1104 1110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g003">fig 3B</xref>
When IFNbeta activation in MEFs in response to transfected pppssRNA or poly-I/C is compared, this activation is largely inhibited by the co-expression of RIG-I-DeltaCARD in both cases (fig 2). In contrast, the co-expression of mda-5-DeltaCARD has no effect on the activation induced by pppssRNA, and a mimimal effect on that induced by poly-I/C. This does not appear to be because mda-5-DeltaCARD is inactive, or because RIG-I-DeltaCARD is acting non-specifically. When the CARD-less helicases are expressed in RIG-I-/- MEFs in which mda-5 is (or is not) expressed by transfection, mda-5-DeltaCARD clearly inhibits the poly-I/C induced activation due to the (over-)expressed mda-5, whereas RIG-I-DeltaCARD does not at all inhibit this activation that is exclusively due to the presence of mda-5 (fig 3A). In contrast, when the CARD-less helicases are expressed in RIG-I-/- MEFs in which RIG-I is (or is not) expressed, mda-5-DeltaCARD now does not inhibit (but rather stimulates) the poly-I/C induced activation due to the (over-)expressed RIG-I, whereas RIG-I-DeltaCARD clearly inhibits this activation in the presence of both helicases (fig 3B). Thus, mda-5-DeltaCARD does indeed act as an inhibitor of poly-I/C induced IFNbeta activation, but only when this activation is due exclusively to mda-5. When both helicases are present, it is RIG-I-DeltaCARD (and not mda-5-DeltaCARD) that inhibits poly-I/C induced IFNbeta activation.

Although the helicase-deficient MEFs transfect poorly with plasmid DNA, they appear to be more efficiently transfected with either relatively small poly-I/C (400 bp on average) or pppRNA (55 nt)(fig 4). When RIG-I-/- MEFs are transfected with poly-I/C or pppRNA, the level of endogenous IFNbeta mRNA increases only in response to poly-I/C as expected (fig 4A), as mda-5 does not respond to pppRNA [12]. In contrast, when mda-5-/- MEFs are transfected with poly-I/C or pppRNA, the level of endogenous IFNbeta mRNA increases in response to poly-I/C as well as to pppRNA (fig 4B), and the supernatants from both these transfections have the capacity to inhibit VSV-GFP replication when used to pretreat other MEFs (fig 4D). These results further indicate that RIG-I responds to poly-I/C as well as to pppRNA.

DExH/D box helicases share 7 conserved sequence motifs that mediate ATP and nucleic acid binding [23]. Nucleic acid binding stimulates the helicase ATPase and results in a conformational power stroke [24]. ATP binding is essential for RIG-I signaling, but the mechanistic role of the ATPase activity in RIG-I signaling to IFNbeta is unclear. In contrast, the RIG-I ATPase is not stimulated by chemically synthesized OHssRNA (data not shown) [9]. Cui et al have reported that pppssRNA is much more efficient in stimulating wt RIG-I (1-925) ATPase than OHdsRNA. This difference is less pronounced, but clear in fig 6B and C.

###xml 1548 1551 1548 1551 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Cui1">[9]</xref>
Two groups have recently reported that the RD of RIG-I alone binds pppssRNA, in a shallow, positively-charged groove [9], [25]. These two studies were largely in agreement, but differed in that the RD in one case also bound relatively short OHdsRNA (25 bp)[25], whereas that of Cui et al did not interact with OHdsRNA (50 bp) even though the ATPase of a RIG-I variant lacking the CARDs was clearly stimulated by OHdsRNA [9]. We have used streptavidin beads containing biotinylated pppssRNA to study RNA/RIG-I interaction. As shown in fig 7, RIG-I binds to pppssRNA beads (lanes "none" (no competitor) vs bald beads). As expected, this binding can be efficiently out-competed with an excess of unmodified pppssRNA (55'mer, top panel), but the same amount of OHssRNA (41'mer, top and middle panels), poly-I or poly-C (middle panel) had no effect. More importantly, RIG-I binding to pppssRNA can be efficiently out-competed with relatively short OHdsRNA (41 bp, bottom panel) under conditions where an equal amount of OHssRNA (41'mer or 53'mer (bottom panel)) has no effect. Poly-I/C also competed with the binding of RIG-I to pppssRNA (middle panel). Thus, the binding of short OHdsRNA or poly-I/C and pppssRNA to RIG-I appear to be mutually exclusive, either because their binding sites on RD overlap [25], or because OHdsRNA binds to the helicase core in an RD-dependent manner. This latter possibility is suggested by the finding that the ATPase of a RIG-I variant lacking RD (residues 1-793) could not be stimulated by either pppssRNA or OHdsRNA [9].

We have repeated and extended these ATPase studies, using RIG-I-DeltaRD (in this case residues 1-796) and RIG-I-DeltaCARD/DeltaRD (242-796), as well as full-length (wt) RIG-I (1-925). As a negative control, we examined RIG-I containing a mutation in the Walker A box of the helicase ATP binding site (RIG-I-K270A). We find that the RIG-I-DeltaRD ATPase is essentially inactive with not only pppssRNA and OHdsRNA, but is inactive as well with poly-G/C, poly-A/U and BTV dsRNA (closed circles, fig 6, and data not shown). Unexpectedly, we found that the RIG-I-DeltaRD ATPase was nevertheless clearly stimulated by poly-I/C (albeit not as efficiently as wt RIG-I; fig 6E). In addition, the RIG-I-DeltaCARD/DeltaRD ATPase was hyper-stimulated by poly-I/C as compared to that of wt RIG-I (fig 6E). These latter results rule out the possibility that the RIG-I ATPase somehow requires the presence of the RD for its activity. These results are thus more consistent with the binding of pppssRNA and OHdsRNA to overlapping sites on RD. It is also possible that the binding of OHdsRNA to RD is helped more by its simultaneous interaction with the helicase core than the binding of pppssRNA to RD. In summary, the ability of poly-I/C to stimulate the ATPase of RIG-I is clearly different from that of heteropolymeric dsRNA. It appears that poly-I/C has the unique ability to bind to both the helicase domain and the RD, and can stimulate the helicase ATPase independently of the RD.

Stimulation of the RIG-I ATPase is necessary but insufficient for IFNbeta activation in vivo
###end title 60
###begin p 61
###xml 115 122 108 115 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 284 289 274 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g008">fig 8</xref>

###xml 1106 1111 1068 1073 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g007">fig 7</xref>

###xml 1457 1461 1419 1423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Takahasi1">[25]</xref>
Given that the RIG-I-DeltaRD ATPase is stimulated by poly-I/C, we examined whether poly-I/C could activate IFNbeta in vivo via this variant of RIG-I. When additional wt RIG-I was expressed in wt MEFs, IFNbeta activation in response to a fixed amount of poly I/C increased ca. 3-fold (fig 8). However, when additional RIG-I-DeltaRD or RIG-I-DeltaCARD/DeltaRD was expressed, there was little or no effect on poly-I/C induced IFNbeta activation. When additional RIG-I-DeltaCARD was expressed, this variant acted as a dominant-negative inhibiter of poly-I/C induced IFNbeta activation, as expected. Similar results were obtained when pppssRNA, rather than poly-I/C was used to induce IFNbeta activation (data not shown). Thus, stimulation of the ATPase is necessary but not sufficient for poly-I/C induced IFNbeta activation. These results are consistent with the finding that the RD is required for RIG-I self-association, and that this self-association is required for downstream signaling [6], [9]. The finding that poly-I/C also competed with the binding of RIG-I to pppRNA (which clearly binds to the RD)(fig 7) suggests that poly-I/C binds to both the helicase core and the RD. These latter results are consistent with the demonstration that the binding of pppRNA or short dsRNA to RIG-I protects a 17kD fragment from trypsin digestion (essentially the entire RD), whereas the binding of poly-I/C produces a 66 kD fragment of the helicase core and the RD [25].

###begin title 64
Discussion
###end title 64
###begin p 65
###xml 273 276 273 276 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 330 333 330 333 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Pichlmair2">[7]</xref>
###xml 335 338 335 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Hornung1">[8]</xref>
###xml 388 393 385 390 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">minus</sup>
###xml 756 762 750 756 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g001">figs 1</xref>
###xml 767 768 761 762 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g002">2</xref>
###xml 865 868 859 862 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 884 886 878 880 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">OH</sub>
###xml 1095 1099 1086 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Takahasi1">[25]</xref>
###xml 1130 1132 1121 1123 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">OH</sub>
###xml 1152 1155 1143 1146 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 1195 1197 1186 1188 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">OH</sub>
###xml 1216 1221 1207 1212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g007">fig 7</xref>
###xml 41 44 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 383 386 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 698 701 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
The dsRNAs generated during nondefective SeV infection (due to convergent transcription) or to copyback DI infection (due to intramolecular annealing of the DI genome's complementary ends) would both contain one 5' tri-phosphate end. As RIG-I is thought to be activated by pppRNA independent of its single- or double-strandedness [7], [8], the finding that IFNbeta activation during SeV Cminus and DI-H4 infections require RIG-I was not unexpected. dsRNA devoid of free 5' tri-phosphate ends, however, is normally found only during (e.g., picornavirus) infections that initiate RNA synthesis with protein primers, and these infections activate IFNbeta via mda-5 rather than RIG-I. The finding that SeV-GFP+/- infections also requires RIG-I (and not mda-5; figs 1 and 2) was thus not expected. This latter finding, however, is consistent with the recent report that pppssRNA and short OHdsRNA bind to overlapping sites of the RIG-I RD, and that short dsRNAs will activate IFNbeta via RIG-I upon transfection if they contain 5' mono-phosphates that apparently stabilize the dsRNAs intracellularly [25]. We have confirmed that short OHdsRNA competes with pppssRNA for its binding to RIG-I, whereas OHssRNA is inactive (fig 7).
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 91 94 91 94 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 100 104 100 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Kato2">[12]</xref>
###xml 106 110 106 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Gitlin1">[13]</xref>
###xml 166 170 166 170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Kato3">[26]</xref>
###xml 544 547 541 544 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 554 559 551 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g005">fig 5</xref>
###xml 897 901 891 895 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Loo1">[27]</xref>
###xml 1064 1068 1058 1062 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Andrejeva1">[28]</xref>
###xml 1070 1074 1064 1068 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Childs1">[29]</xref>
###xml 1233 1237 1227 1231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Strahle1">[15]</xref>
###xml 987 990 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 1086 1089 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 1144 1147 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
###xml 1219 1222 <span type="species:ncbi:11191">SeV</span>
Even though it has been reported that RIG-I by itself does not respond to poly-I/C in mda-5-/- MEFs [12], [13], especially when poly-I/C of 4-8 kbp in length is used [26], this does not exclude the possibility that RIG-I participates in the cellular response to poly-I/C when both helicases are present, or that RIG-I can directly respond to poly-I/C when RIG-I levels are elevated. When RIG-I levels are gradually increased in MEFs, the level of IFNbeta activation is proportional to that of RIG-I expression in response to either poly-I/C or pppssRNA (fig 5). There is other indirect evidence that RIG-I participates in the poly-I/C induced IFNbeta activation when both helicases are present. For example, whereas Huh 7 cells respond well to poly-I/C, a sub-line of these cells containing an incapacitating mutation in a CARD of RIG-I (Huh 7.5 cells) has lost most of its response to this dsRNA [27]. In addition, even though the ability of paramyxovirus V proteins (including that of SeV) to bind mda-5 and prevent poly-I/C induced signaling is well documented [28], [29], it is the SeV C protein (that inhibits RIG-I signaling) and not the SeV V protein which acts to inhibit the cellular response to poly-I/C in a SeV infection [15].
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 124 132 124 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 158 161 158 161 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 168 173 168 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g006">fig 6</xref>
###xml 314 317 314 317 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 349 354 349 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003965-g006">fig 6</xref>
###xml 729 733 729 733 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Colby1">[30]</xref>
###xml 1515 1518 1515 1518 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ppp</sub>
###xml 80 88 <span type="species:ncbi:10891">reovirus</span>
###xml 787 792 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chick</span>
###xml 1834 1839 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We found that both short dsRNAs containing two 5' OH ends (dsRNA-tr41) and BTV (reovirus) dsRNAs stimulate the RIG-I ATPase in vitro almost as efficiently as pppssRNA (fig 6). Unexpectedly, we also found that poly-I/C stimulated the ATPase of RIG-I variants lacking the C-terminal RD, in strong contrast to either pppssRNA or heteropolymeric dsRNA (fig 6). Poly-I/C is an unusual dsRNA, composed of complementary homopolymers, and one strand being composed of the rare base inosine. Poly-I/C has been the transfected dsRNA of choice to study IFN induction for many years, not only because it is commercially available, but because it works so well. It has been known for decades that poly-I/C has a special ability to induce IFN [30]. Using DEAE-dextran to transfect various dsRNAs into chick embryo fibroblasts, the efficiency of IFN induction of poly-I/C was found to be orders of magnitude greater than that of poly-G/C or poly-A/U. Moreover, these differences in activity among the various polynucleotides did not appear to reflect differences in their thermal stability, sensitivity to RNase A, the rate or amount of uptake into the cells or in the rate of intracellular breakdown. Colby and Chamberlin (1969) presciently concluded that the high degree of specificity of the induction process was consistent with the existence of a specific intracellular receptor, most probably a protein. One of the possible reasons for the remarkable ability of poly-I/C to induce IFN may be because, unlike other dsRNAs or pppssRNAs, it can directly bind to both the RIG-I helicase domain as well as to mda-5, stimulating their ATPases and inducing the conformational changes that liberate the CARDs for interaction with IPS-1. At present, there is no clear understanding of the RNA elements of poly-I/C that mimic the PAMPs generated during mouse picornavirus infections, or why poly-I/C alone among various dsRNAs stimulates the RIG-I-DeltaRD ATPase. However, it is becoming increasingly clear that poly-I/C is not a simple analog of dsRNA.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 63 67 63 67 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003965-Kato3">[26]</xref>
While this work was being prepared for publication, Kato et al [26] reported that the length of dsRNA is important for differential recognition by RIG-I and mda.5. Long poly-I/C (4-8 kbp) was found to be a specific ligand for mda-5, whereas relatively short poly-I/C (300 bp) was a specific ligand for RIG-I. We have examined the length of our poly-I/C by agarose gel electrophoresis and found that it was relatively short (200-600 bp relative to DNA markers), and the maximum length of our GFP dsRNA would be 714 bp. Our results are thus consistent with those of Kato et al.
###end p 68
