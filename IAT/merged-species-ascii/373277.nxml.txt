###xml 10 12 10 12 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
Coenzyme B12 riboswitches are widespread genetic control elements in prokaryotes
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 95 118 95 118 <email xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ronald.breaker@yale.edu</email>

###begin p 2
###xml 329 333 329 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 343 359 343 359 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 364 386 364 386 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella typhimurium</italic>
###xml 410 412 410 412 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 906 909 906 909 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 921 934 921 934 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium</italic>
###xml 1096 1098 1096 1098 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 343 359 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 364 386 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">Salmonella typhimurium</span>
###xml 921 934 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
Recent studies have begun to reveal that numerous fundamental metabolic pathways in bacteria are regulated by riboswitches residing within certain messenger RNAs. These riboswitches selectively bind metabolites and modulate gene expression in response to changing ligand concentrations. Previously, we provided evidence that the btuB mRNAs of Escherichia coli and Salmonella typhimurium each carry a coenzyme B12-dependent riboswitch that causes repressed translation of the encoded cobalamin-transport protein at elevated coenzyme concentrations. Herein, we use a phylogenetic analysis to define a consensus sequence and secondary structure model for the ligand- binding domain of this riboswitch class. RNA structures that conform to this model are widespread in both Gram-positive and Gram-negative organisms. In addition, we find that the 5'-untranslated region (5'-UTR) of the cobalamin biosynthesis (cob) operon of S.typhimurium carries an RNA motif that matches this consensus sequence. Biochemical and genetic characterization of this motif confirms that the RNA directly binds coenzyme B12, and that it likely serves as a genetic control element for regulating expression of the 25-gene operon for cobalamin production in this pathogen.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 335 336 335 336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c1">1</xref>
###xml 734 735 734 735 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c2">2</xref>
###xml 736 737 736 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c3">3</xref>
###xml 755 756 755 756 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c4">4</xref>
Riboswitches serve as ligand-responsive genetic control elements that modulate the expression of certain genes in response to changing concentrations of metabolites. These RNA elements are typically embedded within the 5'-UTR of specific prokaryotic mRNAs, and are composed of two functional and sometimes distinct structural domains (1). One domain serves as a natural aptamer that binds the target metabolite with high selectivity. The other domain is an 'expression platform' that harnesses allosteric changes in RNA structure, brought about by aptamer-ligand complex formation, to control expression of the adjacent gene or operon. In prokaryotes, expression platforms have been identified that control transcription termination (2,3) or translation (4).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 170 187 170 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacillus subtilis</italic>
###xml 189 190 189 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c5">5</xref>
###xml 331 333 331 333 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 542 543 542 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c4">4</xref>
###xml 544 545 544 545 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c6">6</xref>
###xml 546 547 546 547 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c8">8</xref>
###xml 776 777 776 777 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c9">9</xref>
###xml 170 187 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">Bacillus subtilis</span>
To date, seven fundamental metabolites are known to be detected by riboswitches, which are responsible for contributing to the genetic regulation of at least 68 genes in Bacillus subtilis (5). These targets include the nucleobases guanine and adenine, the amino acid lysine, and the coenzymes thiamine pyrophosphate, FMN, SAM and B12 (adenosylcobalamin or AdoCbl). Several classes of riboswitches have been shown by database searches to be distributed widely amongst prokaryotic organisms, and some examples have been identified in archeans (4,6-8). Sequence variants of the natural aptamer for thiamine pyrophosphate also have been identified in certain fungal and plant mRNAs, where they bind the coenzyme with affinities that match those of their prokaryotic counterparts (9). These findings indicate that riboswitches are a fundamental and widespread form of genetic control.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 172 176 172 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 185 201 185 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 263 285 263 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Salmonella typhimurium</italic>
###xml 287 289 287 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c10">10</xref>
###xml 318 324 318 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 341 343 341 343 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 597 599 585 587 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 685 688 673 676 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 764 766 752 754 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 784 785 772 773 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 785 786 773 774 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D</sub>
###xml 879 881 867 869 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 904 914 892 902 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B.subtilis</italic>
###xml 1074 1076 1062 1064 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 185 201 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 263 285 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">Salmonella typhimurium</span>
###xml 318 324 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 904 914 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B.subtilis</span>
Initial confirmation that an mRNA domain directly binds to a metabolite in the absence of proteins was achieved by examining a 202-nucleotide fragment of the 5'-UTR of the btuB gene of Escherichia coli, and similarly by examining the homologous mRNA fragment for Salmonella typhimurium (10). The fragment derived from E.coli binds coenzyme B12 with an apparent dissociation constant of approximately300 nM, while related analogs such as cyanocobalamin and methylcobalamin do not exhibit measurable binding. In the current study, a refined consensus sequence and structural model for the coenzyme B12 class of riboswitches is presented. We establish that the 5'-leader sequence for the cob mRNA conforms to this consensus motif and that it directly binds coenzyme B12 with an apparent KD that matches those of related riboswitches. Furthermore, we identify a variant form of the B12 riboswitch class from B.subtilis that retains ligand-binding and genetic control functions despite the absence of nearly half of the conserved core that is typically indicative of a coenzyme B12 aptamer. The latter observation suggests that this class of riboswitches is composed of modular structural domains and that significant alterations to structure and function can occur through evolution.

###begin title 12
Analysis of RNA structures by in-line probing
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 18 26 18 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 143 152 143 152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC&#8211;lacZ</italic>
###xml 217 225 217 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 272 274 272 274 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">32</sup>

###xml 573 574 549 550 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
DNA templates for in vitro transcriptions were generated by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) using appropriate DNA primers (see for example the yvrC-lacZ fusion construction below). All RNA constructs were prepared by in vitro transcription using T7 RNA polymerase, and 5'-32P-labeled using methods described previously (2). Approximately 2 nM of end-labeled precursor RNA was subjected to in-line probing by incubating (in the dark to prevent light-mediated degradation of cobalamins) for approximately40 h at 25degreesC in 50 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.3 at 25degreesC), 20 mM MgCl2 and 100 mM KCl in the presence or absence of ligands as indicated. Spontaneous cleavage products were resolved by denaturing 10% PAGE, visualized by PhosphorImager analysis (Molecular Dynamics), and quantitated by ImageQuanNT software. Apparent dissociation constants of RNA-ligand interactions were determined by plotting the normalized fraction cleaved at indicated sites versus the logarithm of ligand concentration in molar units.

###begin title 23
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
###end title 23
###begin title 24
###xml 35 37 35 37 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
Phylogenetic analysis of coenzyme B12 riboswitches reveals a complex RNA motif
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 19 23 19 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 35 41 35 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 46 59 46 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium</italic>
###xml 158 160 158 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c10">10</xref>
###xml 188 190 188 190 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 379 382 379 382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 393 406 393 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium</italic>
###xml 460 462 460 462 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 468 470 468 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c12">12</xref>
###xml 471 473 471 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c14">14</xref>
###xml 35 41 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 46 59 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
###xml 393 406 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
The 5'-UTRs of the btuB genes from E.coli and S.typhimurium each contain a large RNA domain that functions as a metabolite-dependent genetic control element (10). Both RNAs bind coenzyme B12 (5'-deoxy-5'-adenosylcobalamin or AdoCbl) with high affinity and specificity, and thus function as natural aptamers for this important metabolite. These RNAs, along with the 5'-UTR of the cob gene from S.typhimurium, carry a short conserved sequence domain termed the B12 box (12,14), and therefore they likely share structural and functional homology.

###xml 116 122 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 127 140 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
Although we proposed a partial secondary structural model for the riboswitch domains present in the btuB mRNAs from E.coli and S.typhimurium (10), the model was not in agreement with that proposed recently for the 5'-UTR of the cob gene (14). To improve upon the structural model, additional examples of the coenzyme B12 class of riboswitches were identified in other organisms. This was achieved through an iterative approach to secondary-structure modeling and comparative sequence analysis using a computer algorithm. The algorithm (SequenceSniffer; J. E. Barrick and R. R. Breaker, unpublished) searches genomic databases for domains that correspond closely to specific sequence and structural constraints, and has been used previously to identify homologs for the TPP (9), guanine (5), SAM (7) and lysine riboswitches (15). More specifically, database searches such as these are effective in revealing similar aptamer domains, as the metabolite-binding portions of riboswitches are the most highly conserved amongst various organisms and amongst different mRNAs within a single organism.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 57 59 57 59 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 139 140 139 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f1">1</xref>
###xml 202 204 202 204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c16">16</xref>
###xml 379 380 379 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f2">2</xref>
###xml 427 429 427 429 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
A total of 92 different representatives of the coenzyme B12 class of riboswitches were identified and compared by sequence alignment (Fig. 1; see also the Rfam database for additional representatives) (16). Upon examination of the sequence alignments, a pattern of conserved sequence and secondary-structure elements that are present in all representatives becomes evident (Fig. 2A). The most striking observation is that the B12 box encompasses only a small portion of the conserved sequence and structural domain. The phylogenetic analysis indicates that this complex RNA motif has at least 10 base-paired elements and approximately57 nucleotide positions whose identity is conserved in >90% of the representative sequences.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 74 85 74 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli btuB</italic>
###xml 92 94 92 94 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c10">10</xref>
###xml 367 368 367 368 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f2">2</xref>
###xml 74 80 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The secondary-structure model proposed previously for the riboswitch from E.coli btuB mRNA (10) corresponds only partially with the new model that is based on phylogenetic data. Specifically, pairing elements P4 (part) and P5 to P8 of the original structural model are retained in the revised model with the modified designations P5 and P8 to P12, respectively (Fig. 2B). In addition, the P9 stem from the original model is retained without designation in the revised model, as it resides outside the domain of highest sequence and structural conservation. In contrast, the revised model includes numerous short base-paired elements that explain the role of nucleotides that were left unfolded in the earlier model, and also that preclude formation of certain other pairing possibilities. Specifically, stems P1 to P3, as predicted in the original model, are replaced and supplemented in the revised model by stems P1 to P4, P6, P7 and P10.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 103 105 103 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c17">17</xref>
###xml 106 108 106 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c18">18</xref>
###xml 174 176 174 176 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 277 288 277 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli btuB</italic>
###xml 370 372 370 372 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 505 507 505 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c10">10</xref>
###xml 696 697 696 697 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f1">1</xref>
###xml 791 793 791 793 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 813 814 813 814 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f2">2</xref>
###xml 973 974 973 974 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f1">1</xref>
###xml 1085 1087 1085 1087 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c17">17</xref>
###xml 1088 1090 1088 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c18">18</xref>
###xml 277 283 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The revised secondary-structure model is similar to a model proposed recently for this same RNA motif (17,18). Most notably, these two models differ with respect to how the B12 box is folded, and on the structural details of the bridge between the left and right halves of the E.coli btuB aptamer. Several lines of evidence support our revised structural model for the B12 aptamer. First, the new secondary-structure model is largely consistent with structural probing data that was presented previously (10). Likewise, mutational analysis supported the formation of a bridging stem element that is retained in the revised model (P8). Thirdly, representatives within the sequence alignment (Fig. 1) exhibit considerable sequence covariation within the stem elements that are common to most B12 riboswitches (Fig. 2B), indicating that these pairing elements are important for riboswitch function. Specifically, the covariation in sequences of proposed stems P3 and P6 (Fig. 1) suggest that these structures are more likely to be formed than the alternative structure proposed recently (17,18).
###end p 29
###begin p 30
###xml 305 307 305 307 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
The revised secondary-structure model is more complex than those of other riboswitches. Interestingly, biochemical evidence (see below) suggests that, at least in some cases, RNA constructs that encompass only the minimal aptamer domain are not sufficient to exhibit maximal binding affinity to coenzyme B12. These latter results indicate that the more highly conserved portion of the riboswitch might not comprise the complete and fully functional aptamer motif. Thus, the likely involvement of more distal RNA elements (such as a possible pseudoknot interaction between the loop of P5 and downstream sequences) adds even greater complexity to the RNA's structure.
###end p 30
###begin title 31
###xml 17 21 17 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 26 29 26 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 39 52 39 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium</italic>
###xml 68 70 68 70 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 39 52 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
5'-UTRs from the btuB and cob mRNAs of S.typhimurium bind coenzyme B12
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 5 9 5 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 37 40 37 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 51 64 51 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium</italic>
###xml 94 96 94 96 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 116 118 116 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c14">14</xref>
###xml 119 121 119 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c19">19</xref>
###xml 122 124 122 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c20">20</xref>
###xml 177 179 177 179 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 282 289 282 289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo</italic>
###xml 313 315 313 315 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 491 493 491 493 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c17">17</xref>
###xml 494 496 494 496 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c18">18</xref>
###xml 545 547 545 547 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 585 588 585 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 601 614 601 614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium</italic>
###xml 621 622 621 622 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f1">1</xref>
###xml 51 64 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
###xml 601 614 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
Like btuB, genetic expression of the cob mRNA from S.typhimurium is known to be regulated by B12-related compounds (14,19,20). We speculated that the presence of the conserved B12 box also could be an indication that the mRNA that encodes for the majority of the genes required for de novo synthesis of coenzyme B12 might be negatively regulated by the metabolic end product. Consistent with this hypothesis is the observation that our database search, along with another reported recently (17,18), for sequence elements with similarity to the B12 aptamer identified the 5'-UTR of the cob operon from S.typhimurium (Fig. 1).
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 29 47 29 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium btuB</italic>
###xml 141 147 141 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 161 162 161 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f3">3</xref>
###xml 213 216 213 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 258 262 258 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 326 328 326 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c14">14</xref>
###xml 338 341 338 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 401 403 401 403 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 432 433 432 433 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f2">2</xref>
###xml 461 464 461 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 543 545 543 545 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 654 657 654 657 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 712 713 712 713 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f3">3</xref>
###xml 756 758 756 758 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 779 780 779 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f2">2</xref>
###xml 959 963 959 963 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 968 971 968 971 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 983 996 983 996 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium</italic>
###xml 1056 1058 1056 1058 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 29 42 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
###xml 141 147 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 983 996 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
As expected, the 5'-UTR from S.typhimurium btuB mRNA has considerable sequence and predicted structural similarity to that of the homologous E.coli leader (Fig. 3A). In contrast, the sequence of the corresponding cob 5'-UTR varies significantly from that of btuB. Furthermore, the secondary-structure model proposed recently (14) for the cob RNA differs substantially from our model for the coenzyme B12 aptamer presented in Figure 2. However, we find that the cob 5'-UTR does carry the consensus sequence elements that are indicative of the B12 class of riboswitches, despite the considerable sequence differences between the two RNAs. Furthermore, the cob 5'-UTR can be folded into a secondary structure (Fig. 3B) that carries the major features of the B12 aptamer model (Fig. 2A). The differences in sequence and predicted base-pairing potential reside in portions of the RNA that are not conserved through evolution. Thus, the two leader domains from the btuB and cob mRNAs from S.typhimurium each carry a putative ligand binding aptamer domain for a B12-dependent riboswitch.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 67 71 67 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 76 79 76 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 107 120 107 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium</italic>
###xml 154 156 154 156 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 199 201 199 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c10">10</xref>
###xml 208 212 208 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 229 231 229 231 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 340 346 340 346 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 353 354 353 354 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f3">3</xref>
###xml 450 453 450 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 474 475 474 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f3">3</xref>
###xml 619 621 619 621 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 834 837 834 837 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 868 870 868 870 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 1018 1020 1018 1020 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 107 120 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
###xml 340 346 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
In-line probing analyses were conducted with fragments of both the btuB and cob mRNA leader sequences from S.typhimurium that correspond to the putative B12 aptamer domains. As indicated previously (10), the btuB mRNA undergoes B12-dependent structure modulation with a pattern that is similar to that observed for the homologous mRNA from E.coli (Fig. 3C). The data also reveal that substantial ligand-dependent structure modulation occurs with the cob mRNA fragment (Fig. 3D). Upon closer inspection, the spontaneous cleavage patterns for both RNAs correspond well with the revised secondary-structure model for the B12 aptamer, wherein the sites of modulation largely correlate with conserved bulges and sites of constant high cleavage correlate with the non-conserved loops of stems. Therefore, we conclude that the 5'-UTR of the cob mRNA directly binds coenzyme B12 without the participation of protein factors, and that the biosynthetic pathway for this metabolite is regulated, at least in part, by a coenzyme B12-responsive riboswitch.
###end p 34
###begin title 35
Ligand-binding affinity varies with the length of the 5'-UTR fragment
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 107 108 107 108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 108 109 108 109 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D</sub>
###xml 154 156 154 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c21">21</xref>
###xml 215 216 215 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 216 217 216 217 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D</sub>
###xml 257 259 257 259 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 273 277 273 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 293 299 293 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 324 326 312 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c10">10</xref>
###xml 362 366 350 354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 382 395 370 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium</italic>
###xml 417 418 405 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 418 419 406 407 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D</sub>
###xml 455 456 431 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f4">4</xref>
###xml 293 299 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 382 395 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
The ligand-dependent changes in spontaneous RNA cleavage can be quantitated and used to derive an apparent KD value for ligand-aptamer complex formation (21). This approach was used previously to obtain an apparent KD value for the interaction of coenzyme B12 with the 202 btuB construct from E.coli of approximately300 nM (10). Similarly, the corresponding 206 btuB construct from S.typhimurium exhibits an apparent KD value of approximately400 nM (Fig. 4A).
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 188 192 188 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thiM</italic>
###xml 197 201 197 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thiC</italic>
###xml 211 217 211 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 372 373 372 373 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c4">4</xref>
###xml 211 217 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The binding affinities for certain riboswitch constructs are known to vary depending upon the length of the construct used. For example, the thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP) aptamers from the thiM and thiC mRNAs of E.coli exhibit the highest affinity for TPP, while larger constructs that carry portions of the expression platform each bind with significantly lower affinity (4). This is likely due to the presence of competing RNA structures that detract from tight ligand binding by reducing preorganization of the aptamer domain. In other words, the allosteric nature of the full riboswitch element (aptamer plus expression platform) might be expected to result in a poorer equilibrium constant for complex formation with its target ligand due to these dynamic but mutually exclusive folds.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 104 106 104 106 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 251 255 251 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 273 276 273 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 427 428 427 428 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f4">4</xref>
We examined several RNA constructs that encompass the minimal consensus sequence and structure for the B12 aptamer to determine whether such molecules might exhibit higher affinity for the coenzyme. Interestingly, the shortened constructs for the two btuB RNAs and for the cob RNA experience a significant (>100-fold) loss of binding affinity when the riboswitch is trimmed to represent only the conserved aptamer domain (Fig. 4B). This suggests that sequence or structural element(s) within the presumed non-conserved domain of the riboswitch aid in proper formation of the more highly conserved aptamer.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 109 111 109 111 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 346 348 346 348 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 373 384 373 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli btuB</italic>
###xml 386 388 386 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c10">10</xref>
###xml 700 701 700 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f2">2</xref>
###xml 780 782 780 782 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 373 379 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
In contrast to other instances where extra-consensus sequences detract from binding affinity, the consensus B12 aptamer domain (at least in some cases) makes productive use of flanking sequences to form an aptamer domain with improved affinity. Previously, we had suggested that a pseudoknot might play an important role in the formation of the B12 riboswitch structure of E.coli btuB (10). This speculative proposal was based on the fact that structural probing data were consistent with such an interaction, although mutational analysis was inconclusive. This proposed pseudoknot, which is depicted as a base pairing interaction between the loop of P5 and the loop of a non-conserved hairpin (Fig. 2B), might play a role in supporting the consensus domain in its function as a B12 aptamer. If true, this putative interaction is not obviously present in all other representatives of the phylogeny examined in this study.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
###xml 26 28 26 28 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 29 32 29 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
Confirmation of coenzyme B12-cob RNA interaction by equilibrium dialysis
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 50 53 50 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 93 95 93 95 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 176 178 176 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c10">10</xref>
###xml 202 206 202 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 226 232 226 232 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 288 291 288 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 377 379 377 379 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 423 425 423 425 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 226 232 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Equilibrium dialysis was used to confirm that the cob mRNA sequence directly binds coenzyme B12 in the absence of proteins (data not shown), as had been determined previously (10) for the corresponding btuB mRNA fragment from E.coli. A Scatchard analysis was also carried out for the 386 cob RNA by using equilibrium dialysis with various concentrations of tritiated coenzyme B12 (data not shown). Unfortunately, coenzyme B12 is highly photo-labile, and the data were not of sufficient quality to establish stoichiometry with confidence.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 571 574 571 574 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 668 669 656 657 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f4">4</xref>
###xml 677 678 665 666 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">x</italic>
Specifically, we found that 27% of the tritium in a control equilibrium dialysis assay failed to bind the RNA, which is most likely due to breakdown of the compound during assembly of the assay mixtures, despite taking precautions to minimize exposure to light. The uncertainty with respect to the extent of degradation in each independent equilibrium dialysis run likely contributes scatter in the placement of data points on the Scatchard plot. Although the plot should be interpreted with caution, a best-fit line to the data indicates a dissociation constant for the cob aptamer of approximately200 nM, which is similar to that determined by in-line probing (Fig. 4B). The x intercept of this line resides between 0.75 and 1, which is most consistent with a 1:1 relationship between ligand and RNA when in complex.
###end p 42
###begin title 43
###xml 20 22 20 22 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 48 63 48 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B.subtilis yvrC</italic>
###xml 48 58 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B.subtilis</span>
A variant coenzyme B12 riboswitch occurs in the B.subtilis yvrC mRNA
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 50 52 50 52 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 97 107 97 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B.subtilis</italic>
###xml 114 115 114 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f1">1</xref>
###xml 120 121 120 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f5">5</xref>
###xml 250 254 250 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 256 260 256 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrB</italic>
###xml 262 266 262 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrA</italic>
###xml 271 275 271 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvqK</italic>
###xml 335 338 335 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvr</italic>
###xml 495 497 495 497 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 97 107 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B.subtilis</span>
The phylogenetic survey of domains that resemble B12 riboswitches revealed a single example from B.subtilis (Figs 1 and 5A). This representative resides immediately upstream of an apparent four-gene operon that carries the open reading frames (ORFs) yvrC, yvrB, yvrA and yvqK. Although the functions of these ORFs remain unproven, the yvr genes appear to encode proteins involved in metal import and processing, and thus modulation of their levels of genetic expression in response to coenzyme B12 concentrations seems reasonable since this coenzyme carries a metal ligand.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 19 29 19 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B.subtilis</italic>
###xml 196 197 196 197 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f2">2</xref>
###xml 254 256 254 256 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 275 279 275 279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 427 431 427 431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 452 454 452 454 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 19 29 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B.subtilis</span>
Interestingly, the B.subtilis variant is lacking the P8, P10 and P11 helices, which otherwise would present sequence and structural elements that are conserved in most other representatives (Fig. 2A). The absence of this highly conserved portion of the B12 aptamer makes the yvrC RNA one of the most extreme variants identified in our phylogenetic analysis. Therefore, we conducted several experiments to determine whether the yvrC RNA binds coenzyme B12 and whether the riboswitch-like domain responds genetically to this effector when appended to a reporter gene.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 63 78 63 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B.subtilis yvrC</italic>
###xml 112 116 112 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 125 129 125 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 193 195 193 195 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 264 265 264 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f5">5</xref>
###xml 384 388 384 388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 542 544 542 544 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 619 620 619 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f5">5</xref>
###xml 63 73 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B.subtilis</span>
Three RNA constructs were created based on the UTR sequence of B.subtilis yvrC. The two largest constructs, 259 yvrC and 149 yvrC, both exhibit structural modulation upon addition of coenzyme B12, which indicate that they both retain ligand-binding function (Fig. 5B and C) despite the absence of the P8, P10 and P11 helices and the associated conserved nucleotides. However, the 120 yvrC construct, which carries only those nucleotides that correspond closely to the left-most portion of the consensus aptamer motif, fails to bind coenzyme B12 as indicated by the absence of ligand-induced structural modulation (Fig. 5D).
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 144 148 144 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 205 207 205 207 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 461 465 461 465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 563 569 563 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 574 592 574 592 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium btuB</italic>
###xml 721 725 721 725 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 730 734 730 734 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 782 786 782 786 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 861 863 861 863 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c10">10</xref>
###xml 879 883 879 883 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 900 906 900 906 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 1082 1084 1082 1084 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 1136 1140 1136 1140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 563 569 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 574 587 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
###xml 900 906 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
This finding suggests that a structural element that is critical for high-affinity binding resides within nucleotides 121 and 149 of the larger yvrC constructs. In addition, this result suggests that the B12 riboswitch might make use of a two-lobed aptamer, where the left and right halves of the RNA form distinct folds that independently recognize different parts of the ligand. In this model, the novel 29-nucleotide domain that forms the 3' terminus of 149 yvrC might serve as a functional replacement for the absence of stems P8 to P12 that are found in the E.coli and S.typhimurium btuB RNAs. In a preliminary assessment of this hypothesis, we have examined whether the molecular recognition characteristics of the yvrC and btuB variants might be different. Specifically, the yvrC leader exhibits poorer discrimination against purinyl cobalamin (PurCbl) (10) than does the btuB riboswitch from E.coli (data not shown). Although other explanations for this observation can be envisioned, it is possible that the right half of the aptamer binds the adenosyl moiety of coenzyme B12 and that the 29-nucleotide alternative domain from yvrC recognizes this moiety somewhat differently.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 18 33 18 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B.subtilis yvrC</italic>
###xml 62 64 62 64 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 120 127 120 127 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 159 166 159 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 226 230 226 230 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 246 247 246 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh167f5">5</xref>
###xml 351 361 348 358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B.subtilis</italic>
###xml 465 467 447 449 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 587 589 569 571 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 668 672 650 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
###xml 707 709 689 691 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 18 28 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B.subtilis</span>
###xml 351 361 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B.subtilis</span>
The 5'-UTR of the B.subtilis yvrC mRNA also permits coenzyme B12-dependent modulation of a reporter gene when expressed in vivo. To assess riboswitch function in vivo, a DNA construct carrying the first 275 nucleotides of the yvrC sequence (Fig. 5A) was appended to a beta-galactosidase gene in a transcriptional reporter vector. When integrated into B.subtilis, the construct exhibits an approximately3-fold reduction of beta-galactosidase activity when coenzyme B12 is added to the culture medium. Specifically, Miller units of 142 and 47 were obtained in the absence and presence of B12 supplementation to the medium, respectively. These findings indicate that the yvrC variant is a functional coenzyme B12-specific riboswitch, despite its weaker correspondence to the sequence and structure of most other representatives in the phylogeny.
###end p 48
###begin title 49
CONCLUSIONS
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 247 249 247 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c17">17</xref>
###xml 250 252 250 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh167c18">18</xref>
###xml 282 284 282 284 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 563 565 563 565 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
The established diversity of riboswitches in living systems is continuing to expand as biochemical, genetic and bioinformatic approaches are being applied to find and characterize new examples. Phylogenetic analyses reported herein and elsewhere (17,18) indicate that the coenzyme B12 class of riboswitches is widely distributed amongst both Gram- positive and Gram-negative prokaryotes. In addition, our data indicate that RNA domains matching the consensus sequence and structure of this riboswitch class from three different organisms do indeed bind coenzyme B12 and control gene expression in response to high ligand concentrations.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 58 60 58 60 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 223 225 223 225 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 240 253 240 253 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.typhimurium</italic>
###xml 282 286 282 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 422 425 422 425 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cob</italic>
###xml 610 612 610 612 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 801 803 801 803 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 902 904 902 904 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 240 253 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">S.typhimurium</span>
In many instances, genes under the control of a coenzyme B12 riboswitch are involved in the transport of cobalamin compounds or metals, or associated with the biosynthetic pathway for the coenzyme. For example, a coenzyme B12 riboswitch in S.typhimurium modulates expression of the btuB gene encoding a cobalamin transport protein, and a second riboswitch of this class serves as a genetic control element for the 25-gene cob operon. In certain organisms, these riboswitches control the expression of genes that are more distantly related to the coenzyme. Perhaps most striking is the involvement of coenzyme B12 riboswitches in the control of ribonucleotide reductases that do not use this coenzyme. Presumably, when this compound is at a sufficiently high concentration, expression of the coenzyme B12-independent ribonucleotide reductase is reduced to defer this metabolic activity to the coenzyme B12-dependent form of the enzyme.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 118 120 118 120 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">12</sub>
###xml 308 312 308 312 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">btuB</italic>
###xml 632 636 632 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvrC</italic>
The potential complexity of riboswitch structures is also apparent by examining the structural model of the coenzyme B12 class of riboswitches. In addition to the numerous short helices and conserved sequence elements, long-range RNA interactions likely augment aptamer function, at least in the case of the btuB variants examined. Given the widespread distribution of riboswitches, RNA appears to have sufficient structural and functional diversity to perform reliably as genetic control elements for genes involved in critical biological processes. The substantial deviation in consensus sequence and structure exemplified by the yvrC RNA and several others indicates that considerable changes in riboswitch structure can occur through evolutionary processes. Although the large-scale alteration of this riboswitch class appears to be somewhat limited, it seems reasonable to speculate that variant riboswitches might exist that respond selectively to closely related metabolites.
###end p 52
