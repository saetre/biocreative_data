GATA4/FOG2 transcriptional complex regulates Lhx9 gene expression in murine heart development

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 71 75 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
GATA4 and FOG2 proteins are required for normal cardiac development in mice. It has been proposed that GATA4/FOG2 transcription complex exercises its function through gene activation as well as repression; however, targets of GATA4/FOG2 action in the heart remain elusive.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 37 42 37 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 181 186 181 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 188 198 188 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945; </italic>
###xml 202 210 198 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#946;</italic>
###xml 224 232 217 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9-HD </italic>
###xml 299 314 292 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 393 401 379 387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9-HD </italic>
###xml 557 572 536 544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 654 659 626 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 685 700 657 665 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 782 787 747 752 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 915 920 880 885 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 992 995 957 960 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ki </sup>
###xml 128 133 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 635 640 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Here we report identification of the Lhx9 gene as a direct target of the GATA4/FOG2 complex. We demonstrate that the developing mouse heart normally expresses truncated isoforms of Lhx9 - Lhx9alpha and Lhx9beta, and not the Lhx9-HD isoform that encodes a protein with an intact homeodomain. At E9.5 Lhx9alpha/beta expression is prominent in the epicardial primordium, septum transversum while Lhx9-HD is absent from this tissue; in the E11.5 heart LHX9alpha/beta-positive cells are restricted to the epicardial mesothelium. Thereafter in the control hearts Lhx9alpha/beta epicardial expression is promptly down-regulated; in contrast, mouse mutants with Fog2 gene loss fail to repress Lhx9alpha/beta expression. Chromatin immunoprecipitation from the E11.5 hearts demonstrated that Lhx9 is a direct target for GATA4 and FOG2. In transient transfection studies the expression driven by the cis-regulatory regions of Lhx9 was repressed by FOG2 in the presence of intact GATA4, but not the GATA4ki mutant that is impaired in its ability to bind FOG2.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 16 21 16 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
In summary, the Lhx9 gene represents the first direct target of the GATA4/FOG2 repressor complex in cardiac development.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 245 249 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Unlike many developing organs that remain dormant until the time of birth, a properly functioning embryonic heart is essential for embryo survival. Hence, defects in cardiac function are a common cause for embryonic lethality. Gene targeting in mice revealed multiple genes that are required for cardiac development and function; however, the interplay between these genes often remains a mystery. This is especially true for genes encoding for transcription factors which are expressed in a dynamic fashion in various cellular compartments that constitute the developing heart.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 156 157 156 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 256 257 256 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 260 264 260 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2</italic>
###xml 264 268 264 268 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 591 595 591 595 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2</italic>
###xml 595 599 595 599 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 722 727 722 727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 950 951 950 951 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 952 953 952 953 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 1212 1213 1212 1213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 1221 1222 1221 1222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 45 50 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
###xml 230 234 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 599 603 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Friend of GATA, member 2 gene (FOG2, ZFPM2 - Mouse Genome Informatics) is prominently expressed in multiple cell types that constitute the embryonic heart [1]. To examine the function for FOG2 in cardiac development gene-targeted mice have been generated [2]. Fog2-/- (null) embryos die at mid-gestation (~E13.5) with a cardiac defect characterized by an atrial septal defect, thin ventricular myocardium, common atrioventricular (AV) canal and the Tetralogy of Fallot malformation. Of particular additional interest is the finding that the development of cardiac vasculature was blocked in Fog2-/- mice. Despite the apparently normal formation of an intact epicardial layer and expression of epicardium-specific genes in Fog2 null mutants, markers of cardiac vessel development (ICAM-2 and KDR) are not detected, indicative of failure to activate their expression and/or to initiate the epithelial to mesenchymal transformation of epicardial cells [2,3]. These results are particularly insightful with respect to KDR, since KDR (FLK1, VEGFR2), the major receptor for VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor), is an important marker of vascular cells and is absolutely essential for vascular development (e.g. [4], see [5] for a review).
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 381 382 381 382 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 383 384 383 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 704 705 704 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 840 841 840 841 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 975 977 975 977 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 1043 1048 1043 1048 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Eklf </italic>
###xml 1139 1141 1139 1141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 1142 1144 1142 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
Although gene targeting revealed the requirement for FOG2 in multiple aspects of cardiac development, the specific genetic program (or programs) downstream of FOG2 remained unknown. Research by us and others established that multi-type zinc-finger proteins of the FOG family (FOG1, FOG2, xFOG and dUSH) control biological activities of GATA transcription factors (for review, see [6,7]). Based on the results from the hematopoietic system it has been suggested that FOG proteins serve as co-factors for GATA family members by forming a GATA/FOG complex on specific GATA sites. Indeed, differentially regulated genes have been identified for GATA1-FOG1 complex vs. GATA1 alone in blood development (e.g. [8]. Furthermore, these studies demonstrated that FOG1 could stimulate or inhibit GATA1 activity depending on cell and promoter context [9]. For example, FOG1 stimulates GATA1 activity on the p45 NF-E2 gene promoter, which is active in erythroid cells and megakaryocytes [10]; however, it represses GATA1 activity on the erythroid-specific Eklf and transferrin receptor II promoters, as well as on a synthetic GATA1-dependent promoter [11,12]
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 185 188 185 188 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anf</italic>
###xml 190 194 190 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bnp </italic>
###xml 198 208 198 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;-Mhc </italic>
###xml 243 245 239 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 246 248 242 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 305 310 301 306 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 318 323 314 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gata4</italic>
###xml 323 329 319 325 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ki/ki </italic>
###xml 323 329 319 325 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>ki/ki </italic></sup>
###xml 347 353 343 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gata4 </italic>
###xml 363 365 359 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 522 523 518 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 524 526 520 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 527 529 523 525 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
Earlier studies aimed at the similar characterization of the GATA4-FOG2 relationship with respect to cardiac development relied on transient transfection assays with well-characterized Anf, Bnp and alpha-Mhc promoters that contain GATA sites [13,14]. However, these genes are normally expressed in mutant Fog2 null or Gata4ki/ki hearts (the V217G Gata4 mutation [15] which specifically cripples the interaction between GATA4 and FOG proteins) arguing against the essential role for GATA4/FOG2 complex in their regulation [2,15,16]. Hence, transcriptional targets of GATA4/FOG2 complex in the heart are currently unknown.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 12 17 12 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gata4</italic>
###xml 17 23 17 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ki/ki </italic>
###xml 17 23 17 23 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>ki/ki </italic></sup>
###xml 69 74 69 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 166 168 166 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 611 613 611 613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 614 616 614 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
The cardiac Gata4ki/ki phenotype showed numerous similarities to the Fog2 null heart underscoring the role for GATA4/FOG2 protein complex in cardiac gene regulation [15]. However, the mode of GATA4/FOG2 action, as a repressor or activator, remains unclear and necessitates identification of its downstream targets. In order to identify the targets of GATA4/FOG2 action in the mammalian heart we performed several Affymetrix microarray comparisons of gene expression in normal and mutant E13.5 hearts. As many groups have identified the regions in FOG proteins that mediate their function as GATA co-repressors [17-22], we expected that gene up-regulation (de-repression) should be, at least partially, responsible for causing cardiac syndrome in the GATA4/FOG2 mutants.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 97 102 97 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 383 385 383 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 389 396 389 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LIM-HD </italic>
###xml 524 526 524 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 710 712 710 712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 713 715 713 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 782 787 782 787 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 887 892 887 892 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 927 932 927 932 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx2 </italic>
###xml 933 935 933 935 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 992 997 992 997 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lxh9 </italic>
###xml 1023 1025 1023 1025 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 1100 1105 1100 1105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 1154 1159 1154 1159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 790 794 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Here we describe one of the targets of GATA4/FOG2 complex in its transcriptional repressor role, Lhx9 gene. LHX9 belongs to the family of the LIM-HD (LIM-homeodomain) proteins. The roles for the majority of LIM-HD encoding genes have been mostly defined in the context of nervous system development where they act to specify neuronal identities of post-mitotic neurons (reviewed in [23]). LIM-HD genes act in a context-dependent fashion, cooperating with other factors to establish enormous diversity of the nervous system [24]. The function for LHX9 was proposed in the development of the nervous system as this gene is prominently expressed in the motoneurons of the spinal cord and in the developing brain [25-28]. Despite this prominent neuronal expression pattern, knockout of Lhx9 in mice did not affect animal viability or neuronal development probably reflecting a redundancy of Lhx9 and its close structural relative, Lhx2 [29]; unexpectedly, the knockout revealed a requirement for Lxh9 in early gonad formation [29]. Our studies demonstrate that GATA4/FOG2 transcription complex regulates Lhx9 cardiac expression and suggest that the role for Lhx9 in the development of the heart should be evaluated.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Results
###end title 15
###begin title 16
###xml 26 30 26 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9</italic>
Cardiac expression of the Lhx9 gene splicing isoforms
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 167 172 167 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 369 370 369 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 440 445 440 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 477 482 477 482 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
In order to identify the targets of GATA4/FOG2 action in mammalian heart development we performed an Affymetrix microarray comparison of gene expression in normal and Fog2 null E13.5 hearts. The microarray profiling yielded surprisingly few genes that were differentially (~2.5 times up- or down-regulated) expressed in the mutant samples vs. controls (Additional File 1 and data not shown). Importantly, the probe set corresponding to the Fog2 gene deletion was absent in the Fog2 null sample, as was expected.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 68 73 68 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 111 116 111 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 148 149 148 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 259 261 259 261 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 408 413 408 413 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 639 644 639 644 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 894 896 894 896 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 897 899 897 899 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 941 948 941 948 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9-HD</italic>
###xml 982 991 982 987 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;</italic>
###xml 1064 1066 1060 1062 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 1218 1223 1214 1219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A, B</xref>
###xml 1275 1283 1271 1276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#946;</italic>
###xml 621 626 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
One of the probe sets differentially represented in the control vs. Fog2 mutant RNA sample corresponded to the Lhx9 gene (~3 times; Additional File 1) encoding the LHX9 protein that belongs to the family of the LIM-HD (LIM-homeodomain) transcription factors [23]. The major research interest of this laboratory is in the transcriptional regulation of cardiac development; hence we decided to pursue the FOG2-Lhx9 connection. To correctly evaluate the microarray data it was critical to identify the actual mRNA species corresponding to the microarray oligonucleotide probe set. The NCBI database predicts three different mouse isoforms of Lhx9 mRNA; two of these isoforms have been described previously, while the third isoform has not been characterized. Originally identified transcript 3 encodes a 397 amino acid (aa) long protein with intact homeodomain (HD) that is competent to bind DNA [26,27]; this isoform is further referred to as Lhx9-HD. A previously described isoform, Lhx9alpha, encodes a 330 aa protein with truncated HD and a different C terminus [30]; finally, the uncharacterized transcript (variant 2) would encode a 321 aa protein that has a different N-terminus in addition to a truncated HD (Fig 1A, B). We will refer to the un-characterized isoform as Lhx9beta.
###end p 18

###begin p 20
###xml 66 68 66 68 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">35</sup>
###xml 113 123 113 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945; </italic>
###xml 199 207 195 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9-HD </italic>
###xml 224 226 220 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 337 342 333 338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 434 439 430 435 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 505 514 501 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;</italic>
###xml 522 531 514 520 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#946; </italic>
###xml 586 588 575 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2A</xref>
###xml 613 618 602 607 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 720 725 709 714 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 778 792 767 774 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;+&#946;</italic>
###xml 862 864 844 846 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2B</xref>
###xml 895 900 877 882 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 935 937 917 919 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2C</xref>
It has been reported previously that in situ hybridization using S35-labeled isoform-specific RNA probes detects Lhx9alpha expression in the developing E13.5 heart, while the full-length HD-encoding Lhx9-HD is not detected [30]. To corroborate and extend this data we designed the primers that discriminate between all three isoforms of Lhx9 and performed real-time PCR analysis. By real time RT-PCR we have determined that the major Lhx9 isoform expressed in the developing heart at E13.5 corresponds to Lhx9alpha, while Lhx9beta transcript is expressed at a somewhat lower level (Fig 2A). Cardiac expression of Lhx9 is under strict developmental control: qRT-PCR analysis of total heart RNA demonstrated that combined Lhx9 expression corresponding to both expressed isoforms (Lhx9alpha+beta) is down-regulated between E11.5 and E14.5 approximately 7-fold (Fig 2B); during the same time period Fog2 gene expression is increasing (Fig 2C).

###begin p 21
###xml 25 29 25 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9</italic>
###xml 65 69 65 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2</italic>
###xml 0 81 0 81 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Quantitative analysis of <italic>Lhx9</italic> gene expression in the control and <italic>Fog2</italic> null hearts</bold>
###xml 83 84 83 84 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</bold>
###xml 159 169 159 165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945; </italic>

To label the cells in the epicardial region at this early stage in epicardial development we used an antibody specific for the endothelial integral membrane glycoprotein endoglin (ENG) [33] that recognizes epicardial cells that are already committed to the endothelial lineage both in the control and in Fog2 mutant hearts [2]. Although the majority of the LHX9-positive cells are confined to the epicardium, a weaker immunoreactivity is detectable in the myocardium (Fig 4) suggesting that a low level expression of LHX9alpha/beta could be present in these cells.

Expression of the Lhx9 homologue, Lhx2, in the septum transversum (STM) has been previously reported [45]; however, we noted that the authors in their analysis used a probe that corresponds to the whole-length Lhx2 cDNA (NM_010710; nucleotides 460-1750). A sequence comparison revealed two large regions of almost complete identity between the Lhx2 probe sequence and Lhx9; hence, we believe the authors [45] could not discriminate between Lhx2 and Lhx9 in their in situ assay. To distinguish between these homologues we designed an Lhx2 probe that is limited to the most 5'-region of the Lhx2 sequence (NM_010710; nucleotides 42 to 488) and has no homology to Lhx9; we also generated an in situ probe that is specific for Lhx9-HD. In contrast, the transcript corresponding to the full-length Lhx9-HD could not be detected in the STM (Fig 5A, bottom panels); this rules out the transient role for the Lhx9-HD transcript in the STM. The loss of GATA4/FOG2 interaction leads to an over-expression (de-repression) of Lhx9alpha/beta cardiac expression. Based on the inability of myocardially-restricted FOG2 to down-regulate ectopic epicardial Lhx9alpha/beta expression (Fig 5B), we hypothesized that the Lhx9 gene could be a direct target of GATA4-FOG2 mediated repression. To demonstrate that the repression of the Lhx9 gene requires binding of the GATA4/FOG2 protein complex to the Lhx9 cis-regulatory elements we inspected the Lhx9 genomic locus using ECR (evolutionary conserved region) browser [47] (Additional File 3A). As both Lhx9alpha and Lhx9beta are expressed in the heart we examined the conserved regions corresponding to both isoforms. We limited our analysis to the ~5 kb region upstream of the Lhx9beta transcription start site; this 5 kb region is delimited by the un-characterized ORF (2310009B15Rik in the mouse; C1ORF53 in the human genome) (Fig 6A and Additional File 3A). We identified two evolutionary conserved GATA sites within the annotated ECRs (Fig 6A, B and Additional File 3A); one GATA site (-521) is present within the promoter region corresponding to the Lhx9alpha while the other site (-4441) is located upstream of both isoforms (Fig 6A). To examine the GATA4/FOG2 complex binding to these two elements in vivo, we performed a chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) assay from E11.5 embryonic hearts. This time point was chosen since at E11.5 cardiac expression of Lhx9 is still present in the normal heart and is responsive to Fog2 expression: Lhx9alpha/beta expression is strongly enhanced in the absence of Fog2 (Figs 2D and 3A-D). We demonstrated that either the alpha GATA4 or alpha FOG2 antibody specifically pulls down DNA containing conserved GATA sites indicating the binding of the GATA4/FOG2 complex at E11.5 (Fig 6C). As a negative control we have randomly chosen an internal region (within the Lhx9 gene) that contains two perfect GATA elements (AGATAG; Fig 6A and Additional File 3B). This region tested negative in the GATA and FOG2 ChIP assays (Fig 6D); we have also confirmed the specificity for primers and the FOG2 antibody by a quantitative PCR analysis of ChIP reactions (Fig 6D). We conclude that in the E11.5 heart GATA4/FOG2 complex directly binds Lhx9 cis-regulatory regions.

GATA4 and FOG2 cooperate to inhibit Lhx9 promoters
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 65 70 65 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 189 194 189 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>

###xml 507 510 507 510 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ki </sup>
###xml 604 606 604 606 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 662 667 662 667 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 727 730 727 730 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ki </sup>
###xml 798 803 798 803 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6E, F</xref>
###xml 938 940 938 940 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6E</xref>
###xml 978 983 978 983 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
To confirm that the GATA-harboring DNA sequences upstream of the Lhx9 transcriptional start sites are essential for GATA4/FOG2-dependent regulation we performed luciferase reporter assays. Lhx9 regulatory sequences containing ECRs with ChIPed GATA sites (-4968/-4301 and -1121/+1; Fig 6A) were isolated and used to generate luciferase reporter constructs. The reporter constructs were transiently transfected into the 293 HEK cells along with the GATA4 and FOG2 expression vectors. We also generated a GATA4ki expression vector (encoding for the V217G mutation in GATA4 that does not interact with FOG2 [15]). The wild-type GATA4 cooperates with FOG2 to repress Lhx9 promoter-driven luciferase expression while the mutant GATA4ki version of GATA4 is severely impaired in this repression assay (Fig 6E, F). A luciferase construct containing a GAAA mutation in the (-521) GATA element was not co-repressed by a joint GATA4/FOG2 action (Fig 6E). We conclude that the repression of Lhx9 gene expression in the developing heart requires the presence of the functional GATA4/FOG2 protein complex.

###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 83 98 83 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 355 357 334 336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 526 528 501 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 529 531 504 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 550 552 525 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 936 937 892 893 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>
###xml 105 111 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
We established that the loss of GATA4/FOG2 interaction leads to over-expression of Lhx9alpha/beta in the murine heart. One of the consequences of the excessive LHX9alpha/beta expression could be the 'trapping' of cardiac LHX factors with their interacting proteins into non-functional LHX9 alpha/beta-containing complexes as has been previously proposed [30]. To further test this idea we probed the interactions between LHX9alpha and cardiac proteins that have been reported to interact with the LIM-HD factors, namely ISL1 [48,49] and CITED2/MRG1 [50]. The eukariotic expression vector encoding the epitope-tagged version of LHX9alpha was co-transfected with the epitope-tagged ISL1alpha, ISL1beta or CITED2 into HEK293 cells and protein interactions were tested by immunoprecipitation-western (IP-Western) analysis. We detected the interactions between LHX9alpha and both iso-forms of ISl1, but not between LHX9alpha and CITED2 (Fig 7). This demonstrates the ability of the LHX9 isoform lacking HD to form a complex with other LIM-HD factors, such as ISL1.

###xml 1034 1036 1009 1011 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 1165 1166 1140 1141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
###xml 1294 1295 1269 1270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>

###xml 157 162 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 375 379 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Previous lineage studies in chick embryos have demonstrated that the smooth muscle of the coronary vessels derives from the proepicardium; similarly, in the mouse these cells are derived from the septum transversum. LHX9alpha/beta is prominently expressed in the STM at E9.5 and in the epicardium starting at E11.5 (Figs 3 and 5). Recent lineage tracing analysis in Isl1-Cre mice has demonstrated that the progeny of Isl1-expressing cells contributes to endothelial and vascular smooth muscle lineages, specifically the smooth muscle of coronary vasculature [51]. As our IP-Western analysis showed that LHX9alpha can interact with ISL1 (Fig 7) we sought to determine whether LHX2/LHX9alpha/beta and ISL1 were co-expressed in the epicardium or septum transversum and performed double-fluorescence labeling experiments in E10.5 embryos. We were unable to co-localize ISL1 to LHX9 alpha/beta-positive epicardial cells (Fig 8, top panels; arrows); this data is in agreement with the reported absence of ISL1 expression in the epicardium [51]. Similarly, cells in the septum transversum that stained positive with an anti-LHX2/LHX9 antibody were also ISL1-negative (Fig 8, top panels; arrowheads); we, however, detected a subset of cells that co-express ISL1 and LHX2/9 in the developing liver (Fig 8, bottom panels; arrowheads). Expression of Lhx9 in the developing liver has been previously reported [27].

###begin title 48
Discussion
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 182 184 182 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 185 187 185 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 285 290 285 290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 366 371 366 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 582 587 575 580 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 907 909 900 902 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
Recent evidence suggests that different splicing isoforms of the same transcription factor may have competing/opposing as well as unrelated roles in cellular differentiation (e.g., [52-54]). We have demonstrated that GATA4/FOG2 transcription complex is essential for the repression of Lhx9 gene transcription in cardiac development; we have also determined that the Lhx9 isoform encoding for the protein with intact homeodomain is not present in the embryonic heart, while both alpha and beta isoforms encoding a truncated homeodomain are expressed. This differential expression of Lhx9 isoforms suggests that truncated LHX9 proteins have a separate function independent of the full-length LHX9 molecule. This assertion is supported by the previously reported observation that LHX9 isoforms do not directly compete with each other and instead function in different pathways during neuronal differentiation [55].
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 251 255 251 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2</italic>
###xml 265 270 265 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gata4</italic>
###xml 270 276 270 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ki/ki </italic>
###xml 270 276 270 276 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>ki/ki </italic></sup>
###xml 313 328 313 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 404 419 397 405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 519 520 505 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 544 548 530 534 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2</italic>
###xml 577 592 563 571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 692 697 671 676 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 709 710 688 689 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 755 760 734 739 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 807 812 786 791 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gata4</italic>
###xml 812 818 791 797 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ki/ki </italic>
###xml 812 818 791 797 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>ki/ki </italic></sup>
###xml 826 828 805 807 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 895 910 874 882 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 1007 1012 979 984 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gata4</italic>
###xml 1012 1015 984 987 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ki </italic>
###xml 1012 1015 984 987 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>ki </italic></sup>
###xml 1072 1077 1044 1049 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gata </italic>
###xml 1092 1097 1064 1069 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gata6</italic>
One of the objectives of this study was to identify the downstream targets of GATA4/FOG2 regulation in various sub-compartments of the developing heart and to understand how the mis-regulation of these targets contributes to severe cardiac defects in Fog2-null and Gata4ki/ki embryos. We have now determined that Lhx9alpha/beta is a target of GATA4/FOG2 repression, with wild-type hearts down-regulating Lhx9alpha/beta epicardial expression starting at least on E11.5, while hearts deficient in FOG2 fail to do so (Fig 3). The inability of the Fog2-null heart to down-regulate Lhx9alpha/beta expression may be a contributing factor in the constellation of the cardiac abnormalities caused by Fog2 deficiency [2]. Although this study mostly focused on the Fog2 mutants, real-time PCR analysis with the E12.5 Gata4ki/ki hearts [15] also revealed a significant, albeit weaker, up-regulation of the Lhx9alpha/beta gene expression in this mutant (~3 times; data not shown). This more modest up-regulation in the Gata4ki mutant could be due to partial compensation by the other Gata family member, Gata6, that is expressed in the developing heart.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 31 46 31 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 101 102 94 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 173 184 166 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 414 419 400 405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 478 482 464 468 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2</italic>
###xml 507 508 493 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 552 557 538 543 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 758 763 744 749 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 779 780 765 766 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
We also demonstrated here that Lhx9alpha/beta expression is initiated in the septum transversum (Fig 5) and that these are the epicardial cells that continue expressing LHX9alpha/beta in E11.5 hearts and downregulate this expression shortly thereafter. Further experiments will confirm whether LHX9-positive epicardial cells are direct descendants of the septum transversum cells. The requirement to down-regulate Lhx9 expression is no longer satisfied in the epicardium of the Fog2-null mutant hearts (Fig 3). We propose that abnormally high level of Lhx9 gene expression that is characteristic of an earlier stage in epicardial cell development is likely to be a contributing factor in the impaired differentiation of the epicardially derived cells in the Fog2 mutant embryos [2].
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 105 110 105 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 467 472 467 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 686 691 686 691 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6E, F</xref>
###xml 775 778 775 778 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anf</italic>
###xml 780 784 780 784 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bnp </italic>
###xml 794 798 790 794 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mhc </italic>
###xml 940 945 936 941 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 1149 1154 1145 1150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 1279 1281 1275 1277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 1282 1284 1278 1280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 1285 1287 1281 1283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 1321 1326 1317 1322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 1407 1412 1403 1408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 1643 1648 1639 1644 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 1663 1668 1659 1664 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 1743 1747 1739 1743 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9</italic>
###xml 1932 1937 1928 1933 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 2000 2005 1996 2001 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) confirmed the roles of the two evolutionary conserved GATA sites in Lhx9 gene cis-regulatory elements. Since DNA complexes were precipitating with either anti-GATA4 or anti-FOG2 antibodies, this assay demonstrated that in the E11.5 heart these sites are occupied by the GATA4/FOG2 complex rather than GATA4 alone. We could no longer detect GATA4/FOG2 complex binding to these sites in the E14.5 hearts (not shown) suggesting that Lhx9 repression at this later stage of cardiac development is likely to be GATA4/FOG2-independent. In agreement with the ChiP data, luciferase reporter assays confirmed the cooperative repression by GATA4 and FOG2 (Fig 6E, F). Although GATA sites have been previously identified in the proximal promoters of Anf, Bnp and alpha-Mhc genes, these genes' expression is not affected by the GATA4/FOG2 interaction loss. It is possible that other transcription factors regulating Lhx9 expression ensure the selectivity of GATA4/FOG2 binding; however, computer analysis of ECRs harboring GATA sites did not reveal sequence conservation for any other transcription factors in these regions. Lhx9 gene expression is restricted to a limited number of tissues during development and is likely to be tightly regulated (e.g. [26,27,31]). Transcriptional regulation of Lhx9 is not well understood and the crosstalk between the (yet unknown) activators of Lhx9 expression and its repressors, GATA4/FOG2, remains to be elucidated. In this respect we also cannot exclude the contribution of an indirect regulation mechanism where GATA4/FOG2 would normally activate a yet unknown repressor X of Lhx9 transcription; Fog2 loss in this case will result in down-regulation of X and de-repression of Lhx9. In summary, although our ChIPs as well as transient transfection data argues in favor of a direct repression mechanism, it is possible that, once other trans-acting factors governing Lhx9 regulation are uncovered, they will be also deregulated in the Fog2 knockout.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 55 60 55 60 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 94 103 94 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;Mhc </italic>
###xml 146 150 142 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2</italic>
###xml 207 212 203 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 227 228 223 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 229 231 225 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 270 275 266 271 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 338 356 334 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;Mhc-Fog2/Fog2</italic>
###xml 356 359 348 351 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/-</sup>
###xml 372 374 364 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5B</xref>
###xml 425 430 417 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 464 479 456 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 540 545 525 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 632 650 617 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;Mhc-Fog2/Fog2</italic>
###xml 650 654 631 635 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 712 717 693 698 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 731 749 712 726 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#945;Mhc-Fog2/Fog2</italic>
###xml 749 753 726 730 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 832 842 809 819 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9/LHX9 </italic>
We have previously shown that transgenic expression of Fog2 restricted to the myocardium with alphaMhc promoter extends the life of the otherwise Fog2-null embryos; however this rescue by myocardial-derived Fog2 is incomplete [2,46]. In this respect, increased level of Lhx9 expression still persists in the E14.5 hearts of the 'rescued' alphaMhc-Fog2/Fog2-/-mutants (Fig 5B) indirectly attesting to the fact that myocardial Fog2 is not responsible for regulating Lhx9alpha/beta expression. It is likely that the inability to down-regulate Lhx9 in the epicardium is, at least partially, responsible for the incomplete rescue in the alphaMhc-Fog2/Fog2-/- mutants. Importantly, in a sensitive qRT-PCR the level of Lhx9 expression in alphaMhc-Fog2/Fog2-/- hearts was slightly reduced (not shown) suggesting that a low level myocardial Lhx9/LHX9 expression is present and controlled by GATA4/FOG2.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 86 88 86 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 106 111 106 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 158 163 158 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 266 268 266 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 371 376 371 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 451 452 451 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 500 505 500 505 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
Loss of GATA4/FOG2 interaction has a profound early effect on testis differentiation [46]; similarly, the Lhx9 gene is expressed in the developing gonads and Lhx9 gene targeting that removes two exons encoding for the LIM domains results in an early gonadal defect [29]. However, in contrast to the situation in cardiac development, we do not observe an up-regulation of Lhx9 gene expression in the gonads of GATA4/FOG2 mutants (e.g., Additional File 2) suggesting that GATA4 and FOG2 do not control Lhx9 in this tissue.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 240 245 240 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx6 </italic>
###xml 277 281 277 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx </italic>
###xml 358 372 358 365 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946;</italic>
###xml 396 401 389 394 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx6 </italic>
###xml 437 439 430 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
Currently, the function of LIM-HD factors in cardiac development (with the notable exception of ISL1) is not well understood. Computer analysis of gene expression database (as well as our own microarray analysis, data not shown) shows that Lhx6 is the only other member of the Lhx family that is expressed in the developing heart in the amount comparable to Lhx9alpha/beta; no cardiac defect for Lhx6 loss of function has been reported [56].
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 60 65 60 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 173 178 173 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 384 389 384 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Myf5 </italic>
###xml 439 441 439 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 466 476 466 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945; </italic>
###xml 628 630 624 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 633 638 629 634 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ldb1 </italic>
###xml 684 686 680 682 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 774 776 770 772 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 777 779 773 775 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 795 797 791 793 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 988 990 984 986 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
###xml 991 993 987 989 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
###xml 1346 1347 1321 1322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>
###xml 1645 1646 1606 1607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
Our data confirms and extends the previous observation that Lhx9 splicing isoforms are expressed in a tissue-specific manner [30]. We also demonstrated that these truncated Lhx9 transcripts lead to the expression of the protein; this is an important confirmation since an appearance of an RNA encoding for a regulatory factor does not always correlate with protein accumulation (e.g. Myf5 RNA and not the protein are expressed in neurons [57]). It was proposed that Lhx9alpha encodes for a protein with a truncated HD that can compete with LHX9 (or other LIM-HD factors) for limited amounts of nuclear CLIM cofactors like Ldb1 [55]; Ldb1 knockout animals do not develop heart anlage [58]. CLIM cofactors can dimerize and interact with two LIM-HD proteins at the same time ([48,59]; reviewed in [60]). In addition to the simple sequestration of Ldb1, the adjustment of stoichiometry between various CLIM-LHX complexes can involve differential degradation of some, but not other complexes [61,62]. Finally, one of the consequences of the excessive LHX9alpha/beta-Ldb1 complex formation could be 'trapping' of cardiac LHX factors with their interacting proteins into non-functional LHX9 alpha/beta-containing complexes. Our IP-Western analysis demonstrated that both LHX9alpha and LHXbeta could interact with other LIM-HD proteins such as ISL1 (Fig 7), suggesting that LHX9 alpha/beta could function in mammalian cells as part of the multi-protein complexes. The ISL1 protein, however, is unlikely to serve as a partner for LHX9alpha/beta in the epicardium or septum transversum as cells expressing LHX2/9 in these tissues do not express ISL1 (Fig 8).
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 171 175 164 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx </italic>
###xml 260 264 253 257 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9</italic>
###xml 279 281 272 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 334 339 327 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 428 433 421 426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 518 533 511 519 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LHX9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 663 668 649 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gata4</italic>
###xml 668 674 654 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ki/ki </italic>
###xml 668 674 654 660 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>ki/ki </italic></sup>
###xml 678 683 664 669 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
In summary, our data suggest that the function of the LHX9alpha/beta protein during the proepicardial development should be evaluated. This function maybe masked by other Lhx family members or by unrelated compensatory mechanisms and hence not revealed in the Lhx9-null animals [29]. We also provide evidence that GATA4/FOG2 regulate Lhx9 gene expression directly through binding to the evolutionary conserved GATA sites in the Lhx9 regulatory regions. The loss of GATA4/FOG2 interaction leads to de-repression of the LHX9alpha/beta expression in epicardial cells; this abnormally high expression may account for some of the cardiac malformations observed in the Gata4ki/ki and Fog2 null mutants.
###end p 57
###begin title 58
Conclusion
###end title 58
###begin p 59
###xml 178 183 178 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 185 195 185 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945; </italic>
###xml 199 207 195 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#946;</italic>
###xml 240 245 233 238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 329 344 322 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 535 550 521 529 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 632 637 611 616 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Fog2 </italic>
###xml 663 678 642 650 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946; </italic>
###xml 812 817 784 789 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 936 941 908 913 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9 </italic>
###xml 1013 1016 985 988 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ki </italic>
###xml 1013 1016 985 988 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"><italic>ki </italic></sup>
###xml 1166 1180 1138 1145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lhx9&#945;/&#946;</italic>
###xml 125 130 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 613 618 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
LHX9 belongs to the family of the LIM-HD (LIM-homeodomain) transcription factors. We have now determined that the developing mouse heart normally expresses truncated isoforms of Lhx9 - Lhx9alpha and Lhx9beta. Whereas, the expression of the Lhx9 isoform that encodes a protein with an intact homeodomain is extremely low. At E9.5 Lhx9alpha/beta expression is prominent in the epicardial primordium, septum transversum; in the E11.5 heart LHX9-positive cells are localized to the epicardial mesothelium. Thereafter in the control hearts Lhx9alpha/beta epicardial expression is promptly down-regulated; in contrast, mouse mutants with Fog2 gene loss fail to repress Lhx9alpha/beta expression. Chromatin immunoprecipitation from the E11.5 hearts established the roles of the two evolutionary conserved GATA sites in Lhx9 gene cis-regulatory elements. In transient transfection studies the expression driven by the cis-regulatory regions of Lhx9 was repressed by FOG2 in the presence of intact GATA4, but not the GATA4ki mutant that is impaired in its ability to bind FOG2. This study identifies the first direct target for the GATA4/FOG2 repressor complex in the heart, Lhx9alpha/beta.
