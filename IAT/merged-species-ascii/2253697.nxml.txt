###begin article-title 0
Dynamic regulation of PGC-1alpha localization and turnover implicates mitochondrial adaptation in calorie restriction and the stress response
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Re-use of this article is permitted in accordance with the Creative Commons Deed, Attribution 2.5, which does not permit commercial exploitation.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 857 864 822 829 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 868 872 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
There is increasing evidence that longevity and stress resistance are connected, but the mechanism is unclear. We report that mitochondria are regulated in response to oxidative stress and calorie restriction through a shared mechanism involving peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-gamma co-activator 1alpha (PGC-1alpha). We demonstrate that PGC-1alpha subcellular distribution is regulated, and its transcriptional activity is promoted through SIRT1-dependent nuclear accumulation. In addition, the duration of PGC-1alpha activity is regulated by glycogen synthase kinase beta (GSK3beta), which targets PGC-1alpha for intranuclear proteasomal degradation. This mechanism of regulation permits the rapidity and persistence of PGC-1alpha activation to be independently controlled. We provide evidence that this pathway of PGC-1alpha regulation occurs in vivo in mice, both in the oxidative stress response and with calorie restriction. Our data show how mitochondrial function may be adapted in response to external stimuli, and support the concept that such adaptation is critically involved in cellular survival and in lifespan extension by calorie restriction.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 950 955 950 955 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 946 962 946 962 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b25">Lee <italic>et al</italic>., 1999</xref>

###xml 1174 1179 1174 1179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1167 1186 1167 1186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b17">Hepple <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
###xml 1194 1199 1194 1199 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1188 1206 1188 1206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6">Baker <italic>et al</italic>., 2006</xref>
###xml 1215 1220 1215 1220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1208 1227 1208 1227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14">Faulks <italic>et al</italic>., 2006</xref>
###xml 1552 1573 1552 1573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b20">Horbinski &amp; Chu, 2005</xref>
###xml 1575 1590 1575 1590 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b21">Jazwinski, 2005</xref>
###xml 1592 1614 1592 1614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b38">Schieke &amp; Finkel, 2006</xref>
###xml 1984 2010 1984 2010 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2">Anderson &amp; Weindruch, 2007</xref>
Cellular response to stress generally reflects a balance between cell survival and death. Stress resistance is a measure of the cell's ability to survive under conditions that are detrimental. Manipulations that extend lifespan often increase stress resistance at the cellular level, and a number of factors that play a role in the stress response have also been implicated in longevity. It is unclear if regulation of metabolism is a feature of cellular survival or how the metabolic state of the cell influences stress resistance. Mitochondria are the key organelle in substrate utilization and energy production. Transcriptional profiling studies demonstrate that genes involved in mitochondrial energy metabolism are coordinately up-regulated in multiple tissues with calorie restriction (CR), suggesting a change in dynamic of the electron transport system and a role for this alteration in mitochondrial metabolism in the mechanisms of CR (Lee et al., 1999, 2002). Biochemical analysis suggests that mitochondria from restricted tissues are functionally different from their control counterparts in terms of metabolism and composition (Bevilacqua et al., 2005; Hepple et al., 2005; Baker et al., 2006; Faulks et al., 2006). The factors involved in exerting these CR-dependent changes in mitochondrial function are unknown. Identification of these factors would provide insight into the mechanism of mitochondrial regulation in response to CR. Our understanding of the complexity of signalling pathways to and from the mitochondria is increasing (Horbinski & Chu, 2005; Jazwinski, 2005; Schieke & Finkel, 2006), describing a network through which mitochondria may communicate functional status to the nucleus to impact cellular function. Metabolic reprogramming by CR may be central to the mechanism of lifespan extension, where changes in mitochondrial function confer an energetic shift that is conducive to increased cellular fitness, resulting in the promotion of longevity (Anderson & Weindruch, 2007).

###xml 1029 1045 981 997 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b29">Lin <italic>et al</italic>., 2004</xref>
###xml 1053 1058 1005 1010 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1047 1065 999 1017 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b27">Leone <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
###xml 1309 1314 1253 1258 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1302 1321 1246 1265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b34">Nisoli <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
###xml 1403 1408 1347 1352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1391 1415 1335 1359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b31">Lopez-Lluch <italic>et al</italic>., 2006</xref>
###xml 875 879 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The transcriptional co-activator peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-gamma (PPAR-gamma) co-activator 1alpha (PGC-1alpha) plays a multifaceted role in the regulation of metabolism (Puigserver & Spiegelman, 2003). PGC-1alpha regulates mitochondrial energy metabolism and biogenesis (Finck & Kelly, 2006), and influences carbohydrate and lipid utilization through co-activation of members of nuclear receptor family [e.g. peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-alpha (PPAR-alpha), PPAR-gamma, estrogen related receptor alpha (ERRalpha)] (Corton & Brown-Borg, 2005). PGC-1alpha is involved in skeletal muscle fibre type switching, responding to changes in oxidative demand in skeletal muscle (Lin et al., 2002), and is also induced with exercise (Baar et al., 2002), indicating that it is responsive to conditions of increased oxygen utilization. PGC-1alpha-deficient mice have defects in adaptive metabolism in that the metabolic response to hormonal stimuli, cold or the fasting state is dysfunctional in these animals (Lin et al., 2004; Leone et al., 2005). These data suggest that PGC-1alpha is a key component in coordinating the animal's metabolic response to external stimuli including nutritional status. Consistent with this, CR increases levels of PGC-1alpha mRNA in multiple tissues (Nisoli et al., 2005) and is induced in cells treated with serum from restricted animals (Lopez-Lluch et al., 2006), raising the possibility that PGC-1alpha is directly responsible for the mitochondrial changes induced by CR. We sought to identify factors involved in regulation of mitochondrial function, and determine if these factors could be connected with cellular survival or longevity pathways.

Results
###end title 6
###begin title 7
Effect of PGC-1alpha on mitochondrial function
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 191 198 183 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Fig. 1A</xref>
###xml 243 248 235 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>

###xml 1553 1575 1529 1551 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b40">St-Pierre <italic>et al</italic>., 2006</xref>
###xml 1858 1865 1826 1833 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Fig. 1D</xref>
###xml 1919 1926 1887 1894 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Fig. 1E</xref>
###xml 2008 2015 1976 1983 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Fig. 1F</xref>
###xml 172 177 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We first examined PGC-1alpha function and regulation using a cell culture model. As shown, extra copies of PGC-1alpha conferred increased resistance to oxidative stress in mouse fibroblasts (Fig. 1A), in agreement with previous studies (Valle et al., 2005; St-Pierre et al., 2006). In addition, over-expression of PGC-1alpha causes an increase in mitochondrial membrane potential (Valle et al., 2005). If the increase in stress resistance of the PGC-1alpha over-expressing cells were because of the difference in mitochondrial membrane potential, we would expect regulation of mitochondrial membrane potential to be a component of the stress response. As shown, live cell staining with MitoTraker Red indicated that mitochondrial membrane potential was elevated within 1 h following exposure to low-intensity oxidative stress (Fig. 1B). The increase in mitochondrial membrane potential with hydrogen peroxide treatment was confirmed using JC1 staining. JC1 aggregates fluoresce at a higher wavelength than free JC1 molecules; because JC1 accumulates in proportion to the membrane potential, the ratio of the higher (Fl2) to lower (Fl1) wavelength is an indicator of membrane potential. In agreement with the increase in membrane potential, there was an increase in protein levels of COX IV, a subunit of the mitochondrial electron transport system and a target of PGC-1alpha transcriptional activity (Fig. 1C). Although the free radical scavengers MnSOD and catalase are known to be induced by PGC-1alpha 4 h following treatment with hydrogen peroxide (St-Pierre et al., 2006), levels of both proteins were unaltered in this 1 h time period. This result suggests that the effect of PGC-1alpha in promoting survival in the early response to oxidative stress is not because of the induction of the antioxidant defence. Knock down of PGC-1alpha levels by RNAi (Fig. 1D) increased cellular sensitivity to oxidative stress (Fig. 1E) and prevented the stress-mediated increase in mitochondrial membrane potential (Fig. 1F).

###begin title 10
Changes in intracellular PGC-1alpha distribution and acetylation status
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 250 257 234 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Fig. 1G</xref>
###xml 308 315 292 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Fig. 1H</xref>
To determine the cellular localization of PGC-1alpha, we used a strain over-expressing GFP-tagged PGC-1alpha. Antibodies against GFP or the N-terminal region of PGC-1alpha reveal that PGC-1alpha is localized both in the cytoplasm and in the nucleus (Fig. 1G). These data are confirmed by immunofluorescence (Fig. 1H). The observed difference in nuclear distribution as seen by subcellular fractionation is possibly because of over-expression of GFP-tagged PGC-1alpha. By immunofluorescence, both antibodies detect nuclear accumulation of PGC-1alpha following exposure to oxidative stress. These data suggest that PGC-1alpha subcellular distribution is shifted to the nucleus in the early response to oxidative stress where it regulates mitochondrial function.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 146 167 146 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b30">Longo &amp; Kennedy, 2006</xref>
###xml 280 285 276 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 273 292 269 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b33">Nemoto <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
###xml 302 307 298 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>

###xml 321 326 317 322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 316 333 312 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b7">Baur <italic>et al</italic>., 2006</xref>
###xml 343 348 339 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 335 355 331 351 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b23">Lagouge <italic>et al</italic>., 2006</xref>
###xml 559 564 551 556 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 549 571 541 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9">Bitterman <italic>et al</italic>., 2002</xref>
###xml 580 585 572 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 573 592 565 584 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b4">Avalos <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
###xml 909 916 897 904 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig02">Fig. 2A</xref>
SIRT1 NAD-dependent deacetylase is a member of the sirtuin family that has previously been shown to play a role in the oxidative stress response (Longo & Kennedy, 2006). SIRT1 functionally interacts with and deacetylates PGC-1alpha, promoting its transcriptional activity (Nemoto et al., 2005; Rodgers et al., 2005; Baur et al., 2006; Lagouge et al., 2006). To test if SIRT1 is also regulating PGC-1alpha activity in response to stress, cells were exposed to hydrogen peroxide in the absence or presence of nicotinamide, a potent sirtuin inhibitor (Bitterman et al., 2002; Avalos et al., 2005). Nicotinamide permits the immediate inhibition of SIRT1 activity and avoids the necessity to culture cells in the absence of SIRT1. As shown, stress resistance was impaired in wild-type cells in the presence of nicotinamide, but in cells over-expressing PGC-1alpha, the inhibitory effect was significantly reduced (Fig. 2A). These data place PGC-1alpha downstream of SIRT1 in the stress response because elevated levels of PGC-1alpha can compensate for the negative effect of nicotinamide on cell survival.

###begin p 13
###xml 137 138 133 134 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>

###begin p 14
###xml 346 353 334 341 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig02">Fig. 2B</xref>
###xml 613 620 593 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig02">Fig. 2C</xref>
###xml 669 676 649 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig02">Fig. 2D</xref>

###xml 1301 1308 1265 1272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig02">Fig. 2F</xref>
Prompted by these findings, we set out to explore the immediate response of PGC-1alpha to a low-intensity stress in cells that are not genetically manipulated. Under normal conditions, PGC-1alpha was detected both in the nucleus and the cytoplasm. Following oxidative stress, SIRT1 and PGC-1alpha accumulated in the nucleus and were colocalized (Fig. 2B). This distribution pattern was transient and, 1 h after exposure to stress, PGC-1alpha localization returned to that of untreated cells. Subcellular fractionation revealed that in the presence of nicotinamide, PGC-1alpha failed to accumulate in the nucleus (Fig. 2C), and this was confirmed by immunofluorescence (Fig. 2D). Under these conditions, the stress-induced elevation of mitochondrial membrane potential was impaired (Fig. 2E). These data indicate that SIRT1 regulates a PGC-1alpha-dependent increase in mitochondrial membrane potential in response to stress, and that regulation of mitochondrial function is critical to survival. To test if SIRT1 regulation of PGC-1alpha was a direct or an indirect effect, we probed for acetylation of PGC-1alpha in immunoprecipitates from cells exposed to hydrogen peroxide. As shown, PGC-1alpha acetylation was increased within 15 min of exposure to stress, with subsequent deacetylation by 45 min (Fig. 2F). In the presence of nicotinamide, PGC-1alpha was not deacetylated. The timing of modification coincides with PGC-1alpha accumulating in the nucleus, and when deactylation is prevented PGC-1alpha fails to localize appropriately. This suggests that the deacetylation of PGC-1alpha by SIRT1 is required to sequester it in the nucleus. These data demonstrate that PGC-1alpha activity is regulated by altering subcellular localization in response to stress in cells in culture.

###begin title 15
Nuclear degradation of PGC-1alpha
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 764 771 736 743 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3A</xref>
###xml 1095 1102 1063 1070 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3B</xref>
###xml 1110 1115 1078 1083 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1105 1122 1073 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b37">Sano <italic>et al</italic>., 2007</xref>
The transient nature of PGC-1alpha accumulation in the nucleus led us to ask how stress subsequently resets PGC-1alpha distribution to the cytoplasm. There are two most likely explanations: (i) PGC-1alpha is sequestered in the nucleus and, following transcriptional activation of target genes, is released back to the cytoplasm; and (ii) PGC-1alpha is sequestered in the nucleus but now, following transcriptional activation, the nuclear pool is degraded, and the cytoplasmic pool is replenished by transcriptional activation of the PGC-1alpha gene. To test this, we looked for evidence of nuclear degradation of PGC-1alpha. Subcellular fractionation revealed lower molecular weight forms and reduced levels of PGC-1alpha in the nuclear pool 1 h following stress (Fig. 3A). One of the principal mechanisms for specific depletion of proteins is targeted degradation by the proteasome. In agreement with a recent study, PGC-1alpha levels are increased in the presence of proteasomal inhibitors PSI and lactacystin, indicating that it is a target of the proteasome under normal culture conditions (Fig. 3B) (Sano et al., 2007).
###end p 16

###begin p 18
###xml 227 234 219 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3C</xref>

In the presence of proteasomal inhibitor with subsequent exposure to hydrogen peroxide (45 min), immunofluorescent staining for PGC-1alpha was further elevated, and bright intranuclear foci containing PGC-1alpha were detected (Fig. 3C). The process of targeting proteins for degradation by the proteasome involves poly-ubiquitination of the target by specific ligases. Western blot detects high-molecular-weight bands in PGC-1alpha lysates following exposure of cells to hydrogen peroxide (45 min) (Fig. 3D). These data indicate that PGC-1alpha is a substrate for degradation by the proteasome, and that turnover of PGC-1alpha protein is enhanced under oxidative stress conditions.

###begin title 19
Nuclear degradation of PGC-1alpha in response to stress requires GSK3beta-dependent phosphorylation
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 346 351 340 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 339 358 333 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b13">Essers <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
###xml 458 463 446 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 451 470 439 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1">Aberle <italic>et al</italic>., 1997</xref>
###xml 695 702 669 676 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3C</xref>
###xml 822 829 793 800 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3E</xref>
###xml 905 910 873 878 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 894 917 862 885 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b41">Sutherland <italic>et al</italic>., 1993</xref>
###xml 1083 1090 1048 1055 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3F</xref>
Prior to ubiquitination and degradation, the target protein is usually 'tagged' by phosphorylation allowing it to be recognized by the ubiquitination apparatus. GSK3beta has been previously associated with the stress response where it regulates levels of beta-catenin, and as a result, activity of the forkhead transcription factor Foxo4 (Essers et al., 2005). Phosphorylation of beta-catenin by GSK3beta targets it for degradation by the proteasome (Aberle et al., 1997). We asked if GSK3beta might be also involved in regulating PGC-1alpha stability. In the presence of GSK3beta inhibitor and after 45 min of exposure to hydrogen peroxide, PGC-1alpha immunofluorescent staining was increased (Fig. 3C). Furthermore, cellular survival in response to oxidative stress was diminished in the presence of GSK3beta inhibitor (Fig. 3E). GSK3beta activity is regulated by inhibitory phosphorylation (Sutherland et al., 1993). Western analysis reveals that levels of GSK3beta phosphorylation were reduced within 15 min of exposure to oxidative stress, indicating that it becomes activated (Fig. 3F).
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 281 288 264 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig04">Fig. 4A</xref>
###xml 568 575 536 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig04">Fig. 4B</xref>
To confirm that GSK3beta plays a role in PGC-1alpha processing, we generated cells with reduced levels of GSK3beta using siRNA. In the absence of any other treatment, PGC-1alpha staining is elevated 24 h after knockdown of GSK3beta, and the increase is most evident in the nuclei (Fig. 4A). To test whether GSK3beta might be directly involved in PGC-1alpha processing, we examined PGC-1alpha protein levels by Western blot. Within 15 min of exposure to hydrogen peroxide, a shift in PGC-1alpha protein migration that is characteristic of phosphorylation was observed (Fig. 4B). In the presence of GSK3beta inhibitor, this shift is no longer observed, indicating that PGC-1alpha may be a direct target of GSK3beta.
###end p 21

###begin p 23
###xml 333 340 320 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig04">Fig. 4C</xref>
###xml 643 650 616 623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig04">Fig. 4D</xref>
###xml 1069 1076 1024 1031 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig04">Fig. 4E</xref>
###xml 1146 1151 1098 1103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1141 1158 1093 1110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b16">Fiol <italic>et al</italic>., 1987</xref>
To investigate this further, we conducted immunoprecipitaion experiments using extracts from cells grown in the absence or presence of GSK3beta inhibitor and treated with hydrogen peroxide for 15 min. Immunoprecipitation of GSK3beta coprecipitated PGC-1alpha, and this association was impaired in the presence of GSK3beta inhibitor (Fig. 4C). These data indicate that PGC-1alpha and GSK3beta physically associate during the stress response in a manner that is dependent on GSK3beta activity. Immunoprecipitation using phospho-specific antibodies indicated that PGC-1alpha is phosphorylated at serine and threonine residues in this time frame (Fig. 4D). In the presence of GSK3beta inhibitor, PGC-1alpha is co-immunoprecipitated using phosphoserine-specific antibodies, but not when using phosphothreonine-specific antibodies. In reciprocal experiments, a band was detected in PGC-1alpha immunoprecipitates using anti-phosphoserine- or anti-phosphothreonine-specific antibodies, but in the presence of GSK3beta inhibitor anti-phosphothreonine did not detect PGC-1alpha (Fig. 4E). The consensus site for GSK3beta phosphorylation is S/TxxxS/T (Fiol et al., 1987); phosphorylation of the C-terminal residue of the consensus by a priming kinase is a requisite for GSK3beta-dependent phosphorylation at the N-terminal residue. These data indicate that serine phosphorylation of PGC-1alpha is at least in part independent of GSK3beta, and threonine phosphorylation is GSK3beta dependent.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 99 106 95 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig04">Fig. 4F</xref>
Based on these data, we propose a model for PGC-1alpha regulation in the early response to stress (Fig. 4F). A key component of our model is its dependence on initial conditions prior to PGC-1alpha transcriptional activity. Upon exposure of the cells to hydrogen peroxide, GSK3beta is activated and phosphorylates PGC-1alpha. As a result, PGC-1alpha is targeted for intranuclear proteasomal degradation. Now, when PGC-1alpha is deacetylated by SIRT1 and sequestered in the nucleus, a sustained effect on expression of target genes involved in the mitochondrial electron transport system is prevented. Overall levels of PGC-1alpha are not altered within this time frame as degradation of the nuclear pool is offset by increased transcription of PGC-1alpha gene.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vivo</italic>
In vivo studies
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 71 78 67 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 241 248 237 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Fig. 5A</xref>
###xml 644 651 629 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Fig. 5B</xref>
###xml 1152 1159 1116 1123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Fig. 5C</xref>
###xml 82 86 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 88 92 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 301 306 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We next looked for evidence of PGC-1alpha regulation by this mechanism in vivo in mice. Mice were exposed to the oxidative stressor paraquat, and tissue was harvested and processed for subcellular fractionation at the indicated time points (Fig. 5A). PGC-1alpha accumulated in the nuclear fraction in mouse skeletal muscle following exposure to paraquat. Densitometric analysis revealed that cellular redistribution of PGC-1alpha was biphasic, with an initial increase in nuclear accumulation at 1 h and a second further increase at 5 h post-treatment. Western blot analysis demonstrated that GSK3beta levels were not changed following stress (Fig. 5B). However, after 1 h of treatment, phospho-GSK3beta levels were reduced, indicating activation of GSK3beta. After 5 h, levels of phospho-GSK3beta were increased beyond basal levels observed in the untreated animals. SIRT1 levels were increased at 5 h following treatment. Consistent with our proposed model, the biphasic response of PGC-1alpha regulators was reflected in the pattern of accumulation of PGC-1alpha in the nucleus, and in the levels of COX IV and UCP3 which are targets of PGC-1alpha (Fig. 5C).
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 107 114 100 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>

###begin p 28
###xml 293 300 282 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Fig. 5D</xref>
###xml 865 870 829 834 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 858 877 822 841 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b18">Higami <italic>et al</italic>., 2004</xref>
###xml 1176 1181 1140 1145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1167 1188 1131 1152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19">Hirosumi <italic>et al</italic>., 2002</xref>
We next wished to determine if this mechanism of PGC-1alpha regulation was peculiar to the stress response or if these factors identified in the stress response could regulate PGC-1alpha in a more general manner. As shown, GSK3beta and SIRT1 were both regulated by CR in white adipose tissue (Fig. 5D). Protein levels of SIRT1 were increased in adipose tissue from restricted animals compared to controls. At the same time, GSK3beta levels were reduced and the relative level of phosphorylation was increased, indicating that GSK3beta is less active. SIRT1 positively regulates PGC-1alpha localization and transcriptional activity, and inhibition of GSK3beta would stabilize PGC-1alpha against proteasomal degradation, consistent with the observed increase in PGC-1alpha levels and increased expression of multiple PGC-1alpha gene targets in adipose tissue (Higami et al., 2004). We next wished to determine if other factors associated with the stress response in cells in culture also play a role in CR. The stress-activated kinase c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK) has been linked to insulin resistance, and abnormally elevated JNK activity is associated with obesity (Hirosumi et al., 2002). The fact that CR confers increased insulin sensitivity and causes a 70% reduction in fat mass suggested JNK as a potential candidate regulated by CR. As shown, CR negatively regulated JNK activity (but not amount) in adipose tissue, as decreased levels of phosphorylation are indicative of reduced kinase activity. These data demonstrate that CR impacts stress response pathways and suggests that the link between longevity and stress resistance is through a shared mechanism.
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 206 213 198 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Fig. 5E</xref>
The subcellular distribution of PGC-1alpha provides a mechanism for rapidly altering its transcriptional activity, and PGC-1alpha intranuclear stability determines the duration of transcriptional activity (Fig. 5E). In the case of the acute stress response, changes in mitochondrial function occur rapidly. The mechanistic basis of this observation is that stress activates GSK3beta and SIRT1 resulting in PGC-1alpha activation and subsequent degradation. In the case of CR (a chronic stress), induction of SIRT1 and inhibition of GSK3beta activate and stabilize PGC-1alpha, resulting in a sustained increase in expression of genes involved in the mitochondrial energy metabolism.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
Discussion
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 58 63 58 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 52 70 52 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b39">Short <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
###xml 195 200 195 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 190 207 190 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b43">Zahn <italic>et al</italic>., 2006</xref>
###xml 544 551 544 551 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 553 563 553 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10">Chan, 2006</xref>
###xml 44 50 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Mitochondrial function declines with age in humans (Short et al., 2005), and a decline in the expression of components of the electron transport chain is a hallmark of aging across species (Zahn et al., 2006). However, the extent of the contribution of mitochondrial function to the onset of age-related pathologies like diabetes and heart disease is not yet clear. Mitochondrial dysfunction has pleiotropic effects in mammals, and there is evidence to suggest that changes in mitochondrial function can implement large-scale metabolic changes in vivo (Chan, 2006).
###end p 31
###begin p 32
In this study, we have shown that increased copies of PGC-1alpha are sufficient to exert an effect on mitochondrial function through increase in mitochondrial membrane potential, and that PGC-1alpha is required for the stress-induced increase in mitochondrial membrane potential. These findings indicate that PGC-1alpha plays a role in mitochondrial adaptation and that regulation of mitochondria is a component of the stress response. Why the cellular response to oxidative stress alters mitochondrial function is a matter of speculation: the changes in membrane potential could reflect (i) a response to increased energy demand as cellular survival pathways are induced; (ii) a change in flux of endogenous mitochondrial-derived reactive oxygen species (ROS) implemented as a protective mechanism to minimize mitochondrial damage; or (iii) the alteration in mitochondrial function is itself a secondary signal in the response to stress. While studies in isolated mitochondria indicate that increased ROS causes a decrease in membrane potential, the experiments described here differ significantly in that the cells are intact upon exposure to hydrogen peroxide retaining the possibility for extra-mitochondrial signalling and nuclear response.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 337 344 330 337 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 586 596 575 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b12">Denu, 2005</xref>
###xml 923 928 904 909 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 912 935 893 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b32">Michishita <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
We describe a mechanism of PGC-1alpha regulation through post-translation modification by SIRT1 and GSK3beta, that permits transient or sustained effects on mitochondrial function. In addition, we have provided evidence in support of our proposed model in animal studies where we demonstrate that the same regulators are involved in the in vivo stress response and in CR. In exploring the relationship between SIRT1 and PGC-1alpha, we utilized the inhibitors nicotinamide and sirtinol. These inhibitors are not specific to SIRT1, but also influence other members of the sirtuin family (Denu, 2005). Given the established relationship between SIRT1 and PGC-1alpha and the colocalization of these proteins in response to oxidative stress, it is likely that the effect of these inhibitors on PGC-1alpha acetylation is because of inhibition of SIRT1. However, we cannot rule out the possibility that other sirtuins (Michishita et al., 2005) play a role in the stress response and may contribute to the effect of these inhibitors on cellular survival.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 463 468 445 450 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 458 475 440 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b37">Sano <italic>et al</italic>., 2007</xref>
###xml 1007 1013 973 979 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Fig. 1</xref>
We have shown that PGC-1alpha stability is regulated by GSK3beta, which targets PGC-1alpha for intranuclear proteasomal degradation. We find that PGC-1alpha accumulates in bright foci in the nucleus in the presence of proteasomal inhibitors. The same staining pattern is also observed in the presence of GSK3beta inhibitor. An independent study has shown that these bright foci costain with PML bodies and are associated with the insoluble nuclear fraction (Sano et al., 2007). Interestingly, in our over-expression study using GFP-tagged PGC-1alpha, the nuclear distribution of the over-expressed tagged PGC-1alpha differs from that of the endogenous protein. Nuclear GFP-PGC-1alpha is detected in the soluble and insoluble fractions, whereas the endogenous protein is predominately found in the insoluble fraction. Immunofluorescence using either anti-GFP of anti-PGC-1alpha antibodies detected a similar staining pattern in untreated cells, but a subtle difference in the oxidative-stress-treated cells (Fig. 1). The areas of nuclear exclusion are evident in cells probed with anti-GFP following stress, but not in the cells probed with anti-PGC-1alpha, raising the possibility that the tagged protein is not being processed in the same manner as the endogenous protein.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 202 207 202 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 194 214 194 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b22">Kodadek <italic>et al</italic>., 2006</xref>
###xml 463 468 463 468 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 459 475 459 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b26">Lee <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
###xml 652 657 644 649 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 642 664 634 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b40">St-Pierre <italic>et al</italic>., 2006</xref>
###xml 931 938 923 930 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 966 972 958 964 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Fig. 5</xref>
###xml 1070 1074 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Regulation of transcription factor activity through subcellular localization and protein stability provides mechanism whereby the expression of key genes is facilitated for a specific duration (Kodadek et al., 2006). It is possible that transcription factor activation that is combined with degradation could influence the behaviour of the transcriptional activator, as in the case of the SAGA complex where activity is stimulated by proteasomal association (Lee et al., 2005). We describe an initial transient activation of PGC-1alpha in response to stress, while a sustained increase in PGC-1alpha levels is detected at a later time point (St-Pierre et al., 2006). The transcriptional targets appear to be specialized for either the early or later response, as COX IV levels are increased in the early response, but unlike the later time frame, the expressions of antioxidant scavengers MnSOD and catalase are not increased. The in vivo experiment described here (Fig. 5) revealed a biphasic nature to the regulation of PGC-1alpha localization in skeletal muscle from mice treated with paraquat, and this was reflected in the difference in timing of induction of COX IV and UCP3 proteins. It will be interesting to determine how PGC-1alpha gene target specificity is regulated under these conditions, and if its association with the proteasome is a determining factor.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 234 239 234 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 225 246 225 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">Anderson <italic>et al</italic>., 2003</xref>
###xml 103 108 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
There is evidence to suggest that CR induces specific pathways that promote longevity. For example, in yeast, CR and numerous low-intensity stressors associated with longevity activate a common pathway to influence lifespan (Anderson et al., 2003). Here, we show that PGC-1alpha transcriptional activity is induced in the oxidative stress response and CR through a shared mechanism, suggesting that in mammals, regulation of mitochondrial function is a key element in both cellular survival and longevity. We propose that mitochondrial plasticity may be critical for maintaining cell viability and in orchestrating the program of aging retardation by CR, raising the possibility that loss of mitochondrial plasticity is an underlying cause of aging.
###end p 36

###xml 1016 1020 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Wild-type male C57B16 mice were housed under controlled, specific pathogen-free conditions at the Shared Aging Rodent Facility at Madison VA Geriatric, Research, Education, and Clinical Center. The mice were cared for in accordance with the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at UW Madison. To control calorie intake, the mice were housed singly and fed less than ad libitum intakes every other day. All mice were euthanized by cervical dislocation. The control animals were fed 98 kcal week-1 of AIN-M semipurified diet (Bioserve, Frenchtown, NJ, USA), which is about 90% of the average ad libitum intake for these mice. The restricted mice were fed 58 kcal week-1 (a 41% reduction) from 8 weeks of age. The restricted diet was nearly isocaloric to the control diet, but was enriched in proteins, vitamins and minerals to avoid malnutrition. Under this regimen of controlled intake, animals ingest all the allocated food, and the calorie intake is precisely known. For the oxidative stress experiment, the mice were treated with paraquat (single intraperitoneal injection 50 mg kg-1) and sacrificed at 1, 3, 5 and 7 h after exposure. Tissues were collected, snap frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at -80 degreesC until further processing by microarray or immunoblotting.

Clones were isolated, and expression of PGC-1alpha was confirmed by Western blot.
