Involvement of LPA1 receptor signaling in the reorganization of spinal input through Abeta-fibers in mice with partial sciatic nerve injury
###end article-title 0

###begin p 2
###xml 42 44 42 44 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 145 146 145 146 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 161 162 157 158 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 366 368 354 356 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 828 830 806 808 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 850 855 828 833 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lpar1</italic>
###xml 855 858 833 836 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/-</sup>
###xml 963 965 941 943 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 767 771 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 860 864 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Lysophosphatidic acid receptor subtype LPA1 is crucial for the initiation of neuropathic pain and underlying changes, such as up-regulation of Ca2+ channel alpha2delta-1 subunit in dorsal root ganglia (DRG), up-regulation of PKCgamma in the spinal dorsal horn, and demyelination of dorsal root fibers. In the present study, we further examined the involvement of LPA1 signaling in the reorganization of Abeta-fiber-mediated spinal transmission, which is presumed to underlie neuropathic allodynia. Following nerve injury, the phosphorylation of extracellular-signal regulated kinase (pERK) by Abeta-fiber stimulation was observed in the superficial layer of spinal dorsal horn, where nociceptive C- or Adelta-fibers are innervated, but not in sham-operated wild-type mice. However, the pERK signals were largely abolished in LPA1 receptor knock-out (Lpar1-/-) mice, further supported by quantitative analyses of pERK-positive cells. These results suggest that LPA1 receptor-mediated signaling mechanisms also participate in functional cross-talk between Abeta- and C- or Adelta-fibers.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Findings
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 342 343 342 343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 344 345 344 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 543 544 540 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>

###xml 718 719 715 716 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 846 847 843 844 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 848 849 845 846 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 1006 1007 1000 1001 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 1008 1009 1002 1003 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1223 1224 1210 1211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 1721 1722 1695 1696 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 1314 1318 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1553 1557 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1715 1719 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Peripheral nerve injury often accompanies with neuropathic pain, which is characterized by stimulus-independent persistent pain or abnormal sensory perception of pain such as hyperalgesia (exaggerated pain sensations as a result of exposure to mildly noxious stimuli) and allodynia (pain perception on exposure to innocuous tactile stimuli) [1,2]. The mechanisms of allodynia in particular have been long speculated to involve abnormal spinal input through the sprouted myelinated Abeta-fibers that normally conduct innocuous tactile stimuli [3,4]. Moreover, the functional cross-talk between the damaged peripheral sensory fibers causing abnormal spinal input is also postulated to function in neuropathic allodynia [5]. Phosphorylation of ERK (pERK) has been reported as a specific marker for activated cells responding to nociceptive stimuli [6,7] and recent studies using immunohistochemical analysis of pERK provided evidence for spinal reorganization through Abeta-fibers in neuropathic pain models [8,9]. Most recently we have reported that the acute nerve injury caused Abeta-fiber-induced pERK signals within the superficial region of spinal cord dorsal horn, where nociceptive C- or Adelta-fibers are innervated [8]. In addition, it was found that such novel Abeta-fiber-mediated pERK signals in injured mice were blocked by NMDA receptor antagonists that specifically block the nociceptive behaviors induced by C- or Adelta-fiber stimulation. This suggests a pharmacological switch in Abeta-fiber-mediated spinal neurotransmission in injured mice, considering the result that Abeta-fiber stimulation-induced paw withdrawal behaviors were specifically blocked by AMPA/kainate receptor antagonist in naive mice [8]. These results suggest further that functional reorganization of Abeta-fiber input to the spinal neurons innervated by nociceptive C- or Adelta-fibers may underlie mechanisms for neuropathic allodynia.
###end p 4

###xml 35 37 35 37 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 139 140 139 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 141 142 141 142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 143 145 143 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 272 274 272 274 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>

###xml 299 302 299 302 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/-</sup>
###xml 445 448 445 446 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#174;</sup>
###xml 610 611 601 602 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 658 663 649 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lpar1</italic>
###xml 663 667 654 658 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 673 675 664 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 771 776 762 767 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Lpar1</italic>
###xml 776 780 767 771 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-/- </sup>
###xml 786 788 777 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 789 791 780 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 816 818 807 809 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 998 1006 989 997 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo </italic>
###xml 1149 1151 1140 1142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 1152 1154 1143 1145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 1221 1223 1212 1214 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
###xml 304 308 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 570 574 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 667 671 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 780 784 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We previously demonstrated that LPA1 receptor signaling is involved in the initiation of peripheral nerve injury-induced neuropathic pain [2,5,10]. The nerve injury-induced neuropathic pain, demyelination and underlying molecular events were attenuated or abolished in LPA1 receptor-knock out (Lpar1-/-) mice. Most recently we also characterized neuropathic pain by a novel electrical stimuli-induced paw withdrawal (EPW) test using a Neurometer(R). In this test, there was a significant decrease in sensory perception threshold of Adelta- or Abeta-fiber stimulation in mice with partial sciatic nerve injury [8], and this type of allodynia was abolished in Lpar1-/- mice [11]. From the fact that the basal nociceptive thresholds in behavioral tests were not affected in Lpar1-/- mice [10,11], it is evident that LPA1 receptor signaling works only after occurrence of the nerve-injury. This view was supported by the recent finding that nerve injury-induced neuropathic pain is associated with the de novo synthesis of LPA, which is produced by a conversion of lysophosphatidylcholine (LPC) through autotaxin (ATX) or lysophospholipase D (lysoPLD) [11,12]. In the present study, we further examined the involvement of LPA1 receptor signaling in the spinal reorganization through Abeta-fiber after the peripheral nerve injury.
###end p 5

###xml 32 34 32 34 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>

###xml 12 16 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 82 86 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 571 575 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 617 621 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 887 891 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1160 1164 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1569 1575 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
###xml 2141 2146 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 2398 2402 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Male mutant mice lacking the LPA1 gene (Lpar1-/-)[13] and their sibling wild-type mice from the same genetic background (weighing 20-24 g) were used. All procedures were approved by the Nagasaki University Animal Care Committee and complied with the recommendations of the International Association for the Study of Pain [14]. The partial sciatic nerve ligation injury was carried out according to methods described previously [8,11]. On day 7 after the sham or nerve injury operation, significant thermal hyperalgesia and mechanical allodynia were observed in wild-type mice, which were mostly abolished in Lpar1-/- mice [10]. The procedures for Abeta-fiber specific electrical stimulation were performed as described previously [8]. Briefly, the electrodes (Neurotron Inc., Baltimore, MD) were fastened with tape to the operated right planter surface and instep of deeply anesthetized mice. After 10 min, transcutaneous nerve stimuli of 2000 Hz with the current intensity of 1000 muA was applied using a Neurometer(R) CPT/C (Neurotron Inc.) for 1 min. The control treatment was performed without electrical stimulation. Two min after electrical stimulation, mice were immediately perfused with ice-cold PBS, followed by cold 4% paraformaldehyde solution. The spinal cord (L4-5) was removed and cut on a cryostat at a thickness of 30 mum for pERK1/2 (pERK)-immunostaining. The sections were incubated at 4degreesC overnight with primary antibody (anti-phospho-p44/42 MAP kinase, 1:500, Cell Signaling Technology, MA), followed by incubation with the biotinylated anti-rabbit IgG (1:500, Vector, CA). The immunoreactivity was amplified with ABC kit (Vector, CA) and visualized by incubation with a solution containing 0.02% 3,3'-diaminobenzidine tetrahydrochloride (DAB; Dojindo, Japan). The immunoreactive cells showing S/N ratio of 3.0 or more and a diameter of > 5 mum were counted as pERK-positive neurons, as described previously [8]. The intensity in the gracile fasciculus regions of white matter was considered as background activity. pERK-positive neurons in the superficial laminae (I-II) of dorsal horn from five sections of each mouse were counted. Statistical comparison was performed using one-way ANOVA with Tukey-Kramer multiple comparison post-hoc analysis. The criterion of significance was established at P < 0.05. All results are expressed as means +/- S.E.M. from 4-6 separate mice.
###end p 6

In sham-operated wild-type mice, no significant pERK signals were observed by the control treatment without electrical stimulation or by transcutaneous nerve stimuli for Abeta-fiber (2000 Hz, 1000 muA) in the L4-5 spinal dorsal horn (Fig. 1A, B). Although the nerve-injury alone did not induce pERK-signals (Fig, 1C), the Abeta-fiber-stimulation to the paw of nerve-injured mice induced pERK-positive signals in the ipsilateral superficial dorsal horn (laminae I-II), but not in the deeper regions of dorsal horn (lamina III-V) (Fig. 1D), as previously reported [8]. In the sham-operated Lpar1-/- mice, on the other hand, neither control treatment nor Abeta-fiber-stimulation induced any pERK signals (Fig. 1E, F). Although the nerve-injury alone also failed to induce pERK signals in Lpar1-/- mice, the Abeta-fiber-stimulation-induced pERK signals were largely abolished in nerve-injured Lpar1-/- mice (Fig. 1G, H). The number of pERK-positive cells in spinal dorsal horn was also increased in the nerve-injured wild-type mice after the Abeta-fiber stimuli, and this increase was significantly suppressed in Lpar1-/- mice (Fig. 1I).

###xml 1932 1933 1905 1906 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 2096 2097 2062 2063 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 2098 2100 2064 2066 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 150 154 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1057 1061 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In the present study, we demonstrated that Abeta-fiber-mediated abnormal spinal input after the nerve injury was significantly suppressed in Lpar1-/- mice. These data suggest that the nerve injury-induced spinal reorganization through Abeta-fiber is caused by LPA1 receptor-mediated signaling. Extracellular signal-regulated kinases (ERKs), major subfamilies of mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAPKs), are phosphorylated following membrane depolarization and Ca2+ influx [15]. As the noxious stimulation rapidly activates ERK in superficial dorsal horn neurons, the pERK expression could be used as a biochemical marker of activated neurons [6]. Interestingly, we previously found that the pERK expression was specific for nociceptive C-fiber- and Adelta-fiber perception through substance P-NK1 receptor- and glutamate-NMDA receptor-dependent spinal transmission, using the transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulations to the hind paw using the Neurometer(R) [8]. Few ERK activation was observed after innocuous Abeta-fiber-stimulation in sham-operated mice, which is mediated through AMPA/kainate receptor spinal transmission [8]. As previously discussed, the lack of pERK signals after Abeta-fiber-stimulation may be attributable to the insufficient Ca2+ influx for ERK activation in spinal neurons, because AMPA/kainate receptors have lower Ca2+ permeability than NMDA receptors [16]. From the findings that Abeta-fibers are normally innervated to the neurons in lamina III-IV [17], and Abeta-fiber stimulation-induced abnormal pain was blocked by NK1 or NMDA receptor antagonist, but not by AMPA/kainate antagonist [8], it is suggested that Abeta-fiber stimulation may cause a stimulation of nociceptive pain pathway through a functional cross-talk. We speculate that the direct contact among different modalities of fibers and sprouted fibers following LPA1-mediated demyelination may underlie the functional cross-talk [2,5]. Alternatively sprouted fibers derived from Abeta-fibers may innervate to the second-order spinal neurons, which are normally innervated by C- or Adelta-fibers [3,18].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 18 20 18 20 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1 </sub>
In conclusion, LPA1 receptor-mediated signaling mechanisms contribute to spinal neuronal reorganization through Abeta-fiber and could contribute to mechanisms underlying neuropathic allodynia.
