Ribonucleotide reductases (RNRs) catalyze the conversion of nucleotides to deoxynucleotides in all organisms. The Escherichia coli class Ia RNR is composed of alpha and beta subunits that form an alpha2beta2 active complex. beta contains the diferric tyrosyl radical (Y*) cofactor that is essential for the reduction process that occurs on alpha. [Y*] in vitro is proportional to RNR activity, and its regulation in vivo potentially represents a mechanism for controlling RNR activity. To examine this thesis, N- and C-terminal StrepII-tagged beta under the control of an l-arabinose promoter were constructed. Using these constructs and with [l-arabinose] varying from 0 to 0.5 mM in the growth medium, [beta] could be varied from 4 to 3300 microM. [Y*] in vivo and on affinity-purified Strep-beta in vitro was determined by EPR spectroscopy and Western analysis. In both cases, there was 0.1-0.3 Y* radical per beta. To determine if the substoichiometric Y* level was associated with apo beta or diferric beta, titrations of crude cell extracts from these growths were carried out with reduced YfaE, a 2Fe2S ferredoxin involved in cofactor maintenance and assembly. Each titration, followed by addition of O2 to assemble the cofactor and EPR analysis to quantitate Y*, revealed that beta is completely loaded with a diferric cluster even when its concentration in vivo is 244 microM. These titrations, furthermore, resulted in 1 Y* radical per beta, the highest levels reported. Whole cell Mossbauer analysis on cells induced with 0.5 mM arabinose supports high iron loading in beta. These results suggest that modulation of the level of Y* in vivo in E. coli is a mechanism of regulating RNR activity.

Ribonucleotide reductases (RNRs)1 catalyze the conversion of nucleotides to deoxynucleotides in all organisms, supplying the monomeric precursors required for DNA replication and repair (1-4). The Escherichia coli class I RNR is composed of alpha and beta subunits with an active quaternary structure of alpha2beta2(5). alpha2 houses the active site for nucleoside diphosphate reduction and additional sites that control the rate and specificity of nucleotide reduction by dNTP and ATP effectors. beta contains a diferric tyrosyl radical (Y*) cofactor essential for activity (6,7). The central role of this enzyme in DNA replication and repair and the importance of balanced deoxynucleotide pool sizes for the fidelity of these processes require that RNRs be regulated by many mechanisms. In 1983, Barlow et al. (8) proposed that one mechanism of regulation might involve the control of the concentration of the essential Y*. Studies presented in this paper provide insight into the loading of beta with iron and the levels of Y* in vivo, a first step in understanding the mechanism of regulation of RNR activity by modulation of the active metallo-cofactor. 1Abbreviations: alpha, ribonucleotide reductase large subunit; ara, l-arabinose; beta, ribonucleotide reductase small subunit; apo-beta2, iron-free form of beta2; met-beta2, radical-free beta2 that contains a diiron(III/III) cluster; RNR, ribonucleotide reductase; wt, wild type; Y*, tyrosyl radical; YfaE, 2Fe2S ferredoxin; Abs, antibodies; HU, hydroxyurea; buffer A, 100 mM Tris, 150 mM NaCl, and 5% glycerol (pH 7.6); N-S-beta, N-terminal StrepII-tagged beta; C-S-beta, C-terminal StrepII-tagged beta; PVDF, poly(vinylidene) difluoride; TR, thioredoxin; TRR, thioredoxin reductase.

The results of studies by the Reichard and Fontecave laboratories led to the model for diferric Y* radical cofactor assembly and conversion of the diferric cluster of beta in which the Y* is reduced (diferric tyrosine or met-beta2) to active cofactor (9,10). Our recent discovery of the 2Fe2S cluster ferredoxin, YfaE, in E. coli has resulted in extensive modifications of their original proposal. Our current model is shown in Scheme 1(11) which includes the biosynthetic pathway (A), a maintenance pathway (B), and a regulatory pathway (C).
###end p 10

For biosynthesis of the active, diferric Y* cofactor (pathway A, Scheme 1), apo-beta2 must be loaded with Fe2+ to generate diferrous beta2. The details of this process in vivo, the source of iron, and the control of delivery of the two irons per active site of beta without the generation of destructive metabolites of O2 are currently unknown. Once the diferrous beta2 is formed, the active cofactor can be assembled by addition of O2 and a reducing equivalent that likely is provided by reduced YfaE (11,12).
###end p 11

The Y* in the active cofactor is inherently unstable [the half-life of the E. coli beta2 Y* is several days (13), while that of mouse beta2 is 10 min (14)] and is also susceptible to one-electron reduction by small molecules such as hydroxyurea (HU) (13) or potentially a protein. The Y* in crude extracts of E. coli, for example, is rapidly reduced by an unknown mechanism. The inherent instability and reductants both lead to a diferric tyrosine cluster that is inactive in nucleotide reduction (8,10).
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 18 19 18 19 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="sch1" ref-type="scheme">1</xref>
###xml 266 267 266 267 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 305 307 305 307 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref11">11</xref>
Pathway B (Scheme 1) describes the maintenance pathway. In this pathway, activity is regenerated by reduction of the diferric cluster to a diferrous cluster by YfaE and a ferredoxin reductase (potentially Fre) and this cluster is assembled into the active diferric Y* cluster by the biosynthetic pathway (11).
###end p 13

###xml 43 44 43 44 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="sch1" ref-type="scheme">1</xref>
###xml 90 91 90 91 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 167 168 167 168 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 301 302 301 302 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 373 374 373 374 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 531 532 531 532 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
Finally, a regulatory pathway, C in Scheme 1, could have evolved to control the level of Y* as a mechanism of controlling RNR activity. In this pathway, reduction of Y* and its re-formation might be coordinated. For example, outside the S phase of the cell cycle, RNR activity might be turned off by Y* reduction, but once the S phase of the cycle begins, a mechanism for Y* regeneration, which may or may not overlap with the maintenance pathway, may be invoked. To assess the importance of these pathways in vivo, the levels of Y* and the extent of iron loading of beta under different growth conditions need to be evaluated.
###end p 14

Our approach to testing this model in E. coli is based on our recent experiments that aimed to determine the Y*/betabeta' ratio in Saccharomyces cerevisiae under different growth conditions (15). In those studies, we constructed a FLAG-tagged beta (FLAGbeta) which was integrated into the genome of a number of different S. cerevisiae strains and allowed rapid purification to homogeneity of the active protein (betabeta') by affinity chromatography and quantitation of the Y*/betabeta' ratio by EPR spectroscopy. These results were then compared with those from whole cell EPR spectroscopy on the same strains, where the amount of betabeta' in each strain was determined by quantitative Western analysis. We also determined that the amount of Y* was directly correlated with nucleotide reduction activity in crude cell extracts, providing an independent assessment of the concentration of Y*. Under normal growth conditions, the studies together suggested that the cell assembles stoichiometric amounts of Y* and diiron cluster and, consequently, that modulation of the Y* concentration is not involved in regulation of RNR activity (15). However, altered lifetimes of eukaryotic Y* radicals in beta, relative to those of E. coli beta, suggest that a maintenance pathway might play a very important role in the observed levels of Y*(14). These studies suggest that the regulatory pathway (C, Scheme 1) is unimportant in this organism under the growth conditions thus far examined.

Our studies in S. cerevisiae betabeta' suggested that regulatory mechanisms in play in this organism are different from those suggested to be in play in E. coli. We, therefore, embarked on a similar set of experiments to gain further insight into the role of the Y* concentration in the regulation of RNR activity in E. coli. Instead of using a FLAG-tagged betabeta', we generated an N- or C-terminal StrepII-nrdB and placed the gene behind an l-arabinose (ara) promoter. Levels of beta could thus be modulated by varying the concentrations of ara in the growth medium. Affinity chromatography allowed rapid isolation of beta2 (<4 h) and the determination of the amount of Y* and iron per beta. Whole cell EPR experiments and quantitative Western blots of beta in the crude cell extracts of the same cells were analyzed as a compliment to the studies on the isolated StrepII-beta. Our recent discovery of [2Fe2S]+-YfaE, a ferredoxin that is involved in maintenance and likely the biosynthesis of the diferric Y* cofactor of E. coli beta, has provided us with an additional tool. Titration of met-beta2 in crude cell extracts with variable amounts of YfaE in the presence of O2 and monitoring Y* formation allowed us to determine the extent of iron loading of beta. Whole cell Mossbauer spectroscopy has provided independent evidence of the high iron loading of beta. The studies together suggest that inside the cell, up to 4.4 mM Fe2+ can be directed (redirected) to load beta. However, the level of Y* relative to iron loading is always substoichiometric, suggesting that its concentration and consequently RNR activity are regulated by other proteins or small molecules which remain to be identified.

###end title 56
###begin title 57
###xml 31 32 31 32 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
Determination of the Iron and Y* Content in Purified N-S-beta and C-S-beta.
###end title 57
###begin p 58
###xml 64 65 64 65 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 110 114 104 108 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">nrdB</italic>
###xml 219 221 213 215 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref24">24</xref>
###xml 486 487 471 472 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 519 520 504 505 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 585 598 570 583 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 610 612 589 591 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref15">15</xref>
###xml 790 792 769 771 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 585 598 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
The first approach to determining the in vivo concentration of Y* per beta and Fe per beta was to construct a nrdB fusion with a StrepII tag (WSHPQFEK) at either its N- or C-terminus and to place them in a pBAD vector (24). Each plasmid was transformed into TOP10 cells, and the levels of beta were modulated by the concentrations of ara in the growth medium. The goal was to rapidly isolate N-S-beta or C-S-beta by affinity chromatography and to determine the loading of the diferric Y* cofactor with minimal loss of Y*. This approach has been successful in the rapid purification of S. cerevisiae betabeta' (15). A StrepII tag was chosen for the fusion because in contrast with the more commonly used FLAG and His tags, the StrepII tag has minimal number of side chains that might bind Fe2+ and thus might interfere with iron cluster assembly in vivo.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 289 291 276 278 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref25">25</xref>
###xml 434 435 418 419 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">1</xref>
C-S-beta was generated with no linker between beta and the StrepII tag as the C-terminal tail of all beta subunits is largely responsible for interaction with the alpha subunit, and thus, any manipulation of this end was expected to result in a construct inactive in nucleotide reduction (25). This construct led to efficient expression of beta that was rapidly purified to homogeneity using the Strep-Tactin Sepharose column (Figure 1A, lane 5). The activity assay confirmed that C-S-beta is inactive.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
Purification of C-S-beta (A) and N-S-beta (B) from cells induced with 0.01 mM ara monitored by SDS-PAGE: lane 1, molecular mass standards; lane 2, crude cell extracts; lane 3, flow through from the Strep-Tactin Sepharose column; lane 4, wash from the Strep-Tactin Sepharose column; and lane 5, protein eluted with desthiobiotin from the Strep-Tactin Sepharose column.

###begin p 61

###xml 457 458 448 449 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">1</xref>
Previous N-terminally tagged constructs of beta have met with variable success (26,27). Thus, a StrepII tag followed by linkers of variable lengths (GA, ALGGH, and GSGGSG) was examined (data not shown). The construct with the ALGGH linker was chosen as it gave the best binding to the Strep-Tactin Sepharose column. This construct, N-S-beta, while binding more weakly to the affinity resin than C-S-beta, resulted in its purification to homogeneity (Figure 1B). In this case, the protein is active.

In the case of both constructs, protein purification was complete in 3-4 h, and the expression levels could be induced by addition of ara. Western analysis revealed that the levels of beta varied from 4.4 microM in the absence of ara to 3.3 mM in the presence of 0.5 mM ara (Table 2). The results revealed that regardless of the levels of beta expression, the Y*/beta ratio is 0.25-0.35 and the Fe/beta ratio is 1.7-1.85. The specific activity of N-S-beta also correlates with the amount of Y* per beta. Similar amounts of Y* and Fe per beta in both constructs suggest that the StrepII tag does not interfere with diferric Y* cofactor assembly. Our previous experiments on cofactor assembly and regulation of betabeta' in wt S. cerevisiae showed that whole cell EPR and quantitative Western blots provide an alternative measurement of the Y*/beta ratio in vivo. However, previous whole cell EPR experiments and rocket immunoelectrophoresis experiments on wt and thymine-starved E. coli by Sjoberg et al. failed to detect Y* when beta was 0.3% (0.9%) of the total protein in crude cell extracts. However, when they analyzed E. coli strain KK546 where beta was increased to 2% of the total protein in crude cell extracts, the Y* was readily detected (29). These early studies thus suggested that growth of cells with ara induction would be required to detect Y* in E. coli by whole cell EPR methods.

Titrations of Crude Cell Extracts with Reduced YfaE To Determine Iron Loading of beta.
###end title 87
###begin p 88
###xml 77 78 74 75 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 138 139 135 136 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="tbl3" ref-type="table">3</xref>

###xml 913 914 896 897 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 1049 1050 1019 1020 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="tbl2" ref-type="table">2</xref>

###xml 1111 1112 1081 1082 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 1313 1314 1262 1263 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="tbl2" ref-type="table">2</xref>
###xml 1400 1401 1349 1350 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 1609 1610 1546 1547 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
The experiments with purified beta suggested that despite the low levels of Y*, the protein was almost completely loaded with iron (Table 3). Our recent identification of YfaE, a ferredoxin involved in cofactor maintenance and biosynthesis, provided us with a tool for independently measuring iron loading of beta in crude cell extracts (11). In a typical experiment, the level of N-S-beta from the crude cell extracts was adjusted to 2.3-5.9 microM, determined by Western blot analysis. The extracts were then titrated with reduced YfaE (0-76 microM) to reduce the diferric cluster to the diferrous cluster and then mixed with O2 to reassemble the active diferric Y* cofactor (Scheme 1). The Y* of each sample was then quantitated by EPR spectroscopy. The results are shown in Figure 4 (red signals, A-D) and summarized in Table 5. Strikingly, cells grown with induction of 0.01, 0.02, or 0.05 mM ara contain 1 Y* per beta, indicating that beta is fully loaded with iron even when the in vivo concentration of beta is as high as 244 microM (Tables 2 and 5). In the case of 0.5 mM ara induction, the number of Y* radicals per beta drops to 0.67, suggesting that the cell has reached its limit in terms of delivery of iron to beta. However, given the high in vivo concentration of beta [approximately3.3 mM (Table 2)] under these induction conditions and the stoichiometry of 2 Fe atoms required per Y* generated, up to 4.4 mM iron has been directed or redirected to load beta. These results support the observations with purified beta. Thus, beta appears to be in the met-beta state, ready to regenerate the Y* when needed.

Whole Cell Mossbauer: Detection of Diferric beta at 0.5 mM ara
###end title 92
###begin p 93
###xml 205 207 202 204 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">57</sup>
###xml 455 456 449 450 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">5</xref>
###xml 795 796 786 787 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">5</xref>
###xml 936 937 927 928 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">5</xref>
###xml 1156 1157 1127 1128 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</italic>
###xml 1157 1158 1128 1129 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Q</sub>
###xml 1324 1326 1283 1285 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>

###xml 1490 1493 1445 1448 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">app</sub>
###xml 1518 1519 1469 1470 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</italic>
###xml 1519 1523 1470 1474 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Qapp</sub>
###xml 1921 1922 1860 1861 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">5</xref>
###xml 2055 2056 1989 1990 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">5</xref>
###xml 2134 2135 2060 2061 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</italic>
###xml 2135 2136 2061 2062 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Q</sub>
###xml 2184 2185 2102 2103 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E</italic>
###xml 2185 2186 2103 2104 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Q</sub>
###xml 2272 2274 2187 2189 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref13">13</xref>
###xml 2275 2277 2190 2192 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref31">31</xref>
As a further control to show that beta generated subsequent to induction with 0.5 mM ara was highly loaded with iron in vivo, we examined by Mossbauer spectroscopy samples of whole cells supplemented with 57Fe. Given the well-described spectrum of the diferric cluster of beta, this method was expected to provide an independent verification of iron loading. The spectrum of cells 2 h subsequent to addition of ara to the growth medium is shown in Figure 5A and is dominated by two quadrupole doublets which are assigned to the diferric cluster of beta. In addition, there is a small peak at 2.8 mm/s, which is typical of the high-energy line of high-spin Fe(II), and a shoulder at 0.9 mm/s (indicated by an arrow). These features are also observed in the spectrum of the control sample (Figure 5B) in which cells were treated in a fashion identical to that for the experiment except that no ara was added. The observed signals (Figure 5B) are the superposition of the spectral features of all Fe species present in the cell, according to their relative concentrations. Approximately 40% of the iron is high-spin Fe(II) (delta approximately 1.3 mm/s, DeltaEQ approximately 2.9 mm/s), and the remainder represents several overlapping quadrupole doublets, which may include Fe/S cluster-containing proteins (primarily [4Fe-4S]2+ and [2Fe-2S]2+), low-spin Fe(II) hemes, and possibly fast-relaxing high-spin Fe(III). These features can be approximated as a broad quadrupole doublet with a deltaapp of 0.46 mm/s and a DeltaEQapp of 0.94 mm/s which accounts for approximately60% of the total iron. Mossbauer spectra collected over a wider range of Doppler velocities reveal that the samples contain only a small amount of paramagnetic species (Figure S5, <10% of total iron). Subtraction of the features observed in the spectrum of the control [15% of Fe(II) and 9% of the broad quadrupole doublet] from the spectrum in Figure 5A yields the spectrum of diferric species assumed to be associated with beta, which accounts for 76 +/- 4% of the total iron (Figure 5C). The spectrum can be simulated with parameters [delta(1) = 0.46 mm/s, DeltaEQ(1) = 2.40 mm/s, delta(2) = 0.54 mm/s, and DeltaEQ(2) = 1.65 mm/s] that are similar to those reported for the diferric cluster of beta (13,31). These experiments demonstrate high iron loading of beta and suggest that subsequent to ara induction iron uptake is enhanced. They also suggest that whole cell Mossbauer spectroscopy may be a useful tool for monitoring iron movement in vivo.

###begin title 95
Discussion
###end title 95
###begin p 96
###xml 190 192 190 192 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref32">32</xref>
###xml 434 436 434 436 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref33">33</xref>
###xml 437 439 437 439 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref35">35</xref>
###xml 560 582 560 582 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</italic>
###xml 597 599 597 599 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref36">36</xref>
###xml 713 714 713 714 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 799 800 799 800 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 880 882 880 882 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref37">37</xref>
###xml 922 924 922 924 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref14">14</xref>
###xml 560 582 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
###xml 911 916 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The genomics revolution has identified through operon organization the importance of biosynthetic pathways for the synthesis of many metallo-cofactors and the redundancy of these processes (32). Identification of genes in the pathway has evoked tremendous interest in understanding the complexity of cofactor assembly. In addition to biosynthetic pathways, it is now clear that many metallo-cofactors can be damaged during catalysis (33-35) or during oxidative stress. Thus, repair or maintenance pathways appear to be universal as well. A recent study of the Pseudomonas aeruginosa class Ia RNR (36) provides one of the most compelling examples of the importance of these pathways for RNR cluster assembly. The Y* of this RNR is very unstable, and its re-formation occurs readily upon addition of O2. This observation is reminiscent of the early studies on the herpes viral RNR (37) and the instability of the mouse RNR (14).
###end p 96
###begin p 97
###xml 36 43 36 43 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 249 251 249 251 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref11">11</xref>
###xml 293 294 290 291 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 396 397 393 394 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 647 648 641 642 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 36 43 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
In the case of the class Ia RNRs in E. coli, the biosynthetic and maintenance pathways were proposed many years ago. Only recently, however, has the first protein player directly involved in these pathways been identified, a 2Fe2S ferredoxin, YfaE (11). In vitro studies revealed that met-beta2 can be reassembled into the active cofactor when titrated with reduced YfaE followed by addition of O2. To obtain additional support for the maintenance pathway, we have constructed N- and C-S-beta, the concentration of which can be regulated by the levels of ara in the growth medium. Use of these constructs has allowed measurement of the amount of Y* per beta by two independent methods, whole cell EPR spectroscopy and quantitative Western blots, and by rapid purification of N- or C-S-beta via affinity chromatography followed by analysis by EPR spectroscopy and analytical methods (determination of beta and Fe concentrations).
###end p 97
###begin p 98
###xml 71 72 53 54 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 277 278 253 254 <sub xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 372 373 348 349 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 494 496 455 457 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref38">38</xref>
###xml 519 520 480 481 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 651 652 609 610 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
Purifications of N-S-beta and C-S-beta revealed approximately0.25-0.3 Y* radical per beta over a broad range of expression levels. Control experiments reveal that this substoichiometric radical is not associated with radical loss during purification or the presence of apo beta2. Thus, the explanation that we currently favor is that in the asynchronous cultures, a high Y*:beta ratio is present only in the S-phase of the cell cycle, which represents approximately37% of the total population (38). Measurement of the Y*/beta ratio by whole cell EPR and Western blot analysis provides an independent verification of this ratio. The substoichiometric Y* levels suggest that there is a mechanism in vivo to reduce this radical, and turn off RNR activity, as well as to regenerate it.
###end p 98
###begin p 99
###xml 114 115 111 112 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 180 187 174 181 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 221 234 215 228 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 300 301 294 295 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 312 314 303 305 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref15">15</xref>
###xml 315 317 306 308 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref39">39</xref>
###xml 383 390 374 381 <italic xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 497 498 488 489 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 563 564 554 555 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref8">8</xref>
###xml 565 567 556 558 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref29">29</xref>
###xml 921 922 909 910 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 1072 1073 1060 1061 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 1219 1220 1204 1205 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 180 187 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 221 234 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S. cerevisiae</span>
###xml 383 390 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Measurements of N-S-beta activity in the crude cell extracts were attempted as a third method of determining the Y*:beta ratio. Previous studies have revealed that the activity of E. coli RNR in vitro and the activity of S. cerevisiae RNR in the crude cell extracts is correlated with the amount of Y* per beta (15,39). Efforts to measure activity directly in crude cell extracts in E. coli, however, revealed levels substantially lower than those expected on the basis of the measured amount of Y*. These results are similar to those reported by Sjoberg et al. (8,29), which revealed activity in crude cell extracts incompatible with production of sufficient dNTPs for DNA replication. However, when beta was partially purified, an increase in activity 10 times relative to that of the crude cell extracts is detected. These data suggest that crude cell extracts contain a small molecule or protein capable of reducing Y*. As noted above, this factor may also play a role in regulation of RNR activity. Thus, the activity assay provides further independent support that Y* is present in substoichiometric amounts relative to beta and suggests an important role for a maintenance mechanism in modulating the levels of Y* and, as a consequence, the rates of deoxynucleotide production.
###end p 99
###begin p 100
###xml 315 316 312 313 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
Our recent discovery that YfaE in crude cell extracts results in highly efficient cofactor assembly has provided an important new tool, a method for measuring iron loading. Our titration of crude cell extracts of cells in which expression levels of N-S-beta were induced with 0.01, 0.02, or 0.05 mM ara revealed 1 Y* per beta and 2 Fe atoms per beta! This is the first time that stoichiometric loading of beta in any organism has been observed. The most amazing result is that even when the beta concentration is approximately250 microM it is fully loaded with active cofactor. Our YfaE titration studies and whole cell Mossbauer data further indicate that high levels of iron loading have occurred even at millimolar beta concentrations. This result suggests that the cell is able to take up iron from the growth medium and direct (redirect) iron into beta within the cell at the expense of other iron-requiring or -binding proteins.
###end p 100
###begin p 101
###xml 220 222 220 222 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref40">40</xref>
###xml 223 225 223 225 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref41">41</xref>
###xml 490 491 487 488 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8226;</sup>
###xml 639 641 633 635 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref42">42</xref>
###xml 642 644 636 638 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref46">46</xref>
###xml 988 990 973 975 <sup xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">17</sup>
###xml 1041 1043 1026 1028 <xref xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref47">47</xref>
Our studies provide the foundation for experiments aimed at addressing a major unresolved issue, movement of iron within the cell. The availability of a wide range of deletion mutants related to iron uptake and storage (40,41) and the choice of appropriate growth conditions should allow us to generate a moderately homogeneous source of iron and, subsequent to ara induction of beta, to monitor iron movement by whole cell Mossbauer spectroscopy. Finally, our unexpected observation of 1 Y* per beta suggests that now might be an appropriate time to revisit spectroscopically the structure of intermediate X in diferric cluster assembly (42-46). In all previous studies, the spectroscopy was complicated by the observation that only 0.5 X per beta was observed despite the detection of one cluster per beta at the end of the assembly process. Thus, 0.5 equivalent of an unidentified precursor to the final diferric cluster is unaccounted for. If 1.0 X per beta can now be generated, the 17O ENDOR experiments might prove to be informative (47).
###end p 101
