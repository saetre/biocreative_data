The YvfTU Two-component System is involved in plcR expression in Bacillus cereus

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 49 65 49 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacillus cereus </italic>
###xml 161 171 161 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 182 187 182 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 49 64 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">Bacillus cereus</span>
###xml 161 176 <span type="species:ncbi:86661">B. cereus group</span>
Most extracellular virulence factors produced by Bacillus cereus are regulated by the pleiotropic transcriptional activator PlcR. Among strains belonging to the B. cereus group, the plcR gene is always located in the vicinity of genes encoding the YvfTU two-component system. The putative role of YvfTU in the expression of the PlcR regulon was therefore investigated.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 18 23 18 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 80 85 80 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 104 110 104 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 128 138 128 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 276 281 276 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 299 305 299 305 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 384 389 384 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 407 413 407 413 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 564 570 564 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 582 602 582 602 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Galleria mellonella </italic>
###xml 128 148 <span type="species:ncbi:226900">B. cereus ATCC 14579</span>
###xml 582 601 <span type="species:ncbi:7137">Galleria mellonella</span>
Expression of the plcR gene was monitored using a transcriptional fusion with a lacZ reporter gene in a yvfTU mutant and in its B. cereus ATCC 14579 parental strain. Two hours after the onset of the stationary phase, a stage at which the PlcR regulon is highly expressed, the plcR expression in the yvfTU mutant was only 50% of that of its parental strain. In addition to the reduced plcR expression in the yvfTU mutant, a few members of the PlcR regulon showed a differential expression, as revealed by transcriptomic and proteomic analyses. The virulence of the yvfTU mutant in a Galleria mellonella insect model was slightly lower than that of the parental strain.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 174 178 174 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR</italic>
###xml 214 223 214 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus</italic>
###xml 214 223 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
The YvfTU two-component system is not required for the expression of most of the virulence factors belonging to the PlcR regulon. However, YvfTU is involved in expression of plcR, a major regulator of virulence in B. cereus.
###end p 7

###xml 370 397 <span type="species:ncbi:86662">Bacillus weihenstephanensis</span>
###xml 399 416 <span type="species:ncbi:1405">Bacillus mycoides</span>
###xml 421 444 <span type="species:ncbi:64104">Bacillus pseudomycoides</span>
###xml 452 461 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
###xml 586 595 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
###xml 765 774 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
###xml 788 792 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 816 822 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbit</span>
Bacillus cereus is a Gram-positive, rod-shaped, motile and spore-forming bacterium with opportunistic pathogen properties in human, causing local and systemic infections. B. cereus is mostly observed as a causative agent of food poisoning. This species belongs to the B. cereus group that includes the closely related species Bacillus anthracis, Bacillus thuringiensis, Bacillus weihenstephanensis, Bacillus mycoides and Bacillus pseudomycoides [1,2]. B. cereus produces several secreted proteins, including enterotoxins, cytolysins, phospholipases and proteases that may contribute to B. cereus pathogenicity. Expression of most of these virulence factors is controlled by the pleiotropic transcriptional activator PlcR [3-5]. This global regulator contributes to B. cereus virulence in mice, in insects [6] and to rabbit endophthalmitis [7]. Expression of the PlcR regulon starts at the onset of the stationary phase of growth [8]. It results from a cell-cell communication system that requires PapR, a peptide exported and re-imported in the bacterial cell as a mature form via an oligo-peptide permease [9,10]. The PlcR-PapR active complex binds a DNA target site, designated as the PlcR box, thus activating transcription of the PlcR regulon genes [3,10].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 154 156 154 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 606 608 606 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 609 611 609 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
Bacteria recognize and respond to a variety of environmental stimuli using various signal transduction mechanisms, including two-component systems (TCS) [11]. TCS are characterized by a histidine kinase (HK) sensor coupled with a cognate response regulator. Perception of a particular stimulus by the HK leads to its autophosphorylation. The phosphoryl group is then transferred to the response regulator, usually leading to transcriptional activation of genes. TCS are widespread among bacteria, and the number of TCS encoding genes found in a bacterium is usually proportional to the size of its genome [12,13].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 21 31 21 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 97 99 97 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 100 102 100 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 157 167 157 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 227 240 227 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. anthracis </italic>
###xml 244 261 244 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. thuringiensis </italic>
###xml 262 264 262 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 21 30 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
###xml 157 172 <span type="species:ncbi:86661">B. cereus group</span>
###xml 227 239 <span type="species:ncbi:1392">B. anthracis</span>
###xml 244 260 <span type="species:ncbi:1428">B. thuringiensis</span>
Genome sequencing of B. cereus strain ATCC 14579 has revealed the presence of 55 sensor kinases [14,15]. Most of them are also found in other members of the B. cereus group for which the genome sequence is available, including B. anthracis and B. thuringiensis [16].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 95 104 95 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus</italic>
###xml 120 130 120 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 266 268 266 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 288 298 288 298 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 457 459 457 459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 95 104 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
###xml 120 129 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
###xml 288 297 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
TCS controlling expression of virulence factors have been shown in several bacteria, including B. cereus. Recently, the B. cereus ResDE TCS which is involved in low oxydo-reduction potential adaptation was shown to control enterotoxin production under anaerobiosis [17]. In contrast, the B. cereus YvrGH TCS, of which the encoding genes are located in the vicinity of the cytotoxin K encoding gene, was not required for expression of this virulence factor [18].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 118 130 118 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. subtilis </italic>
###xml 181 183 181 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 184 186 184 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 196 206 196 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 230 236 230 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 273 277 273 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR</italic>
###xml 348 353 348 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 410 416 410 416 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 442 452 442 452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 118 129 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">B. subtilis</span>
###xml 196 211 <span type="species:ncbi:86661">B. cereus group</span>
###xml 442 451 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
TCS encoding genes are often located in the same chromosomal region as the genes that they control, as for example in B. subtilis with the DesKR, CitST, YycFG, BceRS and LiaRS TCS [19-23]. In the B. cereus group, the TCS encoding yvfTU genes are located in the vicinity of plcR. To determine whether the YvfTU TCS plays a role in the expression of plcR and consequently of the PlcR regulon, the phenotype of a yvfTU mutant constructed in the B. cereus strain ATCC 14579 was analyzed.

Results

Genes encoding a TCS (BC5352 and BC5353) were located between 5,263 kb and 5,265 kb on the minus strand of the B. cereus ATCC 14579 chromosome (Fig. 1A). Using BlastP, the deduced amino acid sequences of BC5353 and BC5352 genes appeared to be homologous to both the B. subtilis YocF and YocG (now called DesK and DesR) (37 and 62% identity, with E-values of 2e-60 and 2e-42, respectively) and the B. subtilis YvfT and YvfU (43 and 62% identity, with E-values of 9e-78 and 4e-43, respectively). In B. cereus ATCC 14579, genes encoding a putative ABC transporter (BC5355 and BC5354) were located upstream from the BC5353 and BC5352 genes (Fig. 1A). Orthologous ABC transporter encoding genes were also located upstream from the yvfTU genes in B. subtilis (not shown). The BC5353 and BC5352 genes studied here have been annotated as yocF and yocG in the B. cereus strain ATCC 14579 [15]. However, these genes were previously annotated yvfT and yvfU in ATCC 14579 [5]. Because of (i) their higher sequence homologies with the B. subtilis yvfT and yvfU genes, (ii) their conserved synteny with the B. subtilis yvfTU locus (including ABC transporter encoding genes), and (iii) their previous annotations, the BC5353 and BC5352 genes were named as yvfT and yvfU, respectively, throughout the present work.

In the strain ATCC 14579, the yvfTU genes were located in the vicinity of plcR (BC5350) encoding a transcriptional regulator and its activating peptide encoding gene papR (BC5349) (Fig. 1A). ###begin title 20
Domains in YvfT and YvfU
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 63 68 63 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfT </italic>
###xml 72 77 72 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfU </italic>
###xml 429 434 429 434 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 437 439 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 490 492 490 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 590 592 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 853 858 853 858 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 861 863 861 863 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
Putative motifs present in the deduced amino-acid sequences of yvfT and yvfU were predicted according to the Smart tool: YvfT displays an ATP binding domain (HATPase domain), and a dimerisation and phosphoacceptor domain (PFAM:HisKA_3 domain). YvfT is most probably a membrane protein as revealed by the 4 predicted transmembrane domains in its N-terminal region. This histidine kinase belongs to the class IIa defined by Fabret et al. [26], or to the class 7 according to Grebe and Stock [27]. YvfU, the cognate response regulator of YvfT belongs to the NarL class of response regulators [27]. YvfU displays a phosphoacceptor site (REC domain) in the N-terminal region. The DNA-binding motif (HTH domain) found in the C-terminal region suggests a transcriptional regulator function. The YvfU TCS corresponds to the reference code 56 defined by de Been et al. [16].
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 44 46 44 46 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 138 148 138 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 223 228 223 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfU </italic>
###xml 325 330 325 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfU </italic>
###xml 367 372 367 372 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfU </italic>
###xml 413 415 413 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 609 619 609 619 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 664 665 664 665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 138 153 <span type="species:ncbi:86661">B. cereus group</span>
###xml 609 624 <span type="species:ncbi:86661">B. cereus group</span>
According to the published genome sequence [15], the YvfU response regulator is smaller in strain ATCC 14579 than in other strains of the B. cereus group (not shown). However, our sequences from 3 independent PCR-amplified yvfU fragments differed from the genome data, by the absence of a cytosine at the position 420 in the yvfU gene which created a frameshift. The yvfU gene was consequently 55 bp longer (Fig. 1A), and the C-terminal-end of YvfU (which contains the HTH domain) included therefore 18 additional amino-acids. Thus, YvfU from the ATCC 14579 strain displays a higher similarity with the other B. cereus group members' YvfU protein (Additional file 1).

###xml 0 2 0 2 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d </sup>
###xml 2 3 2 3 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
d P-value < 0.05 obtained with the t-test was considered as significant
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 57 62 53 58 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU</italic>
###xml 74 79 70 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 120 124 116 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">papR</italic>
###xml 213 218 205 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU</italic>
###xml 224 229 216 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 365 382 357 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nprB, nheAB, plcB</italic>
###xml 455 456 447 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 553 558 545 550 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hblC </italic>
###xml 629 635 617 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
Eight of these genes showed a reduced expression in DeltayvfTU, including plcR and its activating peptide encoding gene papR. Relative real-time quantitative PCR (qRT-PCR) was performed and confirmed that in DeltayvfTU, the plcR mRNA levels represented 0.48 fold (mean of 3 independent experiments, range 0.31-0.60) of the mRNA levels in the WT. Some genes such as nprB, nheAB, plcB, encoding toxins or degradative enzymes, were also downregulated (Table 2). In contrast, the three genes encoding the haemolysin BL were overexpressed in the mutant. The hblC mRNA levels in qRT-PCR were 2.49 fold (range 2.39-2.60) higher in DeltayvfTU than in the WT and confirmed the results of the transciptomic analysis.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 28 34 24 30 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 74 80 70 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 146 147 142 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 154 159 150 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 289 295 281 287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 321 326 313 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
Complementation of the DeltayvfTU strain was performed by introducing the yvfTU genes under the control of their own promoter on a plasmid (Table 1). The plcR mRNA level was quantified in the resulting strain harvested 2 hours after the onset of stationary phase. In the complemented DeltayvfTU strain, the mRNA level of plcR was restored: it was 1.4 fold higher than in the WT strain and 1.7 fold higher than in the WT strain harbouring the control plasmid (pHT304).
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 155 161 151 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 349 351 345 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
Because most virulence factors belonging to the PlcR regulon are extracellular proteins, the extracellular proteome from 4 cultures of each of WT and DeltayvfTU strains was also analyzed. Identification of proteins on gels after a two-dimensional electrophoresis was based on previous spots annotations performed in similar experimental conditions [29]. For confirmation, 8 spots were analysed by Mass-Spectrometry.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 316 317 316 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 521 527 521 527 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 611 613 611 613 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P </italic>
###xml 697 702 697 702 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcB </italic>
###xml 830 835 826 831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU</italic>

Using the software ImageMaster Platinum, spot volume quantification was performed after normalization and a Kruskal-Wallis (KW) test was applied to detect reproducible differences between the extracellular proteomes of the two strains. Some differences were observed for spots corresponding to NprB and PC-PLC (Fig. 5). The three spots corresponding to three isoforms of NprB were found in a significant higher amount in the extracellular proteome of the WT (mean 7.09% vol, range 5.28-9.57) when compared to that of the yvfTU mutant (mean 3.31% vol, range 1.54-5.45) (differences in the KW test significant at P = 0.02). Similarly, the mean % vol of spots corresponding to PC-PLC (encoded by the plcB gene) represented 4.24 (range 3.22-6.16)% vol and 1.26 (range 0.16-2.89)% vol in the extracellular proteomes of the WT and DeltayvfTU, respectively (difference significant in KW test at P = 0.05).
###end p 48

###xml 198 204 194 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 286 288 282 284 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P </italic>

###begin p 51
###xml 237 243 237 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
Apart from those differences, the extracellular proteomes of both strains were similar. In particular, the amount of the other extracellular proteins known to be expressed in a PlcR-dependent manner was not significantly affected by the yvfTU mutation, confirming the small number of changes in expression of the PlcR regulon observed by the transcriptomic analysis.

###begin title 52
Role of YvfTU in some virulence features
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 17 23 17 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 49 69 49 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Galleria mellonella </italic>
###xml 143 149 143 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 179 193 179 193 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G. mellonella </italic>
###xml 49 68 <span type="species:ncbi:7137">Galleria mellonella</span>
###xml 179 192 <span type="species:ncbi:7137">G. mellonella</span>
Virulence of the yvfTU mutant was estimated in a Galleria mellonella infection model. Injection of various doses (200 to 20,000 cfu) of WT and yvfTU mutant was performed into the G. mellonella hemocoel (blood of the insect).
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 43 46 43 46 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50 </sub>

###xml 128 130 128 130 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sup>
###xml 166 168 166 168 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sup>
###xml 178 179 178 179 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 202 208 198 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 221 224 217 220 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50 </sub>
###xml 236 238 232 234 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sup>
###xml 274 276 270 272 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sup>
###xml 286 287 282 283 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 348 351 344 347 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50 </sub>
###xml 380 382 376 378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P </italic>
###xml 391 393 387 389 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sup>
###xml 494 500 486 492 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 554 560 542 548 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
By using a Probit analysis software, the LD50 at 24 hours post infection were estimated. For the wild type the LD50 was 2.5 x 103 (95% confidence limits from 1.8 x 103 - 3.6 x 103) CFU and for the DeltayvfTU mutant the LD50 was 4.2 x 103 (95% confidence limits from 3.2 x 103 - 5.8 x 103) CFU. Test of parallelism and the virulence ratios at the LD50 level showed a significant ((P < 0.05, X2 test) but small decrease (1.6 fold with (1.3-2.1, 95% confidence limits)) in virulence with the DeltayvfTU mutant compared to the WT. No difference between DeltayvfTU and the WT in time-to-death was noticed (data not shown).

###xml 41 47 37 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 14 19 <span type="species:ncbi:9940">sheep</span>
When grown on sheep blood agar, the DeltayvfTU and the WT strains displayed an identical hemolysis phenotype. The two strains did not show significant difference in cytotoxicity on HeLa cells and on macrophages (data not shown).

###begin title 56
Discussion
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 22 38 22 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus yvfTU </italic>
###xml 63 72 63 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacillus </italic>
###xml 320 330 320 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 349 355 349 355 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 425 429 425 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR</italic>
###xml 527 532 527 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 559 565 559 565 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 628 634 621 627 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 646 656 639 649 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR'lacZ </italic>
###xml 707 712 700 705 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 773 778 766 771 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 22 31 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
###xml 320 335 <span type="species:ncbi:86661">B. cereus group</span>
Some orthologs of the B. cereus yvfTU genes are found in other Bacillus species. However, in none of these organisms the YvfTU function has been studied. Although the exact function of many TCS is unknown, the TCS encoding genes are often located in the same chromosomal region as the genes that they control. Among the B. cereus group members, the yvfTU genes are highly conserved, and are always located in the vicinity of plcR. Because of this conserved synteny, we hypothesized a link between YvfTU and PlcR. Expression of plcR was therefore studied in a yvfTU mutant. The lower beta-galactosidase activity measured in DeltayvfTU harboring a plcR'lacZ transcriptional fusion revealed a transcription of plcR partly depending on YvfTU. This dependence between YvfTU and plcR expression was confirmed by transcriptome analysis and quantitative real-time PCR.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 26 31 26 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 51 57 51 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 214 220 210 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 316 318 312 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 419 420 415 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 495 501 491 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 533 538 529 534 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 605 610 601 606 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 623 628 615 620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU</italic>
###xml 757 763 749 755 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
Because the expression of plcR was affected by the yvfTU mutation, we wondered about a consequently modified expression of the PlcR regulon. The transcriptome and the extracellular proteome of both the WT and DeltayvfTU strains were analyzed, at the time of maximum protein concentration in the culture supernatant [30] and when 80% of the total amount of the produced proteins is expressed in a PlcR-dependent manner [4] (i.e. two hours after the onset of the stationary phase). At this stage, yvfTU genes were transcribed, and the plcR transcription was partly YvfTU-dependent. Despite a lower level of plcR mRNA in DeltayvfTU, the transcriptomic and proteomic analysis both revealed that the expression of the PlcR regulon was mostly not affected by the yvfTU mutation. This suggests that PlcR can play its regulatory role even when expressed at a low level.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 51 56 51 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 82 88 82 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 249 254 245 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU</italic>
###xml 314 318 310 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hbl </italic>
###xml 346 352 338 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 358 363 350 355 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 400 404 392 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hbl </italic>
###xml 457 458 449 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 459 460 451 452 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 619 622 611 614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hbl</italic>
###xml 642 647 634 639 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 681 683 673 675 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 769 773 761 765 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hbl </italic>
###xml 829 839 821 831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 875 877 867 869 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 878 880 870 872 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 1031 1040 1023 1032 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus</italic>
###xml 1126 1128 1118 1120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 1204 1208 1196 1200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">hbl </italic>
###xml 829 838 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
###xml 1031 1040 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
However, our results revealed that the decrease in plcR mRNA levels caused by the yvfTU mutation was concomitant to a differential expression at the transcriptional level of 11 PlcR-regulated genes, with 8 genes showing a reduced expression in DeltayvfTU. An increase in expression of the 3 genes belonging to the hbl operon was observed in DeltayvfTU where plcR is expressed at a lower level, while hbl is known to be expressed in a PlcR-dependent manner [3,4]. Although performed in different growth conditions, such a lack of congruence between the trends of transcription of several PlcR-regulated genes (including hbl) and expression of plcR itself has been previously shown [31]. Furthermore, the recent discovery of the involvement of another regulator (Fnr) in hbl expression emphasizes the fine regulations occurring in B. cereus in addition to the PlcR regulation [32,33]. The differential response of several genes among a particular regulon has been observed many times elsewhere, and it was recently suggested that in B. cereus, some diversity in the regulation of gene expression occurs within the PlcR regulon [30]. All these findings, coupled with our results, suggest that expression of hbl and some other members of the PlcR regulon can undergo subtle regulations which also involve yet unidentified factors, different from PlcR.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 82 87 78 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU</italic>
###xml 703 705 699 701 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 706 708 702 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
For several genes showing reduced expression at the transcriptional level in DeltayvfTU, a concomitant decrease in the amount of their products was detected by 2D gel analysis. In some cases, differential expression was only observed with the transcriptomic approach (for example, the Cereolysin O encoding gene). The relatively low levels of differential expression observed with the transcriptome analysis suggest that the proteomic approach was less sensitive in our conditions. Furthermore, various post-transcriptional regulations might explain the lack of correlation between transcriptomic and proteomic approaches, as reported in several studies on other organisms (for example, see references [34-37]).
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 78 80 78 80 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 119 125 115 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 132 146 128 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G. mellonella </italic>
###xml 231 235 227 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nprB</italic>
###xml 237 242 233 238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcB </italic>
###xml 246 252 242 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nheAB </italic>
###xml 344 350 336 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 469 475 457 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 581 591 569 579 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 609 610 597 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 132 145 <span type="species:ncbi:7137">G. mellonella</span>
###xml 581 590 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
In bacterial pathogens, several TCS mutants present attenuation in virulence [38]. In our study, the virulence of DeltayvfTU in the G. mellonella insect was also slightly lower than that of the WT strain. The reduced expression of nprB, plcB and nheAB coupled with the lower amount of their products found in the extracellular proteome of DeltayvfTU may account for this reduction in virulence. However, this only slightly impaired virulence is not surprising, as DeltayvfTU did not present a major alteration of the PlcR regulon, and considering the importance of this regulon in B. cereus insect virulence [6].
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 49 54 49 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 79 85 79 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 157 158 157 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 499 500 499 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
Expression of chromosomal genes not regulated by plcR was also affected by the yvfTU mutation, as observed with the transcriptomic analysis (additional file 2). Among them, 29 and 25 genes showed respectively higher and reduced expression by 2 fold or more. However, no major pathway was over-represented in this gene list, giving no clue for the identification of a signal triggering the activation of the YvfTU TCS. An alignment of 1,000 bp promoter regions of the genes listed in additional file 2 was performed using the MEME programme. However, no conserved sequence was identified, suggesting that no highly conserved regulatory motif was responsible for an YvfTU-dependent differential expression in these promoter regions.
###end p 62
###begin title 63
Conclusion
###end title 63
###begin p 64
###xml 137 142 137 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 168 173 168 173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">plcR </italic>
###xml 201 207 197 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 306 316 302 312 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 395 405 391 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 306 315 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
###xml 395 404 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
This work showed that YvfTU and PlcR are genetically linked. A functional link was also shown by the identification of a YvfTU dependent plcR expression. The decreased plcR expression observed in DeltayvfTU only slightly modified the expression of the PlcR regulon, and slightly decreased the virulence of B. cereus in an insect model. A subtle regulation for some virulence factors produced by B. cereus exists in addition to the PlcR regulation.
###end p 64

###begin title 92
Insects and infection experiments
###end title 92
###begin p 93
###xml 36 42 36 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 95 105 95 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. cereus </italic>
###xml 117 123 113 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yvfTU </italic>
###xml 148 151 144 147 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">th </sup>
###xml 194 214 190 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Galleria mellonella </italic>
###xml 222 235 218 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G. melonella </italic>
###xml 337 351 327 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">G. mellonella </italic>
###xml 562 566 552 556 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>
###xml 95 104 <span type="species:ncbi:1396">B. cereus</span>
###xml 194 213 <span type="species:ncbi:7137">Galleria mellonella</span>
###xml 337 350 <span type="species:ncbi:7137">G. mellonella</span>
The virulence-related properties of yvfTU were assessed by comparing the killing effect of the B. cereus WT and DeltayvfTU strains by infection in 5th instar (last larval stage before pupation) Galleria mellonella larvae. G. melonella eggs were hatched at 25degreesC and the larvae reared on beeswax and pollen (Naturalim). Groups of 20 G. mellonella larvae, each weighing about 200 mg, were used. Direct injection into the hemocoel was performed with various doses (ranging from 200 to 20,000 cfu) of vegetative bacteria, collected during exponential growth (OD600 = 1). Tests were run 5 times with 4-5 different doses per test.
###end p 93
###begin p 94
###xml 109 112 103 106 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50s</sub>
###xml 179 181 173 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 182 184 176 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 475 477 469 471 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sup>
###xml 481 482 475 476 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sup>
###xml 545 547 539 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P </italic>
Infected larvae were kept at 37degreesC and mortality was recorded at 24 H and 48 h. The 50% lethal doses (LD50s) values were estimated by Probit analysis at 24 H post infection [55,56]. The Probit program (Praxeme, France) tests for the linearity of dose-mortality curves, provides lethal doses and the slope of each dose-mortality line. It tests the parallelism of 2 or more dose-mortality lines and determines the virulence ratio between the bacterial strains. Using the X2 (Chi2) test, the ratio is considered to be significantly different (P < 0.05) when the confidence limits do not include the value 1.
