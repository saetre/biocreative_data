###begin article-title 0
Imprinting regulates mammalian snoRNA-encoding chromatin decondensation and neuronal nucleolar size
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 257 262 257 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SNRPN</italic>
###xml 271 276 271 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">UBE3A</italic>
###xml 296 300 296 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GTL2</italic>
###xml 996 1007 996 1007 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Snrpn&#8211;Ube3a</italic>
###xml 657 662 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 667 672 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 894 899 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1076 1081 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1104 1109 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1355 1360 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Imprinting, non-coding RNA and chromatin organization are modes of epigenetic regulation that modulate gene expression and are necessary for mammalian neurodevelopment. The only two known mammalian clusters of genes encoding small nucleolar RNAs (snoRNAs), SNRPN through UBE3A(15q11-q13/7qC) and GTL2(14q32.2/12qF1), are neuronally expressed, localized to imprinted loci and involved in at least five neurodevelopmental disorders. Deficiency of the paternal 15q11-q13 snoRNA HBII-85 locus is necessary to cause the neurodevelopmental disorder Prader-Willi syndrome (PWS). Here we show epigenetically regulated chromatin decondensation at snoRNA clusters in human and mouse brain. An 8-fold allele-specific decondensation of snoRNA chromatin was developmentally regulated specifically in maturing neurons, correlating with HBII-85 nucleolar accumulation and increased nucleolar size. Reciprocal mouse models revealed a genetic and epigenetic requirement of the 35 kb imprinting center (IC) at the Snrpn-Ube3a locus for transcriptionally regulated chromatin decondensation. PWS human brain and IC deletion mouse Purkinje neurons showed significantly decreased nucleolar size, demonstrating the essential role of the 15q11-q13 HBII-85 locus in neuronal nucleolar maturation. These results are relevant to understanding the molecular pathogenesis of multiple human neurodevelopmental disorders, including PWS and some causes of autism.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
INTRODUCTION
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 472 473 472 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C1">1</xref>
###xml 550 551 550 551 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C2">2</xref>
###xml 552 553 552 553 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C3">3</xref>
###xml 607 608 607 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C4">4</xref>
###xml 715 716 715 716 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C5">5</xref>
###xml 797 798 797 798 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C6">6</xref>
Epigenetics encompasses multiple mechanisms that temporally and spatially control gene expression throughout development without alteration to the DNA sequence. Non-coding RNAs (ncRNAs), parental imprinting and chromatin organization are among epigenetic mechanisms that contribute to the complexity of gene regulation critical for proper mammalian neurodevelopment. ncRNAs modulate transcription and translation, are found abundantly and sometimes specifically in brain (1) and play pivotal roles in neurodevelopment and long-term memory formation (2,3); 30% of parentally imprinted transcripts are ncRNA (4). Chromatin organization contributes to the spatial context in which genes are developmentally regulated (5), particularly in the highly ordered chromatin of post-mitotic neuronal nuclei (6).
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 301 302 301 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C7">7</xref>
###xml 303 304 303 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C8">8</xref>
###xml 369 370 369 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C9">9</xref>
###xml 371 373 371 373 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C11">11</xref>
###xml 541 543 541 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C12">12</xref>
###xml 544 546 544 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C13">13</xref>
###xml 661 663 661 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C14">14</xref>
###xml 664 666 664 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C16">16</xref>
###xml 28 34 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 53 59 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
Chromosomal loci 15q11-q13 (murine 7qC) and 14q32.2 (murine 12qF1) are imprinted regions directly involved in at least five neurodevelopmental disorders. Prader-Willi syndrome (PWS) is caused by paternal 15q11-q13 deficiency, whereas Angelman syndrome (AS) is caused by 15q11-q13 maternal deficiency (7,8); and 1-3% of autism cases are caused by 15q11-q13 duplication (9-11). Paternal and maternal uniparental disomy (UPD) for chromosome 14 causes two distinct neurodevelopmental disorders, the latter of which phenotypically resembles PWS (12,13). Both loci contain developmentally regulated neuron-specific transcripts regulated by an imprinting center (IC) (14-16).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 198 203 198 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SNRPN</italic>

###xml 553 555 553 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C24">24</xref>
###xml 756 758 756 758 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C25">25</xref>
###xml 759 761 759 761 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C27">27</xref>
###xml 873 875 873 875 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C14">14</xref>
###xml 876 878 876 878 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C16">16</xref>
###xml 1031 1033 1031 1033 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C28">28</xref>
Paternal 15q11-q13/7qC expresses a neuron-specific polycistronic transcript containing at least 148 exons over several hundred kilobases that includes the coding sequence for a splicesomal protein (SNRPN), two clusters of small nucleolar RNAs (snoRNAs) (HB/MBII-85,52), four single copy snoRNAs (HBII-436,13, 438a and 438b) and the antisense transcript to a maternally expressed ubiquitin ligase (UBE3A-AS) (17-20) diagrammed in Figure 1D. Maternal 15q11-q13/7qC is comparatively inactive, but expresses UBE3A and ATP10A in the opposite orientation (21-24). 15q11-q13 transcriptional regulation is highly complex, involving multiple allele-specific epigenetic marks, including DNA methylation, histone modification patterns and DNase hypersensitive sites (25-27). Maternal 14q32.2/12qF1 expresses two clusters of snoRNAs (14qI and 14qII) and over forty microRNAs (miRNAs) (14,16). Interestingly, the 15q and 14q tandemly repeated C/D box snoRNA gene clusters are subject to parental imprinting and are unique to eutherian mammals (28).

###xml 100 102 100 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C29">29</xref>
###xml 381 383 381 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C30">30</xref>
###xml 384 386 384 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C31">31</xref>
###xml 463 465 463 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C32">32</xref>
###xml 535 537 535 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C33">33</xref>
###xml 538 540 538 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C42">42</xref>
###xml 675 677 675 677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C34">34</xref>
###xml 678 680 678 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C35">35</xref>
###xml 543 548 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
###xml 802 807 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
SnoRNAs are ncRNAs localized to the nucleolus and generally involved in guiding rRNA modifications (29). The tandemly repeated C/D Box snoRNA clusters within 15q11-q13 and 14q32.2 have greater homology to one another than to any other mammalian snoRNAs and have minimal rRNA homology; however, three snoRNAs from the 15q11-q13 locus have recently been shown to have rRNA homology (30,31). HBII-52 has been shown to regulate splicing of a serotonin receptor gene (32), whereas HBII-85 deficiency appears to be the primary cause of PWS (33,42). Mouse models of MBII-85 deficiency also recapitulate some features of PWS, including hyperphagia and post-natal growth retardation (34,35). The mechanism by which HBII-85 deficiency causes neurodevelopmental abnormalities in PWS and PWS-like abnormalities in mouse models is currently unknown.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 33 38 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 43 48 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Here we provide evidence in both human and mouse brain that post-natal neurons undergo orchestrated chromatin decondensation specifically at two imprinted snoRNA clusters but not other highly transcribed, imprinted or ncRNA containing loci. Chromatin decondensation of imprinted snoRNAs was neuron-specific, developmentally regulated and transcriptionally controlled through the IC. Importantly, deficiency of HB/MBII-85,52 snoRNAs significantly impacted nucleolar size, suggesting that one essential function of the neuronally transcribed snoRNAs is to modify rRNA, thereby increasing nucleolar size during neuronal maturation.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
RESULTS
###end title 9
###begin title 10
###xml 126 131 126 131 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SNRPN</italic>
###xml 140 145 140 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">UBE3A</italic>
###xml 21 26 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 31 36 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
DNA FISH analysis of human and mouse brain sections reveals paternal-specific and neuron-specific chromatin decondensation at SNRPN through UBE3A
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 176 181 164 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SNRPN</italic>
###xml 190 195 178 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">UBE3A</italic>
###xml 406 407 394 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="DDP373F1">1</xref>
###xml 639 640 627 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="DDP373F1">1</xref>
###xml 231 236 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
To visualize the organization of an imprinted snoRNA cluster in brain, a approximately660 kb DNA FISH probe corresponding to the large paternal neuron-specific transcript from SNRPN through UBE3A within 15q11-q13 was hybridized to human brain, revealing dramatically different signals within neuronal nuclei. Individual neuronal nuclei showed one small compact signal and another strikingly extended (Fig. 1A), indicating differential chromatin organization for the two parental alleles. PWS and AS brain samples revealed that the extended signal was of paternal origin, appearing consistently in AS neurons but never in PWS neurons (Fig. 1B and C).
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 149 150 149 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="DDP373F1">1</xref>
###xml 223 228 223 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Snrpn</italic>
###xml 237 242 237 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Ube3a</italic>
###xml 334 335 322 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="DDP373F1">1</xref>
###xml 489 490 477 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="DDP373F1">1</xref>
###xml 602 603 590 591 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="DDP373F1">1</xref>
###xml 116 122 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 180 185 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
To further investigate the allele-specific chromatin differences in an experimentally tractable system, a series of murine 7qC DNA FISH probes (Fig. 1D, ) were hybridized to adult mouse sagittal brain sections. Probes from Snrpn through Ube3a (approximately888 kb) revealed allele-specific extended signals throughout the brain (Fig. 1E and H, red fluorescence). Extended paternal signals were specific to neurons, as glia always exhibited two compact, relatively equivalent signals (Fig. 1G), a FISH pattern that was consistently observed in nuclei within adult liver, thymus, spleen and kidney (Fig. 1H and data not shown). The possibility of a brain specific copy number change was ruled out by qPCR analyses of brain versus liver genomic DNA (). Furthermore, extensive RNase A treatment did not change the hybridization signals (), indicating that the extended signals were due to DNA:DNA rather than DNA:RNA hybridization. Tissue digestion with DNase I resulted in degradation of the extended paternal signal more rapidly than the compact maternal signal, indicating that the differential signals seen were due to DNA decondensation and decreased nucleosome coverage ().
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 92 103 92 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Snrpn&#8211;Ube3a</italic>
###xml 125 136 125 136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Snrpn&#8211;Ube3a</italic>
###xml 967 972 855 860 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Stmn4</italic>
Over 90% of post-natal day 70 (P70) cortical neuronal nuclei presented an extended paternal Snrpn-Ube3a signal. The paternal Snrpn-Ube3a signal averaged 4.08 microm in length, approximately eight times greater in length than their compact maternal signal counterpart which averaged 0.49 microm. To investigate whether this highly differential, evolutionarily conserved, chromatin decondensation was specific to the Snrpn-Ube3a locus or to a larger group of mammalian loci in neurons, we created a panel of DNA FISH probes for murine loci with similar characteristics to Snrpn-Ube3a (Table 1). This included several imprinted loci (Dlx5/Dlx6, Igf2/H19, Ndn/Mkrn3/Magel2), an imprinted snoRNA cluster (Gtl2), a non-imprinted locus containing a single snoRNA (Htr2c), two loci containing large ncRNAs (Xic and Igf2r/Air), and one containing a large coding transcript (Dmd). Probes were hybridized to adult mouse brain sections and all showed small relatively equally sized signals throughout the brain (Fig. 4B), except Gtl2 (Fig. 4A). The approximately244 kb Gtl2 DNA FISH probe encompassed the maternally expressed snoRNA clusters and multiple miRNAs (Fig. 4C) and also displayed signals similar to Snrpn-Ube3a in mature neuronal nuclei with one small compact signal (presumably the inactive paternal allele) and one decondensed signal (presumably the snoRNA expressing maternal allele). As with Snrpn-Ube3a, the decondensed signal was specific to neuronal nuclei, as it was not observed in glia (Fig. 4A, right nucleus). DNA FISH probes flanking Gtl2 revealed only two small equally sized signals (data not shown). These results implicate transcription of the snoRNA clusters as the driving factor for this high level of chromatin decondensation and not merely imprinting, transcription of ncRNA, nor transcription of a long transcript.

To understand the importance of snoRNA cluster chromatin decondensation and transcription to nucleolar maturation of post-natal neurons, we tested the requirement for Snrpn-Ube3a snoRNAs in nucleolar maturation in +/PWS-ICdel35 kb and PWS-ICHs/+ mouse cerebellum. The nucleoli of Purkinje neurons were measured as they are an easily identifiable subset of neurons within the cerebellum that predominantly contain only a single nucleolus in adult mice. +/PWS-ICdel35 kb Purkinje nuclei, deficient for MBII-85/52 expression, had significantly smaller nucleoli than those of wt littermates (Fig. 4D), whereas PWS-ICHs/+ Purkinje nuclei, which express MBII-85/52 from both alleles, had significantly larger and more numerous nucleoli than those of its wt littermates (Fig. 4E, G and H) indicating a role for MBII-85/52 in nucleolar maturation. These alterations in nucleolar size were recapitulated in human Purkinje nucleoli from PWS individuals which were significantly smaller than age-matched controls (Fig. 4F, I and J). These results demonstrate that transcription from the IC and chromatin decondensation of MBII/HBII snoRNAs plays a significant role in regulating nucleolar size during neuronal maturation.

###begin title 32
DISCUSSION
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 115 117 115 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C38">38</xref>
###xml 118 120 118 120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C39">39</xref>
###xml 475 476 475 476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C5">5</xref>
###xml 477 479 477 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C40">40</xref>
Neuronal chromatin undergoes dynamic changes in compaction and organization during post-natal neuronal maturation (38,39), but little is known about chromatin changes to specific loci. Here we have shown visual evidence for a highly orchestrated chromatin decondensation of essential clusters of snoRNAs during neuronal maturation. Although previous research has shown visual evidence of transcriptionally induced changes to chromatin at specific loci in non-neuronal cells (5,40), our results appear to represent the most dramatic chromatin decondensation of an endogenous locus currently reported in interphase mammalian nuclei.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 644 646 644 646 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C41">41</xref>
###xml 871 873 871 873 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C33">33</xref>
###xml 874 876 874 876 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C42">42</xref>
We have shown that allele- and neuron-specific chromatin decondensation of imprinted snoRNA loci correlates with and is required for increased nucleolar size during neuronal maturation. Why this high level of allele-specific chromatin decondensation appears limited to the only two imprinted snoRNA cluster loci is presumably due to high expression of large clusters of snoRNAs and specific events required for snoRNA processing and biogenesis. Two proteins known to associate with biogenesis of C/D box snoRNAs, Tip49a and Tip49b, are involved in an ATP-dependent chromatin remodeling complex which catalyzes ATP-dependent nucleosome sliding (41). Perhaps these or other snoRNA processing factors are brought to the site of transcription to process snoRNAs and remodel the surrounding chromatin. Lack of one of these snoRNA clusters (HBII-85) is necessary to cause PWS (33,42), yet little is known of the function of these snoRNAs within neuronal nuclei. Our results suggest that one function of neuron-specific snoRNAs may be to modify rRNA, thereby increasing nucleolar size during neuronal maturation.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 232 234 232 234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C33">33</xref>
###xml 235 237 235 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C42">42</xref>
###xml 279 290 279 290 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Snprn-Ube3a</italic>
###xml 386 388 386 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C43">43</xref>
###xml 493 498 493 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">MKRN3</italic>
###xml 500 506 500 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">MAGEL2</italic>
###xml 511 514 511 514 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NDN</italic>
###xml 556 558 556 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C44">44</xref>
###xml 318 323 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 411 416 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 417 425 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Recent publications point to the HBII-85/52 snoRNA clusters as being central to the PWS phenotype and 15q duplication syndrome. Two individuals have now been found with partial overlapping HBII-85 deletions and PWS-like phenotypes (33,42), whereas overexpression of MBII-52 in a Snprn-Ube3a locus paternal duplication mouse appears to contribute to social and behavioral abnormalities (43). In addition, recent human patients with and without PWS have suggested an exclusion of upstream genes MKRN3, MAGEL2 and NDN from a causal role in the PWS phenotype (44). Our results showing imprinted snoRNA chromatin decondensation impacting nucleolar size are consistent with the essential role of the HBII-85/52 locus in post-natal maturation of mammalian neurons.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 277 279 277 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C45">45</xref>
###xml 536 538 536 538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C28">28</xref>
###xml 1115 1117 1115 1117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C46">46</xref>
###xml 400 405 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 554 559 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 606 612 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 858 863 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 868 878 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
While individual snoRNA genes are found in all eukaryotic genomes, snoRNA gene organization and regulation has changed considerably throughout evolution, favoring the colonization of introns to exploit host gene promoters to transcribe multiple snoRNAs from a single promoter (45). The presence of two eutherian-specific C/D snoRNA gene clusters at two evolutionarily distinct chromosomal locations (human 15q and 14q) has been suggested to be a potential evolutionary link to the recent emergence of parental imprinting of these loci (28). Furthermore, human chromosomes 14 and 15 are both acrocentric in humans, encoding repetitive rRNA genes at their p arms, suggesting a potential evolutionary link to localizing snoRNA genes near their target genes for optimal perinucleolar organization. In a recent comparison of gene neighborhood differences between human and chimpanzee, the 15q11-q13 locus was among the most highly divergent in gene neighborhood, suggesting a recent evolutionary advantage in the primate lineage to altered chromosome neighborhood particularly for genes specifically expressed in brain (46). The neuron specificity of the snoRNA chromatin decondensation observed in our study is consistent with a role for the 15q11-q13 snoRNAs in post-natal brain development, a mammalian occurrence under positive selection in the primate lineage.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 198 203 198 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">trans</italic>
###xml 418 420 418 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C47">47</xref>
###xml 421 423 421 423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C48">48</xref>
###xml 463 468 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Epigenetic regulation of both mammalian snoRNA loci is highly complex, requiring imprinting, ncRNA and chromatin remodeling for properly modulated allele- and neuron-specific expression. Homologous trans interactions between maternal and paternal alleles have been reported for both of these loci adding to the complex epigenetic mechanisms required for proper temporal and spatial transcription of these snoRNA loci (47,48). Our DNA FISH results in a transgenic mouse line harboring two additional transcriptionally active copies of the PWS-IC through MBII-85 at another genetic locus revealed that transcriptional activity alone was insufficient to induce full chromatin decondensation, further implicating the importance of snoRNA cluster genomic neighborhood in chromatin remodeling and decondensation.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 101 103 101 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C49">49</xref>
###xml 159 161 159 161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C50">50</xref>
###xml 162 164 162 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C51">51</xref>
###xml 432 434 432 434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C52">52</xref>
###xml 435 437 435 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C54">54</xref>
###xml 465 467 465 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C54">54</xref>
###xml 468 470 468 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C55">55</xref>
###xml 488 490 488 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C56">56</xref>
###xml 504 506 504 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="DDP373C53">53</xref>
###xml 744 750 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
In the primate lineage, chromosome 15q11-q13 is enriched in segmental low copy repeats or duplicons (49) that predispose it to a series of common breakpoints (50,51). These multiple breakpoints give rise not only to the large deletions and duplications seen in AS, PWS and 15q duplication, but also smaller 15q deletions and duplications distal to the snoRNA cluster found in other neurodevelopmental disorders, including epilepsy (52-54), intellectual disability (54,55), schizophrenia (56) and autism (53). Our results suggest the possibility that imprinted mammalian snoRNA chromatin decondensation during neuronal maturation could exert neighborhood effects on the phenotypic manifestations of copy number variations common to 15q11-q13 in humans.
###end p 38

Primary neurons
###end title 44
###begin p 45
###xml 52 72 52 72 <uri xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xlink:type="simple" xlink:href="http://www.lonza.com">http://www.lonza.com</uri>
###xml 30 35 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>

###begin title 46
DNA FISH
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 277 282 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 308 314 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
DNA isolated from BACs (listed in ) ordered from BacPacResources (Oakland, CA, USA) were confirmed by end sequencing. Extracted BAC DNA was labeled with biotin or digoxigenin by nick translation to create DNA FISH probes. Paraffin-embedded tissue was sectioned at 5 microm for human tissue and 10 microm for murine tissue onto glass slides. Slides were baked overnight at 56degreesC, then placed in four 5 min washes with xylene, then two 5 min washes with 100% ethanol and then 1 h at 95degreesC in antigen retrieval solution (DAKO). Slides were then washed in 0.2x SSC, post-fixed in Histochoice for 90 min and then washed 5 min in 1x PBS. Slides were dehydrated in 70, 90 and 100% ethanol (10 min each) and then dried at 50degreesC. A probe mixture containing 3 microl of each probe and 7 microl LSI/WCP buffer (Vysis, Inc.) was warmed to 37degreesC, then added to the slide, coverslipped and sealed with rubber cement. Probe and tissue were simultaneously denatured at 85degreesC for 3 min on a slide cycler (Hybaid). Slides were incubated overnight at 37degreesC, then washed in 50% formamide/50% 2x SSC thrice for 5 min, 2x SSC for 5 min and 2x SSC/0.1% IGEPAL for 5 min, all at 46degreesC. Slides were dried and 200 microl of blocking solution (warmed to 37degreesC) was added to each slide, coverslipped and incubated for 30 min at 37degreesC; and 200 microL of detection solution with alpha-digoxigenin Rhodamine (1:100) and Avidin-FITC (1:100) was next added to each slide, coverslipped and incubated for 2 h at 37degreesC. Slides were then washed three times in 1xPBS/0.5% Tween. Slides were air dried and mounted with 5 microg/ml DAPI in Vectrashield (Vector Laboratories) and coverslipped. To test for RNA:DNA hybridization, 250 microg/ml RNase A was added either before Histochoice fixation or after overnight hybridization for 30 min at 37degreesC. For DNase I digestion slides were incubated in 1 U/ml of DNase I for 10 min at 37degreesC, then chilled on ice before Histochoice fixation.
###end p 47
###begin title 48
RNA FISH
###end title 48
###begin p 49
###xml 369 391 361 383 <uri xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" xlink:type="simple" xlink:href="http://www.ambion.com/">http://www.ambion.com/</uri>
###xml 180 185 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 211 217 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 604 609 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
A custom linked nucleic acid MBII-85 digoxigenin labeled probe (5'-DIG-ttccgatgagagtggcggtacaga-3') was ordered from Exiqon. Paraffin-embedded tissue was sectioned at 5 microm for human tissue and 10 microm for murine tissue onto glass slides. Slides were treated as for DNA FISH up until probe addition with the exceptions that containers were treated with RNase Zap (http://www.ambion.com/) and DEPC water used to dilute solutions. A probe mixture containing 0.2 microl of the MBII-85 probe and 200 microl of hybridization buffer (50% formamide, 5x SSC, 0.1% Tween, 50 microg/ml heparin, 500 microg/ml yeast RNA) was warmed to 37degreesC, then added to the slide, coverslipped and sealed with rubber cement. Slides were incubated overnight at 57degreesC, then washed in 50% formamide/50% 2x SSC thrice for 30 m, 2x SSC for 5 min and 2x SSC/0.1% IGEPAL for 5 m, all at 57degreesC. Slides were dried and 200 microl of blocking solution (warmed to 37degreesC) was added to each slide, coverslipped and incubated for 30 min at 37degreesC; and 200 microl of detection solution with alpha-digoxigenin Rhodamine (1:100) was next added to each slide, coverslipped and incubated for 2 h at 37degreesC. Slides were then washed three times in 1x PBS/0.5% Tween. Slides were air dried and mounted with 5 microg/ml DAPI in Vectrashield (Vector Laboratories) and coverslipped.
###end p 49
###begin title 50
Immunofluorescence
###end title 50
###begin p 51
Paraffin embedded tissue slides were baked overnight at 56degreesC, then placed in four 5 min washes with xylene, then two 5 min washes with 100% ethanol and then 1 h at 95degreesC in antigen retrieval solution (DAKO). Slides were then washed in 0.2x SSC and then 1:100 dilution of alpha-nucleolin antibody (Abcam) was added and incubated at 37degreesC overnight 200 microl of detection solution with alpha-digoxigenin Rhodamine (1:100) was next added to each slide, coverslipped and incubated for 2 h at 37degreesC. Slides were air dried and mounted with 5 microg/ml DAPI in Vectrashield (Vector Laboratories) and coverslipped.
###end p 51
###begin title 52
Microscopy
###end title 52
###begin p 53
Slides were analyzed on an Axioplan 2 fluorescence microscope (Carl Zeiss, Inc., NY, USA) equipped with a Qimaging Retiga EXi high-speed uncooled digital camera, appropriate fluorescent filter sets and automated xyz stage controls. The microscope and peripherals were controlled by a Macintosh running iVision (Scanalytics, Vienna, VA, USA) software. Images were captured for blue, green and red filters at one edge of the specimen, and then repeated at 0.5-1 microm sections through the depth of the tissue. Each image stack was digitally deconvolved to remove out-of-focus light using HazeBuster software (Vaytek, Fairfield, IA, USA). Following haze removal, image stacks for each fluorophore were merged and stacked to create a two-dimensional image representing all of fluorescence within the section. Measurements were taken in preview mode using a 100x oil objective with a 1x zoom, using a drawing tool set to define each pixel with the appropriate number of microns. All measurements for a given experiment were taken with the same exposure times and microscope settings as was appropriate for the fluorescence intensity. For experiments comparing nucleolar size, the investigator making the measurements was blinded to the tissue identity.
###end p 53
