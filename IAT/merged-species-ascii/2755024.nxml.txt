alpha' Subunit of soybean beta-conglycinin forms complex with rice glutelin via a disulphide bond in transgenic rice seeds*
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
This paper is dedicated to the memory of Professor Utsumi who passed away on 1 December 2008. He leaves a legacy of many important contributions on the biology of seeds and seed storage proteins.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
Present address: Institute of Biological Chemistry, Washington State University, Pullman, Washington 99164-6340, USA.
###end p 2
###begin p 3
###xml 32 39 <span type="species:ncbi:3847">soybean</span>
###xml 75 79 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 154 158 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 204 208 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 1121 1125 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 1472 1476 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
The alpha' and beta subunits of soybean beta-conglycinin were expressed in rice seeds in order to improve the nutritional and physiological properties of rice as a food. The alpha' subunit accumulated in rice seeds at a higher level than the beta subunit, but no detectable difference in mRNA transcription level between subunits was observed. Sequential extraction results indicate that the alpha' subunit formed one or more disulphide bonds with glutelin. Electron microscopic analysis showed that the alpha' subunit and the beta subunit were transported to PB-II together with glutelin. In mature transgenic seeds, the beta subunit accumulated in low electron density regions in the periphery of PB-II, whereas the alpha' subunit accumulated together with glutelin in high-density regions of the periphery. The subcellular localization of mutated alpha' subunits lacking one cysteine residue in the N-terminal mature region (alpha'DeltaCys1) or five cysteine residues in the pro and N-terminal mature regions (alpha'DeltaCys5) were also examined. Low-density regions were formed in PB-II in mature seeds of transgenic rice expressing alpha'DeltaCys 5 and alpha'DeltaCys1. alpha'DeltaCys5 was localized only in the low-density regions, whereas alpha'DeltaCys1 was found in both low- and high-density regions. These results suggest that the alpha' subunit could make a complex via one or more disulphide bonds with glutelin and accumulate together in PB-II of transgenic rice seeds.

###xml 758 764 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>

Rice storage proteins are composed of glutelin (acid/alkaline-soluble), prolamin (alcohol-soluble), globulin (saline-soluble), and albumin (water-soluble). Glutelins, account for about 80% of the total proteins in rice seeds (Ogawa et al., 1987; Li and Okita, 1993; Cagampang et al., 1976). They are synthesized as a precursor which is further processed proteolytically into acidic and basic chains connected with a disulphide bridge like 11S globulin. Rice proteins are rich in sulphur-containing amino acids, but are deficient in lysine. By contrast, beta-conglycinin (7S globulin of soybean) and glycinin (11S globulin of soybean) are rich in lysine but are poor in sulphur-containing amino acids (Utsumi, 1992). Moreover, their physiological benefits to humans have been established. beta-conglycinin lowers plasma cholesterol and triglyceride levels in human (Sirtori et al., 1995; Aoyama et al., 2001). The alpha' subunit of beta-conglycinin has been reported to have LDL-cholesterol-lowering activity (Sirtori and Lovati, 2001) and phagocytosis-stimulating activity (Tsuruki et al., 2003). Therefore, an accumulation of beta-conglycinin in rice seeds could lead to the development of a product with high nutritional value, several important physiological activities, and useful physicochemical properties.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 252 258 238 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 243 264 229 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Maruyama <italic>et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>

###xml 732 760 699 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib36">Yamauchi and Yamagishi, 1979</xref>
###xml 762 774 729 741 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib33">Utsumi, 1992</xref>
###xml 994 1000 950 956 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 989 1006 945 962 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Mori <italic>et al.</italic>, 2004</xref>
beta-Conglycinin has a trimeric structure similar to other 7S globulins. The alpha and alpha' subunits contain an N-terminal extension in addition to a core region common to all the subunits. The beta subunit consists of only the core domain (Maruyama et al., 1998). The extension regions of the alpha and alpha' subunits probably protrude from the molecular surface of the core domains and have a minor role in proper folding and assembly (Maruyama et al., 2001). The alpha/alpha' subunits and the beta subunit are synthesized on polysomes as prepro- and pre-forms, respectively. The signal peptides are co-translationally removed, the polypeptides are N-glycosylated with high-mannose glycans and assemble into trimers in the ER (Yamauchi and Yamagishi, 1979; Utsumi, 1992). The resultant alpha/alpha' subunits and the beta subunit are in the pro-form and the mature form, respectively. Each trimer is transported from the ER to the protein storage vacuoles through the Golgi apparatus (Mori et al., 2004). The pro-regions of the alpha and alpha' subunits are proteolytically processed to give their mature forms. Both the alpha and alpha' subunits contain four cysteine residues (Cys) in their pro-regions and one Cys in the mature extension region (Cys13).
###end p 6

###xml 875 900 875 900 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Krishinan and White, 1995</xref>
###xml 3 7 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
In rice seeds, there are two types of protein bodies, PB-I and PB-II. PB-I, derived directly from the ER, contains mainly prolamin (Tanaka et al., 1980; Yamagata and Tanaka, 1986). Binding protein (BiP) facilitates the folding and assembly of prolamin polypeptides (Li et al., 1993a, b). PB-II, derived from the vacuoles, have non-spherical structures and primarily contain glutelin and globulin (Tanaka et al., 1980). Both proteins are synthesized on the rER, assembled in the ER, transported to the Golgi apparatus, and sorted to the vacuole. Both glutelin and globulin are transported from the Golgi apparatus to storage vacuoles by the so-called dense vesicles (Krishnan et al., 1986, 1992). However, glutelin and globulin accumulate in different regions of PB-II (Krishinan and White, 1995). Globulin accumulates in the periphery region of PB-II (Krishnan et al., 1992; Krishinan and White, 1995).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 11 15 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 108 115 <span type="species:ncbi:3847">soybean</span>
###xml 166 170 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 261 265 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 489 493 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
To develop rice having a high nutritional value and physiological function, the alpha' and beta subunits of soybean beta-conglycinin were introduced and expressed in rice seeds. Further, the traffic and accumulation behaviour of the alpha' and beta subunits in rice seeds were analysed. Our results indicate that the alpha' subunit accumulates at a higher level than the beta subunit and that the alpha' subunit forms a complex with glutelin via one or more disulphide bonds in transgenic rice seeds.

###begin p 23
###xml 264 265 256 257 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 1701 1703 1607 1609 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RQ</italic>
###xml 1774 1776 1680 1682 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RQ</italic>

###xml 581 585 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
Transcription levels of alpha' and beta mRNAs were measured by real-time PCR. This assay was carried out in the ABI-PRISM 7000 (Applied Biosystems) using the Taqman system (Applied Biosystems) in a final volume of 50 mul. The reaction mixture including 5.5 mM MgCl2, 0.3 mM each of dATP, dCTP, and dGTP, 0.8 mM dUTP, 0.2 muM forward and reverse primers (5'-TTGTTTGAGATTACCCCAGAGAAAA-3' and 5'-CCTCGTTCATATCCACAACACTGA-3' for alpha' subunit, 5'-GACTACCGGATTGTCCAGTTTCA-3' and 5'-AATCGGCGTCAGCATGGT-3' for beta subunit, and 5'-CGAGGCGCAGTCCAAGA-3' and 5'-CCCAGTTGCTGACGATACCA-3' for rice actin gene (RAc1) as an internal standard), 12.5 U reverse transcriptase, 20 U RNase inhibitor, 1.25 U DNA polymerase, and 0.1 muM Taqman probe. ###begin title 41
###xml 66 70 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
Comparison of transcription levels of alpha' and beta subunits in rice
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 189 195 175 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 650 651 618 619 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 923 930 873 880 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 1009 1013 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 1101 1105 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 1538 1542 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
A difference in the transcription levels or translational efficiencies of alpha' and beta mRNAs might be a reason for the difference in accumulation levels of the alpha' and beta subunits (Fig. 2). To verify this possibility, the quantities of both mRNAs were compared by real-time PCR. The top lines having a single copy were self-pollinated to obtain homozygous lines. The accumulation levels of the alpha' and beta subunits in homozygous lines were 7.9+/-0.7% and 4.4+/-0.8% in total proteins, respectively (data not shown). In the homozygous line seeds, the alpha' subunit accumulated about twice as much as the beta subunit, similarly with the T1 seeds. The total RNAs from 15 d after-flowering (15 DAF) seeds of alpha'6-2 and beta5-4 homozygous lines were subjected to real-time PCR. Ct values of alpha'6-2 and beta5-4, collected from where the half-height bar crossed each DeltaRN, were 18.9 and 18.5, respectively (Table 1). To correct the experimental error between the alpha' and beta subunits, the rice actin gene RAc1 was used as the internal standard. The Ct values of RAc1 in transgenic rice seeds expressing the alpha' and beta subunits were 28.5 and 28.1, respectively. To compare their transcription levels, the Ct values were normalized. The results indicate that the transcription level of alpha'6-2 was close to that of beta5-4, although the accumulation level of alpha'6-2 was about twice as much as that of beta5-4. Thus, transcription levels do not explain the difference in the accumulation of the two proteins in rice seeds. Translational efficiencies or protein stability may therefore account for the difference in the accumulation level between the alpha' and beta subunit.

Both the alpha' and beta subunits are N-glycosylated with high-mannose type glycans in soybean (Yamauchi and Yamagishi, 1979). To examine whether the alpha' and beta subunits synthesized in rice seeds were N-glycosylated, they were subjected to digestion either by PNGase F (this hydrolyses almost all N-glycans, excluding the core-fucosylated complex N-glycan) or by Endo H (that primarily hydrolyses high-mannose glycan but not complex glycan). As a result of the digestion by PNGase F as well as by Endo H, both the alpha' and beta subunits gave a single band of molecular mass lower than that of the intact subunit (Fig. 5). Thus, both the alpha' and beta subunits were glycosylated with high-mannose N-glycan, and not with complex glycan, indicating that the N-glycan modification of the alpha' and beta subunits in rice is similar to those in soybean seeds (Yamauchi and Yamagishi, 1979). Complex glycan modification of the alpha' and beta subunits does not occur in soybean and rice seeds, although most of them traffic through the Golgi apparatus indicating limited access to modifying enzymes to these proteins in developing seeds.

###end title 70
###begin title 71
###xml 67 71 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
Complex formation of the alpha' subunit and glutelin in transgenic rice seeds
###end title 71
###begin p 72
###xml 305 312 288 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 565 571 537 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig5">Fig. 5</xref>
###xml 698 704 663 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig2">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 815 821 776 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig9">Fig. 9</xref>
###xml 898 905 856 863 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig10">Fig. 10</xref>
###xml 1018 1024 972 978 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig6">Fig. 6</xref>
###xml 1486 1492 1396 1402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig4">Fig. 4</xref>
###xml 1656 1662 1547 1553 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig8">Fig. 8</xref>
###xml 1946 1952 1833 1839 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1938 1958 1825 1845 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Arcalis <italic>et al.</italic>, 2004</xref>
###xml 1970 1976 1857 1863 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1960 1982 1847 1869 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">Drakakaki <italic>et al.</italic>, 2006</xref>
###xml 40 47 <span type="species:ncbi:3847">soybean</span>
###xml 119 123 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 260 264 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 411 415 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 442 449 <span type="species:ncbi:3847">soybean</span>
###xml 629 633 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 1305 1309 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 1777 1781 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
In this study, alpha' and beta cDNAs of soybean beta-conglycinin driven by glutelin promoters were introduced into the rice genome to investigate their accumulation behaviour. Transcription levels of the alpha' and beta cDNAs in developing seeds of transgenic rice were found to be similar to each other (Table 1). Further, both alpha' and beta subunits were shown to undergo post-translational modification in rice seeds similar to those in soybean seeds (N-glycosylation by high-mannose glycans and detachment of N-terminal pro-region from the alpha' precursor) (Fig. 5). The accumulation level of the alpha' subunit in mature rice seeds was about two-times higher than that of the beta subunit (Fig. 2). The alpha' subunit and glutelin co-localized in high electron density regions in PB-II in developing seeds (Fig. 9), whereas the beta subunit localized only in a low electron density region (Fig. 10). A sequential extraction experiment showed that the alpha' subunit could form a disulphide bond with glutelin (Fig. 6). Further, the behaviour of two kinds of modified alpha' subunit (alpha'DeltaCys5 devoid of all Cys by means of removal of its pro-region and substitution of Cys13 with serine, and alpha'DeltaCys1 containing an intact pro-region and substituted Cys13) were examined in transgenic rice seeds to elucidate the role of Cys residues in the accumulation of the alpha' subunit. Both alpha'DeltaCys5 and alpha'DeltaCys1, similar to the alpha' subunit, formed trimers (Fig. 4). The alpha'DeltaCys5 localized in low-density regions in PB-II similarly to the beta subunit, whereas alpha'DeltaCys1 localized in low- and high-density regions (Fig. 8). These results suggest that the alpha' subunit makes a complex with glutelin via one or more disulphide bonds in rice seeds. Previous reports suggest that there could be a 'dominant' effect of the storage proteins in directing foreign proteins to the vacuole in seed cells (Arcalis et al., 2004; Drakakaki et al., 2006). Stabilization by disulphide bonds might contribute a tendency for heterotypic interactions of storage proteins.

###begin title 73
###xml 35 39 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
Trafficking of beta-conglycinin in rice seed
###end title 73
###begin p 74
###xml 115 121 115 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 105 127 105 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib30">Takahashi <italic>et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>
###xml 171 177 171 177 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 156 183 156 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Hara-Nishimura <italic>et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>
###xml 93 97 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 231 235 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 319 323 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 524 528 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 715 719 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
Although PAC-like vesicles were observed in late developing stage (15 DAF) of non-transgenic rice seeds (Takahashi et al., 2005), similar to pumpkin seeds (Hara-Nishimura et al., 1998), they were not observed in the non-transgenic rice of this study (10 DAF). This suggests that the traffic ability of early developing rice seed is sufficient to transport storage proteins from the ER to the vacuoles through the Golgi apparatus. However, PAC-like vesicles were observed in the early developing stage (10 DAF) of transgenic rice seeds expressing the beta subunit, although the accumulation level of the beta subunit was lower than that of the alpha' subunit. This suggests that introduction of the beta gene in the rice induced the PAC-like vesicle formation in the early developing stage.

###begin p 75
###xml 229 240 229 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Vigna mungo</italic>
###xml 250 261 250 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 392 403 392 403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 475 486 475 486 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 502 508 502 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 494 514 494 514 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">Okamoto <italic>et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>
###xml 596 614 593 611 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Phaseolus vulgaris</italic>

###xml 1165 1171 1153 1159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 1157 1177 1145 1165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Arcalis <italic>et al.</italic>, 2004</xref>

###xml 1797 1814 1782 1799 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Mori <italic>et al.</italic>, 2004</xref>
###xml 2100 2106 2082 2088 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 2096 2112 2078 2094 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib12">Kim <italic>et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>
###xml 2239 2245 2213 2219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 2230 2251 2204 2225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Maruyama <italic>et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>
###xml 2253 2257 2227 2231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">2002</xref>
###xml 2744 2755 2695 2706 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 2772 2778 2723 2729 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 2763 2784 2714 2735 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Maruyama <italic>et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>
###xml 229 240 <span type="species:ncbi:3915">Vigna mungo</span>
###xml 250 261 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis</span>
###xml 392 403 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis</span>
###xml 475 486 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis</span>
###xml 583 594 <span type="species:ncbi:3885">French bean</span>
###xml 596 614 <span type="species:ncbi:3885">Phaseolus vulgaris</span>
###xml 637 644 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
###xml 948 953 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1050 1055 <span type="species:ncbi:4565">wheat</span>
###xml 1577 1584 <span type="species:ncbi:3847">soybean</span>
###xml 1896 1900 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 2349 2353 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
###xml 2732 2739 <span type="species:ncbi:3847">soybean</span>
###xml 2744 2755 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis</span>
###xml 2915 2919 <span type="species:ncbi:4530">rice</span>
There have been reports on the direct pathway from the ER to the vacuole, introduced by transgenes. Introduction of a gene of sulphhydryl-endopeptidase (SH-EP) which has a KDEL-tail, papain-type vacuolar proteinase of germinated Vigna mungo seeds in Arabidopsis resulted in the formation of KDEL-vesicle, transported to the vacuoles by a Golgi-independent. Such vesicles were not observed in Arabidopsis seeds expressing SH-EP mutant lacking the KDEL-tail and non-transgenic Arabidopsis seeds (Okamoto et al., 2003). When a gene for KDEL-tagged beta-phaseolin, 7S storage protein of French bean (Phaseolus vulgaris), was introduced into tobacco leaf protoplasts, KDEL-tagged beta-phaseolin was transported to the vacuole but the complex glycan was not formed, although normal beta-phaseolin had the complex glycan. These suggest that the KDEL-tagged beta-phaseolin was transported to the vacuoles directly from the ER (Frigerio et al., 2001). When human serum albumin containing N-terminal signal sequence and C-terminal KDEL tag was introduced into wheat and expressed in seeds, it was also transported to the vacuoles through the Golgi-independent route (Arcalis et al., 2004). It is likely that the overexpression of transgenes contained the KDEL-tail coding sequence in the ER caused ER stress, and that the ER stress induced the expression of some kind of molecular chaperons or transmembrane proteins resulting in a Golgi-independent pathway. The beta subunit does not contain the KDEL sequence, but a direct transport pathway from the ER to the vacuoles was induced. In soybean seeds, ER-derived protein bodies (PBs) were observed at high frequency in the mutant line containing glycinin composed of only group I subunits the solubility of which was lower than that of the normal glycinin (Mori et al., 2004). Consequently, there is a possibility that the induction of PAC-like vesicle in rice seeds also depends on physicochemical properties, such as solubility and surface hydrophobicity, of the beta subunit. The pH in the ER is estimated to be approximately 7.1 in resting HeLa cells (Kim et al., 1998). The alpha' subunit is soluble at mu=0.08 and pH 7.1, whereas the beta subunit is insoluble under these conditions (Maruyama et al., 1998, 2002). These findings suggest that the beta subunit is insoluble in the ER lumen environment of rice seed and partly aggregates. By contrast, the alpha' subunit, alpha'DeltaCys1 and alpha'DeltaCys5, containing the hydrophilic domain (extension domain), are soluble in the ER lumen environment, so all of them could be transported to the vacuoles via the Golgi apparatus. Recently, it was shown that the aggregated type of red fluorescent protein forms ER-derived compartments in soybean and Arabidopsis seeds (Maruyama et al., 2008). Application of the aggregated-type of red fluorescent protein could further elucidate a formation of ER-derived compartments in rice seeds.

beta-Conglycinin has many physiological functions. It has been reported that the alpha' subunit decreases plasma cholesterol and triglyceride levels of rat when rats were fed 20 mg (kg-1 body weight d-1) of the alpha' subunit (Duranti et al., 2004). Thus, 1.2 g (60 kg body weight d-1) of alpha' subunit are needed for possible effective physiological functions in human. The maximum accumulation level of the alpha' subunit in total rice seed proteins was about 8%, and rice seed proteins account for 7% of total dry weight of rice seed. If one considers that average daily consumption of rice in Japan is 150 g, which would contain about 0.84 g of alpha', increasing the accumulation levels of alpha' subunit by a factor of 1.5 is necessary to confer physiological functions to rice seeds.
