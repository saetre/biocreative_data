###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 131 143 131 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis </italic>
###xml 148 159 148 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3/IAA17 </italic>
###xml 273 278 273 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
###xml 523 530 523 530 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
The plant hormone auxin exerts many of its effects on growth and development by controlling transcription of downstream genes. The Arabidopsis gene AXR3/IAA17 encodes a member of the Aux/IAA family of auxin responsive transcriptional repressors. Semi-dominant mutations in AXR3 result in an increased amplitude of auxin responses due to hyperstabilisation of the encoded protein. The aim of this study was to identify novel genes involved in auxin signal transduction by screening for second site mutations that modify the axr3-1 gain-of-function phenotype.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 32 61 32 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">partial suppressor of axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 62 68 62 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1</italic>
###xml 132 139 132 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 173 180 173 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-3 </italic>
###xml 245 251 245 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1</italic>
###xml 279 283 279 283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3</italic>
###xml 285 289 285 289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GUS </italic>
###xml 314 321 314 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 349 354 349 354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 375 380 375 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
###xml 399 406 399 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 558 564 558 564 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1</italic>
We present the isolation of the partial suppressor of axr3-1 (pax1-1) mutant, which partially suppresses almost every aspect of the axr3-1 phenotype, and that of the weaker axr3-3 allele. axr3-1 protein turnover does not appear to be altered by pax1-1. However, expression of an AXR3::GUS reporter is reduced in a pax1-1 background, suggesting that PAX1 positively regulates AXR3 transcription. The pax1-1 mutation also affects the phenotypes conferred by stabilising mutations in other Aux/IAA proteins; however, the interactions are more complex than with axr3-1.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 16 21 16 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 47 51 47 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">via </italic>
###xml 66 71 66 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
###xml 110 117 110 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA</italic>
We propose that PAX1 influences auxin response via its effects on AXR3 expression and that it regulates other Aux/IAAs secondarily.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 235 236 235 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 237 238 237 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 354 355 354 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 356 357 356 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 606 608 606 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 636 644 636 644 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
The phytohormone auxin is central to the regulation of plant growth and development. Processes controlled by auxin include apical dominance, adventitious root formation, tropic responses, vascular patterning and root hair development [1-6]. These diverse morphological events are brought about by changes in cell division, expansion and differentiation [7-9], and many of them are mediated by the ability of auxin to control gene expression. Several families of genes containing Auxin Response Elements (AuxREs) in their promoters, are rapidly transcriptionally upregulated as a primary response to auxin [10], including members of the Aux/IAA gene family.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 4 12 4 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 85 87 85 87 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 88 90 88 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 117 129 117 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis </italic>
###xml 249 251 249 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 252 254 252 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 326 328 326 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 425 427 425 427 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 606 608 606 608 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 609 611 609 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 831 833 831 833 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 911 913 911 913 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 914 916 914 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
The Aux/IAA genes form a large multi-gene family found throughout the plant kingdom [11-14]. There are 29 members in Arabidopsis and their expression varies with respect to tissue specificity, auxin induction kinetics and sensitivity to auxin dose [11,15]. Aux/IAA proteins are characterised by four highly conserved domains [11]. C-terminal domains III and IV mediate homo- and hetero-dimerisation between Aux/IAA proteins [16]. Domains III and IV are also found in the Auxin Response Factor (ARF) family of transcription factors, a subset of which activate transcription from AuxRE-containing promoters [17,18]. Conservation of domains III and IV between the Aux/IAAs and ARFs allows combinatorial dimerisation amongst these families. The N-terminal domain I of Aux/IAA proteins functions as a transcriptional repression domain [19]. Thus, dimers between Aux/IAAs and activator ARFs block gene transcription [19-22].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 92 94 92 94 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 95 97 95 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 114 118 114 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">via </italic>
###xml 136 141 136 141 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TIR1 </sup>
###xml 175 179 175 179 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TIR1</sup>
###xml 333 335 333 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 336 338 336 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 416 418 416 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 419 421 419 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 422 424 422 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 644 646 644 646 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 655 663 655 663 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 799 806 799 806 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA</italic>
Most Aux/IAA proteins are extremely unstable, with half-lives ranging from 6 to 80 minutes [23-26]. They interact via domain II with SCFTIR1 (Skp1, Cdc53/cullin, F-box proteinTIR1) and other auxin receptor F-box-containing E3 ubiquitin-protein ligase complexes, which target them for degradation by the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway [25,27]. Auxin promotes their association with such E3s, increasing their turnover [25,27-29]. Thus transcriptional auxin responses are mediated by the auxin-induced destabilisation of Aux/IAA proteins, which relieves associated activator ARFs from repression and in turn, upregulates transcription from AuxREs [30]. Since Aux/IAA genes contain AuxREs in their promoters, this provides a feedback mechanism for regulating their own expression. Furthermore, it allows Aux/IAAs to control each other's expression creating a complex network of cross regulation amongst the family.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 125 133 125 133 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 144 156 144 156 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis </italic>
###xml 157 159 157 159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 160 162 160 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 266 270 266 270 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">TIR1</sup>
###xml 325 335 325 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3/IAA17</italic>
###xml 392 394 392 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 397 404 397 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 408 415 408 415 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-3 </italic>
###xml 685 693 685 693 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 792 794 792 794 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 795 797 795 797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
Dominant or semi-dominant mutations resulting in hyper-stabilisation of individual Aux/IAAs have been isolated in at least 8 Aux/IAA genes from Arabidopsis [31-38]. Such stabilising mutations occur in domain II and act by reducing the affinity of the Aux/IAA for SCFTIR1, conferring pleiotropic auxin related phenotypes. For AXR3/IAA17, two such gain-of-function alleles have been described [39]. axr3-1 and axr3-3 plants show increased apical dominance, increased adventitious root formation, agravitropic roots, no root hairs, and epinastic petioles. These phenotypes are generally consistent with an increase in the magnitude of auxin responses. However, similar mutations in other Aux/IAA genes cause reduced auxin responses in some tissues and increased responses in other tissue types [31-38].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 26 34 26 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 38 42 38 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ARF </italic>
###xml 329 335 329 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1</italic>
###xml 396 401 396 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3 </italic>
###xml 414 421 414 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 541 546 541 546 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 599 607 599 607 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 631 636 631 636 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 653 661 653 661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
Although the study of the Aux/IAA and ARF families has elucidated many of the early events in auxin signal transduction, there is an ongoing requirement to find upstream regulators and novel targets of Aux/IAA and ARF regulated transcription. Here we present the isolation and characterisation of an extragenic recessive mutant, pax1-1, which partially suppresses the phenotypes of semi-dominant axr3 alleles. The pax1-1 single mutant shows pleiotropic phenotypes consistent with altered auxin responses. Double mutant analyses suggest that PAX1 also interacts genetically with other members of the Aux/IAA family. We propose that PAX1 acts upstream of Aux/IAA genes by regulating their transcription.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
Mutant isolation
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 116 120 116 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3</italic>
###xml 152 159 152 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 275 281 275 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gl1-1 </italic>
###xml 538 545 538 545 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 562 564 562 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1c</xref>
###xml 569 571 569 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1d</xref>
###xml 638 645 638 645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 936 938 936 938 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2 </sub>
###xml 976 983 976 983 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 1051 1055 1051 1055 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3</italic>
###xml 1088 1124 1088 1124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">partial suppressor of axr3-1 (pax1-1</italic>
###xml 1172 1179 1172 1179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 1239 1246 1239 1246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
To search for new mutants altered in auxin signalling and more specifically, to find novel genetic interactors with AXR3, we screened x-ray-mutagenised axr3-1 (Columbia) plants for reversion of their strict apical dominance. The mutagenised seed were also homozygous for the gl1-1 mutation (which causes plants to lack leaf trichomes) so that revertant lines could be distinguished from contaminating wild-type plants. One line recovered from the screen showed increased numbers of axillary branches arising from the shoot, compared with axr3-1 controls (Figure 1c and 1d). The dwarf phenotype and dark green, curled leaves caused by the axr3-1 mutation were also partially suppressed in the revertant. In addition, the carpels were relatively longer than the sepals and petals, such that they protruded from the closed flower buds. Revertant plants were outcrossed to wild-type (Columbia) plants and segregation of individuals in the F2 generation morphologically similar to axr3-1 homozygotes indicated that the suppressor mutation is extragenic to axr3. Thus, the new mutant was named partial suppressor of axr3-1 (pax1-1). Segregation analysis also suggested that the pax1-1 mutation is recessive to wild type and that it is linked to axr3-1 (data not shown).
###end p 16
###begin title 17
###xml 28 35 28 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
Morphological phenotypes of pax1-1 seedlings
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 43 47 43 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1</italic>
###xml 76 83 76 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 176 183 176 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 239 246 239 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 372 374 372 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1e</xref>
###xml 379 381 379 381 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1f</xref>
###xml 390 392 390 392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2a</xref>
###xml 495 497 495 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2b</xref>
###xml 547 554 547 554 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 570 572 570 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2c</xref>
###xml 579 586 579 586 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 680 682 680 682 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1e</xref>
###xml 687 689 687 689 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1f</xref>
To gain insight into the wild-type role of PAX1, a detailed analysis of the pax1-1 mutant phenotype in a wild-type genetic background was carried out. Germination is slower in pax1-1 mutants compared with wild-type seeds (data not shown). pax1-1 hypocotyls are longer than wild-type when grown under a long day photoperiod, as a result of an increased growth rate (Figure 1e and 1f, Figure 2a). In contrast, dark grown hypocotyl elongation was not significantly different from wild-type (Figure 2b). However, the rate of root growth is reduced in pax1-1 mutants (Figure 2c), and pax1-1 roots are abnormally straight, indicating that they have a reduced root wave response (Figure 1e and 1f).
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 104 106 104 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1i</xref>
###xml 111 113 111 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1j</xref>
###xml 116 123 116 123 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 216 223 213 220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 291 302 285 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 367 369 361 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 549 556 543 550 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 640 642 634 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1j</xref>
###xml 970 975 964 969 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
pax1-1 plants produce root hairs at a higher density than wild-type and with altered morphology (Figure 1i and 1j). pax1-1 hairs are approximately 34% shorter than wild-type root hairs (wild type = 379 +/- 1.16 mum, pax1-1 = 252 +/- 0.96 mum), and develop in aberrant patterns. In wild-type Arabidopsis, the root epidermis is arranged in longitudinal files of cells [40]. Each file is composed entirely of either hair-forming cells (trichoblasts) or non-hair-forming cells (atrichoblasts), and each trichoblast forms a single root hair. However, in pax1-1 some trichoblasts produce multiple hairs, with up to 5 hairs observed per cell (Fig 1j). These hairs arise from a single initiation site. Thus, the increase in root hair density can be attributed at least partially to the development of multiple hairs from some trichoblasts. Also, some hairs form branches during the rapid tip growth phase of hair elongation, resulting in stalked, branched structures. Therefore PAX1 appears to affect both the orientation and the amount of root hair elongation.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
###xml 18 25 18 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
Auxin response of pax1-1 mutants
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 30 37 30 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 344 346 343 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3a</xref>
###xml 349 356 348 355 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
To test the auxin response of pax1-1 roots, seedlings were transferred to medium containing various concentrations of the natural auxin, indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) and root elongation was measured 5 days later. On increasing concentrations of IAA between 10 nM and 10 muM, wild-type root growth was inhibited in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 3a). pax1-1 root elongation follows a similar dose response to IAA as wild-type.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 9 14 9 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
PAX1 and AXR3 interact genetically
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 45 52 45 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 59 66 59 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 91 98 91 98 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 106 113 106 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>

###xml 284 291 284 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 295 302 295 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-3 </italic>

###xml 410 416 410 416 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1</italic>
###xml 549 556 549 556 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 637 642 637 642 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 707 712 707 712 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3 </italic>

###xml 824 829 824 829 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3 </italic>
###xml 955 957 955 957 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1k</xref>
###xml 964 978 964 978 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 982 996 982 996 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-3 pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 1090 1092 1090 1092 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1k</xref>
###xml 1097 1099 1097 1099 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1l</xref>
To characterise in more detail the effect of pax1-1 on the axr3-1 phenotype, we re-crossed pax1-1 into an axr3-1 background. We also constructed double mutants between pax1-1 and a second, weaker semi-dominant allele, axr3-3, in order to assess the allele specificity of suppression. axr3-1 and axr3-3 show severely reduced root growth compared with wild-type (Figure 4a) [39]. However, in double mutants with pax1-1, root elongation is increased by 32 percent and 23 percent, respectively. This effect is particularly striking considering that the pax1-1 mutation causes a reduction in root length when in a wild-type background. Thus, PAX1 appears to be required for the full inhibition of root growth by axr3 gain-of-function alleles. Other aspects of the root phenotype are also suppressed by pax1-1. In contrast to the axr3 single mutants, where root growth apparently wanders randomly with frequent changes in direction (e.g. kink in root in Figure 1k), the axr3-1 pax1-1 and axr3-3 pax1-1 double mutant roots grow more towards the gravity vector, and produce more root hairs (Figure 1k and 1l).
###end p 23

###xml 138 145 138 145 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 254 256 254 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>

###xml 393 407 393 407 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 470 484 470 484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-3 pax1-1 </italic>
With respect to hypocotyl elongation, a similar interaction to that observed with root growth occurs. During the first few days of growth axr3-1 hypocotyls elongate at an increased rate compared with wild-type, followed by a period of slower elongation [41]. Five days after germination, axr3-1 and axr3-3 hypocotyls are slightly longer than wild-type, similar to pax1-1 (Figure 4b). However, axr3-1 pax1-1 hypocotyls are no longer than either of the single mutants and axr3-3 pax1-1 hypocotyls are shorter than either single mutant.

All of the axr3 shoot phenotypes analysed were partially suppressed by pax1-1. As in the original axr3-1 pax1-1 line isolated from the screen, pax1-1 suppresses the increase in apical dominance caused by axr3-1 (Figure 1c and 1d, Figure 5). axr3-1 pax1-1 double mutants produce more secondary inflorescences from rosette and cauline nodes compared with axr3-1 homozygotes. Conversely, pax1-1 single mutants have slightly fewer second order inflorescences than wild-type plants. Therefore, pax1-1 has opposite effects on shoot branching in axr3-1 and wild-type backgrounds. Similar results were obtained for the axr3-3 pax1-1 double mutant (Figure 5). axr3-1 pax1-1 and axr3-3 pax1-1 plants are less dwarfed than axr3-1 and axr3-3, respectively, despite the fact that pax1-1 causes a dwarf phenotype in plants that are wild-type for AXR3 (Figure 1a to 1d). Also, the leaves of each double mutant are paler green, less epinastic and less curled than the axr3 parent (Figure 1c and 1d).

###begin title 27
Mechanism of suppression
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 33 40 33 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 57 64 57 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 188 195 188 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 241 248 241 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 454 456 451 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 874 876 865 867 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6a</xref>
###xml 914 921 905 912 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 948 955 939 946 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 996 998 987 989 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6b</xref>
###xml 1073 1080 1064 1071 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 1198 1200 1189 1191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 420 427 <span type="species:ncbi:3847">soybean</span>
Two possible mechanisms by which pax1-1 may suppress the axr3-1 phenotype are: (i) by increasing the degradation rate of the over-stable axr3-1 protein, or (ii) by decreasing the level of axr3-1 transcription. To test the first possibility, pax1-1 was crossed with transgenic plants expressing amino-terminal domains I and II of axr3-1 (axr3-1NT) fused to the beta-glucuronidase (GUS) reporter, under the control of the soybean heat shock promoter (HS) [25]. This construct is an established reporter for axr3-1 protein stability. Plants were heat shocked at 37degreesC for 2 h and stained for GUS activity at various intervals following heat shock, to monitor the rate of GUS turnover. In wild-type plants GUS activity remained constant between 30 min and 2 h following heat shock, showing that the axr3-1 protein is not observably degraded during this time period (Figure 6a). Similar results were observed in a pax1-1 background suggesting that pax1-1 has no effect on axr3-1 turnover (Figure 6b). In another experiment, no difference was observed between wild type and pax1-1 on the turnover of AXR3NT-GUS, in which the wild-type domain II destabilisation sequence is present (data not shown) [25].
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 18 25 18 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 34 39 34 39 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
###xml 54 61 54 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 142 147 142 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
###xml 164 168 164 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GUS </italic>
###xml 183 192 183 192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3::GUS</italic>
###xml 350 352 350 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6c</xref>
###xml 364 374 364 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3::GUS </italic>
###xml 414 421 414 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 429 431 429 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6d</xref>
To assess whether pax1-1 controls AXR3 transcription, pax1-1 was crossed to transgenic plants containing a transcriptional fusion between the AXR3 promoter and the GUS reporter gene (AXR3::GUS). Seedlings were stained for GUS activity two days after germination. In a wild-type background, GUS activity was observed in the root and hypocotyl (Figure 6c). However, AXR3::GUS expression was significantly reduced in pax1-1 (Figure 6d). GUS staining was almost completely absent from the hypocotyl and was greatly reduced in the root.

PAX1 interacts genetically with Aux/IAA genes
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 21 26 21 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>

###xml 96 103 96 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 107 113 107 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr2-1</italic>
###xml 115 121 115 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr-1 </italic>
###xml 125 132 125 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">shy2-2 </italic>
###xml 133 135 133 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 136 138 136 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 139 141 139 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 204 208 204 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IAA7</italic>
###xml 210 216 210 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IAA14 </italic>
###xml 220 224 220 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IAA3</italic>
###xml 240 242 240 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 246 248 246 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 256 262 256 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1</italic>
###xml 486 487 486 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
To determine whether PAX1 interacts with other Aux/IAA genes, we created double mutants between pax1-1 and axr2-1, slr-1 and shy2-2 [31,42,43]. These mutants carry semi-dominant mutations in domain II of IAA7, IAA14 and IAA3, respectively [31,33,37]. Like axr3-1, the mutations result in gain-of-function phenotypes due to hyperstabilisation of the encoded proteins. Analysis of the double mutant phenotypes showed effects that could be classified into two different categories (Figure 4).

###begin p 32
###xml 103 117 103 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr2-1 pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 161 168 161 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr2-1 </italic>
###xml 172 179 172 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 246 248 246 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4a</xref>
###xml 270 280 270 280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR2/IAA7 </italic>
###xml 284 289 284 289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 351 358 351 358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr2-1 </italic>
###xml 463 469 463 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr-1 </italic>
###xml 473 480 473 480 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 619 621 619 621 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4a</xref>
###xml 626 628 626 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4b</xref>
###xml 646 653 646 653 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">shy2-2 </italic>
###xml 657 664 657 664 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
In the first class, the two mutations produced an additive effect. For example, the root length of the axr2-1 pax1-1 double mutant is less than that of both the axr2-1 and pax1-1 single mutants, which in turn are both less than wild-type (Figure 4a). This suggests that AXR2/IAA7 and PAX1 function independently in root elongation, although since the axr2-1 mutation is a dominant gain-of-function allele these results must be interpreted with caution. Likewise, slr-1 and pax1-1 had an additive effect on both root and hypocotyl elongation, implying that the two genes act independently during these processes (Figure 4a and 4b). The effects of shy2-2 and pax1-1 on root elongation were also additive.

###xml 163 170 163 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr2-1 </italic>
###xml 174 188 174 188 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr2-1 pax1-1 </italic>

For example, under our growth conditions, five days after germination axr2-1 and axr2-1 pax1-1 hypocotyls were approximately the same length, with axr2-1 suppressing the long hypocotyl phenotype of pax1-1 (Figure 4b). shy2-2 was also epistatic to pax1-1 with respect to hypocotyl elongation.

###begin title 34
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
PAX1 and gibberellic acid response
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 31 38 31 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 182 184 182 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1a</xref>
###xml 189 191 189 191 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1b</xref>
###xml 221 223 221 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1g</xref>
###xml 228 230 228 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1h</xref>
###xml 280 282 280 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 283 285 283 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 444 455 444 455 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 570 572 570 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 575 582 575 582 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 761 768 761 768 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 1065 1067 1065 1067 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 1191 1198 1189 1196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 1235 1236 1231 1232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 1254 1261 1250 1257 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 1569 1576 1565 1572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 1583 1584 1579 1580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Several aerial features of the pax1-1 phenotype are suggestive of defects in gibberellic acid (GA) response. These include slow germination, short stature, dark green leaves (Figure 1a and 1b), protruding pistils (Figure 1g and 1h) and features indicative of slower phase change [44-47]. Plants pass through several distinct phases of development, marked by morphological changes in the lateral organs produced by the shoot apical meristem. In Arabidopsis, the first two rosette leaves are relatively round and each subsequent leaf becomes progressively more elongated [48]. pax1-1 produces rounder leaves than wild type, suggesting that it remains for longer in more juvenile phases of development. To test this, other markers of phase change were compared in pax1-1 and wild-type plants. Juvenile leaves form trichomes on their adaxial (dorsal) surfaces but not their abaxial (ventral) surfaces, and the transition from the juvenile to the adult phase of vegetative development is marked by the onset of trichome production on the abaxial surfaces of the leaves [46]. Under our growth conditions, wild-type plants produced 5.80 +/- 0.09 juvenile leaves, lacking abaxial trichomes, whereas pax1-1 plants produced 8.60 +/- 0.37 (Table 1). As the rate of pax1-1 leaf initiation is reduced, this suggests that the transition to the adult growth phase is delayed both developmentally and temporally. Furthermore, floral transition also occurs both developmentally and temporally later than wild type, although this difference is mostly due to the longer juvenile phase of pax1-1 (Table 1).
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 50 57 50 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 132 133 132 133 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 223 225 223 225 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3 </sub>
###xml 233 235 233 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3b</xref>
###xml 249 255 249 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1</italic>
To test more directly for defects in GA response, pax1-1 plants were assayed for hypocotyl elongation on medium supplemented with GA3. Wild-type hypocotyl elongation was stimulated in a dose-dependent manner by exogenous GA3 (Figure 3b). However in pax1-1, GA-induced growth is reduced compared with wild type.
###end p 36

###xml 451 456 451 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
From the F2 of the outcross of the original pax1-1 axr3-1 double mutant to wild type (Columbia) PAX1 was estimated to map approximately 20 cM proximal to AXR3 on chromosome 1. To refine the map position of the PAX1 locus, pax1-1 (Columbia) plants were crossed to plants of the Landsberg erecta (Ler) ecotype and the F2 generation was scored for segregation of polymorphic molecular markers between the two ecotypes. Segregation analysis revealed that PAX1 maps to a 420 kb region between markers SNP82 (3 recombinants/964 chromosomes) and cer474010 (3 recombinants/964 chromosomes). The region appears to be somewhat recombinationally suppressed, with an estimated 677 kb per cM. Attempts to identify the gene by transformation rescue were thwarted by the discovery that the mutant phenotype is unstable, reverting to wild type at a low frequency that was substantially enhanced by the transformation process (data not shown).
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 55 61 55 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ARF19 </italic>
###xml 72 77 72 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 134 142 134 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 190 196 190 196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ARF19 </italic>
###xml 270 276 270 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1</italic>
###xml 313 319 313 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arf19 </italic>
###xml 373 375 373 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 376 378 376 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 416 422 416 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arf19 </italic>
###xml 433 440 433 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 502 507 502 507 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 511 517 511 517 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ARF19 </italic>
###xml 540 545 540 545 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 599 607 599 607 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
The delimited region includes 113 genes, of which only ARF19 is a clear PAX1 candidate, given its predicted role in the regulation of Aux/IAA gene expression. However, DNA sequencing of the ARF19 locus, including 1.5 kb upstream of the predicted translation start, from pax1-1, revealed no mutations. Furthermore arf19 insertion mutants were reported to have no phenotype [49,50], and trans-heterozygotes between an arf19 mutant and pax1-1 are phenotypically wild-type (data not shown) suggesting that PAX1 and ARF19 are not allelic. Thus, PAX1 is likely to be a previously unknown component of the Aux/IAA regulatory network.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
Discussion
###end title 40
###begin title 41
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX </italic>
###xml 15 20 15 20 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3 </italic>
PAX suppresses axr3 gain-of-function alleles
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 69 81 69 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis </italic>
###xml 89 95 89 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1</italic>
###xml 141 146 141 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3 </italic>
###xml 202 207 202 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3 </italic>
###xml 269 275 269 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1</italic>
###xml 324 331 324 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 369 374 369 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3 </italic>
###xml 580 585 580 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 628 633 628 633 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
###xml 641 646 641 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 801 808 801 808 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 861 871 861 871 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3::GUS </italic>
###xml 904 911 904 911 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 966 971 966 971 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 981 986 981 986 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
###xml 1058 1063 1058 1063 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3 </italic>
In this paper we present the isolation and characterisation of a new Arabidopsis mutant, pax1-1, which partially suppresses the phenotype of axr3 gain-of-function alleles. Virtually every aspect of the axr3 mutant phenotype analysed is suppressed at least partially by pax1-1. This is particularly compelling in cases where pax1-1 confers a more wild-type phenotype on axr3 mutants, despite having the opposite effect in a wild-type background. Examples of such are during root and hypocotyl elongation, and in the outgrowth of axillary inflorescences. These results suggest that PAX1 may encode a general positive regulator of AXR3 action. PAX1 does not appear to act at the level of AXR3 protein stability since axr3-1NT-GUS and AXR3NT-GUS translational fusions were turned over at similar rates in pax1-1 compared to wild type. In contrast, expression of an AXR3::GUS reporter was down-regulated in a pax1-1 mutant background suggesting that in wild-type plants, PAX1 regulates AXR3 transcription positively. Consistent with this idea, suppression of the axr3 phenotypes is not allele specific, as might be expected if the effect was at the protein level.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 45 52 45 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 56 63 56 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-3 </italic>
###xml 77 83 77 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1</italic>
###xml 215 220 215 220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
###xml 237 244 237 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 319 324 319 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3 </italic>
###xml 479 484 479 484 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
###xml 496 503 496 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 735 745 735 745 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3::GUS </italic>
###xml 914 921 914 921 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr3-1 </italic>
###xml 980 984 980 984 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3</italic>
###xml 1029 1036 1029 1036 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
This suggests a model for the suppression of axr3-1 and axr3-3 phenotypes by pax1-1. Such phenotypes are caused by AXR3 hyperstabilisation leading to the accumulation of increased protein levels. Therefore, reduced AXR3 transcription in pax1-1 would result in lower levels of AXR3 protein accumulation, and thus weaker axr3 phenotypes. Although this model is attractive, we have been unable to detect reliable differences from wild-type in the steady state levels the endogenous AXR3 mRNA in the pax1-1 mutant background (JJ and HMOL unpublished results). This might be because of the differences are tissue specific and therefore less easy to detect by RT-PCR than by histochemical GUS staining. However, it is also possible that the AXR3::GUS reporter does not accurately reflect the expression of the endogenous gene, as is often the case with promoter-GUS reporters. Whilst this would argue against a model of axr3-1 suppression by specific transcriptional down-regulation of AXR3, our results none the less suggest that the pax1-1 phenotype may be mediated by changes in transcription of auxin-regulated genes, since the reporter construct is rapidly auxin responsive (MT and HMOL unpublished results).
###end p 43
###begin title 44
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 15 23 15 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAAs</italic>
PAX1 and other Aux/IAAs
###end title 44
###begin p 45
###xml 41 46 41 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 94 102 94 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 124 128 124 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3</italic>
###xml 167 171 167 171 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3</italic>
###xml 282 289 282 289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA</italic>
###xml 298 306 298 306 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"> pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 367 373 367 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr2-1</italic>
###xml 375 381 375 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slr-1 </italic>
###xml 384 390 384 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">shy2-2</italic>
###xml 429 436 429 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 455 463 455 463 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
Double mutant analysis demonstrates that PAX1 interacts genetically with other members of the Aux/IAA family in addition to AXR3. However, unlike its interaction with AXR3, where virtually all phenotypes are suppressed, a more complex set of interactions is observed with the other Aux/IAAs tested. pax1-1 shows combinations of epistatic and additive phenotypes with axr2-1, slr-1 or shy2-2. Furthermore, the type of interaction pax1-1 has with different Aux/IAA mutants, varies in different organs.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 89 94 89 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 118 126 118 126 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 153 161 153 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 247 255 247 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 266 270 266 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3</italic>
###xml 395 400 395 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 412 417 412 417 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3 </italic>
###xml 460 467 460 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA</italic>
###xml 497 504 497 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
One explanation for the more complex set of interactions observed is that the effects of PAX1 on other members of the Aux/IAA family are indirect. Since Aux/IAA genes regulate each others' transcription, alterations in the expression level of one Aux/IAA gene (i.e. AXR3) could have downstream and feedback effects on the transcription of other family members. Therefore, the primary targets of PAX1 may include AXR3 transcription, whereas the effect on other Aux/IAAs may be secondary. Thus in a pax1-1 background, the phenotypes may result from widespread alterations in the balance of the Aux/IAA-ARF network, triggered by primary changes in just a few genes.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
pax1-1 is defective in auxin-regulated development
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 34 41 34 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 133 140 133 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 350 358 350 358 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 362 366 362 366 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ARF </italic>
###xml 424 426 424 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 427 429 427 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 430 432 430 432 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 447 452 447 452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ARF2 </italic>
###xml 524 526 524 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 654 656 654 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 657 659 657 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 802 803 802 803 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 804 806 804 806 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 807 809 807 809 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
A prediction of our model is that pax1-1 should have auxin-related phenotypes in a wild-type background. Indeed, many aspects of the pax1-1 phenotype are reminiscent of defects in auxin transport or signal transduction. For example, root, hypocotyl and stem elongation are regulated by auxin, and mutations in components of auxin signalling, such as Aux/IAA and ARF family members, lead to perturbations in these processes [39,51,52]. A mutant in ARF2 flowers late, suggesting that auxin may also control floral transition [53]. Another auxin-mediated process is root waving, with transport and signalling mutants displaying abnormal patterns of waving [37,54]. Furthermore, exogenous auxin affects root hair elongation and morphology, whilst many auxin response mutants show altered root hair growth [3,55,56].
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 47 52 47 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 83 90 83 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 420 427 420 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA</italic>
###xml 433 436 433 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ARF</italic>
###xml 530 535 530 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
Although the data discussed above suggest that PAX1 is involved in auxin response, pax1-1 roots show a wild-type growth response to exogenous IAA. This is consistent with the idea that the Aux/IAA-ARF network is differently configured but not globally down-regulated in the mutant, so that some phenotypes are suggestive of auxin resistance, but others are not. Analysis of the loss of function phenotypes of individual Aux/IAAs and ARFs demonstrates that the network is very robust with significant functional redundancy. If the PAX1 gene acts to modulate the network, it is therefore likely to affect more than one network member.
###end p 49
###begin title 50
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
PAX1 and GA response
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 22 29 22 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 165 167 165 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 168 170 168 170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 173 180 173 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax1-1 </italic>
###xml 311 316 311 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
Another aspect of the pax1-1 phenotype is apparent defects in GA response. GA promotes germination and phase change, and increases stem and floral organ elongation [44-47]. pax1-1 plants show decreased germination, delayed phase change, reduced stem length, and altered floral organ elongation. Consistent with PAX1 functioning in GA responses, mutant hypocotyls treated with GA are resistant to its growth-promoting effects.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 21 26 21 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 172 174 172 174 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 285 292 285 292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">axr1-12</italic>
###xml 294 299 294 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR1 </italic>
###xml 475 477 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 478 480 478 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 510 515 510 515 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 518 526 518 526 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA </italic>
###xml 740 742 740 742 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 760 765 760 765 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
These data implicate PAX1 in both auxin and GA responses. Auxin is required for GA signalling, by regulating the GA-induced degradation of DELLA growth repressor proteins [57]. Furthermore, GA mediated destabilisation of the DELLA protein RGA is reduced in the auxin resistant mutant, axr1-12. AXR1 encodes a regulator of an E3 ubiquitin-protein ligase responsible for Aux/IAA turnover, and thus AXR1 may control DELLA protein levels through the destabilisation of Aux/IAAs [25,58]. In such a case, effects of PAX1 on Aux/IAA expression levels could be sufficient to alter DELLA protein turnover. In addition, GA metabolism may be affected since AXR3 and other Aux/IAAs are thought to regulate transcription of GA metabolism genes directly[59]. Alternatively, PAX1 might control GA signalling and/or metabolism independently of its effects on Aux/IAA protein levels.
###end p 52
###begin title 53
Conclusion
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 24 32 24 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pax-1-1 </italic>
###xml 57 62 57 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 83 94 83 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3/IAA17 </italic>
###xml 109 114 109 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 152 159 152 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Aux/IAA</italic>
###xml 239 243 239 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AXR3</italic>
###xml 295 299 295 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1</italic>
###xml 310 315 310 315 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
###xml 550 555 550 555 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PAX1 </italic>
Genetic analysis of the pax-1-1 mutant demonstrates that PAX1 positively regulates AXR3/IAA17 transcription. PAX1 also interacts genetically with other Aux/IAAs, although these effects may occur secondarily through its ability to regulate AXR3. In addition, GA responses are clearly affected by PAX1. Thus the PAX1 locus is important for both auxin and GA signalling. Understanding the mechanisms that underlie cross talk between different plant hormones is currently of great interest to plant biologists. Thus the cloning and molecular analysis of PAX1 should have valuable implications for the hormone signalling community.
###end p 54

The M2 was harvested as 30 seed pools, and 1500 plants from each pool were screened for suppression of the axr3-1 shoot phenotype. The pax1-1 single mutant was obtained from the F2 of an outcross between axr3-1 pax1-1 plants and wild-type (Col). pax1-1 homozygotes were backcrossed twice to wild type and for each backcross, the F2 generation was analysed for segregation of the mutant phenotypes. 55 and 94 F2 plants were analysed from the first and second backcrosses, respectively, and all of the phenotypes cosegregated. pax1-1 homozygotes from the second backcross were used in all experiments except for the genetic crosses and in Figure 3a, where the original pax1-1 single mutant line was used.

###begin title 66
Phenotypic analysis
###end title 66
###begin p 67
###xml 48 49 48 49 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
In all experiments except those shown in Figure 2, only seedlings germinating within 3 days after sowing were analysed. Root and hypocotyl growth was measured using Lucia G (version 3.52a, 1991, Laboratory Imaging, Nikon UK Limited, Kingston, UK) image analysis software with a video camera input. Timing of floral transition was scored as the number of days after germination (dag) when floral buds first became visible, and the number of leaves at floral transition was scored at least 7 days later. Leaf senescence was recorded every 2 to 3 days so that the number of leaves counted reflected the total number of leaves produced. To characterise shoot branching phenotypes plants were analysed every 2 to 3 days for the time at which the first flower opened, and the numbers of secondary inflorescences were counted 14 days after this. Only axillary buds that were at least 3 mm long were counted as inflorescences.
