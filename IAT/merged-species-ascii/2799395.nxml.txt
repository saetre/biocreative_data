###begin article-title 0
###xml 107 112 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
PDGF-Ralpha gene expression predicts proliferation, but PDGF-A suppresses transdifferentiation of neonatal mouse lung myofibroblasts
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Background
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 226 231 222 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">pdgfa</italic>
###xml 237 241 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 398 403 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Platelet-derived growth factor A (PDGF-A) signals solely through PDGF-Ralpha, and is required for fibroblast proliferation and transdifferentiation (fibroblast to myofibroblast conversion) during alveolar development, because pdgfa-null mice lack both myofibroblasts and alveoli. However, these PDGF-A-mediated mechanisms remain incompletely defined. At postnatal days 4 and 12 (P4 and P12), using mouse lung fibroblasts, we examined (a) how PDGF-Ralpha correlates with ki67 (proliferation marker) or alpha-smooth muscle actin (alphaSMA, myofibroblast marker) expression, and (b) whether PDGF-A directly affects alphaSMA or modifies stimulation by transforming growth factor beta (TGFbeta).
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Methods
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 67 71 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 234 239 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Using flow cytometry we examined PDGF-Ralpha, alphaSMA and Ki67 in mice which express green fluorescent protein (GFP) as a marker for PDGF-Ralpha expression. Using real-time RT-PCR we quantified alphaSMA mRNA in cultured Mlg neonatal mouse lung fibroblasts after treatment with PDGF-A, and/or TGFbeta.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Results
###end title 5
###begin p 6
The intensity of GFP-fluorescence enabled us to distinguish three groups of fibroblasts which exhibited absent, lower, or higher levels of PDGF-Ralpha. At P4, more of the higher than lower PDGF-Ralpha + fibroblasts contained Ki67 (Ki67+), and Ki67+ fibroblasts predominated in the alphaSMA + but not the alphaSMA- population. By P12, Ki67+ fibroblasts comprised a minority in both the PDGF-Ralpha + and alphaSMA+ populations. At P4, most Ki67+ fibroblasts were PDGF-Ralpha + and alphaSMA- whereas at P12, most Ki67+ fibroblasts were PDGF-Ralpha- and alphaSMA-. More of the PDGF-Ralpha + than - fibroblasts contained alphaSMA at both P4 and P12. In the lung, proximate alphaSMA was more abundant around nuclei in cells expressing high than low levels of PDGF-Ralpha at both P4 and P12. Nuclear SMAD 2/3 declined from P4 to P12 in PDGF-Ralpha-, but not in PDGF-Ralpha + cells. In Mlg fibroblasts, alphaSMA mRNA increased after exposure to TGFbeta, but declined after treatment with PDGF-A.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Conclusion
###end title 7
###begin p 8
During both septal eruption (P4) and elongation (P12), alveolar PDGF-Ralpha may enhance the propensity of fibroblasts to transdifferentiate rather than directly stimulate alphaSMA, which preferentially localizes to non-proliferating fibroblasts. In accordance, PDGF-Ralpha more dominantly influences fibroblast proliferation at P4 than at P12. In the lung, TGFbeta may overshadow the antagonistic effects of PDGF-A/PDGF-Ralpha signaling, enhancing alphaSMA-abundance in PDGF-Ralpha-expressing fibroblasts.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Background
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 574 575 574 575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 576 577 576 577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
Fibroblast proliferation and transdifferentiation (fibroblast to myofibroblast conversion) are key cellular functions required for both normal alveolar development and the progression of fibrotic pulmonary diseases. Whereas many studies have focused on disease states, similar mechanisms may account for both normal lung development and remodelling after lung injury. Increased knowledge of the links between these processes could introduce more effective treatment strategies for terminal diseases such as intrapulmonary fibrosis and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease [1-3].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 704 705 704 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 714 715 714 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 725 726 725 726 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 740 741 740 741 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 873 874 869 870 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 875 876 871 872 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 698 702 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 708 712 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 718 723 <span type="species:ncbi:9940">sheep</span>
###xml 732 738 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Alveolar septal formation involves coordinated expansion of epithelial and mesenchymal cells and establishment of a specialized epithelial-endothelial interface that permits efficient gas exchange. The attenuated alveolar-capillary basement membrane is interrupted at the thickened portions of septum, which reside primarily at the base and at the alveolar entry rings. Differentiated mesenchymal cells such as the myofibroblasts, reside in these regions and deposit extracellular collagen and elastic fibers that support the delicate intervening alveolar-capillary interface. Myofibroblasts have been identified in the developing pulmonary alveolar interstitium of a variety of species, including rats [4], mice [5], sheep [6] and humans [7]. These specialized fibroblasts display smooth muscle cell-like features, such as alpha-smooth muscle actin (alphaSMA) expression [8,9].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 269 271 262 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 585 587 564 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 588 590 567 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 624 631 603 610 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">pdgf-a </italic>
###xml 636 638 615 617 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 639 641 618 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 888 896 863 871 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">pdgfs-b </italic>
###xml 900 903 875 878 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">-c </italic>
###xml 917 926 892 901 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in utero </italic>
###xml 927 929 902 904 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 930 932 905 907 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 574 579 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 597 601 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 908 912 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) family, consisting of the ligands A, B, C and D and the receptor tyrosine kinases PDGF-Ralpha and beta, regulate the proliferation, migration and differentiation of mesenchymal cells during embryonic and postnatal development [10]. All four ligands form dimers; whereas A and B can both homo- and heterodimerize (AA, AB or BB), C and D exist solely as homodimers (CC and DD). PDGF A signals solely via PDGF-Ralpha, B and C via both PDGF-Ralpha and beta, and D solely via PDGF-Rbeta. PDGFs A, B and C are expressed in the developing mouse lung[11,12], but mice that are null for the pdgf-a gene[13,14] display reduced numbers of PDGF-Ralpha-expressing cells, lack myofibroblasts and elastin deposits, and fail to develop alveoli. A role for PDGFs B and C in alveolar septal formation and myofibroblast development has not been identified, as both pdgfs-b and -c null mice die in utero [15,16]. Therefore, the behavior of PDGF-Ralpha-expressing mesenchymal cells may necessarily, but not exclusively reflect the actions of PDGF-A.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 96 98 96 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 186 188 182 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 189 191 185 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 479 481 471 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 730 732 722 724 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
Whereas it is well known that fibroblasts actively proliferate and deposit alveolar septal ECM [17], and that PDGF-A/PDGF-Ralpha signaling is required for myofibroblast differentiation [13,14], it remains unclear how PDGF-Ralpha expression influences the timing of fibroblast proliferation and differentiation. Stimulatory and inhibitory signals normally confine myofibroblasts to the period of pulmonary alveolarization and myofibroblasts are rarely observed in the adult lung [18]. Understanding more about these normal regulatory signals is important, because myofibroblasts reappear in injured and inflamed lung tissue, and contribute to the progression of fibrosis which leads to scarring and eventual loss of lung function [19].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 44 52 44 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo </italic>
###xml 287 289 275 277 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
Using green fluorescent protein (GFP) as an in vivo marker for pulmonary PDGF-Ralpha expression, we have demonstrated that fibroblasts actively expressing the PDGF-Ralpha gene are more likely to contain alphaSMA (characteristic of myofibroblasts) than their non-expressing counterparts [20]. We have now investigated the relationships between proliferation (using ki67 as a marker for proliferation) and alphaSMA gene expression in individual PDGF-Ralpha-expressing + and non-expressing - lung fibroblasts.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 76 78 72 74 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 338 340 324 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 341 343 327 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 388 390 374 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 391 393 377 379 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 611 613 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 614 616 593 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 421 426 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Because PDGF-B antagonizes alphaSMA gene expression in smooth muscle cells [21], we hypothesized that PDGF-Ralpha-expressing lung fibroblasts differ from non-expressing lung fibroblasts in their response to transforming growth factor beta (TGFbeta). TGFbeta1 is well characterized as a mediator of fibroblast to myofibroblast conversion [22-24] and is important for alveolar development [25-27]. Therefore using cultured mouse lung fibroblasts, we have examined potential direct effects of PDGF-A on alphaSMA. We have also investigated whether the nuclear abundance of the TGFbeta signalling complex, SMAD 2/3 [28,29], differs in PDGF-Ralpha expressing and non-expressing lung fibroblasts.
###end p 15

Mice bearing the PDGF-Ralpha-GFP allele were originally obtained from Dr. Philippe Soriano (Fred Hutchinson Cancer Center, Seattle, WA), and details regarding their characterization and phenotype have been reported elsewhere [30,31]. Briefly, the human histone H2B gene was subcloned into a pEGFPN1 cloning vector to generate the H2B-GFP construct encoding a nuclear form of GFP [32]. The resultant H2B-GFP construct was inserted into targeting (knock in) vectors. By homologous recombination in the mouse embryos, these vectors replaced a fragment in the wildtype pdgf-ralpha allele corresponding to the signal peptide and the first two immunoglobulin domains. Thus nuclear GFP expression in the transgenic mice is under the control of the endogenous pdgf-ralpha promoter. Soriano's group observed that GFP expression in the PDGF-Ralpha-GFP mice recapitulated endogenous spatial and temporal PDGF-Ralpha expression [30]. We used mice that were heterozygous for the pdgf-ralpha-gfp allele; thus the mice still carry one functional pdgf-ralpha allele. The heterozygous mice phenotypically resemble wildtype (GFP-) mice [30]. Mice were housed in Thoren cages with access to food and water ad libitum in a thermally regulated environment and a 12-hour light/dark cycle. Protocols for animal use were approved by the Iowa City Veterans Affairs Medical Center animal use committee.

###begin title 21
###xml 13 18 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Isolation of mouse lung fibroblasts
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 183 184 178 179 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 35 39 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Prior to the fibroblast isolation, mice were screened for the PDGF-Ralpha-GFP allele using either PCR or fluorescence microscopy. For PCR, 1 mul of tail DNA was mixed with dNTPs, MgCl2, 10x PCR buffer, and the following GFP primers: EGFP forward - 5' - GCC ATG CCC GAA GGC TAC GTC - 3' EGFP reverse - 5' - GGG TGC TCA GGT AGT GGT TGT C - 3' The amplification reaction was performed in a thermocycler unit (Techne Limited, Cambridge UK). To screen the animals using fluorescence microscopy, a small piece of the lung was compressed on a glass slide using a coverslip, and the presence or absence of GFP fluorescence was observed using an epifluorescence microscope.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 134 136 134 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 8 13 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 67 71 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Primary mouse lung fibroblasts were isolated from 4 and 12-day old mice using a previously reported method, with minor modifications [33]. After the animals had been anesthetized with 25 mul of a mixture of 90 mg/ml ketamine and 10 mg/ml xylazine, their lungs were removed, pooled together and digested for a total period of 1 h, with removal of the dispersed cells every 10 minutes. The enzyme solution consisted of Hanks buffered salt solution (HBSS, without Ca or Mg) with 300 mug/ml collagenase type I, 250 U trypsin (8500 U/mg solid), 75 mug/ml DNAse I and 5 mM calcium chloride. After each digestion interval, the solution containing dispersed cells was passed through 100 mum mesh into Ham's F-12 k media containing 10% FBS, and the undigested lung tissue placed in fresh enzyme solution. Once digestion was complete (after 3-4 digestions), erythrocytes were separated from the fibroblasts by sedimentation through a 40% Percoll solution. To select for fibroblasts, cells were adhered to 100 mm tissue culture dishes for 1 h at 37degreesC. Adherent cells were harvested by trypsinization followed by gentle scraping and centrifugation.
###end p 23
###begin title 24
###xml 32 37 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Preparation of freshly isolated mouse lung fibroblasts for Flow Cytometry analysis of alphaSMA and Ki67
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 81 83 81 83 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5 </sup>
###xml 190 191 190 191 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 193 194 193 194 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 221 222 221 222 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 225 226 225 226 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 17 22 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 700 703 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 764 767 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 949 953 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goat</span>
###xml 959 962 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1083 1088 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Freshly isolated mouse lung fibroblasts were proportioned into aliquots of 5 x 105 cells and washed several times by mixing with 1x phosphate-buffered saline (PBS - 0.145 M NaCl, 0.0015 M KH2PO4, 0.0027 M KCl, 0.0086 M Na2HPO4, pH 7.4) followed by centrifugation. Following rinsing, the fibroblasts were fixed in 2% paraformaldehyde/PBS (v/v) for 20 min with shaking at 4degreesC. After additional rinses in PBS, the fibroblasts were permeabilized in 0.1% (w/v) saponin in PBS at 4degreesC for 10 min. After the permeabilization step, non-specific binding was reduced by incubating the fibroblasts in 5% non-fat dry milk/0.1% saponin/PBS for 2 h at 4degreesC. Following the blocking step, monoclonal rat anti-ki67 primary antibody (1:300 dilution, clone TEC-3) or rat IgG2a was added to the fibroblasts, and the cells incubated overnight at 4degreesC. The next day, the cells were rinsed in 0.1% saponin/PBS. In a sequential manner, Alexa Fluor 647-goat-anti rat secondary IgG (1 mug/ml) and phycoerythrin (PE) - anti-alpha-smooth muscle actin primary antibody (0.0625 mug/ml) or PE-mouse IgG2a were added and the cells incubated for 1 h at 4degreesC. ###begin title 36
Analysis of PDGF-Ralpha and alphaSMA in images acquired by laser scanning confocal microscopy
###end title 36
###begin p 37

###begin title 39
Analysis of nuclear SMAD 2/3 in the alveolar region
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 1330 1332 1322 1324 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">- </sup>
###xml 1403 1405 1395 1397 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+ </sup>
###xml 1448 1450 1440 1442 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+ </sup>
###xml 1494 1496 1486 1488 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+ </sup>
Localization of SMAD 2/3 to nuclei in cells that either expressed PDGF-Ralpha (nuclei with GFP) or did not express PDGF-Ralpha (nuclei without GFP) was ascertained as follows: The fluorescent DNA dye, PoPo3, was assigned a blue pseudocolor. In the blue-only image, we optimized the threshold intensity to accurately select pixels representing all blue nuclei (nuclei stained with PoPo3), and quantified the pixel area covered by all the nuclei (all nuclei comprised both those with and without GFP). Next, in the green-only image, we selected only for the nuclei, which also contained GFP above a fixed threshold intensity, and quantified the total pixel area covered by these nuclei. In order to ascertain the nuclear area occupied by SMAD 2/3 (assigned the color red) but not GFP, uniform segmentation criteria were applied to the red-green-blue merged image, enabling selection of pixel segments which contained blue and red, but not green (magenta). In order to ascertain the nuclear area occupied by SMAD 2/3 and GFP, we applied uniform segmentation criteria to the RGB merged image, enabling selection of pixels which contained green and red (yellow). Finally, we ascertained the proportional pixel areas of SMAD 2/3 in nuclei that did or did not contain GFP using the following formulas: (a) Fraction of SMAD 2/3 within GFP- nuclei = area of magenta pixels / (area of all nuclei minus area of GFP+ nuclei) (b) Fraction of SMAD 2/3 within GFP+ nuclei = area of yellow pixels / area of GFP+ nuclei.

###xml 322 325 313 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F10">10F</xref>
To verify that nuclear localization of SMAD 2/3 depended on TGFbeta-stimulation, we treated Mlg cells with either vehicle or TGFbeta, and stained the cells using the same SMAD 2/3 antibody we used in the intact lung tissue. Compared to vehicle, there was increased nuclear SMAD 2/3 staining with TGFbeta treatment (Figure 10F).
###end p 85
###begin title 86
Discussion
###end title 86
###begin p 87
###xml 32 34 32 34 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 35 37 35 37 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
Although developmental studies [13,14] have clearly demonstrated that PDGF-A is required to expand and maintain alveolar myofibroblasts and septal outgrowth, the timing and mechanisms of PDGF-A action have not been defined. We have used GFP as a marker for endogenous PDGF-Ralpha gene expression to evaluate the proliferative and myofibroblastic characteristics of marked and unmarked cells during alveolar septal eruption (P4) and elongation (P12).
###end p 87
###begin p 88
###xml 314 315 306 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 376 377 368 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 602 603 590 591 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 753 754 733 734 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
Our studies suggest that during septal eruption and elongation, the proliferative characteristics of PDGF-Ralpha-expressing fibroblasts vary with receptor expression levels. Based on fluorescence intensity, we observed differential PDGF-Ralpha expression in newly isolated fibroblasts using flow cytometry (Figure 1) and in intact tissue using fluorescence microscopy (Figure 5). This differential expression of the receptor is functionally significant, because "low" expressing fibroblasts are less proliferative than the "high" PDGF-Ralpha-expressing fibroblasts during septal eruption at P4 (Figure 2), and there are larger pixel area densities of alphaSMA protein proximate to the "high" than "low" PDGF-Ralpha-GFP nuclei at both P4 and P12 (Figure 6).
###end p 88
###begin p 89
###xml 331 333 327 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 352 354 348 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 560 561 548 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 513 518 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
"Low" expressing fibroblasts may be less sensitive to PDGF-induced proliferation, either due to decreased available surface receptor expression, and/or decreased receptor activation. This hypothesis is consistent with previous reports of reductions in PDGF-A-mediated cell growth associated with diminished PDGF-Ralpha expression [40]. Hamilton et al [30] demonstrated using flow cytometry that PDGF-Ralpha expression overlapped with GFP fluorescence intensity in cells that had been derived from PDGF-Ralpha-GFP mouse embryos. Our flow cytometry data (Figure 1) also suggest that either the "low" population decreases as alveolarization progresses, or may become "high" PDGF-Ralpha-expressing. A proportional increase in "high" PDGF-Ralpha-expressing fibroblasts may be advantageous during septal elongation (P12) to support the formation of a mature, elastin-laden ECM. Further studies are required to determine whether "high" PDGF-Ralpha-expressing fibroblasts produce more elastin.
###end p 89
###begin p 90
###xml 443 445 431 433 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2D</xref>
###xml 450 452 438 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3D</xref>
###xml 642 644 622 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2H</xref>
###xml 649 651 629 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3E</xref>
###xml 824 826 792 794 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3F</xref>
###xml 1002 1003 962 963 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 1374 1376 1330 1332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 1458 1459 1414 1415 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sup>
###xml 1574 1576 1530 1532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 1511 1514 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1519 1524 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Our data also suggests that the overall proliferative state of the PDGF-Ralpha-expressing fibroblasts is different from the non-expressing fibroblasts, and may depend on their differentiative state. On postnatal day 4 (P4), at the onset of septal eruption, a larger proportion of fibroblasts within the PDGF-Ralpha- and alphaSMA- expressing groups were proliferative compared to the proportions within the non-expressing counterparts (Figures 2D and 3D). Conversely, at P12 these proportions of proliferating fibroblasts within the PDGF-Ralpha- and alphaSMA-expressing groups were smaller compared to the non-expressing counterparts (Figures 2H and 3E). A larger proportion of the PDGF-Ralpha-GFP positive fibroblasts were alphaSMA-positive compared to their PDGF-Ralpha-GFP negative counterparts at both P4 and P12 (Figure 3F). Of the proliferating fibroblasts, PDGF-Ralpha-expressing cells comprised a majority only at P4 and alphaSMA-containing cells comprised a minority at both P4 and P12 (Figure 4). These results are consistent with a transition of PDGF-Ralpha-expressing fibroblasts from a proliferative to a less proliferative more differentiated, ECM-producing state. A similar transition has been observed in fibrotic states where myofibroblasts initially proliferate at the site of injury, and thereafter become less proliferative and produce more ECM proteins [41]. Our results corroborate earlier electron micrographic studies showing that the 3H-thymidine labelling indices of fibroblasts in the rat and mouse lung was highest at P4, and afterwards declined [42]. Our results suggest that during septal eruption at P4, PDGF-Ralpha-expressing fibroblasts that are alphaSMA expressing and non-expressing proliferate more, potentially in response to PDGF-A. However, as alveolar septal elongation progresses and septal eruption is abating by P12, proliferation is less frequent among myofibroblasts and among PDGF-Ralpha expressing cells.
###end p 90
###begin p 91
###xml 9 14 9 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">pdgfa</italic>
###xml 372 373 360 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>
###xml 382 391 370 379 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro </italic>
###xml 433 441 421 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo </italic>
###xml 539 540 523 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 1066 1075 1038 1047 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro </italic>
###xml 1078 1086 1050 1058 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo </italic>
###xml 1175 1177 1147 1149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 1305 1307 1273 1275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 1308 1310 1276 1278 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 1442 1444 1410 1412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 1484 1486 1452 1454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 1835 1837 1795 1797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 20 24 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 143 148 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 197 202 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Although pdgfa-null mice lack alphaSMA-expressing myofibroblasts, it is not clear whether PDGF-A can directly stimulate alphaSMA expression in mouse lung fibroblasts. We thus stimulated a neonatal mouse lung fibroblast cell line with PDGF-AA and observed that PDGF-AA suppressed alphaSMA mRNA expression only in sub-confluent, but had no effect in confluent cells (Figure 8). These in vitro results do not necessarily contradict our in vivo observations of greater alphaSMA abundance around the "high" compared to the "low" nuclei (Figure 6). Differences in alphaSMA distribution around the "low" and "high" PDGF-Ralpha-expressing nuclei may reflect cells undergoing heterogenous differentiation pathways. PDGF-A/PDGF-Ralpha signalling during alveolar development may not directly induce the myofibroblast pathway, but instead regulate the numbers of fibroblasts entering the transdifferentiation pathway. This theory is consistent with earlier studies demonstrating that lung fibroblasts can switch between the myo- and lipo- fibroblast phenotype, depending on the in vitro or in vivo presence or absence of (i) lipofibroblast-promoting parathyroid hormone-related protein [43], or (ii) agonists of the nuclear lipogenic transcription factor, peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma (PPARgamma)[44,45]. Furthermore, studies in hepatic stellate cells (liver myofibroblasts) show that PDGFs inhibit lipofibroblast-promoting pathways [46], and the formation of adipose tissue [47]. It is possible that PDGF-A/PDGF-Ralpha signalling could suppress lipid storage thereby indirectly enhancing the myofibroblast phenotype during alveolar development. This hypothesis is supported by earlier studies conducted in our lab that showed a higher accumulation of lipid droplets in the "low" compared to the "high" PDGF-Ralpha-GFP nuclei [20].
###end p 91
###begin p 92
###xml 60 68 57 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo </italic>
###xml 72 80 69 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 419 420 398 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F9">9</xref>
###xml 798 800 766 768 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 801 803 769 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
Unlike PDGF-A, TGFbeta promotes the myofibroblast phenotype in vivo and in vitro, and is also required for alveolar development. Therefore we also investigated whether PDGF-A and TGFbeta have competing effects on alphaSMA expression in Mlg cells. We observed that TGFbeta increased alphaSMA mRNA expression. However, subsequent addition of PDGF-AA, or treatment with PDGF-AA alone, reduced alphaSMA mRNA levels (Figure 9). Stimulation or repression of alphaSMA gene expression is complex, and involves the competitive binding of various positively- and negatively- acting cell-specific transcription factor complexes to multiple elements within the alphaSMA promoter-enhancer region, including the CC(AT-rich)6GG (CARG) and AGGAATG (MCAT) sequences, as well as the TGFbeta 1 control element (TCE) [48-52].
###end p 92
###begin p 93
###xml 123 125 119 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 674 676 655 657 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 749 751 730 732 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 918 920 896 898 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 667 670 <span type="species:ncbi:9860">Elk</span>
###xml 730 735 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The suppressive effects of PDGF-AA on alphaSMA mRNA expression are similar to observations made by Yoshida and colleagues [52], who found that the PDGF-BB repressed alphaSMA gene expression in smooth muscle cells by inducing the dissociation of the positive-acting myocardin related transcription factors (MRTFs or MKLs), from the CArG elements. This dissociation resulted from competitive binding between the PDGF-BB downstream effector, Elk1, and the MRTFs at earlier than at later timepoints. PDGF-AA may act through similar mechanisms to suppress TGFbeta-mediated alphaSMA expression in fibroblasts, because PDGF-A/PDGF-Ralpha signaling also activates downstream Elk-1 [53] and MRTFs are expressed in both embryonic and adult mouse lung tissue [54]. Furthermore, TGFbeta enhances the binding of positive-acting transcription factors such as serum response factor (SRF) to the CArG and TCE elements in fibroblasts [50].
###end p 93
###begin p 94
###xml 96 97 89 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F9">9</xref>
###xml 265 267 254 256 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F10">10</xref>
###xml 494 496 483 485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
Whereas in cultured Mlg cells, PDGF-AA antagonized TGFbeta-mediated alphaSMA expression (Figure 9), nuclear SMAD 2/3 localization in PDGF-Ralpha-expressing cells was preserved in the intact tissue during both septal eruption (P4) and septal elongation (P12, Figure 10). However, in the non-expressing cells, nuclear SMAD 2/3 was lower at P12 compared to P4. Our results confirm earlier reports of persistent SMAD 3 and phospho-SMAD 2 staining in the developing alveolar interstitium until P28 [25]. To our knowledge, our findings are the first to address differences in SMAD 2/3 nuclear localization specifically in PDGF-Ralpha-expressing and non-expressing cells within the alveolar interstitium.
###end p 94
###begin p 95
###xml 308 310 293 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3F</xref>
###xml 420 428 405 413 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo </italic>
###xml 595 597 576 578 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 598 600 579 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 659 661 640 642 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 745 747 726 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 748 750 729 731 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 997 999 975 977 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 1014 1016 989 991 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
###xml 1195 1197 1167 1169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
###xml 1417 1419 1378 1380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
###xml 1076 1081 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1189 1193 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1322 1327 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our results also suggest that PDGF-Ralpha expression did not preclude TGFbeta/SMAD signaling at the indicated stages of alveolar formation. We observed that a greater proportion of PDGF-Ralpha-expressing fibroblasts at both P4 and P12 contained alphaSMA compared to their non-expressing counterparts (Figure 3F). Therefore, other predominating factors may have maintained the myofibroblast phenotype, and overridden any in vivo suppressive effects of PDGF/PDGF-Ralpha signaling. Potential overriding factors include the development of focal contacts between the myofibroblasts and ECM proteins [55,56], integrin-mediated signaling via focal adhesion kinases [57], and increased mechanical stretching related to septal formation and respiration [58,59]. An alternative explanation could be that intracellular cross talk between PDGF-A and TGFbeta signaling may occur via SMAD-independent pathways. The transcription factor known as early growth response-1 (Egr-1), which is induced by both PDGF-B [60] and TGFbeta [61], is important for TGFbeta-mediated collagen expression in mouse lung fibroblasts. This Egr-1-dependent activity is maintained even in fibroblasts derived from SMAD-3 null mice [61]. Interestingly, tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNFalpha) diminished TGFbeta-mediated alphaSMA production in primary cultured human neonatal lung fibroblasts, via induction of Egr-1 without changing SMAD phosphorylation [62].
###end p 95
###begin title 96
Conclusion
###end title 96
###begin p 97
###xml 228 236 220 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo </italic>
###xml 654 662 623 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 760 767 722 729 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
Our study has provided valuable information concerning proliferative and differentiative characteristics of PDGF-Ralpha-expressing fibroblasts during alveolar development. We have demonstrated that PDGF-Ralpha expression levels in vivo positively correlate with alphaSMA protein expression during both P4 and P12, and with proliferation at P4, but not at P12. In addition, whereas the majority of proliferating fibroblasts at both stages are undifferentiated (non-alphaSMA-expressing), most of the fibroblasts are PDGF-Ralpha + at P4, but PDGF-Ralpha- at P12. In addition, we have demonstrated that PDGF-AA suppresses TGFbeta-mediated alphaSMA induction in vitro, but PDGF-Ralpha expression does not preclude TGFbeta-dependent nuclear localization of SMAD 2/3 in vivo. Therefore the data support an indirect mechanism for the PDGF-mediated regulation of the myofibroblast (alphaSMA) phenotype, and also imply that fibroblasts respond differently to PDGFs at different stages of alveolarization, possibly to regulate the timing of fibroblast differentiation and function. Furthermore, PDGF-A/PDGF-Ralpha signaling may separately or in combination with TGFbeta, regulate myofibroblast differentiation and function during alveolar development. These interactions among the intracellular pathways may be SMAD-independent, and/or involve other myofibroblast-promoting factors such as mechanical tension and cell-ECM interactions.
###end p 97
###begin p 98
Our results and those of others demonstrate the complex regulatory network that exists in the developing lung, and how the myofibroblast plays a key role in driving the process of alveolarization. Increasing understanding of the delicate molecular balance that regulates myofibroblast differentiation and function could lead to improved therapeutic strategies for fibrotic disease.
###end p 98
