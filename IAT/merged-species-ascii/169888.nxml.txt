###begin article-title 0
DNA polymerase III chi subunit ties single-stranded DNA binding protein to the bacterial replication machinery
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 97 121 97 121 <email xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">curth.ute@mh-hannover.de</email>
To whom correspondence should be addressed. Tel: +49 511 532 3707; Fax: +49 511 532 5966; Email: curth.ute@mh-hannover.de
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 128 144 128 144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 202 208 202 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 430 436 430 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 128 144 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 202 208 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 430 436 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Single-stranded DNA binding (SSB) protein binds to single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) at the lagging strand of the replication fork in Escherichia coli cells. This protein is essential for the survival of the E.coli cell, presumably because it shields the ssDNA and holds it in a suitable conformation for replication by DNA polymerase III. In this study we undertook a biophysical analysis of the interaction between the SSB protein of E.coli and the chi subunit of DNA polymerase III. Using analytical ultracentrifugation we show that at low salt concentrations there is an increase in the stability in the physical interaction between chi and an EcoSSB/ssDNA complex when compared to that of chi to EcoSSB alone. This increase in stability disappeared in high salt conditions. The sedimentation of an EcoSSB protein lacking its C-terminal 26 amino acids remains unchanged in the presence of chi, showing that chi interacts specifically with the C-terminus of EcoSSB. In DNA melting experiments we demonstrate that chi specifically enhances the ssDNA stabilization by EcoSSB. Thus, the binding of EcoSSB to chi at the replication fork prevents premature dissociation of EcoSSB from the lagging strand and thereby enhances the processivity of DNA polymerase III.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 174 175 174 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c1">1</xref>
###xml 176 177 176 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c3">3</xref>
Single-stranded DNA binding (SSB) proteins have been defined as proteins which bind selectively to single- and not double-stranded DNA and have no direct catalytic function (1-3). With these properties in mind, the role of these proteins is clearly one of stabilizing and protecting the single-stranded state of DNA and thus providing a template for DNA polymerization, recombination or repair, thus earning them the title of single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) chaperones.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 183 184 183 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c4">4</xref>
However, it has become clear that these proteins may have other roles, besides that of binding to ssDNA, through their interactions with proteins involved in DNA metabolism (see e.g. 4). Details of these functions depend upon the class and organism these SSB proteins reside in.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 174 175 174 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c5">5</xref>
###xml 299 319 299 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana</italic>
###xml 324 336 324 336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Homo sapiens</italic>
###xml 339 340 339 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c6">6</xref>
###xml 299 319 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
###xml 324 336 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Homo sapiens</span>
Homotetrameric SSB proteins have been found in prokaryotes and eukaryotic mitochondria. These homotetrameric SSB proteins are essential for the survival of prokaryote cells (5), and the fact that genes coding for such homotetrameric SSB proteins can even be found in higher plants and animals (e.g. Arabidopsis thaliana and Homo sapiens) (6) suggests that they are evolutionarily conserved and play a vital role in the sustainment of life.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 189 190 189 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c7">7</xref>
###xml 408 409 408 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c8">8</xref>
###xml 410 412 410 412 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c10">10</xref>
###xml 780 782 780 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c11">11</xref>
###xml 851 853 851 853 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c12">12</xref>
###xml 880 896 880 896 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 905 908 905 908 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ssb</italic>
###xml 979 981 979 981 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c13">13</xref>
###xml 1240 1242 1240 1242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c13">13</xref>
###xml 1457 1459 1457 1459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c11">11</xref>
###xml 269 274 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 880 896 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
The primary structure of prokaryotic homotetrameric SSB proteins can be broken down into three distinct regions. The N-terminal two-thirds of the sequence make up the ssDNA binding region (7), the crystal structure of which has been determined for both prokaryotic and human mitochondrial SSB. These crystal structures have demonstrated no decisive differences between the SSB proteins from these organisms (8-10). In prokaryotes this ssDNA binding domain is followed by a region containing many G, Q and P residues. This region most probably forms no definite secondary structure and hence could not be identified in the crystal structure analysis. At the ultimate C-terminal end prokaryotic homotetrameric SSB proteins possess a negatively charged consensus sequence (-DDDIPF) (11). The last two regions are missing from the mitochondrial proteins (12). A temperature-sensitive Escherichia coli strain (ssb-113) carrying a mutant SSB protein (EcoSSB P176S) has been described (13). This mutation alters the C-terminal consensus sequence to -DDDISF. While the corresponding SSB protein has normal ssDNA binding functions, the phenotype was attributed to an impairment of some protein-protein interaction mediated by the C-terminal region (13). We have previously demonstrated that this mutation drastically reduces the stability of the interaction between EcoSSB and exonuclease I and showed that the C-terminal region is responsible for this interaction (11).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 70 76 70 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 82 83 82 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c4">4</xref>
###xml 84 86 84 86 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c11">11</xref>
###xml 87 89 87 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c14">14</xref>
###xml 90 92 90 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
###xml 98 104 98 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 242 244 242 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c16">16</xref>
###xml 334 336 334 336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c17">17</xref>
###xml 575 577 573 575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
###xml 578 580 576 578 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c18">18</xref>
###xml 683 685 681 683 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
###xml 70 76 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 98 104 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
To date, several proteins have been shown to physically interact with E.coli SSB (4,11,14,15). In E.coli genomic replication the cooperation of SSB and the DNA polymerase III holoenzyme is important for DNA replication of the lagging strand (16), as EcoSSB protein protects the vulnerable ssDNA strand and prevents hairpin formation (17). The precise target of the EcoSSB interactions with DNA polymerase III holoenzyme has been identified as the chi subunit. A direct interaction between these two proteins has been shown using gel filtration and surface plasmon resonance (15,18). The C-terminal consensus sequence of EcoSSB has been identified as taking part in this interaction (15). This study analyses the physicochemical details of this protein-protein interaction by analytical ultracentrifugation, surface plasmon resonance and DNA melting experiments. We present quantitative details of this interaction and provide a model of the role of this interaction in bacterial replication.

Plasmids used for overproduction of SSB proteins were used to transform the E.coli strain BT317 (23) containing the plasmid pRK248 coding for the thermosensitive lambdacI857 repressor. As they carry the ssb gene under control of the lambda pL promotor, protein expression was induced by a temperature shift from 30 to 42degreesC and cells were harvested 3 h after induction. EcoSSB wild type was purified as described by Lohman et al. (24), whereas HsmtSSB and EcoSSB Q152* were purified as previously described for HsmtSSB (22).

The chi gene was amplified from genomic DNA of the E.coli strain JM109 (Stratagene, La Jolla, CA) by PCR using Pfu DNA polymerase (forward primer, 5'-CGG AAG CCC ATG GCA AAC GCG ACG TTC TAC CTT CTG GAC AAT-3'; reverse primer, 5'-GGA AGC TCG AGT CAT TTC CAG GTT GCC GTA TTC AGG TTG AAA-3') and cloned into the NcoI and XhoI sites of pET-15b (Novagen, Madison, WI). Due to the usage of the NcoI site the second amino acid of chi was changed from Lys to Ala. After cloning the gene was completely sequenced and no further mutations were detected. For protein expression the plasmid was used to transform BL21(DE3) pLysS (Novagen). Protein expression and purification was carried out according to Xiao et al. (25) with the following modifications. The ATP-agarose column was omitted; after purification the protein was precipitated with 450 g/l (NH4)2SO4, dissolved in 20 mM HEPES pH 6.9, 0.5 mM EDTA, 2 mM DTT, 20% (v/v) glycerol and dialysed against 1 M NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, 2 mM DTT, 60% (v/v) glycerol, 20 mM potassium phosphate pH 7.4 and stored at -20degreesC. Analytical equilibrium ultracentrifugation gave a molar mass of 16.5 kg/mol, proving the chi protein to be monomeric.

###begin title 23
Surface plasmon resonance
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 358 359 349 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">N</italic>
###xml 1011 1016 1002 1007 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 1019 1021 1010 1012 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
Experiments to detect macromolecular interactions by surface plasmon resonance (SPR) were performed with a BIAcoretrade mark biosensor system 3000 (BIAcore Inc., Uppsala, Sweden). EcoSSB protein corresponding to 1028 response units (RU) was attached to a CM5 research grade sensor chip (BIAcore Inc.) using 1-ethyl-3-(3-dimethylaminopropyl)-carbodiimide and N-hydroxysuccinimide activation chemistry as described in the manufacturer's instructions. After protein coupling remaining carbodiimide groups were blocked with ethanolamine. One of the four flow cells (FC) of each four flow cell chip was reacted with ethanolamine alone to serve as a control for non-specific binding. Data collected from this reference cell, FC1, were subtracted as a blank from the data of the EcoSSB-coated FC. Thus, all data shown are difference signals (e.g. FC2 - FC1). Binding experiments were carried out in standard buffer containing 0.3 M NaCl or 0.15 M NaCl (SPR buffer) [to make data comparable to those reported by Kelman et al. (15)] at 20degreesC at a flow rate of 10 microl/min. Measurements at 5 mM NaCl were done with the low salt buffer mentioned in Buffers and reagents. The interaction between chi and EcoSSB was investigated by injecting different concentrations of chi (e.g. from 40 nM up to 29 microM in random order) over the EcoSSB surface followed by a 60 s washing step. The amount of bound chi protein was determined from the steady-state plateau height 20 s after injection start. After data collection 0.1 M glycine pH 9.5 was injected as a regeneration step to remove the bound chi protein.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 124 126 122 124 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">65</sub>
###xml 398 400 394 396 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">65</sub>
###xml 667 669 655 657 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">65</sub>
###xml 692 696 680 684 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v.s.</italic>
The interaction of EcoSSB and chi in the presence of ssDNA was investigated by immobilization of 686 RU of biotinylated-(dT)65 (MWG Biotech, Germany) on a research grade SA (streptavidin) sensor chip. The EcoSSB/ssDNA complex was prepared by injection of EcoSSB, yielding 2893 RU of bound EcoSSB. After EcoSSB injection a 360 s washing step was included. Interaction between chi and the EcoSSB/(dT)65 complex was investigated by injection of different concentrations of chi (0.5-30 microM in random order) followed by a 90 s washing step. The amount of bound chi protein was determined as described above. After regeneration using 0.1 M glycine pH 9.5 the EcoSSB/(dT)65 complex was reloaded (v.s.) with EcoSSB to ensure complete saturation of the ssDNA for the next injection of chi. As a reference signal for non-specific binding we substracted data from an empty FC. Buffers, temperature and flow rate were as described above.

###begin title 31
DNA melting curves
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 117 119 117 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c28">28</xref>
DNA melting curves were measured in a DMR 10 (Zeiss, Oberkochen, Germany) spectrophotometer as described previously (28) using a heating rate of 20 K/h. The experiments were carried out with 38 microM nucleotides of poly(dA-dT) in standard buffer containing 0.075 M NaCl. Depression of the melting point of poly(dA-dT) was induced by addition of 1.27 microM of the respective SSB protein and varying concentrations of chi protein. No significant differences between heating and cooling curves could be observed, confirming the reversibility of melting. ###begin title 33
RESULTS
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 214 215 214 215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f1">1</xref>
###xml 408 409 404 405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</italic>
###xml 409 413 405 409 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20,w</sub>
###xml 426 427 422 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</italic>
###xml 427 431 423 427 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20,w</sub>
###xml 927 929 901 903 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c29">29</xref>
###xml 958 960 932 934 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c30">30</xref>
###xml 1080 1082 1054 1056 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c26">26</xref>
###xml 1213 1214 1185 1186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f2">2</xref>
###xml 1278 1279 1250 1251 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 1281 1283 1253 1255 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
Whenever two macromolecules interact with each other the resulting complex will have a larger mass than any of its constituents and thus will in almost every case sediment faster than any of its components. Figure 1 shows the sedimentation of a complex between EcoSSB and the chi subunit of DNA polymerase III under low salt conditions. The sedimentation coefficients of EcoSSB and chi were determined to be s20,w = 4.4 S and s20,w = 1.8 S, respectively, whereas the saturated complex of EcoSSB and chi sediments at approximately5.6 S. Whenever free chi protein is present in an EcoSSB/chi mixture, two sedimenting boundaries will be observed. The slow sedimenting boundary represents free chi protein while the fast sedimenting one represents the EcoSSB/chi complex as well as free EcoSSB. A general and quantitative description of such a sedimentation of interacting macromolecules deduced from Lamm's differential equation (29) has been presented before (30). Analyzing the sedimenting boundaries in terms of integral Gaussian distributions using previously described methods (26) yields the relative amounts of free and bound chi protein. These were used to construct a binding isotherm as depicted in Figure 2. From this binding isotherm an equilibrium constant of 4.0 x 105 M-1 for independent binding of four chi proteins to a single tetrameric EcoSSB molecule was deduced.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 111 112 99 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</italic>
###xml 112 116 100 104 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">20,w</sub>
###xml 394 395 368 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f2">2</xref>
###xml 544 545 516 517 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sup>
###xml 547 549 519 521 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 601 602 573 574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f2">2</xref>
###xml 914 915 882 883 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 917 919 885 887 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 1089 1090 1057 1058 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f3">3</xref>
###xml 1123 1124 1091 1092 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">s</italic>
###xml 1236 1237 1202 1203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f3">3</xref>
###xml 1351 1353 1317 1319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c21">21</xref>
###xml 1403 1404 1367 1368 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f3">3</xref>
In low salt buffer a stoichiometric complex of EcoSSB and poly(dT) (length approximately900 nt) sediments with s20,w = 22.3 S. In the presence of chi a ternary complex is formed, which at saturation sediments at approximately35 S. From the concentration of free ligand observed in a slow sedimenting boundary and the known total amount of proteins present, a binding isotherm (as shown in Fig. 2) can be evaluated. Assuming four chi proteins bind to a single EcoSSB [in turn engaged in a complex with poly(dT)], an affinity constant of 7.4 x 106 M-1 can be estimated from the best fit shown in Figure 2. At low salt concentrations (5 mM NaCl) the affinity of chi for an EcoSSB/ssDNA complex is enhanced compared to the affinity for EcoSSB alone. Using high salt conditions (0.3 M NaCl) no differences in the binding isotherms in the absence or presence of poly(dT) could be detected, the affinity of (2 +/- 1) x 105 M-1(data not shown) is similar to the affinity found in low salt experiments without ssDNA. A sedimentation coefficient distribution of such a complex is depicted in Figure 3. EcoSSB alone sediments with an s value of 3.8 S; in the presence of an excess of chi (1.8 S) a complex is formed which sediments at 4.8 S (Fig. 3A). The sedimentation coefficient of EcoSSB Q152*, a truncated version of EcoSSB missing the last 26 amino acids (21), remains unchanged in the presence of chi (Fig. 3B). Thus, the C-terminus of EcoSSB is essential for the interaction with chi.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 127 129 121 123 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">65</sub>
###xml 398 400 388 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
###xml 401 403 391 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c18">18</xref>
In sedimentation analysis we tested whether chi can directly interact with single-stranded nucleic acid. Using 0.83 microM (dT)65 and a 10-fold excess of chi no complex formation could be detected in standard buffer containing 0.05 M NaCl (data not shown). This confirms the findings of previous studies which failed to detect an interaction between chi and single-stranded nucleic acid using SPR (15,18).
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 176 177 174 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f4">4</xref>
###xml 515 516 509 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f4">4</xref>
###xml 521 522 515 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f5">5</xref>
###xml 670 671 662 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f4">4</xref>
###xml 828 829 818 819 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 831 833 821 823 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 908 911 898 901 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">max</sub>
###xml 1050 1055 1038 1043 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 1058 1060 1046 1048 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
A further possibility to test the interaction of two proteins is SPR. We used this method to test the binding of chi to EcoSSB which had been immobilized on a SPR chip. Figure 4 shows measured SPR profiles using varying chi concentrations in standard buffer containing 0.15 M NaCl (SPR buffer). At concentrations at which the magnitude of the SPR signal created by binding of chi to the EcoSSB-coated surface was large enough to be detected, the rate of binding to the surface was too fast to be measured (cf. Figs 4 and 5). Thus, equilibrium (plateau) values of the individual profiles as a function of chi concentration were used to determine binding affinities (Fig. 4, inset). Assuming a model of independent identical binding sites for chi on the EcoSSB tetramer, a least- squares fit yielded a binding constant of 2.7 x 105 M-1. A similar result was obtained at 0.3 M NaCl (data not shown). From the RUmax value obtained in the fit a stoichiometry of approximately 2.5 chi per EcoSSB tetramer can be deduced. In a similar SPR experiment Kelman et al. (15) observed a stoichiometry of 2.2. Both values are significantly lower than the stoichiometry deduced in this study, using analytical ultracentrifugation. Since our analytical ultracentrifugation data are incompatible with the apparent stoichiometry observed in SPR experiments, it is most likely that the chemical coupling of EcoSSB to the chip surface results in an occlusion of possible binding sites.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 40 42 40 42 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">65</sub>
###xml 85 86 85 86 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">9</sup>
###xml 88 90 88 90 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 92 94 92 94 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c31">31</xref>
###xml 95 97 95 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c32">32</xref>
###xml 357 359 357 359 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">65</sub>
###xml 453 455 451 453 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">65</sub>
###xml 610 611 606 607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f5">5</xref>
###xml 654 658 650 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v.s.</italic>
###xml 698 699 694 695 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 701 703 697 699 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 753 756 747 750 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Eco</italic>
###xml 775 776 769 770 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f5">5</xref>
###xml 1060 1062 1052 1054 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
###xml 1394 1398 1384 1388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v.s.</italic>
EcoSSB binds to the oligonucleotide (dT)65 with affinites considerably larger than 109 M-1 (31,32). However, when trying to bind the oligonucleotide to EcoSSB, which had been covalently attached to an SPR chip, we could not detect an appreciable signal, probably due to the electrostatic repulsion between the negatively charged surface of the chip and (dT)65. Thus, to investigate the influence of ssDNA on the EcoSSB/chi interaction, biotinylated (dT)65 was bound to a streptavidin chip and saturated with EcoSSB, before varying concentrations of chi were passed over the resultant immobilized complex (Fig. 5). The evaluation of the binding isotherm (v.s.) yielded a binding constant of 4.2 x 105 M-1 and a stoichiometry of approximately 4.4 chi per EcoSSB tetramer (Fig. 5, inset). Thus, the measured binding constants with and without nucleic acid were seen to be in the same range. The measured stoichiometry of the EcoSSB/chi complex in the presence of DNA is approximately twice that observed in its absence, which is consistent with previous studies (15). This apparent increase in stoichiometry is most likely due to the fact that EcoSSB was attached to the SPR chip via its natural oligonucleotide ligand in this experiment and hence all binding sites are accessible for chi. This interpretation is supported by the results obtained in solution using analytical ultracentrifugation (v.s.).
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 223 225 221 223 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">65</sub>
At 5 mM NaCl, binding of chi to a surface on which EcoSSB has been immobilized could be detected qualitatively as an increase in RU signal, whether or not the EcoSSB had been immobilized to the SPR chip directly or via (dT)65 oligonucleotide. However, these experiments failed to produce reliable quantitative results. A possible reason for this failure is the inherent negative charge of the dextran matrix on the chip, whose effects become overwhelmingly large at low ionic strengths.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 246 247 246 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c7">7</xref>
###xml 481 482 467 468 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f6">6</xref>
###xml 582 584 568 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c21">21</xref>
###xml 627 629 613 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c22">22</xref>
###xml 709 710 693 694 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkg498f6">6</xref>
###xml 594 599 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
EcoSSB specifically binds single- but not double-stranded DNA and, as a consequence, destabilizes the double strand. Thus, the depression of double-stranded DNA melting temperature by EcoSSB is a measure of the affinity of the protein for ssDNA (7). In the case of poly(dA-dT), saturating amounts of EcoSSB reduce the melting temperature from 59 to 42degreesC. Addition of an excess of chi over EcoSSB results in a further depression of the melting temperature to 35degreesC (Fig. 6A). In the presence of EcoSSB Q152*, a truncated version of EcoSSB missing the last 26 amino acids (21), or the human mitochondrial SSB protein (22) the melting point of poly(dA-dT) is also depressed, but addition of chi (Fig. 6B) has no further influence on the melting behavior. Thus, we conclude that binding of chi to EcoSSB enhances the ssDNA binding affinity of EcoSSB and that a specific interaction between the C-terminus of EcoSSB and chi is responsible for this effect.
###end p 40
###begin title 41
DISCUSSION
###end title 41
###begin p 42
In this study we have demonstrated a direct physical interaction between EcoSSB and the DNA polymerase III chi subunit independent of the conditions used. We went on to demonstrate that this interaction stabilizes the complex formed between EcoSSB and ssDNA.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 228 230 212 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
###xml 231 233 215 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c18">18</xref>
###xml 526 527 508 509 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c7">7</xref>
Under low salt (5 mM NaCl) conditions in an analytical ultracentrifuge the interaction of chi with an EcoSSB/ssDNA complex is approximately20-fold stronger than the interaction between chi and EcoSSB alone. Since we and others (15,18) have not been able to observe a direct interaction between chi and ssDNA, this increase in affinity may be an enhancement of the protein-protein interaction. When binding to ssDNA a conformational change of EcoSSB was observed which makes the C-terminus more prone to proteolytic digestion (7). We show that chi interacts with this C-terminus of EcoSSB and thus the conformational change is a likely mechanism for the enhancement of EcoSSB/chi interaction by ssDNA. However, a very weak binding of chi to the ssDNA in this complex cannot be excluded. Addition of salt (0.3 M NaCl) renders the interaction of EcoSSB and chi independent of the presence of ssDNA. We observed a similar result at 0.15 M NaCl using SPR. At 5 mM NaCl our SPR experiments showed binding, but could only be interpreted qualitatively.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 122 124 120 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
In a similar study Kelman and co-workers demonstrated an interaction of chi and EcoSSB at 0.15 M NaCl by SPR experiments (15). However, under low salt conditions using gel filtration experiments the same authors failed to show an interaction between chi and EcoSSB unless ssDNA was present. One possible reason for the discrepancy between these experiments and our demonstration of an EcoSSB/chi interaction at low salt conditions using analytical ultracentrifugation may be due to the fact that the weakly interacting proteins cannot migrate as a complex in a single band in the gel filtration column if the dissociation is too fast. However, in our sedimentation velocity experiments the complex between EcoSSB and chi sediments in the presence of a constant concentration of free chi protein. This allows the detection of a weak and rapidly equilibrating complex.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 223 225 219 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
###xml 226 228 222 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c18">18</xref>
At low salt concentrations EcoSSB and chi do not influence the activity of DNA polymerase III in a replication assay. At high salt concentrations the presence of EcoSSB and chi increases the processivity of the polymerase (15,18). Thus, the interaction between EcoSSB and chi is responsible for an increase in activity of DNA polymerase III at elevated salt concentrations. In this study we could demonstrate a physical interaction between EcoSSB and chi even at high salt concentrations (0.3 M NaCl).
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 459 461 451 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c15">15</xref>
We could also show that the interaction between EcoSSB and chi at high salt concentrations is highly specific. A truncated version of EcoSSB missing the last 26 amino acids (EcoSSB Q152*) does not interact with the chi protein. Thus, the C-terminus of EcoSSB is essential for the interaction of EcoSSB and chi. This adds to the finding that the interaction of chi and EcoSSB is weakened by the exchange of the penultimate amino acid of EcoSSB (EcoSSB P176S) (15).
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 163 164 159 160 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 166 168 162 164 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 170 172 166 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c18">18</xref>
###xml 315 316 309 310 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 318 320 312 314 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 590 592 570 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c18">18</xref>
The interaction of EcoSSB and a complex of DNA polymerase III subunits chi and psi has been determined by Glover and McHenry to have a binding constant of 3.7 x 105 M-1 (18). Our present study shows that this interaction is a result of the direct formation of a SSB/chi complex that has a similar affinity (2.7 x 105 M-1). We also show by analytical ultracentrifugation that including ssDNA in the chi/EcoSSB binding assay leads to an approximately20-fold enhancement of the affinity at low salt and leaves the affinity almost unchanged at high salt. Thus, the 1000-fold increase reported (18) for the interaction of EcoSSB and chi in the presence of ssDNA and the psi and taudeltadelta' subunits of DNA polymerase III is most probably an effect of the additional protein subunits.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 817 818 803 804 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c7">7</xref>
###xml 819 821 805 807 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c21">21</xref>
Since the interaction of EcoSSB and chi is influenced by the presence of ssDNA, and chi does not interact measurably with ssDNA, we questioned how the interaction between EcoSSB and chi influences the DNA binding properties of EcoSSB. Our DNA melting curves show that the stability of the ternary complex of EcoSSB, ssDNA and chi is greater than the stability of the binary complex without chi. The DNA binding properties of other SSB proteins (HsmtSSB and EcoSSB Q152*) which do not physically interact with chi remain unchanged. Interestingly, the double-stranded DNA destabilization effect of chi and EcoSSB together is similar to the effect of the C-terminal deletion mutant EcoSSB Q152* alone. The electrostatic repulsion between this negatively charged C-terminus and the ssDNA weakens the SSB-DNA interaction (7,21). This weakening is possibly overcome by shielding of the C-terminus through chi binding.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 344 346 342 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkg498c33">33</xref>
Through the enhancement of ssDNA binding affinity, chi ties EcoSSB to the replication fork. During DNA synthesis at the lagging strand, DNA polymerase III removes nucleotide by nucleotide from the adjacent EcoSSB protein. EcoSSB can bind up to 65 nt and the binding affinity decreases drastically with decreasing length of the oligonucleotide (33). This lowered affinity would result in the dissociation of EcoSSB from the lagging strand in the absence of interactions between chi and EcoSSB protein. The released DNA could build a hairpin structure and the DNA polymerase would be inhibited. Stabilization of the EcoSSB/DNA complex through interaction with chi could prevent the premature dissociation of EcoSSB and therefore the inhibition of DNA polymerase III. This would result in an increase in the processivity of the polymerase.
###end p 49
