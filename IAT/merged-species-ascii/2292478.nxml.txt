Do teashirt family genes specify trunk identity? Insights from the single tiptop/teashirt homolog of Tribolium castaneum
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Communicated by S. Roth
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 4 23 4 23 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila teashirt</italic>
###xml 331 339 331 339 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">teashirt</italic>
###xml 461 470 461 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 481 489 481 489 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">teashirt</italic>
###xml 529 548 529 548 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium castaneum</italic>
###xml 559 568 559 568 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 824 843 824 843 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila teashirt</italic>
###xml 511 527 <span type="species:ncbi:7070">red flour beetle</span>
###xml 529 548 <span type="species:ncbi:7070">Tribolium castaneum</span>
The Drosophila teashirt gene acts in concert with the homeotic selector (Hox) genes to specify trunk (thorax and abdomen) identity. There has been speculation that this trunk-specifying function might be very ancient, dating back to the common ancestor of insects and vertebrates. However, other evidence suggests that the role of teashirt in trunk identity is not well conserved even within the Insecta. To address this issue, we have analyzed the function of Tc-tiotsh, the lone teashirt family member in the red flour beetle, Tribolium castaneum. Although Tc-tiotsh is important for aspects of both embryonic and imaginal development including some trunk features, we find no evidence that it acts as a trunk identity gene. We discuss this finding in the context of recent insights into the evolution and function of the Drosophila teashirt family genes.

###begin title 6
Introduction
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 284 288 284 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">2005</xref>
###xml 535 539 535 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">1991</xref>
###xml 565 573 565 573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">teashirt</italic>
###xml 575 578 575 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 722 739 722 739 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sex combs reduced</italic>
###xml 755 759 755 759 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">1991</xref>
###xml 774 778 774 778 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">1992</xref>
###xml 828 831 828 831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 831 832 831 832 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1086 1090 1086 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">1991</xref>
###xml 1127 1130 1127 1130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 1207 1211 1207 1211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">1994</xref>
###xml 1248 1251 1248 1251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 1338 1342 1338 1342 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">1992</xref>
Homeotic genes are responsible for defining regional identity, and mutations in these genes often transform one body part into another. The best characterized homeotic genes are the Hox genes, which have been identified in representatives of most animal phyla (reviewed by Martindale 2005) and have been extensively studied in several organisms. However, some non-Hox genes have also been classified as homeotic genes based on their mutant phenotypes. One such gene encodes the zinc finger transcription factor Teashirt (Fasano et al. 1991). Loss-of-function (LOF) teashirt (tsh) mutations are reported to cause partial transformation of the larval prothorax to a labial identity due to ectopic expression of the Hox gene Sex combs reduced (Fasano et al. 1991; Roder et al. 1992). The trunk (thoracic and abdominal) segments of tsh- larvae are shorter than normal and the denticle belts (a trunk-specific feature) are severely disorganized. Moreover, patches of sclerotic cuticle, which have been interpreted as head structures, form between the remaining denticle belts (Fasano et al. 1991). Conversely, ectopic expression of tsh results in the development of denticle belts in the head (de Zulueta et al. 1994). On the basis of these phenotypes, tsh has been suggested to function as a specifier of trunk vs head identity (Roder et al. 1992).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 0 3 0 3 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 61 64 61 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Scr</italic>
###xml 66 78 66 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Antennapedia</italic>
###xml 80 84 80 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Antp</italic>
###xml 87 100 87 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ultrabithorax</italic>
###xml 102 105 102 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ubx</italic>
###xml 108 119 108 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">abdominal-A</italic>
###xml 121 126 121 126 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">abd-A</italic>
###xml 133 144 133 144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Abdominal-B</italic>
###xml 146 151 146 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Abd-B</italic>
###xml 205 209 205 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">1994</xref>
###xml 224 228 224 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">1992</xref>
###xml 556 560 556 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">1985</xref>
###xml 569 573 569 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR41">1983</xref>
###xml 581 584 581 584 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 752 756 752 756 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">1992</xref>
tsh is believed to act in parallel with the trunk Hox genes (Scr, Antennapedia (Antp), Ultrabithorax (Ubx), abdominal-A (abd-A), and Abdominal-B (Abd-B)) to determine segmental identity (de Zulueta et al. 1994; Roder et al. 1992). When all of these Hox genes are inactivated, trunk segments develop with what has been interpreted as a mixed head/trunk identity. That is, sclerotized tissue develops in the posterior compartment of each trunk segment, whereas denticle belts (a trunk-specific feature) still develop in the anterior compartment (Sato et al. 1985; Struhl 1983). When tsh function is also eliminated, the trunk segments are more severely affected in that denticle belts disappear and additional head-like cuticle is observed (Roder et al. 1992).
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 26 29 26 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 131 134 131 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 157 163 157 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tiptop</italic>
###xml 165 168 165 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 231 235 231 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">2005</xref>
###xml 243 246 243 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 251 254 251 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 365 368 365 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 386 389 386 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 419 422 419 422 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 449 452 449 452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
Recent data suggests that tsh may play an even greater role in trunk development than was previously recognized. In the absence of tsh function, its paralog tiptop (tio) is ectopically expressed in the trunk region (Laugier et al. 2005). When tio and tsh functions are both absent, the trunk denticles are even more severely affected than in mutants that lack only tsh, suggesting that tio can partially substitute for tsh and reduce the effects of tsh mutations.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 25 29 25 29 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">2004</xref>
###xml 107 117 107 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 224 227 224 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 262 274 262 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">disconnected</italic>
###xml 276 281 276 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">disco</italic>
###xml 331 334 331 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 336 341 336 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">disco</italic>
###xml 366 379 366 379 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">disco-related</italic>
###xml 460 465 460 465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">disco</italic>
###xml 522 525 522 525 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 671 675 671 675 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">2004</xref>
Robertson and coworkers (2004) proposed the existence of a system of zinc-finger proteins that divides the Drosophila embryo into sections and limits the functional domains of Hox genes. They suggest that within this system tsh specifies the trunk region, while disconnected (disco) specifies the gnathal region. In the absence of tsh, disco (as well as its paralog disco-related) is ectopically expressed in the trunk, and there is some evidence that ectopic disco expression is responsible for at least some features of tsh mutants (e.g., loss of denticles and abnormal sensory neurons) that are thought to represent a switch to head/gnathal identity (Robertson et al. 2004).
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 17 21 17 21 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">2004</xref>
###xml 72 75 72 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 129 132 129 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 220 234 220 234 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila tsh</italic>
###xml 375 378 375 378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 438 441 438 441 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 472 491 472 491 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Thermobia domestica</italic>
###xml 515 535 515 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oncopeltus fasciatus</italic>
###xml 656 660 656 660 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2005</xref>
###xml 678 682 678 682 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">1999</xref>
###xml 717 721 717 721 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2005</xref>
###xml 756 770 756 770 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oncopeltus tsh</italic>
###xml 1025 1044 1025 1044 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium castaneum</italic>
###xml 1081 1091 1081 1091 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1215 1219 1215 1219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">2002</xref>
###xml 1329 1338 1329 1338 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium</italic>
###xml 1352 1360 1352 1360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Deformed</italic>
###xml 1362 1365 1362 1365 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Dfd</italic>
###xml 1368 1371 1368 1371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Scr</italic>
###xml 1373 1377 1373 1377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Antp</italic>
###xml 1379 1382 1379 1382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Ubx</italic>
###xml 1388 1393 1388 1393 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">abd-A</italic>
###xml 1409 1413 1409 1413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR42">1991</xref>
###xml 1526 1530 1526 1530 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">1999</xref>
###xml 1558 1571 1558 1571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium tsh</italic>
###xml 1642 1645 1642 1645 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 1751 1754 1751 1754 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 1767 1777 1767 1777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 144 148 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 462 470 <span type="species:ncbi:89055">firebrat</span>
###xml 472 491 <span type="species:ncbi:89055">Thermobia domestica</span>
###xml 501 513 <span type="species:ncbi:7536">milkweed bug</span>
###xml 515 535 <span type="species:ncbi:7536">Oncopeltus fasciatus</span>
###xml 1007 1023 <span type="species:ncbi:7070">red flour beetle</span>
###xml 1025 1044 <span type="species:ncbi:7070">Tribolium castaneum</span>
Manfroid et al. (2004) speculated that the trunk-specifying function of tsh family members might be conserved in vertebrates, as tsh genes from mice are expressed in the trunk region and can also rescue the phenotype of Drosophila tsh mutants when ectopically expressed. However, such conjecture requires a rather large phylogenetic leap, as it is not clear that the role of tsh in trunk specification is conserved even in other insects. tsh family genes of the firebrat, Thermobia domestica, and the milkweed bug, Oncopeltus fasciatus, are expressed predominantly in the gnathos and thorax, arguing against a function in trunk specification (Herke et al. 2005; Peterson et al. 1999). Furthermore, when Herke et al. (2005) knocked down the function of the Oncopeltus tsh family gene by RNA interference (RNAi), they observed transformations of distal leg to antenna, but did not report any changes in trunk identity. Finally, our previous studies suggest that genetic specification of trunk identity in the red flour beetle, Tribolium castaneum, differs significantly from that in Drosophila. Removal of trunk Hox genes alone is sufficient to produce dramatic transformation of trunk to head identity (Brown et al. 2002). Antennae develop on all gnathal and trunk segments of embryos homozygous for a deficiency that removes the Tribolium orthologs of Deformed (Dfd), Scr, Antp, Ubx, and abd-A (Stuart et al. 1991). Thus, there is apparently no need to invoke the function of another gene for trunk identity. Peterson et al. (1999) predicted that either the Tribolium tsh gene has no function in trunk specification or that the expression of tsh is controlled by Hox genes. Taken together, these observations suggest that the trunk-specifying role of tsh observed in Drosophila might not be conserved in other insects.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 38 41 38 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 94 97 94 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 111 120 111 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium</italic>
###xml 133 142 133 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 185 194 185 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium</italic>
###xml 347 356 347 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 541 544 541 544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 575 585 575 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
To better understand the evolution of tsh family gene function, we cloned and characterized a tsh homolog from Tribolium. This gene, Tc-tiotsh, is the sole member of this family in the Tribolium genome. It has a broad embryonic expression domain, encompassing not only the trunk region but much of the head as well. RNAi experiments revealed that Tc-tiotsh functions in several embryonic and imaginal tissues, but they did not suggest a conserved function in trunk specification. We discuss these results and reexamine the classification of tsh as a trunk-specifying gene in Drosophila.

###begin title 13
Materials and methods
###end title 13
###begin title 14
cDNA cloning
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 26 35 26 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 342 361 342 361 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium castaneum</italic>
###xml 411 415 411 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">2001</xref>
###xml 451 455 451 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR26">1988</xref>
###xml 342 361 <span type="species:ncbi:7070">Tribolium castaneum</span>
A genomic fragment of the Tc-tiotsh gene was amplified using degenerate primers based on the conserved amino acid motifs VWLGKG and CKVCDK (first round) and ILKCMW and CKVCDK (second round). Additional, overlapping fragments were identified by RACE, using the 5' and 3' RACE Systems (GibcoBRL), by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) screening a Tribolium castaneum cDNA library as described previously (Nie et al. 2001), or by inverse PCR (Ochman et al. 1988). To confirm that these overlapping fragments represent a contiguous sequence, primers were designed near the ends of the putative contig, and reverse transcriptase (RT)-PCR was performed using total embryonic RNA (0-30 h of development) as template. The resulting PCR product was cloned and sequenced (GenBank accession number AF356647).
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Sequence analysis
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 26 35 26 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 119 122 119 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 192 215 192 215 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 530 534 530 534 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MEGA</italic>
###xml 561 565 561 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">2004</xref>
###xml 192 215 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
The nucleotide contig for Tc-tiotsh was assembled using the Seqman module of Lasergene (DNASTAR). To identify putative tsh family members in other genomes, we performed tBLASTn searches using Drosophila melanogaster Tsh and Tio as query sequences. Multiple alignments were created with the AlignX module of Vector NTI 8 (InforMax). Minor manual adjustments were performed to maximize the alignments. The final output of the multiple alignment was created using BOXSHADE (available at ). Phylogenetic analyses were performed using MEGA version 3.1 (Kumar et al. 2004) or TreePuzzle (), and TreeView () was used to visualize the output.
###end p 17

In situ hybridization and immunostaining of embryos
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 34 43 34 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 142 147 142 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">1994b</xref>
###xml 245 249 241 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">1989</xref>

A digoxigenin-labeled fragment of Tc-tiotsh was hybridized to whole-mount wild-type (Georgia-1) embryos as previously described (Brown et al. 1994b). Embryos were secondarily immunostained with either alpha-Invected antibody (4D9) (Patel et al. 1989) or with alpha-Even-skipped antibody (2B8) (Patel et al. 1994), which cross-react with Tc-Engrailed and Tc-Even-skipped, respectively. Tc-spalt (Tc-sal) expression was assayed using the probe described by Tomoyasu et al. (2005).
###end p 19
###begin title 20
RNA interference (RNAi)
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 53 62 53 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 146 150 146 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">2000</xref>
###xml 207 216 207 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
dsRNA of two different, non-overlapping fragments of Tc-tiotsh was synthesized using the PCR template method described by Kennerdell and Carthew (2000). Briefly, primers were chosen to amplify a fragment of Tc-tiotsh (bases 1358-1786 or 1848-2581 of GenBank sequence AF356647) and the T7 promoter sequence (TAATACGACTCACTATAGG) was added to the 5' end of each primer. The resulting primers were used to amplify template for RNA synthesis. The resulting product was purified and 1 mug used as template for RNA synthesis using the Megascript T7 High Yield Transcription kit (Ambion).
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 155 159 153 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">1999</xref>
###xml 327 331 325 329 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">2001</xref>
For embryonic RNAi, 0-2 h eggs were collected from wild-type beetles (GA-1) and injected with dsRNA (2-4 mug/mul) as previously described by Brown et al. (1999). After several days, hatched larvae and unhatched eggs were treated with lactic acid/ethanol. Additional larvae were documented by SEM as described by Curtis et al. (2001).
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 243 247 241 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR43">2004</xref>
Injection into non-virgin adult females was performed by manually opening the elytra and wings of ether-anesthetized beetles and injecting 2 mug/mul dsRNA into the dorsal abdomen. Larval RNAi was performed as described by Tomoyasu and Denell (2004).

Results
###end title 24
###begin title 25
###xml 0 9 0 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium</italic>
###xml 23 31 23 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">teashirt</italic>
Tribolium has a single teashirt family gene
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 26 29 26 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>

###xml 81 104 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 170 187 <span type="species:ncbi:7165">Anopheles gambiae</span>
Only three full-length Tsh family proteins have been described from insects: the Drosophila melanogaster paralogs Tsh and Tio, and the single member of the Tsh family in Anopheles gambiae, Ag-TioTsh (Fasano et al. 1991; Laugier et al. 2005). In addition, small fragments of tsh family genes have been amplified from several other insects (Herke et al. 2005; Peterson et al. 1999). Two conclusions have been drawn from these limited data. First, the two Drosophila genes seem to be the result of a recent duplication in the Drosophila lineage. Second, all of the Tsh family members from other insects appear to be more similar in sequence to Tio than to Tsh. In this paper, we present data that support and greatly strengthen these conclusions.

We performed a tBLASTn search of several recently sequenced insect genomes for tsh family members (Table 1). The Tribolium castaneum gene that we cloned is the only member of the tsh family present in the genome sequence of this insect. Likewise, the Apis mellifera (honeybee) genome contains only a single member of this gene family. In contrast, all of the additional Drosophila species we searched (D. pseudoobscura, D. simulans, D. yakuba and D. virilis) have orthologs of both tio and tsh. Consistent with the situation in D. melanogaster (Laugier et al 2005), tio and tsh are closely linked (less than 250 kb apart) in all of the Drosophila species examined, suggesting that these genes likely arose from a relatively recent tandem duplication event. As the Tribolium gene is related to both tio and tsh, we have named it Tc-tiotsh, after the convention established by Laugier et al. (2005) for the single Anopheles gene. We compared the predicted Tc-TioTsh protein to the other insect Tsh family members for which full-length predicted proteins are available. (Although, we were able to identify a tsh family gene in Apis, the 5' portion of the transcription unit was apparently missing from the genome sequence.) Like tio and Ag-tiotsh (Laugier et al. 2005), Tc-tiotsh encodes four zinc fingers (Fig. 1), three of which are clustered near the middle of the protein (Fig. 2a), and one that is located near the C terminus (Fig. 2b). Besides the zinc finger regions, several additional short regions of sequence identity (shaded in Fig. 2 and data not shown) are present in all of the full-length predicted proteins. The three dipteran proteins are highly similar at their N termini, while Tio, Ag-TioTsh, and Tc-TioTsh share a C-terminal motif (in addition to the fourth zinc finger) that is absent in Tsh. Regions shared between the beetle and dipteran proteins have been conserved over a long phylogenetic distance, suggesting that they likely have important functions. To better understand the evolutionary history of the tsh gene family, we have performed phylogenetic analyses using conserved portions of the zinc-finger region (underlined in Fig. 2) from the full-length insect Tsh family proteins that are currently available (Drosophila, Tribolium, and Anopheles). Both maximum likelihood (Fig. 3) and neighbor-joining (data not shown) methods produce trees in which the two Drosophila proteins cluster together, suggesting these genes were produced by duplication after the divergence of the mosquito and fly lineages. The branch lengths of the Drosophila proteins are much longer than those of Anopheles and Tribolium, suggesting rapid divergence of both genes after duplication. The mouse and human genomes each have three tsh family members (Caubit et al. 2000; Manfroid et al. 2004), which are much more similar to one another than they are to any of the insect proteins. Interestingly, vertebrate Tsh family proteins share an N-terminal motif (MPRRKQ) with the Tribolium protein (Fig. 2c), which is distinct from the N-terminal motif shared by the dipteran family members (Laugier et al. 2005). The conservation of this sequence in an insect and vertebrates suggests it has an important function and that it was probably present in the common ancestor of protostomes and deuterostomes.
###end p 36

###xml 258 278 <span type="species:ncbi:10228">Trichoplax adhaerens</span>
###xml 300 322 <span type="species:ncbi:45351">Nematostella vectensis</span>
Taken together, these comparisons of tsh family members indicate that the ancestor of protostomes and deuterostomes had a single tsh family gene. We were unable to identify any tsh family members in the genomes of two non-bilaterian metazoans: the placozoan Trichoplax adhaerens () and the cnidarian Nematostella vectensis (Putnam et al. 2007), suggesting this gene family may have arisen after the bilaterian/non-bilaterian split. Within the vertebrate lineage, duplication events (perhaps in association with whole genome duplications) produced at least three paralogs. A duplication in the Drosophila lineage (after divergence from the mosquito lineage) resulted in the tsh and tio paralogs.

We examined the embryonic expression pattern of Tc-tiotsh (Fig. 4) by in situ hybridization, using the posterior compartment marker Engrailed (Brown et al. 1994a; Patel et al. 1989) to determine the stage of embryonic development (segments are added sequentially) and provide a reference for the register of Tc-tiotsh expression. Tc-tiotsh expression develops in a dynamic, pair rule-like manner (Fig. S1 and S2), but eventually encompasses not only the trunk segments (thorax and abdomen), but also the gnathal segments and the labrum and intercalary segment in the procephalon (Fig. 4a). Tc-tiotsh expression in the thorax and the first abdominal segment appears somewhat stronger than in other regions (Fig. 4b). After germ-band elongation, Tc-tiotsh is expressed in the posterior region of the embryo. Initially, expression is seen in a patch on either side of the ventral midline (Fig. 4c), and later in six buds that extend from the invaginating hindgut (Fig. 4e). These structures are the primordia of the Malpighian tubules; they also express Tc-dachsund (Prpic et al. 2001) and Tc-Cut (data not shown).

###xml 319 342 319 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium spalt (Tc-sal</italic>
###xml 415 428 415 428 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium sal</italic>
###xml 451 455 451 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR44">2005</xref>
###xml 475 481 475 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-sal</italic>
###xml 507 508 507 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="" rid="MOESM3">3</xref>
###xml 541 550 541 550 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 568 574 568 574 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-sal</italic>
###xml 592 601 592 601 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 634 643 634 643 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium</italic>
###xml 730 733 730 733 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 737 740 737 740 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sal</italic>
In Drosophila, the limits of tsh expression are controlled either directly or indirectly by spalt-major (salm). In a salm mutant, the tsh expression domain expands both anteriorly and posteriorly (Roder et al. 1992). To assess whether the broader expression of Tc-tiotsh (compared to tsh) might reflect a difference in Tribolium spalt (Tc-sal) expression, we examined the embryonic expression pattern of the single Tribolium sal gene (Tomoyasu et al. 2005). We find that the Tc-sal expression domain (Fig. S3) significantly overlaps that of Tc-tiotsh, suggesting that Tc-sal does not repress Tc-tiotsh expression. It is possible that Tribolium has lost the regulatory interaction between these genes. Alternatively, regulation of tsh by sal may have evolved more recently, after the divergence of these lineages.
###end p 43

In Drosophila, some aspects of tsh expression are regulated by the Hox genes (Roder et al. 1992). We have examined expression of Tc-tiotsh in embryos homozygous for Df(HOMC), a deletion that removes most of the Tribolium Hox cluster. Tc-tiotsh expression is severely reduced in the ventral region of these embryos (Fig. 4f). These results suggest that reduction of Tc-tiotsh expression might contribute to the transformations seen in Df(HOMC) embryos. However, our functional studies of Tc-tiotsh (presented below) reveal no evidence for this.

We used RNAi to ascertain the probable LOF phenotype of Tc-tiotsh. Pupae injected with Tc-tiotsh dsRNA produced very few eggs upon reaching adulthood, and none of these progeny showed an abnormal phenotype. To circumvent the apparent effect of Tc-tiotsh on oogenesis, we injected Tc-tiotsh dsRNA either directly into eggs or into the abdomens of non-virgin adult females (egg production from these females quickly tapers off, further indicating an effect on oogenesis). Injection of Tc-tiotsh dsRNA by either technique produced embryos with defects in the labial and thoracic segments (Fig. 5, Table 2, and data not shown). During normal Tribolium embryonic development, the two labial appendages fuse at the ventral midline and migrate anteriorly to a position between the maxillary appendages (Fig. 5a). In moderately affected Tc-tiotsh RNAi larvae (Fig. 5b), the labial appendages are smaller than normal, and in the most severely affected embryos (Fig. 5c) these appendages fail to fuse. In all of these cases, the abnormal labial appendages fail to migrate to their normal position between the maxillary palps. ###xml 494 498 494 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">1999</xref>

Short legs were the most common feature seen in Tc-tiotsh RNAi larvae, being present in all larvae with labial defects, as well as in some larvae with apparently normal labial appendages (Table 2). The reduction in leg length stems from effects on the proximal leg segments. The coxa and trochanter are reduced and apparently fused, and the femur is slightly shortened (Fig. 5e). This phenotype is similar to that observed in the adult legs of hypomorphic Drosophila tsh mutants (Erkner et al. 1999) and thus may indicate a conserved role for tsh family genes in proximal limb development.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 19 28 19 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 143 144 143 144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="Fig5" ref-type="fig">5</xref>
###xml 305 306 305 306 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="Fig5" ref-type="fig">5</xref>
We also noted that Tc-tiotsh RNAi larvae show a pronounced curvature of the head and thoracic segments, such that the head bends forward (Fig. 5e). This phenotype apparently results from reduction in length (anterior to posterior) of the ventral portions of the thoracic segments and posterior head (Fig. 5b,c,f).

###begin title 56
Discussion
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 53 56 53 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 74 93 74 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium castaneum</italic>
###xml 175 178 175 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 74 93 <span type="species:ncbi:7070">Tribolium castaneum</span>
We have identified and cloned the lone member of the tsh gene family from Tribolium castaneum. Our analysis of this gene provides important insights into the evolution of the tsh gene family at the levels of both sequence and function.
###end p 57
###begin title 58
###xml 13 16 13 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
Evolution of tsh family genes
###end title 58
###begin p 59
###xml 85 88 85 88 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 232 235 232 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 300 304 300 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2005</xref>
###xml 321 325 321 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">2005</xref>
###xml 372 382 372 382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 389 392 389 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 397 400 397 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 539 542 539 542 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 611 614 611 614 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 658 667 658 667 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium</italic>
###xml 672 681 672 681 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anopheles</italic>
###xml 803 807 803 807 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hox3</italic>
###xml 817 826 817 826 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">zerkn&#252;llt</italic>
###xml 828 831 828 831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">zen</italic>
###xml 837 843 837 843 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bicoid</italic>
###xml 845 848 845 848 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">bcd</italic>
###xml 936 940 936 940 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">2001</xref>
###xml 957 961 957 961 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR39">1999</xref>
###xml 963 967 963 967 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR40">2002</xref>
Searches of several non-drosophilid insect genome sequences identified only a single tsh family member in each genome. These results corroborate previous suggestions (based on more limited data) that most insects have only a single tsh-related gene and that this is the ancestral state (Herke et al. 2005; Laugier et al. 2005). A key implication of this model is that the Drosophila genes tio and tsh are the result of a recent duplication event that occurred sometime after the divergence of mosquitoes and flies. Since that duplication, tsh has apparently diverged from the ancestral sequence faster than has tio (although both have diverged more than the Tribolium and Anopheles homologs). More rapid divergence by one member of a recently duplicated gene pair is not unprecedented. For example, the Hox3 paralogs zerknullt (zen) and bicoid (bcd) are believed to have arisen by duplication late in the dipteran lineage (Brown et al. 2001; Stauber et al. 1999, 2002), but the sequence of Zen is more similar to that of the Hox 3 proteins of other insects than it is to Bcd.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 491 492 491 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="Fig2" ref-type="fig">2</xref>
###xml 515 525 515 525 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 645 654 645 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium</italic>
###xml 852 861 852 861 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Anopheles</italic>
###xml 866 876 866 876 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1059 1069 1059 1069 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1087 1091 1087 1091 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">2004</xref>
Much of the sequence conservation between Tsh family members is found in the zinc finger regions. However, a few additional conserved motifs are present, particularly at the protein termini. The C-terminal region is well conserved between Tc-TioTsh, Ag-TioTsh, and Tio, but this conserved region is not found in Tsh. The conservation of the N-terminal region is particularly interesting. The Tsh family members for which N-terminal sequence is available fall into two distinct classes (Fig. 2c). Ag-TioTsh and both Drosophila proteins share an extended region of identity that is completely different than the short motif (MPRRKQ) shared by the Tribolium and vertebrate proteins. This pattern of conservation suggests that an ancestral Tsh family member had the MPRRKQ motif, which was replaced with a different motif sometime before the separation of Anopheles and Drosophila within the dipteran lineage. It is not clear what the function of these disparate motifs might be, as the vertebrate Tsh proteins function very similarly to Dm-Tsh when expressed in Drosophila (Manfroid et al. 2004). Perhaps these motifs are important in a particular tissue or at a different stage in development than those assayed. As additional insect Tsh family protein sequences become available, it will be possible to more accurately determine the point at which these N-terminal sequences began to diverge.
###end p 60
###begin title 61
###xml 13 16 13 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
Evolution of tsh family gene expression
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 39 42 39 42 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 78 81 78 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 150 153 150 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 192 195 192 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 200 203 200 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 207 217 207 217 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 259 263 259 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">2005</xref>
###xml 266 269 266 269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 332 335 332 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 463 466 463 466 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 485 488 485 488 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 545 548 545 548 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 559 562 559 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 600 603 600 603 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 658 661 658 661 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
Although the sequences of other insect tsh family members are most similar to tio, their expression patterns (where known) seem to be more similar to tsh. The embryonic expression patterns of tsh and tio in Drosophila are strikingly different (Laugier et al. 2005). tsh is expressed throughout the ectoderm of trunk segments, while tio is expressed in only a few cells of the trunk ectoderm (as well as in the Malpighian tubules and parts of the CNS and gut). In tsh mutants, however, tio is more broadly expressed in the trunk, indicating that tsh represses tio expression in this region. Likewise, tsh is ectopically expressed in the stomodeal region when tio expression is knocked down by RNAi.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 4 13 4 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 68 71 68 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 76 79 76 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 224 227 224 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 248 257 248 257 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 318 321 318 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 341 344 341 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 407 416 407 416 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 589 599 589 599 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
The Tc-tiotsh expression pattern includes similiarities to both the tsh and tio expression patterns. The ectodermal expression in the segmented region of the germband is reminiscent of, although more extensive than, that of tsh. On the other hand, Tc-tiotsh is expressed in the Malpighian tubule primordia, similar to tio. The expression of tio near the stomodeum might also correspond to the expression of Tc-tiotsh in the labrum and intercalary segment, although it is difficult to compare expression patterns in the cephalic regions of these insects due to the derived structure of the Drosophila larval head.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 27 36 27 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 84 87 84 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 92 95 92 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 192 195 192 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 200 203 200 203 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 241 250 241 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 587 591 587 591 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">1999</xref>
It is interesting that the Tc-tiotsh expression pattern approximates the sum of the tsh and tio patterns. This may indicate that the expression pattern of the ancestral gene that gave rise to tsh and tio had an expression pattern similar to Tc-tiotsh and that parts of its expression pattern (and perhaps its function) have been split between its daughters. Such division of labor resulting from complementary loss of particular expression domains or functions is termed subfunctionalization and has been suggested to play a key role in the maintenance of duplicated genes (Force et al. 1999).
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 44 47 44 47 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 105 124 105 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Thermobia domestica</italic>
###xml 148 168 148 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oncopeltus fasciatus</italic>
###xml 183 187 183 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2005</xref>
###xml 205 209 205 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">1999</xref>
###xml 459 469 459 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oncopeltus</italic>
###xml 598 602 598 602 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2005</xref>
###xml 95 103 <span type="species:ncbi:89055">firebrat</span>
###xml 105 124 <span type="species:ncbi:89055">Thermobia domestica</span>
###xml 134 146 <span type="species:ncbi:7536">milkweed bug</span>
###xml 148 168 <span type="species:ncbi:7536">Oncopeltus fasciatus</span>
###xml 546 555 <span type="species:ncbi:89055">firebrats</span>
###xml 618 631 <span type="species:ncbi:7536">milkweed bugs</span>
Among other insects, expression patterns of tsh family members have been reported only for the firebrat, Thermobia domestica, and the milkweed bug, Oncopeltus fasciatus (Herke et al. 2005; Peterson et al. 1999). In both cases, these genes are expressed in the gnathal, thoracic, and first abdominal segments, with very weak (if any) expression in the remainder of the abdomen. Both insects also show expression in the posterior tip of the embryo (see below). Oncopeltus has additional expression in the procephalon. The leg expression pattern in firebrats has not been described, but Herke et al. (2005) reported that milkweed bugs show expression throughout the legs, which fades from the distal tips later in development.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 33 36 33 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 369 373 369 373 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">1999</xref>
###xml 404 407 404 407 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 471 475 471 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">1991</xref>
###xml 490 507 490 507 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oncopeltus tiptop</italic>
###xml 561 565 561 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2005</xref>
###xml 590 599 590 599 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 659 662 659 662 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 729 733 729 733 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">2005</xref>
###xml 191 200 <span type="species:ncbi:89055">firebrats</span>
Interestingly, all of the insect tsh family genes whose expression has been described are expressed in the post-abdominal region. However, these domains have been interpreted differently. In firebrats, expression in this region is reported to represent the anlagen of the cerci and the median caudal filament (appendages of the 11th abdominal segment) (Peterson et al. 1999). The posterior expression of tsh has been interpreted as the future anal opening (Fasano et al. 1991), and that of Oncopeltus tiptop is described only as the caudal region (Herke et al. 2005). Our results show that Tc-tiotsh is expressed in the Malpighian tubule primordia. Likewise, tio is expressed in the developing Malpighian tubules (Laugier et al. 2005). Thus, Malpighian tubule expression may have been part of the ancestral expression pattern of this gene family. It is possible that the posterior expression domains in other insects will, upon closer examination, be found to represent the developing Malpighian tubules as well.
###end p 66
###begin title 67
###xml 13 16 13 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
Evolution of tsh family function
###end title 67
###begin p 68
###xml 0 9 0 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 100 110 100 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 148 151 148 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 227 230 227 230 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 281 285 281 285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">1999</xref>
###xml 381 384 381 384 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 460 464 460 464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2005</xref>

###xml 693 696 693 696 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 726 736 726 736 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 738 741 738 741 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 898 902 898 902 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR15">1996</xref>
###xml 916 925 916 925 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 1009 1010 1009 1010 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="Fig4" ref-type="fig">4</xref>
###xml 1073 1076 1073 1076 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 1080 1090 1080 1090 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 1320 1324 1320 1324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2005</xref>
Tc-tiotsh is required for the proper development of the proximal portion of developing larval legs. Drosophila larvae lack external appendages, but tsh is expressed in the proximal regions of leg imaginal discs and hypomorphic tsh alleles cause proximal leg defects (Erkner et al. 1999). These results suggest that proximal leg expression and function were probably a feature of a tsh-like gene in the ancestor of beetles and flies. In contrast, Herke et al. (2005) reported that knockdown of Oncopeltus tiptop caused transformations of the distal legs to antennae. Based on this observation, they concluded that Oncopeltus tiptop acts as a distal leg selector gene, and suggested that tio or tsh might play a similar role in Drosophila. tsh expression is known to be restricted to the portion of the imaginal disc that will form the body wall and proximal leg segments (Gonzalez-Crespo and Morata 1996). Similarly, Tc-tiotsh is expressed in the proximal region of embryonic legs and leg imaginal discs (Fig. 4d and data not shown). We have also examined the expression of tio in Drosophila leg imaginal discs and found that expression is restricted to the body wall and proximal portion of the developing leg. Thus, it is highly unlikely that the "tiptop-like" distal leg-specifying function suggested by Herke et al. (2005) is performed by Tio, either alone or in combination with Tsh.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 98 107 98 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 152 166 152 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila tio</italic>
We cannot rule out additional, internal functions for Tc-TioTsh. In particular, the expression of Tc-tiotsh in the Malpighian tubules is intriguing, as Drosophila tio (and possibly other homologs) show similar expression.
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 31 40 31 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 85 95 85 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 148 151 148 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 334 338 334 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR38">2001</xref>
###xml 430 434 430 434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR37">2002</xref>
###xml 477 481 477 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">1997</xref>
###xml 574 577 574 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 659 662 659 662 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 676 679 676 679 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 694 704 694 704 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 795 798 795 798 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 836 839 836 839 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tio</italic>
###xml 844 847 844 847 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 855 865 855 865 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
The striking effects of larval Tc-tiotsh RNAi would not have been predicted based on Drosophila experiments, as relatively few adult effects of LOF tsh mutations have been reported. Other than the proximal leg defects that have been discussed above, the only other adult phenotypes reported are defects in wing posture (Soanes et al. 2001) and in structures that develop from the eye-antennal disc, namely, the eyes (Singh et al. 2002) and the maxillary palps (Bhojwani et al. 1997). This dearth of adult phenotypes could be a consequence of the pre-adult lethality of many tsh allele combinations. However, it might also be due to functional redundancy with tio. We examined tio expression in Drosophila imaginal discs and found that it is indeed expressed in a pattern very similar to that of tsh (data not shown). Thus, loss of both tio and tsh during Drosophila imaginal development might produce a more severe phenotype.
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 52 71 52 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 132 135 132 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 139 149 139 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 276 279 276 279 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 445 448 445 448 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 501 504 501 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 586 590 586 590 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">1994</xref>
###xml 606 610 606 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">1991</xref>
###xml 625 629 625 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">1992</xref>
###xml 657 660 657 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 665 670 665 670 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">disco</italic>
###xml 786 790 786 790 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">2004</xref>
The principal question prompting our studies of the Tribolium Tc-tiotsh gene was whether the trunk-specifying function reported for tsh in Drosophila was conserved in other insects. However, insights from our work and other recent reports compel us to first reexamine whether tsh itself is really a trunk-specifying gene. The key observations, which originally led to this conclusion, were the presence of head-like sclerites in embryos lacking tsh, and the positive correlation between expression of tsh and the presence of denticle belts (a trunk-specific feature) (de Zulueta et al. 1994; Fasano et al. 1991; Roder et al. 1992). The later discovery that tsh and disco form mutually exclusive domains in the trunk and head, respectively, seemed to support this view (Robertson et al. 2004). Below we synthesize recent studies that may lead to new interpretations of these observations.
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 33 43 33 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 64 67 64 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 242 246 242 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">2005</xref>
###xml 479 482 479 482 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 554 557 554 557 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 229 233 <span type="species:ncbi:51089">Mace</span>
A number of mutant phenotypes in Drosophila, including those of tsh and some Hox genes, feature small patches of sclerotic tissue. The previous interpretation of such patches as ectopic head tissue has been called into question. Mace et al. (2005) showed that the sclerites that form in a segment lacking Hox gene expression are correlated with breaches in epidermal integrity and the resulting activation of a wound response pathway. If in fact, the sclerotized cuticle seen in tsh mutants is scar tissue rather than head cuticle, the interpretation of tsh as a trunk-specifying gene would be less convincing, but still possible.
###end p 72
###begin p 73
###xml 13 16 13 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 96 100 96 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">2004</xref>
###xml 260 265 260 265 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">disco</italic>
###xml 297 300 297 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 401 406 401 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">disco</italic>
###xml 495 500 495 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">disco</italic>
###xml 505 508 505 508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 579 583 579 583 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR29">2007</xref>
###xml 607 617 607 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 651 654 651 654 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 752 755 752 755 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 869 872 869 872 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 1220 1223 1220 1223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 1301 1305 1301 1305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">1994</xref>
###xml 1347 1350 1347 1350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 1467 1475 1467 1475 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">wingless</italic>
###xml 1477 1485 1477 1485 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rhomboid</italic>
###xml 1490 1500 1490 1500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">shavenbaby</italic>
###xml 1519 1524 1519 1524 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">, tsh</italic>
###xml 1631 1641 1631 1641 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
The case for tsh acting as a trunk specifier was apparently strengthened when Robertson et al. (2004) proposed the attractive hypothesis that a system of zinc finger proteins acted in conjunction with the Hox genes to specify body regions. Within this system, disco specified gnathal segments and tsh specified trunk segments. However, the same laboratory recently reported that the conserved role of disco is to specify the medial portion of appendages, and suggested that the boundary between disco and tsh expression is actually an artifact of maggot morphology (Patel et al. 2007). That is, the reduced Drosophila larval head might lack proximal, tsh-expressing tissue (body wall) while retaining medial appendage tissue, whereas the trunk retains tsh-expressing tissue but has lost most appendage tissue. Taking this scenario a step further, ectopic expression of tsh might "restore" the body wall tissue of the head, creating larger, more complete segments. As the genes controlling segment formation also control denticle belt patterning, these enlarged head segments would have denticle belts as well. This interpretation is consistent with the observation that the additional denticle belts produced by ectopic tsh expression are interspersed with head-specific structures (de Zulueta et al. 1994). When considered in this light, perhaps tsh is no more a trunk identity gene than are other genes involved in segment formation and denticle patterning such as wingless, rhomboid, or shavenbaby. On the other hand, tsh does seem to provide the spatial information that limits full segment formation to the trunk, and thus in Drosophila could be considered a trunk-specifying gene.
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 13 22 13 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 107 116 107 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 265 274 265 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 434 443 434 443 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 521 534 521 534 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">prothoraxless</italic>
###xml 536 543 536 543 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-Antp</italic>
###xml 586 595 586 595 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 851 860 851 860 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 911 920 911 920 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
In contrast, Tc-tiotsh does not appear to act in any way as a trunk identity gene. This is not to say that Tc-tiotsh has no role in the development of trunk segments, but rather that we find no evidence that it specifies trunk vs head fates. In embryos depleted of Tc-tiotsh the ventral portions of the labial and thoracic segments are reduced, but not visibly transformed. Interestingly, some of the labial abnormalities observed in Tc-tiotsh RNAi larvae are reminiscent of those produced by loss of function alleles of prothoraxless (Tc-Antp) (Shippy, unpublished data). Knockdown of Tc-tiotsh during imaginal development produces adults with patterning defects in the thoracic and abdominal sclerites, including possible transformations of anterior abdominal sclerites to a more posterior identity. These phenotypes, together with the reduction in Tc-tiotsh expression seen in Df(HOMC) embryos, suggest that Tc-tiotsh is regulated to some extent by Hox genes and may carry out a subset of the functions of these genes in both the head and the trunk.
###end p 74
###begin p 75
###xml 15 24 15 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tc-tiotsh</italic>
###xml 90 93 90 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 161 170 161 170 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Tribolium</italic>
###xml 251 261 251 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Oncopeltus</italic>
###xml 325 328 325 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
###xml 467 470 467 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">tsh</italic>
Our studies of Tc-tiotsh indicate that trunk specification is not a universal function of tsh family members. It is possible that this function has been lost in Tribolium. However, expression pattern data from other insects and functional analysis in Oncopeltus argues against this conclusion. A more likely scenario is that tsh family members acquired a role in defining trunk at some point after the divergence of the coleopteran and dipteran lineages. Analysis of tsh function in additional holometabolous insects should help pinpoint when this event occurred.
