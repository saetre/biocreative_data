Staphylococcus aureus alpha-Hemolysin Activates the NLRP3-Inflammasome in Human and Mouse Monocytic Cells

###xml 1920 1924 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">MRSA</span>
Community Acquired Methicillin Resistant Staphylococcus aureus (CA-MRSA) causes severe necrotizing infections of the skin, soft tissues, and lungs. Staphylococcal alpha-hemolysin is an essential virulence factor in mouse models of CA-MRSA necrotizing pneumonia. S. aureus alpha-hemolysin has long been known to induce inflammatory signaling and cell death in host organisms, however the mechanism underlying these signaling events were not well understood. Using highly purified recombinant alpha-hemolysin, we now demonstrate that alpha-hemolysin activates the Nucleotide-binding domain and leucine-rich repeat containing gene family, pyrin domain containing 3 protein (NLRP3)-inflammasome, a host inflammatory signaling complex involved in responses to pathogens and endogenous danger signals. Non-cytolytic mutant alpha-hemolysin molecules fail to elicit NLRP3-inflammasome signaling, demonstrating that the responses are not due to non-specific activation of this innate immune signaling system by bacterially derived proteins. In monocyte-derived cells from humans and mice, inflammasome assembly in response to alpha-hemolysin results in activation of the cysteine proteinase, caspase-1. We also show that inflammasome activation by alpha-hemolysin works in conjunction with signaling by other CA-MRSA-derived Pathogen Associated Molecular Patterns (PAMPs) to induce secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-1beta and IL-18. Additionally, alpha-hemolysin induces cell death in these cells through an NLRP3-dependent program of cellular necrosis, resulting in the release of endogenous pro-inflammatory molecules, like the chromatin-associated protein, High-mobility group box 1 (HMGB1). These studies link the activity of a major S. aureus virulence factor to a specific host signaling pathway. The cellular events linked to inflammasome activity have clear relevance to the disease processes associated with CA-MRSA including tissue necrosis and inflammation.

CA-MRSA is the most commonly identified cause of skin infections seen in emergency rooms in the United States [1]. CA-MRSA can also cause severe, life threatening infections including necrotizing pneumonias and fasciitis, which are associated with very high mortality rates even in previously healthy patients [2], [3]. These necrotizing infections are characterized by localized necrosis and severe inflammation. People colonized with CA-MRSA suffer from some form of S. aureus infection at rates about seven times higher than people colonized with other S. aureus strains [4]. In addition to resistance to our most commonly used anti-staphylococcal antibiotics, CA-MRSA has increased virulence that is associated with the production of specific factors that assist this pathogen in causing disease [5].

All S. aureus produce secreted exotoxin virulence factors including several cytolytic, pore-forming toxins [6]. Both alpha-hemolysin (Hla) and Panton Valentine leukocidin (PVL) have been implicated in the pathogenesis of S. aureus necrotizing pneumonia [7], [8]. Pulmonary delivery of purified alpha-hemolysin has been shown to induce pulmonary hypertension and inflammation in rat and rabbit [9], [10]. Similar studies in mice with PVL have show that PVL can induce pulmonary inflammation and pneumonitis in mice, in the absence of the bacteria that produce it [8]. More recently, alpha-hemolysin has been shown to be required for S. aureus strains, including CA-MRSA, to promote pneumonia in a mouse model of this disease [7], [11]. Immunization with inactive alpha-hemolysin protects mice from S. aureus pneumonia and prevents production of IL-1beta and other cytokines associated with pulmonary exposure to S. aureus[12]. Most recently, passive immunization with alpha-hemolysin-neutralizing antibodies has been shown to protect mice from fatal S. aureus pneumonia [12], [13].

###xml 708 713 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>

In addition to inducing pulmonary inflammation, S. aureus alpha-hemolysin has been shown to induce inflammatory reactions in the eye, skin, and abdomen when injected into rodents [14], [15], [16]. The mechanisms by which alpha-hemolysin induces inflammation in these intact animals have not been fully evaluated. In endothelial cells, alpha-hemolysin induces platelet activating factor production [17], [18], [19]. In pulmonary epithelial derived cell lines, exposure to alpha-hemolysin causes release of nitric oxide and other inflammatory mediators [20]. alpha-hemolysin has also been shown to act synergistically with S. aureus particles to induce some cytokine secretion, including IL-6 and IL-1alpha in mouse peritoneal macrophages [21]. Finally, alpha-hemolysin has been shown to induce cell death and IL-1beta secretion from human monocytes [22]. The intracellular signaling mechanisms that link alpha-hemolysin to secretion of cytokines and other inflammatory mediators are unknown.

###xml 1087 1091 1081 1085 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Mariathasan1">[32]</xref>
###xml 1093 1097 1087 1091 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Kanneganti1">[33]</xref>
###xml 1099 1103 1093 1097 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Kanneganti2">[34]</xref>
###xml 1105 1109 1099 1103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Franchi1">[35]</xref>
###xml 1287 1291 1281 1285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Willingham1">[36]</xref>
###xml 1293 1297 1287 1291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Saito1">[37]</xref>
Physiologic inflammatory signaling can be triggered by pathogen and host derived molecules. The family of nucleotide binding domain and leucine rich repeat containing proteins known as NLR (formerly NALP, NOD, PYPAF, and CATERPILLER) have recently been implicated in several different inflammatory signaling pathways [23], [24]. Mutations in the gene encoding the NLR family member, NLRP3, are associated with hyperactive inflammatory signaling characterized by excessive IL-1beta [25], [26], [27]. Many NLR proteins, including NLRP3, have been found to oligomerize into a macromolecular complex known as the inflammasome [28], [29]. The NLRP3-inflammasome is a signaling complex that activates procaspase-1 and induces processing of caspase-1 dependent inflammatory cytokines (particularly IL-1beta and IL-18). The NLRP3-inflammasome is activated in response to a multitude of pro-inflammatory stimuli, including many pathogen-derived molecules, endogenous inducers of sterile inflammation, and non-pathogen microbial pore-forming toxins, including nigericin and maitotoxin [30], [31], [32], [33], [34], [35]. Recently, activation of NLRP3 has been found to initiate both caspase-1 dependent cytokine production and a caspase-independent pro-inflammatory program of necrotic cell death [36], [37].

Living S. aureus activates NLRP3-mediated signaling in cell culture, but the S. aureus-derived factors involved in initiating this signaling remained to be identified [32]. We now show that S. aureus alpha-hemolysin induces NLRP3-mediated signaling triggering caspase-1 activation and programmed necrosis.

Prior studies have demonstrated that S. aureus alpha-hemolysin could induce IL-1beta secretion in human monocytes [22]. We sought to determine whether we could see similar induction of IL-1beta secretion by alpha-hemolysin in THP-1 cells, a human monocyte-derived cell line. Commercial preparations of alpha-hemolysin are relatively crude, with manufacturers reporting approximately50% of preparations being protein and the homogeneity of the protein present not reported. In order to study alpha-hemolysin-induced inflammatory signaling we sought to prepare highly purified S. aureus alpha-hemolysin. Recombinant alpha-hemolysin bearing a hexa-histidine-tag was prepared after expression in Escherichia coli. The recombinant alpha-hemolysin was isolated from crude E. coli homogenates using immobilized metal affinity chromatography. In order to eliminate the possibility of E. coli LPS contamination of our preparation, the protein was washed extensively in pyrogen free buffers. After elution, the recombinant protein was exchanged into pyrogen free phosphate buffed saline using size exclusion chromatography. The resulting recombinant alpha-hemolysin runs as a single homogenous protein band on Coomasie Blue stained SDS PAGE (Fig. 1A). This protein is immunoreactive with multiple anti-alpha- hemolysin antibodies (Fig. 1B & Fig. 2C). This preparation of alpha-hemolysin induced IL-1beta secretion from THP-1 cells (Fig. 1C). In order to confirm that the ability to activate IL-1beta secretion depended on functional alpha-hemolysin, and not a contaminating substance in the preparation, we prepared an inactive mutant of alpha-hemolysin (H35L) that lacks hemolytic activity [12]. The mutant alpha-hemolysin was highly purified, just as the wild-type preparation, yet failed to elicit IL-1beta secretion (Fig. 1A, B, & C). A monoclonal antibody that neutralizes alpha-hemolysin activity and has been shown to protect mice from S. aureus pneumonia also reduced IL-1beta secretion induced by alpha-hemolysin when preincubated with the toxin prior to treatment of the cells (Fig. 1D) [13]. In order to demonstrate that alpha-hemolysin was inducing IL-1beta secretion through activation of caspase-1, caspase-1 activation was assessed by immunoblot analysis for the p10 subunit of active caspase-1 after THP-1 cells were treated with wild type or H35L mutant alpha-hemolysin. Caspase-1 p10 was observed only in cells treated with active alpha-hemolysin (Fig. 1E). These data all support the hypothesis that alpha-hemolysin activates IL-1beta processing and secretion in a manner similar to other non-pathogen derived pore forming toxins, nigericin and maitotoxin [32].

Unlike preparations of highly purified alpha-hemolysin, treatment of resting THP-1 cells with commercial preparations of alpha-hemolysin did not induce detectable levels of IL-1beta secretion (Fig. 2A). We sought to further characterize these commercial preparations of alpha-hemolysin. Using SDS-PAGE and Coomassie Blue protein staining, we found that these preparations contained at least 3 predominant protein bands (Fig. 2B). Immunoblot analysis of these proteins using antibodies directed to alpha-hemolysin revealed only a single band of immunoreactivity, suggesting the additional proteins within the preparation were derived from other S. aureus proteins rather than proteolysis of alpha-hemolysin (Fig. 2C). These data indicate that significantly less alpha-hemolysin was added the cells, when compared to highly purified recombinant alpha-hemolysin.

Production of mature IL-1beta is frequently studied in cells pretreated with lipopolysaccharide (LPS) or other Toll-like receptor (TLR) agonists, which are thought to induce expression of pro-IL-1beta. Indeed studies demonstrating that secretion of IL-1beta could be induced by alpha-hemolysin were carried out in LPS-treated monocytes [22]. Because S. aureus does not produce LPS, we sought to determine whether priming of THP-1 cells with heat killed S. aureus (HKSA) could support IL-1beta secretion in a manner analogous to LPS treatment. Treatment of THP-1 cells with heat killed S. aureus also resulted in minimal IL-1beta secretion (Fig. 2A). However, exposure of HKSA-primed THP-1 cells to either commercial or recombinant alpha-hemolysin induced more robust IL-1beta secretion (Fig. 2A). Consistent with its lower alpha-hemolysin content, the commercial alpha-hemolysin preparation induced less IL-1beta secretion than an equivalent dose of recombinant alpha-hemolysin. Two S. aureus derived TLR ligands [38], [39], [40], peptidoglycan, PGN, (TLR2) and lipotechoic acid, LTA, (TLR4) also caused a synergistic induction of IL-1beta secretion in conjunction with alpha-hemolysin treatment, while causing little or no IL-1beta secretion by themselves (Fig. 2D). Immunoblot analysis of cell lysates treated with HKSA, alpha-hemolysin, or both demonstrated that HKSA did indeed induce production of pro-IL-1beta while alpha hemolysin alone did not (Fig. 2E). Processing of IL-1beta, reflected by reduced pro-IL-1beta levels and detectable secreted processed IL-1beta, could only be detected by immunoblot in the HKSA-primed, alpha-hemolysin-treated cells (Fig. 2E). These data suggest that alpha-hemolysin acts downstream of pro-IL-1beta synthesis, at the level of processing or secretion, in order to induce IL-1beta secretion. Overall, these data demonstrate that multiple S. aureus-derived TLR ligands enchance alpha-hemolysin-induced IL-1beta secretion, though they are not required for caspase-1 activation.

The NLRP3 inflammasome has been identified as a critical signaling complex involved in activation of caspase-1 and subsequent IL-1beta secretion in response to many inflammatory stimuli, including nigericin and maitotoxin [32], [41]. We sought evidence that the NLRP3 inflammasome was required for alpha-hemolysin mediated IL-1beta secretion. For these studies, we utilized THP-1 derived cell lines that have been transduced with recombinant retrovirus expressing shRNA that "knock down" expression of the inflammasome components, ASC or NLRP3 [36], [42]. When compared to control cell lines expressing no shRNA or a scrambled shRNA, cells lacking expression of either NLRP3 or ASC failed to mount robust IL-1beta secretion responses to alpha-hemolysin treatment (Fig. 3A). To confirm these findings, we carried out a similar set of experiments in peritoneal macrophages from C57Bl/6 mice with genetic knock out of several inflammasome components: Nlrp3-/-, Asc-/-, and Casp1-/-. IL-1beta secretion was reduced in HKSA-primed, alpha-hemolysin-treated peritoneal macrophages derived from Nlrp3-/-, Asc-/-, and Casp1-/- mice when compared to wild-type controls (Fig. 3B). We also sought further evidence that alpha-hemolysin was inducing IL-1beta secretion through NLRP3-inflammasome dependent activation of caspase-1. We tested whether alpha-hemolysin treatment also led to the secretion of IL-18, another caspase-1 processed cytokine. As expected, control THP-1 cells secreted IL-18 in response to alpha-hemolysin. Like IL-1beta secretion, IL-18 secretion was also enhanced by pretreatment with HKSA. THP-1 cells lacking either NLRP3 or ASC expression failed to secrete significant levels of IL-18 (Fig. 3C). The activation of caspase-1 could be also be detected in THP-1 cells treated with alpha-hemolysin using immunoblot analysis for the active p10 subunit of caspase-1 in the absence of HKSA-priming. Intact NLRP3 and ASC expression was required for alpha-hemolysin induced caspase-1 activation as well (Fig. 3D). Immunoblot analysis also confirmed that the THP-1 cell lines lacking NLRP3 or ASC do induce substantial pro-IL-1beta production in response to priming with HKSA (Fig. 3E). Together these data suggest that the primary defect in alpha-hemolysin responsiveness in NLRP3 inflammasome deficient cells is in activation of caspase-1.

Mariathasan et. al. have demonstrated that bone marrow derived macrophages secrete IL-1beta in response to live S. aureus. This IL-1beta secretion in bone marrow derived macrophages was not significantly reduced when the cells were treated with S. aureus lacking alpha-hemolysin [32]. These reports suggest that either S. aureus expresses multiple toxins that activate the NLRP3-inflammasome or that alpha-hemolysin activates NLRP3 in some but not all monocyte-derived cell lineages. We treated bone marrow derived macrophages with HKSA, alpha-hemolysin, or the combination of agents and measured IL-1beta secretion. Surprisingly, we found wild type, Nlrp3-/-, and Asc-/- derived BMDM failed to secrete significant levels of IL-1beta to any of these stimuli (Fig. 4A). This suggests that BMDM do not respond to alpha-hemolysin at the doses used in these studies, unlike the other monocyte-derived cells studied. These BMDM produced pro-IL-1beta in response to LPS but not to HKSA preconditioning at the doses used (Fig. 4B). As observed by others, treatment of LPS-primed BMDM with ATP-induced processing of pro-IL-1beta and secretion of processed IL-1beta [30], [31], [32], [33]. At the same time, alpha-hemolysin did not activate processing of LPS-induced pro-IL-1beta (Fig. 4B). Our data support the fact that alpha-hemolysin can indeed activate the NLRP3 inflammasome, but suggests that this host response is likely to vary depending on the site of infection and types of host inflammatory cells present to combat it.

###xml 270 274 266 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Fujisawa1">[45]</xref>
###xml 1042 1049 1026 1033 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007446-g006">Fig. 6A</xref>
###xml 1396 1403 1376 1383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007446-g006">Fig. 6B</xref>
###xml 0 9 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
S. aureus alpha-hemolysin has been shown to induce both apoptosis and necrotic cell death in peripheral blood mononuclear cells [43], [44]. Our lab and others have recently demonstrated that NLRP3 activation leads to programmed necrosis, termed pyronecrosis [36], [37], [45]. Pyronecrosis is a form of inflammatory cell death and is distinct and independent of the process of apoptosis, which normally results in an orderly disassembly of the dying cells that limits inflammatory responses to the death. In order to confirm that alpha-hemolysin causes NLRP3-dependent pyronecrosis, we first sought to demonstrate that alpha-hemolysin-induced death lacked hallmarks characteristic of apoptosis including: activation of caspase-3, cleavage of PARP, and intranucleosomal cleavage of DNA. Immunoblot analysis of lysates from cells treated with alpha-hemolysin showed no evidence of caspase-3 activation or cleavage of PARP, both of which could be induced in the THP-1 cells by treatment with Staurosporine (which is known to activate apoptosis) (Fig. 6A). Intranucleosomal cleavage of DNA occurs in the setting of apoptosis and is reflected by reduced propidium iodide staining (sub-G1 DNA content) of fixed cells detected by flow cytometry. While staurosporine induced accumulation of sub-G1 DNA content, alpha- hemolysin treated cells maintained DNA content profiles similar to untreated controls (Fig. 6B). Overall, these studies demonstrate that alpha-hemolysin-induced NLRP3-mediated cell death lacks traditional apoptotic features.

To demonstrate that alpha-hemolysin induces programmed necrosis in THP-1 cells, we used Transmission Electron Microscopy to examine the morphology of alpha-hemolysin treated cells. The alpha-hemolysin treated cells lacked morphologic features of apoptotic cells seen in staurosporine treated cells, including chromatin condensation and blebbing of apoptotic bodies from the plasma membrane (Fig. 6C). Instead they have necrotic morphology with loss of plasma membrane integrity and cytoplasmic contents as well as intact nuclei with loose chromatin. Necrotic cell death results in the release of the chromosomal protein HMGB1. Release of HMGB1 is also observed from apoptotic cells in tissue culture as a result of secondary necrosis, but this occurs at a much later time point than is seen in primary necrosis [36]. Immunoblot analysis of cell culture supernatants from alpha-hemolysin treated THP-1 cells demonstrates that alpha-hemolysin induced death is accompanied by early release of HMGB1 protein, which is not seen in staurosporine treated cells (Fig. 6D). Staurosporine-induced apoptotic death was confirmed in these cultures by immunoblot analysis for PARP cleavage (Fig. 6D). Prior studies have demonstrated that induction of IL-1beta processing through activation of the NLRP3-inflammasome in response to the pore-forming toxin Nigericin requires intracellular potassium depletion. Both NLRP3-inflammasome dependent IL-1beta secretion and necrotic cell death were inhibited when cells were treated with S. aureus alpha-hemolysin in the presence of 130 mM KCl (Fig. 7A & B). This data, combined with the inability of mutant alpha-hemolysin to induce IL-1beta secretion or rapid necrosis, suggests that S. aureus alpha-hemolysin activates the inflammasome through potassium depletion and not through direct interaction with other host signaling proteins. In total, this work demonstrates that alpha-hemolysin actually requires host NLRP3 signaling in order to induce necrotic cell death.

Though the NLRP3 inflammasome activates the cysteine proteinase, caspase-1, NLRP3- dependent programmed necrosis has been shown to be independent of caspase activation. Pretreatment of THP-1 cells with a cell permeable pancaspase inhibitor did not block alpha-hemolysin induced cell death (Fig. 8A). We confirmed the inhibitor was functioning by examining alpha-hemolysin-mediated IL-1beta secretion in the treated cells, which was completely abrogated by treatment with caspase inhibitor (Fig. 8B). We also studied induction of pyronecrosis in peritoneal derived macrophages treated with alpha hemolysin. While alpha-hemolysin induced release of HMGB1 into the culture supernatant at levels equivalent to detergent induced lysis from wild type peritoneal macrophages, Nlrp3-/- macrophages were resistant to alpha-hemolysin induced HMGB1 release (Fig. 8C). Consistent with our findings using caspase inhibitors, release of HMGB1 was induced by alpha-hemolysin in Casp1-/- macrophages (Fig. 8C). Combined, these data confirm that alpha-hemolysin induced pyronecrosis is independent of caspase-1 activation and IL-1beta secretion, which is consistent with reports from other groups.

###begin title 35
Discussion
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 54 63 54 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 530 534 511 515 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Bhakdi1">[22]</xref>
###xml 54 63 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
We have demonstrated that highly purified recombinant S. aureus alpha-hemolysin is capable of inducing activation of caspase-1 through the NLRP3 inflammasome. Activation of NLRP3 by alpha-hemolysin induces cytokine processing and programmed necrosis, both of which can explain many of the pro-inflammatory properties of alpha-hemolysin that have been observed in the past. In fact, these studies identify the mechanism by which alpha-hemolysin induces IL-1beta secretion, an observation that was published almost twenty years ago [22].
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 25 34 25 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 93 99 90 96 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et. al</italic>
###xml 112 121 109 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 218 222 201 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Mariathasan1">[32]</xref>
###xml 449 454 424 429 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 572 581 547 556 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 599 608 574 583 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 734 743 709 718 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 1059 1063 1034 1038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Bhakdi1">[22]</xref>
###xml 25 34 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
###xml 112 121 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
###xml 572 581 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
###xml 599 608 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
###xml 734 743 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
In the initial report of S. aureus activating NLRP3-mediated IL-1beta secretion, Mariathasan et. al. found that S. aureus lacking alpha-hemolysin, beta-hemolysin, or gamma- hemolysin still activated IL-1beta secretion [32]. Our findings using purified toxin clearly demonstrate that NLRP3 is activated by alpha-hemolysin. Because bone marrow derived macrophages do not respond robustly to alpha-hemolysin in our studies, the findings of Mariathasan et al. are not surprising. Additionally, given the large number of pore-forming and membrane disrupting toxins produced by S. aureus, it seems likely S. aureus may have redundant factors that activate NLRP3-signaling. Supporting this possibility, we have found that other pore-forming S. aureus toxins (particularly Panton Valentine Leukocidin) also induce NLRP3-dependent cytolysis (data not shown). Since some of these toxins exert differential cytolytic activity based on cell type, the ability to activate NLRP3-mediated signaling may be important in establishing infection in a variety of infection sites [22].
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 262 266 254 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Bantel1">[46]</xref>
###xml 704 708 688 692 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Essmann1">[44]</xref>
The ability of alpha-hemolysin to activate programmed cell death in host cells has been the subject of numerous studies. In early studies, alpha-hemolysin was shown to activate apoptotic machinery in studies carried out primarily in lympocyte derived cell lines [46]. This report demonstrated that apoptotic caspases were activated and down stream apoptotic effects were observed, including cleavage of alpha-fodrin and intranucleosomal DNA cleavage. However, these effects were studied at times between 4 and 24 hours. A second report by this group later demonstrated that alpha-hemolysin induced cell death, as measured by loss of membrane integrity, occurred even if apoptotic machinery was inhibited [44]. Additionally, they demonstrated that alpha-hemolysin induced death was accompanied by morphologic features of necrosis and release of HMGB1 into the culture supernatant. We now show that pyronecrosis is activated very rapidly by alpha-hemolysin, in a timeframe earlier than has previously been characterized for alpha hemolyin-induced death and with features consistent with these other reports. Namely, we report alpha-hemolysin induced death is independent of caspase-activation, has morphologic features of necrosis, and is accompanied by early release of HMGB1. Interestingly, when taken out to 16 hours, we did observe some cells with sub-G1 DNA content, which may represent activation of apoptotic machinery in cells from the population that resisted activation of pyronecrosis. Overall, we have demonstrated that, in monocytic cells, activation NLRP3-dependent pyronecrosis by alpha-hemolysin is the primary mechanism underlying the cytolytic activity of the toxin.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 439 443 431 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Srivastava1">[47]</xref>
###xml 612 618 597 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007446-g005">Fig. 5</xref>
###xml 994 998 971 975 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Menzies1">[48]</xref>
###xml 1026 1029 999 1002 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Hla</italic>
###xml 1112 1121 1085 1094 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 1132 1136 1105 1109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Bramley1">[49]</xref>
###xml 1138 1142 1111 1115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Patel1">[50]</xref>
###xml 1341 1350 1306 1315 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 1428 1431 1393 1396 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-BubeckWardenburg1">[7]</xref>
###xml 1433 1437 1398 1402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-BubeckWardenburg2">[11]</xref>
###xml 1513 1522 1474 1483 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 1601 1605 1559 1563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-BubeckWardenburg3">[12]</xref>
###xml 1689 1698 1647 1656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 1803 1810 1761 1768 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0007446-g001">Fig. 1D</xref>
###xml 1812 1816 1770 1774 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0007446-Ragle1">[13]</xref>
###xml 2132 2141 2090 2099 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 2307 2316 2265 2274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. aureus</italic>
###xml 1112 1121 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
###xml 1341 1350 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
###xml 1503 1507 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1513 1522 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
###xml 1596 1600 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1689 1698 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
###xml 1744 1748 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 2132 2141 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
###xml 2307 2316 <span type="species:ncbi:1280">S. aureus</span>
The activation of host NLRP3 by alpha-hemolysin requires the hemolytic activity of the toxin. Our data demonstrates that mutations in the toxin that eliminate hemolytic activity also eliminate the toxin's cytolytic activity in monocyte derived cells and ability to induce NLRP3-inflammasome signaling. A recent study of a similar alpha-hemolysin mutant demonstrates that this mutant toxin is capable of inducing an apoptotic host response [47]. Our experiments also demonstrate that although mutant alpha-hemolysin does not induce NLRP3-dependent IL-1beta secretion, it does induce cell death with a time delay (Fig. 5). We suspect that this death may represent apoptotic death observed by others using mutant alpha-hemolysin, though formal demonstration of this requires further investigation. Interestingly, this mutation also renders the toxin incapable of causing death in animal models of alpha-hemolysin induced peritonitis, presumably due to reduced activation of inflammatory signaling [48]. The alpha-hemolysin gene, Hla, is a critical virulence factor in cellulitis, mastitits, and pneumonia models of S. aureus infection [49], [50]. We speculate that activation of inflammatory signaling through the inflammasome may be essential for alpha -hemolysin to mediate virulence. In support of this hypothesis, alpha-hemolysin deficient S. aureus fail to establish lethal infection or induce significant pulmonary infection [7], [11]. Additionally, immunization with mutant alpha-hemolysin protects mice from S. aureus pneumonia and reduces IL-1beta production in the lungs of the challenged mice [12]. Furthermore, we have now shown a monoclonal antibody, which is protective against S. aureus pneumonia in passive immunization studies of mice, inhibits NLRP3-mediated signaling in cultured cells (Fig. 1D) [13]. It is certainly possible that host inflammatory responses and programmed necrosis could damage host tissue architecture and compromise the innate capacity of these tissues to resist infection. Further experimentation is clearly required to determine the role of NLRP3 inflammasome signaling in the pathogenesis of S. aureus infection. If this hypothesis is born out, inhibition of inflammasome signaling may turn out to be a useful adjuvant therapy to appropriate antibiotic use in severe S. aureus infections.

Caspase-1 and actin were then simultaneously immunoprecipitated by incubation with antibodies to caspase-1 p10 (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, sc-515) and actin (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, sc-7210) from Santa Cruz Biotechnology and Ultralink protein G (Thermo Scientific/Pierce, product # 53125) overnight at 4degreesC. The immunoprecipitated proteins were analyzed by immunoblot with antibodies directed to caspase-1 (Imgenex, product # IMG-5028) and actin (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, sc-8432 HRP).
