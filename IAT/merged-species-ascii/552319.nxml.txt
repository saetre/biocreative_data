The small heat-shock proteins IbpA and IbpB reduce the stress load of recombinant Escherichia coli and delay degradation of inclusion bodies
###end article-title 0

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 254 260 254 260 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 389 405 385 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 362 367 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 389 405 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
The permanently impaired protein folding during recombinant protein production resembles the stress encountered at extreme temperatures, under which condition the putative holding chaperones, IbpA/IbpB, play an important role. We evaluated the impact of ibpAB deletion or overexpression on stress responses and the inclusion body metabolism during production of yeast alpha-glucosidase in Escherichia coli.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 12 17 12 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
###xml 330 335 326 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
###xml 745 751 731 737 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
Deletion of ibpAB, which is innocuous under physiological conditions, impaired culture growth during alpha-glucosidase production. At higher temperatures, accumulation of stress proteins including disaggregation chaperones (DnaK and ClpB) and components of the RNA degradosome, enolase and PNP, was intensified. Overexpression of ibpAB, conversely, suppressed the heat-shock response under these conditions. Inclusion bodies of alpha-glucosidase started to disaggregate after arrest of protein synthesis in a ClpB and DnaK dependent manner, followed by degradation or reactivation. IbpA/IbpB decelerated disaggregation and degradation at higher temperatures, but did hardly influence the disaggregation kinetics at 15degreesC. Overexpression of ibpAB concomitant to production at 42degreesC increased the yield of alpha-glucosidase activity during reactivation.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 196 201 196 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
IbpA/IbpB attenuate the accumulation of stress proteins, and - at high temperatures - save disaggregated proteins from degradation, at the cost, however, of delayed removal of aggregates. Without ibpAB, inclusion body removal is faster, but cells encounter more intense stress and growth impairment. IbpA/IbpB thus exert a major function in cell protection during stressful situations.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 58 75 58 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 112 113 112 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 114 115 114 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 191 192 191 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 193 194 193 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 271 272 271 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 273 274 273 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 405 406 405 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 407 408 407 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 409 411 409 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 553 555 553 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 556 558 556 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 645 662 645 662 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 663 665 663 665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 760 762 760 762 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 763 765 763 765 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 58 74 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 645 661 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Production of recombinant proteins as inclusion bodies in Escherichia coli can induce several stress responses [1-3] and may interfere with primary metabolism and cellular protein synthesis [4-6]. The metabolic burden of the synthesis of recombinant and stress proteins [7,8] may result in stop of the synthesis of components of the protein synthesising machinery, and ribosomes may be actively degraded [4,9,10]. Most commonly, the heat-shock response is induced, as the recombinant proteins - unable to fold properly - overload the chaperone network [11,12]. While appropriate execution of stress responses is of vital importance for the host Escherichia coli [13], the high metabolic load of strong foreign protein production can prevent proper adaptation [14,15]. The stress situation can become permanent, and only few cellular proteins except heat-shock proteins are synthesised.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 176 178 176 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 179 181 179 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 540 542 540 542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
Analogously, incubation at extreme temperatures is characterised by near exclusive heat-shock protein synthesis as a consequence of extensive aggregation of cellular proteins [18,19]. To deal with this challenge, the chaperone DnaK changes its function: Under physiological conditions, DnaK promotes folding of kinetically trapped intermediates by ATP-dependent partial unfolding, but at high temperatures it becomes a holding chaperone and binds its client proteins permanently, unable to release them in a folding-competent conformation [20].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 288 290 288 290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 460 462 460 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 463 465 463 465 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 477 485 477 485 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 712 714 706 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 715 717 709 711 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 757 759 751 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 918 927 912 921 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 979 981 973 975 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 982 984 976 978 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 1108 1110 1102 1104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 1111 1113 1105 1107 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 1228 1230 1222 1224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 1231 1233 1225 1227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 1286 1295 1280 1289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 1299 1307 1293 1301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 1347 1349 1341 1343 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 1350 1352 1344 1346 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 1402 1408 1396 1402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 1452 1458 1446 1452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 1459 1461 1453 1455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 1462 1464 1456 1458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 1465 1467 1459 1461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 1614 1616 1608 1610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 1617 1619 1611 1613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 1620 1622 1614 1616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 477 484 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The small heat-shock proteins (sHsps) are intrinsic holding chaperones of multimeric structure. The multimers dissociate at high temperatures into smaller entities for exposure of client binding sites. After binding of misfolded client proteins, the sHsps form large globular assemblies [21]. Thereby, they prevent irreversible aggregation, and under permissive conditions transfer the client proteins to ATP-dependent chaperones for refolding or degradation [22,23]. Also the E. coli sHsps, IbpA and IbpB, reversibly dissociate upon prolonged incubation at 50degreesC from multimers of 2 - 3 MDa to monomers in the case of IbpA and to oligomers of 650 - 700 kDa in the case of IbpB, exposing hydrophobic sites [24,25]. IbpA and IbpB are highly homologous [26], but show distinct features. While IbpB is soluble when overexpressed alone, prevents inactivation or aggregation of proteins upon heat and oxidative stress in vitro and facilitates their subsequent refolding by DnaK [23-25], a role for IbpA in mediating the transfer of IbpB and other proteins into the insoluble cell fraction has been proposed [19,27]. IbpA and IbpB have been found associated with thermally aggregated cellular proteins and with inclusion bodies [26,28]. The resolubilisation of protein aggregates, which in vitro and in vivo is mediated by ClpB together with DnaK [28-31], can be retarded both in strains overexpressing ibpAB as well as in some cases in strains lacking ibpAB [19,32,33]. IbpA and IbpB might control the reversible transition between soluble and aggregated forms that account for the plasticity of inclusion bodies [27,34,35].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 74 80 74 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 127 129 127 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 152 158 152 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 315 321 309 315 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 343 354 337 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB dnaK </italic>
###xml 391 393 385 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 394 396 388 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 716 741 702 727 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 765 773 751 759 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 910 916 896 902 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 924 926 910 912 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 995 1003 981 989 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1035 1037 1021 1023 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 716 740 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 765 772 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 995 1002 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The role of IbpA/IbpB in cell protection is less clear. Overexpression of ibpAB confers resistance to heat and other stresses [37], but deletion of the ibpAB operon does not affect growth even at high temperatures. Only recovery from prolonged exposure to extreme temperatures such as 50degreesC are impaired in an ibpAB mutant, and growth of ibpAB dnaK double mutants at high temperatures [19,36]. In this study we exploit the similarities between incubation at extreme temperature and production of aggregation-protein proteins to examine the functions of the small heat-shock proteins IbpA/IbpB in inclusion body metabolism and stress relief during alpha-glucosidase production. The enzyme alpha-glucosidase from Saccharomyces cerevisiae produced in recombinant E. coli is a suitable model system. It accumulates as inclusion bodies and induces the heat-shock response including prolonged expression of the ibpAB operon [17]. It is, however, also able to fold into its native conformation in E. coli if produced at low temperature [38], enabling the study of in-vivo-reactivation kinetics.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Results
###end title 13
###begin title 14
###xml 70 76 66 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">IbpAB </italic>
IbpA/IbpB levels during alpha-glucosidase production with and without IbpAB coexpression
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 126 132 122 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 272 273 258 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 342 348 328 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 510 516 492 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 786 787 758 759 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 795 801 767 773 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 1187 1188 1159 1160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
The concentrations of IbpA and IbpB were estimated from the proteomes of cells producing alpha-glucosidase for two hours. The ibpAB operon was induced already during alpha-glucosidase production at 30degreesC, with moderately higher levels at elevated temperatures (Table 1), whereas no IbpA/IbpB was detected in the plasmid-free strain. The ibpAB operon including its own promoter was inserted immediately downstream of the alpha-glucosidase gene into the plasmid pKK177-3/GLUCP1, expecting overexpression of ibpAB from its heat-shock promoter and as a tricistronic operon together with alpha-glucosidase. This plasmid gave ten and four times higher concentrations of IbpA and IbpB, respectively, at 30degreesC, and the sHsp level increased further in proportion to temperature (Table 1). With ibpAB overexpression, the IbpA accumulation was intensified more strongly and the ratio of IbpA to IbpB rose from 2-3 to 6-8. As the two sHsp may exert different functions, this change need to be accounted for. Depending on the temperature, overexpression increased the concentrations of IbpA and IbpB relative to their concentrations without overexpression four to more than 25 times (Table 1). This wide range of concentrations served to investigate the impact of IbpA/IbpB on alpha-glucosidase production and in-vivo-reactivation.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Elevated levels of IbpA/IbpB increase alpha-glucosidase accumulation
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 34 42 30 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 66 72 62 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 116 122 112 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 333 334 325 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 519 520 501 502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 34 41 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
alpha-Glucosidase was produced in E. coli MC4100 with and without ibpAB overexpression and in an otherwise isogenic ibpAB deletion mutant. alpha-Glucosidase accumulation was enhanced at elevated IbpA/IbpB level and reduced in the deletion strain in a temperature dependent manner; the effect was strongest at high temperature (Table 2). At temperatures below 30degreesC, the IbpA/IbpB levels did not influence the amount of alpha-glucosidase, which accumulated mainly in the soluble cell fraction in active form (Table 2).
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 29 35 29 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 93 94 93 94 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 133 138 133 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
Post-induction growth of the ibpAB deletion mutant was retarded at higher temperatures (Fig. 1), although there was no impact of the ibpAB deletion on growth without induction of alpha-glucosidase synthesis (not shown) or concomitant to production at lower temperature.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
Thus, the sHsp are effective only under conditions that favour inclusion body formation.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
IbpA/IbpB attenuate accumulation of stress proteins during alpha-glucosidase production
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 268 269 254 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 406 407 392 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 511 512 491 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 586 592 566 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 689 690 669 670 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 800 806 774 780 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 1023 1029 991 997 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
To study the impact of IbpA/IbpB on cellular reactions during alpha-glucosidase production, the proteomes of cultures producing alpha-glucosidase for two hours at various temperatures were separated by two-dimensional gel electrophoresis (shown for 42degreesC in Fig. 2). While chaperones assisting de-novo protein folding such as GroEL and GroES were not significantly influenced by IbpA/IbpB level (Fig. 2), the DnaK concentration showed a negative correlation with IbpA/IbpB availability at 42degreesC (Fig. 3). The disaggregating chaperone ClpB reached higher concentrations in the ibpAB deletion mutant at all temperatures, in addition to the induction at elevated temperatures (Fig. 3). ClpB accumulation was repressed, however, in the wildtype strain at 30degreesC, and with overexpression of ibpAB both at 30 and 37degreesC. As the overall level of IbpA/IbpB was low at these temperatures, and other cellular proteins were not affected, this repression is unlikely to be a passive effect of the metabolic burden of ibpAB expression.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 127 128 127 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 232 234 232 234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 639 640 639 640 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 755 760 755 760 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
###xml 797 798 791 792 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
A similar pattern was found for enolase, which showed higher levels at elevated temperatures only in the deletion mutant (Fig. 3). As enolase is a glycolytic enzyme as well as a putative structural component of the RNA degradosome [39], we examined another metabolic enzyme, glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH), and polynucleotide phosphorylase (PNP), another RNA degradosome member. The positions on the 2D gels of further degradosome components, RNAse E and RhlB, are not listed in the database. While PNP accumulated to higher concentrations at elevated temperatures and reached highest levels in the deletion mutant (Fig. 3), GAPDH concentration was not significantly affected by temperature and had a slightly lower concentration in the ibpAB deletion mutant at 42degreesC (Fig. 3). Thus, enolase seems to be induced as a part of a stress response rather than as a metabolic enzyme.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 222 223 216 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
A typical house-keeping protein, the translational elongation factor EF-Tu, had a two to three times higher level at 42degreesC than at lower temperatures, but the concentrations were not effected by IbpA/IbpB level (Fig. 3).
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 160 166 160 166 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 202 208 202 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
Thus, while housekeeping proteins are not influenced by IbpA/IbpB levels, the accumulation of ClpB and components of the RNA degradosome was intensified in the ibpAB deletion mutant and attenuated with ibpAB overexpression. This indicates that IbpA/IbpB can relieve the cells of the stress encountered during alpha-glucosidase production.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Impact of chaperones on in-vivo-reactivation and degradation of alpha-glucosidase from inclusion bodies
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 106 114 102 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 155 161 151 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 207 212 203 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
###xml 214 218 210 214 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">clpB</italic>
###xml 223 227 219 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dnaK</italic>
###xml 477 479 457 459 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 502 508 482 488 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 839 844 807 812 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dnaK </italic>
###xml 848 852 816 820 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">clpB</italic>
###xml 982 984 950 952 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1</sup>
###xml 1011 1014 979 982 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1 </sup>
###xml 106 113 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The impact of chaperones on the metabolism of alpha-glucosidase inclusion bodies was examined in wildtype E. coli MC4100 with or without overexpression of ibpAB and in isogenic strains carrying deletions of ibpAB, clpB, or dnaK. After production of alpha-glucosidase at 37degreesC for two hours, the cultures were treated with tetracycline to arrest protein synthesis and transferred to 30degreesC to initiate disaggregation of inclusion bodies as reported for other proteins [34]. In the wildtype and ibpAB strains, disintegration of inclusion bodies, increase of alpha-glucosidase activity, and higher concentrations of alpha-glucosidase in the soluble cell fraction could be detected already within 20 min; the disaggregation was accompanied by degradation (cf. below). While the concentration of alpha-glucosidase was 10-25% higher in dnaK and clpB deletion mutants than in the wildtype strain, the specific activities obtained during the production phase were less than 0.1 Umg-1, compared to about 0.3 Umg-1 in the other strains (data not shown). Importantly, neither disaggregation, nor reactivation nor degradation took place in these mutants within two hours, and the concentration of alpha-glucosidase inclusion bodies remained unchanged.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 154 159 154 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
###xml 591 597 577 583 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 698 702 684 688 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4A&#8211;D</xref>
###xml 728 733 714 719 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
###xml 826 828 802 804 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4A</xref>
###xml 967 971 937 941 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4C,D</xref>
###xml 1067 1073 1027 1033 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 1101 1103 1061 1063 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4D</xref>
The kinetics of disaggregation, reactivation and degradation of inclusion bodies at modified levels of IbpA/IbpB, i.e. with deletion or overexpression of ibpAB, were investigated as a function of temperature. After production of alpha-glucosidase at 37degreesC, the cultures were transferred to various temperatures. As protein synthesis was arrested before the transfer, the initial IbpA/IbpB level did not depend on the disaggregation temperature. The alpha-glucosidase concentration in the insoluble cell fractions of all cultures decreased with time, but with different kinetics. In the ibpAB deletion strain, the disaggregation kinetics were not influenced by the incubation temperature (Fig. 4A-D). With overexpression of ibpAB, the removal of alpha-glucosidase from the inclusion bodies was fastest at 15degreesC (Fig. 4A), proceeding with similar rates as in the other strains. The disaggregation was, however, progressively slower at 30 and 37degreesC (Fig. 4C,D). At 37degreesC, alpha-glucosidase was largely preserved in the insoluble cell fraction of the ibpAB overexpressing strain (Fig. 4D). Thus, disaggregation was retarded at higher temperatures in an IbpA/IbpB dependent manner.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 290 294 276 280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4F,G</xref>
###xml 370 373 352 355 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1 </sup>
###xml 478 481 448 451 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1 </sup>
###xml 501 505 471 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4E,H</xref>
###xml 514 520 484 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
Disaggregation of alpha-glucosidase inclusion bodies was accompanied by an increase of the specific alpha-glucosidase activities in the cell extracts. The reactivation showed a clear temperature optimum in the range of 24 to 30degreesC, most evident with wildtype levels of IbpA/IbpB (Fig. 4F,G). The specific alpha-glucosidase activities increased sevenfold up to 2 Umg-1 within 3 h, whereas at both higher and lower temperatures of 37degreesC and 15degreesC, only 0.5-0.75 Umg-1 were obtained (Fig. 4E,H). Both, ibpAB overexpression and deletion, did not improve the maximum reactivation yields. The resolubilisation, measured as an increase of the alpha-glucosidase concentrations in the soluble cell fraction, showed very similar kinetics as the reactivation (data not shown).
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 236 238 222 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4I</xref>
###xml 252 258 238 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 300 304 286 290 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4I&#8211;L</xref>
###xml 355 360 341 346 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
###xml 472 478 458 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 513 515 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4L</xref>
A large part of the disaggregated alpha-glucosidase was subjected to proteolysis: within three hours after arrest of protein synthesis, 30 - 40% of alpha-glucosidase were lost due to degradation at 15degreesC in all three strains (Fig. 4I), and in the ibpAB deletion mutant at all temperatures (Fig. 4I-L). Similar to the effect on disaggregation, higher ibpAB expression levels or elevated temperatures synergistically retarded degradation, reducing the loss to 10% with ibpAB overexpression at 37degreesC (Fig. 4L). IbpA/IbpB thus influenced disaggregation, resolubilisation, reactivation and degradation in a temperature-dependent manner.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
IbpA/IbpB favour reactivation of alpha-glucosidase produced at high temperature
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 359 365 339 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 510 512 490 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5A</xref>
###xml 697 699 677 679 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5A</xref>
###xml 772 774 752 754 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 1027 1029 1007 1009 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 1093 1095 1073 1075 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5A</xref>
As IbpA/IbpB are designed for action at extreme temperature, alpha-glucosidase inclusion bodies were produced at 42degreesC, followed by arrest of protein synthesis and incubation at 30degreesC. The production temperature did not influence the initial disaggregation kinetics (data not shown). About one third of the alpha-glucosidase produced concomitant to ibpAB overexpression was resistant to disaggregation after incubation over night, while it was eliminated apart from traces in the other strains (Fig. 5A). Also the removal of other components of the inclusion bodies, which appear as smear in the SDS PAGE of the insoluble cell fractions, were impaired in the overexpressing strain (Fig. 5A), resembling the core inclusion bodies that remain with other proteins [34], whereas the inclusion bodies produced at low IbpA/IbpB levels were completely dissolved after prolonged incubation. Only outer membrane proteins (indicated by arrows) that result from coprecipitation of cell debris during inclusion body preparation [40] remain at constant level in the insoluble cell fraction (Fig. 5A). Thus, the delay of disaggregation by IbpA/IbpB is not specific to alpha-glucosidase, but effects also cellular proteins in a similar way.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 146 148 130 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4K</xref>
###xml 292 298 260 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 319 320 287 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
###xml 630 632 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4K</xref>
###xml 634 635 594 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref>
Production at 37degreesC resulted in degradation of 20% of alpha-glucosidase during incubation at 30degreesC for 3 h in the wildtype strain (Fig. 4K). After production at 42degreesC, however, there was less than 10% of alpha-glucosidase lost during reactivation at 30degreesC with or without ibpAB overexpression (Fig. 6). Thus, degradation of alpha-glucosidase after arrest of protein synthesis was predetermined by the preceding production temperature. The protection was dependent on IbpA/IbpB availability, as in the deletion mutant the loss of alpha-glucosidase was substantial regardless of the production temperature (Fig. 4K, 6).
###end p 32
###begin p 33
###xml 152 158 142 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 299 301 289 291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5B</xref>
###xml 511 513 497 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5B</xref>
Consequently, disaggregated alpha-glucosidase was quantitatively transferred to the soluble cell fraction after production at 42degreesC concomitant to ibpAB overexpression, and became the most prominent band in the gel. The band intensity increased from 2 to 7% of the total cellular protein (Fig. 5B). In contrast, significantly less alpha-glucosidase could be detected in the soluble fraction of the deletion mutant even after prolonged incubation, finally corresponding to only 4% of cellular protein (Fig. 5B).
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 116 122 112 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 143 145 139 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7B</xref>
###xml 214 220 210 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 384 388 380 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7A,B</xref>
###xml 550 556 536 542 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 577 579 563 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7B</xref>
###xml 736 742 718 724 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 820 824 790 794 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7A,B</xref>
The alpha-glucosidase activity increased during the initial reactivation phase with similar slopes with and without ibpAB overexpression (Fig. 7B). Stationary level of activity was reached earlier, however, in the ibpAB deletion mutant and in the wildtype strain without overexpression. Hence the final activities in these strains were independent of the production temperature (Fig. 7A,B). In contrast, the activity continued to increase for about 12 h after arrest of protein synthesis subsequent to alpha-glucosidase production at 42degreesC with ibpAB overexpression (Fig. 7B), resulting in higher activity yields than obtained in the wild type strain. The final alpha-glucosidase activities after production with overexpression of ibpAB at 42degreesC were two times higher than after production at 37degreesC (Fig. 7A,B). IbpA/IbpB can thus ameliorate reactivation of inclusion bodies produced at high temperature.
###end p 34
###begin title 35
Discussion
###end title 35
###begin title 36
###xml 53 59 53 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
Intensified stress response and growth impairment in ibpAB deletion strain
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 29 35 29 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 198 200 198 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 201 203 201 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 204 206 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 207 209 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 407 413 399 405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 507 513 499 505 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 818 824 806 812 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 956 974 938 956 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacillus subtilis </italic>
###xml 975 977 957 959 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 1017 1029 999 1011 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli eno </italic>
###xml 1084 1086 1066 1068 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 956 973 <span type="species:ncbi:1423">Bacillus subtilis</span>
###xml 1017 1024 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Impairment of cell growth by ibpAB deletion is observed only under conditions characterised by depletion of free DnaK, such as under extremely high temperature or artificially controlled low level [19,33,36,37]. Accumulation of alpha-glucosidase as inclusion bodies mimics the aspect of permanently impaired protein folding, and growth impairment during alpha-glucosidase production was more intense in the ibpAB mutant. Moreover, DnaK controls the expression of heat-shock genes. Consequently, chromosomal ibpAB and other heat-shock genes were induced during alpha-glucosidase production. Especially in the deletion strain, lack of IbpA/IbpB was compensated by enhanced accumulation of the disaggregating chaperone ClpB. Also the levels of two components of the RNA degradosome, enolase and PNP, were elevated in the ibpAB deletion strain at 42degreesC. Enolase homologues have been reported to be induced by heat shock in a variety of species, including Bacillus subtilis [41], but there was no indication that the E. coli eno gene can be induced by heat-shock or high temperature [42].
###end p 37
###begin title 38
Both ClpB and DnaK are essential for disaggregation and de novo protein folding
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 50 56 50 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 144 146 144 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 457 459 453 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 460 462 456 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 463 465 459 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 466 468 462 464 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 469 471 465 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 560 569 556 565 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 618 620 614 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
###xml 621 623 617 619 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 624 626 620 622 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
ClpB and DnaK accumulated to higher levels in the ibpAB deletion strain. These chaperones cooperate with IbpA/IbpB in a "disaggregation triad" [33]. Each chaperone, ClpB and DnaK, was essential for reactivation as well as degradation of alpha-glucosidase, indicating that both processes operate after disaggregation of the inclusion bodies. With other protein aggregates, varying dependencies on DnaK and ClpB for degradation or reactivation were observed [29,30,32,43-45]. To the aggregate-specific factors influencing the chaperone demand for disaggregation in vitro belong their sizes and accessible surface areas [31,46,47]. Also the substrate specificity of chaperones may influence the protein-specific requirements.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 31 36 31 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">clpB </italic>
###xml 40 45 40 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dnaK </italic>
###xml 162 167 158 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">clpB </italic>
###xml 179 187 175 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">de novo </italic>
###xml 251 253 247 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 416 418 412 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 559 561 555 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
The impaired disaggregation in clpB and dnaK mutants reduced the accumulation of active alpha-glucosidase already during production, and a negative impact of the clpB deletion on de novo folding has been observed also with a number of other proteins [48]. Resolubilisation from aggregates might thus be a general route to active protein, in accordance with a quantitative model of inclusion body formation kinetics [16]. This constitutes a major difference to the irreversible aggregation at extreme temperatures, when the client release of DnaK is retarded [20].
###end p 40
###begin title 41
IbpA/IbpB disburden DnaK and reduce degradation
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 182 184 178 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 580 586 568 574 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 614 619 602 607 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
###xml 704 706 688 690 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 707 709 691 693 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 762 768 740 746 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 1079 1085 1049 1055 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 1118 1124 1088 1094 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 1141 1143 1111 1113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
Ongoing attempts to fold alpha-glucosidase will finally result in its degradation. IbpA/IbpB, which are postulated to recognise similar traits of aggregation prone proteins as DnaK [19], will withdraw alpha-glucosidase from the futile cycle of DnaK binding and release. Thereby, IbpA/IbpB on the one hand disburden the DnaK system to make resources available to repress stress responses, and on the other hand protect alpha-glucosidase from degradation. These functions might be related, as the heat-shock response includes also proteases that may accumulate to lower levels with ibpAB overexpression. Deletion of ibpAB, conversely, lowers the amount of alpha-glucosidase and other recombinant proteins [27,49]. Consequently, unlike to incubation at 50degreesC, ibpAB deletion did not increase aggregation during alpha-glucosidase production, rather reduced accumulation of insoluble alpha-glucosidase. Functional replacement of DnaK by IbpA/IbpB is also reflected by the amount of DnaK associated with inclusion bodies and aggregated cellular proteins, that is increased in the ibpAB deletion mutant and decreased by ibpAB overexpression [[19], unpublished results].
###end p 42
###begin title 43
IbpA/IbpB prevent degradation in a temperature-dependent manner
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 350 352 344 346 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 371 377 365 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 437 439 431 433 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
Disaggregation and degradation of inclusion bodies took place after arrest of protein synthesis. The disaggregation at and above 30degreesC was retarded by high levels of IbpA/IbpB. In wildtype cells, IbpA/IbpB accumulation is therefore restricted to requiring conditions, e.g. by degradation of IbpA/IbpB after dissociation from protein aggregates [33] or repression of ibpAB expression despite an otherwise active heat-shock response [14].
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 215 217 215 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 415 420 409 414 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB</italic>
###xml 517 519 511 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 520 522 514 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 523 525 517 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 526 528 520 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 735 741 729 735 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 820 822 814 816 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 823 825 817 819 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
Temperatures during both, formation and disaggregation of inclusion bodies, were important for the rate of disaggregation and yield of reactivation. Irreversible structural changes of IbpA/IbpB at low temperatures [25] may impair the protective function, making the disaggregation kinetics at 15degreesC independent of the IbpA/IbpB level. Hence with other systems, depending on the conditions, no influence of the ibpAB deletion on disaggregation kinetics or even delayed disaggregation in the mutant were observed [19,33,34,50]. The reactivation rate is determined by the difference of disaggregation and degradation rates, which are influenced in parallel by the availability of the sHsps. Hence reactivation was hardly effected by ibpAB deletion or overexpression, in accordance with observation for other proteins [32,34].
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 129 135 129 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 162 168 162 168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 471 474 463 466 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1 </sup>
###xml 479 485 471 477 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 613 615 605 607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
With high temperature during production, degradation during the subsequent disaggregation phase was nearly completely avoided in ibpAB proficient strains. In the ibpAB deletion strain, on the contrary, production temperature did not ameliorate the recovery of soluble alpha-glucosidase. Consequently, the reactivation phase was prolonged with higher IbpA/IbpB concentrations, and the final alpha-glucosidase activities were increased from 4 in the wildtype cells to 5 Umg-1 with ibpAB overexpression. As the yield of reactivated protein is more important for heat-stress survival than the rate of disaggregation [56], the reduction of degradation may also contribute to the cell protective function of the sHsps.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
Conclusions
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 12 18 12 18 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 26 34 26 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 148 154 144 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 243 247 239 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dnaK</italic>
###xml 546 552 538 544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
###xml 26 33 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Deletion of ibpAB renders E. coli more susceptible to growth impairment by production of alpha-glucosidase. Without recombinant protein production, ibpAB deletion affects growth only at extreme temperatures or in combination with mutations of dnaK. Thus, production of an aggregation prone protein constitutes a good model system to study the functions of IbpA/IbpB under non-lethal conditions. Two major functions were found for IbpA/IbpB: First, the sHsps protect cells during alpha-glucosidase production and disburden the DnaK system. In the ibpAB deletion strain, insufficient free DnaK was available to control ClpB accumulation, which was strongly intensified. Also enolase and PNP showed a similar pattern.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 455 461 445 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ibpAB </italic>
Second, IbpA/IbpB reduce degradation of alpha-glucosidase during disaggregation from inclusion bodies. The IbpA/IbpB function depends on elevated temperature. Improved reactivation and protection from degradation were found after production at higher temperature, in accordance with the known heat-activation of sHsps. Also during disaggregation, a higher temperature was beneficial, whereas no protection from degradation was found at 15degreesC. In the ibpAB deletion strain, however, removal of inclusion bodies was accelerated, possibly due to the higher levels of ClpB and DnaK that were found to be essential for reactivation and degradation of alpha-glucosidase. Thus, the sHsp level is in a delicate balance: IbpA/IbpB are required to protect the cell, but abundant IbpA/IbpB impede the necessary adaptation and retards removal of aggregates.
###end p 49

###begin title 53
Culture conditions

###begin p 54
###xml 123 125 122 124 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1</sup>

The cultures were incubated on Luria-Bertani (LB) medium, supplemented with appropriate antibiotics (ampicilline 100 mug mL-1, chloramphenicol 50 mug mL-1), on a rotary shaker at 37degreesC to an OD600 of 0.5, induced with 1 mM IPTG and transferred to different temperatures as indicated in the results section. ###begin title 55
Product analysis
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 106 109 106 109 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1 </sup>
###xml 391 393 391 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
For SDS-PAGE analysis, cell pellets resuspended in phosphate buffer pH 7 were incubated on ice with 33 mgL-1 lysozyme for 30 min and disrupted by sonication for 20 s. Soluble and insoluble fractions were separated by centrifugation for 20 min at 13,000 rpm. The insoluble fractions were washed with phosphate buffer twice. SDS-PAGE analysis was performed according to the method of Laemmli [53]. Levels of alpha-glucosidase were estimated by densitometry of Coomassie-stained gels.

###begin title 58
Two-dimensional gel electrophoresis
###end title 58
###begin p 59
###xml 42 46 42 46 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">600 </sub>
###xml 773 781 766 774 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 773 780 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Cell pellets from a culture volume of 8/OD600 mL were dissolved in 200 muL lysis buffer containing 8 M urea, 4% CHAPS, 60 mM DDT, 2% Pharmalyte 3-10 and 0.002% bromophenol blue and incubated for one hour at room temperature. The first dimension was carried out on an IPGphor using 24 cm pH gradient 3-10 strips (amersham pharmacia biotech), with the voltage profile recommended by the manufacturer. The second dimension was run in an Ettan Dalt six (amersham pharmacia biotech) on 12.5% polyacrylamide gels. Gels were stained with Coomassie brilliant blue for quantification or by silver staining for visualisation. Gels for cultures incubated at 42degreesC were performed in quadruplicate, for other temperatures once. Protein spots were identified by comparison with the E. coli 2D database . Spot identities were verified by N-terminal sequencing for ClpB (RLDRLTN) and enolase (SKIVKII). alpha-glucosidase was not dissolved from the inclusion bodies and is not visible in the gels.
