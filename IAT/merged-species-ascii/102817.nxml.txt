###begin article-title 0
The RNA-binding domain of ribosomal protein L11 recognizes an rRNA tertiary structure stabilized by both thiostrepton and magnesium ion
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1

###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 621 623 621 623 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 764 780 764 780 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 846 848 846 848 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 764 780 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Antibiotics that inhibit ribosomal function may do so by one of several mechanisms, including the induction of incorrect RNA folding or prevention of protein and/or RNA conformational transitions. Thiostrepton, which binds to the 'GTPase center' of the large subunit, has been postulated to prevent conformational changes in either the L11 protein or rRNA to which it binds. Scintillation proximity assays designed to look at the binding of the L11 C-terminal RNA-binding domain to a 23S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) fragment, as well as the ability of thiostrepton to induce that binding, were used to demonstrate the role of Mg2+, L11 and thiostrepton in the formation and maintenance of the rRNA fragment tertiary structure. Experiments using these assays with both an Escherichia coli rRNA fragment and a thermostable variant of that RNA show that Mg2+, L11 and thiostrepton all induce the RNA to fold to an essentially identical tertiary structure.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 116 117 116 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c1">1</xref>
###xml 703 704 703 704 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 714 715 714 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c2">2</xref>
###xml 716 717 716 717 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c5">5</xref>
Many antibiotics that interfere with ribosome function protect specific regions of rRNA from chemical modification (1) and probably derive much of their binding affinity from direct contacts with the rRNA. It is attractive to think that antibiotic-induced stabilization of rRNA structure could be a common mechanism of antibiotic action, for instance by folding the RNA into a non-functional structure or by preventing a conformation change that normally takes place during the ribosome cycle. One candidate for such a mechanism is thiostrepton, which binds to the 'GTPase center' of the large subunit, comprised of a highly conserved region of large subunit rRNA, ribosomal protein L11 and the L10(L12)4 complex (2-5), and inhibits ribosome function.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 520 521 520 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c6">6</xref>
###xml 580 596 580 596 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 640 641 640 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c7">7</xref>
###xml 796 797 796 797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c8">8</xref>
###xml 896 897 896 897 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c9">9</xref>
###xml 580 596 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Two mechanisms have been proposed for the inhibitory effect of thiostrepton. Both suppose that the GTPase center cycles between different conformations as elongation factors bind and are released, and that thiostrepton prevents a conformational transition within the center. In the 'RNA-centric' proposal, the critical conformational transitions take place in the RNA: L11 stimulates translation by promoting the correct transition(s), and thiostrepton inhibits translation by stabilizing one of the rRNA conformations (6). In support of this, binding studies have suggested that Escherichia coli L11 can recognize differently folded RNAs (7). A 'protein-centric' view is based on the recent observation that mutations conferring thiostrepton resistance are located in the L11 N-terminal domain (8). This view has also been proposed for the action of the closely related antibiotic, micrococcin (9). As antibiotic binds normally to these ribosomes but no longer affects translation, it was proposed that thiostrepton prevents a conformation transition in L11.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 150 151 150 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f1">1</xref>
The present work examines the interactions between thiostrepton, a C-terminal RNA binding domain of L11 (L11-C76) and a 103 nt fragment of rRNA (Fig. 1), with two purposes in mind. First, we ask whether thiostrepton and L11-C76 recognize very similar or very different RNA conformations. The answer to this question bears on whether the 'RNA-centric' model of thiostrepton action is feasible. Our second interest is in developing assays that can be used to screen libraries of small molecules for those with potential for antibiotic activity. Two classes of compounds that act at the level of the ribosome can be envisioned. A first class would disrupt a ribosomal protein-RNA interaction, affecting ribosome assembly or function. Such compounds can be detected simply by their ability to prevent protein association with an rRNA target. Alternatively, a compound could stabilize one conformation of an RNA or RNA-protein complex, as proposed in the case of thiostrepton. In the present work we show how a protein-RNA binding assay can be set up to detect compounds that stabilize RNA tertiary structure by their stimulation of protein binding.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 115 116 115 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f1">1</xref>
###xml 269 271 269 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c10">10</xref>
###xml 272 274 272 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c11">11</xref>
###xml 351 353 351 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c12">12</xref>
###xml 567 569 567 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c13">13</xref>
###xml 570 572 570 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c14">14</xref>
###xml 654 656 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c15">15</xref>
###xml 657 659 653 655 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c16">16</xref>
###xml 753 754 749 750 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f1">1</xref>
###xml 756 758 752 754 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c17">17</xref>
###xml 836 838 832 834 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c18">18</xref>
###xml 920 921 916 917 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c6">6</xref>
###xml 922 924 918 920 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c19">19</xref>
###xml 925 927 921 923 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c20">20</xref>
###xml 1007 1009 1003 1005 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c19">19</xref>
###xml 1405 1407 1401 1403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c12">12</xref>
###xml 1408 1410 1404 1406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c18">18</xref>
###xml 1411 1413 1407 1409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c19">19</xref>
The separate interactions of thiostrepton, L11 or Mg ions with the tertiary structure of a 23S rRNA fragment (Fig. 1) have been characterized. Nucleotides 1051-1108 form a tightly folded tertiary structure whose stability depends critically on the presence of Mg ions (10,11). The binding of L11 to this region stabilizes the rRNA tertiary structure (12); recent crystal structures of the protein-RNA complex show the C-terminal domain of L11 directly contacting a key tertiary interaction taking place in the minor groove of the helix on either side of U1060-U1078 (13,14). Thiostrepton binds the same rRNA fragments with microM dissociation constants (15,16), probably in a pocket of the rRNA structure formed by the two hairpin loops shown in Figure 1 (17), and also stabilizes tertiary structure within the 1051-1108 rRNA fragment (18). Binding of thiostrepton is enhanced 100-1000-fold when L11 protein is present (6,19,20), but this stimulation depends on the presence of an N-terminal domain in L11 (19). In the present studies we have used only L11-C76, the C-terminal domain of L11 lacking any intrinsic interactions with thiostrepton, in order to focus on the effects of L11 and thiostrepton on the rRNA tertiary structure. Although thiostrepton and L11-C76 both stabilize tertiary structure within the rRNA domain, it is not known whether the stabilized conformation is the same in both cases (12,18,19).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 313 315 313 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c21">21</xref>
###xml 316 318 316 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c22">22</xref>
###xml 518 520 518 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c23">23</xref>
###xml 521 523 521 523 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c24">24</xref>
A nitrocellulose filter binding assay has been used in previous studies of L11 and thiostrepton interactions. As L11 and thiostrepton both retain rRNA on filters, the assay cannot be used to measure binding of one ligand in the presence of the other. Thus we have turned to scintillation proximity assays (SPAs) (21,22), which have several advantages over filter binding assays for making equilibrium measurements and have been shown to give reliable and reproducible results with well-characterized interactions (see 23,24 as just two such examples). Here we use SPAs to examine the way rRNA tertiary folding is linked to binding of Mg ions, L11-C76 and thiostrepton, and show that all three ligands stabilize essentially the same rRNA tertiary structure.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 9
###begin title 10
###xml 3 4 3 4 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-</bold>
L11-C76 protein
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 58 85 58 85 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bacillus stearothermophilus</italic>
###xml 155 157 155 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c19">19</xref>
###xml 58 85 <span type="species:ncbi:1422">Bacillus stearothermophilus</span>
L11-C76, which contains the C-terminal 75 residues of the Bacillus stearothermophilus L11 protein, was prepared from an overproducing strain as described (19). L11-C76 (2-5 mg) was first concentrated in a Centricon-3 (Amicon) for 3 h at 7000 r.p.m. and resuspended in 500 microl of storage buffer [20 mM phosphate (pH 7.5), 100 mM KCl]. The protein was then biotinylated using the EZ-Link Sulfo-NHS-LC-Biotinylation Kit (Pierce). A 5-fold molar excess of Sulfo-NHS-LC-biotin was added and incubated on ice for 2 h. Biotinylated protein was purified away from free biotin using a desalting column (Pierce) equilibrated in storage buffer. Protein concentration was determined by BCA assay (Pierce). Percent biotin incorporation was determined using the HABA [2-(4'-hydroxyazobenzene) benzoic acid] assay (Pierce). Incorporation of biotin in the L11-C76 protein was typically 1-2 mol biotin/mol L11-C76. Higher levels of biotinylation were generally inhibitory to binding activity (data not shown). The biotinylated protein concentration was adjusted to 1.5 mg/ml (175 microM) with storage buffer and stored at -80degreesC.

###begin title 20
Experimental rationale
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 36 38 36 38 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 146 147 146 147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f2">2</xref>
###xml 234 235 234 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 235 236 235 236 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">I</sub>
###xml 328 330 328 330 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 358 360 358 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c20">20</xref>
###xml 361 363 361 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c28">28</xref>
###xml 921 923 921 923 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1517 1519 1517 1519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c29">29</xref>
We begin with the hypothesis that Mg2+, thiostrepton and L11 all individually promote folding of the same, unique rRNA tertiary structure. Figure 2A shows the binding scheme that is expected if this assertion is true. The first step, KI, is folding of a specific tertiary structure, which is known to be strongly dependent on Mg2+ concentration in this RNA (20,28). In the next two steps, this folded structure may bind L11 (via its C-terminal domain) and thiostrepton, in either order. Finally, interaction of the L11 N-terminal domain further stabilizes the complex. Complex 4 would not normally be observable, as the strong interaction of the L11 N-terminus with thiostrepton would heavily favor formation of complex 5. However, a complex of L11-C76, thiostrepton and RNA, which lacks the L11-thiostrepton interaction, would be equivalent to complex 4. This scheme makes two important predictions: (i) if sufficient Mg2+ is added to drive the RNA entirely into the tertiary structure 2, then L11-C76 should have the same apparent affinity whether or not thiostrepton is present (conversely, binding of thiostrepton should be independent of L11-C76); (ii) if the RNA is largely in the unfolded state 1, then addition of thiostrepton should promote folding of the tertiary structure and enhance binding of L11-C76, and vice versa. This is the simplest possible form of allostery, in which a ligand drives a macromolecular conformational transition and thereby affects the binding of another ligand at a second site (29).
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 245 247 245 247 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 254 255 254 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f2">2</xref>
###xml 549 550 549 550 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f2">2</xref>
###xml 1044 1045 1044 1045 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f2">2</xref>
###xml 1155 1156 1155 1156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f2">2</xref>
###xml 1207 1209 1207 1209 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
There are several more complicated binding schemes for which the above two predictions would not hold. Two are important to consider here. (i) Suppose that thiostrepton and L11 bind different RNA conformations, both of which are stabilized by Mg2+ (Fig. 2B). In that case, thiostrepton and L11 should compete for binding and complex 4 would be less stable than the individual complexes. However, the ternary L11-thiostrepton-RNA complex 5 could still form because of the additional interaction contributed by the N-terminus. (For simplicity, Figure 2B shows complex 5 in the thiostrepton-bound conformation, since the main interaction with L11 is via its N-terminus and not the C-terminal RNA binding domain. Other conformations could be imagined.) This model predicts that L11-C76 and thiostrepton should show competition (or anticooperativity) in RNA binding (complex 4). (ii) Instead of promoting formation of a tertiary structure encompassing the entire rRNA domain, thiostrepton and L11-C76 could induce local conformational changes (Fig. 2C). Binding of one ligand would then be independent of the other, but, in contrast to the situation in Figure 2A, independent binding would persist even at low Mg2+ concentrations that destabilize tertiary structure.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 120 121 120 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f2">2</xref>

###xml 326 328 326 328 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>

###xml 481 482 481 482 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T</sub>

###begin title 35
L11-C76 binding to RNA with stable tertiary structure
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 103 105 103 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c26">26</xref>
###xml 134 135 134 135 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 138 141 138 141 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">125</sup>
###xml 146 148 146 148 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">33</sup>
###xml 571 573 571 573 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">33</sup>
SPAs involve the use of a scintillant-filled bead coated with a capture molecule such as streptavidin (26). When a probe labeled with 3H, 125I or 33P is captured by the bead it brings the isotope close to the scintillant resulting in light emission. Free probe does not excite the scintillant and does not contribute to the signal. This obviates the need for separation of captured and free probe from a reaction mixture. For the work reported here, L11-C76 is biotinylated to interact with a streptavidin coated SPA bead, and the RNA used is homogeneously labeled with [33P]UTP. The concentration of labeled RNA is insignificant in comparison to that of the added protein, so that the concentration of free protein is approximately equal to the total protein concentration.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 75 81 75 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 214 220 212 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 236 237 234 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f1">1</xref>
###xml 240 242 238 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c27">27</xref>
###xml 564 565 562 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f3">3</xref>
###xml 823 825 821 823 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 925 927 919 921 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 956 958 948 950 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1115 1116 1105 1106 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 1131 1133 1121 1123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c28">28</xref>
###xml 1372 1374 1362 1364 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1547 1549 1537 1539 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1638 1640 1628 1630 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 75 81 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 214 220 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
To test the reliability of the SPA, we first used a 103 nt fragment of the E.coli rRNA sequence, U1061A, in which a single U-->A mutation has greatly stabilized the RNA tertiary structure relative to the wild-type E.coli sequence (Fig. 1) (27). The SPA assay measures the ability of the biotinylated L11-C76 protein to bind, or capture, the labeled RNA fragment. Recognition and binding of the RNA by the protein requires that the RNA be folded in a favorable conformation. Titration of the RNA with biotinylated L11-C76 gave a strong signal over background (Fig. 3A). Inclusion of tRNA in the reaction buffer did not alter the signal, indicating the binding reaction was specific (data not shown). Based on the recognition of the RNA fragment by the L11-C76 protein, the tertiary structure appears to be fully folded at Mg2+ concentrations >/=2 mM. Dissociation constants calculated from these data, 28 +/- 3.9 nM at 5 mM Mg2+ and 44 +/- 3.4 nM at 2 mM Mg2+, are quite comparable to the 36 +/- 5 nM determined by filter binding assay for the same RNA under nearly identical conditions (10 mM MOPS pH 7.0, 3 mM MgCl2, 175 mM KCl) (28), but using unmodified L11-C76. Biotinylation of the L11-C76 protein (at 1-1.5 biotins per molecule) does not significantly affect the dissociation constant as determined by filter assay (data not shown). A SPA titration done at 0.5 mM Mg2+ does not as cleanly fit a binding isotherm, but clearly indicates that binding is taking place in approximately the same concentration range as the titrations at higher [Mg2+]. (A fraction of misfolded RNAs may account for the lower extent of binding at 0.5 mM Mg2+, as the RNA renatures to form the tertiary structure very slowly under these conditions.) We conclude from these assays that SPA using biotinylated L11-C76 gives reliable binding constants, and that protein binding to U1061A RNA is not strongly dependent on magnesium ion concentration over the range 0.5-5 mM.

###begin p 39
###xml 4 10 4 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 186 188 186 188 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 203 205 203 205 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 281 283 281 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c10">10</xref>
###xml 370 372 370 372 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 388 390 388 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c12">12</xref>
###xml 458 460 458 460 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 4 10 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The E.coli rRNA fragment binding L11 has a marginally stable tertiary structure: in melting studies, a distinct tertiary unfolding transition is only observed in the presence of >2 mM Mg2+, and >10 mM Mg2+ is required before the structure is completely formed at room temperature (10). Binding of full-length L11 to this RNA was previously shown to depend strongly on Mg2+ concentration (12). Half-maximal stimulation of protein binding occurred at 0.6 mM Mg2+ when assayed at 0degreesC; this concentration is lower than that required to observe the presence of tertiary structure in melting experiments presumably because the protein helps drive the RNA into a folded state.

###xml 544 546 542 544 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 49 55 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
SPA titrations with biotinylated L11-C76 and the E.coli rRNA fragment also show a large effect of Mg2+; binding is nearly undetectable at 0.5 mM Mg2+, but is detectable at 2 mM Mg2+ with a dissociation constant of 140 +/- 23 nM (Fig. 3B). The titration could not be carried out to high enough protein concentrations to reliably measure a Kd in 1 mM Mg2+. The Kd at 2 mM Mg2+ is only 3.2-fold weaker than seen with U1061A RNA, suggesting that the two RNAs are not intrinsically different in their ability to bind L11-C76 as long as sufficient Mg2+ is present to promote folding of the required tertiary structure.

###begin title 41
TS stimulates L11-C76 binding to RNA with unstable tertiary structure
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 174 176 174 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c16">16</xref>
###xml 556 557 548 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f4">4</xref>
###xml 715 716 707 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f3">3</xref>
###xml 987 988 975 976 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 991 993 979 981 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c16">16</xref>
It has not been possible to quantitate L11 binding in the presence of thiostrepton by a filter binding assay since thiostrepton and L11 both retain RNA fragments on filters (16). Here we have circumvented this problem by developing a more versatile protocol based on SPA technology, and use this assay to ask whether thiostrepton affects L11-C76 binding to the two RNAs. With U1061A RNA, addition of 20 microM thiostrepton had only a marginal effect on the apparent protein affinity (16 +/- 1 nM without thiostrepton, 12.2 +/- 1 nM with thiostrepton; Fig. 4A). Ten percent DMSO, needed to solubilize thiostrepton, was included in both titrations; this accounts for the tighter affinity measured here than in Figure 3A. Though we do not have an independent measure of thiostrepton interaction with the RNA in the SPA, the antibiotic is known to bind U1061A RNA with an affinity of 1.1 microM under similar buffer conditions as used here (2% DMSO, 30 mM Tris pH 7.6, 175 mM KCl, 11 mM MgSO4) (16). The weak effect of thiostrepton on L11-C76 binding at this high concentration argues that binding of the two ligands is nearly independent.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 19 25 19 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 64 66 64 66 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>

###xml 19 25 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
When titrating the E.coli RNA fragment with L11-C76 at 0.5 mM Mg2+, 20 microM thiostrepton dramatically stimulates L11-C76 binding (Fig. 4B). In the presence of thiostrepton, the apparent Kd is 312 +/- 48 nM. The titration in the absence of thiostrepton does not reach a high enough level of RNA binding to fit a binding curve, so we cannot quantitate the degree of stimulation.
###end p 43

###xml 10 12 10 12 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 63 69 63 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 101 102 101 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f4">4</xref>

At 5 mM Mg2+, the effect of thiostrepton on L11-C76 binding to E.coli RNA is nearly eliminated (Fig. 4C), with 20 microM thiostrepton decreasing the Kd by a factor of only 1.2. This is the expected result if Mg2+ stabilizes a conformation recognized by both protein and antibiotic, and if there is no interaction between the two ligands.

An alternative way to examine the interplay between RNA folding, thiostrepton and L11-C76 is to titrate protein with thiostrepton in the presence of a constant amount of RNA. Under conditions such that the RNA is largely unfolded, the extent of protein-RNA complex formation is then a readout of thiostrepton interaction with RNA. Such a titration of E.coli RNA at low Mg2+ concentration (0.5 mM) is shown in Figure 5. As expected, the extent of RNA binding starts at a low value in the absence of thiostrepton and saturates at high antibiotic concentrations (>20 microM). The data have been fit to equation 2 using an estimate of KP as 24 nM, the affinity measured in 5 mM Mg2+ (Fig. 4C). The fitted value of KTS is then 1.21 +/- 0.48 microM, and KI is 60 +/- 25. KTS is comparable to values measured by filter binding assays for the same RNA under high Mg2+ conditions (16). There is no independent estimate of KI as the E.coli RNA tertiary structure is undetectable under these conditions (10).

###end title 47
###begin p 48

###xml 1052 1054 1028 1030 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1085 1087 1061 1063 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1103 1109 1079 1085 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 211 217 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 543 549 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 882 888 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 1021 1027 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 1103 1109 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
We have used a SPA assay to examine the ability of L11-C76 to recognize the tertiary structures of a fragment of the 23S rRNA formed in the presence of various amounts of Mg2+ and thiostrepton. When we used the E.coli rRNA variant U1061A in the assay, the binding of L11-C76 was virtually independent of Mg2+ concentration over a wide range (Fig. 3A). The Kd of this interaction (at 2 mM Mg2+) is approximately44 nM. U1061A has been shown previously to fold into a very stable tertiary structure recognized by L11-C76 (27). When the wild-type E.coli RNA fragment corresponding to U1061A is used, binding of L11-C76 is highly dependent on Mg2+ concentration (Fig. 3B). Under relatively high Mg concentrations (2-5 mM) the RNA assumes a conformation recognized by L11-C76, but under lower Mg2+ concentrations (<1 mM) binding is essentially eliminated. At 2 mM Mg2+ the Kd between the E.coli rRNA sequence and L11-C76 is approximately140 nM. The similar Kd values obtained with the intrinsically stable RNA fragment and the E.coli fragment under higher Mg2+ conditions demonstrate that Mg2+ can induce the E.coli RNA fragment to adopt a tertiary conformation that is recognized by L11-C76 and essentially the same as that of the U1061A variant.
###end p 48

###xml 75 76 75 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f4">4</xref>
###xml 375 376 375 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f2">2</xref>
###xml 476 478 476 478 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 545 551 545 551 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 626 628 626 628 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 646 647 646 647 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f4">4</xref>
###xml 669 670 669 670 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 670 671 670 671 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sub>
###xml 960 961 956 957 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f5">5</xref>
###xml 1020 1021 1016 1017 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f2">2</xref>

###xml 545 551 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The binding of L11-C76 to U1061A RNA is not affected by thiostrepton (Fig. 4A), although recent work in our laboratory has demonstrated that thiostrepton does interact directly with this RNA (S.Hofstadler, L.B.Blyn, K.Lowery and J.Drader, unpublished results). The lack of competition between L11-C76 and thiostrepton in binding U1061A RNA argues against the model in Figure 2B and in favor of the two ligands recognizing the same RNA tertiary structure. Thiostrepton, like Mg2+, does have a demonstrable effect on the binding of L11-C76 to the E.coli rRNA sequence. This strong stimulatory effect is demonstrated under low Mg2+ conditions (Fig. 4B), where the L11-C76 Kd is not quantifiable in the absence of thiostrepton but is only 2-fold weaker (312 nM) in the presence of 20 microM thiostrepton than at higher Mg ion concentration. The same stimulatory effect is observed when the L11-C76 concentration is held constant and thiostrepton is titrated (Fig. 5). This strong cooperativity rules out the model in Figure 2C that assumes independent binding of L11 and thiostrepton to local, Mg2+-stabilized RNA structures. Instead, thiostrepton induces folding of the RNA into a conformation recognized by L11-C76 as well as the RNA conformation induced by Mg2+.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 105 107 105 107 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 191 192 191 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f2">2</xref>
###xml 208 210 208 210 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 424 426 424 426 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 599 601 599 601 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 762 764 762 764 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 793 799 793 799 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 809 810 809 810 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 810 811 810 811 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sub>
###xml 890 892 890 892 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 904 910 904 910 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 1166 1168 1166 1168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c29">29</xref>
###xml 793 799 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 904 910 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Our conclusion from these results is that thiostrepton and L11-C76 both recognize essentially the same Mg2+-stabilized RNA tertiary structure, in agreement with the model presented in Figure 2A. Under high Mg2+ conditions, the two ligands bind nearly independently to the RNA, demonstrating that there is neither an intrinsic cooperative interaction between them nor competition for different RNA conformations. Under low Mg2+ conditions, binding of the two ligands is mutually stimulatory because of their binding to the same RNA structure. The RNA structures stabilized by L11, thiostrepton and Mg2+ can differ only in very minor ways: the apparent cooperativity factors between L11-C76 and thiostrepton in binding U1061A RNA is only 1.3-fold; L11-C76 binds Mg2+ and thiostrepton-stabilized E.coli RNA with Kds differing by only 2-fold. (The 3.2-fold difference between L11-C76 binding Mg2+-stabilized E.coli RNA and U1061A RNA, though still very small, could be due to subtle conformational differences between the two RNAs.) A difference of a single stacked or unstacked base in the recognized RNA structures could have caused these ratios to be as large as 10 (29).
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 51 52 51 52 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd289f2">2</xref>
We note that our conclusion in favor of the Figure 2A model for thiostrepton and L11-C76 binding argues against the simplest formulations of an 'RNA-centric' model of L11 and thiostrepton activity, i.e. there is no evidence that the RNA tertiary structure recognized by L11 switches between alternative conformations. Schemes in which the RNA binding domain of L11 transiently dissociates from the RNA and allows unfolding of the tertiary structure, or in which the N-terminal domain of L11 affects rRNA conformation elsewhere in the ribosome, have not been ruled out.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 701 703 701 703 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c13">13</xref>
###xml 704 706 704 706 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd289c14">14</xref>
The information and assays described in this work will aid in the discovery of new types of antibiotics that function at the same site of action as thiostrepton. Solubility and other issues make thiostrepton a relatively poor commercial antibiotic that is mainly used in topical applications. The discovery of new antibiotics that function at the same site as thiostrepton, but are substantially different in nature would be highly valuable. The results described here demonstrate that the RNA structure recognized by thiostrepton and by L11 are fundamentally the same. The recent reports of the L11/23S rRNA fragment crystal structure provide an opportunity to model thiostrepton into the structure (13,14). This will give valuable information towards the effort to design and discover new drugs that bind and function in the same way. The assays described here also provide a mechanism for the high throughput screening of drug candidates for the ability to affect L11/RNA binding and for their ability to stabilize RNA structure as in the case of thiostrepton.
###end p 52
