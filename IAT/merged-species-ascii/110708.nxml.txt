Functional significance of conserved residues in the phosphohydrolase module of Escherichia coli MutT protein

###begin p 2
###xml 0 16 0 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 521 525 517 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 525 526 521 522 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;</sup>
###xml 0 16 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 1122 1127 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Escherichia coli MutT protein hydrolyzes 8-oxo-7,8-dihydro-2'-dGTP (8-oxo-dGTP) to the monophosphate, thus avoiding the incorporation of 8-oxo-7,8-dihydroguanine (8-oxo-G) into nascent DNA. Bacterial and mammalian homologs of MutT protein share the phosphohydrolase module (MutT: Gly37-->Gly59). By saturation mutagenesis of conserved residues in the MutT module, four of the 10 conserved residues (Gly37, Gly38, Glu53 and Glu57) were revealed to be essential to suppress spontaneous A:T-->C:G transversion mutation in a mutT- mutator strain. For the other six residues (Lys39, Glu44, Thr45, Arg52, Glu56 and Gly59), many positive mutants which can suppress the spontaneous mutation were obtained; however, all of the positive mutants for Glu44 and Arg52 either partially or inefficiently suppressed the mutation, indicating that these two residues are also important for MutT function. Several positive mutants for Lys39, Thr45, Glu56 and Gly59 efficiently decreased the elevated spontaneous mutation rate, as seen with the wild-type, hence, these four residues are non-essential for MutT function. As Lys38 and Glu55 in human MTH1, corresponding to the non-essential residues Lys39 and Glu56 in MutT, could not be replaced by any other residue without loss of function, different structural features between the two modules of MTH1 and MutT proteins are evident.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 4 8 4 8 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 19 35 19 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 167 168 165 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c1">1</xref>
###xml 169 170 167 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c4">4</xref>
###xml 302 303 300 301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c5">5</xref>
###xml 327 337 321 331 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli DNA</italic>
###xml 704 705 696 697 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c5">5</xref>
###xml 706 707 698 699 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c7">7</xref>
###xml 19 35 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 327 333 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The mutT mutant of Escherichia coli is a well characterized mutator mutant with an increased spontaneous occurrence of unidirectional A:T-->C:G transversion mutation (1-4). MutT protein catalyzes hydrolysis of an oxidized form of dGTP, 8-oxo-7,8-dihydro-2'-dGTP (8-oxo-dGTP) to the monophosphate form (5). The alpha subunit of E.coli DNA polymerase III inserts 8-oxo-dGTP into the nascent strand opposite adenine in the template, the efficiency being almost equal that of cytosine. During the next round of replication the polymerase may insert dCTP opposite previously incorporated 8-oxo-7,8-dihydroguanine (8-oxo-G), the result being A:T-->C:G transversion mutation after another round of replication (5-7).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 12 16 12 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 97 103 97 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 147 148 147 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c1">1</xref>
###xml 149 150 149 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c4">4</xref>
###xml 232 233 232 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c8">8</xref>
###xml 370 371 370 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c8">8</xref>
###xml 458 464 458 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 97 103 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 458 464 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Lack of the mutT gene increases occurrence not only of this type of transversion mutation in the E.coli genome 1000-fold over the wild-type level (1-4), it also causes transcriptional errors, an event known as non-genomic mutation (8). MutT protein hydrolyzes 8-oxo-GTP, an oxidized form of a ribonucleotide GTP which can be inserted into transcripts by RNA polymerase (8). These findings indicate that a significant level of oxidation of dGTP/GTP occurs in E.coli cells and that MutT eliminates mutagenic nucleotides from nucleotide pools. Thus, MutT protein functions as a guardian for both DNA and RNA synthesis.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 90 106 90 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Proteus vulgaris</italic>
###xml 111 136 111 136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Streptococcus pneumoniae,</italic>
###xml 167 173 167 173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 175 176 175 176 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c9">9</xref>
###xml 177 179 177 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c10">10</xref>
###xml 424 430 424 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 437 439 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c11">11</xref>
###xml 469 475 469 475 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 634 636 630 632 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c12">12</xref>
###xml 637 639 633 635 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c13">13</xref>
###xml 90 106 <span type="species:ncbi:585">Proteus vulgaris</span>
###xml 111 135 <span type="species:ncbi:1313">Streptococcus pneumoniae</span>
###xml 167 173 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 424 430 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 469 475 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Genes for MutT homolog proteins with dGTPase or 8-oxo-dGTPase activity were identified in Proteus vulgaris and Streptococcus pneumoniae, bacteria distantly related to E.coli (9,10). All three MutT homolog proteins have a molecular mass ranging from 13 to 18 kDa, and the degree of identity ranges from 19 to 40%. Most of the identical residues are in a region corresponding to the 23-residue sequence from Gly37 to Gly59 of E.coli MutT (11). Structural analyses of the E.coli MutT protein revealed that the region containing this 23-residue sequence constitutes the active center of dGTPase, with a loop and an alpha-helix structure (12,13).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 58 62 58 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 77 81 77 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MTH1</italic>
###xml 117 121 117 121 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 121 122 121 122 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;</sup>
###xml 201 203 199 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c14">14</xref>
###xml 204 206 202 204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c17">17</xref>
###xml 479 485 477 483 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 748 750 746 748 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c18">18</xref>
###xml 935 937 933 935 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c18">18</xref>
###xml 938 940 936 938 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c19">19</xref>
###xml 464 469 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 479 485 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Mammalian cells also possess 8-oxo-dGTPase encoded by the mutT homolog gene, MTH1, and expression of MTH1 protein in mutT- cells suppresses the increased occurrence of A:T-->C:G transversion mutation (14-17). Despite functional identity, the MTH1 and MutT proteins share only 30 residues (23%), 14 of which constitute the conserved 23-residue module, while the other 16 residues are scattered throughout the molecules. We reported that the 23-residue sequences of human MTH1 and E.coli MutT proteins are exchangeable so that they constitute functionally equivalent modules for 8-oxo-dGTPase, designated as the phosphohydrolase module; however, the chimeric MTH1 protein with the 23-residue module of MutT is thermolabile in structure and function (18). Saturation mutagenesis of the module in MTH1 revealed that six non-conserved residues among MutT homologs are essential for MTH1 function, as are eight of the 10 identical residues (18,19). These results indicate that the phosphohydrolase module of the MutT and MTH1 proteins is functionally equivalent, yet each possesses unique features of function or structure.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
To better define the unique features of the phosphohydrolase module of MutT, we carried out saturation mutagenesis of the 10 conserved residues in the module. We now provide the first evidence that six conserved residues are essential for MutT function while the other four can be replaced by other residues without loss of function. Among the four non-essential residues, Lys39 and Glu56 correspond to the essential residues Lys38 and Glu55 in MTH1. Different structural features between the two modules of MTH1 and MutT proteins are evident.
###end p 8

###xml 498 504 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Saturation mutagenesis was done for the plasmid pALTER:MutT-EM, using the mutagenesis primers listed in Table 1, and according to the Promega technical manual for altered sites II in vitro mutagenesis system, but with the modifications described in Figure 1 (18,19). Mutagenized DNA was digested with a set of restriction enzymes, EcoRI and MluI, and subcloned into pHS:MutT. A mixture of plasmids pHS:MutT(G37X), for example, carrying mutations at the codon for Gly37 thus obtained was applied to E.coli CC101T and cultured on agar medium containing minimal A salts, 0.2% glucose, 0.05% phenyl-beta-d-galactopyranoside and 40 microg/ml of 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl-beta-d-galactopyranoside. After incubation for 5 days at 37degreesC, papillae formation was evident, and appropriate samples were used for further analyses. An appropriate negative mutant for each residue was used as a template for the second cycle of saturation mutagenesis, the objective being to obtain positive mutants. Nucleotide sequence of each mutant was determined using Dye terminator Cycle Sequencing FS Ready Kits and the model ABI373A automated DNA sequencer (PE Applied Biosystems).

Fluctuation test

###begin title 24
Preparation of anti-MutT antibody
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 53 59 53 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 149 151 149 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c26">26</xref>
###xml 507 509 491 493 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c21">21</xref>
###xml 510 512 494 496 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c25">25</xref>
###xml 53 64 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">E.coli BL21</span>
###xml 336 357 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">Japanese white rabbit</span>
Insoluble TrpE-MutT fusion protein was prepared from E.coli BL21 (DE3) cells carrying pET3d:TrpE-MutT and subjected to SDS-12.5% PAGE, as described (26). A region of the gel containing the fusion protein was excised (approximately100 microg/injection), emulsified with adjuvant (Titer Max, Vaxel Inc., Norcross, GA) and injected into a Japanese white rabbit. Sera were obtained 2 weeks after the eighth injection of booster and antibodies were purified using TrpE-MutT-Sepharose and TrpE-Sepharose columns (21,25). The TrpE-MutT-Sepharose column, to which the anti-serum against TrpE-MutT was loaded, was washed using 10 mM sodium phosphate buffer (pH 7.6). The bound antibodies were then eluted, using buffer E (0.2 M glycine-HCl pH 2.5, 0.1 M NaCl, 0.1% Triton-X100), and the eluted fraction was dialyzed against Tris-buffered saline (10 mM Tris-HCl pH 7.6, 150 mM NaCl) and applied onto the TrpE-Sepharose column. The flow-through fractions were collected and designated as anti-MutT.

###begin title 28
RESULTS
###end title 28
###begin title 29
###xml 90 96 90 96 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 90 96 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Saturation mutagenesis of the 10 conserved residues in the phosphohydrolase module of the E.coli MutT protein
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 184 185 184 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd499f3">3</xref>
###xml 279 285 279 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 507 508 507 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd499f1">1</xref>
###xml 516 517 516 517 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd499tb1">1</xref>
###xml 279 285 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Residues in the 23-residue module of MutT were classified into three groups; conserved, semi-conserved and non-conserved, as based on conservation among six MutT family proteins (Fig. 3). To better understand the roles of the conserved residues in the phosphohydrolase module of E.coli MutT, saturation mutagenesis of 10 residues in the module was done, in combination with negative and positive mutant screening, using mutagenesis primers that contain a completely degenerated codon for each residue (Fig. 1; Table 1).
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 99 105 99 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 174 175 174 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd499tb2">2</xref>
###xml 419 420 419 420 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 466 467 466 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd499tb3">3</xref>
###xml 1079 1080 1079 1080 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd499f2">2</xref>
###xml 1088 1089 1088 1089 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd499tb3">3</xref>
###xml 99 105 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
First, we isolated mutants of pHS:MutT that lost the capacity to suppress the mutator phenotype of E.coli strain CC101T, and which were designated as negative mutants (Table 2). Next, we used as a template one of the negative mutants for each residue in the second round of saturation mutagenesis, the objective being to isolate positive mutants that acquired the capability to suppress the increased occurrence of LacZ+ reversion in CC101T cells. As shown in Table 3, mutation rates of CC101T cells harboring negative mutants, used as templates for the second mutagenesis, were as high as that of the cells with vector pBR322. We used nonsense mutants as templates for the second mutaganesis in case of negative mutants of six residues (Lys39, Glu44, Thr45, Glu53, Glu56 and Gly59), because neither MutT protein nor 8-oxo-dGTPase activity was detected in CC101T cells harboring these mutants. Expression levels of the negative mutant proteins with missense mutation were lower than that of wild-type MTH1, and low 8-oxo-dGTPase activity was detectable in only a few cases (Fig. 2; Table 3).
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 91 92 91 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd499tb2">2</xref>
Negative and positive mutants isolated for each amino acid residue are summarized in Table 2. Five or more different negative mutants for residues Gly37, Gly38, Glu53 and Glu57 were isolated, but fewer negative mutants, including nonsense ones were isolated for residues Lys39, Glu44, Thr45, Arg52, Glu56 and Gly59. No positive mutant, except for each true revertant, was isolated for the former residues, indicating that residues Gly37, Gly38, Glu53 and Glu57 cannot be replaced with any other residue without loss of function. On the other hand many different positive mutants were obtained for the latter, suggesting that residues Lys39, Glu44, Thr45, Arg52, Glu56 and Gly59 can be replaced without losing function.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
Our data clearly show that negative and positive mutant screening is a self-complementary approach, because more negative mutants were obtained for all essential residues and for which only a true revertant was obtained, as a positive mutant.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 83 89 83 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 184 188 182 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 188 189 186 187 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;</sup>
###xml 83 89 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
In conclusion, four of the 10 conserved residues of the phosphohydrolase module of E.coli MutT are essential for function capable of suppressing the elevated A:T-->C:G transversion in mutT- cells.
###end p 34

###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 151 152 151 152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gkd499tb2">2</xref>

###xml 457 459 457 459 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;9</sup>
Seven different positive mutants were obtained from the negative mutant K39ochre for the conserved Lys39 residue as well as the true revertant (Tables 2 and 4). Among the positive mutants, the lowest mutation rate of LacZ+ reversion in CC101T was attained by expression of a positive mutant K39Q, the mutation rate of 0.77 x 10-9 being slightly lower than that attained with wild-type MutT. ###begin title 55
DISCUSSION
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 214 215 214 215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd499f3">3</xref>
###xml 378 380 378 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c18">18</xref>
###xml 293 298 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Our main conclusion based on findings in the present study is that in the phosphohydrolase module of MutT, four of the 10 conserved residues, Lys39, Thr45, Glu56 and Gly59 are non-essential for MutT function (Fig. 3). The two residues, Lys39 and Glu56 in MutT correspond to Lys38 and Glu55 in human MTH1 that could not be replaced by any other residue without loss of function (18); thus, different structural features between the two modules of MTH1 and MutT proteins are evident.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 27 33 27 33 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 91 93 91 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c12">12</xref>
###xml 94 96 94 96 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c28">28</xref>
###xml 225 227 217 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c29">29</xref>
###xml 469 471 454 456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c29">29</xref>
###xml 778 780 763 765 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 834 835 819 820 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 991 994 976 979 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4.7</sup>
###xml 998 1001 983 986 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3.0</sup>
###xml 1008 1011 993 996 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0.9</sup>
###xml 1047 1049 1032 1034 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c12">12</xref>
###xml 1050 1052 1035 1037 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c13">13</xref>
###xml 1053 1055 1038 1040 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c30">30</xref>
###xml 27 33 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
The 23-residue sequence in E.coli MutT constitutes the active center for dGTPase activity (12,28), and perhaps also for 8-oxo-dGTPase activity. The module consists of loop I (Gly37-->Thr45) and alpha-helix I (Pro46-->Gly59) (29). The alpha-helix I in MutT is apparently amphipathic and MutT has two hydrophobic cores, one consisting of Val50, Val51, Leu54 from the helix I clustering with Phe65 and Leu67 from loop II, Val87 from beta-strand D and Trp95 from loop III (29). Interactions between the phosphohydrolase module and other secondary structures may be important to support stability or to provide functional moieties for the active center. It was proposed that residues Gly38, Glu56 and Glu57 in the module of MutT are involved in coordination of enzyme-bound metal (Mg2+), Glu57 provides the largest contribution (factor >105) to the catalytic power of MutT enzyme and residues Glu53, Arg52 and Lys39 are involved in the catalytic reaction, each residue contributing a factor of 104.7, 103.0 and 100.9, respectively, to catalytic power (12,13,30).
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 129 134 129 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 137 139 137 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c30">30</xref>
###xml 382 383 382 383 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sup>
###xml 502 505 502 505 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">0.9</sup>
###xml 512 515 512 515 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1.4</sup>
The data presented herein are largely consistent with the proposed mechanism of the MutT pyrophosphohydrolase reaction by Harris et al. (30), because residues Gly37, Gly38, Glu44, Arg52, Glu53 and Glu57 cannot be replaced by any other residue without loss of function, and are therefore essential for MutT function. The essential residues are likely to contribute to a factor of >103 to the catalytic power of MutT. However, Lys39 and Glu56, whose contribution to MutT catalytic power is a factor of 100.9 and 101.4, respectively, can be replaced by other residues without loss of function, thus indicating that the MutT reaction may differ somewhat from the proposed mechanism, as discussed below.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 4 9 4 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 12 14 12 14 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c13">13</xref>
###xml 42 43 38 39 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 348 349 331 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">k</italic>
###xml 349 352 332 335 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cat</sub>
###xml 380 381 363 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 381 382 364 365 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M</sub>
###xml 398 400 381 383 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 429 431 412 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c28">28</xref>
###xml 432 434 415 417 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c30">30</xref>
Lin et al. (13) reported that the delta-CH2 of Lys39 on loop I interacts with the hydrophobic face of the ribose ring of alpha,beta-methyleneadenosine 5'-triphosphate (AMPCPP), and its side chain ammonium group approaches an oxygen of alpha-phosphoryl group at a distance of 4.7 +/- 1.1 A. Mutation of Lys39 to Gln results in an 8-fold decrease in kcat and a 5.3-fold increase in KM(dGTP) in the Mg2+-activated dGTPase reaction (28,30). Based on these data, it was suggested that Lys39 is positioned in the MutT reaction to interact electrostatically with the alpha-phosphoryl group and thereby facilitate departure of the nucleoside monophosphate (NMP) group.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 33 35 33 35 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c28">28</xref>
###xml 36 38 36 38 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c30">30</xref>
###xml 145 149 145 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 149 150 149 150 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;</sup>
###xml 819 823 819 823 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 823 824 823 824 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;</sup>
In contrast to previous reports (28,30), we isolated the K39Q mutant as one of the positive mutants for K39ochre which completely suppresses the mutT- mutator phenotype, and retains 58% of the level of specific activity for 8-oxo-dGTPase of wild-type MutT. Using saturation mutagenesis combined with negative and positive mutant screening, we isolated seven different positive mutants for Lys39, all of which are stable and retain >50% of the specific activity of wild-type MutT. We obtained only two negative mutants, K39ochre (eight clones) and K39P (one clone) from 152 clones examined, and the latter missense mutant, while stable, possesses little 8-oxo-dGTPase activity. Thus, it is likely that most amino acid residues, but not proline, may be able to replace Lys39 without loss of the potential to suppress the mutT- mutator phenotype. Thus, the Lys39 residue is apparently not essential for MutT function. We wish to emphasize that this conclusion can be reached only by using saturation mutagenesis in combination with negative and positive mutant screening.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 95 97 95 97 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c28">28</xref>
###xml 98 100 98 100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c30">30</xref>
###xml 141 142 141 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 142 143 142 143 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M</sub>
###xml 319 320 303 304 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">k</italic>
###xml 320 323 304 307 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cat</sub>
###xml 374 375 358 359 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 375 376 359 360 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M</sub>
For catalytic analysis of MutT, we used 8-oxo-dGTP while other workers used dGTP as substrate (28,30), to which MutT protein exhibits a high KM value (1100 microM), 2200-fold higher than that for 8-oxo-dGTP. With 8-oxo-dGTP, K39Q exhibits approximately58% of the specific activity for wild-type; however, with dGTP its kcat value was decreased 8-fold with a 5.3-fold higher KM value, which means that the specific activity may be <5% of the wild-type. We suggest that the MutT protein interacts with dGTP and 8-oxo-dGTP in a different manner, and that Lys39 has a more crucial role for dGTPase than for 8-oxo-dGTPase of MutT.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 43 46 43 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syn</italic>
###xml 84 86 84 86 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c31">31</xref>
###xml 148 150 148 150 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 160 162 160 162 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 184 188 184 188 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">anti</italic>
###xml 252 255 252 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syn</italic>
###xml 278 286 278 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">syn&#8211;anti</italic>
###xml 344 346 344 346 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c13">13</xref>
8-Oxo-dGTP but not dGTP appears to favor a syn conformation at the glycosidic bond (31), and it has been shown that AMPCPP in the quaternary MutT-Mg2+-AMPCPP-Mg2+ complex takes a high anti torsional angle at the glycosylic bond, which is part of the a syn conformation near the syn-anti boundary, with interaction between Lys39 and the ribose (13). Lys39 may be involved in binding to dGTP or AMPCPP but not to 8-oxo-dGTP, thus contributing to dGTP hydrolysis. It is essential to analyze interactions between MutT and 8-oxo-dGTP or its non-hydrolyzable derivatives in order to delineate substrate recognition by MutT. Identification of a negative mutant, K39P MutT, suggests that substitution of Lys39 for proline altered its secondary structure because the proline residue assumes only two fixed angles in the polypeptide. While loop I seems flexible in structure with most amino acid residues, proline may destroy the flexibility, leading to loss of catalytic potential.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 187 190 187 190 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1.4</sup>
###xml 240 242 240 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c30">30</xref>
###xml 400 404 400 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mutT</italic>
###xml 404 405 404 405 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;</sup>
###xml 561 564 561 564 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5.0</sup>
###xml 633 635 633 635 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c30">30</xref>
It has been proposed that Glu56 contributes to metal binding in the active quaternary complex and its contribution to the catalytic power of MutT enzyme is calculated to be a factor of 101.4, as based on the data from mutants E56D or E56Q (30). In the present work, we isolated E56V as a positive mutant for Glu56. This mutant retained the full capability to suppress the increased mutation rates in mutT- cells, to the level of the wild-type. Since no positive mutant was isolated for the essential residue, Glu57, which is likely to contribute a factor of >105.0 to the catalytic power of MutT enzyme as a ligand-to-metal residue (30), we suggest that Glu56 is not crucial as a ligand-to-metal residue, or rather that valine at the 56th position can act as an efficient ligand-to-metal residue.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 235 237 235 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c18">18</xref>
###xml 557 559 557 559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c18">18</xref>
###xml 560 562 560 562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c19">19</xref>
###xml 766 767 766 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd499f3">3</xref>
###xml 1049 1051 1049 1051 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c18">18</xref>
###xml 714 719 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We reported that the chimeric MTH1 protein carrying the phosphohydrolase module of MutT is functional but thermolabile, suggesting that phosphohydrolase modules of MutT and MTH1 have different features regarding function or structure (18). The functional significance of each conserved residue in the module of MutT is mostly equivalent to that of MTH1 protein, because six and eight out of 10 conserved residues are essential for MutT and MTH1 function, respectively, and two non-essential residues, Thr45/Thr44 and Gly59/Gly58 are shared between the two (18,19). However, we found differences in the requirement of Lys38/Lys39 and Glu55/Glu56 for MTH1/MutT reaction, that is, these two residues are essential in human MTH1 but not in MutT, as summarized in Figure 3. This may be due to different semi- or non-conserved residues between MTH1 and MutT in or surrounding the modules, which may build up a different microenvironment for each phosphohydrolase module. We reported that some of the semi- or non-conserved residues in MTH1 are essential (18). Thus, there are different structural features between the phosphohydrolase modules of MTH1 and MutT proteins, and saturation mutagenesis of the semi- and non-conserved residues in the module of MutT protein is expected to reveal their molecular basis.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 19 20 19 20 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkd499f3">3</xref>
###xml 153 155 153 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c18">18</xref>
###xml 156 158 156 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c19">19</xref>
###xml 387 389 387 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c32">32</xref>
###xml 474 476 474 476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkd499c30">30</xref>
As shown in Figure 3, the residues in MTH1 corresponding to the Gly37, Gly38, Lys39, Glu44, Arg52, Glu53, Glu56 and Glu57 in MutT are essential in MTH1 (18,19). In our recent NMR analysis of MTH1 (N.Itoh, M.Mishima, Y.Nakabeppu and M.Shirakawa, unpublished results), we found that MTH1 essentially has a tertiary structure similar to that previously proposed based on computer analysis (32). We suggest that the proposed mechanism of pyrophosphohydrolase reaction for MutT (30) may be applicable to the MTH1 reaction. That is, Lys38 in MTH1 interacts electrostatically with the alpha-phosphoryl group of 8-oxo-dGTP and thereby facilitates the departure of the 8-oxo-dGMP group, or Glu55 in MTH1 plays a crucial role as a ligand-to-metal residue. To demonstrate this model of MutT or MTH1, it will be necessary to analyze protein complexes with 8-oxo-dGTP or 8-oxo-dGDP, a non-hydrolysable substrate of MTH1.
###end p 65
