Regulation of PKD by the MAPK p38delta in Insulin Secretion and Glucose Homeostasis

###begin title 3
Summary
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 182 190 179 183 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 300 308 290 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 586 594 568 572 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 780 788 755 759 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 831 839 802 806 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 125 129 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 600 604 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 845 849 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Dysfunction and loss of insulin-producing pancreatic beta cells represent hallmarks of diabetes mellitus. Here, we show that mice lacking the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) p38delta display improved glucose tolerance due to enhanced insulin secretion from pancreatic beta cells. Deletion of p38delta results in pronounced activation of protein kinase D (PKD), the latter of which we have identified as a pivotal regulator of stimulated insulin exocytosis. p38delta catalyzes an inhibitory phosphorylation of PKD1, thereby attenuating stimulated insulin secretion. In addition, p38delta null mice are protected against high-fat-feeding-induced insulin resistance and oxidative stress-mediated beta cell failure. Inhibition of PKD1 reverses enhanced insulin secretion from p38delta-deficient islets and glucose tolerance in p38delta null mice as well as their susceptibility to oxidative stress. In conclusion, the p38delta-PKD pathway integrates regulation of the insulin secretory capacity and survival of pancreatic beta cells, pointing to a pivotal role for this pathway in the development of overt diabetes mellitus.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Introduction
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 169 193 166 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib43" ref-type="bibr">von Herrath et&#160;al., 2007</xref>
###xml 553 570 544 561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib22" ref-type="bibr">Kahn et&#160;al., 2006</xref>
###xml 806 835 794 823 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib44" ref-type="bibr">Wellen and Hotamisligil, 2005</xref>
###xml 871 874 859 862 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">jun</italic>
###xml 1074 1116 1062 1104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib19 bib23" ref-type="bibr">Hirosumi et&#160;al., 2002; Kaneto et&#160;al., 2004</xref>
Diabetes results from insufficient (absolute or relative) insulin secretion. In type 1 diabetes, the insulin-producing beta cells are destroyed by an autoimmune attack (von Herrath et al., 2007). Type 2 diabetes is often linked to obesity-related insulin resistance, which initially is compensated by enhanced capacity of beta cells to secrete insulin. However, in a large subset of obese and insulin-resistant individuals, these compensatory mechanisms are impaired, leading to reduced beta cell mass and function and culminating in manifest diabetes (Kahn et al., 2006). beta cell damage and insulin resistance appear to be at least partially triggered by inflammatory, oxidative, and endoplasmic reticulum stress-induced pathways including the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signaling cascade (Wellen and Hotamisligil, 2005). Indeed, activation of the MAPK c-jun N-terminal kinase (JNK) represents a central signal transduction event promoting peripheral insulin resistance, suppressing insulin production and secretion, and increasing apoptosis of islet cells (Hirosumi et al., 2002; Kaneto et al., 2004).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 218 240 218 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib26" ref-type="bibr">Koistinen et&#160;al., 2003</xref>
###xml 498 520 494 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib9" ref-type="bibr">de Alvaro et&#160;al., 2004</xref>
###xml 672 692 665 685 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib31" ref-type="bibr">Makeeva et&#160;al., 2006</xref>
###xml 813 816 806 809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38</italic>
###xml 824 832 817 821 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#945;</italic>
###xml 834 841 823 827 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#946;</italic>
###xml 843 851 829 833 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#947;</italic>
###xml 857 865 839 843 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 208 216 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
The role of the p38 MAPKs (which are closely related to JNK) in these processes remains poorly understood. p38 activity has been reported to be increased in insulin-resistant peripheral tissues from diabetic patients (Koistinen et al., 2003). Moreover, in vitro data have demonstrated that p38 activation upon exposure to TNF-alpha, free fatty acids, and oxidative stress impairs insulin signaling in adipocytes and skeletal muscle cells through mechanisms very similar to those described for JNK (de Alvaro et al., 2004). Finally, activation of p38 appears to trigger pancreatic beta cell dysfunction and apoptosis in response to oxidative stress and cytokines in vitro (Makeeva et al., 2006). Verification of an involvement of p38 in metabolic diseases in vivo is complicated by the existence of four different p38 genes, p38alpha, p38beta, p38gamma, and p38delta.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 80 88 76 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#945;</italic>
###xml 179 240 171 232 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib1 bib32 bib39" ref-type="bibr">Adams et&#160;al., 2000; Mudgett et&#160;al., 2000; Tamura et&#160;al., 2000</xref>
###xml 373 411 361 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib20 bib41" ref-type="bibr">Hui et&#160;al., 2007; Ventura et&#160;al., 2007</xref>
###xml 492 509 476 493 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib24" ref-type="bibr">Kang et&#160;al., 2008</xref>
###xml 665 672 642 646 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#946;</italic>
###xml 776 798 750 772 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib6" ref-type="bibr">Beardmore et&#160;al., 2005</xref>
###xml 98 102 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 691 695 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To date, the best-characterized p38 isoform is p38alpha. It has been shown that p38alpha knockout mice die at midgestation, likely because of a defective placental organogenesis (Adams et al., 2000; Mudgett et al., 2000; Tamura et al., 2000). Recently, it has been demonstrated that p38alpha is a fundamental regulator of cellular proliferation and carcinogenesis in vivo (Hui et al., 2007; Ventura et al., 2007). p38alpha is also required to mediate an inflammatory response in macrophages (Kang et al., 2008). On the basis of inhibitor studies, it was postulated that p38alpha and p38beta functionally cooperate in the context of inflammatory processes. However, p38beta-specific knockout mice revealed no differences in several in vivo and in vitro models of inflammation (Beardmore et al., 2005).
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 130 138 118 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#947;</italic>
###xml 222 245 206 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib36" ref-type="bibr">Perdiguero et&#160;al., 2007</xref>
###xml 497 515 469 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib21" ref-type="bibr">Jiang et&#160;al., 1997</xref>
###xml 671 691 635 655 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib12" ref-type="bibr">Efimova et&#160;al., 2004</xref>
###xml 780 799 744 763 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib13" ref-type="bibr">Feijoo et&#160;al., 2005</xref>
The least-characterized isoforms are p38gamma and p38delta. p38gamma is predominantly expressed in skeletal muscle and heart, and p38gamma-deficient myoblasts exhibited an attenuated cell-to-cell fusion capacity in vitro (Perdiguero et al., 2007). The isoform p38delta shares approximately 60% homology with the other p38 family members and about 40% homology with other MAPKs. Like p38alpha, p38delta is activated by various stress stimuli, including inflammatory cytokines and oxidative stress (Jiang et al., 1997). Recent in vitro studies have demonstrated that p38delta might be involved in keratinocyte differentiation and PKCdelta-dependent keratinocyte apoptosis (Efimova et al., 2004), as well as the progression of neurodegenerative disorders referred to as tauopathies (Feijoo et al., 2005). However, no specific in vivo functions of p38delta have been reported thus far.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 40 48 40 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 209 217 202 206 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 54 58 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 78 82 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To address such functions, we generated p38delta null mice. Remarkably, these mice displayed improved glucose tolerance due to enhanced insulin exocytosis from pancreatic beta cells. Moreover, inactivation of p38delta protected against hyperlipidemia-induced insulin resistance and oxidative stress-imposed beta cell apoptosis. At the molecular level, we discovered that p38delta exerts an inhibitory phosphorylation on protein kinase D 1 (PKD1), a kinase that regulates both stimulated insulin secretion and pancreatic beta cell survival. We propose that p38delta represents a critical regulator of glucose homeostasis in vivo.
###end p 10

To address in vivo functions of p38delta in metabolism, we generated p38delta floxed mice and crossed them with protamine promoter-driven Cre recombinase-expressing mice, a male germline deleter strain (O'Gorman et al., 1997). With this approach, we obtained p38delta null mice (p38deltaDelta/Delta mice) and corresponding wild-type control littermates (p38delta+/+ mice) (Figure S1 available online). p38deltaDelta/Delta mice presented with normal general health, viability, fecundity, body composition, and body weight (data not shown). Assessment of the expression of p38delta in organs involved in glucose homeostasis revealed that p38delta was abundantly expressed both at the mRNA and protein level and in similar amounts in the exo- and endocrine pancreas. In contrast, no expression of p38delta was observed in insulin-sensing organs such as adipose tissue and liver. Very low amounts of p38delta mRNA were detected in skeletal muscle (Figure 1A and Figure S2A).

The observed expression pattern prompted us to investigate the role of p38delta in glucose homeostasis. p38deltaDelta/Delta mice fasted for 16 hr showed a significantly enhanced glucose tolerance compared to p38delta+/+ mice (Figure 1B), while insulin sensitivity was equal in p38deltaDelta/Delta and p38delta+/+ mice (Figure S2B). To test whether enhanced insulin secretion in p38deltaDelta/Delta mice primarily reflects an intrinsic islet/beta cell effect that is independent of the action of incretins or innervation, we isolated pancreatic islets from p38deltaDelta/Delta and p38delta+/+ mice and tested their capacity to secrete insulin in vitro. Knockout islets released more insulin both under basal (2.8 mM glucose) and glucose-stimulated (16.7 mM glucose) conditions compared to control islets (Figure 1D). By contrast, glucagon secretion was unaffected in p38deltaDelta/Delta islets, and high glucose (16.7 mM) inhibited the release of the hormone to the same extent in both wild-type and knockout islets (Figure 1E). These data suggest that lack of p38delta improves glucose tolerance and enhances insulin secretion by a direct and beta cell-specific mechanism.

To determine whether ablation of p38delta affects Ca2+ influx, we measured intracellular calcium concentrations ([Ca2+]i) in response to glucose and KCl in islets isolated from p38deltaDelta/Delta and p38delta+/+ mice. The average [Ca2+]i was similar in the two groups of islets both under basal conditions (2.8 mM glucose) and after stimulation by either glucose (16.7 mM) or KCl (20 mM in the presence of 2.8 mM glucose) (Figures S4A and S4B). To address whether exocytosis is enhanced in p38delta-deficient beta cells, we performed high-resolution capacitance measurements of exocytosis on single beta cells (Gopel et al., 2004). A train of ten depolarization steps from -70 mV to 0 mV evoked larger responses in p38deltaDelta/Delta than in control beta cells, resulting in a 2-fold larger increase of membrane capacitance in p38deltaDelta/Delta cells (Figures 2A and 2B). Exocytosis was enhanced approximately equally in p38deltaDelta/Delta compared to p38delta+/+ beta cells throughout the trains of depolarization. The rate of capacitance increase in cells lacking p38delta was also higher than that of wild-type cells when exocytosis was elicited by clamping [Ca2+] at 1.5 muM, a condition that bypasses any effects on Ca2+ entry (Figures 2C and 2D). Collectively, these results indicate that the enhanced insulin secretion from p38delta-deficient islets is not caused by differences in glucose metabolism, KATP closure, or [Ca2+]i homeostasis between knockout and control beta cells. Rather, ablation of p38delta influences insulin secretion by a direct effect on the exocytotic machinery that is exerted downstream of [Ca2+]i elevation.

We next investigated a possible molecular mechanism by which p38delta attenuates insulin exocytosis using an unbiased proteomic approach. We ectopically expressed a hemagglutinin (HA)-tagged constitutively active form of p38delta, obtained by substitution of phenylalanine 324 with serine (F324S) as previously described (Askari et al., 2007), in 293T cells. Constitutive activity was tested in an in vitro kinase assay using recombinant HIS-tagged ATF-2 as a substrate (Figure S5A). HA immunoprecipitates from HA-p38deltaF324S and HA-expressing cells were analyzed by liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS). A number of putative interactors were obtained among which protein kinase D 1 (PKD1) was most frequently found (represented by a total of 36 unique peptides) (Table S2). PKD is necessary for biogenesis of trans-Golgi network (TGN) to cell surface transport carriers (Bard and Malhotra, 2006) and has been shown to be a positive regulator of secretion in neuroendocrine cells (Li et al., 2004). The interaction between p38delta and PKD1 was confirmed by coimmunoprecipitation experiments in 293T cells (Figure S5B). HA pulldowns also contained endogenous PKD1 in INS1 cells (rat insulinoma-derived pancreatic beta cell line) stably expressing HA-p38deltaF324S but not in cells expressing HA alone (Figure 3A).

We then tested the ability of p38delta to directly phosphorylate PKD1 in vitro. Active recombinant p38delta phosphorylated immunoprecipitated human GFP-tagged wild-type PKD1 (GFP-PKD1-WT) and kinase-dead PKD1 (GFP-PKD1-KD) (Figure S5C), as well as Sf9 cell-derived (Figure S5D) and E. coli-expressed recombinant human GST-PKD1 (Figure 3B). Bacterial PKD1 showed high basal activity that caused high autophosphorylation. However, enhancement of phosphorylation by addition of p38delta became more evident when the PKD inhibitor Go6976 was added to the reactions to reduce autophosphorylation. The LC-MS/MS analysis of in vitro phosphorylated immunoprecipitated murine GST-PKD1 identified an autophosphorylation and a 14-3-3 binding site (Ser203 and Ser206) (Zhang et al., 2005), one of the activating dual phosphorylation sites (Ser748), and a protein kinase C-dependent transphosporylation site (Ser255) (Vertommen et al., 2000) (Figure S6). p38delta specifically phosphorylated Ser403, a p38 MAPK consensus site (SP/TP), as well as the closely located Ser407 residue (Figure S7). LC-MS/MS analysis of the HA-p38deltaF324S immunoprecipitates from 293T cells confirmed Ser403 phosphorylation on endogenous PKD (data not shown). Both phosphorylation sites are conserved between mouse (Ser403 and Ser407), rat, and human (Ser397 and Ser401) and can be found in PKD1 and PKD3 but not in PKD2 (Figure 3C). PKD1 is the predominant isoform expressed in pancreatic beta cells (data not shown).

We next generated the corresponding single and double serine to alanine mutants of kinase-dead PKD1 and double serine to alanine mutants of wild-type PKD1 (GFP-PKD1-AA), as well as double serine to aspartate mutants of wild-type PKD1 (GFP-PKD1-DD). Mutations of either Ser397 or Ser401 to alanines significantly reduced p38delta-dependent phosphorylation in vitro, and mutation of both sites diminished autoradiography almost to background levels (Figure 3D). The ability of kinases to phosphorylate the substrate CREBtide was assayed in vitro. Autoradiography of spotted CREBtide from reactions with GFP-PKD1-KD was markedly reduced compared to reactions with GFP-PKD1-WT, while no signal could be detected with CREBtide from reactions with GFP only. Importantly, CREBtide autoradiography from reactions containing GFP-PKD1-AA was enhanced, whereas it was markedly reduced in reactions with GFP-PKD1-DD compared to reactions with GFP-PKD1-WT. ###xml 571 581 548 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="app2" ref-type="sec">Figure&#160;S10</xref>

###xml 746 754 719 727 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;4</xref>

Activation of PKD induces membrane fission at the TGN, which can be monitored by altered localization of Golgi marker proteins by immunofluorescence (Bossard et al., 2007; Liljedahl et al., 2001). Indeed, the Golgi markers giantin, furin convertase, and GM130 exhibited a diffuse distribution in p38deltaDelta/Delta primary pancreatic beta cells, whereas a characteristic crescent-shaped staining was found in p38delta+/+ cells (Figure 4B and Figure S9). Altered Golgi organization was confirmed in MIN6 cells lacking p38delta by immunofluorescence stainings of giantin (Figure S10). Conversely, INS1 cells ectopically expressing HA-p38deltaF324S showed tubular protrusions from the Golgi apparatus reminiscent of inhibited TGN membrane fission (Figure 4C). The latter cellular phenotype correlated with suppressed stimulated insulin secretion in INS1 cells (see below). Collectively, these data indicate that lack of p38delta leads to constitutive PKD activity and enhanced membrane fission at the TGN in pancreatic beta cells, whereas increased p38delta activity has the opposite effects.

###xml 1235 1243 1180 1188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;5</xref>

Generation of diacylglycerol (DAG) by phosphatidyl-inositol-specific phospholipases C (PI-PLCs; PLC, phospolipase C) activates PKD, which is subsequently recruited to the TGN, where it promotes membrane fission (Diaz Anel, 2007). Pharmacological inhibition of this pathway is expected to reverse cellular phenotypes and related effects caused by deletion of p38delta. To test this, we used the PI-PLC inhibitor U73122 and Go6976, a potent inhibitor of PKD and conventional protein kinases C (PKCs) (Haxhinasto and Bishop, 2003). Both U73122 and Go6976 resulted in relocalization of giantin to the TGN in p38deltaDelta/Delta pancreatic beta cells, whereas none of the compounds altered normal localization of giantin in p38delta+/+ cells (Figure 5A). These cellular effects echoed those on glucose-evoked (16.7 mM) insulin secretion: none of the compounds had any effect on insulin secretion from p38delta+/+ islets, but the enhancement seen in p38deltaDelta/Delta islets was abolished (Figure 5B). Importantly, peritoneal injections of U73122 decreased glucose tolerance of p38deltaDelta/Delta mice to that observed in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)-treated p38delta+/+ controls, without modifying glucose tolerance of the control animals (Figure 5C). Thus, inhibition of PKD reverses the effects of p38delta ablation on Golgi organization, insulin secretion, and glucose tolerance.

###xml 436 444 433 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig6" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;6</xref>

PKD resides in the Gq protein-coupled receptor (GqPCR) pathway, which in beta cells is known to be strongly activated by the insulin secretagogue acetylcholine (Gilon and Henquin, 2001). Indeed, activity of PKD was markedly increased upon stimulation with the acetylcholine analog carbachol. By contrast, exendin-4 (a GLP-1 analog) (Drucker, 2006), forskolin (Wiedenkeller and Sharp, 1983), and glucose did not detectably activate PKD (Figure 6A). The activity of p38delta in response to carbachol was examined in INS1 cells with the Phos-tag technology (Kinoshita et al., 2006). Both phosphorylated p38delta and nonphosphorylated p38delta declined in INS1 cells (Figure 6B), indicating that carbachol-induced activation of PKD1 correlates with p38delta inhibition.

We next assessed the role of PKD1 in insulin secretion. PKD1 was specifically inactivated in INS1 cells by siRNA-mediated knockdown with two independent oligonucleotide sequences as compared to a scrambled control siRNA (Figure S11A). Although an induction of PKD activity was only apparent upon stimulation with carbachol, deletion of PKD1 in INS1 cells completely blocked insulin secretion in response to both carbachol and glucose (Figure S11B), indicating a general requirement of PKD1 in stimulated insulin secretion. We went on to investigate whether PKD activity and insulin secretion in response to glucose and carbachol is p38delta dependent. Carbachol-stimulated activation of PKD was markedly reduced in INS1 cells expressing HA-p38deltaF324S compared to cells expressing HA alone (Figure 6C). Importantly, expression of HA-p38deltaF324S in INS1 cells completely blocked insulin secretion in response to carbachol and also attenuated secretion in response to glucose (Figure 6D).

###xml 924 935 872 875 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;/&#916;</sup>
###xml 982 990 922 926 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 990 1001 926 929 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;/&#916;</sup>
###xml 1085 1093 1013 1017 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 1093 1096 1017 1020 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/+</sup>
###xml 1124 1133 1048 1057 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Figures 7</xref>
###xml 1180 1188 1104 1108 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 1188 1191 1108 1111 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/+</sup>
###xml 424 428 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 562 566 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 786 790 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 811 815 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 936 940 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1002 1006 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1097 1101 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1192 1196 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Oxidative stress is known to contribute to pancreatic beta cell loss in insulin resistance-related diabetes mellitus (Fridlyand and Philipson, 2006). Streptozotocin (STZ)-induced oxidative stress is a widely used model to trigger pancreatic beta cell failure in vivo (Le May et al., 2006). We confirmed that STZ activates p38delta in pancreatic beta cells (Figure S15A). As expected, glucose levels increased in p38delta+/+ mice after STZ injection, reaching up to 15 mmol/l concentrations at day 8. However, no such increase was observed in p38deltaDelta/Delta mice, in which plasma glucose levels remained stable and around 7 mmol/l throughout the observation period (Figure S15B). Plasma insulin levels and pancreatic insulin content were significantly higher in p38deltaDelta/Delta mice than in p38delta+/+ mice treated with STZ (Figures S15C and S15D). The involvement of PKD in the protection of beta cells in p38deltaDelta/Delta mice was tested with the inhibitor U73122. In p38deltaDelta/Delta mice, U73122 increased plasma glucose and lowered insulin to the same levels as in p38delta+/+ mice after STZ injections (Figures 7C-7E). The inhibitor had no additive effect in p38delta+/+ mice.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 66 74 66 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 74 77 70 73 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+/+</sup>
###xml 181 189 174 178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 189 200 178 181 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#916;/&#916;</sup>
###xml 240 248 221 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 287 296 264 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Figures 7</xref>
###xml 78 82 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 201 205 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
TUNEL staining revealed that whereas STZ-induced hyperglycemia in p38delta+/+ mice was associated with a high rate of beta cell apoptosis, the rate of apoptosis was 5-fold lower in p38deltaDelta/Delta mice. The protective effect of lack of p38delta on apoptosis was abolished by U73122 (Figures 7F and 7G). These data raise the interesting possibility that p38delta-imposed inhibition of PKD might contribute to beta cell dysfunction and destruction in diabetic subjects.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
Discussion
###end title 40
###begin p 41
A nonredundant and specific in vivo function of the delta isoform of the MAPK family p38 has not been elucidated so far. We now provide compelling evidence that p38delta represents a key regulator of pancreatic beta cell function. Our work supports a negative regulatory role for p38delta in stimulated insulin secretion through inhibition of PKD1 and regulation of exocytosis. Moreover, immoderate suppression of PKD activity by p38delta may also contribute to beta cell dysfunction in diabetic subjects.
###end p 41
###begin title 42
The p38delta-PKD1 Pathway Regulates Stimulated Insulin Secretion from Pancreatic beta Cells
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 32 40 32 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 577 596 563 582 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib15" ref-type="bibr">Gautam et&#160;al., 2006</xref>
###xml 639 640 625 626 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">q</sub>
###xml 726 727 712 713 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 812 831 798 817 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib35" ref-type="bibr">Oancea et&#160;al., 2003</xref>
###xml 834 842 820 828 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;7</xref>
We demonstrate that ablation of p38delta activates PKD1, and thereby enhances insulin secretion and consequently improves glucose tolerance. Furthermore, we show that the acetylcholine analog carbachol strongly activates PKD in pancreatic beta cells. This physiological function of PKD1 can be completely blocked by enhancement of p38delta activity. Acetylcholine represents the major neurotransmitter of the peripheral parasympathetic nervous system, and its binding on muscarinic acetylcholine receptors located on the pancreatic beta cells potentiates secretion of insulin (Gautam et al., 2006). Indeed, muscarinic receptors belong to Gq protein-coupled receptors stimulating PLC to produce inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP3) and DAG, the latter of which activates numerous PKC family members, including PKD (Oancea et al., 2003) (Figure 7H).
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 197 205 191 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 326 334 316 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;4</xref>
###xml 401 426 388 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib37" ref-type="bibr">Peter-Riesch et&#160;al., 1988</xref>
###xml 543 545 530 532 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 546 547 533 534 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 555 557 542 544 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 585 603 572 590 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib40" ref-type="bibr">Thore et&#160;al., 2007</xref>
###xml 606 614 593 601 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;7</xref>
Importantly, PKD1 deletion also blocks insulin secretion in response to glucose. PKD activity upon glucose stimulation could not be detected in wild-type beta cells. However, in beta cells lacking p38delta, in which PKD activity is constitutively enhanced, a 2-fold increase of PKD activity was seen upon glucose stimulation (Figure 4A). It has been reported that glucose generates DAG in beta cells (Peter-Riesch et al., 1988). This can either occur by a direct effect of glucose metabolism or be secondary to glucose-induced increases in [Ca2+]i- and Ca2+-induced activation of PLC (Thore et al., 2007) (Figure 7H). It is therefore tempting to speculate that activity of PKD is also rising in response to glucose in wild-type cells but is probably below our detection limit. Altogether, our data support a key role of the p38delta-PKD1 pathway in stimulated insulin secretion.
###end p 44
###begin title 45
The p38delta-PKD1 Pathway Regulates Proximal and Distal Steps of Exocytosis in Pancreatic beta Cells
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 230 274 226 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib8 bib30" ref-type="bibr">Bossard et&#160;al., 2007; Liljedahl et&#160;al., 2001</xref>
###xml 332 340 328 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
The findings in capacitance experiments conform to functions of the herein identified p38delta target, PKD1. One of the most established roles of PKD is to promote fission of cell surface-destined transport carriers from the TGN (Bossard et al., 2007; Liljedahl et al., 2001). Enhanced membrane fission at the TGN in the absence of p38delta most likely accounts for late effects (pulses 5 to 10) seen in capacitance experiments.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 261 276 261 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib29" ref-type="bibr">Li et&#160;al., 2004</xref>
###xml 590 605 590 605 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib28" ref-type="bibr">Li et&#160;al., 2008</xref>
###xml 735 737 735 737 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 802 804 802 804 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 887 910 887 910 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib16" ref-type="bibr">Gilon and Henquin, 2001</xref>
###xml 1097 1099 1097 1099 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 1100 1101 1100 1101 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i</sub>
###xml 1103 1122 1103 1122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib2" ref-type="bibr">Ammala et&#160;al., 1994</xref>
Importantly, it has been demonstrated that ectopic expression of constitutively active PKD is sufficient to promote secretion of neurotensin, implying that PKD in addition to its function at the TGN primes vesicles for efficient transport and immediate fusion (Li et al., 2004). Accordingly, enhanced PKD activity might also explain early effects (pulses 1 to 4) observed in capacitance experiments. Indeed, a very recent report suggests that PKD-mediated secretion of neurotensin requires its target, Kidins220, the latter of which is proposed to regulate more distal steps of exocytosis (Li et al., 2008). Moreover, although not specifically reported for PKD so far, PKCs downstream of DAG were shown to increase the efficiency of Ca2+ on insulin exocytosis independent of a rise in cytosolic free Ca2+ levels, a mechanism that also underlies acetylcholine-mediated insulin secretion (Gilon and Henquin, 2001). The observed increase of exocytosis is also reminiscent of that previously reported for the PKC/PKD activator PMA, the latter effect also being exerted distally to the elevation of [Ca2+]i (Ammala et al., 1994).
###end p 47
###begin p 48
Overall, our data indicate that stimulated insulin secretion is increased, at least partially, through enhancement of the efficiency of TGN function, a cellular mechanism, which has not been reported so far in this context.
###end p 48
###begin title 49
The p38delta-PKD1 Axis Might be Pivotal in Maintaining beta Cell Function in Diabetic Conditions
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 214 222 214 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 287 295 283 287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 89 93 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 228 232 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 301 305 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 448 452 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In our study, we have challenged the secretory capacity of pancreatic islets in knockout mice with a high-fat diet, which induces peripheral insulin resistance, leading to adaptive hyperinsulinemia. Interestingly, p38delta null mice developed less severe insulin resistance. Strikingly, p38delta null mice on a high-fat diet became hyperinsulinemic, reaching fasting insulin levels that were significantly higher than those in high fat-fed control mice. The relative importance of increased insulin sensitivity and enhanced insulin secretion to the overall improved glucose tolerance needs to be further investigated in the future.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 237 260 227 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib33" ref-type="bibr">Muoio and Newgard, 2008</xref>
###xml 275 283 265 269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p38&#948;</italic>
###xml 600 621 579 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib38" ref-type="bibr">Storz and Toker, 2003</xref>
###xml 923 931 891 899 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Figure&#160;7</xref>
###xml 293 297 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Additionally, we have shown that p38delta plays yet another key function in the beta cell: the regulation of beta cell destruction during oxidative stress, which is a key pathogenic mechnism in both, type 1 and type 2 diabetes mellitus (Muoio and Newgard, 2008). Strikingly, p38delta knockout mice were protected against STZ-imposed oxidative stress in beta cells. Importantly, this phenotype also appears to be dependent on PKD activity. This is in agreement with the report demonstrating that PKD activation is protective against oxidative stress-induced apoptosis through activation of NF-kappaB (Storz and Toker, 2003). Even though p38delta fine-tunes PKD-mediated insulin secretion in normal physiologic settings, in pathological situations of gradually increasing cellular oxidative stress, p38delta activity might exceed and PKD activity may drop to levels affecting insulin secretion as well as beta cell survival (Figure 7H).
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 176 202 170 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" rid="bib4" ref-type="bibr">Aston-Mourney et&#160;al., 2008</xref>
###xml 619 624 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
There is accumulating evidence that forcing beta cells to secrete insulin by currently used drugs, including sulfonylureas, ultimately results in pancreatic beta cell failure (Aston-Mourney et al., 2008). An ideal pharmacological diabetes therapy should therefore combine an insulinotropic effect with protection against beta cell failure. Our data suggest that pharmacological suppression of p38delta might represent such an approach. Conclusively, the p38delta-PKD pathway modulates both insulin secretion and beta cell turnover and thus provides a unifying mechanism that integrates these two pathogenic features of human diabetes.
###end p 52

For generation of p38delta null mice, a targeting vector harboring LoxP sites within the 5' UTR region and intron 1-2 (floxed exon 1) as well as a neomycin resistance cassette flanked by two FRT sites were electroporated into 129/SvEvBrd (Lex-1) ES cells. ###begin title 56
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
Mouse Experiments
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 48 52 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 280 284 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 448 452 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 660 664 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 832 836 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1017 1021 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1101 1105 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
For the glucose tolerance test, 8-week-old male mice were fasted for 16 hr and then injected intraperitoneally (i.p.) with glucose (2 g/kg body weight [bw]). Glucose was measured with the Accu-Chek Aviva system from Roche. In experiments with the PI-PLC inhibitor U73122 (Sigma), mice were injected i.p. with U73122 (2.5 mg/kg bw) in DMSO or with DMSO 1 hr prior experiment. Insulin tolerance tests were performed on 8-week-old ad libitum-fed male mice injected i.p. with insulin (1 U/kg bw). Plasma insulin levels were measured with an insulin RIA kit (Linco). STZ (150 mg/kg bw) was injected once i.p., and blood glucose was measured each day. After 8 days, mice were sacrificed, serum was harvested, and the pancreas was isolated to measure insulin serum levels and insulin content, respectively. In STZ experiments with U73122, mice were injected i.p. with U73122 (2.5 mg/kg bw) in DMSO or with DMSO only, three times (day 1, 2, and 3) after STZ injection. Peripheral insulin resistance was induced by feeding of mice for 12 weeks with a high-fat diet (Research Diets, D12331). After this period, mice were subjected to insulin tolerance tests (1 U/kg bw) as well as glucose tolerance tests (1 g/kg bw).
###end p 57
###begin title 58
Islets Isolation and Insulin Secretion
###end title 58
###begin p 59
###xml 472 478 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
Islets were isolated by collagenase perfusion (1.9 U/ml) of pancreas and subsequent digestion for 16 min at 37degreesC. Islets were handpicked and transferred to RPMI1640 media containing 5 mM glucose and maintained for 3 hr prior to the experiments or dispersed in calcium-free solution and then cultured as above. For static incubations, islets were subsequently preincubated for 60 min at 37degreesC in Krebs-Ringer bicarbonate buffer (pH 7.4), supplemented with 0.05% bovine serum albumin (BSA) and 2.8 mM glucose. After preincubation, the supernatant was discarded and the islets were incubated in buffer solution containing 2.8 or 16.7 mM glucose for 60 min at 37degreesC. For the rescue experiments, islets were incubated as described above but in presence of 10 muM of U73122 or 1 muM Go6976 or DMSO. The islets were resuspended in acid ethanol and frozen for insulin content measurements. Insulin secretion data were expressed as percentage of pellet. Insulin secretion from INS1 and MIN6 cells was performed as described for islets, and data were normalized to nonstimulated control cells. Total insulin from pancreas was isolated by acid-ethanol extraction. Insulin content was normalized to the total weight of the pancreas.
