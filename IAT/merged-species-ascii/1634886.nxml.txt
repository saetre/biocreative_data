###begin article-title 0
Ablation of PGC-1beta Results in Defective Mitochondrial Activity, Thermogenesis, Hepatic Function, and Cardiac Performance
###end article-title 0
###begin article-title 1
PGC-1beta: A Regulator of Mitochondrial Function with Subtle Roles in Energy Metabolism
###end article-title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 167 172 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 306 310 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 932 936 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1267 1271 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The transcriptional coactivator peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-gamma coactivator-1beta (PGC-1beta) has been implicated in important metabolic processes. A mouse lacking PGC-1beta (PGC1betaKO) was generated and phenotyped using physiological, molecular, and bioinformatic approaches. PGC1betaKO mice are generally viable and metabolically healthy. Using systems biology, we identified a general defect in the expression of genes involved in mitochondrial function and, specifically, the electron transport chain. This defect correlated with reduced mitochondrial volume fraction in soleus muscle and heart, but not brown adipose tissue (BAT). Under ambient temperature conditions, PGC-1beta ablation was partially compensated by up-regulation of PGC-1alpha in BAT and white adipose tissue (WAT) that lead to increased thermogenesis, reduced body weight, and reduced fat mass. Despite their decreased fat mass, PGC1betaKO mice had hypertrophic adipocytes in WAT. The thermogenic role of PGC-1beta was identified in thermoneutral and cold-adapted conditions by inadequate responses to norepinephrine injection. Furthermore, PGC1betaKO hearts showed a blunted chronotropic response to dobutamine stimulation, and isolated soleus muscle fibres from PGC1betaKO mice have impaired mitochondrial function. Lack of PGC-1beta also impaired hepatic lipid metabolism in response to acute high fat dietary loads, resulting in hepatic steatosis and reduced lipoprotein-associated triglyceride and cholesterol content. Altogether, our data suggest that PGC-1beta plays a general role in controlling basal mitochondrial function and also participates in tissue-specific adaptive responses during metabolic stress.

###begin title 4
Introduction
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 122 123 122 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b001">1</xref>
###xml 515 516 515 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b001">1</xref>
Transcriptional coactivators (TCs) have emerged as key proteins that are capable of regulating entire metabolic networks [1]. TCs are often selective with respect to the repertoire of transcription factor partners. The mechanisms used to modulate transcription may vary according to the specific TC, their transcription factor partners, and the nucleic acid environment in which they operate. TCs provide an additional layer of control by facilitating temporal and tissue-specific regulation of metabolic pathways [1].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 109 110 101 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b002">2</xref>
###xml 418 419 406 407 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b002">2</xref>
###xml 420 421 408 409 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b004">4</xref>
###xml 522 523 506 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b005">5</xref>
###xml 554 555 535 536 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b006">6</xref>
###xml 556 557 537 538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b008">8</xref>
###xml 883 884 864 865 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b005">5</xref>
###xml 951 952 925 926 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b007">7</xref>
###xml 953 954 927 928 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b009">9</xref>
###xml 955 957 929 931 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b012">12</xref>
Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-gamma coactivator-1alpha (PGC-1alpha, previously known as PGC-1) [2] is involved in coordinating diverse organ-specific transcription programs. For example, PGC-1alpha not only activates the thermogenic program in brown adipose tissue (BAT) in response to environmental cold exposure but also modulates the metabolic transition from the fed to the fasted state in the liver [2-4]. Two other PGC-1alpha-related genes have subsequently been found, PGC-1-related coactivator (PRC) [5] and PGC-1beta (PERC/ERRL-1) [6-8]. All three family members share a similar basic structure; an N-terminal domain containing nuclear hormone receptor-interacting motifs (the LXXLL motifs) and a C-terminal region containing RNA-binding motifs (RMM) and serine-arginine rich (RS) domains. Whereas PRC is primarily an activator of nuclear respiratory factor-1 [5], PGC-1alpha and PGC-1beta have a broader repertoire of partners [7,9-12].
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 63 64 63 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b005">5</xref>
###xml 196 197 189 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b002">2</xref>
###xml 198 199 191 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b006">6</xref>
###xml 200 201 193 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b007">7</xref>
###xml 495 496 474 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b002">2</xref>
###xml 497 498 476 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b006">6</xref>
###xml 499 500 478 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b007">7</xref>
###xml 696 698 664 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b013">13</xref>
###xml 699 701 667 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b014">14</xref>
###xml 820 821 781 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b006">6</xref>
###xml 822 823 783 784 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b007">7</xref>
###xml 824 825 785 786 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b009">9</xref>
The gene expression level of PRC is conserved between tissues [5], but the highest expression of PGC-1alpha and PGC-1beta is found in oxidative tissues, including BAT, heart, and skeletal muscle [2,6,7]. However, despite this overlap of expression pattern, PGC-1alpha and -1beta display different responses to physiological stimuli. Whereas PGC-1alpha is transcriptionally up-regulated in BAT and skeletal muscle during cold exposure, PGC-1beta expression remains stable under these conditions [2,6,7]. Furthermore, up-regulation of PGC-1alpha is essential for a normal response to acute cold exposure, indicating that PGC-1beta is not able to compensate for all aspects of a lack of PGC-1alpha [13,14]. Similarly PGC-1alpha is up-regulated in liver in response to fasting, whereas PGC-1beta expression remained stable [6,7,9], further suggesting that PGC-1alpha is more often regulated transcriptionally than PGC-1beta.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 308 309 301 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b007">7</xref>
###xml 310 311 303 304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b009">9</xref>
###xml 312 314 305 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b015">15</xref>
###xml 462 464 448 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b015">15</xref>
###xml 882 884 859 861 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b010">10</xref>
###xml 769 773 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Overexpression studies both in vivo and in vitro have revealed that both PGC-1alpha and -1beta are capable of stimulating mitochondrial biogenesis, increasing mitochondrial oxygen consumption, and elevating gene expression of fatty acid oxidation and mitochondrial electron transport chain (ETC) components [7,9,15]. In addition, bioenergetic studies of C2C12 myotubes demonstrated that overexpression of PGC-1alpha or -1beta enhances mitochondrial proton leak [15]. Thus PGC-1beta increases mitochondrial energy production through a combination of activated fatty acid oxidation gene expression with increased mitochondrial activity. A role for PGC-1beta enhancing mitochondrial activity is further supported by the fact that transgenic overexpression of PGC-1beta in mice results in elevated energy expenditure, resistance to high-fat diet (HFD), and genetically induced obesity [10].
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 225 226 214 215 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b003">3</xref>
###xml 227 228 216 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b009">9</xref>
###xml 229 231 218 220 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b013">13</xref>
###xml 232 234 221 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b016">16</xref>
###xml 474 476 452 454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b011">11</xref>
###xml 595 597 570 572 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b017">17</xref>
###xml 754 755 719 720 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b002">2</xref>
###xml 756 757 721 722 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b006">6</xref>
###xml 1042 1044 999 1001 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b018">18</xref>
###xml 1045 1047 1002 1004 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b019">19</xref>
###xml 1173 1175 1126 1128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b014">14</xref>
###xml 1176 1178 1129 1131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b020">20</xref>
###xml 260 264 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1081 1085 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Despite their functional similarities, it has recently been suggested that PGC-1alpha and -1beta may have differing metabolic roles in liver. PGC-1alpha stimulates expression of gluconeogenic genes both in vivo and in vitro [3,9,13,16], and based on data from mice exposed to a diet enriched in saturated fat for a short period of time, it appears that PGC-1beta but not PGC-1alpha interacts with SREBP and LXRalpha to enhance hepatic lipogenesis and lipoprotein secretion [11]. In addition, PGC-1beta interacts with Foxa2 to facilitate hepatic secretion of very low density lipoprotein (VLDL) [17], suggesting a central role for PGC-1beta in hepatic lipid metabolism. The second organ where PGC-1alpha and -1beta are also highly expressed is the heart [2,6], but only the role of PGC-1alpha in cardiac energy metabolism has been studied in detail. Overexpression of PGC-1alpha in cardiac muscle in vivo leads to uncontrolled mitochondrial proliferation, leading to cardiomyopathy and congestive heart failure due to contractile dysfunction [18,19]. Similarly, PGC-1alpha knockout mice also develop heart failure with abnormal heart rate and impaired ventricular function [14,20], again suggesting that despite their similar mode of action, PGC-1beta is unable to compensate fully for a lack of PGC-1alpha.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 77 82 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 123 128 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 619 624 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
To investigate the function of PGC-1beta, we have generated and phenotyped a mouse model lacking PGC-1beta (the PGC1betaKO mouse). Using a combination of physiological experiments guided by a systems biology approach, we showed that PGC-1beta plays a general role in regulating the mitochondrial activity in tissues with active oxidative metabolism. In all tissues studied, lack of PGC-1beta was associated with a reduction in the expression of mitochondrial genes, including intermediary subunits and enzymes connected to ETC and oxidative phosphorylation (OxPhos). Despite these mitochondrial defects, the PGC1betaKO mouse was able to cope when challenged with a range of physiological stressors including cold acclimatisation and adrenergically-mediated BAT and cardiac stimulation but with abnormal compensatory responses. Altogether, these data suggest that PGC-1beta is an important regulator of basal energy homeostasis and essential for the proper metabolic tuning in acute stress situations in multiple organs.
###end p 10

Results
###end title 11
###begin title 12
###xml 27 32 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mouse</span>
Generation of a PGC1betaKO Mouse
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 77 85 74 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0040369-g001">Figure 1</xref>

###xml 960 968 948 956 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0040369-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 129 135 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Murine</span>
###xml 749 753 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 791 795 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 826 830 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 843 847 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
A conditional PGC1betaKO was generated using a triple LoxP targeting vector (Figure 1A) as detailed in the Material and Methods. Murine PGC-1beta is alternatively spliced at the 5' end [7] and at exon 4 [8,10]. Exons 4 and 5, which encode two of the nuclear hormone receptor interacting motifs (LXXLL), were deleted. Ablation of these exons also introduced a premature stop codon. PCR and Southern analyses of the selected clone confirmed the presence of the single LoxP in intron 5 and that the embyronic stem (ES) cell clone contained all three LoxP sites as designed (Figure 1B and 1C). Germ line transmission of the modified PGC-1beta allele was confirmed by PCR analysis of the agouti pups obtained from chimeras bred to C57BL/6 wild-type (WT) mice. Heterozygous triple-LoxP-containing mice were then bred with ROSA26Cre mice to generate mice heterozygous for the PGC-1beta deletion. Total LoxP site recombination was detected using PCR from genomic DNA (Figure 1D) and RT-PCR from isolated RNA. Intercross breeding of heterozygous germ-line knockout animals produced litters that did not deviate significantly from the expected Mendelian ratio of genotypes, and the size of litters from PGC1betaKO heterozygous crosses was normal (averaging seven to eight pups per breeding event per female).

###begin title 14
Effect of PGC-1beta Ablation on Energy Balance: Reduced Body Weight and WAT Content Consistent with Increased Energy Expenditure
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 160 168 160 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pbio-0040369-st001">Table S1</xref>
###xml 346 353 337 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pbio-0040369-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 681 689 669 677 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0040369-g002">Figure 2</xref>
###xml 202 206 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 340 344 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 636 640 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 750 754 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
No differences in food intake, total energy intake, total energy output as faeces, and no change in the ratio of energy in (as food) to energy out (as faeces) (Table S1) were detected in 12-wk-old male mice selected for equal body weight. Oxymax analysis revealed increased minimum metabolic rate at 22 degreesC in 9-wk-old male PGC1betaKO mice (Table 1). No differences in spontaneous activity (unpublished data) or in respiratory exchange ratio (RER) were detected, suggesting that there was no difference in whole-body substrate-type utilization. Consistent with an elevated minimum metabolic rate at ambient temperature, PGC1betaKO mice weighed less than their WT littermates (Figure 2A). A similar pattern was also observed in female PGC1betaKO mice.

###xml 241 249 238 246 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0040369-g002">Figure 2</xref>

###xml 1203 1212 1184 1193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pbio-0040369-sg002">Figure S2</xref>
###xml 208 212 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>

Body composition analysis using dual energy x-ray absorptiometry (DEXA) confirmed decreased body weight at the expense of fat mass rather than lean mass in both young (8-wk) and older (27-wk) male PGC1betaKO mice compared to WT littermates (Figure 2B). This was confirmed in an independent group of weight-matched 14-wk-old male PGC1betaKO mice that had smaller gonadal adipose tissue depots than WT littermates. Other organs examined did not show differences in their weights relative to body weight (Table S2). Histological analysis of male gonadal WAT from PGC1betaKO mice showed a reduced fraction of small adipocytes (up to an area of 2,000 mum2) and an elevated fraction of larger adipocytes in the PGC1betaKO WAT (Figure 2C and 2D). This pattern for larger white adipocytes was also seen in male subcutaneous and omental WAT and in female gonadal WAT (Figure S1). Therefore, when on a chow diet, the PGC1betaKO mice had less total WAT content than WT did, but it was composed of larger adipocytes. Histological analysis of other metabolically relevant tissues-including BAT, liver, skeletal muscle, and pancreas-did not reveal morphological alterations in PGC1betaKO mice under basal conditions (Figure S2).

Examination of BAT in chow-fed 12-wk-old PGC1betaKO male mice and WT littermates showed major changes in the expression of genes involved in intermediary metabolism (Figure 3). At ambient temperature, we identified a 4-fold compensatory increase in the expression of PGC-1alpha when PGC-1beta was ablated. We also observed a 5.9-fold elevation in the thyroid-dependent pro-thermogenic enzyme D2-deiodinase. These changes were associated with up-regulation in PGC1betaKO mice of genes involved in glucose and fatty acid metabolism (including GLUT4, PPARalpha, ADRP, LPL, and HSL) as well as elevations in PPARgamma1 and 2 and all three UCP isoforms. These changes suggested that increased PGC-1alpha expression was overcompensating for a lack of PGC-1beta. Despite this up-regulation of PGC-1alpha, ablation of PGC-1beta still resulted in a specific decreased expression of genes involved in mitochondrial ETC such as Cox5b and CytC. In addition, a further range of mitochondrially encoded (Figure 4A) and nuclear-encoded (Figure 4B) ETC mRNAs were also reduced in PGC1betaKO mouse BAT. The mitochondrially encoded Cox2 was the only ETC subunit measured in PGC1betaKO BAT with increased expression. Western blotting analysis of ETC components in BAT lysates from 15-wk-old female mice revealed decreases in subunits from complexes I to IV in PGC1betaKO mice (Figure 4C). Similarly, assessment of succinate dehydrogenase subunit B (SDHB, complex II) and Cox4 protein levels in isolated mitochondrial preparations were both reduced in PGC1betaKO mice (Figure 4D). ATP synthase subunit beta was unchanged. Taken together, these results demonstrate defective ETC expression and protein composition in the BAT of PGC1betaKO mice, which cannot be fully compensated for by up-regulation of PGC-1alpha. This suggests that the up-regulation of PGC-1alpha in BAT can compensate some, but not all, aspects of the mitochondrial system in this tissue.

###begin title 33
PGC-1beta Modulates the Response of the Liver to an Acute HFD Challenge
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 158 167 155 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pbio-0040369-sg006">Figure S6</xref>

Gene expression analysis of fed-state 12-wk-old male mouse livers showed ablation of PGC-1beta but did not result in major changes in mRNA expression levels (Figure S6). Only Cox4 was decreased in the PGC1betaKO livers, a result that was in agreement with gene expression pathway analysis (Table S3). When 8-wk-old female PGC1betaKO mice were challenged with a 24-h period of Surwit HFD, liver mass increased as a proportion of body weight (Figure 9A). ###begin title 36
Discussion
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 157 158 154 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b004">4</xref>
###xml 159 160 156 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b006">6</xref>
###xml 161 162 158 159 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b007">7</xref>
###xml 163 165 160 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b013">13</xref>
###xml 166 168 163 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b015">15</xref>
###xml 169 171 166 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b019">19</xref>
###xml 362 367 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 390 395 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 603 607 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Accumulating evidence indicated that PGC-1beta may play a role in energy homeostasis through its effects on substrate metabolism and mitochondrial activity [4,6,7,13-15,19]. However, the function and relevance of PGC-1beta in the control of whole-organism energy metabolism is not well defined. To address this question, we generated and phenotyped a PGC1betaKO mouse model. The PGC1betaKO mouse is viable and apparently healthy. However, using a phenotyping strategy that combines physiological stress challenges guided by information obtained from a systems biology approach, we found that PGC1betaKO mice have a general defect in mitochondrial function, a defect that is partly compensated for by mechanisms that are usually involved in adaptation to increased energy demands, such as up-regulation of PGC-1alpha.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
The effect of PGC-1beta controlling mitochondrial activity was investigated at the mRNA level using spotted array technology and bioinformatics pathway analysis. This approach showed that deletion of PGC-1beta results in a significant mitochondrial phenotype. Multi-tissue comparisons demonstrated that PGC-1beta controls the level of expression of ETC and OxPhos genes across all the organs studied and that this defect can only be partially compensated for in BAT and WAT by up-regulation of PGC-1alpha. However, the decrease in mRNA expression of ETC components is not necessarily seen at the protein level. These data suggest that although PGC-1beta may be a controller of mitochondrial gene expression, in the absence of PGC-1beta, additional factors such as protein degradation may be counter-regulated in specific tissues in an attempt to normalise levels of mitochondrial activity.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 119 124 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 206 211 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 894 898 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Our initial hypothesis was that dysregulation of ETC gene expression would increase the likelihood that the PGC1betaKO mouse would become obese. However, despite its mitochondrial phenotype, the PGC1betaKO mouse showed elevated resting metabolic rate and lower body weight compared to wild type littermates under ambient room temperature conditions. This phenotype is possibly due to the compensatory increase in expression of PGC-1alpha and its target genes in BAT. The increase in BAT PGC-1alpha expression may cause the higher degree of energy expenditure and relatively conserved mitochondria volume fraction despite reductions in ETC gene and protein expression. PGC-1alpha was also up-regulated in PGC-1beta-deficient WAT, but its induction was not robust enough to produce a BAT-like histology or induction of typical BAT genes. Instead, within the reduced WAT content of the PGC1betaKO mice, there was a greater proportion of larger, hypertrophic adipocytes. This result suggests that PGC-1beta may play an as yet unknown role in white adipocyte biology, and the nature of this role requires further investigation.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 337 341 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 921 925 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Of interest, despite marked elevation of PGC-1alpha levels in PGC1betaKO BAT, we did not observe a full restoration of the expression of mitochondrial genes back to WT levels. However, unlike the heart or skeletal muscle, organs where PGC-1alpha was not up-regulated, the mitochondrial fraction of BAT tissue was preserved in PGC1betaKO mice. Thus, there may be partial functional overlap between PGC-1alpha and PGC-1beta, concerning mitochondrial structure and function, which allows the development of an appropriate mitochondrial BAT content but fails to correct deficiencies in ETC composition. BAT was the only tissue out of three metabolically relevant tissues examined using electron microscopy that displayed relatively normal mitochondrial fraction, suggesting that further tissue function-specific mechanisms may be able to correct for PGC-1beta ablation. However, the BAT gene expression pattern in PGC1betaKO mice also showed altered expression of genes involved in intermediary metabolism, a pattern of gene expression that may also be primarily the result of PGC-1alpha up-regulation. Our conclusion from these gene expression analyses is that whereas PGC-1beta may be required to set the basal level of mitochondrial ETC gene expression, it is not essential for normal expression of metabolic pathways such as glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, and fatty acid oxidation.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 1077 1084 1023 1030 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pbio-0040369-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 103 107 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 580 585 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 644 649 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 692 696 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 930 935 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 986 990 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1223 1227 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1554 1558 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1758 1763 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
We hypothesized that the elevated resting metabolic rate seen in ambient-temperature housed PGC1betaKO mice may be reversed under conditions of thermoneutrality, particularly if it was caused by compensatory up-regulation of PGC-1alpha. Under thermoneutral conditions, levels of PGC-1alpha gene expression are suppressed in BAT in response to the lower thermogenic and therefore oxidative demands of life at 30 degreesC. At thermoneutral conditions, PGC-1alpha mRNA expression was equivalent in WT and PGC1betaKO BAT. Interestingly, under thermoneutral conditions, the PGC1betaKO mouse exhibited reduced basal metabolic rate compared to the WT mouse. The energy expenditure measurements with mice acclimatised to 4 degreesC and 30 degreesC were performed at 33 degreesC. This measurement at thermoneutrality gives an indication of the basal metabolic rate. Under these experimental conditions, we can conclude that the PGC1betaKO mouse has a reduced basal metabolic rate. The data from mice housed in ambient (22 degreesC) conditions were also generated at ambient conditions (Table 1). Therefore, this measurement represents energy expenditure in "normal" environmental conditions, when the body weights of the PGC1betaKO mice are lower than WT. Under these experimental conditions, this measurement is composed of both basal metabolic rate and any additional metabolic effort required to maintain body temperature in this suboptimal thermal environment. Given that basal metabolic rate is reduced in 30 degreesC- and 4 degreesC-acclimatised PGC1betaKO mice, it could be assumed that at ambient temperatures, basal metabolic rate is also reduced, but that the metabolic adaptation required for body temperature defense is actually greater in the PGC1betaKO mouse. This suggests that PGC-1beta may contribute to BAT thermogenesis in states of low metabolic demand (as is the case for BAT at 30 degreesC), whereas induction of PGC-1alpha regulates BAT thermogenesis as environmental temperatures fall.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 105 106 102 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b006">6</xref>
###xml 750 752 730 732 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b022">22</xref>
###xml 753 755 733 735 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b024">24</xref>
###xml 120 124 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 214 218 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 416 420 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 658 662 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 857 861 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
It has been suggested that PGC-1beta plays a role in BAT accumulation and function during cold exposure [6]. PGC1betaKO mice tolerated cold acclimatisation, despite the fact that under these conditions, PGC1betaKO mice had decreased ETC gene expression and had decreased maximal thermogenic capacity in BAT, as shown by the lower oxygen consumption rate in response to adrenergic stimulation. Both WT and PGC1betaKO mice up-regulated PGC-1alpha to a similar extent under conditions of cold exposure, suggesting that there is a maximal capacity of PGC-1alpha up-regulation in BAT. Alternatively, survival in cold may also be facilitated by the ability of the mice to maintain some fraction of increased metabolic activity through continued shivering [22-24]. Therefore, although the total metabolic capacity provided by PGC-1alpha in cold-exposed PGC1betaKO mice was sufficient for their survival, our results indicate that PGC-1beta regulates a large fraction of the thermogenic capacity of the BAT tissue.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 239 243 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 314 318 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 470 474 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Studies focused in other metabolically relevant organs where PGC-1beta is well expressed also indicated that ablation of PGC-1beta resulted in impaired metabolic capacity. For example, mitochondrial volume fraction in soleus of PGC1betaKO mice was decreased, and permeabilised soleus muscle fibres from PGC1betaKO mice had reduced oxygen consumption and ATP synthesis compared to WT-derived fibres. Similar experiments using isolated soleus mitochondria from PGC1betaKO mice failed to show any abnormalities in metabolic performance, indicating that metabolic defects in PGC1betaKO soleus tissue relate to lower mitochondrial density relative to WT, although individual mitochondria retain similar metabolic properties.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 831 833 809 811 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b014">14</xref>
###xml 290 294 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 651 655 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 735 739 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 825 829 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The heart is the third oxidative tissue that is a major site of PGC-1beta expression. Absence of PGC-1beta in heart also led to a reduction of mitochondrial fraction, consistent with reduced cardiac expression of ETC genes. However, despite these potential bioenergetic defects, PGC1betaKO mice have normal basal heart rates and ventricular contractility. Left ventricular function was also maintained after dobutamine treatment, suggesting that despite their mitochondrial defect, the PGC-1beta-deficient hearts are able to adapt normally to imposed hemodynamic loads. The only difference in adrenergic stress that we observed between the PGC1betaKO mice and WT was a blunting of the expected increase in heart rate in the PGC1betaKO mice. A similar defect in heart rate regulation was also found in the PGC-1alpha knockout mice [14], suggesting that both PGC-1alpha and PGC-1beta may have a direct effect in controlling the activity of heart pacemakers. Overall, our results indicate that ablation of PGC-1beta impairs heart mitochondrial function but that this defect is not severe enough to induce heart failure. However, a more chronic intervention study would be required to establish the relative importance of these PGC isoforms in cardiac energy supply and control.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 186 188 180 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b011">11</xref>
###xml 189 191 183 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b017">17</xref>
###xml 336 339 327 330 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FAS</italic>
###xml 344 351 335 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">SREBP1c</italic>
###xml 989 991 971 973 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b017">17</xref>
###xml 313 317 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 400 404 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 775 779 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To this point, we have discussed exclusively a role for PGC-1beta in oxidative metabolism. Nonetheless, PGC-1beta may also play an important role in regulating hepatic lipid production [11,17]. Contrary to previous reports, we did not observe either PGC-1beta induction after 24-h HFD in the liver of our control mice or differences in FAS and SREBP1c gene expression between controls and PGC1betaKO mice. However, we did observe a reduction in circulating total cholesterol and alterations in the lipoprotein-associated cholesterol pattern in chow-fed conditions. Feeding for 24 h with a saturated fat-enriched HFD was associated with severe hepatic lipid accumulation and decreased total triglycerides, cholesterol, and VLDL and LDL-cholesterol plasma levels in PGC1betaKO mice compared to WT. These results agree with a recent report suggesting that PGC-1beta is a likely mediator of VLDL secretion by acting via interactions with Foxa2 to alter microsomal transfer protein expression [17]. However, it is also possible that PGC-1beta is necessary for additional aspects of hepatic lipid handling, including modification of lipid storage pathways and for controlling the balance between fatty acid synthesis and oxidation. Indeed, our pathway analysis suggests that the ETC in liver is expressed at a lower level and this may contribute to the steatosis after acute HFD treatment.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 628 633 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Our results indicate that PGC-1beta has a well-defined role in controlling mitochondrial gene expression and function in many different organs. Despite this, PGC-1beta can be ablated without overt metabolic failure, at least in unstressed conditions. This may be due in part to robust compensatory mechanisms, as demonstrated by the up-regulation of PGC-1alpha expression in WAT and BAT. Interestingly, other organs such as liver, muscle, or heart did not show up-regulation of PGC-1alpha under the conditions investigated. Following from these differences in PGC-1alpha expression, it is likely that the lean phenotype of this mouse model at ambient temperature should be considered the result of overcompensation mediated by up-regulation of PGC-1alpha, at least in BAT and WAT. Conversely, defects observed in skeletal muscle, heart, and liver are more likely to be the result of the absence of PGC-1beta given the lack of PGC-1alpha induction in those tissues. When considering the roles played by PGC-1alpha and PGC-1beta, our results show that there are specific effects of PGC-1beta that cannot be compensated for by PGC-1alpha. Taken altogether, our results indicate that PGC-1beta seems to cover basal bioenergetic needs whereas PGC-1alpha provides the extra bioenergetic support required under conditions of increased energy demand.

###begin p 53

###xml 672 674 669 671 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pbio-0040369-b025">25</xref>
###xml 989 997 983 991 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pbio-0040369-g001">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 1488 1499 1473 1484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pbio-0040369-sd003">Protocol S1</xref>
###xml 83 87 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 201 206 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 638 642 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 676 680 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 728 732 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1027 1031 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1067 1071 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1267 1271 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1273 1277 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
A triple LoxP strategy was used to target the PGC-1beta locus in order to generate mice with both standard and conditional KO alleles at this locus. The targeting vector was a ~8 kilobase (kb) 129/SvJ mouse genomic subclone containing a floxed neomycin phosphotransferase selectable marker cassette inserted into intron 3 and a single LoxP site inserted into intron 5 (Figure 1A). The targeting construct was electroporated into R1 ES cells, and neomycin-resistant clones were selected in G-418-containing media. The clone was injected into C57Bl/6 blastocysts and chimeric males were crossed to C57Bl/6 females. Heterozygous triple LoxP mice were then bred to ROSA26Cre [25] mice in order to generate heterozygous PGC-1beta KO mice which had undergone Cre-mediated deletion of the intervening region of DNA between the outermost LoxP sites. PCR analysis gave a product of approximately 0.5 kb for the Cre-recombined allele and products of approximately 2.8 and 0.6 kb with the WT allele (Figure 1D). Heterozygous PGC-1beta KO mice were then intercrossed to generate mice that were homozygous for the PGC-1beta deletion. Null mutants were verified using RNA prepared from heart and skeletal muscle to demonstrate that exons 4 and 5 had been deleted in the PGC1betaKO mice. Mice used for this research were backcrossed between three and six times to a C57BL6/J background. Littermate controls were used for all experiments. A more complete description for this section can be found in the Protocol S1.

###begin title 56
Acute dietary measurements and interventions.
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 238 242 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>

###xml 970 974 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1263 1267 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To examine 48-h food intake, cages (23 x 16 cm) were prepared with normal chow and incubated at 80 degreesC for 1 h to correct for any differences in humidity. After 2 h at room temperature, the cages were accurately weighed. 12-h-fasted mice were put in preweighed cages with free access to food and water. To assess the effect of 24-h high-fat feeding, 8-wk-old female mice were assigned into two groups: ad libitum normal food or ad libitum Surwit diet (58% of calories derived from fat, predominantly hydrogenated coconut oil; D12331, Research Diets, New Brunswick, New Jersey, United States). ###begin title 61
Cold acclimatisation and BAT activity.
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 11 15 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 632 636 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1518 1522 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
PGC1betaKO mice and WT littermates were exposed to 30 degreesC for 3 wk, placed at 18 degreesC for 1 wk, and then exposed to 4 degreesC for 3 wk or kept at 30 degreesC for the duration of the protocol. For both 30 degreesC and 4 degreesC acclimated animals, resting metabolic rate was measured in awake animals at 30 degreesC. In addition, NE-stimulated (1 mg NE/kg body weight in saline, (-) arterenol bitartrate, intraperitoneally administered) energy expenditure was evaluated in anaesthetized (pentobarbital, 90 mg/kg) animals at 33 degreesC.
