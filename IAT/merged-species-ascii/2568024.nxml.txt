###begin article-title 0
Deconstructing Survivin: comprehensive genetic analysis of Survivin function by conditional knockout in a vertebrate cell line
###end article-title 0

###begin p 3
###xml 196 204 196 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">survivin</italic>
###xml 213 220 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
Survivin is a key cellular protein thought to function in apoptotic regulation, mitotic progression, or possibly both. In this study, we describe the isolation of two conditional knockouts of the survivin gene in chicken DT40 cells. DT40 cells lacking Survivin die in interphase after failing to complete cytokinesis. However, these cells show normal sensitivity to the chemotherapeutic agent etoposide. Expression of Survivin mutants against a null background to reassess the role of several key residues reveals that DT40 cells can grow normally if their sole Survivin is missing a widely studied cyclin-dependent kinase phosphorylation site or sites reportedly essential for binding to Smac or aurora B. Mutations in the nuclear export sequence or dimerization interface render cells temperature sensitive for growth. As an important caveat for other studies in which protein function is studied by transient transfection, three of the Survivin mutants fail to localize in the presence of the wild-type protein but do localize and indeed support life in its absence.

###begin title 11
Introduction
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 174 192 174 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Cooke et al., 1987</xref>
###xml 194 212 194 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib1">Adams et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 214 235 214 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib28">Gassmann et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 237 257 237 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib65">Ruchaud et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 387 407 387 407 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib65">Ruchaud et al., 2007</xref>
The chromosomal passenger protein complex (CPC), a key regulator of mitosis consisting of aurora B kinase, inner centromere protein (INCENP), Survivin, and Borealin/Dasra B (Cooke et al., 1987; Adams et al., 2000; Gassmann et al., 2004; Ruchaud et al., 2007), is essential for correction of kinetochore attachment errors, completion of cytokinesis, and numerous other mitotic functions (Ruchaud et al., 2007).
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 78 93 78 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib47">Li et al., 1998</xref>
###xml 111 137 111 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib85">Wheatley and McNeish, 2005</xref>
###xml 139 156 139 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib45">Lens et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 188 210 188 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">Chantalat et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 212 233 212 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib53">Muchmore et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 330 350 330 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Bourhis et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 352 376 352 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib35">Jeyaprakash et al., 2007</xref>
Survivin is a cell cycle-regulated protein whose expression peaks in mitosis (Li et al., 1998; for reviews see Wheatley and McNeish, 2005; Lens et al., 2006). Survivin forms both a dimer (Chantalat et al., 2000; Muchmore et al., 2000) and a three-helix bundle with the N terminus of INCENP and the N terminus of Borealin/Dasra B (Bourhis et al., 2007; Jeyaprakash et al., 2007). In the bundle, Survivin is a monomer, with Borealin docking to the surface that forms the interface in Survivin homodimers. The three-helix bundle is essential for CPC targeting and function in mitosis.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 60 81 60 81 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Carvalho et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 83 101 83 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib41">Klein et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 103 122 103 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib42">Knauer et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 124 142 124 142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib80">Vader et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 187 201 187 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus laevis</italic>
###xml 221 240 221 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">Bolton et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 242 266 242 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib58">Petersen and Hagan, 2003</xref>
###xml 304 322 304 322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib34">Honda et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 418 439 418 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Carvalho et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 441 458 441 458 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib44">Lens et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 460 484 460 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib58">Petersen and Hagan, 2003</xref>
###xml 187 201 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus laevis</span>
###xml 206 219 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 350 355 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Survivin helps mediate the mitotic localization of the CPC (Carvalho et al., 2003; Klein et al., 2006; Knauer et al., 2006; Vader et al., 2006) and may contribute to aurora B activity in Xenopus laevis and fission yeast (Bolton et al., 2002; Petersen and Hagan, 2003), although this has been challenged (Honda et al., 2003). Survivin and its budding yeast homologue Bir-1 are required for spindle checkpoint function (Carvalho et al., 2003; Lens et al., 2003; Petersen and Hagan, 2003). However, the exact role of Survivin in mitosis remains controversial.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 167 182 167 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib47">Li et al., 1998</xref>
###xml 200 226 200 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib85">Wheatley and McNeish, 2005</xref>
###xml 228 241 228 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib3">Altieri, 2006</xref>
###xml 377 395 377 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib21">Crook et al., 1993</xref>
###xml 397 419 397 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Ambrosini et al., 1997</xref>
Survivin is an inhibitor of apoptosis protein (IAP) with a single baculovirus IAP repeat (BIR) domain and has been proposed to link cell proliferation and cell death (Li et al., 1998; for reviews see Wheatley and McNeish, 2005; Altieri, 2006). Unlike IAPs involved in apoptosis control, Survivin lacks a C-terminal RING finger and contains only one BIR domain (residues 18-88; Crook et al., 1993; Ambrosini et al., 1997).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 42 64 42 64 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib4">Ambrosini et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 66 74 66 74 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib46">Li, 2003</xref>
###xml 287 302 287 302 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib47">Li et al., 1998</xref>
###xml 304 324 304 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib50">Mahotka et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 326 344 326 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib36">Jiang et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 346 364 346 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib52">Mirza et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 366 387 366 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Carvalho et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 389 407 389 407 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib77">Temme et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 409 430 409 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib7">Beltrami et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 432 449 432 449 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib71">Song et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 494 511 494 511 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib76">Tamm et al., 1998</xref>
###xml 513 528 513 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib48">Li et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 530 549 530 549 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib19">Conway et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 551 568 551 568 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib68">Shin et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 615 633 615 633 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib6">Banks et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 659 684 659 684 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</italic>
###xml 686 703 686 703 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib78">Uren et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 705 742 705 742 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib60">Rajagopalan and Balasubramanian, 2002</xref>
###xml 745 767 745 767 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Caenorhabditis elegans</italic>
###xml 769 788 769 788 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">Fraser et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 790 812 790 812 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib72">Speliotes et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 815 829 815 829 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Xenopus laevis</italic>
###xml 831 850 831 850 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib8">Bolton et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 863 880 863 880 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib79">Uren et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 928 947 928 947 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib83">Walter et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 959 982 959 982 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 1048 1066 1048 1066 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib37">Jones et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 1129 1147 1129 1147 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib55">Okada et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 659 684 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</span>
###xml 745 767 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
###xml 815 829 <span type="species:ncbi:8355">Xenopus laevis</span>
###xml 857 861 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 959 982 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 1073 1079 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
Survivin is overexpressed in many tumors (Ambrosini et al., 1997; Li, 2003), and cells overexpressing the protein are resistant to many apoptotic stimuli. Conversely, loss of Survivin expression or function can cause spontaneous apoptosis or sensitize cancer cells to apoptotic stimuli (Li et al., 1998; Mahotka et al., 1999; Jiang et al., 2001; Mirza et al., 2002; Carvalho et al., 2003; Temme et al., 2003; Beltrami et al., 2004; Song et al., 2004). Survivin may regulate caspase-3 activity (Tamm et al., 1998; Li et al., 1999; Conway et al., 2000; Shin et al., 2001), but it does not inhibit caspase-3 directly (Banks et al., 2000). Survivin homologues in Schizosaccharomyces pombe (Uren et al., 1999; Rajagopalan and Balasubramanian, 2002), Caenorhabditis elegans (Fraser et al., 1999; Speliotes et al., 2000), Xenopus laevis (Bolton et al., 2002), and mice (Uren et al., 2000) lack obvious antiapoptotic functions (but see Walter et al., 2006). However, Drosophila melanogaster deterin can exhibit antiapoptotic activity in transfected cells (Jones et al., 2000), and murine Survivin is essential for thymocyte development (Okada et al., 2004).
###end p 16
###begin p 17
The role of Survivin in mitosis and apoptosis remains unclear, possibly because Survivin is studied in numerous cell types under a wide range of experimental conditions and usually in the presence of the wild-type protein. In this study, we describe a conditional knockout of Survivin in DT40 cells. Our results support some of the published conclusions about Survivin function; however, several structural features previously reported to be essential for Survivin function turn out to be nonessential for cell viability when examined against a null background.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Results
###end title 18
###begin title 19
Isolation of Survivin conditional knockout cells
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 42 50 42 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">survivin</italic>
###xml 82 90 82 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 A</xref>
###xml 92 119 92 119 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib12">Buerstedde and Takeda, 2006</xref>
###xml 378 386 378 386 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">survivin</italic>
###xml 539 547 539 547 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 C</xref>
###xml 758 766 758 766 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 B</xref>
###xml 54 61 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 728 735 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
We deleted the entire 725-bp ORF encoding survivin in chicken DT40 B lymphocytes (Fig. 1 A; Buerstedde and Takeda, 2006). Two knockouts were isolated. The first wild-type allele was replaced with a neomycin (KO1) or histidinol (KO2) selectable marker. Heterozygotes were cotransfected with two constructs, one encoding the tetracycline transactivator (tTA) plus a second with a survivin cDNA under control of a tTA-responsive promoter (tetO). The remaining allele was replaced with a histidinol (KO1) or puromycin (KO2) selectable marker (Fig. 1 C). The two knockouts differed in the control of the tTA transcription factor, which was under the control of the strong cytomegalovirus promoter in KO1 and the much weaker cellular chicken KIF4 promoter in KO2 (Fig. 1 B). Addition of doxycycline blocks tTA binding to the promoter driving the rescue cDNA, resulting in the shutoff of wild-type Survivin expression.
###end p 20

Specific targeting events were confirmed by Southern blotting using EcoRI digestion and a 5' external probe (Fig. 1, A and D). For KO1, the probe recognized a 4.1-kb band corresponding to the wild-type survivin allele and a 4.9-kb or 6-kb band after targeted integration of the histidinolres or neomycinres constructs, respectively. For KO2, the correctly targeted alleles gave bands of 4.9 (histidinolres) and 4.8 kb (puromycinres).
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 131 139 131 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">survivin</italic>
###xml 251 259 251 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">survivin</italic>
###xml 338 353 338 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1, E and F</xref>
Conventional and quantitative RT-PCR confirmed that doxycycline addition caused a rapid and dramatic decrease in expression of the survivin rescue cDNA, which could be monitored by RT-PCR, as it carries a 36-bp deletion in the 3' untranslated region. survivin mRNA levels fell by 75% at 4 h after doxycycline addition and by 99% at 24 h (Fig. 1, E and F).
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 132 140 132 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 H</xref>
###xml 206 214 206 214 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 G</xref>
###xml 398 406 398 406 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2 C</xref>
###xml 826 832 826 832 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2</xref>
###xml 730 737 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
The level of Survivin protein in KO2 cultures growing without doxycycline was similar to or slightly below that in wild-type cells (Fig. 1 H, 0 h), whereas in KO1, Survivin was substantially overexpressed (Fig. 1 G). Remarkably, the growth of KO1 and KO2 cells appeared to be identical; thus, an excess of the canonical isoform of Survivin did not appear to confer a growth advantage on KO1 cells (Fig. 2 C). Doxycycline addition caused a rapid drop in Survivin levels, which became essentially undetectable by 36 h in KO2. Loss of Survivin was more variable for KO1, and in some experiments cells died before the protein was completely lost from cultures. Indirect immunofluorescence staining of KO2 cells using antibody against chicken Survivin showed no detectable Survivin signal during mitosis after 60 h in doxycycline (Fig. 2, A'' and A''').

Survivin is essential for life in DT40 cells
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 8 10 8 10 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ON</sup>

###xml 48 51 48 51 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>

Live cell imaging revealed that in many SurvivinOFF mitoses, the chromosomes appeared to separate but cytokinesis failed, giving rise to binucleated progeny (Fig. 3 C, and Videos 3 and 4, available at ). ###begin title 36
###xml 8 11 8 11 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
SurvivinOFF cells die after completing mitosis and failing cytokinesis
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 21 24 21 24 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 206 235 206 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib29">Gillespie and Henriques, 2006</xref>
###xml 246 249 246 249 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 484 492 484 492 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 D</xref>
###xml 587 595 587 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 E</xref>
The death of SurvivinOFF cells was linked to the cell cycle, as shown in experiments using centrifugal elutriation, a noninvasive selection synchrony procedure that does not perturb cell cycle progression (Gillespie and Henriques, 2006). SurvivinOFF cells were elutriated after incubation in doxycycline for 36 h, at which time Survivin protein had fallen to undetectable levels. Fractions of cells synchronized in G1 (fraction 2), S (fraction 3), and G2/M phases (fractions 4 and 5; Fig. 3 D) were harvested and cultured, and apoptotic cells were scored every 2 h by visual inspection (Fig. 3 E) and annexin V and propidium iodide staining (not depicted).
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 1 2 1 2 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 146 147 146 147 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 165 166 165 166 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 271 279 271 279 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 F</xref>
G1- and S-phase cells (fractions 2 and 3) remained healthy for 10-15 h after synchronization, by which time many had passed through a subsequent G2/M. In contrast, G2/M cells (fractions 4 and 5) died much more quickly. Most cells had become binucleated before they died (Fig. 3 F), suggesting that Survivin-depleted cells died in interphase after failing in cytokinesis.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 46 61 46 61 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Fig. 4, A and B</xref>
###xml 418 426 418 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Fig. 4 B</xref>
###xml 593 596 591 594 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
Live cell analysis confirmed this hypothesis (Fig. 4, A and B; and Videos 6 and 7, available at ). In one study, 46% (19/41) of elutriated cells from fraction 2 failed in cytokinesis and 12% died in interphase. A further 27% were binucleate at the start of the video and died before reentering mitosis. In another study, 29 cells entered anaphase during filming. Of these, 15 died in interphase after exiting mitosis (Fig. 4 B and Video 7). The mean interval between anaphase onset and death was 276 +/- 114 min (range of 40-450 min). Given the 12-h cell cycle time for DT40, death of SurvivinOFF cells likely occurs in late G1 or S phase. This cell death was not delayed by the pancaspase inhibitor Z-VAD (unpublished data) and therefore was not caused by disregulation of caspases.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
###xml 39 42 39 42 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
Spindle checkpoint function in SurvivinOFF cells
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 33 54 33 54 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Carvalho et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 56 73 56 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib44">Lens et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 84 87 84 87 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 159 167 158 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Fig. 4 C</xref>
###xml 274 277 273 276 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 379 394 378 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Fig. 4, C and D</xref>
###xml 452 455 449 452 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
As expected from published work (Carvalho et al., 2003; Lens et al., 2003), SurvivinOFF DT40 cells exhibited a normal mitotic arrest in 0.5 mug/ml nocodazole (Fig. 4 C). Their response to taxol was more complex, however. At taxol concentrations between 5 and 50 nM, SurvivinOFF cells showed a significantly decreased mitotic index, indicative of a defective checkpoint response (Fig. 4, C and D). However, at taxol concentrations of >/=100 nM, SurvivinOFF cells experienced a normal mitotic arrest.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 72 75 72 75 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 129 132 129 132 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 143 151 143 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Fig. 4 E</xref>
###xml 268 271 268 271 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 394 397 394 397 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 638 641 638 641 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
FACS analysis showed a 15% increase in TUNEL-positive apoptotic SurvivinOFF cells at 10 nM taxol compared with untreated SurvivinOFF controls (Fig. 4 E). This increase was not seen at high doses of taxol (200 nM) or with nocodazole. This could be explained if SurvivinOFF cells override the checkpoint at low doses of taxol, exit mitosis, and are more susceptible to die in interphase. SurvivinOFF cells exposed to high doses of taxol and blocked in mitosis might be more protected from dying during the time course of the experiment. These data further support our hypothesis that exit from an aberrant mitosis leads to death of SurvivinOFF cells during interphase.
###end p 42
###begin title 43
###xml 8 11 8 11 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>

###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 152 174 152 174 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib5">Ambrosini et al., 1998</xref>

Many published studies report that down-regulation of Survivin by RNAi or antisense oligonucleotides can enhance spontaneous or drug-induced apoptosis (Ambrosini et al., 1998; Li et al., 1999; Jiang et al., 2001; Pennati et al., 2002; Ling and Li, 2004; Pennati et al., 2004; Wang et al., 2005; Zaffaroni et al., 2007). Furthermore, a wide range of studies showed that Survivin overexpression can have a cytoprotective effect (for reviews see Wheatley and McNeish, 2005; Altieri, 2006).
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 163 166 163 166 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 439 447 439 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Fig. 4 F</xref>
###xml 580 582 580 582 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ON</sup>
###xml 645 647 645 647 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ON</sup>
###xml 693 708 693 708 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1, G and H</xref>
These effects on apoptosis are not universal, as loss of Survivin from DT40 cells had no effect on their response to a well-defined proapoptotic stimulus. SurvivinOFF cells were incubated with etoposide for 3 h after exposure to doxycycline for 30 or 36 h to repress Survivin. When cells were collected and analyzed by TUNEL staining, we observed no significant difference in levels of apoptosis between cultures with or without Survivin (Fig. 4 F). Likewise, Survivin overexpression did not appear to provide a protective effect against etoposide-induced cell death. KO1-SurvivinON cells significantly overexpress Survivin, whereas KO2-SurvivinON cells express Survivin at endogenous levels (Fig. 1, G and H, compare 0 h with wild type), yet both cell lines undergo a similar response to etoposide.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 171 192 171 192 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib38">Kawamura et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 212 215 212 215 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 318 320 318 320 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ON</sup>
###xml 69 74 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Exposure to staurosporine can significantly increase the fraction of mouse embryos positive for activated caspase-3 after exposure to antisense survivin oligonucleotides (Kawamura et al., 2003). However, SurvivinOFF DT40 cells pretreated with doxycycline for 30 h were not more sensitive to staurosporine than SurvivinON or wild-type DT40 cells (unpublished data). Thus, with the exception of low doses of taxol, the loss of Survivin does not change the sensitivity of DT40 cells to drugs that induce the intrinsic apoptotic response.

###end title 51
###begin p 52

###begin title 55
Cdk phosphorylation on T34 is dispensable for Survivin function
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 57 78 57 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib54">O'Connor et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 125 129 125 129 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T34A</sup>
###xml 243 264 243 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib32">Grossman et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 266 282 266 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib86">Yan et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 413 417 413 417 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T34A</sup>
###xml 491 508 491 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib82">Wall et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 179 184 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Survivin can be phosphorylated on T34 by Cdk1-cyclin B1 (O'Connor et al., 2000). Expression of the nonphosphorylated SurvivinT34A mutant produced a dominant-negative phenotype in human cancer cells, resulting in caspase-9-dependent apoptosis (Grossman et al., 2001; Yan et al., 2006). Treatment of cells with the CDK inhibitor flavopiridol resulted in a decrease in Survivin levels, and nonphosphorylated SurvivinT34A reportedly exhibited decreased stability relative to wild-type Survivin (Wall et al., 2003).
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 24 28 24 28 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T36A</sup>
###xml 45 49 45 49 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">T36E</sup>
###xml 130 145 130 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Fig. 5, F and G</xref>
###xml 254 262 254 262 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Fig. 5 I</xref>
###xml 384 392 384 392 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Fig. 5 H</xref>
###xml 498 506 498 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Fig. 5 G</xref>
###xml 0 7 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">Chicken</span>
Chicken mutants SurvivinT36A-GFP and SurvivinT36E-GFP (equivalent to hSurvivin T34A and T34E) were stably expressed in KO1 cells (Fig. 5, F and G). Both mutant Survivins localize correctly on centromeres at metaphase and the spindle midzone in anaphase (Fig. 5 I and Fig. S3 B, available at ). Surprisingly, cells expressing both mutants grew normally in the presence of doxycycline (Fig. 5 H) even though the tet-regulated wild-type rescue Survivin was entirely undetectable at any blot exposure (Fig. 5 G). Thus, contrary to prediction, CDK phosphorylation of T36 is dispensable for Survivin function in DT40 cells.

###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 144 148 144 148 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D55A</sup>

###xml 450 454 450 454 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D55A</sup>
###xml 548 556 548 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Fig. 7 D</xref>
###xml 128 135 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>

These cells grew normally in the absence of doxycycline and continued to grow, after a lag, in its presence with SurvivinD55A-GFP as the only form of Survivin detectable in the cells (Fig. 7 C). SurvivinD55A-GFP localized properly to centromeres, the central spindle and the midbody in mitotic cells (Fig. 7 D). Thus, residue D55 (and presumably interaction with Smac) is not essential for Survivin function in DT40 cells.

###begin title 62
Asp residues required for interaction with aurora B are required for Survivin targeting to centromeres
###end title 62
###begin p 63
###xml 186 202 186 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Cao et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 262 271 262 271 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
###xml 273 281 273 281 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Fig. 7 E</xref>
###xml 296 302 296 302 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ON/OFF</sup>
In addition to interfering with putative antiapoptotic functions, hSurvivin mutant D70A/D71A was reported to no longer interact with aurora B and to cause multinucleation of HeLa cells (Cao et al., 2006). Therefore, we introduced the analogous mutation (SurvivinD72A/D73A; Fig. 7 E) into SurvivinON/OFF cells. This Survivin mutant revealed unexpected aspects of CPC behavior in early mitosis.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 38 47 38 47 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
###xml 113 121 113 121 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Fig. 7 F</xref>
###xml 236 253 236 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Fig. 7 G, a and b</xref>
###xml 377 380 377 380 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 406 415 406 415 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
###xml 555 570 555 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Fig. 7, F and H</xref>
###xml 660 665 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Immunoblotting confirmed that SurvivinD72A/D73A-GFP was expressed at relatively high levels in these cell lines (Fig. 7 F); however, it was unable to localize to centromeres or the spindle midzone in the presence of wild-type Survivin (Fig. 7 G, a and b). These cells grew with normal kinetics, so this mutant did not exhibit a dominant-negative phenotype. Strikingly, SurvivinOFF cells expressing SurvivinD72A/D73A-GFP continued to grow with normal kinetics in the presence of doxycycline, even though the wild-type rescue Survivin was now undetectable (Fig. 7, F and H). Therefore, the antiapoptotic and aurora B-binding role attributed to these residues in human Survivin is dispensable for Survivin function in DT40 cells.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 67 76 67 76 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
###xml 115 118 115 118 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 146 155 146 155 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
###xml 244 255 244 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Fig. 7 G, c</xref>
###xml 380 391 380 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Fig. 7 G, d</xref>
###xml 405 408 405 408 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 602 610 602 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Fig. 7 G</xref>
An unexpected result was obtained when the localization of SurvivinD72A/D73A-GFP was examined in detail in SurvivinOFF cells. Remarkably, SurvivinD72A/D73A-GFP failed to localize to centromeres during prophase through metaphase in these cells (Fig. 7 G, c). However, the protein localized to the anaphase spindle midzone and subsequently concentrated at the midbody in telophase (Fig. 7 G, d). In SurvivinOFF cells, this protein induced an identical behavior in INCENP and aurora B, which were diffuse during prophase through metaphase but localized to the spindle midzone and midbody in late mitosis (Fig. 7 G and Fig. S3).
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 16 25 16 25 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
###xml 205 208 205 208 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
###xml 234 243 234 243 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
Because SurvivinD72A/D73A-GFP was defective in centromere localization during prometaphase, we hypothesized that these cells might exhibit a defective spindle checkpoint response to taxol. Indeed, SurvivinOFF cells expressing SurvivinD72A/D73A-GFP showed a slight but significant checkpoint defect in the presence of low-dose taxol (Fig. S2 C, available at ).
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 30 39 30 39 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
Thus, analysis of the SurvivinD72A/D73A-GFP mutant reveals the surprising fact that INCENP and Survivin concentration at centromeres is not essential for mitotic progression, at least when the proteins subsequently target to the spindle midzone and midbody during anaphase.

###begin title 68

###end title 68
###begin p 69

The linker region (residues 82-102) between the BIR domain and C-terminal alpha helix is a site of molecular contact with Borealin in the CPC (Bourhis et al., 2007; Jeyaprakash et al., 2007) and with Survivin itself in the homodimer (Chantalat et al., 2000; Sun et al., 2005). Human Survivin also contains a nuclear export sequence (NES) that overlaps this region (Colnaghi et al., 2006; Stauber et al., 2006; Engelsma et al., 2007; Knauer et al., 2007). We analyzed the role of this region in Survivin function by mutating four highly conserved residues: L98, V100, L104, and L106. V100, which is conserved in mouse, zebrafish, and Drosophila, corresponds to L98 in hSurvivin.
###end p 69

###xml 771 781 729 739 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L98A/V100A</sup>

We isolated SurvivinON/OFF cells expressing either SurvivinL98A/V100A (equivalent to hSurvivinL96A/L98A) or SurvivinL104A/L106A (equivalent to hSurvivinL102A/L104A) at levels similar to the wild-type Survivin (Fig. 8 B, 39degreesC). Strikingly, neither linker mutant could rescue SurvivinOFF cells at 41degreesC, a temperature at which DT40 cells grow normally. At 41degreesC, SurvivinOFF cells expressing either mutant started dying after 48-h growth in doxycycline, approximately24 h later than SurvivinOFF cells (Fig. 8, E and F). Indeed, if SurvivinOFF:SurvivinL98A/V100A-GFP cells grown at permissive temperature with doxycycline for 1 wk were shifted to 43degreesC, they failed to grow, whereas wild-type DT40 or SurvivinOFF:Survivin-GFP cells grew normally (Fig. S4 C, available at ). We conclude that both Survivin linker mutants are temperature sensitive for function.

###begin title 75
Discussion
###end title 75
###begin p 76
###xml 173 181 173 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">survivin</italic>
###xml 201 238 201 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib59">Rajagopalan and Balasubramanian, 1999</xref>
###xml 240 257 240 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib78">Uren et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 263 273 263 273 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C. elegans</italic>
###xml 275 294 275 294 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">Fraser et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 296 318 296 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib72">Speliotes et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 455 475 455 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib64">Ruchaud et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 193 199 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeasts</span>
###xml 263 273 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
Survivin's role in mitosis and apoptosis remains poorly understood. A bias (>1,500/2,000 PubMed listings) toward studies of apoptosis belies the fact that the phenotypes of survivin mutants in yeasts (Rajagopalan and Balasubramanian, 1999; Uren et al., 1999) and C. elegans (Fraser et al., 1999; Speliotes et al., 2000) indicate a role in mitosis rather than cell death regulation. Indeed, even though DT40 cells have widely been used to study apoptosis (Ruchaud et al., 2006), our experiments show that it is the mitotic and not an antiapoptotic role for Survivin that is essential for life in these cells.
###end p 76
###begin p 77
###xml 127 146 127 146 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib26">Fraser et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 148 163 148 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib48">Li et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 165 187 165 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib72">Speliotes et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 189 210 189 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Carvalho et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 212 229 212 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib44">Lens et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 613 630 613 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib79">Uren et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 110 115 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 492 497 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 656 661 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Studies of Survivin in vertebrates have used RNAi, antisense oligonucleotides, dominant-negative mutants, and mouse knockouts (Fraser et al., 1999; Li et al., 1999; Speliotes et al., 2000; Carvalho et al., 2003; Lens et al., 2003). Of these, the first three are subject to the caveat that the elimination of Survivin is never complete, and because aurora B is a kinase, even low levels of Survivin could in principle support significant amounts of kinase activity. Phenotypic analysis of the mouse knockouts is challenging, as Survivin is essential for embryonic life, with death occurring by 4.5 d after coitum (Uren et al., 2000). As yet, no conditional mouse mutant for Survivin has been made.
###end p 77
###begin p 78
###xml 520 542 520 542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib67">Scrittori et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 600 610 600 610 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L98A/V100A</sup>
###xml 620 631 620 631 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L104A/L106A</sup>
###xml 645 654 645 654 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
###xml 72 79 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
In this study, we characterize two conditional knockouts of Survivin in chicken DT40 cells. A principal advantage of this system is that after shutoff of the regulated Survivin cDNA responsible for keeping cells alive, levels of the wild-type protein and its mRNA become essentially undetectable in the culture. This is significant, as a previous RNAi study of aurora kinase localization produced very different results depending on whether or not the endogenous protein was present when various mutants were expressed (Scrittori et al., 2005). We obtained similar results for three mutants, SurvivinL98A/V100A, SurvivinL104A/L106A, and SurvivinD72A/D73A, which fail to localize in the presence of wild-type Survivin but do so, at least partially (and are functional), in its absence. Thus, the null background was essential for study of these features of the Survivin molecule.
###end p 78
###begin p 79
###xml 296 313 296 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib87">Yang et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 315 332 315 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib33">Guse et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 334 350 334 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib89">Zhu et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 352 370 352 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib41">Klein et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 381 401 381 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib22">Delaval et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 410 437 410 437 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib16">Chalamalasetty et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 446 463 446 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib31">Goto et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 465 485 465 485 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib13">Burkard et al., 2007</xref>
Our experiments confirm several widely held beliefs about Survivin but contradict others. For example, Survivin is essential for the completion of cytokinesis. Survivin function is apparently linked with that of the CPC, and the cytokinesis phenotype could reflect aberrant regulation of MKLP-1 (Yang et al., 2004; Guse et al., 2005; Zhu et al., 2005; Klein et al., 2006), taxins (Delaval et al., 2004), Ect2 (Chalamalasetty et al., 2006), PLK1 (Goto et al., 2006; Burkard et al., 2007), or other aurora B substrates.
###end p 79
###begin p 80
###xml 208 226 208 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib2">Adams et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 228 249 228 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib15">Carvalho et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 251 268 251 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib44">Lens et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 270 291 270 291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib28">Gassmann et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 399 416 399 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib63">Rosa et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 507 527 507 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib30">Giodini et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 529 547 529 547 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib55">Okada et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 549 566 549 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib63">Rosa et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 647 650 647 650 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">OFF</sup>
Cells lacking Survivin execute the earlier stages of mitosis through metaphase chromosome alignment without obvious difficulties. This contrasts with previous RNAi analyses of Survivin and other CPC members (Adams et al., 2001; Carvalho et al., 2003; Lens et al., 2003; Gassmann et al., 2004). A recent RNAi study also found that Survivin was not required for chromosome movements in early mitosis (Rosa et al., 2006); however, that and other studies described abnormal spindles in Survivin-depleted cells (Giodini et al., 2002; Okada et al., 2004; Rosa et al., 2006). In contrast, we observed no gross spindle abnormalities in mitosis of SurvivinOFF cells.
###end p 80
###begin p 81
###xml 669 686 657 674 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib87">Yang et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 603 608 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Although cell death after loss of Survivin is clearly linked to the cell cycle, this death occurs in interphase, not during mitosis or mitotic exit. Using centrifugal elutriation, a nonperturbing method for selecting synchronized cell populations, we found that cells harvested later in the cell cycle die significantly before those harvested earlier. Indeed, our live cell analysis indicates that cells die during midinterphase on mean approximately5 h after mitotic exit. Considering the 12-h division cycle of DT40 cells, this may correspond to a point in G1/S analogous to that at which some normal human cells arrest in a p53-dependent manner after Survivin RNAi (Yang et al., 2004). DT40 cells lack functional p53, and the absence of a checkpoint could explain why they die. At present, no CPC-dependent functions have been described during interphase, so this forms an intriguing point for future study.
###end p 81
###begin p 82
###xml 337 355 337 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib23">Derry et al., 1995</xref>
###xml 357 377 357 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib39">Kelling et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 872 894 872 894 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib10">Brito and Rieder, 2008</xref>
Although Survivin is essential for a spindle checkpoint response to low-dose taxol, surprisingly, higher doses of taxol did cause a tight mitotic arrest in cells lacking Survivin. The effects of taxol on microtubule plus end dynamics are not simple. As the taxol concentration increases, the plus ends become progressively less dynamic (Derry et al., 1995; Kelling et al., 2003). One possible explanation for our results, suggested to us by M.A. Jordan, is that Survivin might be required to impose a spindle checkpoint when microtubule plus ends are minimally dynamic but may no longer be required when those dynamics are completely suppressed (unpublished data; Jordan, M.A., personal communication). Alternatively, it may be that in low-dose taxol, the spindle checkpoint can eventually be silenced, perhaps transiently, even though microtubule dynamics are perturbed (Brito and Rieder, 2008). Survivin could promote checkpoint function by somehow decreasing the probability of transient checkpoint inactivation. Whatever the explanation, it is clear that one cannot simply generalize that cells lacking Survivin are unresponsive to taxol.
###end p 82
###begin p 83
###xml 78 84 78 84 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ON/OFF</sup>
###xml 356 364 356 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig9" ref-type="fig">Fig. 9 C</xref>
###xml 7 14 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 18 23 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Either chicken or human Survivin fused to GFP can support the life of SurvivinON/OFF cells for extended periods in culture after shutoff of the wild-type rescue cDNA. Thus, this system is well suited to test the functionality and phenotype of various mutant forms of Survivin in the absence of detectable levels of wild-type protein (mutants summarized in Fig. 9 C).
###end p 83
###begin p 84
###xml 225 240 225 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib48">Li et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 392 421 392 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib73">Srinivasula and Ashwell, 2008</xref>
###xml 475 497 475 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib17">Chantalat et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 546 566 546 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Bourhis et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 568 592 568 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib35">Jeyaprakash et al., 2007</xref>
Although some Survivin mutants behaved as expected, others clearly did not. For example, perturbation of the Survivin BIR domain renders the protein completely nonfunctional during mitosis. However, contrary to expectations (Li et al., 1999), two Survivin BIR domain mutants failed to exhibit dominant-negative activity. Because the BIR domain probably promotes protein-protein interactions (Srinivasula and Ashwell, 2008) but does not participate either in dimer formation (Chantalat et al., 2000) or in formation of the CPC three-helix bundle (Bourhis et al., 2007; Jeyaprakash et al., 2007), its role in Survivin function remains to be determined.
###end p 84
###begin p 85
###xml 71 92 71 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib54">O'Connor et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 94 115 94 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib32">Grossman et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 117 133 117 133 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib86">Yan et al., 2006</xref>
CDK phosphorylation of Survivin is reportedly essential for cell life (O'Connor et al., 2000; Grossman et al., 2001; Yan et al., 2006). However, DT40 cells whose sole Survivin had its conserved CDK site mutated to either Ala or Glu grew normally and exhibited normal CPC behavior in mitosis. Thus, regulation by CDKs is not essential for Survivin function.
###end p 85
###begin p 86
###xml 135 156 135 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib53">Muchmore et al., 2000</xref>
###xml 158 175 158 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib70">Song et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 177 181 177 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib71">2004</xref>
###xml 183 203 183 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib51">McNeish et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 205 221 205 221 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib75">Sun et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 223 239 223 239 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib40">Kim et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 254 258 254 258 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D55A</sup>
###xml 357 374 357 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib71">Song et al., 2004</xref>
Several studies implicate Smac/Diablo, the mitochondrial antagonist of IAPs, as a binding partner of Survivin in apoptosis regulation (Muchmore et al., 2000; Song et al., 2003, 2004; McNeish et al., 2005; Sun et al., 2005; Kim et al., 2006). The SurvivinD55A mutant was reported to abolish this interaction and to have a significant proapoptotic phenotype (Song et al., 2004). However, contrary to expectations, we find that DT40 cells can live, albeit growing more slowly, with this mutant as the sole Survivin.
###end p 86
###begin p 87
###xml 109 131 109 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib61">Rodriguez et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 133 137 133 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib62">2006</xref>
###xml 139 160 139 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib18">Colnaghi et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 209 228 209 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib42">Knauer et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 332 352 332 352 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib9">Bourhis et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 354 378 354 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib35">Jeyaprakash et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 439 460 439 460 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Engelsma et al., 2007</xref>
Survivin residues 80 and 106 contain an NES enabling hSurvivin to shuttle between the nucleus and cytoplasm (Rodriguez et al., 2002, 2006; Colnaghi et al., 2006) and required for CPC targeting to centromeres (Knauer et al., 2006). This region forms a docking surface for Survivin itself in the homodimer or for Borealin in the CPC (Bourhis et al., 2007; Jeyaprakash et al., 2007). Survivin dimer formation impedes Crm1 binding by the NES (Engelsma et al., 2007), and Survivin may be preferentially exported from the nucleus as a monomer.
###end p 87
###begin p 88
###xml 131 141 131 141 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L98A/V100A</sup>
###xml 153 164 153 164 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L104A/L106A</sup>
###xml 378 391 348 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib69">Simpson, 1912</xref>
###xml 429 439 399 409 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L98A/V100A</sup>
###xml 451 462 421 432 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L104A/L106A</sup>
###xml 573 584 543 554 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F101A/L102A</sup>
###xml 642 663 612 633 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib25">Engelsma et al., 2007</xref>
###xml 720 730 690 700 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L98A/V100A</sup>
###xml 742 753 712 723 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">L104A/L106A</sup>
###xml 327 335 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chickens</span>
###xml 601 608 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
We isolated two mutants of the docking surface that render DT40 cells temperature sensitive for growth. Cells dependent on SurvivinL98A/V100A or SurvivinL104A/L106A grow at 39degreesC or 37degreesC, respectively, but die at 41degreesC. DT40 cells normally grow at 41degreesC, and indeed, the diurnal deep muscle temperature of chickens is 45degreesC during periods of activity (Simpson, 1912). Because cells dependent on SurvivinL98A/V100A or SurvivinL104A/L106A are viable at permissive temperature, these mutants must support CPC formation in vivo. In contrast, hSurvivinF101A/L102A (F103A/L104A in chicken Survivin) is a monomer in vitro (Engelsma et al., 2007). We suggest that at nonpermissive temperature, SurvivinL98A/V100A or SurvivinL104A/L106A mutants may be unable to form either the CPC or homodimer interactions.
###end p 88
###begin p 89
###xml 64 82 64 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib80">Vader et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 321 330 321 330 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
###xml 399 415 399 415 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib14">Cao et al., 2006</xref>
###xml 638 647 638 647 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
###xml 903 912 903 912 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
Survivin may direct the localization of the CPC to centromeres (Vader et al., 2006), but the significance of this localization is unclear. Is centromere localization required for CPC activities in correcting microtubule attachment errors or in the spindle checkpoint response? Remarkably, cells expressing solely SurvivinD72A/D73A, which was reported to block the binding of both Smac and aurora B (Cao et al., 2006), grow normally even though the mutant Survivin is unable to target to centromeres under all conditions tested. In the absence of wild-type Survivin, INCENP also fails to localize in early mitosis, suggesting that SurvivinD72A/D73A cannot target it to centromeres. Surprisingly, when these cells enter anaphase, INCENP exhibits a robust localization to the central spindle and midbody. This may explain why these cells complete mitosis, execute cytokinesis, and proliferate with SurvivinD72A/D73A as their sole Survivin. These observations suggest that Survivin is not responsible for INCENP targeting during anaphase/telophase, or that if it is, targeting must occur by a different mechanism from that in early mitosis.
###end p 89
###begin p 90
###xml 12 21 12 21 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
###xml 285 294 285 294 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D72A/D73A</sup>
The SurvivinD72A/D73A mutant reveals that INCENP and Survivin concentration at centromeres is not essential for mitotic progression, at least when the proteins subsequently target to the spindle midzone and midbody during anaphase. Interestingly, cells whose growth depends on SurvivinD72A/D73A exhibit a compromised spindle checkpoint in response to taxol but do not show a dramatic increase in the fraction of kinetochore attachment errors. It could be that lower amounts of transient CPC at centromeres serve to correct attachment errors but that higher levels or more stably associated CPC is required for the checkpoint response to low-dose taxol. This is the first evidence that CPC function at centromeres is required for a normal spindle checkpoint response to low-dose taxol. It will be important in the future to identify the ligands that interact with D72 and D73 on the BIR domain to target Survivin to centromeres.
###end p 90
###begin p 91
###xml 393 413 393 413 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib64">Ruchaud et al., 2006</xref>
Survivin continues to guard its secrets closely, and the fact that three different Survivin mutants localize differently in the presence and absence of wild-type Survivin points to the importance of a genetically clean system such as that described here for the definitive functional dissection of the protein. DT40 cells have proven to be a good system for the study of apoptotic cell death (Ruchaud et al., 2006). Thus, even though our studies show that cell death after loss of Survivin is linked to the cell cycle rather than apoptotic disregulation, this system may in the future be able to answer remaining questions concerning the roles of Survivin in mitosis and apoptosis.
###end p 91

###begin title 93
Cell culture and targeting constructs
###end title 93
###begin p 94
###xml 126 127 126 127 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 143 170 137 164 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib11">Buerstedde and Takeda, 1991</xref>
###xml 319 327 312 320 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">survivin</italic>
###xml 412 420 400 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">survivin</italic>
###xml 583 591 571 579 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1 A</xref>
###xml 1168 1189 1156 1177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib66">Samejima et al., 2001</xref>
###xml 1369 1375 1357 1363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1</xref>
###xml 104 111 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 927 934 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
###xml 991 998 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
DT40 cells were grown in Roswell Park Memorial Institute culture media supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% chicken serum in 5% CO2 at 39degreesC (Buerstedde and Takeda, 1991). Doxycycline at a final concentration of 0.5 mug/ml was added to the culture medium to repress transcription of the survivin rescue transgene. The survivin gene locus was isolated from the lambda Fix II DT40 genomic library. To disrupt the survivin gene, targeting vectors (vector pUHG10.3 backbone) containing a selectable marker that confers resistance to neomycin, puromycin, or histidinol were constructed (Fig. 1 A).The resistance cassettes were flanked by a 5' genomic arm situated upstream of the initiation codon of the survivin ORF and a 3' genomic arm situated downstream of its stop codon. For the survivin rescue construct, primers AAGAGCTCAAATGGCGGCCTATGCTGAAATGCTGCCC and CAGTTATTGAGACAGCGTGGCCTAAGGGCCCATGTTCTCTATC were used to amplify the chicken survivin cDNA. The PCR product consisting of the ORF of chicken survivin and a smaller 3' untranslated region was cloned into pUHG10.3. After linearization, all constructs were transfected by electroporation as previously described (Samejima et al., 2001). After drug selection, DNA from resistant clones was extracted and analyzed by Southern blotting after digestion with EcoRI, PflMI, or AflII and probed with a 5' external probe (Fig. 1).
###end p 94
###begin title 95
Immunoblotting and antibodies
###end title 95
###begin p 96
###xml 413 431 409 427 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib20">Cooke et al., 1987</xref>
###xml 433 457 429 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="bib24">Earnshaw and Cooke, 1991</xref>
###xml 319 325 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">Rabbit</span>

###xml 591 598 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">chicken</span>
Whole cell lysates were prepared, and the equivalent to one million cells was loaded onto a polyacrylamide gel. SDS-PAGE and immunoblotting were performed according to standard procedures. Anti-alpha-tubulin antibody (clone B512) and anti-H3 phospho-Ser10 were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich and Millipore, respectively. Rabbit polyclonal (WCE1186) and mouse monoclonal anti-INCENP (3D3) were previously described (Cooke et al., 1987; Earnshaw and Cooke, 1991). Rabbit polyclonal anti-chicken Survivin (WCE43D) was raised against 6xHis-GgSurvivin and affinity purified. ###begin title 97
Indirect immunofluorescence microscopy
###end title 97
###begin p 98
###xml 184 185 178 179 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 188 189 182 183 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 200 201 194 195 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 203 204 197 198 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 215 216 209 210 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
Cells were incubated at 39degreesC on polylysine-coated slides (Polysine; VWR International) for 15 min before fixation in 4% PFA /cytoskeletal buffer (137 mM NaCl, 5 mM KCl, 1.1 mM Na2HPO4, 0.4 mM KH2PO4, 2 mM MgCl2, 2 mM EGTA, 5 mM Pipes, and 5.5 mM glucose) at 37degreesC and permeabilization in 0.15% Triton X-100 in cytoskeletal buffer. After blocking in 1% BSA/PBS, cells were probed with the aforementioned antibodies, and slides were mounted using Vectashield (Vector Laboratories). All image stacks were taken using a microscope (IX-70; Olympus) with a charge-coupled device camera (CH350; Photometrics) controlled by DeltaVision SoftWorx (Applied Precision, LLC) and a 100x S Plan Apocromat NA 1.4 objective using a Sedat filter set (Chroma Technology Corp.) and running at RT. Image stacks were deconvolved, and maximum projections were generated using SoftWorx. All files were saved as TIFF files and exported to Photoshop (Adobe) for final presentation. Levels were adjusted similarly for each experimental dataset to lower nonspecific background haze using the standard Photoshop adjust levels tool.
###end p 98
###begin title 99
Live cell imaging
###end title 99
###begin p 100
###xml 19 27 19 27 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Fig. 3 C</xref>
###xml 247 254 <span type="species:ncbi:9031">Chicken</span>
All videos (except Fig. 3 C and Video 4) were taken using a Perfect Focus microscope (TE-2000E; Nikon) with a camera (CoolSnap HQ2; Photometrics) controlled by Metamorph (MDS Analytical Technologies) using a 100x NA 1.4 Plan Apochromat objective. Chicken DT40 Survivin knockout cells stably expressing H2B-RFP were elutriated, and fraction 2 cells were transferred onto 40-mm coverslips coated with concanavalin A (EMD). These coverslips were placed into a Nikon chamber (Bioptechs) and kept at 39degreesC in the presence of Roswell Park Memorial Institute culture media without phenol red. Three-dimensional datasets were collected every 2 or 10 min, and video frames were processed with ImagePro Plus software (version 6.0; Media Cybernetics, Inc.).

The videos shown in Fig. 3 C and Video 4 were taken using a microscope (IX-70; Olympus) with a charge-coupled device camera (CH350; Photometrics) controlled by DeltaVision SoftWorx and a 100x NA 1.4 S Plan Apocromat objective using a Sedat filter set. The temperature was controlled using a Weather Station (Precision Control) and set for 39degreesC. DT40 cells were placed on concanavalin A-coated coverslips and imaged every minute as z stacks. Image stacks were deconvolved, and maximum projections were generated using SoftWorx. Video files were constructed from selected stills and saved as .mov files.

###begin title 102
Cell elutriation
###end title 102
###begin p 103
###xml 2 3 2 3 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
108 cells resuspended in 5 ml of culture media supplemented with 1 mM EDTA were elutriated using an elutriator (JE-5 rotor; Beckman Coulter) at a steady flow rate of 40 ml/s and a starting speed of 3,750 rpm. After equilibration, the speed was adjusted to collect the different fractions (fraction 2, 3,250 rpm; fraction 3, 3,000 rpm; fraction 4, 2,750 rpm; and fraction 5, 2,500 rpm). Fractions were spun down, immediately placed in fresh warm medium, and kept at 39degreesC.
###end p 103
###begin title 104
Site-directed mutagenesis
###end title 104
###begin p 105
Survivin point mutants were generated by site-directed mutagenesis (QuikChange Site-Directed Mutagenesis kit; Stratagene) using the plasmid WTGg-Survivin-pEGFPN1 and transfected into the knockout cells by electroporation. Stable knockout lines homogeneously expressing the GFP fusion protein were isolated and kept at 39degreesC.

###begin title 106
Growth curves and multinucleation/mitosis index measurements
###end title 106
###begin p 107
###xml 73 74 73 74 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 254 255 248 249 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">5</sup>
###xml 296 297 290 291 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">6</sup>
Growth curves were calculated by seeding the various cell lines at 2 x 105 cells/ml at 39degreesC (unless indicated otherwise) and counting the cell number every 12 h using a hemocytometer. To avoid the effects of overgrowth, cells were diluted to 2 x 105 cells/ml whenever the number exceeded 106 cells/ml. The cell number at each time point was multiplied by the appropriate dilution factor to get a true count. This protocol gives linear cell growth profiles regardless of the length of the experiment.
###end p 107
###begin p 108
For the assessment of the multinucleation/mitosis indexes, a total of 1,000 cells were scored for each time point. The multinucleation index was calculated by dividing the number of multinucleated interphase cells by the total number of interphase cells. The mitotic index was calculated by dividing the number of mitotic cells by the number of interphase and mitotic cells.
###end p 108
