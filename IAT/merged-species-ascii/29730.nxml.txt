Determination of the binding constants of the centromere protein Cbf1 to all 16 centromere DNAs of Saccharomyces cerevisiae

###begin p 2
###xml 11 35 11 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 294 306 294 306 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 649 656 649 656 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 810 813 810 813 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 1235 1238 1235 1238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 11 35 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 294 306 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
Cbf1p is a Saccharomyces cerevisiae chromatin protein belonging to the basic region helix-loop-helix leucine zipper (bHLHzip) family of DNA binding proteins. Cbf1p binds to a conserved element in the 5'-flanking region of methionine biosynthetic genes and to centromere DNA element I (CDEI) of S.cerevisiae centromeric DNA. We have determined the apparent equilibrium dissociation constants of Cbf1p binding to all 16 CDEI DNAs in gel retardation assays. Binding constants of full-length Cbf1p vary between 1.7 and 3.8 nM. However, the dissociation constants of a Cbf1p deletion variant that has been shown to be fully sufficient for Cbf1p function in vivo vary in a range between 3.2 and 12 nM. In addition, native polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis revealed distinct changes in the 3D structure of the Cbf1p/CEN complexes. We also show that the previously reported DNA binding stimulation activity of the centromere protein p64 functions on both the Cbf1 full-length protein and a deletion variant containing only the bHLHzip domain of Cbf1p. Our results suggest that centromeric DNA outside the consensus CDEI sequence and interaction of Cbf1p with adjacent centromere proteins contribute to the complex formation between Cbf1p and CEN DNA.

###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 327 352 327 352 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae </italic>
###xml 392 400 392 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 524 536 524 536 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 665 666 665 666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c1">1</xref>
###xml 667 668 667 668 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c5">5</xref>
###xml 327 351 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 524 536 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
Chromosome segregation in mitosis and meiosis depends on a particular chromosomal structure, the centromere. It provides a chromosomal attachment site, the kinetochore, for the spindle microtubules. Once attached, kinetochore proteins resembling molecular motors actively move chromosomes along microtubules. The centromere of Saccharomyces cerevisiae is ideal for a molecular dissection and in vitro reconstitution because its structure is much less complex than that of higher eukaryotes. The DNA involved in building the S.cerevisiae kinetochore, the centromere DNA, is short (125 bp) whereas it is very long and repetitive in higher eukaryotes (for reviews see 1-5).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 21 24 21 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 33 45 33 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 135 136 135 136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c3">3</xref>
###xml 137 138 137 138 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c6">6</xref>
###xml 471 472 471 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c7">7</xref>
###xml 513 514 513 514 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c8">8</xref>
###xml 619 620 619 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c9">9</xref>
###xml 746 748 746 748 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c10">10</xref>
###xml 850 852 850 852 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c11">11</xref>
###xml 986 987 986 987 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c5">5</xref>
###xml 988 990 988 990 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c12">12</xref>
###xml 33 45 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
 The centromere DNA (CEN DNA) of S.cerevisiae is organized into three elements, the centromere DNA element I (CDEI), CDEII and CDEIII (3,6). CDEI (8 bp) and CDEIII (26 bp) represent consensus sequences, whereas CDEII (78-86 bp) is an AT-rich sequence that separates CDEI and CDEIII. The CDEI element contains the DNA binding site for the Cbf1 protein. So far, no protein has been reported that specifically binds to the CDEII element although Cse4, a histone H3 homolog (7), has been genetically linked to CDEII (8). CDEII likely contributes to the structure of the centromere with no protein specifically bound to it (9). Deletions or insertions altering the length of the AT-rich CDEII domain decrease mitotic chromosome stability (reviewed in 10). High AT content and specific length rather than nucleotide sequence seem to be critical for CDEII (11). CDEIII is essential for centromere function and specifies the binding site for the multi-subunit protein complex CBF3 (reviewed in 5;12).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 178 179 178 179 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">l</sc>
###xml 192 194 192 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c13">13</xref>
###xml 251 253 251 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c14">14</xref>
###xml 271 273 271 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c15">15</xref>
###xml 374 386 374 386 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 388 390 388 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c13">13</xref>
###xml 391 393 391 393 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c16">16</xref>
###xml 495 497 495 497 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c15">15</xref>
###xml 498 500 498 500 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c17">17</xref>
###xml 625 627 625 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c18">18</xref>
###xml 735 737 735 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c19">19</xref>
###xml 738 740 738 740 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c20">20</xref>
###xml 840 842 840 842 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c15">15</xref>
###xml 843 845 843 845 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c17">17</xref>
###xml 989 991 989 991 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c15">15</xref>
###xml 1100 1102 1096 1098 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c21">21</xref>
###xml 1224 1226 1202 1204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c22">22</xref>
###xml 374 386 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
 Cbf1p is not only involved in kinetochore complex formation at the CDEI site but is also part of a transcription activator complex regulating the biosynthesis of the amino acid l-methionine (13) as well as the transcription activator complexes CYT1 (14), GAL2 and TRP1 (15). Inactivation of Cbf1p leads to a partial loss of centromere function and methionine auxotrophy in S.cerevisiae (13,16). Furthermore, Cbf1p deletion mutants show a 10-fold reduction in chromosome segregation efficiency (15-17). It has been suggested that the major cellular role of Cbf1p is to safeguard the biochemical integrity of the kinetochore (18). Cbf1p consists of 351 amino acids and binds to the CDEI concensus sequence 5'-dRTCACRTG-3' (R = purine) (19,20) as a homodimer via a basic region helix-loop-helix zipper (bHLHzip) domain at its C-terminal end (15-17). Protein function is mediated by the bHLHzip domain since deletion of the N-terminal 209 amino acids has no effect on chromosome segregation (15). Two alpha-helical basic regions of bHLH dimers bind into the major grooves of the DNA (for comparison see 21). Cbf1p was shown to bend CDEI upon binding by approximately70degrees towards the major groove of the CDEI binding site (22).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 111 114 111 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 152 153 152 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c9">9</xref>
###xml 236 239 236 239 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 209 214 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
 Polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) revealed different degrees of retarded migration behavior of all 16 CEN DNAs indicating intrinsic curvature (9) and providing further evidence for a model of budding yeast centromeres in which CEN DNA structure is important for the assembly, activity and/or regulation of the centromere protein-DNA complex.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 167 179 167 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 201 203 201 203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c23">23</xref>
###xml 360 361 360 361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c5">5</xref>
###xml 479 483 479 483 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEP3</italic>
###xml 506 510 506 510 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cbf1</italic>
###xml 581 588 581 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 590 592 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c18">18</xref>
###xml 593 595 593 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c24">24</xref>
###xml 689 697 689 697 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 753 756 753 756 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 762 764 762 764 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c25">25</xref>
###xml 167 179 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
 There is increasing genetic and biochemical evidence for a regulated network of protein-protein and protein-DNA interactions that contributes to the structure of the S.cerevisiae kinetochore complex (23). The CBF3 multiprotein complex consists of the four essential subunits: p110, p64, p58 and p23, which form the core element of the centromere (reviewed in 5). In analyses of genetic interactions between centromere protein genes, synthetic lethality was observed between the CEP3 gene encoding p64 and Cbf1. These analyses suggested that Cbf1p may physically interact with p64 in vivo (18,24). This idea was supported by the finding that CBF3 subunits can directly interact with Cbf1p in vitro and that p64 and p23 stimulate the binding of Cbf1p to CEN DNA (25).
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 87 90 87 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 507 510 503 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
 In this paper, the apparent affinity constants for the interaction of Cbf1p to all 16 CEN DNAs were determined in saturation binding experiments followed by gel retardation analyses. The data reveal that the affinities of a truncated Cbf1p version carrying only the DNA binding domain (Cbf1DeltaN209p) vary in a broad range when compared with full-length Cbf1p. The variations appear to depend on CDEI flanking centromere DNA as well as on the N-terminal part of Cbf1p. The CBF3 subunit p64 stimulates the CEN DNA binding activity of both, Cbf1DeltaN209p and the full-length Cbf1p protein indicating that differences in the binding affinities of the two proteins may be overruled through interaction of Cbf1p/CDEI with p64.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 10
###begin title 11
Centromere DNAs
###end title 11
###begin p 12
###xml 111 112 111 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c9">9</xref>
###xml 197 201 197 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN1</italic>
###xml 206 211 206 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN16</italic>
###xml 356 357 356 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c9">9</xref>
###xml 489 492 489 492 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 667 670 667 670 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 71 76 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 345 350 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
DNA fragments (300 bp) carrying the centromere DNA from each of the 16 yeast chromosomes were cloned recently (9) in pBlueScript II SK+ vectors (Amersham, Freiburg, Germany) yielding the plasmids pCEN1 to pCEN16. The cloned 300 bp fragments comprising CDEI, CDEII and CDEIII are flanked by the centromere-specific 90 or 91 bp of genomic budding yeast DNA (9). The consensus binding site of Cbf1p, 5'-dRTCACRTG-3', within CDEI is present only once per centromere fragment. The 16 different CEN DNA fragments were amplified by PCR using the primers PH9 (5'-dCGCTCTAGAACTAGTGGATC-3', 20 bases) and PH10 (5'-TCGAGGTCGACGGTATC-3', 17 bases) which are complementary to the CEN DNA flanking vector sequences. Thus, the amplified fragments are 337 bp in length. PCR products were purified (QIAquick kit, Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) and analyzed on 4% polyacrylamide gels. DNA purity and concentration were determined in a UV spectrophotometer (Carl Zeiss, Specord M 500, Jena) between 200 and 300 nm wavelength. All fragments were diluted to a concentration of 5 nM and stored at -20degreesC.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Recombinant proteins
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 147 163 143 159 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 320 326 312 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al.</italic>
###xml 328 330 320 322 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c26">26</xref>
###xml 408 410 400 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c27">27</xref>
###xml 411 413 403 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c28">28</xref>
###xml 147 168 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">Escherichia coli BL21</span>
Full-length Cbf1p protein and Cbf1DeltaN209p containing only the DNA binding domain of Cbf1p were expressed as histidine-tagged fusion proteins in Escherichia coli BL21(DE3) from plasmids pET-Cbf1p and pET-Cbf1DeltaN209, respectively (kindly provided by D.Thomas), and purified according to a protocol detailed in Kuras et al. (26). His-tagged p64 protein was expressed and purified as described previously (27,28). Protein concentrations were determined using the micro BSA protein assay kit (Pierce, USA).
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Bandshift assay
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 82 84 82 84 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c29">29</xref>
###xml 291 294 291 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 573 575 573 575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c25">25</xref>
###xml 1304 1307 1304 1307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 1566 1570 1566 1570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN6</italic>
The binding constants were determined by bandshift assays in polyacrylamide gels (29). In the reaction mixtures, the amount and concentration of DNA was kept constant while the protein concentration was varied. The binding reactions contained protein at the indicated concentrations, 0.5 nM CEN DNA, 25 mM HEPES buffer pH 7.6, 50% glycerine and 10 mg/ml BSA. Reaction mixtures were kept on ice for 20 min. The reaction mixture was loaded onto 4% native polyacrylamide gels pre-electrophoresed for 1 h. DNA binding stimulation assays were performed as described previously (25). Gels were run in 0.5x TBE (1x TBE contains 100 mM Tris, 83 mM borate, 0.1 mM EDTA pH 8.0) at 20 mA at room temperature and stained in SYBR Gold nucleic acid stain solution (Molecular Probes, Eugene, USA) for 10 min in the dark. The fluorescence of the gel bands was visualized by UV light. Digital pictures were taken with a high resolution CCD camera and stored as tag image files (TIFF). The digital files were analyzed using the gel scan program MacCAM (Cybertec, Berlin, Germany). The intensity of free DNA bands and complex bands in each lane were determined. For calculating the binding constants, the intensities of those bands representing DNA complexes with one or more Cbf1 proteins (multimers) were added. For some CEN DNAs the binding constant of Cbf1p was determined in four completely independent experiments. Deviations of a few percent were observed at concentrations at which most of the DNA is complexed. The deviations of the binding constants from the mean value (for CEN6, mean = 3.69 nM) were <1%.

The centromere binding protein Cbf1p and its deletion variant Cbf1DeltaN209p containing only the bHLHzip DNA binding motif (Fig. 1A) were expressed in E.coli and purified. The purity of the two proteins was analysed in denaturing SDS-PAGE indicating a protein purity >95% (Fig. 1B). The binding constants of the full-length protein (His)6-Cbf1p and the deletion mutant (His)6-Cbf1DeltaN209 to all 16 CEN DNAs of S.cerevisiae were determined in gel retardation experiments. In binding reactions, 0.5 nM CEN DNA was incubated with increasing amounts of recombinant proteins: 0.625-25 nM (His)6-Cbf1p and 2.82-112.5 nM (His)6-Cbf1DeltaN209 (Fig. 2). Binding mixtures were incubated on ice for 20 min and loaded on native polyacrylamide gels, which separate unbound DNA from the protein-DNA complexes. Typical results are displayed for CEN15 DNA in Figure 2. With increasing amounts of protein in the binding mixture, the intensities of the bands representing free DNA decrease and those of the protein-DNA complex bands increase. At high protein concentrations, high molecular weight complexes appear which most likely represent multimere aggregates. The quantitative analyses of the gels in Figure 2 are shown in Figure 3.

 A previous analysis had shown that the 16 CEN DNAs display different degrees of reduced mobility in native polyacrylamide gels, indicating intrinsic curvature of S.cerevisiae centromeric DNA (9). Since the Cbf1p protein has been shown to bend the CDEI sequence of at least three of the 16 CEN DNAs (22), we tested whether Cbf1p would show any additional effects on the CEN DNA migration behavior (Fig. 4). Bandshift experiments were performed that detected all 16 CEN DNAs and the corresponding Cbf1DeltaN209-CEN complex in the same gel (Fig. 4A). The pattern of migration of free CEN DNAs was identical to the one observed previously (9). The Cbf1p-CEN complexes displayed different mobilities. However, the pattern of migration differences of the Cbf1p-CEN complexes was clearly distinct from the pattern of the free CEN DNAs. Pairwise comparison revealed that CEN DNAs that migrated at different positions in their free form were detected at the same position when in complex with Cbf1p (compare CEN4 with CEN5, or CEN14 with CEN15, Fig. 4A), or, vice versa, CEN-DNAs which migrated at similar positions in their free form, were retarded to distinct positions with Cbf1p bound to it (compare CEN8 with CEN9, Fig. 4A). Figure 4B represents an evaluation of the band shift results for each of the CEN DNAs as the ratio between the migration length of the free CEN and the respective Cbf1p-CEN complex. The largest difference (14%) of the complex:CEN migration ratio was observed between CEN5 and CEN12 (Fig. 4B). We obtained similar migration patterns using the full-length Cbf1p derivative indicating that the N-terminal part of Cbf1p does not influence the Cbf1p-CEN structure (data not shown). These results indicate that the altered migration behavior of Cbf1p-complexed CEN may be the result of both Cbf1p-induced bending of CDEI (22) and the intrinsic curvature of the CEN DNA (9).

###begin title 42
DISCUSSION
###end title 42
###begin p 43
###xml 360 364 360 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN1</italic>
###xml 368 373 368 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN16</italic>
###xml 569 570 569 570 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c5">5</xref>
###xml 677 679 677 679 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;4</sup>
###xml 710 712 710 712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c26">26</xref>
###xml 925 926 925 926 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gke220tb1">1</xref>
We analyzed the binding of the Cbf1p protein and its deletion mutant containing only the DNA binding domain to 16 different centromeric DNA fragments representing a degenerate set of specific binding sites. Each of the 337 bp DNA centromere fragments used in this study contains a single CDEI binding site of the consensus sequence 5'-RTCACRTG-3' specific for CEN1 to CEN16, respectively. Cbf1p binds to these sites as a homodimer. In addition, the DNA fragments carry the CDEIII region, which represents the binding site of the hetero-oligomeric protein complex Cbf3 (5). The binding constant of Cbf1p to DNA sequences other than the consensus site was determined to be 2 x 10-4 M in competition experiments (26). Assuming 329 independent non-specific binding sites per centromere fragment, one can estimate their contribution to the binding constant as two orders of magnitude off the constants for specific binding (Table 1). Thus, non-specific binding to the fragments can be neglected.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 121 127 121 127 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al.</italic>
###xml 129 131 129 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c30">30</xref>

###xml 210 211 210 211 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">8</sup>
###xml 213 215 213 215 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;1</sup>
###xml 253 254 251 252 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</bold>

###xml 262 263 260 261 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sub>
###xml 281 286 279 284 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 289 291 287 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c30">30</xref>
###xml 698 700 696 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c31">31</xref>
###xml 329 334 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 369 374 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 553 558 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
 The binding constants of full-length Cbf1p agree with literature values obtained for related proteins. Studies by Baker et al. (30) revealed that the Cbf1p binding equilibrium constant KE to CEN3 DNA is 3 x 108 M-1 (+/-40%) which corresponds (equation 4) to a Kd of 3.3 nM. Baker et al. (30) had purified the Cbf1p protein from yeast, thus the endogenous protein from yeast exhibits a very similar binding constant when compared to the binding constant of 2.5 nM obtained for the recombinant Cbf1 protein used in this work. The binding constant of the human upstream stimulatory factor USF, also containing a bHLH DNA binding motif, to its binding sequence 5'-dCACGTG-3' was determined to 1.3 nM (31).

###begin p 45
###xml 8 11 8 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 202 205 202 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 376 381 376 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN11</italic>
###xml 400 404 400 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN8</italic>
###xml 447 451 447 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN8</italic>
###xml 470 474 470 474 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN3</italic>
###xml 728 733 720 725 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 736 738 728 730 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c20">20</xref>
###xml 770 778 762 770 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 795 803 787 795 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo </italic>
###xml 966 974 958 966 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 979 986 971 978 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1076 1079 1068 1071 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 1089 1091 1081 1083 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c20">20</xref>
 The 16 CEN DNAs offer a variation of binding sequences. It could be speculated that the binding constants are related to the particular CDEI DNA sequence suggesting similar binding constants for those CEN fragments with 5'-dRTCACATG-3' or with 5'-dRTCACGTG-3'. However, this is not observed. For example, within the CDEI sequence group 5'-dRTCACATG-3' we find the strongest (CEN11) and the weakest (CEN8) binding by full-length Cbf1p. Very weak (CEN8) and very strong (CEN3) binding within this DNA sequence group is also observed for Cbf1pDeltaN209p. Thus, DNA sequences next to the consensus binding site seem to influence the DNA binding of Cbf1p and Cbf1DeltaN209p. Similar findings have been reported previously by Wilmen et al. (20) who showed that Cbf1p binding in vitro as well as full in vivo centromere function requires a 10 bp recognition sequence including the 8 bp CDEI. Moreover, these studies indicated that Cbf1p interaction with CDEI is different in vitro and in vivo suggesting that the CDEI-Cbf1p complex interacts with other centromere components in the CEN complex (20).
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 292 293 288 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gke220tb1">1</xref>
###xml 312 316 308 312 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN5</italic>
###xml 326 330 322 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN4</italic>
 Cbf1p and Cbf1DeltaN209p contain the same bHLHzip DNA binding motif. Nevertheless, both proteins bind differently to the 16 DNA sequences indicating that the N-terminal domain of the protein has an influence on binding. The relative value of the binding constants of the two proteins (Table 1) varies from 1.2 (CEN5) to 4.4 (CEN4) showing that the N-terminal part contributes to the binding in a DNA sequence specific way either by interacting with CDEI-surrounding DNA sequences or by modulating the Cbf1 protein structure and thus stabilizing the various interactions between the protein and the 16 different CDEI elements.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 63 66 59 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 152 159 148 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 255 257 247 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c15">15</xref>
###xml 687 689 679 681 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c23">23</xref>
###xml 915 922 907 914 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
 Cbf1p and Cbf1DeltaN209p bind to the degenerate set of the 16 CEN DNAs with binding constants varying by a factor of 2.2 and 3.8, respectively. Since, in vivo, both the Cbf1p-CDEI and the Cbf1DeltaN209p-CDEI complexes exert the same biological function (15), this system gives us an idea of the spectrum of binding strengths of a particular protein-DNA interaction tolerated by the cell. We are not aware of reports on other biological systems with such a DNA sequence and binding strength variation but common function within one cell. It is likely that a variety of protein-protein and protein-DNA interactions are necessary for the formation of fully assembled centromere complexes (23). Thus, the binding strength variations observed in our system represent only one contributing part of the biologically active complex. Obviously, a binding strength variation of a factor of 3.8 appears to be fully tolerated in vivo.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 99 102 99 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 110 122 110 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 178 179 178 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c9">9</xref>
###xml 207 210 207 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 319 322 319 322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 424 427 424 427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 443 444 443 444 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c9">9</xref>
###xml 486 488 486 488 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c22">22</xref>
###xml 518 521 518 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 110 122 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
 The mobility of DNA molecules in native polyacrylamide gels depends on their 3D structure. All 16 CENDNAs of S.cerevisiae display different degrees of reduced mobility in gels (9). Here we show that all 16 CEN DNA-Cbf1p complexes display a migration pattern that is clearly distinct from the pattern observed for free CEN DNA. It is conceivable that the migration characteristics of the complexes are a result of intrinsic CEN DNA curvature (9) combined with bending of CDEI by Cbf1p (22). The intrinsic curvature of CEN DNAs may also influence the accessibilty of Cbf1 proteins to CDEI, which could also be responsible for the variety of binding constants.
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 24 28 24 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cbf1</italic>
###xml 44 48 44 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Cbf1</italic>
###xml 53 57 49 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">N209</italic>
###xml 126 128 122 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c32">32</xref>
###xml 389 392 385 388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 421 428 417 424 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in&#160;vivo</italic>
###xml 577 579 573 575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c13">13</xref>
###xml 773 775 769 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c23">23</xref>
###xml 947 949 943 945 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c18">18</xref>
###xml 950 952 946 948 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c23">23</xref>
###xml 953 955 949 951 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c24">24</xref>
###xml 956 958 952 954 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c33">33</xref>
###xml 1177 1179 1169 1171 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c25">25</xref>
###xml 1201 1204 1193 1196 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 1250 1252 1238 1240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c25">25</xref>
###xml 1308 1309 1296 1297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke220f5">5</xref>
###xml 1348 1355 1336 1343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1453 1456 1437 1440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 1525 1528 1505 1508 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CEN</italic>
###xml 1644 1651 1624 1631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1653 1655 1633 1635 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke220c33">33</xref>
###xml 1 6 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Yeast</span>
###xml 766 771 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
 Yeast mutants in which Cbf1 is replaced by Cbf1DeltaN209 show no difference in their growth rate when compared to wild-type (32). Obviously, for centromere function the N-terminal protein domain does not seem to be essential and thus for the biological function a considerable difference in the binding constant of Cbf1p to the 16 different DNA sites, as well as differences in the Cbf1p-CEN DNA structure are tolerated in vivo. It was shown previously that binding of Cbf1p to its binding motif in the methionine promoter can be stimulated by the transcription factor Met28 (13). It is possible that similar mechanisms act at centromeres. There are more than 12 established kinetochore proteins contributing to structure and function of the centromeres in budding yeast (23). Based on biochemical and genetic data, the centromere proteins Mif2p, Cse4p, Mcm21p and the Cbf3 subunits p110 and p64 have been proposed as Cbf1p-interacting proteins (18,23,24,33). These proteins may function at the centromere to influence Cbf1p-CDEI complex formation. This has been reported recently for the p64 and p23 CBF3 subunits, which stimulate the DNA binding activity of Cbf1DeltaN209p (25). p64 stimulated the CEN DNA binding activity of both Cbf1DeltaN209p (25) and full-length Cbf1p with similar efficiencies (Fig. 5). If this interaction does also occur in vivo, we speculate that the observed differences in binding affinities of Cbf1DeltaN209p to different CENs are tolerated because p64 shifts the affinity of Cbf1DeltaN209p to CEN DNA into a range that makes these differences negligable. Since Cbf1p does not bind to CDEI in the absence of CBF3 in vivo (33), the observed p64 activity may be a critical event that decides whether Cbf1p will bind to CDEI or not.
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 94 106 94 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 94 106 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
 Taken together the results presented here provide further evidence that the structure of the S.cerevisiae centromere complex is determined by a dynamic network of specific DNA-protein and protein-protein interactions.
