Salmonella Induced IL-23 and IL-1beta Allow for IL-12 Production by Monocytes and Mphi1 through Induction of IFN-gamma in CD56+ NK/NK-Like T Cells
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: DvdW RAdP EvdV. Performed the experiments: DvdW RAdP. Analyzed the data: DvdW EvdV. Wrote the paper: DvdW JTvD EvdV.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 111 122 111 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonellae</italic>
###xml 127 139 127 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacteria</italic>
###xml 497 509 493 505 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacteria</italic>
###xml 515 525 511 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">almonellae</italic>
The type-1 cytokine pathway plays a pivotal role in immunity against intracellular bacterial pathogens such as Salmonellae and Mycobacteria. Bacterial stimulation of pattern recognition receptors on monocytes, macrophages and dendritic cells initiates this pathway, and results in the production of cytokines that activate lymphocytes to produce interferon (IFN)-gamma. Interleukin (IL)-12 and IL-23 are thought to be the key cytokines required for initiating a type-1 cytokine immune response to Mycobacteria and Salmonellae. The relative contribution of IL-23 and IL-12 to this process is uncertain.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methodology/Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 268 278 266 276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 345 355 340 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 529 530 513 514 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 692 693 670 671 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 99 104 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 128 133 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 519 524 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
We show that various TLR agonists induce the production of IL-23 but not IL-12 in freshly isolated human monocytes and cultured human macrophages. In addition, type 1 pro-inflammatory macrophages (Mphi1) differentiated in the presence of GM-CSF and infected with live Salmonella produce IL-23, IL-1beta and IL-18, but not IL-12. Supernatants of Salmonella-infected Mphi1 contained more IL-18 and IL-1beta as compared with supernatants of Mphi1 stimulated with isolated TLR agonists, and induced IFN-gamma production in human CD56+ cells in an IL-23 and IL-1beta-dependent but IL-12-independent manner. In addition, IL-23 together with IL-18 or IL-1beta led to the production of GM-CSF in CD56+ cells. Both IFN-gamma and GM-CSF enhanced IL-23 production by monocytes in response to TLR agonists, as well as induced IL-12 production.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions/Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
The findings implicate a positive feedback loop in which IL-23 can enhance its release via induction of IFN-gamma and GM-CSF. The IL-23 induced cytokines allow for the subsequent production of IL-12 and amplify the IFN-gamma production in the type-1 cytokine pathway.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 59 70 59 70 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonellae</italic>
###xml 75 87 75 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Mycobacteria</italic>
###xml 127 130 127 130 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-vandeVosse1">[1]</xref>
###xml 625 631 617 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al.</italic>
###xml 854 857 842 845 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Abdi1">[2]</xref>
###xml 1022 1027 1006 1011 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">bovis</italic>
###xml 1042 1050 1026 1034 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1050 1053 1034 1037 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Feinberg1">[3]</xref>
###xml 919 927 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 1019 1031 <span type="species:ncbi:33892">M. bovis BCG</span>
Immunity against intracellular bacterial pathogens such as Salmonellae and Mycobacteria depends on the type-1 cytokine pathway [1]. This pathway is initiated by bacterial stimulation of pattern recognition receptors on monocytes and macrophages, resulting in the production of cytokines that activate lymphocytes and induce IFN-gamma production. The IFN-gamma in turn activates monocytes and macrophages, to enhance bactericidal effector mechanisms and to further pro-inflammatory cytokine production. Thus, the type-1 cytokine pathway critically depends on the cross-talk between monocytes/macrophages and lymphocytes. Abdi et al. state that IL-12p70 cannot be the primary trigger that initiates Th1 T-cell responses, as it is not produced in response to bacterial stimulation when costimulation in the form of activated T cells or IFN-gamma are absent [2]. In addition, in whole blood assays with blood obtained from patients with complete IFN-gammaR deficiency, no IL-12p70 production can be detected in response to M. bovis BCG infection in vitro[3]. How the type-1 pathway is initiated, therefore, has remained uncertain.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 84 87 84 87 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Happel1">[4]</xref>
###xml 307 310 305 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Verreck1">[5]</xref>
###xml 312 315 310 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Hayes1">[6]</xref>
###xml 441 444 435 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Verreck1">[5]</xref>
###xml 528 531 518 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Oppmann1">[7]</xref>
###xml 555 558 545 548 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-vandeWetering1">[8]</xref>
###xml 782 785 768 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Hunter1">[9]</xref>
Interleukin-23 (IL-23) is a cytokine which is produced early in the immune response [4]. Monocytes as well as type 1 macrophages (Mphi1) produce IL-23 in response to the binding of pathogens and pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), such as lipopolysaccharide (LPS), to Toll-like receptors (TLRs) [5], [6]. In contrast to IL-23, for the production of IL-12 in response to PAMPs an additional stimulus such as IFN-gamma is required [5]. IL-23 is known to induce IFN-gamma production in naive T cells, in memory T cells [7] and in NK-like T cells [8], thereby potentially providing the necessary, additional stimulus to induce IL-12 production. Next, IL-12 and IL-18 enhance IFN-gamma production in NK, NK-like T cells and Th1 cells by binding to their respective receptors [9]. Though IFN-gamma is not required for the induction of IL-23, the precise role of IFN-gamma in the regulation of IL-23 is not well established.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 171 175 171 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Fehniger1">[10]</xref>
###xml 245 249 245 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Bermudez1">[11]</xref>
###xml 250 254 250 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Carryn1">[13]</xref>
###xml 401 405 398 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Wing1">[14]</xref>
###xml 407 411 404 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Burchett1">[15]</xref>
###xml 541 544 536 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Verreck1">[5]</xref>
###xml 460 465 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Granulocyte-Macrophage Colony Stimulating Factor (GM-CSF) is a cytokine produced by NK cells and T cells in response to a variety of stimuli, for instance IL-15 and IL-18 [10]. GM-CSF activates monocytes and enhances their bactericidal activity [11]-[13]. Moreover, monocytes pre-stimulated with GM-CSF secrete increased quantities of tumor necrosis factor (TNF) and IL-1beta when stimulated with LPS [14], [15]. Furthermore, GM-CSF induces differentiation of human monocytes into Mphi1, a macrophage type that is capable of producing IL-23 [5].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 499 509 488 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 297 302 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 417 422 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 585 590 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 700 705 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In this study we addressed the roles of the cytokines IL-23, IL-1beta, IL-18, GM-CSF and IFN-gamma in the crosstalk between NK/NK-like T cells and monocytes/macrophages in the early activation of the type-1 cytokine pathway. To this end we determined which TLR agonists induce IL-23 production in human monocytes and macrophages and assessed the roles of GM-CSF and IFN-gamma in the regulation of IL-23 production by human monocytes in response to TLR agonists. Furthermore, we explored the role of Salmonella-induced IL-23, IL-1beta and IL-18 in the induction of IFN-gamma in primary human NK/NK-like T cells, and tested the capacities of IL-23, IL-1beta and IL-18 to induce GM-CSF and IFN-gamma in human NK and NK-like T cells.
###end p 12

Materials and Methods

###begin title 14
Cells and Culture Conditions
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 10 11 10 11 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 26 27 26 27 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
Human CD14+ cells and CD56+ cells were isolated from buffy coats from healthy donors (Sanquin) by Ficoll-Amidotrizoate density gradient centrifugation and subsequent selection with anti-CD14 MACS beads or anti-CD56 MACS beads (Miltenyi Biotech).
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 4 5 4 5 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 181 182 177 178 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 379 380 369 370 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
CD14+ cells were cultured in RPMI-1630 medium, supplemented with 20 mM GlutaMAX (Gibco/Invitrogen), 10% FCS, 100 U/ml Penicillin, 100 microg/ml Streptomycin (Gibco/Invitrogen). CD56+ cells were cultured in Iscove's modified Dulbecco's medium (IMDM, Bio-Whittaker) supplemented with 20 mM GlutaMAX, 10% FCS, 100 U/ml Penicillin, 100 microg/ml Streptomycin. To generate Mphi1, CD14+ cells were cultured for 6 days in RPMI 1640 with 10% FCS and 5 ng/ml GM-CSF (Biosource).

To determine cytokine production by monocytes, CD14+ beads isolated cells were seeded in a 96-well plate at 1.105 cells per well and cultured for 24 h in the presence or absence of the following TLR agonists: 100 ng/ml S. minnesota LPS (Sigma), 200 ng/ml recombinant flagellin (tlrl-flic, InvivoGen), 1 microg/ml Pam3CSK4 (tlrl-pms, InvivoGen), 100 microg/ml Zymosan A (Sigma), 1 microg/ml CL075 (tlrl-c75, InvivoGen) or 1 microg/ml CL087 (tlrl-c87, InvivoGen) in a final volume of 200 microl. Supernatants were taken and IL-23 (eBioscience), IL-1beta (Biosource) IL-18 (MBL) and IL-12p70 (Sanquin) concentrations were determined by ELISA. Subsequently Mphi1 were stimulated with TLR agonists, infected with group B Salmonella at a 10:1 multiplicity of infection as described below, or left unstimulated. Twenty-four hours after stimulation, supernatants were collected and cytokine production was determined by ELISA. To test the effect of GM-CSF and IFN-gamma pre-stimulation, 1.105 CD14+ monocytes were cultured for 16 hours with 50-5000 pg/ml GM-CSF, 50-5000 pg/ml IFN-gamma (Biosource) or left unstimulated. Subsequently cells were stimulated with 100 ng/nl LPS plus 1 microg/ml CL075 for 24 hours, or left unstimulated. After stimulation cell free supernatants were collected and IL-23 production was determined by ELISA (eBioscience).

To generate supernatants from Salmonella stimulated Mphi1, 1.106 cells were seeded in a 12-well plate and infected with group B Salmonella at a 10:1 multiplicity of infection. To promote the uptake of bacteria, the bacteria were spun onto the macrophages by centrifugation at 300xg for 5 min and the cells were allowed to internalize the bacteria for 30 min at 37degreesC, 5% CO2. ###begin title 25
Results
###end title 25
###begin title 26
Monocytes and Mphi1 Produce IL-23, but Not IL-12, in Response to Various TLR Agonists
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 65 68 63 66 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Verreck1">[5]</xref>
###xml 524 531 520 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0008396-g001">Fig. 1A</xref>
###xml 722 723 718 719 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 239 244 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
LPS is known to induce IL-23, but not IL-12, production in Mphi1 [5]. To determine whether various TLR agonists are capable of inducing IL-23 or IL-12 production in monocytes and Mphi1, we first tested these capacities in freshly isolated human monocytes. Unstimulated monocytes did not produce IL-23, whereas Zymosan A (agonist for TLR2/6) induced minimal IL-23 production, LPS (TLR4) and CL075 (TLR8/7) each induced small amounts of IL-23, while LPS and CL075 synergized in the induction of IL-23 production in monocytes (Fig. 1A). IL-23 production was also observed in response to LPS plus flagellin (TLR5) but the amount released was similar to that induced by LPS alone. Of note, the amounts of IL-23 produced by CD14+ monocytes varied somewhat between different donors, the relative amount released upon the various TLR stimulations was however similar (data not shown). In none of the supernatants IL-12p70 was detected, given a detection limit of the IL-12p70 ELISA of 3 pg/ml (data not shown).
###end p 27

TLR stimuli and Salmonella infection induce IL-23 production in human monocytes and Mphi1.

###begin p 29
###xml 5 6 5 6 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5</sup>

To determine whether not only PAMPs such as the TLR agonists, but also live pathogens induce IL-23 rather than IL-12 production in Mphi1, we infected cultured Mphi1 with live Salmonella and assessed cytokine release in the culture supernatants. Mphi1 exposed to and containing ingested Salmonellae produced IL-23 whereas Mphi1 left unstimulated did not (Fig. 1C). We have shown previously that for IL-23 to induce IFN-gamma production in NK-like T cells, an additional stimulus such as IL-18 is required [8]. Unstimulated cells produced minimal IL-18, but no IL-1beta. Salmonella-infected Mphi1 produced markedly more IL-18 and IL-1beta as compared with cells stimulated with LPS (Fig. 2A and B).

###xml 1849 1856 1765 1772 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0008396-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 1990 1991 1902 1903 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>

###xml 2022 2029 1930 1937 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="pone-0008396-t001">Table 1</xref>
###xml 2243 2249 2137 2143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0008396-g004">Fig. 4</xref>
Infection of Mphi1 with live Salmonella resulted in the production of IL-23, IL-18 and IL-1beta. IL-23 is reported to induce IFN-gamma production in NK, NK-like T cells and in T cells, in synergy with IL-18 [7], [8], [18]. As mentioned before, IL-1beta is known to enhance IL-12 induced IFN-gamma production [16], [17]. Therefore, we tested whether perhaps IL-23 in combination with IL-1beta could also induce IFN-gamma production in CD56+ (NK and NK-like T) cells. Each cytokine alone was not able to induce IFN-gamma production (Fig. 3). However, when IL-23 and IL-1beta were combined, they synergized in inducing IFN-gamma production (Fig. 3). Isolated CD56+ cells consist of CD56+/CD3- NK cells and CD56+/CD3+ NK-like T cells. We next determined which of these two populations were responsible for the observed IFN-gamma production by IL-23 plus IL-1beta stimulated CD56+ cells. Because sorting CD3+ cells with an anti-CD3 antibody induces undesired activation of the cells, we used intracellular staining for IFN-gamma in combination with CD3/CD56 labeling. We arbitrarily designated a population positive for IFN-gamma when more than 1% of the cells stained IFN-gamma positive. CD56+ cells obtained from eight donors were stimulated with IL-23, IL-1beta, IL-18, IL-23 plus IL-1beta, IL-23 plus IL-18, or left unstimulated for 48 hours. In response to IL-23 plus IL-1beta in half of the donors IFN-gamma production was observed in both CD56+/CD3- NK cells and CD56+/CD3+ NK-like T cells, whereas in the other half IFN-gamma production was only observed in CD56+/CD3- NK-like T cells (Table 1). In response to IL-23 plus IL-18 IFN-gamma production was observed in both populations in seven donors, while in one donor only CD56+CD3- cells produced IFN-gamma (Table 1). On average, significant IFN-gamma production was observed in response to IL-23 plus IL-1beta or IL-18 in NK cells, while in NK-like T cells only IL-23 plus IL-1beta resulted in significant IFN-gamma production (Fig. 4).

###begin title 66
Discussion
###end title 66
###begin p 67
###xml 230 240 225 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 320 321 312 313 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 529 539 517 527 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 590 591 572 573 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 740 741 712 713 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 34 39 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In this study we demonstrate that human monocytes and cultured pro-inflammatory macrophages (i.e., Mphi1) both produce IL-23, IL-18 and IL-1beta in response to various TLR agonists, during co-incubation and upon ingestion of live Salmonella. Subsequently, in response to IL-23 in combination with IL-18 or IL-1beta, CD56+ cells produce IFN-gamma and GM-CSF. These cytokines in turn enhance IL-23 production by monocytes and macrophages in response to TLR agonists, implying a positive feedback loop. Furthermore, supernatants of Salmonella infected Mphi1 induce IFN-gamma production in CD56+ cells and this induction of IFN-gamma was critically dependent on IL-23 and IL-1beta. Finally, supernatants from IL-23 plus IL-1beta stimulated CD56+ cells allowed monocytes to produce IL-12p70 in response to LPS.
###end p 67

###xml 91 92 91 92 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</bold>

###xml 1126 1127 1090 1091 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>

###xml 1461 1462 1410 1411 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1576 1579 1525 1528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-vandeWetering1">[8]</xref>
###xml 1632 1642 1581 1591 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 1753 1763 1686 1696 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 1881 1885 1810 1814 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Cooper1">[17]</xref>
###xml 1887 1891 1816 1820 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Tripp1">[23]</xref>
###xml 2069 2070 1992 1993 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</bold>
###xml 2175 2176 2094 2095 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5</bold>
###xml 2276 2277 2192 2193 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 2454 2455 2367 2368 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
The assumption of an IL-23 positive feedback loop is based on the following observations: (1) We demonstrated that monocytes as well as Mphi1 are able to produce IL-23 in response to various TLR agonists. In addition, monocytes and Mphi1 produced small amounts of IL-18 and IL-1beta in response to individual TLR agonists, while cells infected with live Salmonella produced large amounts of IL-18, IL-1beta and IL-23. In line, Verreck et al. showed that Mphi1 produce IL-23 and IL-1beta in response to LPS or mycobacterial sonicate [21]. (2) Guia et al. reported that in the presence of macrophages infected with Salmonella in vitro, CD56+ NK-like T cells, but not CD56- T cells produce IFN-gamma in absence of TCR stimulation [22]. Here we show that IL-23 and IL-1beta in these supernatants of Salmonella infected Mphi1 were critical components for the induction of IFN-gamma production in the CD56+ NK/NK-like T cells, because neutralization of these interleukins resulted in abrogation or a strong reduction of IFN-gamma production, respectively. (3) Recombinant IL-18 was able to enhance the induction of IFN-gamma in CD56+ in response to supernatants of Salmonella infected Mphi1. However, neutralization of IL-18 did not significantly reduce the IFN-gamma production. These results support that IL-23 and IL-1beta, but not IL-18, in the supernatant of Salmonella infected Mphi1 are critical for the induction of IFN-gamma in CD56+ cells. Moreover, in CD56+ cells stimulated with IL-23 no synergy is seen with IL-18 when concentrations lower than 20 ng/ml IL-18 are used [8], indicating that the IL-18 concentration induced by Salmonella (which was approximately1-4 ng/ml) may be too low to synergize with IL-23 in inducing IFN-gamma. In addition, Salmonella infection is known to induce TNF production in macrophages and TNF has been reported to enhance IFN-gamma production [17], [23]. Therefore, in our experiments, TNF in the supernatants of infected Mphi1 may also enhance IFN-gamma production. However, we did not determine the role of TNF in this respect. (4) IFN-gamma and GM-CSF both enhanced IL-23 production in monocytes in response to various stimulations. (5) Moreover, both cytokines could be induced by IL-23, in combination with IL-18 or IL-1beta, in CD56+ cells. Depending on the concentrations used, IL-23 plus IL-18 or IL-1beta induced more than 200 pg/ml GM-CSF, a concentration which we have shown to be sufficient to prime CD14+ monocytes for enhanced IL-23 production.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 240 262 232 254 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Francisella tularensis</italic>
###xml 296 300 288 292 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Butchar2">[24]</xref>
###xml 372 376 360 364 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Hayes2">[25]</xref>
###xml 481 484 465 468 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Verreck1">[5]</xref>
###xml 562 572 543 553 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 729 739 704 714 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 854 858 825 829 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Gerosa1">[26]</xref>
###xml 240 262 <span type="species:ncbi:263">Francisella tularensis</span>
###xml 280 285 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 794 799 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Together these observations confirm the presence of a positive feedback loop in which IL-23 can enhance its own expression via the induction of IFN-gamma and GM-CSF. In line with our observations, IFN-gamma was recently reported to enhance Francisella tularensis induced IL-23 in human monocytes [24]. IFN-gamma allows for the transcription of IL-12p35 in response to LPS [25] and for the induction of IL-12 in response to a PAMP an additional stimulus such as IFN-gamma is needed [5]. Herein we show that IL-23 together with IL-1beta present in supernatants of Salmonella infected Mphi1 can provide this costimulus by the induction of IFN-gamma in NK-like T cells, thereby allowing subsequent IL-12p70 production in response to Salmonella and the initiation of a Th1 response. In contrast, in human DCs IFN-gamma can inhibit IL-23p19 mRNA transcription [26], emphasizing the complexity of the regulation of this cytokine.
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 238 242 238 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Pirhonen1">[27]</xref>
###xml 346 350 346 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Hochrein1">[28]</xref>
###xml 351 355 351 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Marshall1">[30]</xref>
In this study we report on a positive feedback of IL-23 production, but negative feedback mechanisms may exist as well. For instance, IL-4 and IL-10 are both reported to inhibit the induction of IL-23 and may serve as a negative feedback [27]. IL-4 may be important in balancing IL-23 and IL-12 production as IL-4 can enhance IL-12p70 production [28]-[30]. It would be interesting to explore the effects of these and other potential negative regulators of IL-23.
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 251 261 245 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 445 455 439 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 835 845 829 839 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 972 982 964 974 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 1042 1046 1034 1038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Bouter1">[31]</xref>
###xml 1434 1435 1422 1423 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
To reach the conclusion on a positive feedback loop in which IL-23 enhances its own expression via the induction of IFN-gamma and GM-CSF, the following pitfalls of this study need to be considered. Firstly, in supernatants of Mphi1 infected with live Salmonella, we neutralized IL-23 using an antibody which is able to neutralize IL-12p70 as well. Though we did not detect IL-12p70 and IL-12p35 mRNA expression was not induced by infection with Salmonella, one should bear in mind that the detection limit of the IL-12p70 ELISA used was 3 pg/ml and that we can not exclude that the effect observed after neutralizing the IL-23 is due to the neutralization of small, undetectable amounts of IL-12p70. Secondly, the concentrations of the TLR agonists used may exceed physiological relevant conditions. For example, the concentrations of Salmonella LPS added to the cells used in this study are likely to differ from LPS concentration when Mphi1 are being infected with live Salmonella. During sepsis 5-10 pg/ml LPS can be detected in the blood [31]. Thirdly, between donors we observed interindividual differences in cytokine production in response to the stimulations used. This may reflect differences in responsiveness to stimulation and differences in capacity to produce cytokines between cells obtained from different donors. We observed for example remarkable interindividual differences in the subset of IFN-gamma producing CD56+ cells in response to IL-23 in combination with IL-1beta or IL-18.
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 482 492 478 488 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 622 626 618 622 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Napolitani1">[32]</xref>
###xml 688 703 684 699 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 703 707 699 703 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Jo1">[33]</xref>
###xml 709 713 705 709 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0008396-Davila1">[34]</xref>
###xml 612 617 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 688 703 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
In addition to the elucidation of the positive feed-back loop of IL-23 expression we observed a synergy between LPS (TLR4) and flagellin (TLR5) and between LPS and CL075 (TLR8/7) with respect to the induction of IL-23 production in monocytes and Mphi1. During an infection, synergy between TLRs is likely of importance because pathogens express multiple TLR agonists. LPS and flagellin for instance, which in synergy induce large quantities of IL-23 in Mphi1, are both expressed by Salmonella. Synergy between LPS (TLR4) and R848, another TLR8/7 agonist, has been observed in the induction of IL-12 and IL-23 in human DCs [32]. Both TLR8 and TLR4 are implicated in the resistance against M. tuberculosis[33], [34], suggesting the synergy we observed between the TLR4 and the TLR8/7 agonists may be relevant in mycobacterial infections.
###end p 72
###begin p 73
###xml 74 84 74 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella</italic>
###xml 523 524 511 512 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
In conclusion, we have shown that various TLR agonists and infection with Salmonella can induce IL-23, IL-18 and IL-1beta, but not IL-12, production in monocytes and Mphi1. Furthermore, our findings indicate that a positive feedback loop exists in which IL-23 can enhance its own production via the induction of IFN-gamma and GM-CSF, which both prime monocytes for enhanced IL-23 production. Last, IL-23, in combination with IL-1beta, could prime monocytes to produce IL-12p70 in response to LPS, via the activation of CD56+ cells, thereby amplifying the type-1 cytokine pathway to IFN-gamma production.
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 55 56 48 49 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 85 86 78 79 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 90 91 83 84 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 109 110 102 103 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 113 114 106 107 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
IL-23 plus IL1beta induced IFN-gamma production in CD56+ cells, which consist of CD56+/CD3- NK cells and CD56+CD3+ NK-like T cells. In half of the donors IFN-gamma production was observed in both NK cells and NK-like T cells, whereas in the other half IFN-gamma production was observed only in the NK cells. Possible explanations for these interindividual differences may be previous (recent) exposure to different pathogens or genetic differences between donors; these may be elucidated when individual donors are assayed at regular intervals over a longer time period to determine whether or not the individual cytokine production profiles are constant.
