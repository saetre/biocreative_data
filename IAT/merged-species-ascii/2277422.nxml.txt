###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 42 46 42 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce </italic>
###xml 57 83 57 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 150 174 150 174 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium smegmatis </italic>
###xml 178 194 178 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 222 227 222 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 240 245 240 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 287 292 287 292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 57 83 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
###xml 150 173 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">Mycobacterium smegmatis</span>
###xml 178 193 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
mce3 is one of the four virulence-related mce operons of Mycobacterium tuberculosis. In a previous work we showed that the overexpression of Mce3R in Mycobacterium smegmatis and M. tuberculosis abolishes the expression of lacZ fused to the mce3 promoter, indicating that Mce3R represses mce3 transcription.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 40 56 40 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 106 112 106 112 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 186 191 186 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 204 213 204 213 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 286 291 286 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 302 317 302 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 336 340 336 340 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1</italic>
###xml 342 347 342 347 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce2 </italic>
###xml 351 356 351 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 447 453 447 453 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 585 594 585 594 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 605 620 605 620 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 40 61 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 221 227 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 302 317 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 605 620 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
We obtained a knockout mutant strain of M. tuberculosis H37Rv by inserting a hygromycin cassette into the mce3R gene. The mutation results in a significant increase in the expression of mce3 genes either in vitro or in a murine cell macrophages line as it was determined using promoter-lacZ fusions in M. tuberculosis. The abundance of mce1, mce2 and mce4 mRNAs was not affected by this mutation as it was demonstrated by quantitative RT-PCR. The mce3R promoter activity in the presence of Mce3R was significantly reduced compared with that in the absence of the regulator, during the in vitro culture of M. tuberculosis.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 35 40 35 40 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 126 130 126 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1</italic>
###xml 132 137 132 137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce2 </italic>
###xml 141 146 141 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 157 172 157 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 157 172 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Mce3R repress the transcription of mce3 operon and self regulates its own expression but does not affect the transcription of mce1, mce2 and mce4 operons of M. tuberculosis.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 47 73 47 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 47 73 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</span>
Tuberculosis (TB), a chronic illness caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, is still a major worldwide disease. Pathogenic mycobacteria species have demonstrated a remarkable ability to survive in diverse conditions encountered during the infection process. However, even after decades of investigation, there is still little knowledge about mycobacterial pathogenesis. Understanding the infective process at the molecular and cellular levels will lead to new strategies to control this disease and even to the development of an effective vaccine.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 45 61 45 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 115 119 115 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce </italic>
###xml 188 189 188 189 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 337 364 337 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mycobacterium tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 381 382 381 382 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 383 384 383 384 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 45 66 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 337 371 <span type="species:ncbi:77643">Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex</span>
The analysis of the complete sequence of the M. tuberculosis H37Rv genome revealed the presence of four paralogous mce genes, all encoded in an operon structure consisting of eight genes [1]. The biological function of Mce proteins is not known, but increasing evidence has demonstrated that they are clearly related to the virulence of Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex species [2-8].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 173 174 173 174 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 225 230 225 230 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1 </italic>
###xml 340 348 340 348 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rv0165c </italic>
###xml 441 446 441 446 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1 </italic>
###xml 518 524 518 524 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rv0586</italic>
###xml 561 566 561 566 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce2 </italic>
###xml 628 633 628 633 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce2 </italic>
###xml 752 756 752 756 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce </italic>
###xml 767 783 767 783 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 784 786 784 786 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 787 789 787 789 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 868 872 868 872 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce </italic>
###xml 941 946 941 946 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Mce3R</italic>
###xml 1008 1013 1008 1013 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 1031 1040 1031 1040 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 1051 1067 1051 1067 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 1068 1070 1068 1070 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1135 1148 1135 1148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. smegmatis </italic>
###xml 1152 1168 1152 1168 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 1221 1226 1221 1226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 767 782 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1051 1066 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1135 1147 <span type="species:ncbi:1772">M. smegmatis</span>
###xml 1152 1167 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Gene regulation is considered to play a central role in host-microbe interactions, and many virulence genes are regulated in response to the host. Casali and collaborators [9] identified a regulatory mechanism which controls mce1 expression. They have demonstrated that a homologue of the FadR subfamily of GntR transcriptional regulators, Rv0165c (designated Mce1R), is a negative regulator that intracellularly represses expression of the mce1 operon. In addition, a gene encoding a putative transcriptional factor, Rv0586, is located immediately upstream of mce2 operon and it is transcribed in the same direction as that of mce2 genes. Furthermore, it has been found that there are growth phase and tissue specific differences in the expression of mce operons in M. tuberculosis [10-12] which is in agreement with the presence of regulatory mechanisms controlling mce transcription. In a previous work, we found evidence indicating that Mce3R, a TetR family transcriptional regulator, down-regulates the mce3 operon during the in vitro growing of M. tuberculosis [13]. We have demonstrated that the overexpression of Mce3R in both M. smegmatis and M. tuberculosis abolishes the expression of a gene reporter fused to mce3 promoter.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 56 58 56 58 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 59 61 59 61 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 194 210 194 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 256 258 256 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 461 463 461 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 464 466 464 466 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 467 469 467 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 492 497 492 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R</italic>
###xml 527 532 527 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 194 210 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
TetR family members often regulate their own synthesis [14-18]. The classic example of self regulation in members of this family protein is a repressor involved in resistance to tetracycline of Escherichia coli, which has given the name TetR to the group [19]. In a number of TetR-autoregulated systems the regulator and the structural genes are divergently transcribed and the region for protein binding overlaps the promoters placed in the intergenic region [15,18,20]. That is the case of mce3R, which is placed upstream of mce3 operon, oriented in the opposite direction and separated from it by a region of 880 bp.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 61 66 61 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 83 99 83 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 133 138 133 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R</italic>
###xml 148 164 148 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 228 233 228 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 246 250 246 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce </italic>
###xml 83 98 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 148 163 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
In this work we validate the role of Mce3R in repressing the mce3 transcription in M. tuberculosis by analyzing gene expression in a mce3R-knockout M. tuberculosis strain. We also found that this regulation is exclusive for the mce3 operon among mce genes and that the Mce3R repressor regulates its own expression.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Results
###end title 14
###begin title 15
###xml 18 24 18 24 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 34 49 34 49 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 34 49 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Construction of a mce3R mutant in M. tuberculosis
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 30 35 30 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 118 134 118 134 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 184 190 184 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 231 247 231 247 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 329 331 329 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 352 357 352 357 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 396 400 396 400 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sacB</italic>
###xml 459 460 459 460 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 464 465 464 465 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</sup>
###xml 474 475 474 475 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">R</sup>
###xml 504 506 504 506 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>
###xml 713 715 713 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1B</xref>
###xml 756 761 752 757 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R</italic>
###xml 118 139 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 231 246 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
As a first step to assess the mce3 operon expression in the absence of Mce3R, we obtained a knockout mutant strain of M. tuberculosis H37Rv by inserting a hygromycin cassette into the mce3R gene. The site-directed mutant strain of M. tuberculosis was obtained by two-step mutagenesis strategy by using the p2NIL shuttle plasmid [21], which carries the lacZ gene and the counter selectable marker sacB. Allelic exchange was confirmed in the selected clones (HyR, KmS, and SacR) by Southern blotting (Fig. 1A), since the mutant showed a hybridizing fragment of about 1.5 kb absent in the wild-type strain. This polymorphism is due to the introduction of an extra EcoRI site present in the hygromycin cassette (Fig. 1B). The mutant strain was designated Deltamce3R. The mutation was complemented by transforming the plasmid pSummce3R into the mutant.
###end p 16

###begin title 18
###xml 0 9 0 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In vitro </italic>
In vitro characterization of Deltamce3R
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 21 27 21 27 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 68 77 68 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 111 117 107 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 321 322 317 318 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 385 394 381 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 404 419 400 415 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 404 419 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
To determine whether mce3R disruption introduces alterations during in vitro growth, growth curves of the Deltamce3R mutant, complemented, and parental strains were compared under standard culture conditions. All assayed strains showed similar doubling time and growth characteristics throughout the culture period (Fig. 2). This result indicates that the mutation does not affect the in vitro growth of M. tuberculosis.
###end p 19

###begin p 22
###xml 41 46 41 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 84 89 84 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 106 122 106 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 252 254 252 254 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 363 368 363 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 432 438 432 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 446 462 446 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 106 127 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 446 461 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
We have previously demonstrated that the mce3 promoter allows the expression of the lacZ reporter gene in M. tuberculosis H37Rv but that this expression is completely abolished when Mce3R is overexpressed in the H37Rv strain from a multi-copy plasmid [13]. Although these findings constitute initial evidence demonstrating the role of Mce3R as a repressor of the mce3 operon transcription, the presence of an endogenous copy of the mce3R gene in M. tuberculosis did not enable us to determine the conditions in which the regulator system operates.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 45 50 45 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 127 132 127 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 144 149 144 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ </italic>
###xml 188 194 188 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 221 225 221 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">lacZ</italic>
###xml 428 444 421 437 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 450 459 443 452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 514 519 507 512 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 567 583 560 576 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 608 610 601 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 611 613 604 606 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 633 638 626 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 713 714 706 707 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 428 443 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 567 582 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
Here, in order to compare the expression the mce3 operon either in the absence or in the presence of Mce3R, DNA fusions of the mce3 promoter to lacZ reporter, containing or not containing mce3R were cloned within pYUB178-lacZ. The resulting plasmids, pP3-mce3R and pP3 respectively, were integrated into the chromosome of the Deltamce3R strain. The beta-galactosidase activity was measured at different points along cultures of M. tuberculosis grown in vitro and in a macrophages cell line. Since transcription of mce3 genes has previously shown to be increased when M. tuberculosis was grown in rich media [10-12] the expression of mce3 operon was assessed in both synthetic (7H9) and rich (Dubos) media (Figure 3 and data not shown).
###end p 23

###begin title 35

###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 23 29 23 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>

To investigate whether mce3R is subject to transcriptional autoregulation, a transcriptional fusion was constructed between the mce3R promoter and the lacZ reporter. Since mce3R is located adjacent to mce3 operon and divergently oriented, the mce3R promoter is situated in mce3R-yrbE3A intergenic region. The entire intergenic region was fused to the lacZ gene within pYUB178-lacZ to create plasmid pPR3-lacZ. The pPR3-lacZ plasmid was transformed into the wild type M. tuberculosis H37Rv and the M. tuberculosis Deltamce3R mutant, and beta-galactosidase activity was measured to assess the levels of mce3R promoter activity with and without Mce3R regulator. As shown in figure 5, in the presence of Mce3R (wild type H37Rv strain) the activity of mce3R promoter is steadily and significantly reduced as compared with that in the absence of the Mce3R regulator (mutant Deltamce3R strain). This reduction in mce3R promoter activity was more evident during the stationary growth phase. These experiments demonstrate that the Mce3R protein is able to transcriptionally repress expression of the mce3R promoter in M. tuberculosis during the in vitro culture condition tested.

###begin title 38
Discussion
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 62 78 62 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 858 874 858 874 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 1023 1039 1023 1039 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 1040 1042 1040 1042 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1066 1071 1066 1071 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al</italic>
###xml 1074 1076 1074 1076 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 1150 1155 1150 1155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ethA </italic>
###xml 62 77 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 858 873 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1023 1038 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 1301 1309 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Little is known about gene regulation of virulence factors in M. tuberculosis due to its slow growth rate and the late development of mycobacterial genetics. Prokaryotic transcriptional regulators are classified in families on the basis of sequence similarity and structural and functional criteria. The TetR family, a family of transcriptional regulators that is well represented and widely distributed among bacteria, has a helix turn-helix (HTH) signature, the most recurrent DNA binding motif, to bind its target DNA. Members of the TetR family of repressors control transcription of proteins involved in multidrug resistance, enzymes implicated in different catabolic pathways, biosynthesis of antibiotics, osmotic stress, and pathogenicity of gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria. At least 40 putative TetR-family regulator genes are spread on the M. tuberculosis genome. Most of them are similar to the TetR/AcrR family, but just a few have been characterized. Mce3R was the first TetR-like regulator studied in M. tuberculosis [13]. Then, Engohang-Ndong et al. [22] found that a member of the TetR/CamR family represses the expression of ethA that encodes a protein that catalyses the activation of ethionamide (ETH). ETH is an important second-line anti TB drug used for the treatment of patients infected with multidrug-resistant strains.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 33 38 33 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 58 67 58 67 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 88 104 88 104 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 182 187 182 187 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 264 266 264 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 267 269 267 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 353 355 353 355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 404 409 404 409 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 423 432 423 432 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 443 459 443 459 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 546 551 546 551 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 656 665 653 662 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 819 824 816 821 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 876 881 873 878 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 88 103 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 443 458 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
###xml 689 695 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
The transcription profile of the mce3 operon in different in vitro growth conditions of M. tuberculosis has been addressed by RT-PCR in a number of publications and transcription of mce3 genes has been found when bacteria were cultured both on LJ and Dubos media [11,12] but not in 7H9 synthetic medium in both exponential and stationary growth phases [10]. Using similar methodology we detected mRNA of mce3 operon during in vitro culture of M. tuberculosis either in synthetic or in rich (data not shown) media. However, transcription from the mce3 promoter, measured as beta-galacosidase activity, was completely absent in the presence of Mce3R both in in vitro conditions and inside a murine macrophages cell line. These last results, together with the finding that the elimination of Mce3R significantly increases mce3 transcription, indicate that the potential level of mce3 expression is repressed in the conditions of growth assayed.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 39 43 39 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce </italic>
###xml 120 124 120 124 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce </italic>
###xml 186 191 186 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce2 </italic>
###xml 226 227 226 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 228 229 228 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 306 311 306 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 399 404 399 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 423 425 423 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
Interestingly, the high homology among mce operons is not conserved among their regulator genes, since Mce1R, the other mce regulator described, as well as the putative regulator of the mce2 operon, belong to the GntR family [9,1]. In addition, no putative regulatory gene is placed in the vicinity of the mce4 operon; however, it was recently proposed Rv3574, a TetR-type regulator, as represor of mce4 operon expression [23].
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 30 36 30 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 73 77 73 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1</italic>
###xml 79 84 79 84 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce2 </italic>
###xml 88 93 88 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
Here it was demonstrated that mce3R is not involved in the expression of mce1, mce2 nor mce4 operons. Therefore, it is tempting to speculate that both facts, i.e. gene redundancy and differential regulation, ensure the production of Mce proteins in different environments.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 26 31 26 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 57 63 57 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 77 80 77 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce</italic>
###xml 129 138 129 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
Here we demonstrated that mce3 promoter is stronger than mce3R promoter, and mce3 expression seems to be mostly shut down during in vitro growth, but it is also likely that under unknown particular conditions of growth it would turn on.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 143 149 143 149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 303 308 303 308 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 353 359 353 359 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 361 368 361 368 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yrbE3A </italic>
As it happens to many other proteins of the TetR family, we found that Mce3R negatively regulates its own expression. The experiments with the mce3R promoter indicate that there is in average ten folds decreased in transcriptional activity when the Mce3R is provided. In this condition, the activity of mce3 promoter is sixty times repressed. Since the mce3R - yrbE3A intergenic region spans 880 bp, it is likely that Mce3R binds to consensus motifs located between the divergent genes in both promoter regions.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 89 93 89 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce </italic>
###xml 312 317 312 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R</italic>
###xml 429 434 429 434 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
Although this study provides more insights to the role of Mce3R in the regulation of the mce operons, the information is still limited and further studies are necessary to detect any other gene regulated by this system. Elucidation of other promoters targeted by Mce3R will lead to the definition of a consensus mce3R-binding site and the possibility of define the Mce3R-regulon. Thus, one could hope to decipher the function of mce3 genes by the identification of the Mce3R regulon.
###end p 45
###begin title 46
Conclusion
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 95 100 95 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 107 116 107 116 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 180 184 180 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce1</italic>
###xml 186 191 186 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce2 </italic>
###xml 195 200 195 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce4 </italic>
###xml 215 224 215 224 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro </italic>
###xml 235 250 235 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis</italic>
###xml 388 393 388 393 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3 </italic>
###xml 235 250 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
The available evidence demonstrates that while Mce3R represses powerfully the transcription of mce3 operon in vitro and inside macrophages, it does not affect the transcription of mce1, mce2 and mce4 operons during in vitro culture of M. tuberculosis. It was also demonstrated that Mce3R negatively regulates its own expression but the level of expression is lower than that observed for mce3 operon.
###end p 47

###begin title 51
General DNA methodology
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 86 88 86 88 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 204 205 204 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>
###xml 273 275 273 275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>

PCR amplifications from genomic DNA templates were performed as previously described [13]. Each primer contained base mismatches that introduced a restriction site suitable for directional cloning (Table 2). Chromosomal DNA samples were obtained as described vanSoolingen [24]. Purification of plasmids and DNA fragments were performed using the GFX Micro Plasmid Prep Kit (GE Healthcare) and DNA and Gel Band Purification Kit (GE Healthcare), respectively, according to the manufacturer's instructions. Plasmid pYUB178-lacZ was created by insertion of the beta-galactosidase gene from plasmid pMC1871 (AmershamPharmacia) into HindIII and NheI sites of pYUB178 mycobacterial integrative vector [25]. M. tuberculosis H37Rv and M. tuberculosis Deltamce3R (see below) were transformed by electroporation, as described by Parish and Stoker [26].

Construction of M. tuberculosis Deltamce3R mutant strain
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 28 34 28 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 101 117 101 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 217 220 217 220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Not</italic>
###xml 240 242 240 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 269 275 269 275 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 393 397 393 397 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Hind</italic>
###xml 418 423 418 423 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R</italic>
###xml 489 492 489 492 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Pac</italic>
###xml 634 636 634 636 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 711 714 711 714 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Eco</italic>
###xml 859 875 859 875 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 880 885 876 881 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R</italic>
###xml 101 122 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
###xml 859 874 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
A genomic region containing mce3R and about 2 kb flanking 5' and 3' regions was obtained by PCR from M. tuberculosis H37Rv total DNA by using primers: upMutReg and lowMutReg. The amplified fragment was cloned in site NotI of p2NIL plasmid [21] and the mutant allele of mce3R was generated by inserting a cassette conferring hygromycin resistance from pUC-Hy7 (AmershamPharmacia) into a unique HindIII site internal to mce3R. The final delivery vector was generated by incorporation of the PacI cassette from pGOAL 17 into this last p2NIL recombinant vector. Mutants were constructed using a two-step strategy as described previously [21]. Chromosomal DNA was prepared from the selected clones and digested with EcoRI and then analyzed by Southern blotting by using the wild-type gene as probe. The mutant strain resulting from allelic exchange was designated M. tuberculosis Deltamce3R.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 26 32 26 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 66 72 66 72 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 76 83 76 83 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Rv1964 </italic>
###xml 204 210 204 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R </italic>
###xml 281 284 281 284 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Eco</italic>
###xml 291 294 291 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bam</italic>
###xml 321 323 321 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 390 406 390 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 502 507 494 499 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R</italic>
###xml 509 514 501 506 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce3R</italic>
###xml 390 405 <span type="species:ncbi:1773">M. tuberculosis</span>
DNA fragment encompassing mce3R and the intergenic region between mce3R and Rv1964 was PCR amplified with primers: mce3R-P3up and P3rev and cloned into TOPO 2.1 vector (Invitrogen). A fragment containing mce3R and its promoter was released from this last plasmid by digestion with EcoRI and BamHI and cloned into pSUM41 [27] to produce plasmid pSummce3R. This plasmid was used to transform M. tuberculosis Deltamce3R strain by electroporation. The resulting complemented strain was referred to as Deltamce3R::mce3R.
###end p 57
###begin title 58
RNA preparation
###end title 58
###begin p 59
###xml 169 172 169 172 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-1 </sup>
###xml 350 352 349 351 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g </italic>
Culture pellets of 50 ml were resuspended in 1 ml of TRIzol (Invitrogen). Cells were disrupted using a Fastprep FP120 bead-beater (Savant) for 20 s at a speed of 6.0 m s-1 with Lysing Matrix B (Q-Biogene). Then, 200 mul of chloroform (Merck) was added, and the mixture was incubated for 15 min at room temperature. Tubes were centrifuged at 10,000 x g for 15 min at 4degreesC, and the supernatant was extracted again with 100 mul of chloroform and alcohol precipitated with 600 mul of isopropanol and 60 mul of 3 M ammonium acetate (pH 5.3) at -70degreesC overnight. Pellets were washed with 75% ethanol and resuspended in 50 mul of diethyl pyrocarbonate-treated water (Sigma-Aldrich). The RNA preparations were treated with DNase amplified grade (Invitrogen).
###end p 59
###begin title 60
RT-Q-PCR
###end title 60
###begin p 61
###xml 79 95 78 94 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">M. tuberculosis </italic>
###xml 79 100 <span type="species:ncbi:83332">M. tuberculosis H37Rv</span>
DNA-free RNA (1 mug) extracted from middle logarithmic-phase culture of either M. tuberculosis H37Rv or Deltamce3R was mixed with 50 ng of random primers (Invitrogen) in 20 mul of final volume and reversely transcribed to total cDNA with SuperScript III reverse Transcriptase (Invitrogen) following the manufacturer's instructions. Identical reactions lacking reverse transcriptase were also performed to confirm the absence of genomic DNA in all samples.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 219 220 218 219 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 399 401 398 400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 444 447 443 446 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mce</italic>
###xml 477 482 476 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sigA </italic>
###xml 625 627 624 626 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 732 737 731 736 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">slope</italic>
###xml 728 738 727 737 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">[-1/<italic>slope</italic>]</sup>
Q-PCR was performed in the Applied Biosystems 7000 DNA sequence detection system (Perkin-Elmer Corp.), by using Master Mix QuantiTect SYBR Green (Qiagen), 1 mul of template cDNA and the pairs of primers listed on Table 1. Each reaction was performed in duplicate. Results were presented as ratios calculated with the Relative expression software tool (REST@) application described by Plaffl et al. [28]. Relative quantification of each target (mce) gene was performed by using sigA as reference gene and a subsequent test for significance of derived results was performed by using Pair Wise Fixed Reallocation Randomisation [29]. The value of PCR efficiency for all transcripts was 2, as calculated following the formula: E = 10[-1/slope].

DNA fragments encompassing the intergenic region between mce3R and operon mce3, either containing or not containing the coding sequence of mce3R, were generated by PCR amplification with the pairs of primers upP3mce3Rint/P3mce3Rrevint and upP3int/P3revint, respectively.
