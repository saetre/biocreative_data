The Cytoskeletal Protein alpha-Actinin Regulates Acid-sensing Ion Channel 1a through a C-terminal Interaction*

###begin p 4
###xml 679 685 671 677 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">versus</italic>
The acid-sensing ion channel 1a (ASIC1a) is widely expressed in central and peripheral neurons where it generates transient cation currents when extracellular pH falls. ASIC1a confers pH-dependent modulation on postsynaptic dendritic spines and has critical effects in neurological diseases associated with a reduced pH. However, knowledge of the proteins that interact with ASIC1a and influence its function is limited. Here, we show that alpha-actinin, which links membrane proteins to the actin cytoskeleton, associates with ASIC1a in brain and in cultured cells. The interaction depended on an alpha-actinin-binding site in the ASIC1a C terminus that was specific for ASIC1a versus other ASICs and for alpha-actinin-1 and -4. Co-expressing alpha-actinin-4 altered ASIC1a current density, pH sensitivity, desensitization rate, and recovery from desensitization. Moreover, reducing alpha-actinin expression altered acid-activated currents in hippocampal neurons. These findings suggest that alpha-actinins may link ASIC1a to a macromolecular complex in the postsynaptic membrane where it regulates ASIC1a activity.

###xml 796 798 796 798 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref13">13</xref>
###xml 928 929 928 929 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 994 995 994 995 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref5">5</xref>
###xml 997 998 997 998 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref7">7</xref>
Acid-sensing ion channels (ASICs)2 are H+-gated members of the DEG/ENaC family (1-3). Members of this family contain cytosolic N and C termini, two transmembrane domains, and a large cysteine-rich extracellular domain. ASIC subunits combine as homo- or heterotrimers to form cation channels that are widely expressed in the central and peripheral nervous systems (1-4). In mammals, four genes encode ASICs, and two subunits, ASIC1 and ASIC2, have two splice forms, a and b. Central nervous system neurons express ASIC1a, ASIC2a, and ASIC2b (5-7). Homomeric ASIC1a channels are activated when extracellular pH drops below 7.2, and half-maximal activation occurs at pH 6.5-6.8 (8-10). These channels desensitize in the continued presence of a low extracellular pH, and they can conduct Ca2+ (9, 11-13). ASIC1a is required for acid-evoked currents in central nervous system neurons; disrupting the gene encoding ASIC1a eliminates H+-gated currents unless extracellular pH is reduced below pH 5.0 (5, 7).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 140 141 140 141 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref5">5</xref>
###xml 143 145 143 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref14">14</xref>
###xml 147 149 147 149 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref15">15</xref>
###xml 187 193 187 193 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ASIC1a</italic>
###xml 357 358 357 358 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref5">5</xref>
###xml 360 362 360 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref16">16</xref>
###xml 541 543 541 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref17">17</xref>
###xml 544 546 544 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref19">19</xref>
###xml 708 710 708 710 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref20">20</xref>
###xml 712 714 712 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref21">21</xref>
###xml 882 884 882 884 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref15">15</xref>
###xml 886 888 886 888 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref22">22</xref>
###xml 890 892 890 892 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref23">23</xref>
###xml 1039 1041 1039 1041 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref24">24</xref>
###xml 1054 1056 1054 1056 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1126 1128 1126 1128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref25">25</xref>
###xml 199 203 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Previous studies found ASIC1a enriched in synaptosomal membrane fractions and present in dendritic spines, the site of excitatory synapses (5, 14, 15). Consistent with this localization, ASIC1a null mice manifested deficits in hippocampal long term potentiation, learning, and memory, which suggested that ASIC1a is required for normal synaptic plasticity (5, 16). ASICs might be activated during neurotransmission when synaptic vesicles empty their acidic contents into the synaptic cleft or when neuronal activity lowers extracellular pH (17-19). Ion channels, including those at the synapse often interact with multiple proteins in a macromolecular complex that incorporates regulators of their function (20, 21). For ASIC1a, only a few interacting proteins have been identified. Earlier work indicated that ASIC1a interacts with another postsynaptic scaffolding protein, PICK1 (15, 22, 23). ASIC1a also has been reported to interact with annexin II light chain p11 through its cytosolic N terminus to increase cell surface expression (24) and with Ca2+/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II to phosphorylate the channel (25). However, whether ASIC1a interacts with additional proteins and with the cytoskeleton remain unknown. Moreover, it is not known whether such interactions alter ASIC1a function.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 275 277 267 269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref26">26</xref>
###xml 523 525 507 509 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref26">26</xref>
###xml 526 528 510 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref28">28</xref>
###xml 835 837 811 813 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref29">29</xref>
###xml 838 840 814 816 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref35">35</xref>
###xml 998 1000 970 972 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1041 1043 1013 1015 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref36">36</xref>
###xml 1044 1046 1016 1018 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref38">38</xref>
###xml 1167 1169 1135 1137 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 1181 1182 1149 1150 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1211 1213 1179 1181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref39">39</xref>
###xml 1214 1216 1182 1184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref41">41</xref>
In analyzing the ASIC1a amino acid sequence, we identified cytosolic residues that might bind alpha-actinins. alpha-Actinins cluster membrane proteins and signaling molecules into macromolecular complexes and link membrane proteins to the actincytoskeleton (for review, Ref. 26). Four genes encode alpha-actinin-1, -2, -3, and -4 isoforms. alpha-Actinins contain an N-terminal head domain that binds F-actin, a C-terminal region containing two EF-hand motifs, and a central rod domain containing four spectrin-like motifs (26-28). The C-terminal portion of the rod segment appears to be crucial for binding to membrane proteins. The alpha-actinins assemble into antiparallel homodimers through interactions in their rod domain. alpha-Actinins-1, -2, and -4 are enriched in dendritic spines, concentrating at the postsynaptic membrane (29-35). In the postsynaptic membrane of excitatory synapses, alpha-actinin connects the NMDA receptor to the actin cytoskeleton, and this interaction is key for Ca2+-dependent inhibition of NMDA receptors (36-38). alpha-Actinins can also regulate the membrane trafficking and function of several cation channels, including L-type Ca2+ channels, K+ channels, and TRP channels (39-41).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 327 328 315 316 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 377 379 365 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref42">42</xref>
###xml 381 383 369 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref43">43</xref>
To better understand the function of ASIC1a channels in macromolecular complexes, we asked if ASIC1a associates with alpha-actinins. We were interested in the alpha-actinins because they and ASIC1a, both, are present in dendritic spines, ASIC1a contains a potential alpha-actinin binding sequence, and the related epithelial Na+ channel (ENaC) interacts with the cytoskeleton (42, 43). Therefore, we hypothesized that alpha-actinin interacts structurally and functionally with ASIC1a.
###end p 10

Expression Constructs-Mouse ASIC1a was cloned into pMT3 (44) for heterologous expression. Human alpha-actinin-1 was a gift of C. Otey, University of North Carolina, and alpha-actinin-2, -3, and -4 were gifts of M. Sheng, Massachusetts Institute of Technology. Full-length human alpha-actinin-1-4 were cloned into pEGFP vectors (Clontech Laboratories) to generate enhanced green fluorescent protein (EGFP)-alpha-actinin fusion proteins; previous studies showed that the localization and function of alpha-actinins were unaffected by GFP tags (45-47). A dominant-negative N-terminal-truncated (missing amino acid 3-249) human alpha-actinin-1 (rod-actinin) (48) was a gift of A. Huttenlocher, University of Wisconsin. Rod-actinin contains the central alpha-actinin spectrin domain including the C terminus but lacks the N-terminal head domain. Dimers containing rodactinin can bind other proteins, but the absence of the head domain interrupts cross-linking to the filamentous actin cytoskeleton (48, 49). Rod-actinin would be expected to interfere with all alpha-actinins, and consistent with that conclusion, it disrupted the effect of alpha-actinins in human embryonic kidney cells, which express both alpha-actinin-1 and -4.3 Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells also express alpha-actinin-1 and alpha-actinin-4 (50). HA-tagged ASIC1a was made by adding the influenza hemagglutinin epitope (YPYDVPDYAGV) to the N terminus of ASIC1a.

Cell Culture and Transfection-For patch clamp studies CHO cells were transfected with 1-6 mug of DNA using the TransFast Lipidtrade mark reagent (Promega Madison, WI) and cultured on glass coverslips in 35-mm Petri dishes. To identify transfected cells using epifluorescence, we used EGFP at an ASIC1a:EGFP ratio of 6:1. Channel and alpha-actinin constructs were transfected at 1:1 ratio. Empty vector DNA was used to maintain a constant final DNA concentration for all transfections. Experiments were performed at room temperature 2-3 days after transfection.

Immunoprecipitation and Immunoblotting-Whole mouse brains from adult animals were homogenized in ice-cold buffer (5 ml/brain) containing 300 mm sucrose, 10 mm Tris (pH 7.5), 20 mm NaCl plus protease inhibitors (Complete Mini, EDTA-free, Roche Applied Science) using a glass Teflon homogenizer. Samples were spun for 2 min in a tabletop centrifuge at 2000 x g to remove large debris. Supernatants were centrifuged for 30 min at 135,000 x g in a Beckman TLA100.3 rotor at 4 degreesC. The membrane pellet was washed once on ice and then solubilized in 6 ml of 50 mm Tris (pH 7.5), 150 mm NaCl, protease inhibitors (Complete Mini) with 1% Triton X-100. For immunoprecipitation, samples were centrifuged (10 min, 700 x g) to remove particulate debris and precleared by the addition of protein G-agarose (Roche Applied Science, 50 mul, 15 mg/ml). Either undiluted affinity-purified ASIC1 antibody (raised in rabbits against the 22-amino acid peptide from the C terminus of ASIC1 and purified as described previously (16)) or a mouse monoclonal alpha-actinin antibody BM-75.2 that binds to all four actinin isoforms (1 mul, Sigma-Aldrich) was added to 750 mul of protein extract and incubated for 3 h while rotating at 4 degreesC. For precipitation, protein G-agarose beads (Roche Applied Science, 50 mul 15 mg/ml) were added, and samples were rotated overnight at 4 degreesC. After centrifugation, 3 wash steps were performed to clear samples (buffer 1: 50 mm Tris (pH 7.5), 150 mm NaCl, 1% Nonidet P-40, 0.5% sodium deoxycolate, protease inhibitors (Complete Mini); buffer 2: 50 mm Tris (pH 7.5), 500 mm NaCl, 0.1% Nonidet P-40, 0.05% sodium deoxycolate; buffer 3: 10 mm Tris (pH 7.5), 0.1% Nonidet P-40, 0.05% sodium deoxycolate). Bound proteins were extracted at 95 degreesC in SDS sample buffer (0.125 mm Tris (pH 7.5), 3.4% SDS, 17% glycerol, 67 mm dithiothreitol, 0.008% bromphenol blue) for 10 min and were resolved by SDS-PAGE and immunoblotted with the indicated antibodies.

###begin p 24
###xml 0 43 0 43 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ASIC1a Contains a Putative Binding Site for</italic>
###xml 49 57 45 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-Actinin</italic>
###xml 296 302 288 294 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1</xref>
###xml 305 307 297 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref38">38</xref>
###xml 309 311 301 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref51">51</xref>
###xml 313 315 305 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref52">52</xref>
ASIC1a Contains a Putative Binding Site for alpha-Actinin-In the C terminus of ASIC1a, we identified an amino acid motif that resembled sequences in the cytosolic portions of the NMDA receptor subunit 1 and the P2X7 purinoceptor, two ion channels known to physically interact with alpha-actinin (Fig. 1) (38, 51, 52). The potential alpha-actinin-binding site in ASIC1a lies in the cytosolic portion of the subunit and would, therefore, be accessible for interaction with other cytoskeletal proteins. The sequence was not present in ASIC2 or ASIC3, suggesting that alpha-actinin might specifically interact with ASIC1. Because ASIC1a and -1b splice variants share the same C terminus, interactions would be predicted to occur with both subunits.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 0 22 0 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ASIC1a Associates with</italic>
###xml 28 42 24 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-Actinin-1 and</italic>
###xml 48 58 40 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-Actinin-4</italic>
###xml 283 289 259 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2</xref>
ASIC1a Associates with alpha-Actinin-1 and alpha-Actinin-4-To test the hypothesis that ASIC1a and alpha-actinin interact, we expressed ASIC1a with each of the four alpha-actinin isoforms in COS-7 cells. Immunoprecipitating ASIC1a co-precipitated alpha-actinin-1 and alpha-actinin-4 (Fig. 2). Conversely, precipitating alpha-actinin-1 or alpha-actinin-4 co-precipitated ASIC1a. ASIC1a weakly co-precipitated alpha-actinin-3, but not the converse, and we did not detect an interaction with alpha-actinin-2. These data suggest that ASIC1a resides in close proximity with at least two of the actinins, alpha-actinin-1 and alpha-actinin-4.

alpha-Actinin Influences ASIC1a Current Density-To learn whether alpha-actinin also regulates the function of ASIC1a, we co-expressed them and measured currents. alpha-Actinin-4 reduced ASIC1a current density, whereas alpha-actinin-1 had no effect (Fig. 5, A and B). These results suggest that even though both alpha-actinins interact with ASIC1a, they have selective functional effects. As an additional test of the effect of alpha-actinin, we co-expressed ASIC1a with rod-actinin, a dominant-negative construct (48, 49). We found that the dominant-negative rod-actinin fragment had the opposite effect of alpha-actinin-4 and increased current density (Fig. 5, A and B).

###begin p 37
###xml 184 187 180 183 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mut</sub>
###xml 315 318 307 310 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mut</sub>
###xml 363 364 355 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A</italic>
###xml 369 370 361 362 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B</italic>
###xml 355 370 347 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Fig. 5, <italic>A</italic> and <italic>B</italic></xref>
###xml 464 467 448 451 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mut</sub>
Finding that rod-actinin increased current amplitude suggested that it disrupted an interaction between ASIC1a and an endogenous alpha-actinin. Such an interaction predicts that ASIC1amut, which did not interact with alpha-actinin, would have a greater current density than ASIC1a. Consistent with this idea, ASIC1amut generated more current than ASIC1a (Fig. 5, A and B). In addition, alpha-actinin-1 and alpha-actinin-4 failed to alter current produced by ASIC1amut. These data also suggest that the effect of alpha-actinin-4 on current results from a direct interaction with ASIC1a.

###begin p 38
###xml 5 54 1 50 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">-Actinin Alters the Properties of ASIC1a Currents</italic>

###xml 225 228 219 222 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">des</sub>
###xml 231 237 225 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Fig. 7</xref>
###xml 300 303 290 293 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mut</sub>
###xml 358 361 346 349 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">des</sub>
After acid-evoked activation, ASIC1a currents desensitize in the continued presence of acid (Fig. 5A). We found that neither alpha-actinin-1 nor -4 coexpression altered the time constant of ASIC1a current desensitization (taudes) (Fig. 7). However, disrupting the alpha-actinin-binding site in ASIC1amut or co-expressing rod-actinin with ASIC1a increased taudes. Moreover, disrupting the alpha-actinin binding motif prevented the effect of rod-actinin. These data suggest that alpha-actinin can alter the desensitization rate of ASIC1a channels, an effect that is mediated in part by interactions between ASIC1a and ubiquitously expressed alpha-actinins.
###end p 45

###xml 108 109 108 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref8">8</xref>

###xml 376 377 372 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B</italic>
###xml 370 377 366 373 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig8" ref-type="fig">Fig. 8<italic>B</italic></xref>
###xml 507 510 499 502 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mut</sub>
ASIC1a currents also show a characteristic time-dependent recovery from desensitization in acidic solution (8, 10). We activated and desensitized ASIC1a currents with a 60-s exposure to pH 6.0 solution (Fig. 8A). Then, after varying time intervals at pH 7.4, we re-exposed the channels to pH 6.0. Co-expressing alpha-actinin-4 accelerated recovery from desensitization (Fig. 8B). Conversely, co-expressing a dominant-negative rod-actinin with ASIC1a or disrupting the alpha-actinin binding motif with ASIC1amut slowed the rate of recovery.

###begin p 49
###xml 264 267 256 259 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mut</sub>
These results indicate that an association with alpha-actinin-4 altered several characteristics of ASIC1a channel function. They also implicate the alpha-actinin binding motif in the ASIC1a C terminus. The effects of the dominant-negative rod domain and the ASIC1amut variant suggest that ASIC1a interacts with alpha-actinins that are endogenous in the heterologous cells we used to express ASIC1a. Therefore, in the studies described below we tested for an effect of endogenous alpha-actinins.

###xml 762 770 734 742 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig10" ref-type="fig">Fig. 10<italic>B</italic></xref>
alpha-Actinin Regulates Hippocampal Acid-activated Currents-In the brain ASIC1a has been implicated in learning and memory, and an association of ASIC1a with the cytoskeletal linker protein alpha-actinin could be one mechanism underlying synaptic plasticity. Our data suggested that in heterologous cells endogenous alpha-actinins regulate ASIC currents; therefore, we asked if this was the case in neurons. We used RT-PCR to learn which alpha-actinin genes are expressed in the hippocampus and found alpha-actinin-1, -2, and -4 but not alpha-actinin-3 (Fig. 10A). To reduce their expression, we constructed vectors encoding siRNAs directed against each alpha-actinin isoform. The efficacy and specificity of the RNA interference were shown by Western blotting (Fig. 10B). With this information, for subsequent studies we reduced alpha-actinin expression by co-transfecting primary cultures of hippocampal neurons with a mix of the siRNAs against alpha-actinin-1, -2, and -4 (RNAi to alpha-actinin-1, -2, and -4). As controls, we used either siRNA directed against alpha-actinin-3 or dsRed.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 104 105 104 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref7">7</xref>
###xml 107 109 107 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref53">53</xref>
###xml 111 113 111 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref54">54</xref>
###xml 230 231 226 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">C</italic>
###xml 223 231 219 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig10" ref-type="fig">Fig. 10<italic>C</italic></xref>
###xml 324 325 320 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">D</italic>
###xml 317 325 313 321 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig10" ref-type="fig">Fig. 10<italic>D</italic></xref>
###xml 447 448 443 444 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E</italic>
###xml 440 448 436 444 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig10" ref-type="fig">Fig. 10<italic>E</italic></xref>
###xml 626 629 620 623 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">des</sub>
###xml 723 724 713 714 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">F</italic>
###xml 716 724 706 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="fig10" ref-type="fig">Fig. 10<italic>F</italic></xref>
Acid-evoked currents in hippocampal neurons are produced by a mixture of ASIC1a, -2a, and -2b subunits (7, 53, 54). RNAi against alpha-actinin-1, -2, and -4 increased endogenous ASIC current density in hippocampal neurons (Fig. 10C). In addition, it reduced the pH sensitivity of hippocampal acid-activated currents (Fig. 10D). Although the effects were small, it also slowed the rate of recovery from desensitization within the first 2 s (Fig. 10E). These results are consistent with our findings in a heterologous system expressing ASIC1a alone. In contrast to our results with ASIC1a homomultimers studied in CHO cells, taudes of hippocampal ASIC currents were not affected by reducing endogenous alpha-actinins (Fig. 10F).

###begin title 59
DISCUSSION
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 91 93 87 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref29">29</xref>
###xml 94 96 90 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref35">35</xref>
###xml 193 194 189 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref5">5</xref>
###xml 196 198 192 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref14">14</xref>
###xml 200 202 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref15">15</xref>
Previous studies localized alpha-actinin to dendritic spines and the postsynaptic density (29-35). Likewise, ASIC1a localized to dendritic spines and was enriched in the postsynaptic membrane (5, 14, 15). Those earlier studies positioned alpha-actinin-4 and ASIC1a where they could interact. In this study, we now show physical and functional links between these two proteins both in heterologous cells and in neurons. The data also indicate that the ASIC1a-alpha-actinin interaction occurred through a motif in the ASIC1a C terminus. This interaction appears to be specific, as the alpha-actinin binding motif is not conserved in ASIC2 or -3. In addition, ASIC1a associated with alpha-actinin-1 and -4 but not with alpha-actinin-2 or -3. These results suggest specificity in the interaction between these two families of proteins.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 232 234 224 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref55">55</xref>
###xml 236 238 228 230 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref56">56</xref>
###xml 372 374 360 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref21">21</xref>
###xml 376 378 364 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref33">33</xref>
###xml 380 382 368 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref57">57</xref>
###xml 505 507 489 491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref21">21</xref>
###xml 531 533 515 517 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref25">25</xref>
###xml 649 651 629 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref38">38</xref>
###xml 653 655 633 635 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref58">58</xref>
###xml 662 668 642 648 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">et al.</italic>
###xml 670 672 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref25">25</xref>
###xml 708 710 688 690 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 875 876 855 856 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref5">5</xref>
###xml 894 896 870 872 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref37">37</xref>
###xml 915 917 891 893 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref58">58</xref>
###xml 927 929 903 905 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref59">59</xref>
The association with alpha-actinins likely incorporates ASIC1a into a postsynaptic macromolecular signaling complex. alpha-Actinins bind actin and may thereby link ASIC1a to the predominant cytoskeletal element of dendritic spines (55, 56). The C termini of alpha-actinins also contain PDZ binding motifs that attach to several scaffolding proteins, including densin-180 (21, 33, 57). Thus, alpha-actinin may tie ASIC1a to multiple other proteins to form a signaling complex in the postsynaptic membrane (21). A potential example (25) is the functional coupling between ASIC1a and NMDA receptors; NMDA receptors are also regulated by alpha-actinin (38, 58). Gao et al. (25) suggested that during acidosis, Ca2+/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II is recruited to NMDA receptors where it phosphorylates ASIC1a, thereby increasing its pH sensitivity. Interestingly, ASIC1a (5), alpha-actinin (37), NMDA receptors (58), actin (59), and many of their associated components contribute to synaptic plasticity.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 342 343 330 331 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 691 692 671 672 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 744 745 724 725 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 840 843 816 819 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mut</sub>
###xml 1126 1127 1090 1091 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 1329 1330 1293 1294 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref7">7</xref>
###xml 1516 1517 1476 1477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref4">4</xref>
###xml 1519 1521 1479 1481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref60">60</xref>
###xml 1522 1524 1482 1484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref62">62</xref>
The mechanism by which alpha-actinin-4 reduced current density appears to be a direct effect on channel function. This conclusion is supported by the finding that alpha-actinin-4 altered multiple characteristics of the current without changing the amount of ASIC1a on the cell surface. In CHO cells, overexpressing alpha-actinin-4 increased H+-evoked current density and increased pH sensitivity, whereas we saw the opposite effect when we expressed the dominant-negative rod-actinin or mutated the alpha-actinin interaction motif in ASIC1a. Likewise, in neurons, knockdown of alpha-actinin increased current density and reduced pH sensitivity. However, there were some differences between H+-gated currents in CHO cells expressing ASIC1a and H+-gated currents in neurons. In CHO cells alpha-actinin-4 accelerated and rod-actinin and ASIC1amut slowed recovery from desensitization, whereas alpha-actinin siRNA had minor effects on recovery from desensitization in neurons. Moreover, the interaction with alpha-actinin altered the desensitization rate of ASIC1a currents in CHO cells, but alpha-actinin siRNA had no effect on H+-activated current in neurons. We suspect these differences result from the fact that in CHO cells we studied ASIC1a homomeric channels, whereas in neurons much of the ASIC1a exists in heteromultimers (7). The conclusion that alpha-actinin-4 increased pH sensitivity in both cases is interesting, because pH sensitivity is a property usually associated with the ASIC extracellular domain (4, 60-62). Therefore, we speculate that the alpha-actinin binding motif in the ASIC1a intracellular C terminus somehow links ASIC1a to structures that control gating and pH sensitivity.
###end p 62

alpha-Actinin regulated H+-gated current in hippocampal neurons.A, rat hippocampal neurons were tested for the expression of endogenous alpha-actinins. RT-PCR with isoform-specific primer pairs revealed expression of alpha-actinin-1, -2, and -4, whereas transcription of alpha-actinin-3 was not detected. Muscle from the same donor contained transcripts for all four alpha-actinin isoforms including alpha-actinin-3. In negative control experiments (-RT) reverse transcriptase was omitted, but the specific primers pairs for all four alpha-actinin isoforms were present. B, to assess the efficacy and specificity of siRNA, CHO cells were co-transfected with EGFP-tagged alpha-actinin-1,-2,-3, or -4 and isoform-specific siRNAs. Western blot was probed with either anti-GFP or anti-tubulin antibody. Each of the four siRNAs attenuated expression of its corresponding alpha-actinin isoform. C-F, reduction of endogenous alpha-actinin affected properties of acid-activated current in hippocampal neurons. For controls, neurons were transfected either with vector encoding dsRed or siRNA directed against alpha-actinin-3. C, current density was increased by simultaneous knockdown of alpha-actinin-1, -2, and -4 (n = 23-47, *, p < 0.02). D, pH sensitivity was decreased by knockdown of endogenous alpha-actinin-1, -2, and -4 (blacksquare, square, filled, pH50 = 6.16, n = 9-36; *, p < 0.05) compared with controls (dsRed (o), pH50 = 6.30, n = 7-21 and RNAi to alpha-actinin-3 (triangle up), pH50 = 6.36, n = 17-28). I indicates peak current amplitude at given pH, and IpH5.0 indicates peak current amplitude at pH 5.0. *, p < 0.05. E, time-dependent recovery of desensitized current evoked by pH 6.0 (RNAi to alpha-actinin-1, -2, and -4 (blacksquare, square, filled), n = 5-11 and RNAi to alpha-actinin-3 (triangle up), n = 3-11; *, p < 0.05). ###xml 609 611 605 607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="ref24">24</xref>
alpha-Actinin is not the only protein that interacts with ASIC1a; PICK1 (protein interacting with C kinase), stomatin, and annexin II light chain p11 also bind the channel. In PICK1, a PDZ domain interacts with the ASIC1a C terminus, and the two proteins resided in dendrites, probably in postsynaptic membranes (15, 22). Stomatin associated with ASIC1a in heterologous systems, although the site of interaction, remains unknown (63). Neither PICK1 nor stomatin altered the amplitude or properties of ASIC1a currents. The annexin II light chain p11 associated with an undefined site in the ASIC1a N terminus (24). In heterologous cells, it increased cell surface expression of ASIC1a and increased ASIC1a current amplitude without altering the characteristics of the current. Thus, of the proteins known to associate with ASIC1a, only alpha-actinin affected its channel function.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
The connections between alpha-actinin and ASIC1a and their previously established distribution in the postsynaptic membrane position these proteins at a key site for influencing neuronal activity. Together, these observations suggest that alpha-actinins may have an important impact on ASIC1a function at this location.
###end p 66
