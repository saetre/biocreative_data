A physiological connection between tmRNA and peptidyl-tRNA hydrolase functions in Escherichia coli
###end article-title 0

The bacterial ssrA gene codes for a dual function RNA, tmRNA, which possesses tRNA-like and mRNA-like regions. The tmRNA appends an oligopeptide tag to the polypeptide on the P-site tRNA by a trans-translation process that rescues ribosomes stalled on the mRNAs and targets the aberrant protein for degradation. In cells, processing of the stalled ribosomes is also pioneered by drop-off of peptidyl-tRNAs. The ester bond linking the peptide to tRNA is hydrolyzed by peptidyl-tRNA hydrolase (Pth), an essential enzyme, which releases the tRNA and the aberrant peptide. As the trans-translation mechanism utilizes the peptidyl-transferase activity of the stalled ribosomes to free the tRNA (as opposed to peptidyl-tRNA drop-off), the need for Pth to recycle such tRNAs is bypassed. Thus, we hypothesized that tmRNA may rescue a defect in Pth. Here, we show that overexpression of tmRNA rescues the temperature-sensitive phenotype of Escherichia coli (pthts). Conversely, a null mutation in ssrA enhances the temperature-sensitive phenotype of the pthts strain. Consistent with our hypothesis, overexpression of tmRNA results in decreased accumulation of peptidyl-tRNA in E.coli. Furthermore, overproduction of tmRNA in E.coli strains deficient in ribosome recycling factor and/or lacking the release factor 3 enhances the rescue of pthts strains. We discuss the physiological relevance of these observations to highlight a major role of tmRNA in decreasing cellular peptidyl-tRNA load.

###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 207 208 207 208 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c1">1</xref>
###xml 209 210 209 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c2">2</xref>
###xml 465 466 465 466 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh924f1">1</xref>

###xml 478 479 478 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c5">5</xref>
###xml 599 600 599 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c6">6</xref>
###xml 637 638 637 638 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c7">7</xref>
###xml 639 641 639 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c10">10</xref>
###xml 660 666 660 666 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 928 930 928 930 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c11">11</xref>
###xml 1036 1038 1036 1038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c12">12</xref>
###xml 1039 1041 1039 1041 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c13">13</xref>
###xml 660 666 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
For various physiological reasons, not all ribosomes that begin to translate an mRNA reach the termination codon. A significant population of the translating ribosomes stalls in between the decoding cycles (1,2). Stalled ribosomes pose a serious impediment to mRNA translation as they deplete pools of free tRNA and ribosomes, bringing protein synthesis to a halt. One of the cellular strategies to process stalled ribosomes involves peptidyl-tRNA drop-off (Figure 1, upper) (3-5). While the mechanisms responsible for the drop-off of peptidyl-tRNAs from the ribosomes are not very well understood (6), genetic and biochemical evidence (7-10) suggests that in E.coli, ribosome recycling factor (RRF) and EFG, as well as other factors such as RF3, IF1, IF2 and RelA contribute to this phenomenon. The peptidyl-tRNAs are recycled by peptidyl-tRNA hydrolase (Pth), which hydrolyzes the ester link between the tRNA and the peptide (11). As accumulation of peptidyl-tRNAs leads to cellular toxicity, Pth is an essential protein in bacteria (12,13).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 102 107 102 107 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trans</italic>
###xml 121 123 121 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c14">14</xref>
###xml 146 150 146 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ssrA</italic>
###xml 571 573 571 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c15">15</xref>
###xml 727 733 727 733 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>

###xml 932 934 932 934 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c18">18</xref>
###xml 1168 1169 1168 1169 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh924f1">1</xref>
###xml 1283 1285 1283 1285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c19">19</xref>
###xml 1286 1288 1286 1288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh924c23">23</xref>
###xml 1291 1296 1291 1296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trans</italic>
###xml 1458 1464 1458 1464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 727 733 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 1458 1464 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Another cellular strategy that leads to the rescue of ribosomes stalled on fragmented mRNAs, involves trans-translation (14). Here the product of ssrA gene, tmRNA (also known as SsrA), is aminoacylated by alanyl-tRNA synthetase, and recruited to the ribosomal A site with the help of EFTu and an ancillary protein, SmpB. The peptidyl-transferase activity of the ribosome transfers the peptide from the P-site bound peptidyl-tRNA to the alanine on the -CCA end of the tmRNA. Consequently, the tRNA that was sequestered as peptidyl-tRNA in the stalled complex is released (15). The elongation process then resumes to translate a 30 nt long open reading frame (mRNA portion) of the tmRNA to encode the ANDENYALAA sequence tag (in E.coli). When the ribosomes reach the termination codon in the tmRNA, the C-terminally tagged peptide is released by the activity of release factors, and subjected to degradation by cellular proteases (16-18). Interestingly, as the tmRNA-mediated mechanism utilizes the peptidyl-transferase activity of the stalled ribosomes to liberate tRNA (as opposed to peptidyl-tRNA drop-off), the need for Pth to recycle such tRNAs is bypassed (Figure 1, lower). Since, a number of cellular processes result in accumulation of stalled ribosomes with fragmented mRNA (19-23), trans-translation may represent a major mechanism to recycle both the stalled ribosomes and the tRNAs. This raises an important question as to whether excess tmRNA in E.coli can alleviate the requirement for Pth activity.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 138 144 138 144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>

###xml 138 144 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 309 315 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
###xml 804 810 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Earlier studies have shown that RRF and RF3 are involved in peptidyl-tRNA drop-off from the ribosome. Thus, conditional lethal strains of E.coli, with a temperature-sensitive phenotype because of a mutation in pth could be rescued by downregulation of RRF expression (8). Conversely, overproduction of RRF in E.coli is toxic under conditions of limiting Pth activity (7). It was also observed that disruption of the prfC gene coding for RF3 resulted in suppression of the temperature-sensitive phenotype of a pthts strain. In vitro analyses suggested that disruption of prfC and downregulation of RRF result in decreased levels of peptidyl-tRNA drop-off (8). Also, the tRNALys appears to be one of the first species of tRNAs that is depleted by its sequestration as peptidyl-tRNA in the pthts strains of E.coli. Overproduction of this tRNA increases the temperature threshold at which the strains continue to grow (4,24).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 92 95 92 95 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pth</italic>
###xml 95 97 95 97 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ts</sup>
###xml 314 317 314 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pth</italic>
###xml 317 319 317 319 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ts</sup>
###xml 349 355 349 355 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli</italic>
###xml 408 411 408 411 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pth</italic>
###xml 349 355 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Here, we show that overproduction of tmRNA rescues the temperature-sensitive phenotype of a pthts strain and that a major cellular function of tmRNA is to lessen the peptidyl-tRNA load. Further, coupling of tmRNA overproduction with downregulation of RRF and depletion of RF3, results in a stronger suppression of pthts. However, the role of Pth in E.coli is central and it was not possible to dispense with pth function even upon integration of the known suppressors in a single strain.
###end p 7

To test the hypothesis if overproduction of tmRNA could rescue the pthts phenotype of E.coli AA7852, we introduced into this strain, various plasmid constructs (Table 1) by genetic transformation and checked for the growth of the transformants at different temperatures. As shown in Figure 2A, true to its genotype, the strain AA7852 harboring the pTrc99C vector alone grew at the permissive and semi-nonpermissive temperatures of 30 and 37degreesC, respectively but ceased to grow as the temperature was raised to 40degreesC (sectors 2). As a control, when the strain harbored a plasmid-borne copy of E.coli pth gene (pTrcEcoPth), it grew at 40degreesC (sectors 1). Interestingly, a plasmid-borne copy of ssrA gene (sectors 3) also rescued the growth of AA7852 at 40degreesC (compare sectors 2 and 3). Simultaneous presence of the extrachromosomal copy of smpB gene (coding for SmpB protein) along with ssrA (pTrc-ssrA-smpB) conferred no apparent advantage towards rescue by plasmid-borne ssrA (compare sectors 3 and 4), and was not analyzed any further. To examine the extent of tmRNA overproduction in the transformants, we analyzed the levels of tmRNA and 5S rRNA [Figure 2B, panels (i) and (ii)]. Based on the relative levels of tmRNA and 5S rRNA [Figure 2B, panel (iii)], in the transformants harboring plasmid-borne copies of ssrA (pTrc-ssrA or pTrc-ssrA-smpB), production of tmRNA was about 4.5-fold higher than the chromosomal background suggesting that a moderate level of overproduction of tmRNA lessens the severity of the pthts phenotype in E.coli.

In another approach to demonstrate that the chromosomally encoded tmRNA contributes to rescue of the pthts phenotype, we disrupted the ssrA gene in the E.coli AA7852 with Kn marker to generate E.coli AA7852ssrA::Kn strain. This procedure resulted in the complete absence of tmRNA in the AA7852ssrA::Kn strain [Figure 2B, panel (i)]. As seen in Figure 3A (sectors 3), the AA7852ssrA::Kn strain became hypersensitive to temperature and was not viable at 37degreesC, a temperature at which the parent strain AA7852 sustained good growth (Figure 2A, sector 2). Introduction of the plasmid-borne copy of ssrA into the AA7852ssrA::Kn strain, rescued its growth not only at 37degreesC but also at 40degreesC (Figure 3A, sectors 4) ruling out that lack of growth of AA7852ssrA::Kn strain at the semi-nonpermissive temperature was due to some non-specific effects emanating from the alterations at the chromosomal level. Further, as shown in Figure 3B, in liquid cultures also, while at 30degreesC, the AA7852ssrA::Kn strain harboring the vector alone, a plasmid-borne copy of ssrA or pth genes grew with similar rates; at 39degreesC, the AA7852ssrA::Kn strain harboring vector alone failed to show any significant growth. However, the growth of the strain at 39degreesC was rescued by the presence of a plasmid-borne copy of ssrA or pth. Expectedly, the rescue by pth was better than that by ssrA. In a control experiment, when the ssrA locus was disrupted in E.coli CP78 (pth+ isogenic strain of AA7852) in exactly the same manner, under the same conditions, no adverse effects of ssrA disruption were noticed (Figure 3A, compare sectors 1 and 2). Taken together, the observations in Figures 2 and 3 clearly suggest that ssrA is a suppressor of pthts phenotype in E.coli.

To understand the mechanism of tmRNA-mediated rescue of the pthts phenotype, we analyzed the effect of its overexpression on the levels of peptidyl-tRNAs (peptidyl-tRNAHis and peptidyl-tRNATyr) in the cells. To ensure preservation of the sensitive ester bond that links amino acid or the peptide to the tRNA, RNA was prepared under cold and acidic conditions, and analyzed using acid urea gels. Further, since the absolute levels of total tRNA vary in the preparations, to compare changes in the steady-state levels of peptidyl-tRNAs, their abundance as percent fraction of total tRNA (for tRNAHis or tRNATyr) was determined within each sample. As shown in Figure 4A, expectedly, at the permissive temperature of 30degreesC where the Pth is active, none of the transformants accumulate any significant levels of peptidyl-tRNAHis (lanes 1, 3 and 5). Expressing a wild-type copy of pth in AA7852, reduced the extent of accumulation of peptidyl-tRNAHis at 37degreesC to approximately11% (lane 2). Similar results were obtained with tRNATyr (Figure 4B). At 37degreesC, accumulation of tRNATyr as peptidyl-tRNATyr was 32% in transformants harboring vector alone (lane 4) which was decreased to approximately20% in transformants harboring the ssrA gene (lane 6). The level of peptidyl-tRNATyr in the transformants with plasmid-borne copy of wild-type pth at 37degreesC, was approximately9% (lane 2). With the identification of tmRNA as an additional suppressor of pth, there are now at least five factors (RRF, RF3, RelA, tRNALys and tmRNA) whose influence on Pth is well characterized in E.coli. Of these, RF3, RRF and RelA are responsible for increased drop-off of peptidyl-tRNA, and tmRNA leads to a decrease in the level of peptidyl-tRNA. On the other hand, tRNALys (in valU operon) functions at a step downstream of the peptidyl-tRNA release. These observations, together with the fact that both of the identifiable pth genes in Saccharomyces cerevisiae could be deleted, raise the question of whether the pth gene in E.coli can be dispensed with. To address this, we first integrated one or many of the conditions that lead to rescue of pthts, into E.coli CP79 (relA-). We also generated an E.coli strain wherein approximately0.1 kb segment of the chromosomal pth open reading frame was replaced with a kanamycin resistance marker (pth::Kn) in the presence of a helper plasmid harboring a pth gene of B.subtilis origin under the control of an E.coli promoter (Figure 7A). Phage P1 lysate was raised on this strain to attempt knockout of the pth gene in the E.coli CP79 derivatives by transduction. As shown in Table 2, a number of approximately1000 transductants on a Kn plate with a control strain harboring plasmid-borne pth gene of B.subtilis origin suggests a high efficiency of tranduction. However, despite many attempts, the number of transductants on the test strains, which lacked a pth support plasmid but into which various suppressors of pth had been integrated, was very low (Table 2). Further, PCR screening (Figure 7A) of the entire test transductants obtained, revealed that they either possessed an intact copy, or both an intact and a disrupted copy of the pth genes in their chromosomes (a representative gel is shown in Figure 7B, and data are summarized in Table 2). On the other hand, the control transductants (obtained in the presence of support plasmid) possessed only a disrupted copy of the E.coli pth gene (Figure 7B, lane 1). Use of several other strains of E.coli in such experiments yielded identical results (data not shown). Taken together, these observations suggest that pth plays a central role and is indispensable in E.coli.

In bacteria, the product of ssrA gene, SsrA is a well-conserved molecule of approximately360 nt in length. In E.coli, SsrA was originally identified as 10Sa RNA upon further fractionation of the 10S RNA (35). Subsequently, SsrA was shown to function both as tRNA and mRNA, hence the new name tmRNA (14). The 3' end of tmRNA terminates with a -CCA sequence to which alanine is ligated by alanyl-tRNA synthetase. The alanylated-tmRNA is recruited to the ribosomal A-sites of the stalled ribosomes for the process of trans-translation, which attaches an AANDENYALAA sequence (in E.coli) to the C-terminal of the incomplete peptides and directs the aberrant proteins for degradation by the cellular proteases (4,36-38). In this study, we have uncovered yet another facet of tmRNA function in decreasing the peptidyl-tRNA load in the cells.

Ribosomes may stall on either a full-length or a truncated mRNA. While the stalled complexes with truncated mRNAs (with no or very short 3' extensions) are direct and preferred substrates for tmRNA, even those with an intact mRNA may be directed to trans-translation following the action of codon-specific ribonuclease (19-22). The observation that in the wild-type ssrA background, overproduction of tmRNA raises the threshold of the temperature sensitivity of the pthts strain suggests that in the cells, there exists a population of stalled ribosomes, which is the substrate for trans-translation, but remains unprocessed by the available tmRNA. Consequently, this population of stalled ribosomes (which may possess either a truncated or intact mRNA) must be processed via alternate pathways resulting in dissociation of peptidyl-tRNAs from them, which in turn should be processed by Pth to recycle the tRNAs. Thus, we believe that rescue of the pthts strain AA7852, by overproduction of tmRNA is due to diversion of this population of the stalled ribosomes from dropping-off peptidyl-tRNAs to engaging them in trans-translation (Figure 1, lower). This will in turn, decrease the substrate load on Pth, and make the available Pth activity sufficient to process peptidyl-tRNAs that arise from other physiological processes. Further, although the exact mechanisms by which RRF and RF3 result in peptidyl-tRNA drop-off are not understood, it is clear that downregulation of RRF and/or absence of RF3 result in decreased peptidyl-tRNA load in the cell (7,8). Interestingly, as shown in Figure 6, overproduction of tmRNA in a strain harboring frr1 and DeltaprfC mutations in single or in combination, results in a synergistic effect on the rescue of the pthts phenotype. The synergism between the two mutations themselves and with ssrA suggests that the stalled ribosomes are channeled to utilization by all the three factors and, while the RRF and RF3 pathways rescue them by drop-off of peptidyl-tRNAs (Figure 1, upper), ssrA rescues them by trans-translation (Figure 1, lower).

Studies on mutants of E.coli and its phages have unraveled crucial aspects of the host's physiology (39). It was observed that rap (same as pth) mutation in E.coli resulted in defective growth of bacteriophage lambda (40,41). Interestingly, yet another independent study, reported a similar phenotype for the sip (same as ssrA) mutation in E.coli (42,43). In fact, at least in Neisseria gonorrhoeae, the ssrA is an essential gene (44). It will be interesting to investigate the status of peptidyl-tRNA/Pth in this organism.

The genetic and biochemical analyses of the suppressor mutations (bar mutations) in phage lambda that rescue the growth on rap (pth) mutants of E.coli have shown that the phage-encoded minigenes (wild-type bar regions, at least one of which is located in the imm region) are responsible for excess drop-off of the peptidyl-tRNAs and depleting the availability of tRNA for translation (45). Such peptidyl-tRNAs are recycled by Pth. Hence, the effect of Pth on phage growth is a direct one. Whether the effect of ssrA on phage growth is also a direct one (e.g. by trans-translation) is not clear. At least, the lack of tmRNA-mediated targeting of aberrant proteins to degradation is not responsible for the bacteriophage lambda growth defect in sip (ssrA) mutants (38). On the other hand, based on recent reports that the ribosomes with truncated mRNAs with no or very short 3' extensions are the real substrates for trans-translation by tmRNA (46,47) and that the minigene toxicity is more a consequence of frequent re-initiation events (48), it appears that the ssrA effect on bacteriophage lambda growth is an indirect one. The presence of ssrA most likely decreases peptidyl-tRNA load by engaging the stalled ribosomes into trans-translation (thereby withdrawing the chances of the factor-dependent or spontaneous processes that result in drop-off of peptidyl-tRNAs). This process, frees Pth to now hydrolyze the peptidyl-tRNAs arising out of the minigenes of the bacteriophage lambda, or other host activities. However, this interpretation does not rule out a direct role for ssrA in the bacteriophage growth. Recently, it has been shown that at least within certain codon contexts, much of the peptidyl-tRNA population arising from minigene expression remained in the ribosomes (49). Such 'stand-by' stalled ribosomal complexes can be further processed to become substrates for trans-translation (19-22). Therefore, it will be interesting to study the mechanism by which ssrA contributes to the lambdoid phage growth.

Finally, biochemical studies show that Saccharomyces cerevisiae possesses at least two distinct types of Pth activities similar to those found in bacteria and archaea. Recently, it was shown that deletions in S.cerevisiae of the two identifiable pth genes (individually or in combination) were viable (50,51). A large number of factors in E.coli (RRF, RF3, IF1, IF2, EFG, tmRNA, RelA, tRNALys, etc.) affect peptidyl-tRNA levels in cell. And, the fact that many of these have also been characterized as suppressors of Pth suggests that the role of Pth in E.coli (and possibly in other organisms) is central to the process of mRNA translation. On the other hand, the knowledge of a large number of suppressors that decrease the peptidyl-tRNA load in E.coli also makes it desirable to attempt to delete the only known pth gene in E.coli. Thus, we integrated many of the known suppressor markers in E.coli to attempt deletion of pth gene. However, our studies (Figure 7) clearly support the fact that the Pth activity is indeed an essential function. In fact, as has also been suggested by the authors of the S.cerevisiae study (51), there may well be several Pth-like activities in eukaryotes to recycle tRNAs. In rabbit reticulocyte lysates, one of the activities that recycles tRNAs from the peptidyl-tRNAs is a phosphodiesterase that cleaves between the C and A, the last two nucleotides in the tRNA (52). The tRNA is subsequently repaired by tRNA nucleotidyltransferase (53). Whether such Pth activity is present in yeast is not known. Certainly, the reaffirmation of the essentiality of Pth activity in E.coli is a good indicator of the presence of additional Pth activities in yeast.
