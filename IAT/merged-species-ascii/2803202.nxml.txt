###begin article-title 0
###xml 18 24 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
The repertoire of equine intestinal alpha-defensins
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Background
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 789 803 774 788 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Laurasiatheria</italic>
###xml 716 722 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 850 855 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 860 866 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 936 942 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 975 981 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
Defensins represent an important class of antimicrobial peptides. These effector molecules of the innate immune system act as endogenous antibiotics to protect the organism against infections with pathogenic microorganisms. Mammalian defensins are classified into three distinct sub-families (alpha-, beta- and theta-defensins) according to their specific intramolecular disulfide-bond pattern. The peptides exhibit an antimicrobial activity against a broad spectrum of microorganisms including bacteria and fungi. Alpha-Defensins are primarily synthesised in neutrophils and intestinal Paneth cells. They play a role in the pathogenesis of intestinal diseases and may regulate the flora of the intestinal tract. An equine intestinal alpha-defensin (DEFA1), the first characterised in the Laurasiatheria, shows a broad antimicrobial spectrum against human and equine pathogens. Here we report a first investigation of the repertoire of equine intestinal alpha-defensins. The equine genome was screened for putative alpha-defensin genes by using known alpha-defensin sequences as matrices. Based on the obtained sequence information, a set of oligonucleotides specific to the alpha-defensin gene-family was designed. The products generated by reverse-transcriptase PCR with cDNA from the small intestine as template were sub-cloned and numerous clones were sequenced.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Results
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 13 19 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
Thirty-eight equine intestinal alpha-defensin transcripts were determined. After translation it became evident that at least 20 of them may code for functional peptides. Ten transcripts lacked matching genomic sequences and for 14 alpha-defensin genes apparently present in the genome no appropriate transcript could be verified. In other cases the same genomic exons were found in different transcripts.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Conclusions
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 363 378 355 370 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Laurasiatheria </italic>
###xml 24 30 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 218 223 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 484 489 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
The large repertoire of equine alpha-defensins found in this study points to a particular importance of these peptides regarding animal health and protection from infectious diseases. Moreover, these findings make the horse an excellent species to study biological properties of alpha-defensins. Interestingly, the peptides were not found in other species of the Laurasiatheria to date. Comparison of the obtained transcripts with the genomic sequences in the current assembly of the horse (EquCab2.0) indicates that it is yet not complete and/or to some extent falsely assembled.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Background
###end title 7
###begin p 8
###xml 151 152 151 152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 275 276 275 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 492 493 492 493 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 511 512 511 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 531 532 531 532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 658 659 655 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 900 901 886 887 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 1052 1053 1038 1039 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 1063 1064 1049 1050 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 1089 1090 1075 1076 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1320 1322 1306 1308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 1323 1325 1309 1311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
Antimicrobial peptides are effector molecules of the innate immune system which provides the first line of defense against a wide variety of microbes [1]. These peptides act as endogenous antibiotics protecting the organism against infections with pathogenic microorganisms [2]. Antimicrobial peptides are synthesised by circulating phagocytic cells, leucocytes and epithelial cells of mucosal tissues. Defensins are an important class of antimicrobial peptides which can be found in plants [3], invertebrates [4] and vertebrates [5]. Defensins are cationic and cysteine-rich peptides with a molecular structure consisting of three antiparallel beta-sheets [2]. They contain six highly conserved cysteine residues forming characteristic intramolecular disulfide bonds. Mammalian defensins are classified into three distinct sub-families due to the disulfide array: alpha-, beta- und theta-defensins [6]. The peptides exhibit a direct antimicrobial activity against a broad spectrum of microorganisms including Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria [7], fungi [8] and enveloped viruses [9]. Defensins are thought to kill bacteria by an initial electrostatic interaction with the negatively charged phospholipids of the microbial cytoplasmatic membrane, followed by membrane permeabilisation and lysis of the microbes [10,11].
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 256 258 249 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 278 280 271 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 289 291 282 284 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 303 305 296 298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 321 323 314 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 336 338 329 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 355 357 348 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 386 396 379 389 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico </italic>
###xml 426 428 419 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 462 464 455 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 517 519 510 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 530 532 523 525 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 638 640 627 629 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 870 885 859 874 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Laurasiatheria </italic>
###xml 997 999 978 980 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1182 1184 1159 1161 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 1338 1340 1311 1313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 1465 1467 1434 1436 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 1533 1535 1502 1504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 1550 1552 1519 1521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 242 248 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 250 254 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 261 276 <span type="species:ncbi:9544">rhesus macaques</span>
###xml 283 287 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 294 301 <span type="species:ncbi:9986">rabbits</span>
###xml 308 319 <span type="species:ncbi:10141">guinea pigs</span>
###xml 348 353 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 431 439 <span type="species:ncbi:99490|species:ncbi:9783|species:ncbi:9785">elephant</span>
###xml 509 515 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 525 528 <span type="species:ncbi:9615">dog</span>
###xml 828 833 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 923 929 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 990 995 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 1164 1170 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
Unlike beta-defensins that have been found in numerous tissues and in all mammals studied so far, alpha-defensins are presumably unique to a few tissues and are absent in some mammalian species. Alpha-defensin gene expression was observed in humans, mice [12], rhesus macaques [13], rats [14], rabbits [15], guinea pigs [16], hamsters [17] and the horse [18]. They were also identified in silico in the genome of the opossum [19], elephant, and hedgehog tenrec [20]. The peptides are absent in the genomes of cattle [21] and dog [22]. Additionally, alpha-defensins are mainly synthesised in neutrophilic granulocytes and in Paneth cells [23]. Paneth cells are secretory epithelial cells, which are most abundant in the distal small intestine at the base of the crypts of Lieberkuhn. Based on the current state of knowledge, the horse is the only species in the group of Laurasiatheria expressing alpha-defensins. The first equine alpha-defensin transcript was found in the intestine of the horse [18]. The authors showed that the transcript, named DEFA1, was exclusively produced in the small intestine together with another alpha-defensin (DEFA5L), known from an equine BAC-clone [24]. Enteric alpha-defensins play an important role in the defence of the intestinal tract against microbes and may regulate the flora of enteric bacteria [25]. Dysfunctions in the regulation of intestinal alpha-defensins or mutations can lead to serious diseases like Morbus Crohn [26], and to a higher susceptibility to inflammatory bowel diseases [27] or diarrhea [28].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 200 202 200 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 203 205 203 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 265 287 265 287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Staphlylococcus aureus</italic>
###xml 289 311 289 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</italic>
###xml 313 329 313 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 331 353 331 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella typhimurium</italic>
###xml 355 374 355 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Streptococcus equi </italic>
###xml 378 394 378 394 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Rhodococcus equi</italic>
###xml 44 50 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 193 198 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 233 238 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 243 249 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 289 311 <span type="species:ncbi:287">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</span>
###xml 313 329 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 331 353 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">Salmonella typhimurium</span>
###xml 355 373 <span type="species:ncbi:1336">Streptococcus equi</span>
###xml 378 394 <span type="species:ncbi:43767">Rhodococcus equi</span>
The antimicrobial properties of recombinant equine DEFA1 were comprehensively studied. An antimicrobial effect was observed against 22 Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacterial strains and one yeast [18,29]. Among them are prominent human and equine pathogens like Staphlylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Escherichia coli, Salmonella typhimurium, Streptococcus equi and Rhodococcus equi.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 315 325 311 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico </italic>
###xml 29 35 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 254 260 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
The antimicrobial potency of equine DEFA1 and the general importance of intestinal defensins on animal health make them interesting candidates for further antimicrobial and functional characterisations. This motivated us to analyse the repertoire of the equine intestinal alpha-defensins in detail. Initial genomic in silico analyses with DEFA1- and DEFA5L sequences as matrices led to an identification of numerous unknown putative alpha-defensin sequences. Based on this information a set of oligonucleotides specific to the alpha-defensin gene-family was designed and RT-PCR products were generated with cDNA from the small intestine as template.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Methods
###end title 12
###begin title 13
Tissue collection and RNA extraction
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 65 70 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
Tissue from the centre of the outstreched small intestine of one horse was collected immediately after death during slaughtering and was frozen in liquid nitrogen. Approval of an ethics committee was not necessary since the tissue collection was done during the routine processes in an abattoir. Approximately 300 mg of frozen tissue were disrupted with a mortar and homogenised using a rotor-stator homogeniser. Total RNA was extracted by using the RNeasy Midi Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) according to the manufacturer's guidelines. The RNA concentration was determined photometrically by measuring the UV-absorption of the sample. One unit of the optical density at 260 nm equates to a concentration of 40 mug total RNA. The extracted RNA was stored at -80degreesC.
###end p 14
###begin title 15
Genomic analysis with alpha-defensin sequences as matrices
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 72 74 68 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 101 103 97 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 19 25 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 184 189 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
By using the known equine alpha-defensin sequences DEFA5L ([GenBank:]; [24]) and DEFA1 ([GenBank:]; [18]) as matrices, a BLAST-search ("blastn") in the nr/nt- and wgs-databases of the horse assembly EquCab1.0 was performed to obtain nucleotide sequences resembling alpha-defensin genes. This genomic sequence information represented the basis to design a set of oligonucleotides for the following RT-PCR studies.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Conceptual design of oligonucleotides for RT-PCR-reactions
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 92 102 92 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico </italic>
###xml 453 463 449 459 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico </italic>
Forward oligonucleotides were designed according to the genomic sequences determined by the in silico approach with following assumptions: (1) Oligonucleotides should include the ATG-start codon of the putative alpha-defensin genes or alternatively should bind a few bases upstream of the ATG so that in both cases the complete cDNA sequence could be amplified. (2) The set of forward oligonucleotides should amplify all the sequences determined by the in silico approach if combined with an oligo-dT-nucleotide used as a reverse oligonucleotide. Therefore some forward oligonucleotides contain wobble-bases. (3) Each forward oligonucleotide should amplify a single product in combination with the oligo-dT-nucleotide.
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 80 81 80 81 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
The 18 forward oligonucleotides and the oligo-dT-nucleotide are listed in Table 1. The oligonucleotides were synthesised by biomers.net (Ulm, Germany).
###end p 19
###begin p 20
Oligonucleotides for reverse-transcriptase PCR and sequencing reactions.
###end p 20
###begin title 21
RT-PCR
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 156 157 156 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 188 190 188 190 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
The cDNA was synthesised by using the Superscript-Transcriptase (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, USA) in combination with an oligo-dT-nucleotide (Oligo-dT-Bio, Table 1) according to Schramm et al. [30]. Total RNA from the small intestine served as a template. The cDNA was stored at -20degreesC.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 74 75 74 75 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 130 131 130 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
PCR reactions were performed by using the forward oligonucleotides (Table 1) paired with the reverse primer (Tail-Primer3', Table 1) and the cDNA from the small intestine as template. The optimal annealing temperature for the PCR reactions was determined by performing a temperature-gradient-PCR in a range from 50-68degreesC. The reaction mixtures were incubated at 94degreesC for 3 min, followed by 33 cycles at 55degreesC (alpha1-alpha7), 58degreesC (alpha2-2, 2-6, 2-8), 60degreesC (alpha2-7), 62degreesC (alpha2-3, 2-9, 2-11) or 67degreesC (alpha2-1, 2-4, 2-5, 2-10) for 50 s, 68degreesC for 45 s, and 94degreesC for 30 s. A final extension step was performed at 68degreesC for 10 min. Hot-Start-polymerase (Eppendorf, Hamburg, Germany) was used for PCR-reactions (1.5 U/reaction) with the appropriate buffer. The final dNTP concentration was 3 nM, the oligonucleotide concentration 3.35 pM. PCR reactions were performed with the Thermal-Cycler PTC-200 (GMI, Minnesota, USA).
###end p 23
###begin p 24
The amplified products were purified by gel extraction from a 1.5% agarose gel with the Gel-Extraction-Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) according to the manufacturer's specifications and enriched with 1 mM guanosine to stabilise the DNA.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Subcloning
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 112 120 112 120 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli </italic>
###xml 112 119 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
After purification 65 ng of each amplified product was cloned into a pDrive-cloning vector and transformed into E. coli cells (Qiagen competent cells, Hilden, Germany) according to the manufacturer's specifications (Subcloning-Kit, Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). A volume of 100 mul of the bacterial suspension was transferred to an LB-agar plate containing 100 mug/ml ampicillin, 80 mug/ml XGal and 50 muM IPTG for blue/white screening. A single agar plate was used for each oligonucleotide combination or PCR-product, respectively. Accordingly 18 agar plates were inoculated. The plates were incubated for 18 h at 37degreesC and finally for 3 h at 4degreesC.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 326 327 325 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Twenty colonies per plate were selected (a total of 360 colonies) and transferred to 24-well plates (Sarstedt, Numbrecht, Germany) whose wells were filled with LB-agar enriched with 100 mug/ml ampicillin. A colony PCR was performed with the oligonucleotides T7 promoter-oligo in combination with the SP6 promoter-oligo (Table 1) to confirm the presence of the inserted product. Therefore, each colony was used as a template and the reaction mixtures were incubated at 94degreesC for 3 min, followed by 35 cycles at 50degreesC for 50 s, 68degreesC for 50 s, and 94degreesC for 25 s. A final extension step was performed at 68degreesC for 10 min. The Hot-Start-polymerase (Eppendorf, Hamburg, Germany) was used (1.5 U/reaction) with the appropriate buffer. The oligonucleotide concentration was 3.35 pM, the dNTP concentration 3 nM.
###end p 27
###begin title 28
Sequencing of individual clones
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 396 397 396 397 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Two hundred and sixty-nine positive clones were selected for sequencing. The sequencing reactions were performed at the Institute for Services in Molecular Biology and Biochemistry (DLMBC) in Berlin, Germany. The colonies embedded in the 24-well plates were directly sent to the DLMBC. Sequencing reactions were performed with the oligonucleotides T7 promoter-oligo and SP6 promoter-oligo (Table 1), products were sequenced forward and reverse.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
Evaluation of the cDNA-sequences
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 87 89 87 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>
The obtained sequences were evaluated by using the software programs BioEdit 7.0.4.1 ([31]; ) and Sequencher 4.8 (Gene Codes Corporation, Ann Arbor, USA; ).
###end p 31
###begin p 32
Matching forward and reverse sequences were compared (BioEdit 7.0.4.1). Sequences with discrepancies based on the sequencing reaction were excluded. Sequences with an identity exceeding 99% were pooled into one contig (Sequencher 4.8) and the consensus sequence was created. Ambiguities at certain base positions (nucleotide polymorphisms) can be due to an amplification error of the polymerase, a sequencing error, possible gene duplications, or an SNP. The most frequent base at a position was chosen for the consensus sequence. Some of the sequences could not be integrated into a contig and were consequently treated separately.
###end p 32
###begin p 33
To prove this method of evaluation, the sequences were processed in an alternative way: DNA-sequences with an identity of 100% were pooled into contigs (Sequencher 4.8) and the consensus sequences were created. The contigs and discrete sequences were translated. The resulting amino acid sequences were aligned and sequences with an identity of more than 99% were again pooled into contigs (Sequencher 4.8). In the case of varieties at one position, the most frequent amino acid at this position was chosen for the consensus sequence but according to the following premises: First, an amino acid was exchanged only if solely this amino acid at the specific position inside the contig was ambiguous. If two or more amino acids were ambiguous at one specific position, a new contig was created. Secondly, inside an amino acid sequence only one amino acid exchange was allowed, otherwise the sequence was processed as an individual peptide. Finally, the amino acid sequences obtained by using the first method were aligned with the sequences obtained from the second one.
###end p 33
###begin title 34
Comparison of the transcripts with genomic sequences
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 67 73 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 317 322 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">Horse</span>
To confirm the transcripts a genome-screening was performed in the equine database with the cDNA-sequences as templates to recover the genes associated with the transtripts. To determine the genomic positions of the obtained transcripts we performed a database search using BLAT  with the following settings: genome, Horse; assembly, September 2007; query type, DNA.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 475 481 475 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">DEFA3 </italic>
The cDNA sequences from the ATG-start codon to the end of the 3'-UTR of the transcripts were used. If the identity of a transcript and a genomic region exceeded a value of 99%, they were defined as matching. We compared not only the whole transcript with the genomic sequence but also the individual exons. We excluded false-positive matches of whole transcripts based on a high identity of a single exon (>99.5%) but low identity (<99%) of the other exon. For example: With DEFA3 we found a matching genomic sequence showing an identity of 99.1%, whereas exon 1 shows an identity of 100% and exon 2 only 97.5% (this is equivalent to a difference of 3 bp). Accordingly, a complete transcript was only matched to a gene if both the first and the second exon showed an identity exceeding 99%.

###xml 1428 1430 1416 1418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1431 1433 1419 1421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
All sequences are in a range of 25 to 124 amino acid residues but not all sequences seem to be potentially functional alpha-defensins. In some sequences typical conserved alpha-defensin motives are missing in the mature peptide segment. These include the cysteine residues, necessary for the typical defensin disulfide-bond connectivity; arginine and glutamate residues, forming a conserved salt bridge [32]; and a highly conserved glycine residue, essential for correct folding [33]. The peptides DEFA20 to DEFA29 contain the six cysteine residues at position 66, 68, 74, 84, 96, and 97 and the glycine residue at position 82 but they have lost the intramolecular salt bridge. DEFA30L is also missing the salt bridge and additionally the two cysteine residues at positions 66 and 84. The peptides DEFA31L to DEFA37L exhibit a premature stop codon, the translation stopped at position 77 (DEFA31L to DEFA34L), 78 (DEFA35L, DEFA36L) or at position 26 visible in the appropriate cDNA sequence. DEFA30L to DEFA37L appear to be pseudogenes. In the case of DEFA20 to DEFA29 it has to be proved whether the intramolecular salt bridge leads to a loss of the biological function. DEFA1 to DEFA19 and DEFA5L show all conserved amino acid motives typical for alpha-defensins. It can be assumed that at least these 20 peptides are functional molecules and have the potential to exhibit antimicrobial properties as already shown for DEFA1 [18,29].

###begin title 57
Discussion
###end title 57
###begin p 58
###xml 142 147 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 236 242 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horses</span>
###xml 672 678 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horses</span>
In this study we demonstrated the expression of a large repertoire of different alpha-defensin transcripts in the small intestine of a single horse. Consequently, this study is not to be considered representative for alpha-defensins in horses generally and conclusions regarding differences between races or states of health cannot be drawn. However, the extensive quantity of at least 38 detected alpha-defensin transcripts (30 probably functional peptides, DEFA1 to DEFA29 including DEFA5L, and eight obvious pseudogenes, DEFA30L to DEFA37L) in the intestinal tissue has rarely been observed before in other organisms and may indicate an important role of these AMPs in horses. The functional context of this high number of different transcripts is yet unclear.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 92 94 88 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 278 280 270 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 476 478 464 466 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 479 481 467 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 7 12 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 127 132 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 209 223 <span type="species:ncbi:9544">rhesus macaque</span>
###xml 379 384 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 655 659 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In the human Paneth cells two different alpha-defensins are synthesised (DEFA 5 and DEFA6; [34]) in addition to the neutrophil human alpha-defensin peptides 1-4, also known as HNP 1-4. In the intestine of the rhesus macaque four different peptides are observed, RED-1 to RED-4 [35]. The only known organism with a numerically related repertoire of enteric alpha-defensins is the mouse. At least 23 different peptides are known, named cryptdins and cryptdin-related sequences [36-38]. They were not only found in the small intestine but also in the colon, the cecum and the rectum. The antimicrobial activities of some of these cryptdins are not known. In mice three different observations highlight possible advantages to express a high amount of different defensin peptides and indicate their biological relevance.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 236 244 236 244 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli </italic>
###xml 248 270 248 270 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Salmonella typhimurium</italic>
###xml 294 296 294 296 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 236 243 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 248 270 <span type="species:ncbi:90371">Salmonella typhimurium</span>
First, the peptides can exhibit different specificities against microorganisms. The cryptdins 1 to 6 were analysed regarding their antimicrobial activity against different bacteria. The killing potency against the targets, particularly E. coli and Salmonella typhimurium, showed variabilities [37].
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 122 124 122 124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 986 987 982 983 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T3">3</xref>
###xml 1265 1270 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1628 1633 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
Secondly, the expression level of the cryptdin genes along the intestinal tract showed large variations. Karlsson et al. [36] observed a differential gene expression of cryptdins in the duodenum, jejunum and ileum. They concluded that the varying cryptdin synthesis in different tracts of the intestine may play an important role in the local regulation of bacterial colonisation in addition to the role in protection against infection. In having analysed only a single intestinal location, similar conclusions cannot be drawn from our data. However, different cloning frequencies of the transcripts found in this study may indicate different mRNA-expression levels at least at this location. The number of cDNA-fragments coding for a specific peptide varies from one (e.g. DEFA2, DEFA3) to 39 (DEFA25) in randomly selected clones. Although 38 different alpha-defensin transcripts were observed in this study, only seven cDNAs were cloned frequently (DEFA4, 5, 8, 9, 12, 17, 31L, Table 3), whereas in all other cases the number of obtained clones varies between one and four. Since only a single set of amplification products was cloned and variants due to error-prone PCR are possible, these data are less representative. However, the same situation exists in the mouse, where over 30 alpha-defensin genes were included in the latest assemblies and over 20 different cDNAs were reported, but only six cDNAs have been cloned at high frequencies. It should be taken into account that despite the apparent diversity, it might be unlikely that more than 7-8 peptides accumulate to considerable levels in the small intestine of the horse. Furthermore, it is not known whether and to what extent the gene expression is inducible.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 84 86 84 86 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 470 472 470 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 42 46 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Thirdly, the gene expression of different mice cryptdins shows circadian variances [39]. The expression level varies about 100% between simulated dark and light phases, primary between cryptdin 1 and 4. An interrelation with the time of ingestion was assumed. Moreover, the gene expression level of cryptdin 2 and 5 changes about two or three orders of magnitude in the first days after birth whereas the gene expression of cryptdin 1, 3, and 6 increases at lower pace [40]. Probably the differential gene expression protects the organism during the first adaptation to a new environment.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 184 186 180 182 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 227 229 223 225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 288 290 284 286 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 339 354 335 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Laurasiatheria </italic>
###xml 401 402 397 398 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 530 532 518 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 543 545 531 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 663 679 647 663 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Euarchontoglires</italic>
###xml 685 693 669 677 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Equidae </italic>
###xml 771 785 751 765 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Laurasiatheria</italic>
###xml 1140 1154 1112 1126 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Perissodactyla</italic>
###xml 1348 1355 1316 1323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Equidae</italic>
###xml 1495 1497 1459 1461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 1596 1598 1556 1558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 1599 1601 1559 1561 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 1738 1740 1694 1696 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 76 82 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horses</span>
###xml 193 201 <span type="species:ncbi:99490|species:ncbi:9783|species:ncbi:9785">elephant</span>
###xml 297 302 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 365 371 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 373 376 <span type="species:ncbi:9615">dog</span>
###xml 522 528 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 538 541 <span type="species:ncbi:9615">dog</span>
###xml 614 622 <span type="species:ncbi:99490|species:ncbi:9783|species:ncbi:9785">elephant</span>
###xml 841 847 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 852 855 <span type="species:ncbi:9615">dog</span>
###xml 925 930 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 1059 1064 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 1121 1126 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 1427 1432 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 1638 1646 <span type="species:ncbi:9258">platypus</span>
Alpha-defensin transcripts were formerly only known in primates, glires and horses. In recent studies the existence of alpha-defensin genes was observed in the genomes of the opossum [19], the elephant and the hedgehog tenrec [20]. According to the Bayesian phylogenetic tree of mammals [41], the horse is classified into the group of the Laurasiatheria as well as cattle, dog, bat and hedgehog (Fig. 4). Nothing is known about the existence of alpha-defensins in bat and hedgehog, and no alpha-defensin gene was found in cattle [21] and dog [22]. Whereas alpha-defensin genes exist in basal mammals like opossum, elephant and hedgehog tenrec and in the group of Euarchontoglires, the Equidae are the only known family expressing alpha-defensin genes within the group of Laurasiatheria. In future studies it will be necessary to clarify why cattle and dog presumably lost their complete set of alpha-defensin genes while the horse increased the gene number extensively. Additionally, the presence or absence of alpha-defensins in the closest relatives of the horse like tapir and rhinoceros (which form together with the horse the group of Perissodactyla) has to be analysed. This may lead to new aspects in the development, reorganisation and separation of defensin genes. It is very unlikely that alpha-defensins evolved independently within the Equidae, indicated by the high analogy between the amino acid sequences of the horse compared with known alpha-defensins from primates and glires [18]. It is assumed that alpha-defensins evolved from one or two ancestral genes by gene-duplication [22,42]. According to the observation that platypus being the most basal mammal that already has been sequenced has four alpha-defensin genes [43], one can hypothesise that alpha-defensins may have been lost independently in different clades during the divergence of the phylogenetic tree.
###end p 63

###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 92 93 92 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>

###xml 483 490 483 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">DEFA15 </italic>

###xml 1377 1378 1365 1366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 2049 2051 2021 2023 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 1188 1193 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1313 1318 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 1413 1419 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 1614 1619 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
The genomic positions of the transcripts or of single exons, respectively, as shown in Fig. 3 indicate a false assembly of EquCab2.0 or unidentified gaps in the genomic sequence. In some cases only one exon of a transcript was found with an identity of 100% in the genome whereas an appropriate second exon was missing (DEFA6, DEFA8, DEFA13). In other cases the same genomic exons were found in different transcripts (DEFA6 and DEFA7 (Exon 2), DEFA12 and DEFA13 (Exon 2), DEFA12 and DEFA15 (Exon 1)). The second exons of the DEFA6 and DEFA7 transcripts were located at the same genomic position whereas only the first exon of DEFA7 was identified at an appropriate distance of approximately 700 bp upstream. The first exon of DEFA6 could not be detected in the genome indicating a gap. In addition to assembly errors, comprehensive gene reduplication and mutations of alpha-defensins and/or alternative splicing might be possible explanations, although the latter was never shown for alpha-defensins before. Additionally it is possible that transcripts with missing genomic sequences in the assembly EquCab2.0 could be also due to copy number variations (that have e.g. been reported for human alpha-defensins; [44]). The animal used in our investigations might have possessed additional copies compared with the horse used for establishing the reference genome. However, Fig. 3 shows a typical clustering of the equine alpha-defensins with average distances of approximately 10 kb between single genes. No transcripts were found in the small intestine for some alpha-defensin genes annotated in the genome of the horse or identified by BLAST-searches. It is possible that these genes are expressed in other tissues and cells, e.g. bone marrow, or are expressed at very low levels in the small intestine. In other species that have enteric alpha-defensins, they are the products of Paneth cells. Their number varies in different sections of the small intestine (duodenum, jejunum and ileum) and certain alpha-defensins were only found in the ileum [36]. In our studies we most probably used tissue samples of the jejunum. Consequently, our results do not allow a differentiation between the single sections of the small intestine and it is possible that some transcripts may be additionally found exclusively in the duodenum and/or ileum.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 308 309 300 301 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1</sub>
###xml 313 314 305 306 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sub>
###xml 319 320 311 312 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 324 326 316 318 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4 </sub>
###xml 333 334 325 326 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sub>
###xml 338 339 330 331 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5</sub>
###xml 378 379 370 371 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5</sub>
###xml 388 390 380 382 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">13</sub>
###xml 425 427 417 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 437 439 429 431 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">17</sub>
###xml 518 520 510 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 1181 1183 1157 1159 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5 </sub>
###xml 1190 1192 1166 1168 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">13</sub>
###xml 1320 1322 1292 1294 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 1920 1922 1888 1890 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 544 550 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 1904 1909 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The primary structure of mature alpha-defensin shows highly conserved residues, which are indispensable for the structural stability and the full function of the peptides. Among them are six invariant cysteine residues, necessary for the typical alpha-defensin intramolecular disulfide-bond connectivity (Cys1-Cys6, Cys2-Cys4 and Cys3-Cys5), two charged amino acid residues, Arg5, and Glu13, forming a conserved salt bridge [32], and Gly17, which constitutes a structural motif which is essential for correct folding [33]. By analysing the new equine alpha-defensins considering that these prerequisites must be fulfilled for an active alpha-defensin, it became evident that 20 of the 38 peptides (including the known DEFA1) may be active alpha-defensins with an antimicrobial potential. DEFA1 to DEFA19 and the DEFA5L transcript show the six conserved cysteine residues, the arginine and the glutamic acid residues necessary for the intramolecular salt bridge, and the highly conserved glycine residue necessary for correct folding. The alpha-defensins DEFA20 to DEFA29 exhibit also the cysteine and glycine residues but they have lost the intramolecular salt bridge formed by Arg5 and Glu13. Nonetheless, they should be considered functional because this bridge is not required for correct pro-alpha-defensin folding [32]. In contrast, the peptides DEFA30L to DEFA37L appear to be pseudogenes. Either some cysteine residues were absent (DEFA30L) or the transcription stops before completion because of a premature stop codon visible in the appropriate cDNA sequence. It is not known whether these molecules may have adopted new functions or are involved in regulatory systems. Predictions (e.g. as non-coding RNAs) at this point would be highly speculative. Interestingly, the premature stop codon of DEFA35L and DEFA36L at position 78 is at the same position that gives rise to theta-defensins in non-human primates [45].
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 622 624 610 612 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 1134 1158 1114 1138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Ornithorhynchus anatinus</italic>
###xml 167 172 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 401 407 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 543 549 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 605 610 <span type="species:ncbi:9940">sheep</span>
###xml 615 620 <span type="species:ncbi:9925">goats</span>
###xml 788 794 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 1124 1132 <span type="species:ncbi:9258">platypus</span>
###xml 1134 1158 <span type="species:ncbi:9258">Ornithorhynchus anatinus</span>
Typically, alpha-defensins have an anionic propeptide and a cationic mature peptide, and the charges are counterbalanced. Taking all intestinal alpha-defensins of the horse into account that can produce a mature peptide (DEFA1-DEFA29, DEFA5L) they follow the pattern of anionic propiece and cationic mature peptide, but only six of them are counterbalanced (DEFA8, 12, 13, 15, 21, 27). Several of the equine alpha-defensins (DEFA18-20, DEFA30L) have proline-rich C-terminal extensions resembling certain proline-rich antimicrobial peptides of cattle, named cathelicidins (CATHL2 and CATHL3) and others in sheep and goats [46]. Also DEFA30L exhibits a proline-rich C-terminal region and four cystein residues, typical for cathelicidins, but the spacing of the cysteins is different. Other equine alpha-defensin peptides have a C-terminal glycine residue (DEFA8 to DEFA11). Many cathelicidin precursors also have a C-terminal glycine, which allows the peptide's C-terminus to be amidated. To our knowledge no defensins from other species are amidated. The only other alpha-defensin containing a C-terminal glycine is DEFA3 of platypus (Ornithorhynchus anatinus, acc. Nr. P0C8A3, Swissprot: ).
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 153 155 149 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 463 465 451 453 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 485 487 473 475 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 1022 1024 1006 1008 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 1025 1027 1009 1011 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 539 544 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 691 697 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horses</span>
###xml 1051 1057 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 1193 1199 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 1242 1247 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
Different infectious diseases may appear if the expression level or intracellular processing of Paneth cell alpha-defensins is abnormal. Wehkamp et al. [26] discovered a reduced Paneth cell alpha-defensin synthesis in ileal Crohn's disease (Morbus Crohn), a chronic disease of the intestine, by using real-time PCR and immunohistochemical methods. An enhanced expression of epithelial alpha-defensin genes in colonic inflammations was reported by Wehkamp et al. [47]. Ferguson et al. [27] found that single nucleotide polymorphisms in the human Paneth cell alpha-defensin DEFA5 may confer susceptibility to inflammatory bowel disease. Inflammatory bowel diseases have also been described in horses, for example the duodenitis/proximal jejunitis syndrome which is characterised by catarrhal enteritis with mucosal hyperaemia or necrosis or colitis, which causes intramural oedema and haemorrhagic inflammation of the large intestine and is often fatal. Bacteria have been implicated as etiological agents of both diseases [48,49]. The high quantity of equine intestinal alpha-defensins and the considerable biological effect of enteric DEFA1 against microorganisms emphasises the importance of equine intestinal defensins in protection of the horse against infections of the intestinal tract.
###end p 68
###begin title 69
Conclusions
###end title 69
###begin p 70
###xml 34 40 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 716 722 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 799 804 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 908 913 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
###xml 948 954 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 1073 1079 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">equine</span>
###xml 1105 1110 <span type="species:ncbi:9796">horse</span>
The large quantity of at least 38 equine alpha-defensin transcripts in the intestinal tissue has rarely been observed before in other species. In previous studies we could show the comprehensive antimicrobial activities of one of these molecules. The biological context of expressing such a high number of different alpha-defensins in the intestine remains unclear. It is likely that the peptides exhibit different specificities against microorganisms, or different requirements for antimicrobial peptides along the intestinal tract may lead to spatial expression patterns. Also temporal expression variations may be possible, e.g. a regulation of alpha-defensins by circadian rhythms. However, the high quantity of equine intestinal alpha-defensins emphasises their importance in the health of the horse's gut. Our study provides the basis for further gene-expression studies in the intestinal tract of the horse or biological analyses of further equine alpha-defensins to understand the importance of defensins in the enteric innate immunity. Moreover, the repertoire of equine alpha-defensins make the horse an excellent species to study the biological properties of alpha-defensins regarding animal health. From other species it is known that deficiencies or dysregulations of Paneth cell alpha-defensins lead to enteric infectious diseases.
###end p 70
