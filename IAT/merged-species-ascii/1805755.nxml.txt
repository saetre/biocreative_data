###begin article-title 0
Comparative genomic analysis of regulation of anaerobic respiration in ten genomes from three families of gamma-proteobacteria (Enterobacteriaceae, Pasteurellaceae, Vibrionaceae)
###end article-title 0

###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 30 46 30 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 211 218 211 218 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 30 46 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 211 218 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Gamma-proteobacteria, such as Escherichia coli, can use a variety of respiratory substrates employing numerous aerobic and anaerobic respiratory systems controlled by multiple transcription regulators. Thus, in E. coli, global control of respiration is mediated by four transcription factors, Fnr, ArcA, NarL and NarP. However, in other Gamma-proteobacteria the composition of global respiration regulators may be different.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 308 317 308 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 652 664 652 664 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atpIBEFHAGCD</italic>
###xml 668 669 668 669 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 706 715 706 715 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nqrABCDEF</italic>
###xml 756 761 756 761 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dadAX</italic>
In this study we applied a comparative genomic approach to the analysis of three global regulatory systems, Fnr, ArcA and NarP. These systems were studied in available genomes containing these three regulators, but lacking NarL. So, we considered several representatives of Pasteurellaceae, Vibrionaceae and Yersinia spp. As a result, we identified new regulon members, functioning in respiration, central metabolism (glycolysis, gluconeogenesis, pentose phosphate pathway, citrate cicle, metabolism of pyruvate and lactate), metabolism of carbohydrates and fatty acids, transcriptional regulation and transport, in particular: the ATP synthase operon atpIBEFHAGCD, Na+-exporting NADH dehydrogenase operon nqrABCDEF, the D-amino acids dehydrogenase operon dadAX. Using an extension of the comparative technique, we demonstrated taxon-specific changes in regulatory interactions and predicted taxon-specific regulatory cascades.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
A comparative genomic technique was applied to the analysis of global regulation of respiration in ten gamma-proteobacterial genomes. Three structurally different but functionally related regulatory systems were described. A correlation between the regulon size and the position of a transcription factor in regulatory cascades was observed: regulators with larger regulons tend to occupy top positions in the cascades. On the other hand, there is no obvious link to differences in the species' lifestyles and metabolic capabilities.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 0 16 0 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 0 16 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Escherichia coli, the best-studied representative of gamma-proteobacteria, can adapt to a wide variety of environmental conditions. One source of this capability is the presence of numerous aerobic and anaerobic respiratory systems. To adapt to different growth conditions, this bacterium alter the composition of their respiratory systems by changing the repertoire of substrate-specific dehydrogenases and terminal oxidoreductases. The concentration of each component is strictly regulated in order to optimize the respiratory chains according to the available substrates and the physiological needs of the cell.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 24 31 24 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 440 441 440 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 442 443 442 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 24 31 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
In facultative anaerobe E. coli, regulation of respiration depends on the availability of electron acceptors, which are used in a specific order. Thus, molecular oxygen represses all other types of respiration and fermentation. Under anaerobiosis, nitrate, the most favorable electron acceptor in such conditions, represses other types of anaerobic respiration and fermentation. This control is effected by a variety of regulatory systems [1,2].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 368 369 368 369 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 370 371 370 371 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 539 540 539 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 541 542 541 542 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 548 555 548 555 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 672 673 672 673 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 674 675 674 675 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 548 555 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The first level of regulation is implemented via the Fnr protein. Fnr is an oxygen-sensitive transcription factor. Oxygen is sensed by iron-sulfur clusters formed by the N-terminal domain. Under anaerobic conditions, Fnr forms dimers that are capable of binding DNA, whereas in the presence of oxygen these clusters are reversibly destroyed and the dimers dissociate [3,4]. Thus, Fnr is active only under anaerobic conditions, when it activates genes necessary for the anaerobic metabolism and represses genes for the aerobic respiration [2,5]. In E. coli, Fnr is the top regulator of respiration, as it controls the expression of genes for other transcriptional factors [6,7].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 232 240 232 240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 332 333 332 333 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 334 335 334 335 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 441 443 441 443 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 444 446 444 446 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 649 650 649 650 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 651 653 651 653 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 654 656 654 656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
Another transcription factor regulating respiration is the ArcA protein. ArcA is a part of the ArcA-ArcB two-component system, where ArcB is an inner membrane sensor protein. ArcB is belived to sense the redox state of ubiquinones: in vitro, the activity of this protein depends on the redox status of a ubiquionone soluble analog [8,9]. In the absence of corresponding electron acceptors, ArcB phosphorylates ArcA, enabling it to bind DNA [10,11]. Thus, under the anaerobic conditions, ArcA regulates expression of genes for respiration and central metabolism, and probably controls the switch between the respiration and fermentation metabolisms [5,12-14].
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 726 728 726 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 729 731 729 731 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 900 902 900 902 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 903 905 903 905 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
The next level of regulation is provided by two homologous transcription factors, NarL and NarP. These proteins are activated in the presence of nitrate and nitrite by two homologous sensor kinases NarX and NarQ. The duplicated two-component system allows for the fine tuning of the nitrate and nitrite respiration system to a dynamic ratio of two alternative substrates. NarX and NarQ respond differentially to nitrate and nitrite. Thus, in this subsystem three levels of the response specificity may be distinguished: (i) interaction between sensor proteins and respiratory substrates, (ii) interaction between sensor proteins and transcription factors, and (iii) interaction between factors and their binding sites in DNA [15-18]. Upon activation, NarL and NarP activate genes for the nitrate and nitrate respiration and repress genes for other, less effective, pathways of anaerobic respiration [19-22].
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 88 90 88 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 405 407 405 407 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
Nevertheless, in some gamma-proteobacteria, only the single NarQ-NarP system was found [23], accompanied by reduction of the nitrate and nitrite respiratory system, and the presence of only the periplasmic respiratory system. So, in most gamma-proteobacteria the fine tuning of the system is not required and a single NarQ-NarP system is sufficient for the control of the nitrate and nitrite respiration [24].
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 57 65 57 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 320 328 320 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 329 331 329 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 332 334 332 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>
###xml 57 64 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 237 259 <span type="species:ncbi:727">Haemophilus influenzae</span>
###xml 320 327 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Although the physiology and regulation of respiration in E. coli has been studied in considerable depth, the regulation in other organisms is poorly understood. Moreover, in other representatives of gamma-proteobacteria, for example, in Haemophilus influenzae, regulation of respiration looks to be quite different from E. coli [25,26].
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 76 78 76 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 87 89 87 89 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 102 104 102 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 406 408 406 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29">29</xref>
###xml 409 411 409 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
Previously, comparative genomic analyses were performed separately for Fnr [27], ArcA [28], and NarP [24] in a small number of microorganisms. Here we report the results of a comparative genomic analysis of the respiration regulation in multiple genomes belonging to several representatives of the three families of gamma-proteobacteria. Although the NarL-DNA interactions have been studied in experiment [29-32], the existing methods for prediction of transcription factor binding sites do not allow for the reliable identification of candidate NarL sites. Because of that, we analyzed the organisms containing only the NarP regulator. In such organisms, we studied three structurally different, but functionally related Fnr, ArcA and NarP systems.
###end p 16
###begin title 17
Results
###end title 17
###begin title 18
Structure of the Fnr, ArcA and NarP regulogs
###end title 18
###begin p 19
The procedure described in "Methods" was applied to the analysis of regulation by Fnr, ArcA and NarP. All genes predicted to form regulogs (orthologous regulons) were classified according to their functions.
###end p 19
###begin p 20
###xml 129 131 129 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 132 134 132 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 135 137 135 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 445 446 445 446 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 582 583 582 583 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S1">1</xref>
###xml 588 589 588 589 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
In comparison with previous studies, where single regulatory systems had been studied in several gamma-protoeobacterial genomes [24,27,33], here we predicted additional regulatory interactions. These predictions were made possible by the use of total pairwise comparison and detailed simultaneous analysis of multiple regulatory systems. Here we consider in detail only the relevant predictions. ###begin title 21
Respiration
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 104 117 104 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atpIBEFHAGCD </italic>
###xml 241 243 241 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>
###xml 288 292 288 292 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp </italic>
###xml 302 310 302 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 360 364 360 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp </italic>
###xml 417 419 417 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 500 504 500 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp </italic>
###xml 517 521 517 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fnr </italic>
###xml 528 535 528 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 610 612 610 612 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B36">36</xref>
###xml 686 690 686 690 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp </italic>
###xml 713 718 713 718 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arcA </italic>
###xml 733 735 733 735 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B37">37</xref>
###xml 807 811 807 811 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp </italic>
###xml 821 829 821 829 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 1028 1032 1028 1032 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">atp </italic>
###xml 1047 1056 1047 1056 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 302 309 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 528 535 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 821 833 <span type="species:ncbi:83333">E. coli K-12</span>
One of the most significant results was the identification of conserved candidate sites upstream of the atpIBEFHAGCD operon. This operon encodes all subunits of the ATP synthetase complex, the key component of the oxidative phosphorylation [34]. Experimental data about regulation of the atp operon in E. coli are conflicting. In particular, expression of the atp operon was claimed to be independent of Fnr or ArcA [35]. On the other hand, 1.5- to 8-fold increase of the expression level of various atp genes in the fnr mutant E. coli, as compared to the wild-type strain, was shown using a microarray assay [36]. Similarly, 1.9- to 14-fold increase of the expression level of various atp genes was shown in the arcA mutant strain [37]. Recently, candidate Fnr and ArcA sites were observed upstream of the atp operon in E. coli K-12 genome; these sites were conserved in many Enterobacteriaceae genomes [Tsiganova and Ravcheev, unpublished observation]. In the present study, candidate ArcA sites were observed upstream of the atp operon in both Yersinia spp., and Fnr sites were detected in all Vibrionaceae. Thus, in at least some gamma-proteobacteria, expression of the ATP synthetase genes is controlled by the global regulators of respiration.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 54 64 54 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nqrABCDEF </italic>
###xml 82 83 82 83 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 114 116 114 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 276 285 276 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 377 381 377 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nqr </italic>
###xml 405 412 405 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 450 472 450 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Klebsiella pneumoniae </italic>
###xml 518 520 518 520 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 521 523 521 523 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 545 553 545 553 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 561 562 561 562 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 615 619 615 619 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nuo </italic>
###xml 700 702 700 702 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 703 705 703 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 724 728 724 728 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nuo </italic>
###xml 765 769 765 769 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nqr </italic>
###xml 793 797 793 797 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nuo </italic>
###xml 822 831 822 831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 405 412 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 450 471 <span type="species:ncbi:573">Klebsiella pneumoniae</span>
###xml 545 552 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Candidate regulatory sites were found upstream of the nqrABCDEF operon encoding Na+-exporting NADH dehydrogenase [38]. Candidate Fnr-binding sites are conserved in all studied genomes, whereas ArcA sites have been found in all Vibrionaceae and Pasteurellaceae, but not in the Yersinia spp. The regulation by NarP seems to be specific for the Pasteurellaceae. Orthologs for the nqr genes were not found in E. coli, but such orthologs were detected in Klebsiella pneumoniae and various Vibrionaceae and Pasteurellaceae [38-43]. On the other hand, E. coli has an H+-exporting NADH dehydrogenase enzyme, encoded by the nuo operon, whose expression is regulated by the NarL and Fnr transcription factors [44,45]. No genes of the nuo operon demonstrate homology with the nqr genes. Orthologs of the nuo operon were found in the Yersinia genomes, but they were not preceded by candidate Fnr, ArcA or NarP binding sites. Thus, in the studied genomes, non-homologous displacement with a partial change of function leads to the sodium-dependent energetics used in conjunction with the proton-dependent one.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 35 41 35 41 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dadAX </italic>
###xml 149 151 149 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 196 205 196 205 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 246 252 246 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dadAX </italic>
One more new regulog member is the dadAX operon. The first gene of this operon encodes the small subunit of respiratory D-amino acids dehydrogenase [46]. Candidate ArcA sites were detected in the Yersinia and Vibrionaceae genomes upstream of the dadAX operon. Thus, the use of D-amino acids as electron donors in some genomes may be controlled by ArcA.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 194 201 194 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">torCAD </italic>
###xml 205 210 205 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">torYZ</italic>
###xml 226 233 226 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 236 238 236 238 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 239 241 239 241 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 248 255 248 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">torCAD </italic>
###xml 361 363 361 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 364 366 364 366 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 377 383 377 383 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">torYZ </italic>
###xml 423 425 423 425 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 476 483 476 483 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">torCAD </italic>
###xml 577 583 577 583 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">torYZ </italic>
###xml 624 631 624 631 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">torCAD </italic>
###xml 686 692 686 692 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">torYZ </italic>
###xml 744 745 744 745 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 226 233 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
An interesting example of taxon-specific regulation was observed for duplicated operons for trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) reductases and formate dehydrogenases. Two operons for TMAO reductases, torCAD and torYZ, were found in E. coli. [47,48]. The torCAD expression is known to be repressed by the NarL protein and activated by the transcriptional factor TorR [49,50], whereas torYZ is transcribed at a constant low level [47]. In the three Pasteurellaceae genomes, where the torCAD genes are absent, candidate sites for all three studied regulators were found upstream of the torYZ operon. In the Vibrionaceae genomes, the torCAD transcription is likely controlled by Fnr, whereas the torYZ expression seems to be regulated by NarP (see Table 1).
###end p 25
###begin p 26
###xml 36 44 36 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 87 91 87 91 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fdn </italic>
###xml 160 162 160 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>
###xml 163 165 163 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B52">52</xref>
###xml 198 202 198 202 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fdo </italic>
###xml 222 224 222 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B53">53</xref>
###xml 313 323 313 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y. pestis </italic>
###xml 324 326 324 326 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 420 421 420 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S3">3</xref>
###xml 442 451 442 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 464 468 464 468 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fdo </italic>
###xml 477 481 477 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fdn </italic>
###xml 496 505 496 505 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 528 532 528 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fdo </italic>
###xml 627 632 627 632 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fdhD </italic>
###xml 720 722 720 722 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B54">54</xref>
###xml 777 782 777 782 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fdhD </italic>
###xml 824 828 824 828 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fdo </italic>
###xml 831 835 831 835 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fdn </italic>
###xml 36 43 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 313 322 <span type="species:ncbi:632">Y. pestis</span>
A similar situation was observed in E. coli for two formate dehydrogenase operons: the fdn operon is known to be regulated by the Fnr, NarL, and NarP proteins [51,52], whereas the expression of the fdo operon is constant [53]. Previously Fnr binding site was predicted upstream of the formate reductase operon in Y. pestis [27]. Careful analysis of the phylogenetical trees for the Fdn and Fdo proteins (additional file 3) revealed that both Yersinia spp. contain fdo but lack fdn genes. In both Yersinia spp. expression of the fdo operon is regulated by all three studied regulators. Further, regulation was predicted for the fdhD gene whose product is essential for the formation of the Fdn and Fdo protein complexes [54]. Accordingly, candidate regulatory sites upatream of fdhD were found only in genomes containing the fdo or fdn operon.
###end p 26
###begin title 27
Central metabolism and fermentation
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 121 122 121 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 123 125 123 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B55">55</xref>
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B56">56</xref>
###xml 151 153 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 154 156 154 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 355 359 355 359 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sfcA</italic>
###xml 361 365 361 365 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ppsA</italic>
###xml 367 371 367 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pckA</italic>
###xml 373 376 373 376 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eno</italic>
###xml 378 381 378 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pgk</italic>
###xml 383 388 383 388 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">talB </italic>
###xml 392 396 392 396 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">aldB</italic>
###xml 562 565 562 565 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eno</italic>
###xml 567 570 567 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pgk</italic>
###xml 572 576 572 576 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdh </italic>
###xml 580 588 580 588 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sucABCD </italic>
###xml 599 600 599 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
Previously, respiration-dependent regulation of some genes involved in the central metabolism was studied in experiment [5,55,56] and computationally [27,28]. Here, candidate regulatory sites were observed upstream of some more genes involved in the glycolysis, the gluconeogenesis, the pentose phosphate pathway, and the pyruvate and lactate metabolism: sfcA, ppsA, pckA, eno, pgk, talB and aldB. Unexpectedly, in the Pasteurellaceae, candidate NarP binding sites were found upstream of operons for glycolysis/gluconeogenesis and citrate cycle enzymes, such as eno, pgk, mdh and sucABCD (see Table 1).
###end p 28
###begin title 29
Metabolism of carbohydrates
###end title 29
###begin p 30
###xml 398 400 398 400 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B57">57</xref>
###xml 472 482 472 482 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ptsHI-crr </italic>
###xml 486 493 486 493 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mtlADR </italic>
###xml 567 569 567 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B58">58</xref>
###xml 573 581 573 581 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">deoCABD </italic>
###xml 694 696 694 696 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 700 705 700 705 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nagB </italic>
###xml 741 743 741 743 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
###xml 747 753 747 753 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gntXY </italic>
###xml 782 784 782 784 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>
###xml 788 793 788 793 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">malQ </italic>
###xml 808 810 808 810 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
###xml 818 827 818 827 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glgBXCAP </italic>
###xml 866 868 866 868 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B63">63</xref>
Regulation of sugar metabolism genes by the three considered regulators has not been shown experimentally. Here, conserved candidate sites for Fnr, ArcA or NarP were found upstream of some operons involved in the metabolism of various sugars. This is not surprising: indeed, the sugar metabolism is closely related to the central metabolism and sugars provide substrates for the energy production [57]. In particular, candidate sites were observed upstream of the operons ptsHI-crr and mtlADR (glucose- and mannitol-specific phosphotransferase systems, respectively [58]), deoCABD (degradation of pyrimidine deoxynucleosydes via deoxyribose-phosphates to acetaldehyde and glycerol-3-phosphate [59]), nagB (glucosamine-6-phosphate deaminase [60]), gntXY (gluconate transport system [61]), malQ (amylomaltase [62]), and glgBXCAP (glycogen biosynthesis and catabolism [63]).
###end p 30
###begin p 31
###xml 42 46 42 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glg </italic>
###xml 151 160 151 160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 193 197 193 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glg </italic>
###xml 223 228 223 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">malQ </italic>
###xml 263 277 263 277 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">malQ-glgBXCAP </italic>
###xml 293 302 293 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 355 360 355 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glgB </italic>
###xml 444 449 444 449 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">malQ </italic>
###xml 536 540 536 540 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">glg </italic>
An unusual situation was observed for the glg genes. According to criteria described in "Methods", these genes were assigned to a single operon in the Yersinia spp. In the Pasteurellaceae, the glg genes are preceded by the malQ gene that belongs to the candidate malQ-glgBXCAP operon. In both Yersinia spp., candidate Fnr sites were found upstream of the glgB gene, whereas in the Pasteurellaceae, Fnr and NarP sites were found upstream of the malQ gene. Site conservation despite operon reorganization shows that the regulation of the glg genes by respiratory factors is important for the bacterial cell.
###end p 31
###begin p 32
However, the degree of conservation of candidate sites for the carbohydrate metabolism genes is relatively low compared to genes from other groups, the observed sites are often taxon-specific, and there are almost no cases of double or triple combinations. This also is not surprising, since regulation of carbohydrate transport and catabolism is extremely flexible and fast-evolving (O.Laikova, personal communication). On the other hand, each individual predictions should be considered as preliminary.
###end p 32
###begin title 33
Fatty acids metabolism
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 178 180 178 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 181 183 181 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>
The regulation of operons involved in the fatty acids metabolism was not described previously, although there were some indirect indications to the existence of such regulation [27,64]. Some of these operons have conserved binding sites and thus are likely to be regulated.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 15 21 15 21 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fabBA </italic>
###xml 25 31 25 31 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fadIJ </italic>
###xml 263 265 263 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>
###xml 266 268 266 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B65">65</xref>
###xml 299 304 299 304 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arcA </italic>
###xml 320 326 320 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fadBA </italic>
###xml 400 402 400 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 506 507 506 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
The homologous fabBA and fadIJ operons encode protein complexes for beta-oxidation of fatty acids. The FadBA protein complex is used in the fatty acids degradation in both aerobic and anaerobic conditions, whereas FadIJ is used predominantly during anaerobiosis [64,65]. Previously an effect of the arcA mutation on the fadBA operon expression was observed, although direct regulation was not shown [13]. Here, conserved candidate sites for Fnr and/or ArcA were found upstream of both these operons (Table 1).
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 42 46 42 46 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fadD</italic>
###xml 48 57 48 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">acpP-fabF</italic>
###xml 63 68 63 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fadL </italic>
The regulation was also predicted for the fadD, acpP-fabF, and fadL operons involved in the fatty acids metabolism and transport.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 229 231 229 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B66">66</xref>
###xml 232 234 232 234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B67">67</xref>
###xml 347 349 347 349 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>
There are two possible explanations for the regulation of the fatty acids metabolism by the respiratory regulators. First, the fatty acids metabolism is closely related to the central metabolism, for example, through acetyl-CoA [66,67]. Second, different enzymes for the fatty acids oxydation are preferred under aerobic and anaerobic conditions [64].
###end p 37
###begin title 38
Nucleotide reductases
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 29 35 29 35 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nrdDG </italic>
###xml 45 53 45 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 142 147 142 147 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nrdAB</italic>
###xml 185 193 185 193 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 194 196 194 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B68">68</xref>
###xml 234 243 234 243 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 354 360 354 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nrdAB </italic>
###xml 380 390 380 390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. ducreyi</italic>
###xml 402 408 402 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nrdDG </italic>
###xml 468 473 468 473 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nrdAB</italic>
###xml 45 52 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 185 192 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 380 390 <span type="species:ncbi:730">H. ducreyi</span>
Fnr is known to activate the nrdDG operon in E. coli under anaerobiosis, whereas expression of one more operon encoding nucleotide reductase, nrdAB, is uneffected by the Fnr protein in E. coli [68]. This situation is conserved in the Yersinia spp. and the Vibrionaceae. In contrast, in the Pasteurellaceae, candidate Fnr sites were found upstream of the nrdAB operon. However, in H. ducreyi, where the nrdDG operon is absent, no candidate sites were found upstream of nrdAB. So, the overall picture seems to be that at least one nucleotide reductase operon must be transcribed constantly.
###end p 39
###begin title 40
Transport
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 106 114 106 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 159 160 159 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 106 113 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Previously, regulation by at least one of the studied regulators was shown experimentally for a number of E. coli transporter operons, see references in Table 1. Here, we identified additional transporters in the studied regulogs and demonstrated changes in the regulatory interactions.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 58 62 58 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dcuA</italic>
###xml 64 69 64 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dcuB </italic>
###xml 73 78 73 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dcuC </italic>
###xml 92 99 92 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli </italic>
###xml 124 125 124 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 243 248 243 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dcuA </italic>
###xml 252 257 252 257 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dcuB </italic>
###xml 282 291 282 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 373 377 373 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dcuC</italic>
###xml 92 98 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Previously regulation of dicarboxylate transporters genes dcuA, dcuB and dcuC was shown for E.coli (see references in Table 1). However, in other gamma-proteobacteria the regulation of these genes may be different. Thus, the regulation of the dcuA and dcuB genes is typical for the Yersinia spp. and Pasteurellaceae, whereas in Vibrionaceae the regulation was observed for dcuC.
###end p 42
###begin title 43
Regulators of transcription
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 154 157 154 157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fnr</italic>
###xml 159 163 159 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arcA</italic>
###xml 165 169 165 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narP</italic>
###xml 175 180 175 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narQ </italic>
###xml 242 250 242 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 275 276 275 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="T1">1</xref>
###xml 242 249 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Prediction of regulation of genes encoding different transcription factors seems to be one of the most interesting results of this study. Previously, the fnr, arcA, narP, and narQ genes were shown to be regulated by respiratory regulators in E. coli (see references in Table 1).
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 104 106 104 106 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B69">69</xref>
###xml 146 153 146 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 217 221 217 221 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nrf </italic>
###xml 222 224 222 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B70">70</xref>
###xml 227 231 227 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nir </italic>
###xml 232 234 232 234 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B71">71</xref>
###xml 237 241 237 241 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ndh </italic>
###xml 242 244 242 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B72">72</xref>
###xml 247 252 247 252 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">adhE </italic>
###xml 253 255 253 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B73">73</xref>
###xml 258 263 258 263 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfiD </italic>
###xml 264 266 264 266 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B74">74</xref>
###xml 269 285 269 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sdhCDAB-sucABCD </italic>
###xml 286 288 286 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B75">75</xref>
###xml 291 296 291 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">acnB </italic>
###xml 297 299 297 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B76">76</xref>
###xml 306 311 306 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narK </italic>
###xml 312 314 312 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B77">77</xref>
###xml 328 335 328 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 341 345 341 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fis </italic>
###xml 372 377 372 377 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dusB </italic>
###xml 378 380 378 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B78">78</xref>
###xml 407 416 407 416 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dusB-fis </italic>
###xml 539 544 539 544 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dusB </italic>
###xml 573 577 573 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fis </italic>
###xml 783 787 783 787 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fis </italic>
###xml 146 153 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 328 335 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Fis is one of the most abundant DNA architectural proteins. It regulates supercoiling of bacterial DNA [69]. Experimentally it was shown that, in E. coli, Fis together with Fnr and/or ArcA regulates expression of the nrf [70], nir [71], ndh [72], adhE [73], yfiD [74], sdhCDAB-sucABCD [75], acnB [76], and narK [77] operons. In E. coli, the fis gene is cotranscribed with dusB [78] and the structure of the dusB-fis locus is conserved in all studied genomes. In the Pasteurellaceae, candidate Fnr and ArcA sites were found upstream of the dusB gene. Thus, in these genomes fis transcription is likely to be controlled by the respiratory regulators. Since Fis participates in the control of expression of respiration genes, it is possible that Fnr and ArcA regulate expression of the fis gene to optimize the transcription control of these genes.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 4 10 4 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cpxRA </italic>
###xml 106 108 106 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B79">79</xref>
###xml 196 204 196 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 205 207 205 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B80">80</xref>
###xml 293 297 293 297 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cpx </italic>
###xml 196 203 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The cpxRA operon encodes a two-component regulatory system involved in the environmental stress response [79]. Some data point to cross-interactions between the CpxR-CpxA and ArcA-ArcB systems in E. coli [80]. In the Pasteurellaceae, conserved Fnr and ArcA sites were detected upstream of the cpx operon.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 70 72 70 72 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B81">81</xref>
###xml 146 151 146 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">oxyR </italic>
###xml 349 353 349 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">oxyR</italic>
OxyR is the transcriptional factor for the oxidative stress response [81]. In all Pasteurellaceae, candidate ArcA sites were observed upstream of oxyR gene. Since ArcA is active as a transcription factor only in the absence of the oxygen, it may be possible that in these conditions it represses the transcription of the unneeded oxygen sensor gene oxyR. However, the regulatory logic in this case is not absolutely clear.
###end p 47
###begin p 48
###xml 71 75 71 75 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fur </italic>
###xml 114 118 114 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fur </italic>
###xml 216 218 216 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B82">82</xref>
###xml 355 356 355 356 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 401 403 401 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B83">83</xref>
In the Pasteurellaceae, conserved Fnr sites were found upstrean of the fur gene, whereas in the Vibrionaceae, the fur gene has candidate sites for ArcA. The observation that the Fur protein, ferric uptake regulator [82], is controlled by the respiratory regulators is not unexpected, because iron is an obligatory component of most respiratory complexes [2] and the transcriptional factor Fnr itself [83].
###end p 48
###begin title 49
Other members of regulogs
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 306 307 306 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
###xml 312 313 312 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S5">5</xref>
In addition to the regulogs members described above, some genes were assigned to regulogs based on the formal criterion (see "Methods"), although their relevance to the respiration and central metabolism is not obvious. These genes and the candidate Fnr, ArcA, and NarP sites are shown in additional files 4 and 5. Although in the absence of a functional link, these predictions are somewhat weaker then the ones in the previous sections, we belive that they still warrant experimental verification.
###end p 50
###begin title 51
Divergons and false-positive predictions
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 227 242 227 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fadR/nhaB-dsbB </italic>
###xml 267 287 267 287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yfiC/brnQ, torD/torR</italic>
###xml 293 309 293 309 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">yhdWXYZ/VV12702 </italic>
###xml 334 344 334 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fkpA/slyX </italic>
###xml 351 360 351 360 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 480 481 480 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S5">5</xref>
In some cases we found sites in intergenic regions between two genes forming divergons. In such cases it is not immediately clear, which of the two divergently transcribed operons is regulated. These divergent operon pairs are fadR/nhaB-dsbB in most studied genomes, yfiC/brnQ, torD/torR, and yhdWXYZ/VV12702 in the Vibrionaceae, and fkpA/slyX in the Yersinia spp. and the Pasteurellaceae. The data about candidate sites upstream of divergent genes are present in additional file 5.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 180 184 180 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">argR</italic>
###xml 186 189 186 189 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ung</italic>
###xml 191 195 191 195 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nfo </italic>
###xml 199 207 199 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">potABCD </italic>
###xml 232 233 232 233 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S6">6</xref>
In other cases candidate regulog members in divergons were dismissed, as functional analysis made it clear which of two divergent operons was regulated. This category includes the argR, ung, nfo and potABCD operons (additional file 6).
###end p 53
###begin title 54
Discussion
###end title 54
###begin title 55
Composition of regulogs in different families
###end title 55
###begin p 56
The above analysis shows that regulation often differs between bacterial families. To assess the relative role of each studied regulator in different families, we calculated the number of genes and operons belonging to each regulog in each studied family. As expected, the composition of regulogs turned out to be quite diverse.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 20 29 20 29 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 116 118 116 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1a</xref>
###xml 305 307 305 307 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
For example, in the Yersinia spp., the NarP regulog is significantly smaller than in other studied families (Figure 1a). It is not surprising, given that in these bacteria the nitrate- and nitrite-respiration system is strongly reduced, and only nitrate, but not nitrite, is used as an electron acceptor [24]. Accordingly, in these organisms, the nitrate respiration plays a less prominent role, and the nitrate-sensing system also is less important as compared to other studied genomes.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 310 312 310 312 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1b</xref>
In contrast, in Pasteurellaceae, the NarP regulog is somewhat enlarged in comparison with the other two taxons. One more feature of this group is the increased overlap between regulogs. For example, conserved sites for both Fnr and NarP regulators were found upstream of 13 operons containing 53 genes (Figure 1b).
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 182 184 182 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1c</xref>
In the Vibrionaceae, the ArcA regulog is extended as compared to the other groups. In this family, conserved ArcA sites were found upstream of 47 operons containing 99 genes (Figure 1c).
###end p 59
###begin title 60
Regulatory cascades
###end title 60
###begin p 61
###xml 91 100 91 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fnr, arcA</italic>
###xml 106 111 106 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narP </italic>
Group-specific regulatory cascades were predicted based on candidate sites upstream of the fnr, arcA, and narP genes.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 3 10 3 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 63 65 63 65 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2a</xref>
###xml 68 69 68 69 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 70 71 70 71 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 72 74 72 74 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B84">84</xref>
###xml 232 238 232 238 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narXL </italic>
###xml 246 247 246 247 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 281 286 281 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arcA </italic>
###xml 292 293 292 293 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 310 314 310 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fnr </italic>
###xml 337 338 337 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 345 350 345 350 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arcA </italic>
###xml 401 403 401 403 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 548 560 548 560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narXL, narP </italic>
###xml 564 569 564 569 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narQ </italic>
###xml 611 617 611 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narXL </italic>
###xml 632 634 632 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B84">84</xref>
###xml 3 10 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
In E. coli, such cascades were analyzed experimentally (Figure 2a) [6,7,84]. There, the Fnr protein is the main regulator of the respiration that controls expression of genes for other regulators. Fnr represses transcription of the narXL operon [6] and regulates expression of the arcA gene [7]. Moreover, the fnr gene is autoregulated [6]. The arcA gene was predicted to be positively autoregulated [28]. The regulators of the nitrate and nitrate respiration, in turn, form a complicated regulatory network, where NarL activates expression of the narXL, narP and narQ operons, and NarP, in turn, activates the narXL transcription [84].
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 94 103 94 103 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 138 143 138 143 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arcA </italic>
###xml 163 167 163 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fnr </italic>
###xml 171 176 171 176 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arcA </italic>
###xml 208 210 208 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2b</xref>
###xml 260 265 260 265 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narP </italic>
###xml 269 274 269 274 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narX </italic>
###xml 294 303 294 303 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 364 371 364 371 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 425 434 425 434 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 364 371 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
However, this situation is not conserved in the genomes of other gamma-proteobacteria. In the Yersinia spp., Fnr apparently regulates the arcA expression and both fnr and arcA genes are autoregulated (Figure 2b). No regulatory sites were found upstream of the narP and narX genes. Thus, in the Yersinia spp., Fnr is the main regulator of respiration, similarly to E. coli. However, unlike the situation in the latter, in the Yersinia spp., the nitrate- and nitrite-responsive regulatory system does not participate in the regulatory cascades.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 186 190 186 190 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fnr </italic>
###xml 203 205 203 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2c</xref>
###xml 268 273 268 273 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arcA </italic>
###xml 277 282 277 282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narP </italic>
###xml 395 402 395 402 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 435 445 435 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. ducreyi</italic>
###xml 494 498 494 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fnr </italic>
###xml 511 513 511 513 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2c</xref>
###xml 395 402 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 435 445 <span type="species:ncbi:730">H. ducreyi</span>
Even more remarkable changes in the regulatory cascades were observed in the Pasteurellaceae. In most of these genomes, candidate sites for Fnr, ArcA and NarP were found upstream of the fnr gene (Figure 2c), whereas no conserved regulatory sites were observed for the arcA and narP genes. This suggests that in this family the role of Fnr in the regulatory cascades decreases in comparison with E. coli. Minor differences were seen in H. ducreyi, where no NarP sites were found upstream of the fnr gene (Figure 2c).
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 61 71 61 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fnr, arcA </italic>
###xml 75 79 75 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narP</italic>
###xml 139 144 139 144 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narP </italic>
###xml 193 198 193 198 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">arcA </italic>
###xml 202 207 202 207 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narP </italic>
###xml 221 223 221 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2d</xref>
###xml 373 380 373 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 373 380 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
In the Vibrionaceae, autoregulation for all regulator genes, fnr, arcA and narP, was observed. The ArcA protein also seems to regulate the narP gene, whereas Fnr controls the expression of the arcA and narP genes (Figure 2d). Thus, in these organisms, Fnr again is the main respiration regulator, but the role of ArcA in the regulatory cascades is increased as compared to E. coli.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 10 17 10 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E.coli </italic>
###xml 10 16 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E.coli</span>
Thus, the E.coli pattern of regulatory cascades is not conserved in other gamma-proteobacteria, and the relative role of each regulator in the regulatory cascades differs between families. When more genomes become available, it might be possible to describe the evolution of this regulatory system in more detail.
###end p 66
###begin title 67
Family-specific regulatory interactions
###end title 67
###begin p 68
###xml 174 175 174 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 177 178 177 178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 180 181 180 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
In addition to differences in the regulatory cacades, there are major differences in the functional content of the respiration regulogs. These data are summarized in Figures 3, 4, 5.
###end p 68
###begin title 69
###xml 20 32 20 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia spp</italic>
Enterobacteriaceae (Yersinia spp.)
###end title 69
###begin p 70
###xml 19 28 19 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 243 251 243 251 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 294 295 294 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 243 250 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Two genomes of the Yersinia spp. were selected for this study because of the presence of a single regulator of the nitrate and nitrite respiration regulator NarP. These bacteria demonstrate a number of features that distinguish them from both E. coli and the two other studied families (Figure 3).
###end p 70
###begin p 71
Most of these features relate to the nitrate and nitrite respiration. First of all, regulation is mediated by the unusual pair NarX-NarP, in contrast to other bacteria that have the pair NarQ-NarP (for details see below).
###end p 71
###begin p 72
###xml 23 32 23 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 125 132 125 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli</italic>
###xml 451 470 451 470 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nap, ccm, fdo, fdhD</italic>
###xml 493 497 493 497 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nir </italic>
###xml 125 132 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Another feature of the Yersinia spp. regulation is simplification of the regulatory cascades (see above). In comparison with E. coli, only the Fnr-ArcA cascade is conserved, whereas nitrate and nitrite responsive proteins seem to be excluded from cascades. This has an effect on the NarP regulog: in the respiration subsystem, candidate sites were found only upstream of genes essential for the nitrate respiration using formate as an electron donor (nap, ccm, fdo, fdhD), and upstream of the nir operon, whose products protect the cell from the toxic nitrite.
###end p 72
###begin p 73
On the contrary, the Fnr and ArcA regulogs seemed to be quite similar to the corresponding regulogs in other studied genomes.
###end p 73
###begin title 74
Pasteurellaceae
###end title 74
###begin p 75
These bacteria demonstrate considerable changes in the regulatory cascade structure (see above). In the organisms from this group, Fnr loses the role of the main regulator.
###end p 75
###begin p 76
###xml 166 188 166 188 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Shewanella oneidensis </italic>
###xml 194 196 194 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B85">85</xref>
###xml 323 329 323 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cpxRA </italic>
###xml 143 164 <span type="species:ncbi:86473">gamma-proteobacterium</span>
###xml 166 192 <span type="species:ncbi:211586">Shewanella oneidensis MR-1</span>
A conspicious feature is the absence of orthologs for the ArcA-activating sensor ArcB (data not shown). Such situation was observed in another gamma-proteobacterium, Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 [85]. It is possible that the role of the ArcA-activating protein is played by CpxA that is homologous to ArcB. Regulation of the cpxRA operon by Fnr and ArcA also points to this possibility.
###end p 76
###begin p 77
###xml 136 153 136 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cpxRA, fis, oxyR </italic>
###xml 157 160 157 160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fur</italic>
###xml 234 237 234 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">fur</italic>
A specific feature of the Pasteurellaceae is also the existence of downstream cascades, as several transcription factor-encoding genes, cpxRA, fis, oxyR and fur, are preceded by conserved candidate sites. All of these operons, except fur, have no candidate sites in other studied genomes. One possible explanation might be that drastic re-structuring of the regulatory cascades during evolution of the Pasteurellaceae required additional fine tuning of the respiratory metabolism.
###end p 77
###begin p 78
###xml 142 143 142 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>
###xml 221 225 221 225 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cyd </italic>
###xml 229 233 229 233 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nqr </italic>
###xml 327 336 327 336 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdh, eno </italic>
###xml 340 344 340 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">suc </italic>
###xml 528 532 528 532 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">eno </italic>
###xml 536 541 536 541 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">talB </italic>
The increased role of the NarP transcriptional factor in the regulatory cascades is consistent with the expansion of the NarP regulog (Figure 4). This expansion is realized in different ways. For example, upstream of the cyd and nqr operons, NarP sites emerged in addition to the existing Fnr and ArcA ones. In the case of the mdh, eno and suc operons, we observe a change in the regulatory interactions, where NarP candidate sites appear instead of Fnr of ArcA sites seen in other families. Finally, NarP sites upstream of the eno and talB genes appear in the Pasteurellaceae, while there are no sites for the studied factors in other genomes.
###end p 78
###begin title 79
Vibrionaceae
###end title 79
###begin p 80
In these genomes, the Fnr role as the main regulator is conserved, but the role of the ArcA in the regulatory cascades slightly increases.
###end p 80
###begin p 81
###xml 134 135 134 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">5</xref>
###xml 234 235 234 235 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S4">4</xref>
Again, this agrees with the expansion of the ArcA regulog, as there appear ArcA sites upstream of genes of central metabolism (Figure 5). Conserved candidate ArcA binding sites were also found upstream of other genes (additional file 4).
###end p 81
###begin p 82
###xml 89 100 89 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">H. ducreyi </italic>
###xml 125 127 125 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B86">86</xref>
###xml 130 139 130 139 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y. pestis</italic>
###xml 141 158 141 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y. enterocolitica</italic>
###xml 160 172 160 172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P. multocida</italic>
###xml 174 198 174 198 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A. actinomycetemcomitans</italic>
###xml 215 228 215 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. vulnificus</italic>
###xml 230 250 230 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. parahaemolyticus </italic>
###xml 254 266 254 266 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. cholerae </italic>
###xml 293 295 293 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B87">87</xref>
###xml 296 298 296 298 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B94">94</xref>
###xml 305 317 305 317 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">V. fischeri </italic>
###xml 338 340 338 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B95">95</xref>
###xml 89 99 <span type="species:ncbi:730">H. ducreyi</span>
###xml 130 139 <span type="species:ncbi:632">Y. pestis</span>
###xml 141 158 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
###xml 160 172 <span type="species:ncbi:747">P. multocida</span>
###xml 174 198 <span type="species:ncbi:714">A. actinomycetemcomitans</span>
###xml 200 213 <span type="species:ncbi:727">H. influenzae</span>
###xml 215 228 <span type="species:ncbi:672">V. vulnificus</span>
###xml 230 249 <span type="species:ncbi:670">V. parahaemolyticus</span>
###xml 254 265 <span type="species:ncbi:666">V. cholerae</span>
###xml 305 316 <span type="species:ncbi:668">V. fischeri</span>
Ten analyzed representatives of gamma-proteobacteria occupy different ecological niches: H. ducreyi is an obligate pathogen [86], Y. pestis, Y. enterocolitica, P. multocida, A. actinomycetemcomitans, H. influenzae, V. vulnificus, V. parahaemolyticus and V. cholerae are facultative pathogens [87-94], and V. fischeri is a squid symbiont [95]. However, there are no obvious links between the bacterial lifestyle and specific regulatory interactions or regulog composition.
###end p 82
###begin title 83
Variability of the nitrate and nitrite responsive regulatory system
###end title 83
###begin p 84
Comparison of three global transcription regulators, Fnr, ArcA and NarP, demonstrates that the NarP-dependent regulation is the most flexible.
###end p 84
###begin p 85
###xml 334 343 334 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nap, ccm </italic>
###xml 347 351 347 351 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nrf </italic>
###xml 435 443 435 443 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narGHJI </italic>
###xml 447 451 447 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">narK</italic>
###xml 494 503 494 503 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 542 546 542 546 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nir </italic>
###xml 572 574 572 574 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 744 745 744 745 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
As we could not construct a NarL-site recognition rule, we considered only genomes containing NarP but not NarL. In all these genomes, the cytoplasmic nitrate respiratory system is absent and all reactions for the reduction of nitrate through nitrite to ammonium must occur in the periplasm. For this purpose, the proteins encoded by nap, ccm and nrf are sufficient. On the other hand, the genes for the cytoplasmic nitrate reduction, narGHJI and narK, are absent. In some studied genomes, the Yersinia spp. and most of the Vibrionaceae, the nir operon also is conserved [24]. This operon is used for the cytoplasmic nitrite reduction to ammonium, neutralizing toxic nitrite and providing ammonium as a substrate for the nitrogen assimilation [2]. Without the use of the cytoplasmic nitrite reduction, there is no need to select between the more effective, but also more dangerous, nitrate reduction in the cytoplasm, and the less effective but safer periplasmic reduction. Thus, all studied organisms do not require duplicated two-component regulatory systems and use the single pair NarP-NarQ.
###end p 85
###begin p 86
###xml 7 16 7 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 97 98 97 98 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S7">7</xref>
###xml 101 103 101 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 215 217 215 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 218 220 218 220 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 259 268 259 268 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
In the Yersinia spp., an unusual sensor-regulator pair, NarX-NarP, was observed (additional file 7) [24]. However, in these genomes the sensor protein lacks the cysteine cluster typical for NarX from other species [23,24]. Thus, we can suppose that NarX from Yersinia spp. acts as NarQ.
###end p 86
###begin p 87
###xml 170 193 170 193 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">mdh, eno, pgk, sucABCD </italic>
###xml 197 201 197 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nqr </italic>
###xml 225 231 225 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cydAB </italic>
###xml 427 429 427 429 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B96">96</xref>
Another interesting result was the observation of conserved candidate NarP sites upstream of genes for aerobic respiration, glycolysis/gluconeogenesis and citrate cycle, mdh, eno, pgk, sucABCD and nqr in Pasteurellaceae, and cydAB in the Pasteurellaceae and the Vibrionaceae. Regulation of genes for the aerobic metabolism by NarQ-NarP system may be explained by the fact that the NarQ sensor protein is sensitive to aeration [96].
###end p 87
###begin title 88
Conclusion
###end title 88
###begin p 89
###xml 94 111 94 111 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Escherichia coli </italic>
###xml 94 110 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
Previously the regulation of respiration in the gamma-proteobacteria was well studied only in Escherichia coli and two experimental approaches prevailed. The first one was studying the regulation of a particular operon by multiple factors. The second approach was the analysis of multiple genes controlled by a specific regulatory system in one genome. With sequencing of complete genomes and development of comparative genomic methods, extensive analysis of a complex regulatory system in multiple genomes has become possible.
###end p 89
###begin p 90
Here we applied a comparative genomic technique to the analysis of global regulation of respiration in numerous gamma-proteobacterial genomes. We analyzed three structurally different but functionally related regulatory systems.
###end p 90
###begin p 91
One of the advantages of the applied technique is the possibility to study genomes for which no experimental data are available. It also allows one to find new members of regulons and to detect taxon-specific features of regulation. Thus, the saturation of the taxonomic space by complete genome sequences increases the opportunities for the application of comparative genomics.
###end p 91
###begin p 92
In this article and supplementary materials we present the data about candidate sites, both conserved and non-conserved, in multiple genomes. These data are interesting from the point of view of evolution and also may be of use for experimental biology. At that, prediction of the specific sites upstream of particular operons facilitates the work of experimentalist in finding new members of regulons.
###end p 92
###begin p 93
###xml 436 438 436 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B97">97</xref>
###xml 459 461 459 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B98">98</xref>
In the particular case of the respiration regulation, we were able to predict not only taxon-specific members of regulons, but also taxon-specific changes in regulatory cascades. We demonstrated considerable changes of regulatory interactions in different families and relative stability of the metabolic and regulatory systems taken as a whole. Similar observations were made in studies of dissimilatory metabolism of nitrogen oxides [97], iron homeostasis [98] and nitrogen fixation (work in progress). Overall, existence of a stable system core, re-wiring of regulatory cascades accompanied by shuffling of the regulators, and existence of taxon-specific periphery seem to be common features of all complex regulatory systems. At present, it is not clear what drives these changes. We have observed a correlation between the regulon size and the position of a regulator in regulatory cascades: regulators with larger regulons tend to occupy top positions in cascaeds, and thus re-wiring of cascades is accompanied with extension of regulons. On the other hand, there is no obvious link to differences in the species' lifestyles, metabolic capabilities, etc.

###begin title 99
Comparative genomics of family-specific regulation
###end title 99
###begin p 100
###xml 92 95 92 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B109">109</xref>
###xml 286 289 286 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B110">110</xref>
###xml 553 556 553 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B109">109</xref>
###xml 567 569 567 569 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>
###xml 578 580 578 580 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>
###xml 592 594 592 594 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 697 698 697 698 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S8">8</xref>
###xml 703 704 703 704 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S9">9</xref>
To describe three interacting regulatory systems, comparative genomic approaches were used [109]. Candidate sites were identified in upstream regions of annotated genes, including hypothetical ones. A gene was considered a putative member of a regulog (a group of regulated orthologs) [110], if it had an upstream candidate site in several genomes. Site search was performed in gene upstream regions, -500 to +100 nucleotides relative to the gene start, excluding coding regions of upstream genes. The recognition profiles (nucleotide weight matrices) [109] for Fnr [27], ArcA [28] and NarP [24] were described previously. The recognition profiles and sequence logos are shown in additional files 8 and 9, respectively.
###end p 100
###begin p 101
###xml 70 78 70 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 114 117 114 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B111">111</xref>
###xml 124 132 124 132 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 185 193 185 193 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E. coli </italic>
###xml 251 255 251 255 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">pflA</italic>
###xml 549 565 549 565 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nap, nrf and ccm</italic>
###xml 70 77 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 124 131 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 185 192 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The cutoff profile scores for ArcA and FNR sites were set using known E. coli sites from the dpinteract database [111]. All E. coli FNR-binding sites passed the threshold 3.75, and all E. coli ArcA binding sites, but one outlier (upstream of the gene pflA), scored above the used threshold 4.00. With these parameters about 600 genes per genome had candidate sites in upstream regions. The cutoff for candidate NarP binding sites (3.50) was defined as the minimum score of candidate NarP sites upstream of the nitrate and nitrite respiration genes (nap, nrf and ccm) in all studied genomes. This cutoff produced about 300 genes per genome.
###end p 101
###begin p 102
Clearly, most candidate sites at this stage were false positives, but the threshold could not be made stricter without loss of true sites. Anyhow, after the filtration procedure described below the number of candidate regulog members significantly decreased. Only few genes with no obvious functional link to the studied systems remained, and in most cases this was caused by sites occurring upstream of divergently transcribed genes. These observations demonstrate that the filtration procedure seems to be sufficiently effective to remove most false positives. On the other hand, each individual prediction should be considered as preliminary. Another caveat is that "orphan" sites (species-specific sites upstream of genes not regulated by other considered factors) cannot be identified by the applied procedure.
###end p 102
###begin p 103
###xml 362 365 362 365 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B111">111</xref>
Possible operon structure was taken into account as follows. Genes were assumed to belong to one operon if (i) they were transcribed in the same direction, (ii) the intergenic distances did not exceed 200 nucleotides, and (iii) two previous conditions were met in genomes from different families. These conditions are known to perform well in operon prediction [111].
###end p 103
###begin p 104
###xml 436 445 436 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Yersinia </italic>
###xml 539 549 539 549 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y. pestis </italic>
###xml 553 571 553 571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y. enterocolitica </italic>
###xml 539 548 <span type="species:ncbi:632">Y. pestis</span>
###xml 553 570 <span type="species:ncbi:630">Y. enterocolitica</span>
As the regulation of respiration had not been experimentally analyzed in any of the studied organisms, we developed a technique based on exhaustive pairwise comparison of genomes within taxonomic groups. For the Pasteurellaceae and the Vibrionaceae, a gene was assigned to a regulog if candidate regulatory sites were found upstream of an operon containing this gene in at least three genomes of organisms from the same family. For the Yersinia spp., a gene was considered to be a member of a regulog if candidate sites were found in both Y. pestis and Y. enterocolitica genomes. Such sites were called "conserved sites". Sites that did not satisfy these requirements were called "nonconserved". Further, if a site for one regulator was found upstream of a particular gene in one taxon, we searched for sites for all three regulators upstream of this gene and its orthologs in all studied genomes.
###end p 104
###begin p 105
This approach allowed us to find new members of regulogs and to describe taxon-specific features of regulation. Its limitation is that it allows one to detect only the fact of regulation but, due to the absence of mapped promoters in the studied genomes, does not predict the type of regulation, such as repression or activation.
###end p 105
