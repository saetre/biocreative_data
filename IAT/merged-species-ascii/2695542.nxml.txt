Impaired Interleukin-1beta and c-Fos Expression in the Hippocampus Is Associated with a Spatial Memory Deficit in P2X7 Receptor-Deficient Mice
###end article-title 0

###xml 758 762 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Recent evidence suggests that interleukin-1beta (IL-1beta), which was originally identified as a proinflammatory cytokine, is also required in the brain for memory processes. We have previously shown that IL-1beta synthesis in the hippocampus is dependent on P2X7 receptor (P2X7R), which is an ionotropic receptor of ATP. To substantiate the role of P2X7R in both brain IL-1beta expression and memory processes, we examined the induction of IL-1beta mRNA expression in the hippocampus of wild-type (WT) and homozygous P2X7 receptor knockout mice (P2X7R-/-) following a spatial memory task. The spatial recognition task induced both IL-1beta mRNA expression and c-Fos protein activation in the hippocampus of WT but not of P2X7R-/- mice. Remarkably, P2X7R-/- mice displayed spatial memory impairment in a hippocampal-dependant task, while their performances in an object recognition task were unaltered. Taken together, our results show that P2X7R plays a critical role in spatial memory processes and the associated hippocampal IL-1beta mRNA synthesis and c-Fos activation.

###begin title 3
Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 214 217 207 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Dinarello1">[1]</xref>
###xml 327 330 320 323 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Rothwell1">[2]</xref>
###xml 488 491 481 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Coogan1">[3]</xref>
###xml 602 605 595 598 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Avital1">[4]</xref>
###xml 606 609 599 602 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Yirmiya1">[7]</xref>
###xml 806 809 799 802 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Yirmiya1">[7]</xref>
###xml 900 903 893 896 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Avital1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1066 1069 1049 1052 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Yirmiya1">[7]</xref>
###xml 1240 1248 1220 1228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1253 1260 1233 1240 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1260 1263 1240 1243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Goshen1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1265 1268 1245 1248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Schneider1">[6]</xref>
###xml 834 838 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The interleukin-1 (IL-1) family is composed of three closely-related proteins that are products of different genes: two agonists, IL-1alpha and IL-1beta, and a naturally-occurring IL-1 receptor antagonist (IL-1ra) [1]. All the members of this family are synthesized and/or released in the brain by both glial cells and neurons [2]. Although most of the evidence gathered so far indicates that pathophysiological levels of IL-1 detrimentally affects neural plasticity and memory processes [3], recent evidence suggests that this cytokine is required for the physiological regulation of memory processes [4]-[7]. Blocking IL-1 receptors with IL-1ra impairs the maintenance of long-term potentiation (LTP) and hippocampal-dependent memory performance as measured in water maze and passive avoidance paradigms [7]. IL-1 receptor knockout mice exhibit a marked inability to express LTP in the hippocampus [4]. IL-1beta, rather than IL-1alpha, could be involved in memory processes, since IL-1beta administration improves avoidance memory and contextual fear conditioning [7]. This is further reinforced by the observation that hippocampal IL-1beta mRNA expression is increased substantially after contextual fear conditioning and after LTP both in vitro and in vivo[5], [6].
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 128 131 125 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Ferrari1">[8]</xref>
###xml 331 334 328 331 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-DiVirgilio1">[9]</xref>
###xml 425 428 419 422 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Rothwell1">[2]</xref>
###xml 441 442 435 436 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 444 448 438 442 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Ferrari2">[10]</xref>
###xml 450 454 444 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Yu1">[11]</xref>
###xml 472 473 466 467 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 592 595 580 583 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Ferrari1">[8]</xref>
###xml 629 637 617 625 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 772 776 757 761 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Mingam1">[12]</xref>
###xml 778 782 763 767 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Sanz1">[13]</xref>
###xml 784 791 769 776 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In vivo</italic>
###xml 872 873 854 855 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 874 877 856 859 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 905 909 887 891 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Mingam1">[12]</xref>
###xml 878 882 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 900 904 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Extracellular ATP has been reported to trigger the release of IL-1beta by binding to type 2 purinergic receptors (P2 receptors) [8]. These receptors are subdivided into two subgroups: P2Y metabotropic receptors (P2YR), which are G protein-coupled receptors, and P2X ionotropic receptors (P2XR), which are ligand-gated ion channels [9]. Interestingly, microglial cells, which are the main cell source of IL-1beta in the brain [2], express P2X7R [10], [11]. ATP binds to P2X7R to induce the activation of caspase-1, the IL-1beta processing enzyme, also releasing mature IL-1beta in the process [8]. We and others have demonstrated in vitro that ATP triggers the maturation and release of IL-1beta in response to the bacterial endotoxin lipopolysaccharide (LPS) in microglia [12], [13]. In vivo, LPS-induced IL-1beta mRNA expression is significantly lower in the brain of P2X7R-/- mice compared with WT mice [12].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 283 284 277 278 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 358 362 349 353 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Mingam1">[12]</xref>
###xml 460 463 448 451 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Goshen1">[5]</xref>
###xml 535 536 523 524 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 655 656 640 641 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 657 660 642 645 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 827 828 812 813 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 829 832 814 817 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1055 1056 1037 1038 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 1057 1060 1039 1042 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 661 665 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 833 837 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1061 1065 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Although the last decade has seen a wealth of knowledge on the effects of IL-1beta in the hippocampus, much less is known about the mechanism of IL-1beta release under physiological conditions, especially during hippocampal-dependent memory processes. We have recently identified P2X7R as a key component in LPS-mediated mature IL-1beta release by microglia [12], and it has been shown that IL-1beta synthesis is induced in the hippocampus by memory processes [5]. Hence, the aim of the present study was to investigate the role of P2X7R in memory processes and hippocampal IL-1beta production. For this purpose, we examined the memory performances of P2X7R-/- mice in two spontaneous recognition paradigms (Y maze and object recognition). Our results show that after 30 minutes of retention, spatial memory was impaired in P2X7R-/- mice, while their performances were unaltered in an object recognition memory task. Moreover, the induction of IL-1beta synthesis and c-Fos expression in the hippocampus in response to the spatial task was abolished in P2X7R-/- mice, suggesting that hippocampal IL-1beta production may be involved in spatial memory performance.
###end p 6

Animal experiments were carried out according to the INRA Quality Reference System (1), and to relevant French (Directive 87-148, Ministere de l'Agriculture et de la Peche) and international (Directive 86-609, November 24th 1986, European Community) legislation. Every effort was made to minimize suffering and the number of animals used. Experiments were performed on 3-month-old C57BL/6 (WT, n = 33) and homozygous P2X7R-/- (n = 33) male mice weighing 25-30 g. The P2X7R-/- mice were raised on a C57Bl/6 background and were kindly provided by Dr. Gabel (Pfizer, Groton, USA) [14]. ###begin title 12
Spatial recognition
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 91 95 91 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Conrad1">[16]</xref>
###xml 154 158 154 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Dellu1">[17]</xref>
###xml 159 163 159 163 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Palin1">[20]</xref>
###xml 1436 1440 1436 1440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Dellu1">[17]</xref>
###xml 1624 1628 1624 1628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Mayo1">[21]</xref>
###xml 525 530 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 805 809 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 940 944 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1102 1107 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The spontaneous spatial recognition in the Y-maze was used as a hippocampal-dependent test [16] as part of a two-trial procedure, as previously described [17]-[20]. The apparatus was a Y-shaped maze made of gray plastic. Each arm was 34 cm long, 8 cm wide and 14 cm high. Extramaze visual cues were placed in the testing room and kept constant during the test. Because the three arms of the maze were identical, discrimination of novelty versus familiarity was only based on the different aspects of the environment that the mouse can perceive from each of them. The floor of the maze was covered with dirty sawdust from the home cages of several animals, and was mixed between sessions in order to eliminate olfactory cues. In the first trial, one arm of the Y-maze was closed with a guillotine door and mice (n = 9/experimental group) were allowed to visit two arms of the Y-maze for 5 min. After a 30 min inter-trial interval (ITI), the mice were again placed in the start arm for the trial 2 and allowed free access to all three arms for 5 min. Start and closed arms were randomly assigned to each mouse. Arm entries were defined as all four paws entering the arm. Preference for novelty was also measured using a 2-min ITI between acquisition and retrieval. This 2 min ITI was employed to control for potential motivational disturbances and verify that all groups made more visits to the novel arm when mnemonic demand was minimal [17]. The different ITI tests were separated by a 7-day interval. Analyses were based on the time spent exploring the novel and the familiar arms during the first 3 minutes of the trial 2 [21].
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Object recognition
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 78 82 78 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Dere1">[22]</xref>
###xml 83 87 83 87 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Winters1">[25]</xref>
###xml 945 949 945 949 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Dere1">[22]</xref>
###xml 89 93 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 307 311 <span type="species:ncbi:381124">corn</span>
The visual object recognition test was used as a hippocampal-independent task [22]-[25]. Mice (n = 9/experimental group) were acclimatized in a 40x40 cm square cage made of white coated plywood with 16 cm-high walls for 15 min per day during the week before training. The floor of the maze was covered with corn cob litter which was mixed between each trial in order to remove olfactory cues. During a 10-min training period, two identical plastic blocks with particular shapes and colors were presented. After a 30 min ITI, one of the familiar blocks was replaced by a novel object, with a different shape and color, to test for memory retention. During the 5 min of testing, exploration of an object was defined as pointing the nose to the object and/or touching the object with the nose. Longer ITI (1-hour and 24-hour) were also employed to determine whether all groups recognized the novel object when retention was longer (data not shown) [22]. Analyses were based on the time spent exploring the novel and the familiar objects and the object recognition score was calculated as the difference in time spent exploring the novel and the familiar objects.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
IL-1beta real-time PCR
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 10 11 10 11 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 12 15 12 15 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 680 684 680 684 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">i.e.</italic>
###xml 16 20 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 232 236 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
WT and P2X7R-/- mice were sacrificed with a lethal dose of pentobarbital immediately after completion of behavioral testing [2-min ITI or 30-min ITI Y-maze (Y-maze group) or free exploratory behavior (control group)]. Control-group mice were allowed to freely explore a new cage for the same amount of time as their spatially-trained counterparts (i.e. twice during 5 min each with either a 2-min ITI or a 30-min ITI) in the experimental Y-maze room. This control condition allowed the assessment of activation that results from exploratory behavior, sensorimotor stimulation, stress, and other factors not related to the spatial task. Hence, difference between the two profiles, i.e. the activation induced in the Y-maze group and in the control group, may reflect specific learning-induced changes.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
###xml 491 495 487 491 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Mingam1">[12]</xref>
###xml 908 912 898 902 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Mingam1">[12]</xref>
###xml 1553 1566 1515 1520 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;&#916;&#916;Ct</sup>
###xml 0 4 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
###xml 269 275 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Murine</span>
###xml 1262 1266 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Mice (n = 6/experimental group) were transcardially perfused with Phosphate Buffer Saline (PBS) and the hippocampi and hypothalamus were quickly removed and frozen in dry ice. Two microg of total RNA obtained from each brain area were reverse-transcripted with Moloney Murine Leukemia Virus reverse transcriptase (Invitrogen, Cergy-Pontoise, France). Quantitative PCR was then performed using the Applied Biosystems Assay-on-Demand Gene Expression Products protocol, as previously described [12]. Briefly, cDNAs for IL-1beta and housekeeping gene (beta2-microglobulin) were amplified by PCR by using an oligonucleotide probe with a 5' fluorescent reporter dye (6-FAM) and a 3' quencher dye (NFQ). Fluorescence was determined using an AB 7500 Real-Time PCR system (Applied Biosystems, Foster city, CA) and final quantification was performed using the comparative threshold (Ct) method as previously described [12]. For each experimental sample, the difference between target and housekeeping gene Ct values (DeltaCt) was used to normalize for differences in the amount of total nucleic acid added to each reaction and in the efficiency of the RT step. Values were then expressed as relative fold change to the mean DeltaCt value obtained for the group of control mice (free exploratory behavior, see below) (calibrator DeltaCt) by subtracting DeltaCt for each experimental sample from the calibrator DeltaCt ( = DeltaDeltaCt). The amount of target gene (linear value) normalized to the housekeeping gene and relative to the calibrator was determined by 2-DeltaDeltaCt (relative fold change: RFC).

c-Fos expression was measured by immunohistochemistry as previously described [27]. After intracardiac perfusion with PBS (pH 7.4) followed by 4% paraformaldehyde, the brains were removed, post-fixed in the same fixative for 4 h at 4degreesC, cryoprotected in 30% sucrose for 24 h, and then quickly frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at -80degreesC before sectioning. Coronal cryostat sections of 30 microm width through the hippocampus (bregma: from -0.9 mm to -3.1 mm) were collected free-floating. Briefly, rabbit polyclonal antiserum raised against c-Fos (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Santa Cruz, CA) was diluted 1:1000 in Tris Buffer Saline (TBS) containing 0.3% Triton X-100, 2% donkey serum and 1% bovine serum albumin (BSA), and sections were incubated overnight at room temperature (RT) before being incubated for 2 h with biotinylated donkey anti-rabbit antibody (1:1000; Amersham Parmacia Biotech Europe, Freiburg, Germany), for 2 h with avidin-biotin peroxidase complex (1:1000; Vectastain ABC kit, Vector laboratories, Burlingame, CA), and finally revealed with diaminobenzidine via the nickel-enhanced glucose oxidase method [28]. Image-editing software (Adobe Photoshop, Adobe Systems, San Jose, CA) was used to adjust image size, brightness, and contrast. Quantification of c-Fos immunoreactive cells was performed with the aid of Scion Image public domain NIH-imaging software (Frederick, MD). The number of c-Fos-positive cells was quantified in the dentate gyrus (DG) and CA1 of the hippocampus from consecutive sections (n = 5-6 sections per mice; n = 6 mice per group) across both hemispheres, and these counts were averaged to produce a mean. Dorsal and ventral regions of the hippocampus were analyzed, but only the data of the dorsal region were presented as there were no significant differences in the ventral part. The number of c-Fos-positive cells was also quantified in the arcuate nucleus of the hypothalamus to control for specific hippocampal activation.

We first measured the spatial memory performances of WT and P2X7R-/- mice using a spatial recognition test designed as a hippocampus-dependent task [16]. After 30 minutes of retention, the spatial memory performance was different across the genotype (Fig. 1A). A two-way ANOVA revealed no effect of genotype (F(1,16) = 0.46, n.s.) or arm (F(1,16) = 0.21, n.s.), but there was a significant interaction between genotype and arm (F(1,16) = 4.5, p<0.05). ###begin title 45
Discussion
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 42 43 42 43 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 44 47 44 47 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 264 265 264 265 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 266 269 266 269 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 434 435 431 432 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 33 37 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 48 52 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 270 274 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
We show here that compared to WT mice, P2X7R-/- mice exhibit spatial memory deficits in the Y maze, a hippocampal dependent task, whereas their memory performance was intact in an object recognition paradigm. We further report that, contrary to the WT animals, P2X7R-/- mice had no increase of hippocampal IL-1beta mRNA and c-Fos expression after the Y maze exposure. Taken together, our results clearly support a crucial role for P2X7R-mediated IL-1beta expression in memory processes involving the hippocampus.
###end p 46

Our study employed spontaneous recognition tasks (spatial and object), which exploit the natural tendency of rodents to explore novel place or stimuli more than familiar ones and have been widely validated as measure of recognition memory (reviewed in [16], [25]). These paradigms do not require the learning of a rule, and factors that may particularly influence performance in mice such as emotional states generated by commonly used procedures (food deprivation, water avoidance or electric foot-shock) are minimized, making it useful for studying memory in rodents [30]. Spontaneous recognition is based on novelty preference, however, when a delay of several minutes (e.g. 30 min), the decrease of the exploration of the familiar arm (or object) during the trial 2 reflects the memory of the previous exploration during the first trial [17], [19], [20], [30]-[33]. Following a 30-min ITI, WT mice showed significant spatial recognition, while P2X7R-/- mice did not. Furthermore, P2X7R-/- mice display intact performance in an object recognition task with the same 30-min ITI, suggesting that memory is not affected in a non-spatial task. Even if we cannot rule out that with longer ITI (such as 1h30 or 2h), the ability of mice to recognize object would decrease faster in P2X7R-/ mice than in WT mice, the differential impairment between spatial and object recognition tasks for a similar delay is particularly interesting regarding the considerable literature showing that spatial memory and object memory do not involved similar brain areas [22]-[25], [34]. Hippocampus is clearly involved for spatial memory; in contrast, visual/object recognition requires intact perirhinal area, but hippocampus seems less crucial [35]. Taken together, our results suggest that the lack of P2X7R specifically affects hippocampal functioning as well as the downstream events stemming from it that are specifically involved in spatial recognition. This possibility is well supported by the high expression of P2X7R both by presynaptic terminals of mossy fibers targeting CA3 pyramidal neurons [36] and by excitatory synaptic terminals in the CA1 and dentate gyrus regions of the hippocampus [37].

###xml 484 488 472 476 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Balschun1">[38]</xref>
###xml 598 601 586 589 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Goshen1">[5]</xref>
###xml 603 607 591 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Depino1">[39]</xref>
###xml 844 845 832 833 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 846 849 834 837 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1099 1102 1087 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Avital1">[4]</xref>
###xml 1103 1106 1091 1094 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Yirmiya1">[7]</xref>
###xml 150 154 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 474 478 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 850 854 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
An important new finding of our study is the demonstration of the reduced expression of learning-induced IL-1beta mRNA in the hippocampus of P2X7R-/- mice. Interestingly, IL-1beta was induced in the hippocampus but not in the hypothalamus, suggesting that IL-1beta induction is specifically linked to spatial memory performance. This observation is consistent with previous reports showing that IL-1beta gene expression is induced in the hippocampus by LTP in freely-moving rats [6], [38] and by two other hippocampal-dependent memory tasks, i.e. passive avoidance and contextual fear conditioning [5], [39]. Since, spontaneous recognition memory tasks do not allow a subtle assessment of memory processes (learning, flexibility, reference memory etc.), it would be interesting to evaluate whether the impaired spatial recognition memory in P2X7R-/- mice can be generalized to other hippocampal-dependent tasks. In this context, it has previously been shown that IL-1 receptor activation is necessary for memory tasks such as passive avoidance, contextual fear conditioning or the Morris water maze [4]-[7].
###end p 48
###begin p 49
###xml 73 74 70 71 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 75 78 72 75 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>

###xml 185 188 182 185 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 284 288 278 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Mingam1">[12]</xref>
###xml 313 314 307 308 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 420 424 414 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Armstrong1">[36]</xref>
###xml 426 430 420 424 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Sperlagh1">[37]</xref>
###xml 432 436 426 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Ireland1">[40]</xref>
###xml 485 486 479 480 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 487 490 481 484 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1057 1058 1039 1040 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 1073 1076 1055 1058 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Ferrari1">[8]</xref>
###xml 1078 1082 1060 1064 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Sanz1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1084 1088 1066 1070 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Brough1">[41]</xref>
###xml 1090 1094 1072 1076 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Boucsein1">[42]</xref>
###xml 1295 1303 1274 1282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1303 1306 1282 1285 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Ferrari1">[8]</xref>
###xml 1308 1312 1287 1291 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Mingam1">[12]</xref>
###xml 1314 1318 1293 1297 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Sanz1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1320 1324 1299 1303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-LeFeuvre1">[15]</xref>
###xml 1326 1330 1305 1309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Brough1">[41]</xref>
###xml 79 83 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 491 495 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
IL-1beta mRNA expression was induced in the hippocampus of WT but not P2X7R-/- mice submitted to the spatial recognition task, independently of the ITI. These results suggest that P2X7R-/- is a prerequisite for IL-1beta mRNA expression, as previously demonstrated after LPS treatment [12]. In the hippocampus, P2X7R stimulation may either directly or indirectly activate neurons and elicit changes in their excitability [36], [37], [40]. Since an intact performance was reported in P2X7R-/- mice after the 2-min ITI, despite a lack of IL-1beta after the Y maze, we hypothesized that the induction of IL-1beta in the hippocampus was an important factor underlying the spatial memory processes (e.g. retention of the spatial information), but not the spatial novelty preference. We did not identify the cell type that synthesizes and releases IL-1beta in the hippocampus after the spatial task. Both neurons and microglia have been reported to express IL-1beta in the non-pathological brain. There is a large body of evidence demonstrating the presence of P2X7R on microglia [8], [13], [41], [42]. The activation of these receptors, via downstream inflammatory stimuli such as LPS or direct activation by ATP, triggers microglial release of IL-1beta, as we and others have previously demonstrated in vitro[8], [12], [13], [15], [41].
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 104 105 101 102 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 106 109 103 106 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 293 297 290 294 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Guzowski1">[43]</xref>
###xml 459 460 456 457 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 1001 1002 995 996 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 110 114 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 624 628 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Similarly to IL-1beta mRNA expression, c-Fos expression was induced in the hippocampus of WT but not P2X7R-/- mice submitted to the spatial recognition task. c-Fos is an immediate early gene rapidly induced by a variety of stimuli, including stimuli involved in regulating synaptic plasticity [43]. This suggests that the increased neuronal activity in hippocampus observed after the exposure to the spatial task is linked to the hippocampal activation of P2X7R. Moreover, in our study, we found that c-Fos activation in the DG and CA1 of the hippocampus is positively correlated to spatial recognition performance. Indeed, mice exhibiting the best recognition scores also show the highest hippocampal c-Fos expression after the spatial task. In contrast, c-Fos levels in the hypothalamus were not linked to the spatial recognition score. The hypothalamus showed neither c-Fos expression nor IL-1beta mRNA induction after the spatial task. This last observation further reinforces the link between P2X7R, IL-1beta and c-Fos activation in the hippocampus in response to spatial memory performance.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 318 326 303 311 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 331 338 316 323 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 362 365 347 350 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 418 419 403 404 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 516 520 498 502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Ferrari2">[10]</xref>
###xml 522 526 504 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Solle1">[14]</xref>
###xml 528 532 510 514 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Ferrari3">[44]</xref>
###xml 533 537 515 519 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Chessell1">[46]</xref>
###xml 590 591 572 573 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 791 792 767 768 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 794 798 770 774 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Mingam1">[12]</xref>
###xml 908 909 881 882 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 1192 1195 1159 1162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Goshen1">[5]</xref>
###xml 1233 1234 1200 1201 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 1235 1238 1202 1205 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 366 370 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1148 1152 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1187 1191 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1239 1243 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
IL-1beta is synthesized in the cell cytoplasm as a leaderless procytokine (pro-IL-1beta) which tends to accumulate in the absence of a maturation-promoting second stimulus. IL-1beta-converting enzyme (ICE) is the key protease responsible for the intracellular processing of pro-IL-1beta into mature IL-1beta. Numerous in vitro and in vivo experiments using P2X7R-/- mice have conclusively linked the stimulation of P2X7R by extracellular ATP to the activation of ICE and the downstream production of mature IL-1beta [10], [14], [44]-[46]. Therefore, there is little doubt that a lack of P2X7R necessarily impacts IL-1beta production, resulting in severe reduction if not a complete loss. Very importantly, IL-1beta induced its own synthesis in the brain, and this process is dependent on P2X7R [12]. It is therefore possible that an initial small increase in IL-1beta secretion through the recruitment of P2X7R could up-regulate the IL-1beta mRNA induction in the hippocampus linked to a spatial recognition memory task. This idea is supported by the fact that contextual fear conditioning induces IL-1beta mRNA expression in the hippocampus of WT mice but not of IL-1 receptor knockout mice [5]. The impaired memory processes in P2X7R-/- mice could thus be linked to hippocampus-deficient IL-1beta synthesis. Therefore, our results are in accordance with recent studies demonstrating that physiological levels of hippocampal IL-1beta are required for short-term spatial memory performance, whereas deficient hippocampal IL-1beta expression (as here) results in impaired memory.
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 41 42 41 42 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>

###xml 453 457 444 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Mingam1">[12]</xref>
###xml 476 480 467 471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Perregaux1">[48]</xref>
###xml 482 486 473 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Colomar1">[49]</xref>
###xml 532 533 523 524 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 580 584 571 575 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Armstrong1">[36]</xref>
###xml 586 590 577 581 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Sperlagh1">[37]</xref>
###xml 717 720 708 711 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">via</italic>
###xml 758 762 749 753 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Werry1">[50]</xref>
###xml 805 806 796 797 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 933 934 924 925 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 1027 1031 1018 1022 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Sperlagh1">[37]</xref>
###xml 1033 1037 1024 1028 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Sperlagh2">[51]</xref>
###xml 1062 1066 1053 1057 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-North1">[52]</xref>
###xml 1120 1124 1111 1115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Edwards1">[53]</xref>
###xml 1125 1129 1116 1120 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Khakh1">[55]</xref>
###xml 1187 1188 1178 1179 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 1260 1264 1251 1255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Armstrong1">[36]</xref>
The mechanisms underlying the role of P2X7R in the synthesis of IL-1beta mRNA in relation to memory processes remain an enigma. P2X7R has been associated to IL-1beta precursor processing, through the rapid activation of a specific caspase-1-dependent multiprotein complex [47]. The ATP-induced activation of P2X7R decreases cytoplasmic potassium which in turn induces the maturation and the release of mature IL-1beta by monocytes [48], [49], microglia [12] and Schwann cells [48], [49]. In addition ATP by acting on presynaptic P2X7R, modulates hippocampal synaptic transmission [36], [37]. In the present work, it could be hypothesized that the Y maze exposure stimulates the ATP release by neighboring glial cells via an increase of the glutamate release [50]. This could in turn affect presynaptic P2X7R. Alternatively, or additionally, the memory task may modulate the neuronal release of ATP, which could then act on nearby P2X7R since ATP is co-stored and co-released with classical neurotransmitters from brain neurons [37], [51] in an exocytotic manner [52] and acts as a fast neurotransmitter within the brain [53]-[55]. However, up to now, the sole direct demonstration of P2X7R modulation of hippocampal synaptic transmission reported a depression [36].
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 46 47 46 47 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 269 270 266 267 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">7</sub>
###xml 343 347 340 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Chen1">[56]</xref>
###xml 349 353 346 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-DiVirgilio2">[57]</xref>
###xml 376 380 373 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Barden1">[58]</xref>
###xml 381 385 378 382 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006006-Romagnoli1">[60]</xref>
In conclusion, our study demonstrated that P2X7R is involved in spatial memory processes and in the associated neuronal activity and IL-1beta mRNA synthesis in the hippocampus. These results therefore corroborate clinical observations showing that the impairment of P2X7R function, due to mutation or polymorphism, leads to both immunological [56], [57] and brain dysfunction [58]-[60], putatively due to the lack of IL-1beta synthesis and release.
