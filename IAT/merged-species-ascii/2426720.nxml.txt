###begin title 2
Purpose
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 322 324 314 316 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 325 329 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Transcription factors of the nuclear factor-kappa beta (NF-kappaB) family have been demonstrated to play an important role in the regulation of gene expression in the chronic neurodegenerative disorders. The aims of the current study were to investigate the alteration of NF-kappaB activity during retinal degeneration in rd mice and further explore its role in photoreceptor apoptosis.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 71 73 67 69 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 74 78 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Activation of NF-kappaB and its nuclear translocation in the retina of rd mice at postnatal days (P) 8, 10, 12, 14, 16, 18, and 28 were studied by immunohistochemical analysis using NF-kappaB P65 antibody. The amount of NF-kappaB P65 protein and NF-kappaB DNA-binding activity in the whole retina were assessed by western blot analysis and gel shift analysis, respectively. Expression of NF-kappaB in microglial cells labeled with CD11b was determined by double labeling.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Results
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 92 94 88 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
NF-kappaB P65 nuclear translocation and its DNA binding activity started to increase in the rd retina at P10 and reached a peak at P12. Expressions of P65 remained at high levels from P12 to P18. Double labeling of P65 with CD11 at P14 showed colocalization of P65 in the microglial cells in the outer nuclear layer.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Conclusions
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 55 57 51 53 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 58 62 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
NF-kappaB was activated in the retinal degeneration of rd mice. NF-kappaB modulation may play a role in the retinal degeneration through microglial activation.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Introduction
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 192 193 192 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
###xml 599 600 599 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 617 618 617 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 666 667 662 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 668 669 664 665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
Recent studies have shown that there is a strong link between inflammation and chronic neurodegenerative diseases, such as Alzheimer disease, Parkinson disease, and Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease [1]. The common feature of these diseases is neuroinflammation, defined as the presence of activated microglia and inflammatory mediators. Microglia, resident macrophages of the central nervous system (CNS), are central to the inflammatory response. These cells actively monitor their environment and can become over-activated in response to diverse stimuli to produce cytotoxic factors, such as superoxide [2], nitric oxide [3], and tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-alpha) [4,5].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 499 501 499 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 508 509 508 509 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 786 787 783 784 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r7">7</xref>
###xml 788 789 785 786 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 1089 1091 1082 1084 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 502 506 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1092 1096 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is a group of inherited retinal degenerations characterized by progressive loss of photoreceptor cells. More than 158 genes causing this inherited retinal disease have been identified in about two-thirds of the cases (). However, the molecular mechanisms by which these gene mutations lead to photoreceptor apoptosis have not been clearly elucidated. In a recent study, we showed several inflammatory events and factors were involved in the retinal degenerative process of rd mice [6]. In this widely used retinitis pigmentosa animal model, a mutation in the gene encoding the beta subunit of retinal cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP) phosphodiesterase results in elevation of cGMP levels in rod photoreceptors, leading to massive cell death by apoptosis [7,8]. In that study, the activation of microglia, as well as expression of chemokines and microglia-derived neurotoxic cytokines (TNF-alpha), coincided with or preceded the occurrence of photoreceptor apoptosis, suggesting inflammatory response may play an important role in the retinal degeneration in rd mice.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 169 170 165 166 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 278 280 270 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 322 324 314 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 457 459 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 584 586 564 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 738 740 714 716 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 741 743 717 719 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 830 832 802 804 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 728 736 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 833 837 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
The proinflammatory responses of immune cells, including microglia, involve the signal transduction molecule transcription factor nuclear factor-kappa beta (NF-kappaB) [9]. Activation of NF-kappaB contributes to microglia activation and production of proinflammatory molecules [10] that can lead to neurotoxicity in vivo [11]. NF-kappaB signaling begins with phosphorylation and degradation of IkappaB, a key component of the cytoplasmic NF-kappaB complex [12], and releases the p50 and p65 subunits that translocate to the nucleus and promote transcription of proinflammatory genes [13]. In many neurodegenerative diseases, a significant increase of NF-kappaB activity was detected in neurons and microglial cells in brains of patients [14-17]. In this study, we examined the activity of NF-kappaB during retinal degeneration of rd mice and defined its role in the inflammatory process of this neurodegenerative model.
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Methods
###end title 14
###begin title 15
Animals
###end title 15
###begin p 16
###xml 35 37 35 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 271 272 271 272 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 343 345 343 345 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 56 60 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 259 263 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 346 350 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 448 452 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 476 480 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">Mice</span>
This study used 120 inbred C3H/HeJ rd and wild-type C3H mice (Jackson Laboratories, Bar Harbor, ME) . All animals were treated in accordance with the ARVO statement for the use of Animals in Ophthalmic and Vision Research. Euthanasia was performed by placing mice in a CO2 chamber for 60 s, followed by cervical dislocation. The globes of the rd mice were collected on postnatal days (P) 8, P10, P12, P14, P16, P18, and P28. Age-matched normal C3H mice were used as controls. Mice were euthanized at the same time of the day (1:00 PM).

###begin title 18
Tissue preparation
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 10 14 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
After the mice were sacrificed, their eyes were rapidly enucleated and fresh-frozen in optimal cutting temperature compound (Tissue-Tek; Sakura Finetek, Tokyo, Japan) in liquid nitrogen and stored at -80 degreesC until sectioning. The tissue blocks were cut vertically with a cryostat at 8 mum through the optic nerve head and ora serrata.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
Immunohistochemistry of NF-kappaB and fluorescent double-labeling of NF-kappaB and microglia
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 210 211 206 207 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 212 213 208 209 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 246 251 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
NF-kappaB P65 immunolabeling was performed with a kit (MOM; Vector laboratories, Burlingame, CA) according to the manufacturer's instructions. In brief, frozen sections were fixed in acetone, quenched in 0.3% H2O2 in methanol, and incubated in a mouse IgG blocking solution for 1 h. A monoclonal antibody that recognizes the active form of NF-kappaB (NF-kappaB P65; Santa Cruz Biotechnology; Santa Cruz, CA) was applied at a 1:100 dilution, and the sections were incubated at 4 degreesC overnight. NF-kappaB P65 immunoreactivity was detected using a biotinylated secondary antibody, and 3,3'-diaminobenzidine was used as the chromogen. Negative controls were performed by replacing the primary antibody with PBS.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
###xml 136 141 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 167 170 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
For double labeling, the tissue sections were incubated with two primary antibodies from different species (NF-kappaB P65 antibody from mouse and CD 11b antibody from rat) at 4 degreesC overnight. After the sections were washed in PBS for 15 min, they were incubated with secondary antibodies from their corresponding species, conjugated with either 1:100 fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC; Jackson ImmunoResearch Laboratory, West Grove; PA) or 1:200 tetramethylrhodamine isothiocyanate (TRITC) for 45 min at room temperature. The sections were observed with a confocal laser scanning microscope (TCS-NT; Leica, Wetzlar, Germany) with 488 nm filter for FITC green and 568 nm filter for TRITC. Images were captured and postprocessed by digital photography (DXL-5500; Sony, Tokyo, Japan) and confocal software (Leica).
###end p 22

###begin title 24
Western blot analysis
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 507 509 499 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
###xml 892 903 <span type="species:ncbi:3704">horseradish</span>
Retinal tissues were homogenized using a tissue grinder (Thomas 3431E15; Phoenix Equipment, Inc.; Rochester, NY) in 500 mul of cold suspension buffer (20 mM HEPES-KOH (pH7.5), with 250 mM sucrose, 10 mM KCL, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM EGTA, and 1 mM DTT) containing proteinase inhibitor cocktail (Roche Diagnostics, Indianapolis, IN). The homogenates were centrifuged at 750x g at 4 degreesC. Each 5 mug sample was separated on a 12% SDS-PAGE gel, and western blot analysis was then performed as previously described [18]. Briefly, the electrophoresed proteins were electrophoretically transferred to nitrocellulose membrane for immunodetection. Western blots were blocked with buffer containing 5% non-fat milk and incubated with primary polyclonal antibody NF-kappaB P65 (sc-372; Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Santa Cruz, CA). Primary antibody binding was identified with secondary antibody conjugated to horseradish peroxidase (1:2000; Santa Cruz Biotechnology) followed by chemiluminescent detection (ECL kit, Amersham Corp., Arlington Heights, IL). The polyclonal antibody (sc-372; Santa Cruz Biotechnology), which reacts with an epitope consisting of 20 amino acids at the COOH-terminus of NF-kappaB P65, was used for immunodetection. Protein concentration of all samples were measured using a bicinchoninic acid (BCA) protein assay kit (Pierce, Rockford, IL) before western blot analysis, and equivalent amount of protein were used for the assay. In each blot, GAPDH was used as an internal control for the loading of protein level. Fluorescence bands were digitally captured for analysis of intensity (Quantity One Image Software; Bio-Rad Laboratories, Santa Cruz, CA).
###end p 25
###begin title 26
Electrophoretic mobility shift assay
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 640 642 628 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
The preparation of retinal nuclear extracts and determination of the NF-kappaB DNA-binding activity were performed with a nuclear and cytoplasmic reagent kit (NE-PER; Pierce) and an electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) chemiluminescence kit (LightShift; Pierce), respectively, according to the manufacturer's protocols. A double-stranded oligonucleotide containing an NF-kappaB DNA-binding consensus sequence, 5'-AGT TGA GGG GAC TTT CCC AGG C-3' (Santa Cruz Biotechnology), and a mutant double-stranded oligonucleotide, 5'-AGT TGA GGC GAC TTT CCC AGG C-3', were used to study NF-kappaB DNA-binding activity, as previously described [19]. Briefly, 2 microg of nuclear extracts from the whole retina was preincubated in a reaction mixture for 20 min, and biotin end-labeled, double-stranded oligonucleotide containing the kappaB consensus sequence was added. Next, 5 microl of loading buffer was added to each sample. A 20 microl aliquot of the samples was electrophoresed through a 6% nondenaturing polyacrylamide gel. The hybridization signal was quantified by intensitometry using Quantity One Image Software (Bio-Rad Laboratories)
###end p 27
###begin title 28
Statistical analysis
###end title 28
###begin p 29
The data were presented as mean +/-standard deviation. Statistical significance was assessed with a one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey's HSD Multiple Comparisons test. A p<0.05 was considered statistically significant.
###end p 29

Results
###end title 30
###begin title 31
###xml 66 68 62 64 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
Nuclear translocation of NF-kappaB in the microglial cells in the rd retina
###end title 31
###begin p 32
###xml 28 37 28 37 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1A</xref>
###xml 291 293 287 289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 430 439 426 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1B</xref>
###xml 526 535 522 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1C</xref>
###xml 659 668 655 664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1D</xref>
###xml 813 824 805 816 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f1">Figure 1E-G</xref>
In the control retina (P14, Figure 1A), NF-kappaB P65 positive cells were scattered in the inner retinal layers, including the ganglion cell layer (GCL), inner plexiform layer (IPL), and outer plexiform layer (OPL). P65 immunoreactivity mainly appeared in the cytoplasm of the cells. In the rd retina, P65 immunoreactivity was increased and peaked at P12. At this time point, prominent nuclear translocation in the OPL was noted (Figure 1B). At P14, cells with P65 nuclear labeling were found in the outer nuclear layer (ONL; Figure 1C). At P16, P65 immunoreactivity in the retina was still prominent, but the positive cells in the outer retina were reduced (Figure 1D). Double labeling of NF-kappaB p65 and CD11b at P14 showed P65 immunoreactivity was predominantly in the microglial cells infiltrating the ONL (Figure 1E-G).

Western blot analysis was performed to assess the protein level of NF-kappaBP65 in retinas during retinal degeneration of rd mice. In the rd retinas, the amount of NF-kappaB P65 was notably increased at P10 and reached a peak at P12. The expression was kept a high level up to P18. The expression of P65 was markedly reduced at P28. (Figure 2B,C)

###begin title 38
Discussion
###end title 38
###begin p 39
###xml 159 161 151 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 464 466 444 446 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 744 746 716 718 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 162 166 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 467 471 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In this study, we demonstrated increased expression of NF-kappaB protein and NF-kappaB DNA-binding activity in the retina during photoreceptor degeneration of rd mice. Nuclear translocation of NF-kappaB was noted in the microglial cells in the OPL and ONL. In a previous study [6], we showed cytokine TNF-alpha, a representative target and inducer gene for NF-kappaB activation, was also primarily produced in the activated microglial cells in the outer retina of rd mice. Taking our previous study and the present one together, we propose that activation of NF-kappaB may be associated with photoreceptor cell death through regulation of gene expression of proinflammatory and neurotoxic molecules (e.g., TNF-alpha) in microglial cells in the rd retina.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 182 184 178 180 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r20">20</xref>
###xml 440 442 428 430 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 788 790 768 770 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 1049 1051 1025 1027 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 207 212 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1052 1056 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
As in CNS neurodegenerative diseases, alteration of NF-kappaB expression has been reported in several retinal degenerations both in vitro and in vivo. Krishnamoorthy and colleagues [20] showed cultured 661W mouse photoreceptor cells constitutively expressed NF-kappaB, and light exposure of these cells resulted in lowering of NF-kappaB levels in both the nuclear and cytosolic fractions in a time-dependent manner. In contrast, Wu et al. [21]. reported in vivo expression of NF-kappaB in photoreceptor cells was increased at 3 h and its nuclear translocation was evident at 12 h after continuous light exposure for 3 h. Activation of NF-kappaB was also observed in neuronal cells in the inner nuclear layer and ganglion cell layer 24 h following retinal ischemia and reperfusion injury [22]. In the latter study, P65 labeling was partially colocalized with apoptotic cells in the inner nuclear cell layer. Consistent with the aforedescribed two animal models of retinal degeneration, our study revealed marked NF-kappaB activation in the retina of rd mice, a typical model for inherited retinal dystrophy.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 89 91 85 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 528 530 516 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r23">23</xref>
###xml 531 533 519 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r25">25</xref>
###xml 666 668 650 652 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r26">26</xref>
###xml 669 671 653 655 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
###xml 855 857 835 837 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r29">29</xref>
###xml 858 860 838 840 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r30">30</xref>
###xml 937 939 917 919 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r31">31</xref>
###xml 1245 1246 1210 1211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 1719 1721 1676 1678 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 1870 1872 1823 1825 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 2143 2145 2092 2094 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 2152 2153 2101 2102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 2146 2150 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In contrast to previous studies on retinal degenerations, activation of NF-kappaB in the rd retina in the present study was primarily in microglial cells, rather than in neuronal cells, including photoreceptor cells. This observation might imply a distinct function of NF-kappaB activation in retinal degeneration in this animal model. Pro-apoptotic and anti-apoptotic roles of NF-kappaB in neuronal and glial cells including microglia and astrocytes have been identified and proposed in several CNS neurodegenerative diseases [23-25]. These studies reported that activation of NF-kappaB in neurons played a neuroprotective role in the neurodegenerative conditions [26-28]. In this scenario, NF-kappaB influences the neurodegenerative process by directly promoting cell survival gene expression, such as Bcl-2, Mn-SOD, and inhibitor of apoptosis protein [29,30], or reducing cell death gene expression, such as Bxl-x and Bax in neurons [31]. In contrast, the role of NF-kappaB activation in glial cells, especially microglia are usually neurotoxic as production of many proinflammatory cytokines and proapoptotic substances including TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, iNOS, and the cell adhesion molecule (ICAM-1) were regulated by NF-kappaB in glial cells [4,32]. In retinal degenerations, NF-kappaB showed a neuroprotective role in photoreceptor apoptosis in vitro as transfection of these cells with a dominant negative mutant IkB greatly enhanced kinetics of down modulation of NF- B, resulting in a faster photo-oxidative stress-induced apoptosis [20]. The function of NF-kappaB in the retina in vivo appeared unclear, although P65 immunolabeling positive cells were TUNEL negative in light-induced photoreceptor degeneration [21]. The present study suggests a neurotoxic role of NF-kappaB in photoreceptor apoptosis by not only showing its persistent high expression levels in rd retinas spanning from P12 to P18 when the photoreceptor cell loss took place, but also revealing its activation in microglial cells and co-occurrence of increased TNF-alpha production in microglial cells in a typical period (from P12 to P14) of retinal degenerations of rd mice [6].
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 518 520 486 488 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 521 525 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
NF-kappaB is an important signaling molecule in microglial activation. Activation of NF-kappaB indirectly influences neurodegenerative process by regulating gene expression in microglial cells, including pro-inflammatory cytokines TNF-alpha [33]. TNF-alpha may serve as both target and inducer gene of NF-kappaB activation. Like other neurodegenerative diseases, TNF-alpha and other NF-kappaB-dependent gene products may be responsible for persistent NF-kappaB activation and sustained chronic retinal inflammation in rd mice, even as the photoreceptor cells disappeared in the advanced stage.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 127 129 123 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 268 270 260 262 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">rd</italic>
###xml 130 134 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In summary, the present study showed marked activation of NF-kappaB in microglial cells in the retinal degenerative process of rd mice. Alteration of NF-kappaB activity may promote photoreceptor apoptosis via initiation and perpetuation of chronic inflammation in the rd retina, making it an extremely attractive target for therapeutic intervention.
###end p 43
