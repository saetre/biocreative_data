Comparison of the properties of the five soluble guanylyl cyclase subunits in Drosophila melanogaster
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 4 27 4 27 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">Drosophila melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 573 580 566 573 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 728 730 721 723 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 752 756 745 749 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vmax</italic>
###xml 775 776 768 769 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 776 777 769 770 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</sub>
###xml 4 27 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 979 994 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
The Drosophila melanogaster genome contains 5 genes that code for soluble guanylyl cyclase subunits. Two of these genes code for subunits, Gycalpha-99B and Gycbeta-100B, which form a conventional NO-sensitive guanylyl cyclase and the other three code for atypical subunits, Gyc-88E, Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db. The properties and distribution of Gyc-88E and Gyc-89Db have previously been described and here Gyc-89Da is described. Gyc-89Da only forms an active guanylyl cyclase when co-expressed with Gyc-88E. The three atypical subunits probably form two different heterodimers in vivo: Gyc-88E/89Da and Gyc-88E/89Db. Both of these heterodimers were slightly stimulated by NO donors and Gyc-88E/89Da showed a greater activation by Mn2+, with an increase in Vmax and a decrease in Km, compared to Gyc-88E/89Db. Both Gyc-88E/89Da and Gyc-88E/89Db were expressed in neurons in both the peripheral and central nervous system. Although all three heterodimeric soluble guanylyl cyclases in D. melanogaster can be activated by NO and inhibited by ODQ, the atypical enzymes can be distinguished from the conventional soluble guanylyl cyclase by their sensitivity to the NO-independent activators YC-1 and BAY 41-2272, which will only activate the conventional enzyme.

###begin title 5
Introduction
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 173 190 166 183 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-lucas1" ref-type="bibr">Lucas et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 407 419 393 405 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton1" ref-type="bibr">Morton 2004a</xref>
###xml 487 500 470 483 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">Manduca sexta</named-content>
###xml 502 521 485 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-nighorn1" ref-type="bibr">Nighorn et al. 1999</xref>
###xml 556 574 536 554 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-koglin1" ref-type="bibr">Koglin et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 719 737 696 714 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-koglin1" ref-type="bibr">Koglin et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 739 755 716 732 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-gibb1" ref-type="bibr">Gibb et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 799 818 773 792 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-nighorn1" ref-type="bibr">Nighorn et al. 1999</xref>
###xml 820 844 794 818 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton3" ref-type="bibr">Morton and Anderson 2003</xref>
###xml 487 500 <span type="species:ncbi:7130">Manduca sexta</span>
Conventional soluble guanylyl cyclases are heterodimeric heme-proteins containing an alpha and a beta subunit that are activated by the gaseous messenger nitric oxide (NO) (Lucas et al. 2000). More recently, another class of soluble guanylyl cyclase subunits has been described, which although clearly closely related to the conventional alpha and beta subunits, have quite distinct biochemical properties (Morton 2004a). These atypical soluble guanylyl cyclases include MsGC-beta3 from Manduca sexta (Nighorn et al. 1999) and the mammalian beta2 subunit (Koglin et al. 2001), which are both capable of forming an active enzyme without the need for additional subunits. The beta2 subunits are slightly activated by NO (Koglin et al. 2001; Gibb et al. 2003), whereas MsGC-beta3 is insensitive to NO (Nighorn et al. 1999; Morton and Anderson 2003).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 14 37 14 37 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">Drosophila melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 112 134 112 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton5" ref-type="bibr">Morton and Hudson 2002</xref>
###xml 259 277 245 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-shah1" ref-type="bibr">Shah and Hyde 1995</xref>
###xml 368 390 354 376 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton5" ref-type="bibr">Morton and Hudson 2002</xref>
###xml 474 494 460 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 512 527 498 513 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 641 649 624 632 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-89Db</italic>
###xml 702 722 685 705 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 841 861 824 844 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 910 917 893 900 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-88E</italic>
###xml 922 930 905 913 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-89Db</italic>
###xml 1087 1107 1070 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 14 37 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
###xml 512 527 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
The genome of Drosophila melanogaster contains 5 genes predicted to code for soluble guanylyl cyclase subunits (Morton and Hudson 2002). Two genes, Gycalpha-99B and Gycbeta-100B code for the alpha and beta subunits of a conventional soluble guanylyl cyclase (Shah and Hyde 1995) whereas the remaining three genes appear to code for atypical guanylyl cyclase subunits (Morton and Hudson 2002). We have recently described the properties of two of these, Gyc-88E and Gyc-89Db (Langlais et al. 2004). Gyc-88E is the D. melanogaster orthologue of MsGC-beta3 and was active in the absence of other subunits, presumably forming homodimers, whereas Gyc-89Db was inactive except when co-expressed with Gyc-88E (Langlais et al. 2004). Both Gyc-88E homodimers and Gyc-88E/ Gyc-89Db heterodimers were slightly activated by some, but not all, NO donors (Langlais et al. 2004). In situ hybridization experiments showed that Gyc-88E and Gyc-89Db were expressed in the central and peripheral nervous systems and in the peripheral nervous system were frequently co-localized in the same sensory neurons (Langlais et al. 2004).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 352 374 348 370 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton5" ref-type="bibr">Morton and Hudson 2002</xref>
###xml 417 424 413 420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in situ</italic>
This report describes the properties and localization of the remaining soluble guanylyl cyclase subunit, Gyc-89Da. Gyc-89Da is adjacent to Gyc-89Db on the genome, codes for a predicted protein that is 82% identical (with 87% similarity) to Gyc 89Db and was predicted to form an active enzyme only when co-expressed with either Gycalpha-99B or Gyc-88E (Morton and Hudson 2002). Transient expression in COS-7 cells and in situ hybridization shows that Gyc-89Da has similar properties and distribution compared to Gyc-89Db.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Materials and Methods
###end title 9
###begin title 10
Cloning of Gyc-89Da
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 1185 1205 1185 1205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1410 1412 1410 1412 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nd</sup>
The predicted ORF of Gyc-89Da was cloned by PCR from embryonic cDNA (Clontech, ) using Expand DNA polymerase (Roche, ). Primers were designed to span the predicted ATG start site and Kozac sequence and the stop codon: 5'- TTTAATCGTCCATTG TTT TCC, 5'-ATCAGAACATGGTGCTATAAA. The 2095 bp product was cloned into the pCR-II TOPO vector (Invitrogen, ) and sequenced to ensure that the cloned product matched the genomic sequence. The Gyc-89Da ORF was then subcloned into the mammalian expression vector pcDNA3.1 (Invitrogen) using the XbaI and HindIII restriction sites. To identify any 5' untranslated region (UTR) of Gyc-89Da splice site prediction software () was used to predict the intron/exon structure of the 5' region of the gene. This predicted a similar organization to Gyc-89Db that included an untranslated first exon, followed by a 950 bp intron and the second exon containing the entire ORF and a short region of 5'UTR (11bp) immediately 5' of the ATG start site. Primers were designed to amplify the 272 bp 5'UTR starting just after the predicted transcriptional start site to the ORF. Total RNA was isolated from mixed stage larvae and used to synthesize cDNA as described (Langlais et al. 2004). The cDNA was then used as a template for nested PCR using the following primers: round 1; 5'- AAGTCCTGTTTAACCGTTAT, 5'-ACTCCTCCTGCACGTAGT, round 2; 5'-CATCACTCCTGCAAGGAAT, 5'- GCACACTCTCATACAGCAT. The 2nd round product was cloned into pCR-II TOPO and sequenced.
###end p 11
###begin title 12
Transient expression and guanylyl cyclase activity
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 224 244 217 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 461 481 454 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
cDNAs coding for Gycalpha-99B and Gyc-89Db were obtained as expressed sequence tags from the Berkeley Drosophila Genome Project and those for Gyc-88EL and Gycbeta-100B were cloned as RT-PCR products as previously described (Langlais et al. 2004). Each were then subcloned into the mammalian expression vector pcDNA3.1 (Invitrogen), transiently transfected into COS-7 cells and the cell homogenates assayed for guanylyl cyclase activity as previously described (Langlais et al. 2004).
###end p 13
###begin title 14
Reverse transcription PCR (RT-PCT)
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 86 88 86 88 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">st</sup>
###xml 91 93 91 93 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nd</sup>
###xml 99 101 99 101 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">rd</sup>
###xml 782 789 772 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-t01" ref-type="table">table 1</xref>
Total RNA was isolated from embryos (mixed ages), larvae (approximately equal mix of 1st, 2nd and 3rd instars), pupae (mixed ages) and adults using Trizol (Invitrogen) and used for synthesizing cDNA using Superscript II reverse transcriptase (Invitrogen) with an oligo dT primer. The cDNA was then used as a template for two rounds of PCR amplification using 1 mul of the RT reaction for the first round and 0.5 mul of the first round reaction for the second round. For both PCR amplifications, primers were used at 0.5 muM and included 1.5 units of taq DNA polymerase (Invitrogen) in a total volume of 50 mul. Both reactions used 25 cycles with an annealing temperature of 52 degreesC. The primers used to amplify Gyc-88E, Gyc-89Da, Gyc-89Db and the control gene rp49 are given in table 1. The amplification of rp49 only required a single round of PCR.
###end p 15

###begin title 18
Results
###end title 18
###begin title 19
Cloning of Gyc-89Da
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 123 138 123 138 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 140 160 140 160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 183 198 183 198 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 502 519 502 519 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">Anopheles gambiae</named-content>
###xml 629 651 629 651 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton5" ref-type="bibr">Morton and Hudson 2002</xref>
###xml 123 138 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
###xml 183 198 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
###xml 502 519 <span type="species:ncbi:7165">Anopheles gambiae</span>
We previously described the initial characterization of two atypical guanylyl cyclase subunits, Gyc-88E and Gyc-89Db, from D. melanogaster (Langlais et al. 2004). The sequence of the D. melanogaster genome also predicts a third atypical guanylyl cyclase subunit immediately 5' of Gyc-89Db that was designated Gyc-89Da. The two genes are predicted to code for proteins that are over 80% identical and this together with their close proximity on the genome and the finding that another fly-the mosquito, Anopheles gambiae-only has a single orthologue suggested that Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db represent a recent gene duplication event (Morton and Hudson 2002). To determine whether Gyc-89Da generates a transcript, PCR was used to clone the predicted ORF from cDNA obtained from embryos. Primers were designed that encompassed the predicted ATG start site and the predicted stop codon that yielded the expected 2.1 kb product when cloned and sequenced.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 650 658 650 658 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f01" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 1)</xref>
As Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db are up to 80% identical throughout their coding regions it was necessary to identify any UTR present in Gyc-89Da in order to generate gene-specific probes. Gyc-89Db was obtained as an expressed sequence tag, which included 279 bp of 5' UTR that showed no similarity to upstream regions of Gyc-89Da. To identify the 5' UTR of Gyc-89Da, software was used to predict the likely transcriptional start site and splice sites. Primers were designed to amplify this region from cDNA generated from larval animals. Using this method 5' UTRs were obtained from 2 splice variants of Gyc-89Da that were 260 and 274 nucleotides in length (Fig. 1). Both Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db had a similar gene structure, with the entire coding region on a single exon preceded by an intron and a 5' exon containing the majority of the 5'UTR. The 5' UTRs of the two genes share no significant similarity with each other.
###end p 21
###begin title 22
Gyc-89Da forms an active guanylyl cyclase when co-expressed with Gyc-88EL
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 470 490 470 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 638 640 638 640 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 707 715 707 715 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f02" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 2)</xref>
###xml 816 836 816 836 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1348 1368 1341 1361 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
To investigate the biochemical properties of Gyc-89Da, the ORF was subcloned into a mammalian expression vector, transiently transfected into COS-7 cells and the cell contents assayed for guanylyl cyclase activity. Cells that had been transfected with only Gyc-89Da showed no guanylyl cyclase activity above that seen for cells transiently transfected with empty vector (data not shown). By contrast, COS-7 cells that had been co-transfected with Gyc-89Da and Gyc-88EL (Langlais et al. 2004) showed significantly higher levels of activity (p < 0.001; two-way ANOVA followed by Bonferroni post-test) when assayed in the presence of 4 mM Mn2+ than cells transfected with either empty vector or Gyc-88EL alone (Fig. 2). Two alternatively spliced transcripts were generated from the Gyc-88E gene, Gyc-88EL and Gyc-88ES (Langlais et al. 2004). No biochemical differences were detected between the products of these two transcripts and the present study only uses Gyc-88EL. When Gyc-89Da was co-transfected with either of the two conventional guanylyl cyclase subunits, Gycalpha-99B or Gycbeta-100B, no increase in enzyme activity was detected above that seen for vector-transfected controls. These results are similar to those seen with Gyc-89Db, where guanylyl cyclase activity was only seen when Gyc-89Db was co-transfected with Gyc-88EL or Gyc-88ES (Langlais et al. 2004).
###end p 23

###xml 1530 1531 1523 1524 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>

To investigate possible differences between Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db, COS-7 cells were transiently transfected with Gyc-88EL alone or in combination with Gyc-89Da or Gyc-89Db and assayed the cell extracts with varying concentrations of GTP (Fig. 3). Curves were fit to the data using the Michaelis-Menten equation and used to estimate the Vmax and Km for each combination (Table 2). These data revealed a difference in the biochemical properties between the Gyc-88EL/89Da and the Gyc-88EL/89Db combinations. There was no significant difference in the estimated value for Vmax for GTP in the presence of Mg2+ between Gyc-88EL, Gyc-88EL/89Da and Gyc-88EL/89Db (two-way ANOVA followed by Bonferroni post-test) whereas in the presence of Mn2+ Gyc-88EL/89Da yielded a significantly higher estimate for Vmax than either Gyc-88EL or Gyc-88EL/89Db (p < 0.001; two-way ANOVA followed by Bonferroni post-test). Comparing the ratios of Vmax in Mn2+ compared to Mg2+ most easily shows this difference where less than a 2-fold increase is seen for Gyc-88EL and Gyc-88EL/89Db whereas this ratio is 46.9 for Gyc-88EL/89Da. By contrast, Mn2+ has a greater effect on the Km of Gyc-88EL and Gyc-88EL/89Db (greater than 50 fold change) than Gyc-88EL/89Da, which only showed a 13.7 fold change. These comparisons showed that the Gyc-88EL/89Da combination was more similar to the conventional guanylyl cyclase subunits, Gycalpha-99B/beta-100B, which showed an almost 100 fold increase in Vmax in the presence of Mn2+ compared to only 11.9 fold change in Km.
###end p 24
###begin title 25
Both Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db form heterodimers with Gyc-88EL that are more sensitive to NO donors than Gyc-88EL alone
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 161 181 161 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 391 399 391 399 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f04" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 4)</xref>
###xml 509 529 509 529 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 532 541 532 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f04" ref-type="fig">Figure 4A</xref>
###xml 952 954 950 952 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50</sub>
###xml 1289 1291 1278 1280 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50</sub>
Our previous studies showed that Gyc-88EL was slightly sensitive to some, but not all, NO donors and that Gyc-88EL/89Db showed enhanced activation by NO donors (Langlais et al. 2004). To determine if the NO donors had differential effects on the different subunit combinations the effects of varying concentrations of NO donors were tested on extracts of transiently transfected COS-7 cells (Fig. 4). One of the more potent activators of Gyc-88EL and Gyc-88EL/89Db was S-nitroso-N-acetylpenicillamine (SNAP) (Langlais et al. 2004). Figure 4A shows that the Gyc-88EL/ 89Da combination was also slightly activated by SNAP, with a maximum stimulation of about 3 fold at 100 muM, which was similar to the activation seen for Gyc-88EL/89Db and greater than seen for Gyc-88EL alone (less than 2 fold). In addition to enhanced activation by 100 muM SNAP, both heterodimeric combinations showed a greater sensitivity to SNAP compared to Gyc-88EL alone, with EC50 values of about 10 muM compared to Gyc-88EL, which was not saturated with 100 muM SNAP. Nevertheless, all of these combinations of guanylyl cyclase subunits showed substantially less activation by SNAP compared to the conventional soluble guanylyl cyclase, Gycalpha-99B/beta-100B, which had a maximal stimulation of 60 fold with an EC50 of about 2 muM.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 138 153 138 153 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 306 315 306 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f04" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 4B)</xref>
###xml 535 543 535 543 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-88EL</italic>
###xml 577 579 577 579 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50</sub>
###xml 608 616 607 615 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-88EL</italic>
###xml 712 714 704 706 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50</sub>
###xml 896 904 887 895 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-88EL</italic>
###xml 908 916 899 907 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-88EL</italic>
###xml 944 964 934 954 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1144 1153 1134 1143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f04" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 4C)</xref>
###xml 1191 1199 1181 1189 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-88EL</italic>
###xml 1203 1211 1193 1201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-88EL</italic>
###xml 1222 1230 1212 1220 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-88EL</italic>
###xml 1442 1444 1424 1426 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50</sub>
###xml 138 153 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
The effects of two other structurally unrelated NO donors were compared regarding their ability to activate the different combinations of D. melanogaster guanylyl cyclase subunits. NOC-12, a NONOate with a relatively long half-life of 327 minutes, was also effective at activating all subunit combinations (Fig. 4B) but as with SNAP, the maximal activation of the atypical subunits was less than 4 fold compared to the conventional subunits, which were stimulated greater than 30 fold. Also, both heterodimeric combinations containing Gyc-88EL were more sensitive to NOC-12 (EC50 of about 2 muM) compared to Gyc-88EL alone. Again, the conventional Gycalpha-99B/beta-100B combination was more sensitive with an EC50 of about 0.2 muM. Our previous studies had shown that another NONOate, diethylamine- (DEA)-NONOate, which has a short half-life of 16 minutes, did not significantly activate either Gyc-88EL or Gyc-88EL/89Db when used at 100 muM (Langlais et al. 2004). To determine whether this lack of activation was concentration dependent, the effects of different combinations of DEA-NONOate were tested on the different subunit combinations (Fig. 4C). No stimulation was seen with either Gyc-88EL or Gyc-88EL/89Da, but Gyc-88EL/89Db was stimulated about 4 fold and showed a pronounced bell-shaped curve with less than 2 fold activation seen at 100 muM. By contrast, Gycalpha-99B/beta-100B was stimulated over 30 fold by DEA-NONOate. The EC50 values for DEA-NONOate were similar for Gyc-88EL/89Db and Gycalpha-99B/beta-100B at about 0.1 muM.
###end p 27
###begin p 28
###xml 196 216 196 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
In our previous study the soluble guanylyl cyclase inhibitor, 1H-[1,2,4]oxadiazolo [4,3,-a]quinoxaline-1-one (ODQ) was ineffective at inhibiting the activation of Gyc-88E by sodium nitroprusside (Langlais et al. 2004). Here the effect of ODQ on the stimulation of Gyc-88E/89Db by 1 muM DEA-NONOate was tested. Basal levels of activity were unaffected: 208 +/- 19 and 237 +/- 13 fmoles cGMP/min/mg protein in the absence and presence of 100 muM ODQ, whereas DEA-NONOate-stimulated activity was completely abolished: 676 +/- 70 fmoles cGMP/min/mg protein in the absence and 141 +/- 21 fmoles cGMP/min/mg protein in the presence of 100 muM ODQ. Similarly, NOC-12-stimulated activity of both Gyc-88E/89Da and Gyc-88E/89Db was also inhibited by 100 muM ODQ (data not shown).
###end p 28
###begin p 29
###xml 55 73 55 73 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-stasch1" ref-type="bibr">Stasch et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 99 114 99 114 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 141 150 141 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f04" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 4D)</xref>
###xml 381 384 373 376 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">50-</sub>
###xml 99 114 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
The effect of a NO-independent activator, BAY 41-2272 (Stasch et al. 2001), was also tested on the D. melanogaster soluble guanylyl cyclases (Fig. 4D). None of the atypical guanylyl cyclase subunit combinations showed any activation with BAY 41-2272 whereas this compound was the most potent activator of Gycalpha-99B/beta-100B showing over 100-fold activation at 30 muM with an EC50- of 7.3 muM.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
All three atypical soluble guanylyl cyclase subunits are expressed throughout development
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 326 334 326 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f05" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 5)</xref>
To determine the developmental expression pattern of the atypical guanylyl cyclase subunits RT-PCR was used to examine the levels of mRNA of Gyc-88E, Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db at selected developmental stages. Results from this experiment showed that each subunit appeared to be expressed at similar levels throughout development (Fig. 5). To reliably detect each transcript it was necessary to use two rounds of PCR and no specific effort was made to ensure that the amount of second round product was in the linear range of amplification. Therefore, it is possible that there are variations of expression throughout development although none of the transcripts are absent at any stage.
###end p 31
###begin title 32
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In situ</italic>
In situ hybridization reveals Gyc-89Da is expressed in a similar pattern to Gyc-88E and Gyc-89Db
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 186 206 186 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 257 264 257 264 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in situ</italic>
###xml 352 360 352 360 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f06" ref-type="fig">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 531 551 531 551 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 629 641 629 641 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f06" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 6B, C)</xref>
###xml 643 651 643 651 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">les, ves</italic>
###xml 656 660 656 660 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">v'td</italic>
###xml 733 745 733 745 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f06" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 6D, E)</xref>
###xml 801 813 801 813 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f06" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 6F, G)</xref>
###xml 815 835 815 835 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
Our previous study showed that Gyc-88E and Gyc-89Db were expressed in neurons in the central and peripheral nervous systems and were frequently co-localized in specific sensory neurons (Langlais et al. 2004). To determine the expression pattern of Gyc-89Da in situ hybridization of whole embryos was used with a digoxygenin-labeled probe for Gyc-89Da. Figure 6 shows that using full-length probes Gyc-89Da stained a very similar set of peripheral neurons to those had been previously identified that expressed Gyc-88E and Gyc89Db (Langlais et al. 2004). These cells include neurons in the dorsal and terminal ganglia of the head (Fig. 6B, C); les, ves and v'td neurons in the thoracic segments 2 and 3 and abdominal segments 1 and 2 (Fig. 6D, E) and 12 neurons that innervate the caudal sensory cones (Fig. 6F, G) (Langlais et al. 2004). Given the sequence similarity between Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db, it was possible that some of this apparent overlap was due to each probe recognizing both Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db transcripts. In an attempt to overcome this problem digoxygenin-labeled RNA probes were generated complementary to the 5' UTR of each gene. There is no sequence similarity between the genes in this region. There is, however, less than 300 bp of 5' UTR for each gene and probes made to these sequences failed to reveal any specific staining, probably due to a lack of sensitivity using shorter probes (data not shown). Thus, with the available tools it was not possible to determine whether Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db were expressed in a separate set of cells or whether they were co-localized in the same cells. Gyc-89Da was also expressed in cells in the CNS and although it is not possible to determine whether any of the subunits are co-localized in the CNS, a similar pattern of staining was seen with Gyc-89Da compared to Gyc-88E and Gyc-89Db (data not shown).
###end p 33
###begin p 34
###xml 358 367 358 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f06" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 6B)</xref>
###xml 635 647 635 647 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f06" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 6H, I)</xref>
###xml 771 780 771 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f06" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 6I)</xref>
###xml 852 861 852 861 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-f06" ref-type="fig">(Fig. 6H)</xref>
There are two specific regions where staining for all three probes are not co-localized. We had previously shown that Gyc-89Db stained cells in both the terminal and dorsal ganglia of the head, whereas Gyc-88E only stained cells in the terminal ganglion. Gyc-89Da was similar to Gyc-89Db in that it also stained cells in both the terminal and dorsal ganglia (Fig. 6B). As cells in the dorsal ganglion do not appear to express Gyc-88E, we predict that Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db do not form an active guanylyl cyclase in these cells. In a similar fashion, one area where Gyc-88E was expressed, but Gyc-89Db was not, was in the embryonic gut (Fig. 6H, I), where apparently randomly distributed cells were detected in the midgut epithelium when hybridized to a probe for Gyc-88E (Fig. 6I), but no cells were seen when hybridized to a probe for either Gyc-89Da (Fig. 6H) or Gyc-89Db (data not shown). In this case, as Gyc-88E forms an active guanylyl cyclase in the absence of additional subunits it would be expected that this expression represents a functional enzyme.

###begin title 35
Discussion
###end title 35
###begin title 36
###xml 55 70 55 70 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 55 70 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
Multiple distinct soluble guanylyl cyclase isoforms in D. melanogaster
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 4 19 4 19 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 101 123 101 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton5" ref-type="bibr">Morton and Hudson 2002</xref>
###xml 427 445 413 431 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-shah1" ref-type="bibr">Shah and Hyde 1995</xref>
###xml 447 462 433 448 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-liu1" ref-type="bibr">Liu et al. 1995</xref>
###xml 512 532 498 518 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 618 638 604 624 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 712 720 698 706 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">M. sexta</named-content>
###xml 722 746 708 732 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton3" ref-type="bibr">Morton and Anderson 2003</xref>
###xml 852 872 838 858 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1103 1123 1082 1102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 4 19 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
###xml 712 720 <span type="species:ncbi:7130">M. sexta</span>
The D. melanogaster genome contains 5 genes predicted to code for soluble guanylyl cyclase subunits (Morton and Hudson 2002). Four of these have previously been described and the present study provides a preliminary characterization of the fifth. The previously described subunits include Gycalpha-99B and Gycbeta-100B, the alpha and beta subunits that form a conventional, NO-sensitive heterodimeric soluble guanylyl cyclase (Shah and Hyde 1995; Liu et al. 1995) and the atypical subunits Gyc-88E and Gyc-89Db (Langlais et al. 2004). Gyc-88E can form an active guanylyl cyclase in the absence of additional subunits (Langlais et al. 2004), presumably forming homodimers in a manner similar to the orthologue in M. sexta (Morton and Anderson 2003). Gyc-89Db forms an active enzyme only when co-expressed with Gyc-88E, presumably forming a heterodimer (Langlais et al. 2004). Both Gyc-88E and Gyc-88E/89Db enzymes are slightly activated by some NO donors, but are stimulated no more than 2-3 fold compared to at least 20 fold activation seen for the Gycalpha-99B/beta-100B conventional guanylyl cyclase (Langlais et al. 2004).
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 530 537 530 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 708 723 708 723 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 1066 1073 1059 1066 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1164 1171 1157 1164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 708 723 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
The present study describes an additional subunit, Gyc-89Da, with very similar properties to Gyc-89Db. Both Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db only form an active guanylyl cyclase when co-expressed with Gyc-88E and both heterodimers are only slightly activated by NO donors. The present study also shows that both Gyc-88E/89Da and Gyc-88E/89Db heterodimers are more sensitive to NO donors than the homodimeric Gyc-88E. This property, in addition to the finding that both Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db appear to be frequently co-expressed with Gyc-88E in vivo, suggests that the native enzymes are most often heterodimers. If this prediction is correct, there are three distinct heterodimeric soluble guanylyl cyclase isoforms in D. melanogaster. The conventional cyclase, Gycalpha99B/beta-100B is potently activated by NO whereas the atypical cyclases, Gyc-88E/89Da and Gyc-88E/89Db, are only slightly activated by NO. In the midgut epithelial cells, as Gyc-88E was apparently expressed in the absence of either Gyc-89Da or Gyc-89Db, it is possible that the homodimeric form also exists in vivo. It is likely that exogenously applied NO or NO donors will activate all of these enzymes in vivo making it difficult to unambiguously determine which guanylyl cyclase is being activated. This is compounded by the finding that the soluble guanylyl cyclase inhibitor, ODQ, is effective at blocking the NO-stimulation of all forms of the soluble guanylyl cyclases. A useful reagent for distinguishing between the two classes of guanylyl cyclases is likely to be the NO-independent activator BAY 41-2272 that potently activates Gycalpha99B/beta-100B but has no effect on the atypical guanylyl cyclase.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 737 753 733 749 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-gibb1" ref-type="bibr">Gibb et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 841 843 837 839 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 858 860 854 856 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 909 911 905 907 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2+</sup>
###xml 956 960 952 956 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Vmax</italic>
###xml 986 987 982 983 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K</italic>
###xml 987 988 983 984 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">m</sub>
###xml 1196 1218 1192 1214 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">Caenorhabditis elegans</named-content>
###xml 1233 1249 1229 1245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-gray1" ref-type="bibr">Gray et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1379 1394 1375 1390 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 1864 1880 1857 1873 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-zhao1" ref-type="bibr">Zhao et al. 1998</xref>
###xml 1932 1951 1925 1944 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-schmidt1" ref-type="bibr">Schmidt et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1962 1970 1955 1963 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-89Da</italic>
###xml 1975 1983 1968 1976 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Gyc-89Db</italic>
###xml 2043 2065 2036 2058 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton5" ref-type="bibr">Morton and Hudson 2002</xref>
###xml 2092 2107 2085 2100 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 2196 2218 2189 2211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-garthwaite1" ref-type="bibr">Garthwaite et al. 1995</xref>
###xml 2220 2236 2213 2229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-zhao2" ref-type="bibr">Zhao et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 1196 1218 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">Caenorhabditis elegans</span>
###xml 1379 1394 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
###xml 2092 2107 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
As the expression patterns of Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db were very similar to each other the question is raised as to whether they are fully interchangeable. There were some subtle differences between the biochemical properties of Gyc-88E/89Da and Gyc-88E/89Db. One difference was the response to the NO donor, DEA-NONOate. Although Gyc-88E/89Da and Gyc-88E/89Db responded in a similar fashion to the NO donors, SNAP and NOC-12, only Gyc-88E/89Db responded to DEA-NONOate, showing a pronounced bell-shaped response with maximal activation at about 1muM DEA-NONOate. The reduced activation at higher concentrations of DEA-NONOate could reflect rapid inactivation kinetics as is seen with the mammalian atypical guanylyl cyclase beta2 subunit (Gibb et al. 2003). A more pronounced difference was seen in the kinetic parameters in the presence of Mg2+ compared to Mn2+. Gyc-88E/89Da showed a stronger activation in Mn2+ than Gyc-88E/89Db with a larger increase in Vmax and a larger decrease in Km. This might reflect differences in the activation of these two enzymes by the natural ligand. It is not known what this natural ligand is, but the recent finding that an atypical soluble guanylyl cyclase in Caenorhabditis elegans binds oxygen (Gray et al. 2004) raises the possibility that a gaseous messenger other than NO, possibly oxygen or carbon monoxide, could regulate either of the D. melanogaster atypical guanylyl cyclases. Although the endogenous ligand for the three soluble guanylyl cyclases might differ, it is likely that the mechanism of activation is similar: binding a small ligand to the heme group for activation. A critical residue for binding heme and NO activation of conventional soluble guanylyl cyclases is a histidine residue (His-105 in the mammalian beta1 subunit) that acts as the axial ligand for binding heme and is displaced upon NO binding (Zhao et al. 1998). Other critical residues are Tyr-135 and Arg-139 (Schmidt et al. 2004) Gyc-88E, Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db all have these three residues at the equivalent positions (Morton and Hudson 2002). In addition, all of the D. melanogaster soluble guanylyl cyclases are sensitive to ODQ, which acts by oxidizing the heme group (Garthwaite et al. 1995; Zhao et al. 2000), again suggesting a common mechanism of activation.

###xml 32 47 32 47 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 279 286 279 286 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 437 445 434 442 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">M. sexta</named-content>
###xml 447 466 444 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-nighorn1" ref-type="bibr">Nighorn et al. 1999</xref>

###xml 857 879 851 873 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton5" ref-type="bibr">Morton and Hudson 2002</xref>
###xml 935 943 929 937 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">M. sexta</named-content>
###xml 1002 1012 996 1006 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">C. elegans</named-content>
###xml 1124 1142 1118 1136 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton4" ref-type="bibr">Morton et al. 1999</xref>
###xml 1203 1215 1197 1209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton1" ref-type="bibr">Morton 2004a</xref>
###xml 1322 1340 1316 1334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-cheung1" ref-type="bibr">Cheung et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1425 1443 1416 1434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-koglin1" ref-type="bibr">Koglin et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 1578 1594 1561 1577 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-gibb1" ref-type="bibr">Gibb et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 32 47 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
###xml 437 445 <span type="species:ncbi:7130">M. sexta</span>
###xml 639 647 <span type="species:ncbi:7130">M. sexta</span>
###xml 737 745 <span type="species:ncbi:7460">honeybee</span>
###xml 750 759 <span type="species:ncbi:7091">silk moth</span>
###xml 935 943 <span type="species:ncbi:7130">M. sexta</span>
###xml 1002 1012 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
The suggestion that most of the D. melanogaster guanylyl cyclases primarily exist as heterodimers is based on the increased sensitivity to NO donors of the heterodimers compared to the homodimer and the finding that Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db are frequently co-expressed with Gyc-88E in vivo. This model might also extend to other atypical soluble guanylyl cyclases. The first atypical guanylyl cyclase subunit identified was MsGC-beta3 from M. sexta (Nighorn et al. 1999). While MsGC-beta3 is enzymatically active in heterologous cells as a homodimer (Morton and Anderson 2003), it is possible that it exists in vivo as a heterodimer with the M. sexta orthologue of Gyc-89Da or Gyc-89Db. The genomes of other insects including the mosquito, honeybee and silk moth all appear to contain sequences with similarity to Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db in addition to Gyc-88E (Morton and Hudson 2002; DB Morton, unpublished observations), suggesting that M. sexta also has more than one atypical guanylyl cyclase subunit. C. elegans has seven soluble guanylyl cyclase subunits, all of which appear to be members of the atypical subunit family (Morton et al. 1999) and modeling suggests that they all can form heterodimers (Morton 2004a). Genetic evidence supporting the formation of one of these heterodimers has also recently been reported (Cheung et al. 2004). The mammalian beta2 subunit is also active in the absence of additional subunits (Koglin et al. 2001) suggesting the formation of homodimers. However it can also form a functional heterodimer with either the alpha1 or alpha2 subunits (Gibb et al. 2003) again suggesting that the native enzyme might be a heterodimer.
###end p 40
###begin title 41
###xml 44 59 44 59 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 44 59 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
Temporal and spatial expression patterns of D. melanogaster soluble guanylyl cyclases
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 304 328 304 328 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-wildmann1" ref-type="bibr">Wildmann and Bicker 1999</xref>
The RT-PCR results showed that all three of the atypical soluble guanylyl cyclase subunits were expressed throughout development. Previous studies have shown using cGMP-IR that cells that respond to NO donors, presumably expressing the two conventional subunits, are also present throughout development (Wildmann and Bicker 1999). Thus all five subunits are expressed at every stage.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In situ</italic>
###xml 185 205 185 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-langlais1" ref-type="bibr">Langlais et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 509 519 509 519 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">C. elegans</named-content>
###xml 622 634 622 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton1" ref-type="bibr">Morton 2004a</xref>
###xml 828 846 828 846 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-cheung1" ref-type="bibr">Cheung et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 986 1001 986 1001 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 509 519 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 986 1001 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
In situ hybridization experiments strongly suggest that Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db are primarily co-expressed with Gyc-88E in selected neurons in the central and peripheral nervous systems (Langlais et al. 2004 and the present study). Is there any functional significance for the expression of all three subunits in the same cells? Their biochemical properties when expressed in heterologous cells show that they function in a very similar manner, suggesting a redundancy between Gyc-88E/89Da and Gyc-88E/89Db. In C. elegans five of the seven soluble guanylyl cyclase subunits are also co-expressed in the same four cells (see Morton 2004a). Deletions of the genes coding for two of these subunits, GCY-35 and GCY-36, disrupt social feeding behaviors whereas deletions for two of the other three did not generate the same phenotype (Cheung et al. 2004). This suggests that there is not complete functional redundancy between all these subunits. No mutant phenotypes are known for any of the D. melanogaster atypical subunits. A genetic analysis of such mutants when they become available should help resolve this issue.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 368 386 368 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-zitnan1" ref-type="bibr">Zitnan et al. 1993</xref>
###xml 388 408 388 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-veenstra1" ref-type="bibr">Veenstra et al. 1995</xref>
###xml 560 578 560 578 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-zitnan1" ref-type="bibr">Zitnan et al. 1993</xref>
###xml 580 600 580 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-veenstra1" ref-type="bibr">Veenstra et al. 1995</xref>
###xml 922 930 922 930 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">M. sexta</named-content>
###xml 932 950 932 950 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-kingan1" ref-type="bibr">Kingan et al. 1997</xref>
###xml 952 956 952 956 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-kingan2" ref-type="bibr">2001</xref>
###xml 958 980 958 980 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-gammie1" ref-type="bibr">Gammie and Truman 1997</xref>
###xml 922 930 <span type="species:ncbi:7130">M. sexta</span>
In addition, Gyc-88E was expressed in an additional population of cells, a subset of epithelial cells in the gut, which did not express either Gyc-89Da or Gyc-89Db. The positions and numbers of the labeled cells in the gut epithelia suggest that they are enteroendocrine cells, which have been shown in several insect species to contain a variety of peptide hormones (Zitnan et al. 1993; Veenstra et al. 1995). Peptides released from these cells have been suggested to regulate gut contractility and/or the release of digestive enzymes in response to feeding (Zitnan et al. 1993; Veenstra et al. 1995). Although a role for cGMP in these cells is unknown, it is possible that increases in cGMP could mediate the release of these peptides. An increase in the levels of cGMP in the epitracheal glands and in a subset of peptidergic neurons in the CNS has been implicated in the release of peptide hormones in another insect, M. sexta (Kingan et al. 1997; 2001; Gammie and Truman 1997). As Gyc-88E forms a functional guanylyl cyclase when expressed in heterologous cells in the absence of additional subunits, it is likely that this expression represents the formation of an active homodimer in the midgut cells. By contrast, in the dorsal ganglion Gyc-89Da and Gyc-89Db appear to be expressed in the absence of Gyc-88E. Based on the properties of these subunits in heterologous cells, these are likely to represent non-functional guanylyl cyclases. It is possible that at a later stage in development these cells begin to express Gyc-88E and thus form a functional enzyme.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 117 132 117 132 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 849 859 842 852 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">C. elegans</named-content>
###xml 895 911 888 904 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-gray1" ref-type="bibr">Gray et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1090 1105 1083 1098 <named-content xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" content-type="genus-species">D. melanogaster</named-content>
###xml 1183 1195 1176 1188 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" rid="i1536-2442-005-12-0001-morton2" ref-type="bibr">Morton 2004b</xref>
###xml 117 132 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
###xml 849 859 <span type="species:ncbi:6239">C. elegans</span>
###xml 1090 1105 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">D. melanogaster</span>
In summary, we have presented evidence that suggests that there are multiple isoforms of soluble guanylyl cyclase in D. melanogaster. Gycalpha-99B/beta-100B represents the conventional NO-sensitive cyclase and Gyc-88E, Gyc-88E/89Da and Gyc-88E/89Db are atypical enzymes. The atypical guanylyl cyclases are slightly activated by NO, but can be distinguished from the conventional NO-sensitive enzymes by their insensitivity to the NO-independent activators YC-1 and BAY 41-2272. Gyc-88E/89Db and Gyc-88E/ 89Da are expressed in neurons in the central and peripheral nervous systems and Gyc-88E is expressed in gut epithelia, possibly in enteroendocrine cells. A major question remains which is to determine the endogenous activator of these and other atypical soluble guanylyl cyclases. The recent finding that an atypical soluble guanylyl cyclase in C. elegans mediates oxygen sensing behaviors (Gray et al. 2004) suggests that other gaseous ligands such oxygen or carbon monoxide are prime candidates. In support of this idea, we have recently shown that when expressed in COS-7 cells, the D. melanogaster atypical guanylyl cyclases are stimulated by anoxia and inhibited by oxygen (Morton 2004b).
