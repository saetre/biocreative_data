###begin article-title 0
COOH-terminal truncations and site-directed mutations enhance thermostability and chaperone-like activity of porcine alphaB-crystallin
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Purpose
###end title 1
###begin p 2
The COOH-terminal extension segment of alphaB-crystallin, a member of small heat shock protein (sHSP) family, appears to be a flexible polypeptide segment susceptible to proteolytic truncation and modifications under physiological conditions. To investigate its role on the structure and chaperone-like activity, we constructed various mutants of porcine alphaB-crystallin with either COOH-terminal serial truncations or site-specific mutagenesis on the last two residues.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Methods
###end title 3
###begin p 4
The structures of these mutants were analyzed by circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy, fluorescence spectra, mass spectrometry, Gel-permeation FPLC, and dynamic light-scattering spectrophotometry. Chaperone activity assays were performed under thermal and non-thermal stresses. The stability of proteins was examined by turbidity assays and CD spectra.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Results
###end title 5
###begin p 6
All mutants showed similar secondary and tertiary structural features to the wild-type alphaB-crystallin as revealed by circular dichroism. However, truncations of the COOH-terminal segment generated crystallin aggregates with a molecular size slightly smaller than that of the wild-type alphaB-crystallin. The deletion of 12 residues from the COOH-terminal end greatly reduced the solubility, thermostability, and chaperone activity of alphaB-crystallin. On the contrary, the truncation of only 10 residues or less resulted in increased thermostability and enhanced anti-aggregation chaperone activity of alphaB-crystallin, with a maximal effect occurring on elimination of the last two residues. Moreover, displacing the last two lysines with glutamates or other neutral amino acids tended to show even higher chaperone activity than the deletion mutants.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Conclusions
###end title 7
###begin p 8
Our study clearly demonstrated that both the length and electrostatic charge of the COOH-terminal segment play crucial roles in governing the structural stability and chaperone activity of alphaB-crystallin.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Introduction
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 385 386 385 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r1">1</xref>
The traditional view that proteins fold spontaneously in vivo was revised upon the finding that many proteins in a living cell will not fold correctly without the assistance of molecular chaperones. In the cytosol of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, molecular chaperones of different structural classes form a network of pathways, leading to correct folding states for most proteins [1]. The heat shock protein (HSP) family is one of the first and most prevalent protein chaperones induced under heat and mechanochemical stresses in most animal and plant cells.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 559 560 531 532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r2">2</xref>
###xml 582 583 554 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 628 638 600 610 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 840 841 800 801 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r4">4</xref>
###xml 993 994 949 950 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r5">5</xref>
###xml 1136 1137 1080 1081 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r6">6</xref>
###xml 1138 1139 1082 1083 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r8">8</xref>
###xml 1261 1262 1202 1203 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 1263 1265 1204 1206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r10">10</xref>
###xml 1712 1736 1641 1665 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Methanococcus jannaschii</italic>
###xml 1807 1809 1736 1738 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r11">11</xref>
###xml 2049 2056 1974 1981 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Archaea</italic>
###xml 2058 2066 1983 1991 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Bacteria</italic>
###xml 2072 2079 1997 2004 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Eukarya</italic>
###xml 2081 2083 2006 2008 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r12">12</xref>
###xml 2084 2086 2009 2011 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r13">13</xref>
###xml 2256 2257 2177 2178 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r3">3</xref>
###xml 2258 2260 2179 2181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r14">14</xref>
###xml 2261 2263 2182 2184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r15">15</xref>
###xml 2298 2299 2219 2220 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 2484 2485 2405 2406 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 2745 2746 2655 2656 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
###xml 2931 2933 2833 2835 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r16">16</xref>
###xml 628 638 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
###xml 1712 1736 <span type="species:ncbi:2190">Methanococcus jannaschii</span>
alpha-Crystallin extracted from the lens, exists in solution as polydisperse aggregates ranging from 600 to 1,200 kDa with an average molecular mass of approximately 800 kDa. There are two alpha-crystallins known as alphaA and alphaB. alphaA-Crystallin is usually the most abundant subunit in the lens, although the alphaA/alphaB ratio varies considerably among species. Both subunits are about 20 kDa in size and have 55-60% sequence similarity. They were previously regarded as lens-specific polypeptides and exclusively being structural protein in nature [2]. Ingolia and Craig [3] first reported that heat shock proteins of Drosophila showed similarity to mammalian alpha-crystallin. It was shown later that alphaB-crystallin is not restricted to the lens even though alphaA-Crystallin is highly specialized for expression in the lens [4]. In 1992 it was demonstrated that alpha-crystallin functioned as a molecular chaperone by suppressing the aggregation of other polypeptides in vitro [5]. While both alphaA and alphaB-crystallins are molecular chaperones, only alphaB-crystallin is a functional small heat-shock protein (sHSP) [6-8]. Both subunits possess largely beta-sheet structures and consist of two domains and a flexible COOH-terminal extension [9,10], as revealed by NMR studies. However, in the absence of an alpha-crystallin subunit 3D structure, the detailed protective chaperone mechanism of alpha-crystallin remains elusive and a matter of controversy. Due to its large size and the lack of crystallographic data available, the detailed molecular packing in the quaternary structure of alpha-crystallin has yet to be established. Recently, the structure of a homologous sHSP (sHSP16.5) from Methanococcus jannaschii, a hyperthermophilic archaeon, has been solved to 0.29 nm resolution [11]. Nevertheless, there still exist some structural differences between the sHSP 16.5 protein and alpha-crystallin. It is well known that small heat-shock proteins (sHSPs) form a structurally divergent protein family with members present in Archaea, Bacteria, and Eukarya [12,13]. All members of the sHSP family are characterized by the presence of a homologous sequence of about 80 residues, which has been denoted as the alpha-crystallin domain [3,14,15]. This domain is preceded by an NH2-terminal domain, which is highly variable in size and sequence, and is followed by a short, poorly conserved COOH-terminal extension. For all species, the secondary structure of the NH2-terminal domain is mainly an unordered structure while the COOH-terminal domain is rich in beta-sheet structure. Based on NMR analysis, the COOH-terminal 8 and 10 residues of alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin, respectively, occur as solvent-exposed random coils [9]. Overall, previous studies suggested that a highly flexible COOH-terminal extension in mammalian sHSPs including alphaA- or alphaB-crystallin is required for full chaperone activity [16].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 744 746 717 719 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r17">17</xref>
###xml 747 749 720 722 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r18">18</xref>
Since the alphaB subunit of alpha-crystallin is a more universal and authentic member of the sHSP family than alphaA-crystallin, we have previously focused on alphaBeta-crystallin and shown that COOH-terminal lysine truncation increases thermostability and enhances chaperone-like function of porcine alphaB-crystallin. In this study we have further truncated peptide segments from the COOH-terminal end of recombinant porcine alphaB-crystallin and constructed several site-specific mutants with the aim of providing insights into the structure-functional correlation on this important and novel molecular chaperone purported to play diverse roles in various pathological conditions including cataracts, cancers and neurodegenerative diseases [17,18].
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Methods
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Construction of expression vector
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 80 82 76 78 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 470 471 462 463 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8217;</sup>
###xml 918 925 903 910 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 950 953 935 938 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">(+)</sup>
###xml 918 925 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The wild-type alphaB-crystallin vector was constructed as described previously [19]. The mutant DNA fragments were made by polymerase chain reaction (PCR). The designed primers used for the construction of recombinant alphaB-crystallin and its various deletion plus COOH-terminal replacement mutants are listed in ; Table S1. The forward primer contains an ATG initiator codon and an Nde I restriction site. The reverse primers were complementarily overlapped with the 3'-end of the coding region containing the Hind III site linked to the translational stop codon. The truncated nucleotide fragments were generated by PCR amplification of the corresponding segments in the open-reading frame of the porcine alphaB-crystallin expression vector. After twice-digesting with Nde I and Hind III, the product was purified and ligated into Nde I/Hind III sites of the isopropyl-beta-D-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG)-inducible E. coli expression vector pET21a(+) (Novogen Inc., an Affiliate of Merck KGaA, Darmstadt, Germany).
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Expression and purification of porcine alphaB-crystallin and its mutants
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 167 172 161 166 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">600nm</sub>
###xml 0 7 <span type="species:ncbi:110766|species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
E. coli strain BL21 (DE3) was transformed with expression constructs. The cells were incubated at 37 degreesC until the culture reached an optical density of 0.6 at OD600nm. Protein expression was incubated with additional IPTG to a final concentration of 1 mM. Four hours after incubation, cells were harvested and resuspended in 8 M urea and lysed by ultrasonication. After ultrasonication, the mixture was then centrifuged for 20 min at 20,000x g to remove any remaining insoluble debris. Soluble recombinant proteins were purified by TSK HW-55 gel filtration, followed by C4 reverse-phase HPLC. The purified proteins were lyophilized, and then analyzed by electrospray mass spectrometry to confirm their correct molecular masses of these subunits.
###end p 17

Recombinant proteins were solubilized in 8 M urea individually, and then loaded onto gel filtration column (TSK HW-55). The fractions of proteins were then collected and concentrated. Concentrations were determined by an optical method based on extinction coefficients of amino-acid chromophores [20].

Various truncated mutants of porcine alphaB-crystallin have been constructed to study the function of COOH-terminal extension segment of alphaB-crystallin (Figure 1A). These constructs were transformed in E. coli strain BL21 (DE3) and overexpressed in the presence of IPTG. The recombinant alphaB-crystallin and mutants were thus reassociated and refolded after removal of urea. The purity of these proteins was checked by SDS-PAGE (Figure 2A). The deletion mutants Delta2, Delta5, and Delta7 show similar solubility with recombinant alphaB-crystallin. The Delta10 mutant showed a lower solubility than wild-type alphaB-crystallin when measured at low temperature; however, the Delta12 mutant showed even worse solubility and stability under ambient temperature. We also constructed various mutants by replacing amino acids to study the effects of COOH-terminal lysines (Figure 1B). The purity of these proteins was also checked and confirmed by SDS-PAGE (Figure 2B,C). The pI values of recombinant alphaB-crystallin and mutants were shown in Figure 3A,B. Recombinant alphaB-crystallin and mutants show distinct focusing zones with broad distribution of pI instead of sharp bands characteristic of pI marker proteins. The anomaly of broad distribution of pI values for recombinant alphaB-crystallin and its mutants may be due to their inherent polydisperse properties in their surface charges, and the existence of a rapid equilibrium of association-dissociation behavior associated with crystallin aggregation during electrophoresis.

###begin title 40
Secondary and tertiary structures of truncated mutants
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 216 218 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 612 614 549 551 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r23">23</xref>
###xml 992 994 914 916 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 995 997 917 919 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r24">24</xref>
Recombinant alphaB-crystallin and deletion mutants Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10 all showed a similar beta-sheet secondary structure to that reported previously as revealed in far-UV CD spectra (; Figure S1A) [22]. The near-UV CD spectra of recombinant alphaB-crystallin, and Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10 were also similar (; Figure S1B). The intrinsic fluorescence spectra of recombinant alphaB-crystallin, Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10 were all found to show emission maxima at 339-340 nm (; Figure S2), manifest of a largely hydrophobic microenvironment for the buried tryptophan residues [23]. However, the red-shift emission maxima Trp at 339-340 nm were observed indicating that Trp residues are relatively exposed in alphaB crystallin as compared with beta- or gamma-crystallin (Emax at 330 nm). We have also studied surface hydrophobicity differences among recombinant alphaB-crystallin and mutants by using the extrinsic fluorescent ANS probe as described before [22,24]. The ANS binding assays also showed similar surface hydrophobicity for recombinant alphaB-crystallin and mutants (Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10;  Figure S3). Overall, recombinant alphaB-crystallin and all deletion mutants Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10 possess similar secondary and tertiary structures.

###begin title 46
Comparison of the chaperone activity of truncated mutants
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 704 713 648 657 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f4">Figure 4A</xref>
###xml 1172 1174 1087 1089 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 1175 1177 1090 1092 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r22">22</xref>
###xml 1625 1634 1517 1526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f4">Figure 4B</xref>
###xml 1916 1925 1786 1795 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f4">Figure 4C</xref>
###xml 2427 2436 2255 2264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f5">Figure 5A</xref>
###xml 2869 2878 2657 2666 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f5">Figure 5B</xref>
To distinguish whether the alphaB-crystallin and mutants have different chaperone activity under chemical denaturants, we assayed the chaperone-like activity of these mutants by using insulin as a substrate. At a molar ratio of 1:7.4 (chaperone/insulin), the truncated mutants (Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10) showed similar chaperone activity to that of wild-type alphaB-crystallin at 37 degreesC. Under this condition, all of them showed almost complete inhibition of insulin B chain aggregation (; Figure S4). At a molar ratio of 1:16 (chaperone/insulin), truncated mutants (Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10) also show similar chaperone activity to wild-type alphaB-crystallin at 37 degreesC (Figure 4A). On the other hand, by using alcohol dehydrogenase as a substrate to study the chaperone activity of these truncated mutants at 37 degreesC, the truncated mutants Delta2, Delta5, and Delta7 showed similar chaperone activity to wild-type alphaB-crystallin while Delta10 showed only a slight loss of chaperone activity. If alcohol dehydrogenase was substituted by porcine betaL-crystallin as a substrate for heat denaturation similar to our previous reports [21,22], all truncated mutants also showed similar chaperone activity to wild-type alphaB-crystallin at a molar ratio of 1:1 (chaperone/betaL-crystallin). However, when the molar ratio of betaL-crystallin to these chaperones was increased, differences in chaperone activity of these mutants performed at 60 degreesC could be found. At a molar ratio of 1:3.5 (chaperone/betaL-crystallin), Delta2 possessed the best chaperone activity among these mutants (Figure 4B). Mutants Delta5 and Delta7 were found to show similar chaperone activity with each other whereas Delta10 showed the least chaperone activity among these mutants. In this study we have also tried gamma-crystallin as a substrate and assayed at 66 degreesC. At a molar ratio of 1:1 (Figure 4C), Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10 show similar chaperone activity among one another. However, wild-type alphaB-crystallin was found to show little or no chaperone activity at such a high temperature. We also compared the chaperone activity of truncated mutants (Delta10, Delta11, and Delta12) with wild-type alphaB-crystallin. When insulin is used as a substrate at a molar ratio of 1:11 (chaperone/insulin) and assayed at 38 degreesC, these 3 truncated mutants show different chaperone activity (Figure 5A), with Delta10 showing similar or slightly better chaperone activity than wild-type alphaB-crystallin, Delta11 and Delta12 showing little or no chaperone activity. When we adopted betaL-crystallin as the substrate and increased the assay temperature to 59 degreesC, the wild-type alphaB-crystallin, Delta11 and Delta12 were all found to lose most of their chaperone activity at a molar ratio of 1:3.4 (chaperone/ betaL-crystallin) (Figure 5B). However, the truncated mutant Delta10 still maintained about 50% chaperone activity.

###begin title 50
Structural stability of recombinant alphaB-crystallin and truncated mutants
###end title 50
###begin p 51
###xml 3 12 3 12 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6A</xref>
###xml 481 490 417 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6A</xref>
In Figure 6A, we studied the protein stability by incubating recombinant alphaB-crystallin and truncated mutants at the same concentration in a temperature range between 20 degreesC and 90 degreesC. Wild-type alphaB-crystallin solution became turbid at 62 degreesC, while Delta2, Delta5, and Delta7 solutions resisted higher temperatures and showed a slight turbidity at about 70 degreesC. Mutant Delta10 became turbid at 67 degreesC, whereas Delta12 turned turbid at 57 degreesC (Figure 6A). Mutants Delta1 and Delta11 were also found to be stable up to 67 degreesC and 62 degreesC, respectively. These thermostability tests suggest that the removal of COOH-terminal residue (Lys) in Delta1 and the 11th residue (Glu) from the COOH-terminal end in Delta11 appears to play opposite roles in the structural stability of deletion mutants under heat stress.

In order to investigate the roles of lysine174 and lysine175, we constructed various mutants and analyzed their chaperone activities at high temperature. At a molar ratio of 1:4 (chaperone/betaL-crystallin) at 60 degreesC, the double mutant, K174/175A, showed an almost complete inhibition of betaL-crystallin aggregation (; Figure S5). Mutant Delta1 showed slightly better chaperone activity than wild-type alphaB-crystallin. Both single-replacement mutants, K174A and K175A, showed better chaperone activities than Delta1 and wild-type alphaB-crystallin. Mutants Delta1/K174A and K174/175A showed better chaperone activities than Delta2. The results indicate that two COOH-terminal lysines do not appear to play positively enhancing roles regarding chaperone activity of alphaB-crystallin. We then compared the chaperone activities of various mutants by changing electrostatic-charge states at the COOH-terminal end (; Figure S5). Mutants Delta1/K174A, Delta1/K174S, Delta1/K174E, K174/175A, and K174/175E all show better chaperone activities than Delta2, indicating again that the positive lysine residues may not contribute much to the chaperone activity. When we increased the temperature to 65 degreesC and used gamma-crystallin as a substrate at a molar ratio of 2:3 (chaperone/gamma-crystallin), similar results to betaL-crystallin were observed (Figure 7). These results are summarized in Figure 7C as percentages of protection, (Igamma-IB)/Igammax100, where Igamma is the intensity of scattered light for alpha-crystallin without chaperone protein in chaperone assays, and IB is the intensity of scattered light in the presence of alphaB-crystallin or various mutants. Wild-type alphaB-crystallin showed about 26% protection whereas Delta1/K174E and K174/175E showed about 96% protection under the same assay conditions. Mutant Delta1 showed only 34% protection and Delta2 about 75% protection. Most prominently Delta1/K174A, Delta1/K174S, and K174/175A each showed about 87% protection. It is of interest to find that K175A showed 71% and K174A 58% protection, emphasizing that replacing lysine at position 174 contributes much less in increasing chaperone activity than at position 175. We further increase the temperature to 70 oC and perform the same assay (Figure 7D). Wild-type alphaB-crystallin and Delta1 become extensively turbid after 20 min incubation in this assay. Mutants Delta1/K174A, Delta1/K174S, and K174/175A showed near 50% protection at 70 oC. Both Delta1/K174E and K174/175E still showed good protective activities under this condition.

###begin title 61
Discussion
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 107 109 99 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r26">26</xref>
###xml 110 112 102 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r27">27</xref>
###xml 247 249 235 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r28">28</xref>
###xml 250 252 238 240 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r31">31</xref>
###xml 389 391 373 375 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r32">32</xref>
###xml 425 427 409 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r33">33</xref>
###xml 428 430 412 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r34">34</xref>
###xml 454 456 438 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r35">35</xref>
###xml 574 576 554 556 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r36">36</xref>
###xml 610 612 590 592 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r37">37</xref>
###xml 613 615 593 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r38">38</xref>
Like other sHSPs, both alphaA- and alphaB-crystallins provide thermotolerance when overexpressed in cells [26,27]. Outside the lens, alphaA-crystallin is only found at very low levels in some cell types, notably in the retina, kidney, and thymus [28-31]. In contrast, alphaB-crystallin is implicated in various important cellular processes, such as the suppression of protein aggregation [32], cytoskeletal protein dynamics [33,34], and cell protection [35]. alphaB-Crystallin is also overexpressed in some tissues of distinct pathological states, including retinoblastoma [36] and neurodegenerative diseases [37,38].
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 107 109 103 105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r39">39</xref>
###xml 110 112 106 108 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r42">42</xref>
###xml 309 311 301 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r43">43</xref>
###xml 574 576 562 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r44">44</xref>
###xml 577 579 565 567 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r48">48</xref>
###xml 601 603 589 591 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r49">49</xref>
###xml 856 863 840 847 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 1129 1131 1098 1100 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r50">50</xref>
###xml 45 50 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1065 1070 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The major post-translational modification in human alpha-crystallin occurs in its COOH-terminal extension [39-42]. It was previously found that COOH-terminal truncated alphaA-crystallin by trypsin digestion showed a decreased ability to protect proteins from heat-induced aggregation using an in vitro assay [43]. The presence of an exposed COOH-terminal extension is in accordance with prevalent observations that the last 20 or so residues of the alpha-crystallin are especially liable to truncations and modifications by cellular proteases or other modification enzymes [44-48]. A previous report [49] also indicated that the COOH-terminal lysines of alphaB-crystallin are required for protection against ischemic damage in cardiomyocytes. The deletion of these two lysines produced a significant decrease in the molecular size of the native aggregate (Table 1), and this alteration was reported to diminish its stabilization of the microtubular cytoskeleton. The chaperone activity, determined with ADH and betaL-crystallin as target proteins, was increased in human alphaA1-172 and decreased in alphaA1-168 and alphaA1-162 [50].
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 237 239 225 227 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r19">19</xref>
###xml 634 636 618 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r43">43</xref>
###xml 754 762 722 730 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 890 892 854 856 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r51">51</xref>
Previous literature abounds with reports characterizing sHSPs and various alphaA- or alphaB-crystallin mutants, often with inconsistent and contradictory findings. Since we have previously cloned and expressed porcine alphaB-crystallin [19], it is very useful to apply site-specific mutagenesis for deriving the structure-function correlation of this molecular chaperone. In this study, we have focused on the comparison of the structural thermostability and chaperone activity of alphaB-crystallin and its recombinant site-specific mutants, plus various COOH-terminal truncated mutants. In contrast to the report of Takemoto et al. [43], it is very intriguing to find that some of COOH-terminal truncated mutants (Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10 in Figure 6) with higher thermostability actually perform better as molecular chaperones than the intact alphaB-crystallin. Plater et al. [51] also reported that mutations to COOH-terminal lysines of alphaB-crystallin reduced the chaperone-like activity of this crystallin. However, these authors adopted native alpha-crystallin that is composed of alphaA and alphaB with vastly different chaperone activity as a reference and control protein for activity comparison, which could lead to ambiguous conclusions regarding the structure-functional roles of these two lysine residues. In order to clear some discrepancy and contradictory results among different groups, we have directly used wild-type alphaB-crystallin as a control for a more meaningful activity comparison among various truncated or site-directed mutants.
###end p 64
###begin p 65
###xml 421 430 393 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f5">Figure 5A</xref>
###xml 763 772 712 721 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f5">Figure 5B</xref>
###xml 1161 1162 1098 1099 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r9">9</xref>
To investigate the effect the length of COOH-terminal extension has on alphaB-crystallin chaperone function, we compared the chaperone activities of truncated mutants (Delta10, Delta11, and Delta12) with wild-type alphaB-crystallin. Chaperone activity assays based on insulin B chain aggregation induced by DTT showed that the truncated mutant Delta10 possesses similar chaperone activity to wild-type alphaB-crystallin (Figure 5A). Under similar assay conditions, the truncated mutant Delta11 showed less chaperone activity than wild-type alphaB-crystallin, whereas Delta12 showed no chaperone activity at all. Chaperone assays based on thermal denaturation of betaL-crystallin showed wild-type alphaB-crystallin and Delta11 possess little chaperone activities (Figure 5B). On the other hand, the truncated mutant Delta10 still preserves about 50% chaperone activity whereas Delta12 shows no chaperone activity under similar heating conditions. In summary, the maximal truncated length in COOH-terminal extension of alphaB-crystallin to preserve chaperone function should be about ten amino acids. The result is in accord with that obtained from NMR analysis [9], which indicated that the COOH-terminal 8 and 10 residues of alphaA- and alphaB-crystallin, respectively, occur as solvent-exposed random coils.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 1037 1039 965 967 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r52">52</xref>
###xml 1499 1507 1411 1419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f6">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 1631 1633 1535 1537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r53">53</xref>
###xml 1784 1793 1680 1689 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f7">Figure 7A</xref>
###xml 2145 2154 2029 2038 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f7">Figure 7C</xref>
###xml 2277 2286 2151 2160 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f7">Figure 7D</xref>
###xml 2365 2366 2231 2232 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">o</sup>
###xml 2526 2527 2392 2393 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">I</italic>
###xml 2580 2588 2446 2454 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f3">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 1657 1662 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1723 1728 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Recombinant alphaB-crystallin, Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, Delta10, and Delta12 showed similar near-UV CD spectra, which indicated that these proteins have similar tertiary structure. The intrinsic fluorescence spectra of recombinant alphaB-crystallin, Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10 were also similar; all of them showing emission maxima at 339-340 nm (; Figure S2). The emission maxima of Trp residues inside these proteins implied that Trp residues might be buried in the hydrophobic microenvironment. The ANS binding assays also showed similar surface hydrophobicity of these mutants (Delta2, Delta5, Delta7, and Delta10) with wild-type alphaB-crystallin (; Figure S3). The available data appeared to show COOH-terminal extension adopted no preferred conformation and extended outside the domain core of alphaB-crystallin. It has been proposed that the COOH-terminal extension segments in alpha-crystallin play an important role in solubilizing the protein and the formation of HMW complex during the process of chaperoning action [52]. It may bear some relevance to the observation that the deletion mutant Delta12 lost its solubility in water. In addition to acting as a solubilizer, the COOH-terminal extension of alphaB-crystallin may also play an important role in the stabilization of alphaB-crystallin assemblies. Our results of thermostability tests provided experimental evidence that the COOH-terminal extension indeed plays a critical role in the stabilization of alphaB-crystallin (Figure 6). Deletion of E164-K175 (Delta12) greatly reduces the structural stability of alphaB-crystallin. Recently, Treweek et al. [53] showed that K174/175A human alphaB-crystallin has better thermostability than wild-type human alphaB-crystallin, which is consistent with our study. Figure 7A shows that porcine alphaB-crystallin does not require two positive-charge lysines in protecting gamma-crystallin from thermal aggregation. The positive-charge lysines may interfere with the chaperone activity at this site. Substitution of alphaB-crystallin Lys174-175 with glutamate leads to better chaperone activity than alanine- or serine-mutants (Figure 7C). It is noteworthy that Delta1/K174E and K174/175E showed remarkable chaperone activity at high temperature (70 degreesC, Figure 7D). Wild-type alphaB-crystallin and Delta1 become turbid after incubation at 70 oC for 15 min. K174A and K175A show slightly higher turbidity than others (: Figure S6). It is to be noted that pH value of the PBS buffer used was 7.4 whereas pI values of these mutants lied between 5.85 and 6.55 (Figure 3), indicating that precipitation of proteins was caused by thermal denaturation instead of isoelectric precipitation of alphaB-crystallin. The results also indicated that COOH-terminal lysines may be the main residues to cause the aggregation of alphaB-crystallin at high temperature. In contrast, Delta1/K174E and K174/175E showed remarkable thermostability and chaperone activity at high temperature. The results suggest that charge repulsion of COOH-terminal lysines and some positive-charge groups of alphaB-crystallin may decrease the thermostability of alphaB-crystallin. On the other hand, charge attraction of COOH-terminal glutamates and these positive-charge groups of alphaB-crystallin can promote thermostability of K174/175E mutant.
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 278 280 274 276 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r54">54</xref>
###xml 48 53 <span type="species:ncbi:4565">wheat</span>
Recently, the crystal structure of a eukaryotic wheat sHSP 16.9 was solved, revealing the mode of interaction between alpha-crystallin domain and flanking extensions leading to the assembly of sHSP into a dodecameric double disk similar to most prokaryotic chaperonin proteins [54]. The ability of the COOH-terminal extension to build different assemblies of oligomeric aggregates may stem from a hinge between 9 and 10 strands, thus allowing the angle between the alpha-crystallin domain and the COOH-terminal extension to vary by 30degrees and providing some mobile flexibility of COOH-terminal segments. Such a hinge mechanism may contribute to the size polydispersity observed in the assemblies of most sHSPs and alpha-crystallin subunits. The elimination of two positive Lys-Lys residues from the COOH-terminal segment may decrease charge-charge interaction of COOH-terminal coil with other parts of alphaB polypeptide chains, thereby increasing thermostability. However, the elimination of more than 10 residues from the COOH-terminal segment may decrease the ability of the terminal coil to hold neighboring subunits, thus decreasing thermostability and chaperone activity.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 142 144 138 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r51">51</xref>
###xml 523 525 493 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r21">21</xref>
###xml 526 528 496 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="r55">55</xref>
###xml 840 849 794 803 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f8">Figure 8A</xref>
###xml 1115 1124 1053 1062 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="f8">Figure 8B</xref>
In great contrast to the report that mutations to the COOH-terminal lysines greatly reduced the chaperone-like activity of alphaB-crystallin [51], our results clearly show that mutations to COOH-terminal lysines actually enhance the chaperone-like activity of alphaB-crystallin. Finally, we have shown that native alpha-crystallin behaves differently from alphaB-crystallin. For example, alphaB-crystallin is unstable at temperatures higher than 62 degreesC, whereas alpha-crystallin is stable even at higher temperatures [21,55]. Herein we compared directly various mutants with wild-type alphaB-crystallin and showed that all site-specific mutants at lysine 174 and 175 possess better chaperone activities than wild-type alphaB-crystallin. Comparison of chaperone activities between alpha-, alphaB-, K174/175A, and K174/175E crystallins (Figure 8A) revealed that alpha-crystallin, K174/175A, and K174/175E can protect gamma-crystallin from thermal aggregation while alphaB-crystallin fails to protect gamma-crystallin under identical conditions. We also used aldolase as a substrate for chaperone activity assay (Figure 8B), showing that K174/175A mutant possesses better chaperone activity than alphaB-crystallin even though it still shows lower chaperone activity than alpha-crystallin. However, K174/175E shows similar chaperone activity with that of alpha-crystallin. In conclusion, our detailed characterization clearly demonstrated that both length and electrostatic charge of the COOH-terminal segment play crucial roles in governing the structural stability and chaperone activity of alphaB-crystallin.
