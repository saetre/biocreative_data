###begin article-title 0
Genome sequences of two novel phages infecting marine roseobacters
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 76 97 72 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Silicibacter pomeroyi</italic>
###xml 108 121 104 117 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sulfitobacter</italic>
###xml 283 299 279 295 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>
###xml 76 103 <span type="species:ncbi:246200">Silicibacter pomeroyi DSS-3</span>
###xml 108 131 <span type="species:ncbi:52598">Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36</span>
###xml 227 243 <span type="species:ncbi:10752">bacteriophage N4</span>
###xml 283 303 <span type="species:ncbi:83333">Escherichia coli K12</span>
###xml 1091 1099 <span type="species:ncbi:10752">phage N4</span>
Two bacteriophages, DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1, which infect marine roseobacters Silicibacter pomeroyi DSS-3 and Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36, respectively, were isolated from Baltimore Inner Harbor water. These two roseophages resemble bacteriophage N4, a large, short-tailed phage infecting Escherichia coli K12, in terms of their morphology and genomic structure. The full genome sequences of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 reveal that their genome sizes are 74.6 and 73.3 kb, respectively, and they both contain a highly conserved N4-like DNA replication and transcription system. Both roseophages contain a large virion-encapsidated RNA polymerase gene (> 10 kb), which was first discovered in N4. DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 also possess several genes (i.e. ribonucleotide reductase and thioredoxin) that are most similar to the genes in roseobacters. Overall, the two roseophages are highly closely related, and share 80-94% nucleotide sequence identity over 85% of their ORFs. This is the first report of N4-like phages infecting marine bacteria and the second report of N4-like phage since the discovery of phage N4 40 years ago. The finding of these two N4-like roseophages will allow us to further explore the specific phage-host interaction and evolution for this unique group of bacteriophages.

###xml 592 597 588 593 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 587 604 583 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10">Chen <italic>et al</italic>., 2006</xref>
###xml 625 636 621 632 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Roseobacter</italic>
The Roseobacter lineage in alpha-Proteobacteria comprises up to 25% of the bacterial community in seawater (Wagner-Dobler and Biebl, 2006). Roseobacters are diverse and ubiquitous in marine environments, and play an important role in marine biogeochemical cycles (Buchan et al., 2005; Wagner-Dobler and Biebl, 2006; Moran et al., 2007). Due to their ecological relevance, complete or draft genome sequences for more than 40 marine roseobacters are available (Brinkhoff et al., 2008). Recently, phage-like gene transfer agents (Lang and Beatty, 2007; Paul, 2008) and inducible prophages (Chen et al., 2006) have been found in Roseobacter genomes, indicating that virus-mediated gene transfer could be an important driving force for their genomic diversification and ecological adaptation.
###end p 3

###xml 497 516 497 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b40">Wang and Chen, 2008</xref>
###xml 596 615 596 615 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b40">Wang and Chen, 2008</xref>
###xml 79 100 <span type="species:ncbi:391588">Roseobacter sp. SIO67</span>
Currently, only one lytic phage (SIO1), which infects a marine roseobacterium (Roseobacter sp. SIO67) has been reported (Rohwer et al., 2000). SIO1 is a T7-like podovirus containing the T7-like DNA replication genes and the genes involved in phosphate metabolism. To date, the vast majority of known marine podoviruses are the members of the T7 supergroup (i.e. P60, SIO1, P-SSP7, Syn5, S-CBP1, S-CBP2 and S-CBP3) (Rohwer et al., 2000; Chen and Lu, 2002; Sullivan et al., 2005; Pope et al., 2007; Wang and Chen, 2008), and they all contain a conserved DNA polymerase in their replication module (Wang and Chen, 2008).
###end p 4

###xml 415 438 <span type="species:ncbi:52598">Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36</span>
Silicibacter pomeroyi DSS-3 and Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36 are among those roseobacters whose genomes have been sequenced. Both strains were isolated from Georgia coastal waters. Silicibacter pomeroyi DSS-3, the first roseobacterium with a sequenced genome, has been served as a model organism for studying the eco-physiological strategies of heterotrophic marine bacteria (Moran et al., 2004; Burgmann et al., 2007). Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36 has a high inorganic sulfur oxidation activity and has been a model organism for studying sulfur cycle in coastal environments (Roseobase: ).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 144 155 144 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. pomeroyi</italic>
###xml 166 179 166 179 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sulfitobacter</italic>
###xml 144 161 <span type="species:ncbi:246200">S. pomeroyi DSS-3</span>
###xml 166 189 <span type="species:ncbi:52598">Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36</span>
In a study undertaken to isolate bacteriophages from marine roseobacters, two novel phages (not seen in known marine phages) were isolated from S. pomeroyi DSS-3 and Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36 respectively. Here, we report the morphology, basic biology and genome sequences of these two newly discovered roseophages.

Results and discussion
###end title 7
###begin title 8
Morphology and basic biology of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 290 301 278 289 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. pomeroyi</italic>

###xml 956 991 900 935 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19">Kazmierczak and Rothman-Denes, 2006</xref>

###xml 1855 1871 1785 1801 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Escherichia coli</italic>

###xml 1976 1989 1906 1919 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sulfitobacter</italic>

###xml 290 307 <span type="species:ncbi:246200">S. pomeroyi DSS-3</span>
###xml 312 335 <span type="species:ncbi:52598">Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36</span>
###xml 899 911 <span type="species:ncbi:10752">coliphage N4</span>
###xml 1180 1188 <span type="species:ncbi:10752">phage N4</span>
###xml 1824 1832 <span type="species:ncbi:10752">phage N4</span>
###xml 1855 1871 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
###xml 1954 1971 <span type="species:ncbi:246200">S. pomeroyi DSS-3</span>
###xml 2034 2041 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Phages DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 were isolated from Baltimore Inner Harbor Pier V using an enrichment method. Both phages formed clear plaques. DSS3Phi2 produced large, clear plaques with irregular edges, while EE36Phi1 produced small, clear, round plaques. DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 infected only S. pomeroyi DSS-3 and Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36, respectively, and did not cross-infect 13 other diverse marine Roseobacter strains (listed in the Experiment procedures). These two phages are morphologically similar to each other with icosahedral capsids (approximately70 nm in diameter) and visible short tails (approximately26 nm long) (Fig. 1). The capsids of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 are larger than those of T7-like podoviruses. The tails of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 are longer that those of T7-like podoviruses, but much shorter than those of typical myoviruses and siphoviruses. Morphologically, they resemble coliphage N4 (with a capsid size of approximately70 nm) (Kazmierczak and Rothman-Denes, 2006), a unique phage isolated from a sewage source in 1960s (Schito et al., 1967). The phage family Podoviridae currently consists of four genera (T7-like, Phi29-like, P22-like, N4-like), and phage N4 is the only member within N4-like genus (). The infectivities of the DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 were not affected by chloroform treatment (2%), indicating that neither of them is membrane-coated. Both phages had a prolonged lysis period. The latent periods of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 were about 3 and 2 h, respectively, followed by a gradual increase of released viral particles (Fig. 2A and B). It took about 15 and 10 h for DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1, respectively, to reach their growth plateaus and this resulted in the burst sizes approximately 350 and 1500 viral particles respectively. Delayed lysis and large burst size were also found in phage N4. A single N4-infected Escherichia coli produces c. 3000 viruses 3 h post infection (Schito, 1974). It is noteworthy that S. pomeroyi DSS-3 and Sulfitobacter sp.EE-36 grow nearly four times slower than E. coli, and this may partially explain the longer lysis period of these two roseophages compared with N4.

###begin title 12
General genomic features of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1
###end title 12
###begin p 13
###xml 300 301 290 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">c</italic>
###xml 501 512 487 498 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S. pomeroyi</italic>
###xml 529 542 515 528 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sulfitobacter</italic>
###xml 290 298 <span type="species:ncbi:10752">phage N4</span>
###xml 445 453 <span type="species:ncbi:10752">phage N4</span>
###xml 501 518 <span type="species:ncbi:246200">S. pomeroyi DSS-3</span>
###xml 529 552 <span type="species:ncbi:52598">Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36</span>
DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 are linear, double-stranded DNA viruses. The genome sizes of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 are 74.6 and 73.3 kb, respectively, much larger than most known podoviruses, such as T7-like (37-46 kb), P22-like (39-50 kb) and Phi29-like (11-21 kb) podoviruses (), but comparable to phage N4 (c. 70 kb, NCBI Accession No. EF056009). The G+C contents of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 genomes are 47.9% and 47.0%, respectively, higher than that of phage N4 (41%) but much lower than those of their hosts S. pomeroyi DSS-3 (64%) and Sulfitobacter sp. EE-36 (60%). A total of 81 open reading frames (ORFs) were identified in DSS3Phi2 and 79 ORFs were identified in EE36Phi1.
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 142 148 142 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3</xref>
###xml 370 377 370 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SD1">Fig. S1</xref>
###xml 532 534 532 534 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">ca</italic>
###xml 569 576 569 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 852 859 848 855 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1</xref>
Despite their different host origin, the two roseophages share approximately 85% ORFs (70 ORFs) and have similar overall genome organization (Fig. 3). The ORFs shared between these two phages are 80-94% identical at the DNA level, and 83-98% identical at the amino acid level. The ORFs unique to each phage are mostly distributed on the left half side of their genomes (Fig. S1). Among all the identified ORFs, 26 ORFs from both roseophages are most closely related to genes from N4, with 26-57% amino acid identity, accounting for ca. 30% of both roseophages genomes (Table 1). Roseophages and N4 share DNA metabolism and replication genes, transcription genes, structural genes, host interaction genes and some additional genes without known function. DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 also contain ORFs similar to genes from other types of phages and bacteria (Table 1), indicating the mosaic feature of the phage genomes. Approximately 40% of both roseophages ORFs have no matches in the database.

###xml 951 975 943 967 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b20">Kazmierczak <italic>et al</italic>., 2002</xref>
###xml 1140 1145 1132 1137 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1128 1152 1120 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b20">Kazmierczak <italic>et al</italic>., 2002</xref>
###xml 1195 1202 1187 1194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SD1">Fig. S2</xref>
###xml 296 304 <span type="species:ncbi:10752">phage N4</span>
Strikingly, both DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 contain a large ORF, which flanks more than 10 kb of phage genome (Table 1, Fig. 3). Those ORFs can be translated into 3632 aa and 3786 aa proteins for DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1, respectively, which match a 3500 aa virion-encapsidated RNA polymerase (vRNAP) in phage N4 (27% amino acid identity). Among all the known phages, N4 is the only one that contains this super large vRNAP gene (Falco et al., 1980). It is intriguing that these phages carry a conserved gene that makes up one-seventh of their genome. N4 vRNAP is responsible for early transcription (Falco et al., 1977) and DNA replication (Kazmierczak and Rothman-Denes, 2006). N4 vRNAP is packed in viral particles and injected into host cell upon infection (Falco et al., 1977; 1980). Similar to N4 vRNAP, the two roseophage vRNAPs do not contain any cysteine residues. The lack of cysteine residues could be important for v-RNAP to enter the host cells (Kazmierczak et al., 2002). Four conserved T7-like RNAP motifs (motifs T/DxxGR, A, B and C) and their catalytic residues (R424, K670, Y678, D559 and D951) described in N4 vRNAP (Kazmierczak et al., 2002) are also present in the two roseophages (Fig. S2), suggesting the similar function of vRNAP in DSS3Phi2, EE36Phi1 and N4. The advantage of a > 10 kb RNA polymerase gene to a phage is still not clear. However, it is interesting that this unique feature is conserved among the N4-like phages.

###xml 320 339 320 339 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b41">Willis <italic>et al</italic>., 2002</xref>
###xml 452 458 436 442 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3</xref>
The two roseophages also encode two different RNA polymerase subunits (RNAP1 and RNAP2) which are similar to N4 RNAP1 and RNAP2. RNAP1 and RNAP2 constitute N4 RNA polymerase II (RNAP II) (Willis et al., 2002). RNAP II together with gp2, which also appears in roseophages, activates the transcription of N4 middle genes (Willis et al., 2002). In DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1, there are several small insertions (approximately2 kb) between the RNAP1 and RNAP2 (Fig. 3). However, there is only a 47 bp gap between N4 RNAP1 and RNAP2. The presence of vRNAP and RNAP II in roseophages suggests that these two roseophages may use similar early and middle transcription machinery to that in N4.

###xml 1788 1807 1772 1791 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6">Buchan <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
Both DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 possess the complete N4-like DNA replication system, including DNA helicase, DNA polymerase (DNA pol), single-stranded DNA binding protein (ssb), gp43 and vRNAP, and the relative gene order of these DNA replication genes is conserved among the two roseophages and N4 (Fig. 3). These genes are essential for in vivo N4 DNA replication (Kazmierczak and Rothman-Denes, 2006). The DNA pol genes of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 share high amino acid identity (53%) with N4 DNA pol (Table 1). DNA pol of the two roseophages and N4 all contain the DNA polymerase A domain and 3'-5' exonuclease domain but lacks 5'-3' exonuclease domain (Kazmierczak and Rothman-Denes, 2006). DNA pol gene phylogenetic analysis shows that DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 are closely related to N4, but distantly related to T7 supergroup podoviruses (Fig. 4). When the N4-like DNA pol sequences were searched against the metagenomic databases, a limited amount of environmental sequences (11 hits) were obtained (Table 2). Interestingly, all the close hits were found in the nearshore GOS sites (i.e. harbors, basins, or mangrove-associated habitats), but not in the open ocean sites. In addition, we also searched the GOS database using other N4 like genes from DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 genomes. Seventeen of 26 genes from these two roseophages could find homologous sequences in 13 GOS sites, resulting in a total of 96 N4-like environmental sequences (data not shown). Among these sequences, only two were found in the open ocean, and the rest 94 sequences were from coastal sites. These results suggest that N4-like roseophages are more abundant in the coastal waters than the open ocean water. It has been known that roseobacters are more abundant in the coastal water compared with the oceanic water (Buchan et al., 2005). Whether the geographic pattern of N4-like phages could be related to the distribution of roseobacters warrants further study.

Both roseophages possess a single-stranded DNA-binding protein (ssb) homologous to N4 ssb. N4 ssb belongs to a novel protein family and is involved in DNA replication, DNA recombination (Lindberg et al., 1989; Choi et al., 1995) and activation of E. coli RNA polymerase at late N4 transcription (Cho et al., 1995). The function of roseophages ssb genes is not clear. Previous research suggested that the single-stranded DNA binding activity and transcriptional activation of N4 ssb are separable, and the residues S260, K264 and K265 in the C-terminal of N4 ssb constitute part or all of an 'activating region' required for transcriptional activation, and were proposed to activating region (Miller et al., 1997). However, alignment of the amino acids of the ssb from roseophages and N4 shows that the C-terminal of roseophages ssb do not contain Lys residues at the end of polypeptide (Fig. S3). Moreover, the ssb residue Y75, which is essential for ssDNA binding activation in N4 was also not found in DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1. Further study on roseophages ssb could provide a better understanding on the function of these binding activity sites.

Aside from the N4-like genes, DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 also contain certain Roseobacter-related genes, suggesting that genetic exchange occurred between roseobacters and their phages. For examples, the roseophage ribonucleotide reductase (rnr) genes share the highest amino acid identity (56%) with nucleotide reductase from their Roseobacter hosts. The rnr phylogenetic analysis shows that DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 cluster together with roseobacters (Fig. S4). Interestingly, N4 does not contain this rnr gene. The rnr converts ribonucleosides to deoxynucleotide, and is a key enzyme involved in DNA synthesis (Jordan and Reichard, 1998). The rnr gene is rarely seen in non-marine podoviruses, such as T7, T3 and P22. However, several podoviruses isolated from marine environments, such as SIO1, P60, P-SSP7 and syn5 contain rnr (Rohwer et al., 2000; Chen and Lu, 2002; Sullivan et al., 2005; Pope et al., 2007). Perhaps, obtaining sufficient free nucleotides for phage DNA synthesis is critical in the phosphorus-limited marine environment (Chen and Lu, 2002; Sullivan et al., 2005). Viral metagenomic analysis has shown that rnr is among the most abundant genes found in Sargasso Sea (Angly et al., 2006).

DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 both contain a thioredoxin (trx) gene (105 aa) that shares high sequence homology with bacterial trx (Table 1). When binding with host thioredoxin, T7 DNA polymerase could increase its processing speed (Mark and Richardson, 1976; Huber et al., 1987). Thioredoxin has been found in other T7-like marine phage genomes (Rohwer et al., 2000; Chen and Lu, 2002; Pope et al., 2007) and appears to be a universal accessory cofactor of the replication module in these marine phages (Hardies et al., 2003). In contrast, neither coliphage N4 nor T7 contains trx. The finding of host-related thioredoxin in marine roseophages and other marine phages suggests that trx might be important to the phage survival in marine environments.

###xml 480 506 468 494 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">Bailly-Bechet <italic>et al</italic>., 2007</xref>
Using tRNA scan-SE, three tRNA genes were identified in both DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 genomes (Table 3). DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 both encode the tRNA gene CCA (Pro) and TCA (Ser). In hosts DSS-3 and EE-36, CCA and TCA are the rarest codons that code for Pro and Ser respectively. In contrast, CCA and TCA are abundant codes for Pro and Ser in phages DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 (Table 3). It is possible that these two tRNAs are important for the roseophages during their translation stage (Bailly-Bechet et al., 2007). However, the reason for the presence of tRNA ATG (Met) in DSS3Phi2 and ATC (Ile) in EE36Phi1 is unclear.

###begin title 33
DNA metabolism genes
###end title 33
###begin p 34
###xml 153 158 153 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 148 165 148 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b23">Lohr <italic>et al</italic>., 2005</xref>
###xml 168 175 168 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 604 611 596 603 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 613 619 605 611 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3</xref>
Both roseophages encode a putative deoxycytidylate deaminase (dCMP deaminase) gene, which is most similar to the dCMP deaminase in phage Phi JL001 (Lohr et al., 2005) (Table 1). Deoxycytidylate deaminase catalyses the dCMP to dUMP. The roseophages also code for putative thymidylate synthase (thyX) protein, which generates thymidine monophosphate (dTMP) from deoxyuridine monophosphate (dUMP). Both roseophages encode a putative HNH endonuclease homologous to N4 gp22 (ORF 43 in DSS3Phi2, and ORF40 in EE36Phi1). Compared with DSS3Phi2, EE36Phi1 encodes two additional endonucleases (ORF 8 and ORF 51) (Table 1, Fig. 3).
###end p 34
###begin title 35
Lysis gene
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 84 117 84 117 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b36">Stojkovi&#263; and Rothman-Denes, 2007</xref>
###xml 415 433 403 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Sagittula stellata</italic>
###xml 415 438 <span type="species:ncbi:388399">Sagittula stellata E-37</span>
It is noteworthy that homologue of N4 lysis gene, a new family of murein hydrolase (Stojkovic and Rothman-Denes, 2007), was not detected in DSS3Phi2 or EE36Phi1 genomes. However, a hypothetical protein (190 aa, ORF 71 in DSS3Phi2 and ORF 69 in EE36Phi1) located in the late region of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 genomes likely act as a lysis gene because this protein is similar to a lytic enzyme found in roseobacterium Sagittula stellata E-37 (37% amino acid identity).
###end p 36
###begin title 37
Structural genes
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 61 68 61 68 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 70 76 70 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Fig. 3</xref>
###xml 139 142 135 138 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">mcp</italic>
###xml 220 227 216 223 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 328 333 324 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 323 340 319 336 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b12">Choi <italic>et al</italic>., 2008</xref>
Four structural genes are shared between roseophages and N4 (Table 1, Fig. 3). Both DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 encode the major capsid protein (mcp) and putative phage portal gene, which are similar to their N4 counterparts (Table 1). The homologues of N4 structural genes gp52 and gp54 were also identified in the roseophages (Choi et al., 2008).
###end p 38
###begin title 39
Conclusions
###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 0 8 <span type="species:ncbi:10752">Phage N4</span>
###xml 150 162 <span type="species:ncbi:10752">coliphage N4</span>
Phage N4 has been studied for 40 years without a comparable system. The genome sequences of DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 reveal a close relationship between coliphage N4 and these two roseophages. Discovery of the two N4-like marine phages serves as a good reference system for further understanding of phage biology and evolution. The two podovirus-like roseophages are distantly related to all the known marine podoviruses. DSS3Phi2 and EE36Phi1 are warranted to investigate the ecological role of N4-like phage on marine roseobacters.
###end p 40

###begin title 52
Preparation of roseophage DNA
###end title 52
###begin p 53
###xml 51 54 51 54 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">600</sub>
###xml 135 137 123 125 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">10</sup>
###xml 140 142 128 130 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">11</sup>
###xml 161 163 149 151 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;1</sup>
###xml 312 313 300 301 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 529 530 511 512 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 607 608 589 590 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sub>
###xml 790 791 772 773 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 1101 1127 1071 1097 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b32">Sambrook and Russell, 2001</xref>
Each phage was added into a 500 ml host culture (OD600 = 0.1approximately0.2) with moi of 3, and incubated overnight. Phage lysates (1010-1011 phage particles ml-1) was mixed with 10 ml chloroform (2% v/v) and 20 g NaCl, and left on ice for 30 min before the cell debris was pelleted by centrifugation at 10 000 g for 30 min. The supernatant was mixed well with polyethylene glycol 8000 to a final concentration of 10% (w/v) and incubated overnight at 4degreesC. The phage particles were precipitated by centrifugation at 15 000 g for 30 min and then re-suspended in 10 ml of TM buffer (Tris-HCl 20 mM, MgSO4 10 mM, pH 7.4). Polyethylene Glycol-concentrated phage lysates were overlaid onto a 10-50% iodixanol (OptiPrep, Sigma-Aldrich, MO, USA) gradient, and centrifuged for 2 h at 200 000 g, using a T-8100 rotor in a Sorvall Discovery 100S centrifuge. The visible viral band was extracted using a 18-gauge needle syringe and then dialysed twice in TM buffer overnight at 4degreesC. Purified phages were stored at 4degreesC in the dark. Phage DNA was extracted using the method described previously (Sambrook and Russell, 2001).

###begin title 54
Genome sequencing and analysis
###end title 54
###begin p 55
###xml 482 484 481 483 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2,</sub>
###xml 1580 1608 1548 1576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b4">Besemer and Borodovsky, 1999</xref>
###xml 1676 1682 1644 1650 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">blastp</sc>
###xml 1693 1698 1661 1666 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">et al</italic>
###xml 1684 1705 1652 1673 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1">Altschul <italic>et al</italic>., 1990</xref>
###xml 1759 1778 1727 1746 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b24">Lowe and Eddy, 1997</xref>
###xml 1785 1795 1753 1763 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">countcodon</sc>
To prepare DNA template for genome sequencing, purified phage DNA was amplified using Genomiphi V2 kit (GE Healthcare, Piscataway, NJ, USA) according to the manufacturer's protocol. The initial sequence segments were obtained by random PCR amplification of phage DNA using degenerate primer RP-1 (5'-ATHGAYGGNGAYATHCAY-3') and RP-2 (5'-YTCRTCRTGNACCCANGC-3'). The PCR was performed in 50 mul volume containing 1x reaction buffer (Genescript, Scotch Plains, NJ, USA) with 1.5 mM MgCl2, 100 muM of dNTPs, 50 pmol of each primer, 1 U Taq DNA polymerase (Genescript) and 10 ng phage DNA as templates. PCR program consists of an initial denaturing at 94degreesC for 2 min, followed by 30 cycles of denaturing at 94degreesC for 30 s, annealing at 48degreesC for 1 min and extension at 72degreesC for 1 min and a final extension at 72degreesC for 10 min. Multiple PCR amplicons could be obtained for both phages. The most dominant bands for each phage were excised and the DNAs were purified using gel purification kit (Qiagen, Valencia, CA, USA) and sequenced bi-directionally using the same primer set. The three fragments with unambiguous sequences are used as starting templates for primer walking. All the subsequent primer walking was done by using an automated sequencer ABI 310 (PE Applied Biosystems) in the Biological and Analytical Laboratory at the Center of Marine Biotechnology, UMBI. From each primer walking, unambiguous sequences were assembled together using AssemblyLIGN program (GCG, Madison). Open reading frames were predicted by using ORF Finder () and GeneMarkS (Besemer and Borodovsky, 1999). Translated ORFs were compared with known protein sequences using blastp (Altschul et al., 1990). tRNA sequences were searched by using tRNAscan-SE (Lowe and Eddy, 1997). The countcodon program was used to determine codon usage ().
###end p 55
###begin p 56
Sequences alignment and phylogenetic analysis were performed using MacVector 7.2 program (GCG, Madison, WI). Jukes-Cantor distance matrix analysis was used to calculate the distances from the aligned sequences, and the neighbour-joining method was used to construct the phylogenetic tree.
###end p 56
