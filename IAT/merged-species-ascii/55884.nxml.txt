Detection of in vivo protein interactions between Snf1-related kinase subunits with intron-tagged epitope-labelling in plants cells
###end article-title 0

###begin p 2
###xml 595 602 584 591 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 811 824 800 813 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>
###xml 837 848 826 837 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1072 1083 1051 1062 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1123 1130 1102 1109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 23 28 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 287 292 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Plant orthologs of the yeast sucrose non-fermenting (Snf1) kinase and mammalian AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) represent an emerging class of important regulators of metabolic and stress signalling. The catalytic alpha-subunits of plant Snf1-related kinases (SnRKs) interact in the yeast two-hybrid system with different proteins that share conserved domains with the beta- and gamma-subunits of Snf1 and AMPKs. However, due to the lack of a robust technique allowing the detection of protein interactions in plant cells, it is unknown whether these proteins indeed occur in SnRK complexes in vivo. Here we describe a double-labelling technique, using intron-tagged hemagglutinin (HA) and c-Myc epitope sequences, which provides a simple tool for co-immunopurification of interacting proteins expressed in Agrobacterium-transformed Arabidopsis cells. This generally applicable plant protein interaction assay was used to demonstrate that AKINbeta2, a plant ortholog of conserved Snf1/AMPK beta-subunits, forms different complexes with the catalytic alpha-subunits of Arabidopsis SnRK protein kinases AKIN10 and AKIN11 in vivo.

###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 39 59 39 59 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis thaliana</italic>
###xml 193 194 193 194 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c1">1</xref>
###xml 195 196 195 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c2">2</xref>
###xml 304 305 304 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c3">3</xref>
###xml 438 439 438 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c4">4</xref>
###xml 440 441 440 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c5">5</xref>
###xml 668 675 668 675 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 39 59 <span type="species:ncbi:3702">Arabidopsis thaliana</span>
###xml 362 367 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 488 493 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
The genome sequence of the model plant Arabidopsis thaliana has recently been completed and annotated providing a wealth of information for emerging proteomics and functional genomics studies (1,2). Construction of new platforms for transcript profiling aim at an integrated analysis of gene expression (3), whereas systematic protein interaction screens in the yeast two-hybrid system offer a means for characterisation of the proteome (4,5). As detection of protein interactions in the yeast two-hybrid system does not necessarily imply that the same proteins also interact in plants, there is a need for development of new techniques to facilitate the detection of in vivo protein interactions in plant cells. Current problems in the analysis of subunit composition of plant protein complexes are well illustrated by the example of Snf1-related plant protein kinases (SnRKs).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 124 125 124 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c6">6</xref>
###xml 418 419 403 404 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c7">7</xref>
###xml 435 459 420 444 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 496 500 477 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SNF1</italic>
###xml 641 642 622 623 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c8">8</xref>
###xml 722 723 699 700 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c9">9</xref>
###xml 759 763 732 736 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SNF4</italic>
###xml 891 892 860 861 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c7">7</xref>
###xml 1042 1044 1000 1002 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c10">10</xref>
###xml 1183 1185 1138 1140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c11">11</xref>
###xml 1213 1217 1168 1172 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIP1</italic>
###xml 1219 1223 1174 1178 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SIP2</italic>
###xml 1228 1233 1183 1188 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">GAL83</italic>
###xml 1338 1340 1287 1289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c12">12</xref>
###xml 1525 1527 1471 1473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c13">13</xref>
###xml 41 46 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 429 434 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 435 459 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</span>
###xml 772 777 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1265 1270 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1361 1366 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
 SnRKs belong to the conserved family of yeast sucrose non-fermenting (Snf1) kinase and animal AMP-activated kinase (AMPK) (6). These conserved protein kinases are heterotrimeric enzymes consisting of alpha-, beta- and gamma-subunits. The catalytic alpha-subunit carries an N-terminal serine/threonine protein kinase domain followed by C-terminal regulatory sequences which function as a kinase autoinhibitory domain (7). In the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae the alpha-subunit is encoded by the SNF1 gene, which is required for proper regulation of glycogen storage, sporulation and transcriptional derepression of glucose-repressed genes (8). In comparison, two different isoforms of alpha-subunit are known in mammals (9). The gamma-subunit encoded by the SNF4 gene in yeast is involved in maintaining the active conformation of the alpha-subunit by binding to its autoinhibitory domain (7). The beta-subunit mediates the formation of a heterotrimeric complex because it can independently interact with both the alpha- and gamma-subunits (10). Co-transfection experiments indicate that the beta-subunit is also required for reconstitution of AMPK kinase activity in animal cells (11). Remarkably, three genes (SIP1, SIP2 and GAL83) code for the beta-subunits in yeast, whereas only two beta-isoforms have been found so far in mammals (12). Recent studies in yeast demonstrate that, in addition to the assembly of active kinase complex, the beta-subunits are required for specific recognition of diverse kinase substrates (13).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 108 110 97 99 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c14">14</xref>
###xml 111 113 100 102 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c20">20</xref>
###xml 265 267 250 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c21">21</xref>
###xml 409 420 394 405 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 514 518 495 499 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">snf1</italic>
###xml 529 531 510 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c16">16</xref>
###xml 561 572 542 553 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 736 747 707 718 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 806 810 773 777 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">snf4</italic>
###xml 832 834 799 801 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c17">17</xref>
###xml 835 837 802 804 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c19">19</xref>
###xml 872 883 839 850 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1033 1035 997 999 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c17">17</xref>
###xml 1177 1185 1141 1149 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 1187 1189 1151 1153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c17">17</xref>
###xml 1190 1192 1154 1156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c19">19</xref>
###xml 1312 1319 1269 1276 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 508 513 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 825 830 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1122 1127 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
 Orthologs of the Snf1/AMPK alpha-, beta- and gamma-subunits have been identified in several plant species (14-20). Phylogenetic analysis indicates a remarkable homology between the catalytic alpha-subunits of plant SnRKs and other members of the Snf1/AMPK family (21). According to this classification, plant protein kinases showing the highest similarity to Snf1 and AMPKs belong to the SnRK1 subfamily. In Arabidopsis two SnRK1 alpha-subunits are known which are capable of functionally complementing the yeast snf1 mutation (16). In addition, two different Arabidopsis proteins sharing homologous CbetaS (cystathione beta synthase) domains with the Snf1/AMPK gamma-subunits have been identified. However, only one of these putative Arabidopsis SnRK gamma-subunits (AtSNF4) was observed to suppress the snf4 deficiency in yeast (17,19). Based on sequence homology, two Arabidopsis genes encoding potential orthologs of Snf1/AMPK beta-subunits were identified and characterised by different patterns of transcriptional regulation (17). The interaction properties of these putative SnRK subunits have been studied in the yeast two-hybrid system and protein interaction assays in vitro (17,19). Nonetheless, it is still an open question whether the highly variable putative beta- and gamma-subunits indeed occur in vivo in common complexes with SnRK alpha-subunits, which are remarkably conserved in plant cells.
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 56 63 56 63 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in&#160;vivo</italic>
###xml 198 211 198 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>
###xml 224 235 224 235 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 243 245 243 245 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c22">22</xref>
###xml 395 408 395 408 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>
###xml 869 880 865 876 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 981 988 974 981 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 569 574 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
 To characterise the interactions between SnRK subunits in vivo, we exploited an epitope tagging technique which was recently developed for facile detection and cellular localisation of proteins in Agrobacterium-transformed Arabidopsis cells (22). By generating fusions between intron-tagged epitope coding domains and plant cDNAs, this technique eliminates artificial expression of proteins in Agrobacterium. Thus, the expression of proteins in plant cells can be specifically and sensitively detected as early as 5 days after transformation. To verify the results of yeast two-hybrid tests, a co-immunopurification assay was developed based on co-expression of interacting proteins carrying hemagglutinin (HA) and c-Myc epitopes in plant cells. Using this generally applicable plant protein interaction assay, we demonstrate here that the catalytic alpha-subunits of Arabidopsis protein kinases AKIN10 and AKIN11 form different SnRK complexes with a regulatory AKINbeta2 subunit in vivo.
###end p 7

Vectors carrying plant gene expression cassettes with an intron-tagged HA epitope coding domain were described previously (22). Similar vectors with an intron-tagged c-Myc epitope were constructed using a series of PCR reactions. A HindIII fragment of p35S GUS INT (23), containing intron IV2 of the potato gene ST-LS1 (24), was used as a template in combination with the primers MYCPIV1 (5'-GGAGATCTGAGCAAAAGTTGATTGTAAGTTTCTGCTTC TACCT-3') and MYCPIV2 (5'-GGGTCGACAAGATCCTCCTCAGACTGCACATCAACAAATTTTG-3'), both of which carried five codons from the c-Myc epitope coding sequence (in bold). The PCR product was cloned as a BglII-SalI fragment in pPE1000 (25) and sequenced to yield the vector pMESHI. The MYCPIV2comp primer (5'-GGGTCGACTCAAGATCCTCCTCAGACTGCACATCAACAAATTTTG-3') in combination with MYCPIV1 (see above) was used to similarly amplify and clone a PCR fragment in pPE1000 to obtain pLOLA. The vector pGIGI was obtained by SalI digestion and religation of plasmid pMESHI.

 The intron-tagged c-Myc epitope coding domain was fused in multiple steps to the 3'-end AKIN11 coding region to create a vector for expression of AKIN11 tagged with a C-terminal c-Myc epitope. First, AKIN11 sequences in pGEM (19) were used as template for PCR amplification of a 3' segment from the coding region by removing the stop codon using the primer pair 5'-CCCGGGAATTCCGTGTTCCAAGTGGCTATCT-3' and 5'-GGGGTCGACGATATCCACACGAAGCTCGCGAAG-3' (AKIN11 sequences in bold; restriction sites in italic). After filling in the ends with T4 DNA polymerase, a NotI fragment carrying the plant expression cassette from pAKIN11-myc was inserted into a filled-in HindIII site of binary vector pPCV002 (26), yielding pAKIN11-myc-pPCV002.

###begin title 19
Preparation of protein extracts and co-immunopurification assays
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 212 213 206 207 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">2</sub>
###xml 450 451 440 441 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">g</italic>
###xml 1510 1512 1454 1456 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c22">22</xref>
###xml 929 934 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1560 1563 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1623 1628 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1766 1769 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1778 1783 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
To prepare crude protein extracts, plant cells were collected by filtration using a Millipore holder system, frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at -70degreesC. The cells were homogenised to a powder in liquid N2 and resuspended in extraction buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.6, 10% glycerol, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM DTT, 1% Igepal CA-630 and 20 microl/ml Sigma plant protease inhibitor mix). A cleared cell lysate was obtained by centrifugation (twice at 3000 g for 10 min at 4degreesC). Following the determination of protein concentration, the cleared lysate was subjected to immunoaffinity purification. The protein extract (1-2 mg) was supplemented with 150 mM NaCl and pre-cleared with 50 microl of protein G-Sepharose (Sigma) (equilibrated in IPW buffer, 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl and 0.1% Igepal CA-630). The pre-cleared lysate was incubated for at least 3 h at 4degreesC with 50 microl of affinity matrix carrying either mouse monoclonal anti-HA.11 IgG (clone 16B12; BAbCO) or anti-c-Myc IgG (clone 9E10; BAbCO). The matrix was placed on a 1 ml Mobicol (MobiTec) column and washed with a 100 bead volume (5 ml) of IPW buffer. The bound proteins were eluted by treating the beads twice with 1 bead volume (approximately50 microl) of either HA or c-Myc peptide solution (Genosys) (1 mg/ml in 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl) for 15 min at 30degreesC. The eluted proteins were separated by SDS-PAGE and analysed by western blotting using Immobilon membrane (PVDF 0.45 microm; Millipore) as described (22). For immunodecoration, either a high affinity rat monoclonal anti-HA IgG (clone 3F10; Boehringer-Roche) or a mouse monoclonal anti-c-Myc IgG (clone 9E10; BAbCO) was used as primary antibody, followed by treatment with either peroxidase-conjugated anti-rat or anti-mouse IgG Fab fragment (Boehringer-Roche) and subsequent enhanced chemiluminescence detection (ECL; Pharmacia-Amersham).

###end title 23
###begin title 24
Construction of vectors with intron-tagged HA and c-Myc epitope coding domains
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 115 128 115 128 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>
###xml 185 191 185 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ST-LS1</italic>
###xml 193 195 193 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c23">23</xref>
###xml 196 198 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c24">24</xref>
###xml 351 364 351 364 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>
###xml 614 616 614 616 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c22">22</xref>
###xml 896 897 896 897 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke491f1">1</xref>
###xml 1140 1142 1140 1142 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c22">22</xref>
###xml 1417 1420 1417 1420 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bam</italic>
###xml 1537 1538 1537 1538 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke491f1">1</xref>
###xml 1802 1805 1802 1805 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Not</italic>
###xml 1875 1888 1875 1888 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>
###xml 173 179 <span type="species:ncbi:4113">potato</span>
###xml 1491 1496 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
To develop a co-immunopurification assay for detection of interacting proteins labelled with different epitopes in Agrobacterium-transformed plant cells, an intron from the potato gene ST-LS1 (23,24) was inserted in the coding domains of HA and c-Myc epitopes. This was necessary to eliminate artificial expression of epitope-tagged plant proteins in Agrobacterium, which could interfere with specific detection of epitope-labelled proteins in transformed plant cells. The construction of plant expression vectors pPILY and pMENCHU, carrying intron-tagged HA epitope coding domains, has been previously described (22). Using a similar approach, a set of vectors carrying intron-tagged c-Myc epitope coding domains were constructed to facilitate the labelling of plant proteins with either N- or C-terminal c-Myc peptide tags (see Materials and Methods). In the c-Myc epitope fusion vectors (Fig. 1) the polylinker sequences located 5'-upstream of the c-Myc coding domains are identical, but the cloning sites located 3'-downstream of the c-Myc epitope sequences are different. Plasmid pLOLA and pGIGI are compatible with pPILY and pMENCHU (22), respectively, allowing a fusion of protein coding sequences with HA and c-Myc epitopes using the same reading frames. Vector pMESHI provides a different reading frame to generate alternative translational fusions. Plasmid pGIGI is a derivative of pMESHI, which contains a BamHI site for generation of in-frame fusions with cDNAs derived from the yeast two-hybrid vectors pACT2 and pAS2 (Fig. 1). Plant gene expression cassettes in all HA and c-Myc epitope vectors carry a CaMV 35S promoter with a duplicated enhancer domain and 3' transcription terminator sequences from the nopaline synthase gene. The plant gene expression cassettes are flanked by unique NotI sites to facilitate further cloning of epitope-tagged constructs in Agrobacterium binary vectors for plant transformation.

Optimisation of co-expression of HA and c-Myc epitope-tagged proteins in Arabidopsis cells by Agrobacterium-mediated transformation
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 87 100 87 100 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>

###xml 364 366 361 363 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c26">26</xref>
###xml 389 400 386 397 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 421 423 418 420 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c22">22</xref>
###xml 530 541 527 538 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 872 883 869 880 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1099 1100 1096 1097 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke491f2">2</xref>
###xml 1514 1515 1511 1512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke491f2">2</xref>
To optimise the conditions for detection of c-Myc epitope-tagged proteins expressed in Agrobacterium-transformed plant cells, a beta-glucuronidase (GUS) reporter protein was labelled with an N-terminal c-Myc epitope. A uidA/gusA coding sequence was inserted in pLOLA, then the plant gene expression cassette was moved into the Agrobacterium binary vector pPCV002 (26) and transformed into Arabidopsis cells as described (22). The amount of bacterial cells used for transformation was adjusted such that on average 80% of infected Arabidopsis cells showed staining with the histochemical GUS substrate X-Gluc 5 days after infection. From the transformed cells protein extracts were prepared, separated by SDS-PAGE and subjected to western blotting with different commercially available anti-c-Myc antibodies. In crude protein extracts prepared from control non-transformed Arabidopsis cells all anti-c-Myc IgGs tested in western blotting (including a BAbCO IgG from clone 9E10, which provided the highest sensitivity of detection) showed a non-specific cross-reaction with a protein of 100 kDa (Fig. 2, TOTAL). Nonetheless, the anti-c-Myc antibodies specifically detected a myc-GUS reporter protein with the expected molecular mass. Unlike western blotting, preclearing of protein extracts with protein G-Sepharose followed by immunoprecipitation with immobilised anti-c-Myc IgG resulted in specific detection of the myc-GUS protein, providing a reliable tool for development of a co-immunopurification assay (Fig. 2, IP).

###xml 374 385 374 385 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 498 500 498 500 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">10</sup>
###xml 500 513 500 513 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>
###xml 592 594 592 594 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">10</sup>
###xml 697 710 697 710 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>
###xml 794 805 794 805 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1268 1269 1268 1269 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke491f3">3</xref>
###xml 1414 1427 1414 1427 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>
###xml 1508 1519 1508 1519 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
 To simultaneously express two different proteins in plant cells, the conditions for co-transformation were optimised using Agrobacterium strains which carried reporter gene constructs coding for the Discosoma red fluorescent protein DsRed in pR97 (28) and the green fluorescent protein mGFP4 in pBI121 (29). Titration of the inoculum size by infection of 50 ml aliquots of Arabidopsis cell cultures during the logarithmic growth phase indicated that the cultures could tolerate a maximum of 1 x 1010Agrobacterium cells without losing their viability (data not shown). Using a total of 1 x 1010 bacterial cells for infection, the transformation frequencies ranged between 70 and 80% with a single Agrobacterium strain, whereas double infections with two strains yielded only 40-50% transformed Arabidopsis cells which displayed expression of either green or red fluorescent protein as visualised by epifluorescence microscopy (data not shown). To measure the co-transformation frequency, the proportion of cells showing both green (GFP) and red (DsRed) fluorescence was determined using confocal laser scanning microscopy. Sequential recording of green, red and merged yellow (GFP + DsRed) images provided a precise count for single and double transformed cells (Fig. 3). On average, 55% of cells growing in aggregates (i.e. microcalli) displayed both GFP and DsRed emission, indicating that it is feasible to use Agrobacterium-mediated co-transformation for expression of two different proteins in cultured Arabidopsis cells.

AKINbeta2, an ortholog of the Sip2 beta-subunit of yeast Snf1 kinase, forms different complexes with Arabidopsis SnRK alpha-subunits AKIN10 and AKIN11 in vivo

###begin p 30
###xml 152 163 149 160 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 557 559 548 550 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c17">17</xref>

###xml 885 896 870 881 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1061 1065 1039 1043 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">LacZ</italic>
###xml 1199 1200 1174 1175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke491f4">4</xref>
###xml 81 86 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 432 437 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 675 680 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 821 826 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1324 1329 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1339 1346 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
###xml 1380 1385 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
To identify potential orthologs of Snf1/AMPK beta-subunits, different domains of yeast Sip2 protein were used to search for homologous sequences in the Arabidopsis database using the TBLAST program. The best hit identified a gene (accession no. gb:ATFCA6, chromosome 4, ESSA I contig fragment no. 6) which coded for a protein showing partial homology to conserved kinase-interacting sequences (i.e. KIS domains) of beta-subunits of yeast Snf1 and animal AMPKs. The same gene was recently identified as encoding a putative SnRK subunit designated AKINbeta2 (17). A cDNA encoding AKINbeta2 in fusion with the Gal4 activation domain was isolated from a cDNA library made in the yeast two-hybrid vector pACT2 (16) using a specific probe obtained by PCR amplification of the last exon. This cDNA clone was used as prey in the yeast strain Y187 to test for interaction of AKINbeta2 with the Arabidopsis SnRK1 alpha-subunits AKIN10 and AKIN11 used as baits in two-hybrid assays. Quantitative measurement of beta-galactosidase enzyme levels, reflecting activation of a LacZ reporter gene by the bait-prey interactions, suggested that AKINbeta2 is a putative binding partner for both AKIN10 and AKIN11 (Fig. 4). The specificity of observed two-hybrid interactions was controlled using distantly related SnRK1 alpha-subunits, such as yeast Snf1 and tobacco NPK5, self-activation tests with yeast strains carrying the AKIN10 and AKIN11 baits alone and interaction assays with several unrelated preys, including pVA3 and pLAM (Clontech) (data not shown).

###xml 138 151 135 148 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>

###xml 1570 1571 1558 1559 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke491f5">5</xref>
###xml 1744 1745 1729 1730 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke491f6">6</xref>
 To verify the results of two-hybrid interactions, AKINbeta2 was co-expressed with either AKIN10 or AKIN11 in Arabidopsis cells using the Agrobacterium-mediated co-transformation method described above. AKIN10 and AKIN11 were labelled with N- and C-terminal c-Myc epitopes, respectively, whereas the N-terminus of AKINbeta2 was fused to a HA epitope as described (see Materials and Methods). First, the epitope-tagged constructs were independently transformed into Arabidopsis cells to evaluate the expression level and stability of modified AKIN10, AKIN11 and AKINbeta2 proteins. Following 5 days of Agrobacterium infection, protein extracts were prepared from the transformed cells, resolved by SDS-PAGE and immunoblotted using monoclonal anti-HA and anti-c-Myc antibodies. Both c-Myc-tagged AKIN10 and AKIN11 proteins (both 61 kDa) were detected at apparently low levels (as compared to an HA epitope-tagged GUS reporter protein; data not shown). As noted earlier, western blotting revealed a non-specific cross-reaction of monoclonal anti-c-Myc antibody with a protein of 100 kDa in all samples prepared from transformed and non-transformed Arabidopsis cells. Intriguingly, AKIN11-myc was typically detected as a doublet, suggesting post-translational modification or specific degradation of this protein (Fig. 5A). In comparison to myc-AKIN10 and AKIN11-myc, HA-AKINbeta2 was produced at an apparently higher level, but appeared to be less stable because, in addition to its intact form, some degradation products were also detected with the anti-HA antibody (Fig. 5B). These characteristics observed by immunoblotting of epitope-tagged proteins did not change when HA-AKINbeta2 was co-expressed with either myc-AKIN10 or AKIN11-myc (Fig. 6A). The apparent reduction in the intensity of the bands with respect to the single transformations probably reflects a reduced number of transformed cells when performing co-transformation experiments.

###begin p 32
###xml 603 604 593 594 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke491f6">6</xref>
###xml 1418 1429 1379 1390 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
 To examine whether HA-AKINbeta2 occurred in common complexes with the c-Myc epitope-tagged SnRK alpha-subunits, protein extracts were prepared from control non-transformed cells and cultures transformed separately with each construct, as well as from cells co-transformed with HA-AKINbeta2 and either myc-AKIN10 or AKIN11-myc. The extracts were subjected to immunaffinity purification on immobilised anti-HA and anti-c-Myc IgG matrices. Proteins eluted from the anti-HA and anti-c-Myc IgG matrices by HA and c-Myc peptides, respectively, were immunoblotted with anti-HA and anti-c-Myc antibodies (Fig. 6B). HA-AKINbeta2 was detected by the anti-HA antibody after immunaffinity binding of both myc-AKIN10 and AKIN11-myc. Interestingly, only full-length HA-AKINbeta2 was recovered in complex with the immunopurified SnRK alpha-subunits, suggesting a lack of interaction of C-terminally truncated AKINbeta2 derivatives with myc-AKIN10 and AKIN11-myc. Similarly, both c-Myc-tagged SnRK alpha-subunits were detected in association with HA-AKINbeta2 after immunoaffinity purification on anti-HA IgG. The specificity of these protein interactions was corroborated by control co-transformation experiments which revealed no interaction of HA-AKINbeta2 with an affinity purified myc-GUS protein (data not shown). Thus, these data demonstrated that AKINbeta2 is a beta-subunit of two different SnRK protein kinase complexes in Arabidopsis.

###xml 1738 1739 1701 1702 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 1746 1750 1709 1713 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;196</sup>
###xml 1847 1848 1810 1811 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">P</italic>
###xml 1863 1866 1826 1829 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;74</sup>
###xml 1872 1875 1835 1838 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;71</sup>
###xml 1884 1887 1847 1850 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">&#8211;70</sup>
###xml 618 623 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 789 794 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 915 920 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 1492 1497 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Recent annotation and comparative sequence analysis of the Arabidopsis genome reveals an unexpectedly high level of redundancy. A comparison of the chromosome 2 and 4 sequences indicates that approximately75% of all predicted gene products show a significant similarity to another protein encoded elsewhere in the genome (1). A typical example of this potential functional redundancy is provided by components of the SnRK family in Arabidopsis. Until completion of the genome sequence, two Arabidopsis genes coding for SnRK catalytic alpha-subunits had been identified and demonstrated to complement the corresponding yeast snf1 mutation (16). Similarly, two different genes were identified to code for putative homologs of Snf1/AMPK gamma-subunits based on sequence homology searches and yeast two-hybrid interaction studies (17,19). In addition, two different proteins showing domain homology to beta-subunits of yeast Snf1 (Sip/Gal83 family) and animal AMPKs were identified and tested for interaction with an Arabidopsis SnRK alpha-subunit in the two-hybrid system (17). However, our survey of the completed Arabidopsis genome sequence indicates the presence of additional genes coding for putative SnRK subunits. Using the coding sequence of AKINbeta2 in a BLASTP search, in addition to AKINbeta1 we have identified five sequences (accession nos At2g28060, AT3g01510, At1g27070, AT5g39790 and AT3g52180) which show an overall similarity and significant domain homology to members of the yeast Sip protein family (P values of 1.3e-20, 4.8e-08, 4.8e-06, 8.4e-04 and 2.3e-02, respectively). A similar BLASTP search with the coding region of AKIN10 reveals a new member of the highly conserved alpha-subunit gene family (16) (AT5g39440, P = 2.9e-196), as well as three additional genes for predicted proteins (AT4g24400, AT5g25110 and AT5g10930, P values of 3.7e-74, 2.0e-71 and 1.4e-70, respectively) showing significant homology to the regulatory domains of AKIN10 and AKIN11 catalytic alpha-subunits. As conserved sequences of regulatory domains of alpha-subunits are used as characteristic modules to identify members of the SnRK1 subfamily, it is likely that these proteins represent novel SnRK1 alpha-subunits. This surprising redundancy of genes coding for putative SnRK1 subunits suggests that many alternative kinase complexes could be formed by combinations of different subunits in plants. Determination of the subunit composition of SnRK complexes thus represents a demanding task for future studies.
###end p 34
###begin p 35
###xml 248 256 248 256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 350 352 350 352 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">35</sup>
###xml 464 472 464 472 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 824 831 824 831 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 47 52 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
 Among the currently available techniques, the yeast two-hybrid system offers a simple and reliable means for sorting of protein interactions. Nonetheless, the results of two-hybrid interaction assays often require confirmation. Therefore, control in vitro protein binding studies are usually performed with recombinant proteins in combination with [35S]methionine labelling of their binding partners in various coupled transcription-translation systems. In these in vitro binding assays the interacting proteins are assembled in heterologous systems which lack potentially necessary endogenous factors (such as other subunits of a protein complex) and post-translational modifications. These evident problems call for development of new protein interaction assays which facilitate the characterisation of protein complexes in vivo.
###end p 35
###begin p 36
###xml 27 34 27 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 274 285 274 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 306 319 306 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Agrobacterium</italic>
###xml 541 543 541 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c22">22</xref>
###xml 746 757 746 757 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 839 850 839 850 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 881 883 875 877 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c17">17</xref>
###xml 892 899 886 893 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 992 994 982 984 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c16">16</xref>
###xml 1285 1296 1269 1280 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
 With the aim of detecting in vivo protein interactions between subunits of SnRK protein kinases in plant cells, we have developed a simple co-immunopurification assay based on a previously described intron-tagged epitope-labelling technique and efficient transformation of Arabidopsis cell suspensions by Agrobacterium. In our previous studies we have exploited an intron-tagged HA epitope coding domain to affinity purify proteins and determine their localisation in plant cells using either transient expression or stable transformation (22). In the present work we have optimised the application of another epitope in order to establish a reliable assay for monitoring the interaction of plant proteins labelled with HA and c-Myc epitopes in Arabidopsis cells. We used this technique to determine whether a recently described putative Arabidopsis SnRK beta-subunit (AKINbeta2; 17) occurs in vivo in complexes with two previously characterised different alpha-subunits, AKIN10 and AKIN11 (16). We have observed that both AKIN10 and AKIN11 interact with the putative AKINbeta2 regulatory subunit in the two-hybrid system. To verify the results of two-hybrid assays, AKINbeta2 was labelled with an HA epitope and co-expressed with c-Myc epitope-labelled AKIN10 and AKIN11 proteins in Arabidopsis cells. A considerable difference was noted between the expression levels of c-Myc-tagged alpha-subunits and HA-tagged AKINbeta2 protein by immunoblotting. However, this observation must be treated with necessary caution due to likely differences in the sensitivity and specificity of anti-HA and anti-c-Myc antibodies. Whereas western blotting suggested apparently low level expression, both myc-AKIN10 and AKIN11-myc proteins were isolated in association with comparable amounts of HA-AKINbeta2 protein using immunoaffinity binding to anti-HA and anti-c-Myc IgG beads.
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 202 204 199 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c13">13</xref>
###xml 217 226 214 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in planta</italic>
###xml 631 633 628 630 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke491c16">16</xref>
###xml 195 200 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
 Recent studies suggest that formation of different Snf1 kinase enzyme complexes with the Sip/Gal83 beta-subunits may account for specific targeting of the Snf1 kinase to different substrates in yeast (13). Using the in planta protein interaction assay presented here, it is feasible now to label as pair-wise combinations all putative subunits of plant SnRK1 kinases with epitope tags. In addition to immunoaffinity purification of SnRK complexes, this approach could help to decipher the combinations of different subunits and determine their role in the recognition of different substrates and SnRK-binding signalling proteins (16). Although our present results are confined to suspension cultured cells, the described epitope-tagged constructs can also be used for generation of transgenic plants to facilitate the identification of multiprotein complexes that may show different subunit compositions in diverse cell types and phases of plant development.
###end p 37
