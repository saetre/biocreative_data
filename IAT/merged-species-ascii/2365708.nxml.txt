The MutS-based mismatch repair (MMR) system has been conserved from prokaryotes to humans, and plays important roles in maintaining the high fidelity of genomic DNA. MutS protein recognizes several different types of damaged base pairs, including methylated guanine-containing base pairs. Here, we looked at the relationship between MutS and the effects of methylating versus ethylating agents on mutagenesis, using a MutS deficient strain of E. coli. We found that methylating agents induce mutations more effectively in a MutS-deficient strain than in wild-type, whereas the genetic background did not affect mutagenicity of ethylating agents. Thus, the role of the MMR system in repair with methylation-induced mutagenesis appears to be greater than its role with ethylation-induced mutagenesis. To further understand E. coli MutS, we compared binding of the protein to O6-alkylated guanine paired with thymine, which could lead to mutation, versus cytosine, which cannot. Moreover, we compared binding of MutS to oligoduplexes containing different base pairs; namely, O6-MeG:T, O6-MeG:C, O6-EtG:T, O6-EtG:C, G:T and G:C. The dissociation constants (Kd's), which reflect the strength of binding, followed the order G:T- > O6-MeG:T- > O6-EtG:T- = O6-EtG:C- >/= O6-MeG:C- > G:C. These results suggest that a thymine paired with O6-methylated guanine is recognized as a mismatch by MutS and removed more efficiently than a thymine opposite O6-ethylated guanine. Taken together, the data suggest that in E. coli, the MMR system plays a more significant role in repair of methylation-induces lesions than those caused by ethylation.

###begin title 3
1. Introduction
###end title 3
###begin p 4

###xml 872 874 872 874 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R10">10</xref>
###xml 876 878 876 878 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R11">11</xref>
###xml 899 901 899 901 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R12">12</xref>
###xml 924 926 924 926 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R13">13</xref>
###xml 928 930 928 930 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R14">14</xref>
The MutS-based mismatch repair (MMR) system is important for recognition and removal of mismatched base pairs and small insertion/deletion loops during DNA replication, and in maintaining the high fidelity of genomic DNA by preventing the accumulation of mutations [reviewed by 1-3]. MMR pathway components such as MutS-related proteins have been widely conserved from prokaryotes to eukaryotes. Additionally, deficiency in MMR pathway components increases both the rate of spontaneous mutation and the rate of mutation induced by UV light [4] or alkylating agents [5-7], and has also been implicated in carcinogenesis, including hereditary nonpolyposis colon cancers [8] and skin cancers [9]. MutS protein initiates mismatch repair by binding to mismatched base pairs in DNA. MutS protein can also bind to sites between normal and damaged bases such as UV photoproducts [10, 11], methylated bases [12], and base analogues [13, 14].

###xml 782 789 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The results of several reports suggest that MutS specifically binds to O6-methylguanine (O6-MeG):T base pairs and is responsible for reducing mutations caused by those lesions. Additionally, in eukaryotic cells, binding of MutS to damaged DNA appears to trigger a signaling cascade that leads to DNA strand breaks, cell cycle arrest and apoptosis [12, 15-17]. Alkylating agents produce O6-alkylguanine and O4-alkylthymine as major mutagenic DNA lesions. However, it is unclear if O6-ethylguanine in DNA is a target of MutS and thus, the MMR pathway. In this study, we compared the mutagenic activities of methylating agents to that of ethylating agents in a MutS-negative E. coli strain. We also determined if O6-ethylguanine (O6-EtG):T and O6-AlkylG:C base pairs are recognized by E. coli MutS protein using a gel shift assay. The frequency at which alkylating agents induce mutations was elevated in a MutS-deficient strain, whereas for ethylating agents, we observed no difference in the frequency of mutations induced in MutS-deficient and wild-type strains. Consistent with this, we found that O6-EtG:T was recognized by MutS less efficiently than it recognizes O6-MeG:T.

Bacterial strains and mutation assay
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 123 129 123 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">prolac</italic>

###xml 523 525 523 525 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R18">18</xref>
###xml 1114 1115 1108 1109 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 379 386 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The bacterial strains used for detection of mutations were as follows: a wild type strain, NR10832, containing F'CC102 (F' prolac episome); and a mismatch repair deficient strain, NR12896 (mutS201::Tn5, F'CC102). These strains were derived from the parental strain KA796 as described by K. Negishi et al. [14]. The lac allele of F'CC102 reverts only by G.C to A.T transition. An E. coli lacZ reversion assay was preformed to detect mutagenicity of alkylating agents in the wild type or in mismatch repair deficient strain [18]. For mutagenesis, overnight cultures of each strain (0.1 ml) were incubated with the mutagen in 0.1 M sodium phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) for 1 h at 37degreesC. Next, 0.1 ml the treated cultures were spread on lactose agar plates to determine the number of revertants, and adequately diluted samples were also spread on minimal glucose plates to determine the total variable cell number. Mutational frequencies were calculated by dividing the number of revertant colonies by the number of total colony-forming cells on glucose plates. Statistical analysis was performed using the Student's t test.

###begin title 13
Purification of MutS protein
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 3 4 3 4 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sub>
###xml 67 69 67 69 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R20">20</xref>

His6-tagged MutS protein was purified as described by Worth et al [20], using the QIAexpressionist kit (Qiagen). Briefly, M15pREP4 cells transformed with pLW10, a MutS expression plasmid, were grown. Next, production of recombinant MutS protein was induced with IPTG. Cells were then sonicated and the soluble and insoluble fractions were separated. MutS protein was prepared from the soluble fraction using the TALON Metal Affinity Resin (Clontech) according to the manufacturer's protocol. Purity of the His6-tagged MutS protein was checked by SDS-PAGE followed by immuno-blotting analysis and detection via the Anti-His HRP Conjugate Kit (Qiagen). Purified MutS protein was stored at -80degreesC until its use in the Gel-shift assay.

###end title 15
###begin p 16

Each labeled and purified oligonucleotide was incubated with 0 - 520 nM MutS protein for 15 min at 37degreesC in band shift buffer (20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.6; 50 mM KCl; 5 mM MgCl2; 1 mM dithiothreitol; 0.05 mg/ml BSA). ###begin title 18
Mutagenic activity of alkylating agents
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 219 226 219 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 554 566 554 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1A, C</xref>
###xml 749 759 749 759 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1 B</xref>
###xml 871 887 871 887 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">Figure 3 D and E</xref>
###xml 219 226 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
To evaluate the involvement of MMR in protection against mutagenesis induced by DNA methylation and ethylation, the mutagenicity of MNU, MNNG, MMS, ENU, ENNG or EMS was measured in repair proficient- and MutS-deficient E. coli CC102 cells, in which GC to AT reversions can be detected. For both strains, the mutational frequency increased with an increase in the dose of each alkylating agent applied to the cells. In addition, the mutational frequencies for MNU and MMS were significantly elevated in the MutS-deficient strain as compared to wild type (Figure 1A, C). The frequency of mutation induced by MNNG in appeared to be somewhat higher in the MutS (-) strain than in MutS (+) strain but there is not a statistically significant difference (Figure 1 B). Additionally, the mutational frequency by ENU and ENNG were not significantly different between the strains (Figure 3 D and E) but the frequency of mutation by EMS was significantly higher in MutS (-) than that in wild type, similar to what was observed for MMS. Treatment of cells with mutagens at these concentrations did not result in significant levels of cell death.

The difference in the effects of MutS deficiency on the outcomes of mutagenesis induced by methylating or ethylating agents suggests that there may be different affinities of the protein toward base pairs containing methylated versus ethylated DNA. Indeed, we found that MutS protein binds more tightly to O6-MeG:T than to O6-EtG:T pairs. Using a gel shift assay, we examined binding of MutS protein to each of six duplexes, which contained O6-MeG:T, O6-MeG:C, O6-MeG:T, O6-EtG:T, O6-EtG:C, G:T or G:C pair (Table 1). As shown in Figure 2, the band corresponding to MutS-bound to the O6-MeG:T duplex was clearly observable at 75 nM of MutS. However, the oligonucleotide duplex containing O6-EtG:T has a weaker affinity to MutS than the O6-MeG:T duplex. The band corresponding to MutS in a complex with the O6-EtG:T duplex was only seen at MutS concentrations of more than 150 nM. The O6-MeG:C and O6-EtG:C duplexes showed similar affinities to MutS. The percentage of MutS bound to each duplex was determined after gel separation (Table 2; Figure 3). The dissociation constant (Kd) of each complex was obtained by determining the concentration at which half the duplexes were bound to MutS (Table 3). The Kd values followed the order G/T > O6-MeG:T > O6-EtG:T = O6-MeG:C >/= O6-EtG:C >> G:C pairs. This suggests that among base pairs containing alkylated guanines, the O6-MeG:T base pair in DNA can be recognized and repaired efficiently.

###begin title 22
4. Discussion
###end title 22
###begin p 23
###xml 215 216 215 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 216 217 216 217 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 258 259 258 259 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 259 260 259 260 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 323 325 323 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R12">12</xref>
###xml 378 379 378 379 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 379 380 379 380 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 546 547 546 547 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R7">7</xref>
###xml 529 534 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Several lines of evidence suggest that the MMR pathway components are involved in repair of several types of DNA lesions, including those induced by alkylating agents, which result primarily in the mutagenic lesion O6-alkylguanine. Among modified DNA bases, O6-methylguanine has been demonstrated to be recognized by MutS [12]. In contrast, little is known about whether or not O6-ethylguanine is also a substrate for MMR pathway components, although there is a report that the mutagenicity of ENU is higher in an Msh2 deficient mouse cell line [7]. In the present study, we compared the effects of ethylating with methylating agents in a genetic background deficient in MMR.
###end p 23
###begin p 24
###xml 157 164 157 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 444 445 444 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">N</italic>
###xml 468 469 468 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">N</italic>
###xml 511 512 511 512 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 512 513 512 513 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 536 537 536 537 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</sc>
###xml 617 618 617 618 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</sc>
###xml 157 164 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The results suggest that ethylated bases are less readily recognized by the MMR system than methylated bases. The frequency of mutations in a MutS deficient E. coli strain treated with ENU was similar to what was observed for a wild type strain, whereas MNU induced mutation was at a higher frequency in MutS-deficient cells than in wild type. Additionally, MNNG and ENNG showed mutagenic potentials similar to MNU and ENU, respectively. These N-alkyl-nitrosourea and N-alkyl-nitrosoguanidine compounds produce O6-alkylguanine via the Sn1 reaction more efficiently than alkyl-methanesufonates produce it through the Sn2 reaction.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 92 99 92 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 135 136 135 136 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 136 137 136 137 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 160 161 160 161 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 161 162 161 162 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 236 237 236 237 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 237 238 237 238 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 266 267 266 267 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 267 268 267 268 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 520 521 520 521 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 521 522 521 522 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 742 743 742 743 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 743 744 743 744 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 774 775 774 775 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 775 776 775 776 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 92 99 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
Based upon this differential reactivity and the results presented here, we hypothesize that E. coli MutS protein binds more tightly to O6-methylguanine than to O6-ethylguanine, and that this affinity results in more efficient repair of O6-methylguanine than that of O6-ethylguanine. We demonstrated that this is true when the target sequence is CC102, where the GC-to-AT reversion can be readily detected. The mutagenic process by which a GC pair is converted to an AT pair starts with incorporation of thymine opposite O6-methylguanine. In MMR proficient cells, recognition by MutS will result in efficient removal of the thymine by the MMR system, effectively suppressing mutagenesis. In contrast, the relatively inefficient recognition of O6-EtG:T by MutS seems to a the O6-EtG:T base pair to persist, eventually resulting in mutation even in MutS-proficient or MutS-deficient strains. When the modified guanine residue correctly pairs with cytosine, the resultant base pair is poorly recognized by MutS, and thus, the cell avoids repairing correctly paired bases, even in the presence of a methylated or ethylated guanine.

###xml 1907 1908 1899 1900 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 1908 1909 1900 1901 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 252 257 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 306 313 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
###xml 1210 1220 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila</span>
Recently, Yoshioka et al. (15) reported that MutSalpha, which is a heterodimer of mammalian MutS homologues, binds to the O6-MeG:T base pair to the same extent that it binds to G:T but does not bind effectively to O6-MeG:C or G:C base pairs. Thus, the human MutSalpha appears to be more selective than its E. coli homologue, distinguishing between the mutagenic O6-MeG:T and the inconsequential O6-MeG:C. It is thought that accumulation of O6-MeG:T base pairs can lead to cell death or apoptosis, as O6-MeG in template DNA induces reiterative repair attempts via the MMR system, a process termed futile cycling, followed by DNA strand breaks [22]. Yoshioka et al. have also demonstrated that in HeLa cells, the presence of O6-MeG-DNA adducts initiates ATR checkpoint signaling through MMR, which leads to cell cycle arrest and apoptosis [15]. Moreover, Armstrong and Galloway [25] have studied the genotoxicity of methylating and ethylating agents in mammalian cells with and without a functional MMR system. Their data indicate that O6-MeG:T base pairs induce not only mutations but also sister chromatid exchange, whereas O6-EtG:T base pairs do not have a similar effect [23]. Previously, we found that in a Drosophila somatic mutation assay, which primarily detects recombination events, the mutagenicity of the methylating agent N-nitrosodimethylmine (NDMA) is 30 times higher than that of the ethylating agent N-nitrosodiethylamine (NDEA). We also found that the levels of O6-alkyldeoxyguanosine in DNA were similar after cells were exposed to the same dose of NDMA or NDEA. We concluded that a methylated base can induce recombinational events in the chromosome more often than an ethylated base can [26]. This suggests that O6-MeG residues may be more efficient at inducing futile cycling than O6-EtG. These results suggest that eukaryotic MutS homologues also bind O6-MeG:T better than they bind to O6-EtG:T; however, this remains to be addressed by direct experimentation.
###end p 26
###begin p 27
###xml 41 48 41 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 76 77 76 77 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 77 78 77 78 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 132 133 132 133 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">O</italic>
###xml 133 134 133 134 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">6</sup>
###xml 41 48 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
In the present study, we have shown that E. coli MutS protein can recognize O6-MeG:T base pairs more efficiently than it recognizes O6-EtG:T base pairs, and that this can explain the difference in the ability of MNU and ENU to induce mutations in MutS-proficient and deficient strains. However, alkylated DNA lesions are repaired not only via the MMR system but also via other means, such as the excision repair and alkylguanine-DNA-alkyltransferase repair mechanisms. Further investigation will be necessary to help uncover the extent to which MMR is involved in the repair of alkylated DNA lesions.
