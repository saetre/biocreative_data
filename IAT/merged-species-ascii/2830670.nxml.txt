###xml 122 124 119 121 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
Calmodulin-dependent protein kinase kinase-beta activates AMPK without forming a stable complex: synergistic effects of Ca2+ and AMP
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 75 78 75 78 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 185 187 182 184 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 261 264 255 258 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 1031 1033 1008 1010 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 1119 1122 1096 1099 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
Activation of AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase) by phosphorylation at Thr172 is catalysed by at least two distinct upstream kinases, i.e. the tumour suppressor LKB1, and CaMKKbeta (Ca2+/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase kinase-beta). The sequence around Thr172 is highly conserved between the two catalytic subunit isoforms of AMPK and the 12 AMPK-related kinases, and LKB1 has been shown to act upstream of all of them. In the present paper we report that none of the AMPK-related kinases tested could be phosphorylated or activated in intact cells or cell-free assays by CaMKKbeta, although we did observe a slow phosphorylation and activation of BRSK1 (brain-specific kinase 1) by CaMKKalpha. Despite recent reports, we could not find any evidence that the alpha and/or beta subunits of AMPK formed a stable complex with CaMKKbeta. We also showed that increasing AMP concentrations in HeLa cells (which lack LKB1) had no effect on basal AMPK phosphorylation, but enhanced the ability of agents that increase intracellular Ca2+ to activate AMPK. This is consistent with the effect of AMP on phosphorylation of Thr172 being due to inhibition of dephosphorylation, and confirms that the effect of AMP is independent of the upstream kinase utilized.

INTRODUCTION
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 150 151 150 151 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 152 153 152 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 823 824 779 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 825 826 781 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 961 964 917 920 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 1201 1202 1149 1150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1203 1204 1151 1152 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 1249 1250 1197 1198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 1251 1252 1199 1200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 1488 1491 1432 1435 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 1554 1555 1498 1499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 1556 1558 1500 1502 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 1766 1768 1710 1712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 2000 2002 1940 1942 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 968 971 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1182 1187 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase) is an energy-sensing system involved in regulating energy balance at both the cellular and the whole-body levels [1,2]. The kinase occurs as heterotrimeric complexes composed of a catalytic alpha subunit and regulatory beta and gamma subunits, with each subunit existing in mammals as isoforms encoded by multiple genes (alpha1, alpha2; beta1, beta2; gamma1, gamma2, gamma3). Metabolic stresses that inhibit ATP synthesis (e.g. hypoxia, hypoglycaemia) or that stimulate ATP consumption (e.g. muscle contraction) cause an increase in the cellular ADP/ATP ratio, which is amplified by adenylate kinase into an even larger increase in the AMP/ATP ratio. AMP and ATP bind antagonistically to two sites formed by the four tandem CBS (cystathionine beta-synthase) motifs on the gamma subunit [3,4]. The kinase is only active after phosphorylation of a critical threonine residue within the activation loop of the kinase domain (Thr172 in rat alpha1/alpha2) by upstream kinases. The major upstream kinase in most cells was identified to be a complex between the tumour suppressor LKB1 and two accessory subunits, STRAD (Ste20-related adaptor) and MO25 (mouse protein 25) [5,6]. LKB1 appears to be constitutively active [7,8] and may therefore phosphorylate AMPK continually, but under basal conditions the phosphate appears to be immediately removed by protein phosphatases. However, binding of AMP to the AMPK gamma subunits inhibits dephosphorylation of Thr172, an effect that is antagonized by high concentrations of ATP [9-11]. In addition, binding of AMP (but not ATP) triggers a further allosteric activation of the phosphorylated kinase by up to 10-fold, with the combination of these two effects producing >1000-fold activation [11]. Both stimulatory effects appear to occur because AMP binding relieves the inhibitory effects of an auto-inhibitory domain on the alpha subunit, which binds to the kinase domain on the opposite side to the substrate-binding site [12]. This represents a sensitive switch mechanism that produces a large activation of AMPK in response to a small increase in the cellular AMP/ATP ratio.

###xml 368 370 365 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 399 401 389 391 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 486 488 476 478 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 550 552 536 538 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 656 657 642 643 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 693 695 679 681 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 744 746 730 732 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 791 793 777 779 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 852 854 838 840 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 905 907 891 893 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 5 10 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Some human tumour cells (e.g. HeLa cells) do not express LKB1, but AMPK can still be phosphorylated at Thr172 and activated in such cells using Ca2+ ionophores. This led to the discovery that the CaMKKs [CaMK (Ca2+/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase) kinases], especially CaMKKbeta, could act as alternative upstream kinases that can phosphorylate Thr172 on AMPK [13-15]. CaMKKalpha and CaMKKbeta [16] were originally discovered as CaMKs that acted upstream of CaMKI and CaMKIV. The Ca2+-->CaMKK-->AMPK pathway is triggered by a rise in cytosolic Ca2+ without any requirement for an increase in AMP, and is responsible for AMPK activation in response to K+-induced depolarization in neurons [13], muscarinic activation in neuroblastoma cells [17], thrombin activation of endothelial cells [18], treatment of smooth muscle cells with vasoconstrictors [19] and stimulation of antigen receptors in T-cells [20].
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 23 26 23 26 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 79 87 75 83 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 218 220 214 216 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 312 315 308 311 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;/&#8722;</sup>
###xml 492 495 488 491 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 943 944 939 940 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 945 947 941 943 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 204 209 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 316 321 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
The sequence around Thr172 on the AMPK alpha subunits is highly conserved (see Figure 1) in the kinase domain sequences of kinases [termed ARKs (AMPK-related kinases)], that lie on the same branch of the human kinome [21]. By studying phosphorylation in cell-free assays and in cells lacking LKB1, including LKB1-/- mouse embryo fibroblasts and HeLa cells, it was shown that at least 12 of these were also dependent on LKB1 for basal phosphorylation of the threonine residue equivalent to Thr172, and hence for basal activity. These include the brain-specific kinases BRSK1 and BRSK2 (also known as SAD-B and SAD-A), SIK (salt-inducible kinase) 1, SIK2 and SIK3 (also known as SIK, QIK and QSK respectively), NUAK [SNF1 (sucrose-non-fermenting kinase-1)-like kinase] 1 and NUAK2 (also known as ARK5 and SNARK), MARK (microtubule affinity-regulating kinase) 1, MARK2, MARK3 and MARK4, and the testis-specific kinase SNRK (SNF1-related kinase) [7,22].
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 359 361 345 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 459 462 445 448 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 675 676 658 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
In the present study we have addressed three questions regarding the regulation of AMPK and the ARKs by CaMKKs. First, it had not been established whether the ARKs are substrates for CaMKKs, as is AMPK itself. Secondly, it has recently been claimed that CaMKKbeta can form a complex with the alpha and beta subunits of AMPK in the absence of a gamma subunit [23], a hypothesis that we wished to confirm. Thirdly, if the effect of AMP on phosphorylation of Thr172 on AMPK is due to inhibition of dephosphorylation we were puzzled as to why, in cells such as HeLa cells that express CaMKKbeta but lack LKB1, AMPK is not activated by treatments that increase intracellular AMP [5].

###begin title 20
Estimation of the cellular ADP/ATP ratio
###end title 20
###begin p 21
###xml 307 308 307 308 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">g</italic>
###xml 307 308 307 308 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><italic>g</italic></bold>
###xml 445 446 439 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</italic>
Culture medium was quickly aspirated from cells grown on 10 cm diameter culture dishes and cells were washed with 1 ml of ice-cold PBS. After rapid aspiration of PBS, a minimal volume of icecold 5% perchloric acid was added and the samples vortex-mixed to ensure complete lysis. After centrifugation (14000 g for 3 min at 4 degreesC) to remove acid-insoluble material, the supernatant was extracted with two washes of an equal volume of 1:1 tri-n-octylamine and 1,1,2-trichlorotrifluoroethane. The nucleotides remaining in the aqueous phase were then separated by capillary electrophoresis with on-column isotachophoretic concentration, using run buffers consisting of 50 mM sodium phosphate and 50 mM NaCl (pH 5.2; leading buffer) and 100 mM Mes/Tris (pH 5.2; tailing buffer). To each buffer was added 0.2% hydroxyethylcellulose to decrease electro-osmotic flow. Nucleotide peaks were detected by UV absorbance at 260 nM, and integrated using System Gold software (Beckman). Nucleotide ratios were calculated from peak areas after correction for retention times. Identification of peaks as ATP, ADP and AMP were confirmed by additional runs spiked with internal standards and analysis of absorbance spectra of individual peaks.

Alignment of sequences around activation loop phosphorylation sites
###end title 27
###begin p 28
###xml 0 8 0 8 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 288 291 273 276 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 811 813 792 794 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
###xml 816 824 797 805 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
Figure 1 shows an alignment of the activation loops (lying between the conserved DFG and APE motifs) of the ARKs, as well as the four isoforms of CaMKI (CaMKI-alpha, -beta, -delta and -gamma) and the single form of CaMKIV. All contain a threonine residue in the position equivalent to Thr172 in AMPK-alpha2, and a conserved hydrophobic residue (usually leucine) two residues N-terminal to this. Many of the other residues conserved between these sequences are also found in other protein kinases not downstream of LKB1 and/or CaMKKs, such as the DFG motif prior to the threonine, and the CG and YAAPE motifs following it. Using a peptide library approach, it has been shown that LKB1 only phosphorylates threonine residues, and strongly prefers a leucine two residues N-terminal to the threonine (i.e. at P-2) [33]. Figure 1 shows that leucine is present at P-2 in all ARKs and in CaMK1-alpha and -beta, although in CaMK1-delta, CaMKI-gamma and CaMKIV, the residue at P-2 is methionine. The features of primary sequence recognized by CaMKKalpha and CaMKKbeta, if any, are not known.

As an initial test of whether CaMKKs would activate any of the ARKs we utilized HeLa cells, which express both CaMKKalpha and CaMKKbeta [13], but do not express LKB1 due to a biallelic deletion of the gene in the cervical cancer from which the cells were originally derived in 1951 [34]. We have shown previously that the Ca2+ ionophore A23187 caused activation of endogenous AMPK in HeLa cells, and that this effect was blocked by the CaMKK inhibitor, STO-609 [13]. Activation of AMPK (Figure 2A), and phosphorylation of Thr172 (results not shown), occurred in T-Rextrade mark HeLa cells not only in response to A23187, but also in response to stable expression of wild-type active LKB1. These results show that the CaMKKs do not activate any of the endogenous ARKs that are expressed in HeLa cells.

Anderson et al. [23] have recently reported evidence suggesting that CaMKKbeta can form a stable complex with the alpha and beta subunits of AMPK in the absence of a gamma subunit. To test this hypothesis, we initially studied whether overexpressed CaMKKbeta would co-precipitate with endogenous AMPK from extracts of HEK-293 cells. Figure 5(A) shows that, although CaMKKbeta was readily detectable by Western blot analysis in the crude cell lysates, none was detected by Western blot analysis in immunoprecipitates made using anti-AMPK-alpha1/alpha2 antibodies (although we could readily detect the alpha, beta and gamma subunits of AMPK). [23] claimed that the interaction between CaMKKbeta and AMPK was Ca2+-dependent, but the same results were obtained regardless of whether EDTA and EGTA were present in the lysis and immunoprecipitation buffers. ###xml 849 852 843 846 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 994 996 988 990 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 1078 1079 1072 1073 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1198 1201 1192 1195 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 1312 1314 1302 1304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1424 1432 1413 1421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 1480 1488 1469 1477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">Figure 6</xref>
###xml 1501 1509 1490 1498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">Figure 6</xref>
Although it had been proposed that AMP promoted phosphorylation of AMPK by LKB1 [5], previous studies suggest that this may have been caused by trace contamination of the kinase preparations used with protein phosphatase, and that the effect of AMP on Thr172 phosphorylation is entirely via inhibition of dephosphorylation [10]. This raised the question as to why drugs such as AICAR (5-amino-4-imidazolecarboxamide riboside), which is converted into the AMP mimetic ZMP [36], or phenformin, which increases cellular AMP [5], do not activate AMPK in HeLa cells [5], even though CaMKKbeta is expressed and is capable of phosphorylating Thr172 in those cells [13]. One explanation was that the activity of CaMKKbeta in cells with basal Ca2+ is so low that the endogenous protein phosphatase activity is sufficient to cause net dephosphorylation of Thr172, even though dephosphorylation is being inhibited by AMP. To test this idea, we incubated HeLa cells with increasing concentrations of the Ca2+ ionophore A23187 in the presence and absence of phenformin. As found previously [5], in the absence of A23187, phenformin did not give a significant stimulation of AMPK activity, phosphorylation of Thr172 on the AMPK alpha subunits, or phosphorylation of the AMPK site on ACC (acetyl-CoA carboxylase). As expected [13], in the absence of phenformin, concentrations of A23187 up to 10 muM increasingly stimulated AMPK activity (Figure 6A), as well as phosphorylation of AMPK and ACC (Figure 6B). However, Figure 6 reveals the new result that the presence of phenformin sensitized the cells to A23187. Thus the presence of phenformin shifted the curve of AMPK phosphorylation and activation against A23187 concentration to the left. A stimulation of the activation of AMPK, and the phosphorylation of AMPK and ACC by phenformin was particularly evident at 3 muM A23187. These results show that, under appropriate conditions, phenformin can activate AMPK even in cells lacking LKB1.

We repeated the experiments using 3 muM A23187 in the presence and absence of phenformin or 2-deoxyglucose, another agent that can deplete cellular ATP, and measured AMPK activity, phosphorylation of ACC and AMPK, and levels of cellular nucleotides (Figure 7). The level of AMP in HeLa cells under basal conditions was too low to measure accurately, although it could be detected in cells treated with phenformin and A23187 (results not shown). We therefore used ADP/ATP as a surrogate for AMP/ATP, since the latter is expected to vary as the square of ADP/ATP [37]. Phenformin and 2-deoxyglucose did not cause phosphorylation of ACC or AMPK, or activation of AMPK, in HeLa cells that had not been treated with a Ca2+ ionophore, despite the fact that 2-deoxyglucose produced a large rise in ADP/ATP ratio (any small effect of phenformin on the ADP/ATP ratio was not significant). However, in cells treated with 3 muM A23187, both phenformin and 2-deoxyglucose significantly stimulated AMPK activity, and increased phosphorylation of ACC and AMPK, over and above the effect of A23187 alone, with the effect of phenformin being larger than that of 2-deoxyglucose (Figures 7A and 7B). The increase in ADP/ATP induced by 2-deoxyglucose was not affected by A23187 but, surprisingly, a much larger increase in ADP/ATP occurred in the presence of A23187 in cells treated with phenformin (Figure 7C). Despite the contrasting effects of phenformin and 2-deoxyglucose on cellular ADP/ATP ratios, the results in Figure 7 confirm that, under appropriate conditions, both agents are capable of activating AMPK, even in cells completely lacking LKB1.

###begin title 55
DISCUSSION
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 61 64 61 64 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 123 131 123 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 233 242 233 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">Figures 2</xref>
###xml 247 248 247 248 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 274 282 274 282 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 533 535 523 525 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 560 562 550 552 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 896 904 876 884 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">Figure 3</xref>
Despite the close similarity in the sequences surrounding Thr172 on AMPK and the equivalent threonine residue on the ARKs (Figure 1), it is clear that the latter are not phosphorylated significantly by CaMKKs either in intact cells (Figures 2 and 3) or in cell-free assays (Figure 4). We have shown previously that HeLa cells lack LKB1 but express both CaMKKalpha and CaMKKbeta, although it was only when CaMKKbeta was knocked down using siRNA (small interfering RNA) that the phosphorylation and activation of AMPK in response to Ca2+ ionophores was reduced [13]. Thus it seems that neither CaMKKalpha nor CaMKKbeta are capable of activating any of the ARKs that are expressed in HeLa cells, i.e. SIK1 (SIK), SIK2 (QIK), SIK3 (QSK), NUAK2 (SNARK) or any of the MARKs. Similarly, overexpression of CaMKKbeta caused activation of AMPK in HeLa cells, but failed to activate any of the ARKs tested (Figure 3).
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 114 116 114 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 361 364 353 356 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">189</sup>
###xml 383 386 375 378 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 550 558 524 532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 925 933 888 896 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F4">Figure 4</xref>
###xml 1299 1301 1247 1249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>
###xml 1478 1485 1419 1426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 1536 1538 1477 1479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 1685 1688 1626 1629 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Cre</italic>
###xml 1710 1714 1651 1655 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Emx1</italic>
###xml 1841 1844 1782 1785 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 1896 1899 1837 1840 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 2125 2127 2059 2061 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 2242 2244 2173 2175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>
###xml 163 166 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 1618 1622 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1669 1673 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 2290 2295 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
BRSK1 (SAD-B) and BRSK2 (SAD-A) are brain-specific kinases that are not expressed in HeLa cells. Fujimoto et al. [38] recently reported a search for proteins from rat brain extracts that interacted with the kinase domain of CaMKKalpha, and detected both the alpha1 subunit of AMPK and BRSK1 (SAD-B). They also reported that BRSK1 could be phosphorylated (at Thr189, equivalent to Thr172 on AMPK-alpha1) and activated by both CaMKKalpha and CaMKKbeta, although the rate of phosphorylation was much lower with CaMKKbeta than CaMKKalpha. The results in Figure 4 confirm that CaMKKalpha is capable of phosphorylating and activating BRSK1 in cell-free assays. However, in the present study neither BRSK1 nor BRSK2 was significantly phosphorylated by CaMKKbeta, although the concentration used was sufficient to fully activate AMPK. It should also be pointed out that the concentration of CaMKKalpha used to phosphorylate BRSK1 in Figure 4 was approx. 20-fold higher than that required to obtain a comparable level of phosphorylation of AMPK-alpha1 (results not shown). In addition, AMPK is itself a relatively poor substrate for CaMKKalpha, with CaMKKalpha being approx. 7-fold less active against AMPK than CaMKKbeta when the activities of the two CaMKKs were normalized using phosphorylation of CaMKI [13]. Thus BRSK1 appears to be a poor substrate for CaMKKalpha and an even poorer subtrate for CaMKKbeta, and we propose that LKB1 is the major upstream kinase for BRSK1 and BRSK2 in vivo. In support of this are results of Barnes et al. [39], who knocked-out LKB1 in pyramidal neurons of the cerebral cortex by crossing mice harbouring a conditional 'floxed' allele with mice expressing Cre recombinase from the Emx1 promoter. This almost abolished phosphorylation of BRSK2 (SAD-A) and BRSK1 (SAD-B) at the threonine residues equivalent to Thr172, while not affecting phosphorylation of AMPK at Thr172. This finding can be explained if CaMKKbeta (or CaMKKalpha) provides an alternative upstream pathway for activation of AMPK, but not BRSK1 and BRSK2, in the cerebral cortex. Also in agreement with our results, Bright et al. [40] reported that neither BRSK1 nor BRSK2 was activated in CCL13 cells overexpressing CaMKKbeta, while Uboha et al. [41] reported that CaMKK partially purified from mouse cortex (isoform not specified) failed to phosphorylate MARK2 in cell-free assays.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 573 575 562 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 674 676 659 661 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B42">42</xref>
###xml 677 679 662 664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
Given that the sequences around the threonine residue in the activation loop are highly conserved between AMPK and the ARKs, it remains unclear why the latter should be phosphorylated and activated by LKB1 complexes, but not by CaMKK-alpha or -beta. Our findings suggest that some feature of the structure of the ARKs, other than the primary sequence of the activation loop, may be required to determine this specificity. This is supported by previous studies showing that a peptide derived from CaMKIV was a very poor substrate for CaMKKalpha compared with CaMKIV itself [42], and that thermal denaturation of CaMKI or CaMKIV abolished their phosphorylation by CaMKKalpha [42,43]. Thus the CaMKKs appear to recognize determinants on CaMKI and CaMKIV other than just the primary sequence of the activation loop.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 182 184 175 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 395 396 388 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 518 520 511 513 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 823 825 816 818 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>
###xml 931 933 924 926 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B46">46</xref>
###xml 1139 1141 1132 1134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 796 800 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rats</span>
###xml 914 917 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
###xml 942 947 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Our findings have important implications regarding the regulation of the ARKs. None of them appears to be interacting with the regulatory beta or gamma subunits of the AMPK complex [44] and, consistent with this, those that are expressed in skeletal muscle (SIK2, SIK3, MARK2/3 and MARK4) are not phosphorylated or activated when AMPK is activated in muscle by contraction, AICAR or phenformin [8]. The results of the present study show that the ARKs, unlike AMPK itself, are not regulated by increases in cytosolic Ca2+ via activation of CaMKK. That being the case, what are the upstream signals that regulate the ARKs? In most cases this remains unclear, although there is some information available in the case of SIK1. SIK1 was discovered as a protein kinase induced in the adrenal glands of rats fed a high-salt diet [45], and is also induced at the transcriptional level by glucagon and forskolin in primary rat hepatocytes [46]. In Y1 mouse adrenocortical tumour cells treated with adrenocorticotrophic hormone, SIK1 is phosphorylated at a C-terminal site by PKA (protein kinase A), resulting in its translocation to the cytoplasm [47]. Thus SIK1 may be regulated by altered transcription and/or by altered subcellular localization rather than by altered phosphorylation by LKB1.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 17 19 17 19 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 406 409 389 392 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 424 426 407 409 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">79</sup>
###xml 494 496 477 479 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 579 581 562 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 1174 1182 1115 1123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 1257 1259 1198 1200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 1428 1436 1347 1355 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 1513 1515 1432 1434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
Anderson et al. [23] recently provided evidence suggesting that CaMKKbeta (also known as CaMKK2) can form a stable complex with the AMPK alpha and beta subunits. They expressed FLAG-tagged CaMKKbeta in HEK-293 cells and, using anti-FLAG antibodies, showed co-precipitation not only of calmodulin, but also of AMPK alpha subunits and ACC, the latter two detected using phosphospecific antibodies against Thr172 (AMPK) and Ser79 (ACC). These co-precipitation events appeared to be dependent on Ca2+ because they were not observed in the presence of EDTA and EGTA. Anderson et al. [23] also detected in the anti-FLAG immunoprecipitates the alpha1, alpha2, beta1 and beta2 subunits of AMPK, but not gamma1, although they could detect the latter in cell extracts. They therefore proposed that CaMKKbeta could replace the gamma subunit within the AMPK complex. The results of the present study do not support this concept. When CaMKKbeta was overexpressed in HEK-293 cells, we could not detect any AMPK-alpha, -beta or -gamma subunits in an immunoprecipitate made using anti-CaMKKbeta antibodies, either with or without EDTA and EGTA in the lysis and immunoprecipitation buffers (Figure 5B). In the converse experiment (which was not reported by Anderson et al. [23]) we found that no CaMKKbeta was detected in an anti-AMPK-alpha1/alpha2 immunoprecipitate, although we could readily detect the alpha, beta and gamma subunits of AMPK (Figure 5A). This suggests to us that the AMPK subunits recovered by Anderson et al. [23] in their anti-FLAG immunoprecipitates may have been an artefact caused by the high level of FLAG-CaMKKbeta expression in the cells.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 183 185 172 174 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>
###xml 411 412 389 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 775 783 729 737 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">Figure 5</xref>
###xml 1105 1113 1031 1039 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F5">Figure 5</xref>
We also addressed the same question in a different way, making use of previous findings that the alpha subunits of AMPK are unstable unless co-expressed with beta and gamma subunits [35]. This phenomenon can be explained by recent structural studies showing that the C-terminal region of the beta subunit forms extensive interactions with the C-terminal domain of alpha and with the N-terminal region of gamma [4]. If these subunits do not find their interaction partners, they may be recognized as misfolded proteins and become degraded. We argued that, if the AMPK alpha and beta subunits can form a complex with CaMKKbeta in the absence of the gamma subunit, then co-expression with CaMKKbeta should stabilize the alpha subunit in the presence of a beta subunit. However, Figure 5(C) shows that this is not the case: in the presence of beta1 the gamma1 subunit stabilizes the alpha1 subunit, but CaMKKbeta does not substitute for gamma1 in this respect. Rather than stabilizing the AMPK alpha and beta subunits, overexpression of CaMKKbeta decreased the level of expression of all three AMPK subunits (Figure 5C), perhaps because the transfected DNA was competing for the available biosynthetic machinery.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 30 39 30 39 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">Figures 6</xref>
###xml 44 45 44 45 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>
###xml 235 237 232 234 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 350 351 347 348 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 571 574 568 571 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">172</sup>
###xml 633 635 630 632 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 812 814 803 805 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 949 958 939 948 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F6">Figures 6</xref>
###xml 963 964 953 954 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>
###xml 1010 1012 1000 1002 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
Finally, the results shown in Figures 6 and 7 show that the two known mechanisms by which phosphorylation of AMPK is activated, i.e. inhibition of dephosphorylation by AMP and increased phosphorylation via activation of CaMKKbeta by Ca2+ can, under the right circumstances, be synergistic. They also provide an explanation for our previous findings [5] that agents that cause increases in AMP (e.g. phenformin) or that are converted into an AMP mimetic (AICAR) do not increase phosphorylation and activation of AMPK in HeLa cells. The basal rate of phosphorylation of Thr172 in these cells, which lack LKB1 and would only have the Ca2+-independent activity of CaMKKbeta, appears to be too low for any effect of increased AMP on dephosphorylation to become evident. However, if CaMKKbeta is activated using the Ca2+ ionophore A23187 at sub-optimal concentration (3 muM), the effect of a rise in AMP to inhibit dephosphorylation now becomes apparent (Figures 6 and 7). In agreement with our findings, Yu et al. [48] have recently reported that A23187 and AICAR act in an additive manner to inhibit cell proliferation and/or survival in HeLa cells.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 39 47 39 47 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 470 472 469 471 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 473 475 472 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
###xml 568 570 567 569 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 616 618 615 617 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 679 681 678 680 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 1297 1305 1296 1304 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 1357 1359 1356 1358 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 1475 1483 1474 1482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F7">Figure 7</xref>
###xml 1621 1623 1620 1622 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
A somewhat unexpected finding shown in Figure 7 was that, although treatment with 3 muM A23187 alone did not significantly increase the ADP/ATP ratio, when combined with 10 mM phenformin it caused a much larger increase in this ratio than that caused by phenformin alone. By contrast, 2-deoxyglucose treatment increased ADP/ATP, but there was no additional effect when it was added together with A23187. Phenformin is an inhibitor of complex I of the respiratory chain [49,50], thus potentially causing inhibition of mitochondrial ATP synthesis, whereas A23187 is a Ca2+ ionophore that causes release of cytosolic Ca2+, potentially increasing ATP turnover due to activation of Ca2+ pumps. Although 2-deoxyglucose is often described as an inhibitor of glycolysis, its major effect may be to accelerate ATP consumption, because it is rapidly phosphorylated within the cell to 2-deoxyglucose 6-phosphate. We propose that a combination of treatments that both inhibit ATP synthesis and accelerate ATP turnover (phenformin and A23187), as opposed to two different treatments that accelerate ATP turnover (2-deoxyglucose and A23187) may make it more difficult for the cells to maintain their energy status. The very large increase in AMPK activity in response to addition of both A23187 and phenformin (Figure 7A) could be explained by an increase in cytosolic Ca2+ coupled with a larger increase in AMP than that caused by 2-deoxyglucose. Whatever the explanation, the results in Figure 7 show that treatments which elevate cellular AMP (phenformin and 2-deoxyglucose) can, under the right circumstances (elevated cytosolic Ca2+), cause increased phosphorylation and activation of AMPK even in cells lacking LKB1. Thus the effect of AMP on phosphorylation of AMPK is independent of the upstream kinase utilized.
###end p 63
###begin p 64
###xml 22 24 22 24 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 209 211 209 211 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
Increased cytosolic Ca2+ tends to switch on many processes that consume ATP, such as operation of ion pumps or channels, interaction of motor proteins, and membrane trafficking and secretion. The ability of Ca2+ and AMP to additively activate AMPK to restore cellular energy balance may therefore have physiological relevance.
###end p 64
