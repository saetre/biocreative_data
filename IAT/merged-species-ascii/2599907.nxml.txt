###xml 863 867 860 864 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB2</underline></italic>
###xml 332 336 <span type="species:ncbi:4456">apes</span>
###xml 420 425 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 430 440 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 515 520 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
CGB1 and CGB2 have sequence homology with the other LHB/CGB genes but encode a novel hypothetical protein. We explore the evolution, variation and putative regulatory regions of these genes with the aim to gain indirect evidence for their functionality. The origin of CGB1/2 is suggested among the common ancestors of African great apes. In comparison to LHB, CGB1/2 genes exhibit the same level of conservation between human and chimpanzee as well as three times lower diversity and no ORF disrupting mutations in human genes. Consistently, the 5'-upstream region of CGB1/2 contains almost full promoter sequence of hCGbeta. Additionally, CGB1/2 possess a novel putative proximal promoter segment created by the CGB1/2-specific insertion. In silico prediction of putative transcription factor binding sites supports the hypothesis of the involvement of CGB1 and CGB2 gene products in implantation and placental development.

1. Introduction
###end title 2
###begin p 3

###xml 0 5 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 471 476 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Human luteinizing hormone/chorionic gonadotropin beta (LHB/CGB) gene cluster, localized on chromosome 19q13.3 consists of seven highly homologous genes (85-99% DNA sequence identity): one LHB gene and six CGB genes that originate from an ancestral LHB gene as a result of duplication during primate evolution (Fiddes and Goodman, 1980; Talmadge et al., 1984a; Maston and Ruvalo, 2002; Fig.1A). Four of the genes (CGB, CGB5, CGB7 and CGB8) code for the of beta subunit of human chorionic gonadotropin (163 amino acid protein), one of the first proteins synthesized by conceptus.
###end p 3

###xml 512 518 506 512 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Fig.1B</xref>
###xml 543 552 537 546 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1/CGB2</underline>
###xml 543 552 537 546 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1/CGB2</underline></italic>
Despite high sequence similarity with the other genes in the cluster, CGB1 and CGB2 encode a novel hypothetical protein (132 amino acid in length) that is homological to neither the functional hCG beta-subunit nor to any other known protein (Bo and Boime, 1992). This change has been caused by an inserted DNA fragment (736 bp for CGB1, 724 bp for CGB2) that replaces 52 bp of the proximal end of promoter area and the whole 5'UTR of the hCGbeta-subunit coding genes (Bo and Boime, 1992; Hollenberg et al. 1994; Fig.1B). Instead, it creates a CGB1/CGB2 specific putative promoter fragment, an alternative 5'untranslated region (5'UTR) and a novel exon 1, leading to one basepair shifted open reading frame (ORF) for exons 2 and 3.

Although the appropriate protein has not been isolated, mRNA of CGB1 and CGB2 has been detected in the placenta (Bo and Boime, 1992; Rull and Laan, 2005) as well in the testis (Berger et al., 1994), pituitary (Dirnhofer et al., 1996), and breast cancer tissue (Giovangrandi et al., 2001) predicting the functionality of these genes. Interestingly, in transgenic mice carrying a 36-kb cosmid insert containing all the six CGB genes, the CGB1 and CGB2 transcripts were also observed in brain at levels comparable with placenta, the expression site for all the CGB genes (Strauss et al., 1994).

We explore the evolution and genomic context of CGB1 and CGB2 genes and present indirect evidence for their functionality. As a reference we use LHB gene, the ancestral member of the gene cluster with conservative function in vertebrate reproduction. We explore the following questions: (i) What is the origin of CGB1 and CGB2? (ii) How conserved are CGB1 and CGB2 among species? (iii) Does the variation pattern of human CGB1 and CGB2 support the functionality? (iv) Could the upstream region of human CGB1/2 function as a putative promoter and possibly predict the functional importance of these genes?

###xml 462 467 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Sequence diversity parameters were calculated by DnaSP software (version 4.0) (Rozas and Rozas, 1999). The direct estimate of per-site heterozygosity (pi) was derived from the average pairwise sequence difference, and Watterson's theta (Watterson, 1975) represents as an estimate of the expected per-site heterozygosity based on the number of segregating sites (S). Tajima's D (DT) statistic (Tajima, 1986) was performed to determine if the observed patterns of human CGB1, CGB2 and a reference LHB gene diversity are consistent with the standard neutral model. The basis of DT value is the difference between the pi and theta estimates: under neutral expectation pi=theta and DT =0. Significant positive DT values indicate an excess of intermediate-frequency alleles in a population consistent with either balancing selection or population bottleneck, whereas significant negative DT values indicate an excess of rare SNPs consistent with either recent directional selection or an increase in population size.
###end p 17

A simple neutral model (Kimura, 1983) predicts that drift and mutation rate determine the level of nucleotide variation accumulating within and between species. Therefore, the relative amount of within-species polymorphism should reflect the amount of between-species fixation under neutrality. Genetic diversity of human CGB1 and CGB2 was compared with fixation between human and chimpanzee, as well as human and gorilla sequences to test neutrality. 2.4. In silico prediction of TFBS to human CGB1 and CGB2 5' upstream region
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 100 123 100 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R6">Cartharius et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 144 155 144 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R16">Grabe, 2002</xref>
###xml 283 301 283 301 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R29">Matys et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 521 533 521 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R43">Stormo, 2000</xref>
Prediction of transcription factor binding sites (TFBS) was performed using the MatInspector 2.2 (; Cartharius et al., 2005) and Alibaba 2.1 (; Grabe, 2002) programs. Both approaches rely on the information about the experimentally defined TFBS collected in the TRANSFAC database (; Matys et al., 2003). MatInspector identifies TFBS in nucleotide sequences using a large library of position weight matrices (PWM). PWM is a common way to represent the degenerate sequence preferences of a DNA-binding protein (reviewed by Stormo, 2000). Briefly, the elements of PWM correspond to scores reflecting the likelihood that particular nucleotide at the particular position can be observed as the known or candidate TFBS. A weight matrix pattern definition is superior to a simple IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) consensus sequence as it represents the complete nucleotide distribution of each single position. It also allows the quantification of the similarity between the weight matrix and a potential TFBS detected within the sequence. Alibaba program starts directly at the known binding sites instead of using predefined matrices in the database. The analysis is a process consisting of three steps: (1) it pairwise aligns of known sites to the unknown sequence; (2) it forms small sets of sites by their position and their according class of factor; (3) it constructs matrices from these sets. We run the Alibaba 2.1 under the following conditions: Pairsim to known sites 64, matrix width 10 bp, minimum number of sites 4-5, minimum matrix conservation 75%, similarity to sequence matrix 1%, factor class level 4-5.
###end p 20

First, we addressed the question of conservation of CGB1 and CGB2 genes among the species. Human-specific primers were used to amplify a unique gene product of CGB1 for chimpanzee (primers CGB1_2F and CGB1_6R, predicted length based on human sequence 2312 bp; Genbank Accession no. DQ238547) and of CGB2 for gorilla (CGB2_1F and CGB2_3R, 1812 bp; DQ238550). Chimpanzee CGB2 was inferred from the jointly amplified CGB1/2 products (primers CGB2_1F and CGB2_5R, 2269 bp; DQ238549) using the chimpanzee CGB1 sequence as a reference. With a similar approach, the gorilla CGB2 was used as a reference to derive CGB1 from a common CGB1/2 product amplified from gorilla DNA (primers CGB1_1F and CGB1_2R, 1600 bp; DQ238548). None of the human-specific CGB1 and CGB2 primer combinations were capable to amplify the expected products from orangutan genomic DNA. Therefore, either the orangutan CGB1 and CGB2 sequences are highly divergent from other studied primates; or this species lacks CGB1/2 insertion region (target of one of the primers), and consequently CGB1/2 genes. The latter scenario is also supported by a recent study suggesting the total copy number of orangutan CGB genes to be four (Maston and Ruvolo, 2002). Consequently, we raise the hypothesis of the origin of CGB1 and CGB2 in the common ancestor of African great apes.

We performed two alternative analyses to test whether the human LHB, CGB1 and CGB2 have evolved under standard neutral model. Tajima's test examines whether the average number of pairwise nucleotide differences between sequences (pi) is larger than expected from the observed number of polymorphic sites (theta). The expected difference (Tajima D) between pi and theta is roughly zero under the standard neutral model. As differences between pi and theta for the studied genes were small and Tajima D values were close to zero (Table 3), the hypothesis of neutral evolution of these genes was not rejected. The HKA test was performed to test the neutral evolution of CGB1 and CGB2 among the studied species with LHB as a reference (Table 4). The test is based on prediction from the Neutral Theory of Molecular Evolution (Kimura, 1983) that the amount of within-species diversity should be correlated with levels of between-species divergence, due to the dependence of both on the neutral mutation rate. Consistently with Tajima's test, we could not reject the null hypothesis of neutral evolution. The exception was the Estonian sample set, where the significant result could be spurious outcome of the population history in Europe shaped by bottlenecks (Barbujani and Goldstein, 2004) and thus capturing the least the human intrapopulation variation applied in HKA test. However, as neither Tajima's nor HKA test takes into account gene conversion shown to shape the sequences and variation of LHB/CGB genes within a species (Maston and Ruvolo, 2002; Hallast et al., 2005), we should interpret the overall test results with caution and could not entirely exclude selection. Innan (2003) has shown that statistical tests of neutrality based on the standard coalescent theory for a single-copy gene may not be appropriate for duplicated genes.

###xml 1269 1293 1254 1278 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R20">Hollenberg et al. (1994)</xref>
###xml 1346 1350 1331 1332 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</underline>
###xml 1346 1350 1331 1332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>&#946;</underline></italic>
###xml 1427 1431 1409 1410 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</underline>
###xml 1427 1431 1409 1410 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>&#946;</underline></italic>
###xml 1441 1447 1420 1426 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1/2</underline>
###xml 1441 1447 1420 1426 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1/2</underline></italic>
###xml 1527 1533 1506 1512 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1/2</underline>
###xml 1527 1533 1506 1512 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1/2</underline></italic>
###xml 1621 1627 1600 1606 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1/2</underline>
###xml 1621 1627 1600 1606 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1/2</underline></italic>
In order to predict the regulatory elements and patterns putatively involved in driving the expression of CGB1 and CGB2, we investigated the upstream regions of these genes. Alignment of the experimentally identified hCGbeta promoter (-311 bp from hCGbeta 5'-CAP; Otani et al., 1988; Hollenberg et al., 1994) with the 5'-upstream region of CGB1 and CGB2 genes revealed a more proximal location of an almost complete hCGbeta promoter sequence (-757 to -481 for CGB1 and -745 to -469 for CGB2 from predicted transcription start site), lacking only 52 bp of proximal promoter segment of hCGbeta (Fig. 1B,C). Despite the absence of the minimal promoter region (MPR; -37 to +104; Hollenberg et al., 1994) including 2 Ets-2 binding sites (Ghosh et al., 2003), the other sequence motifs playing a crucial role in regulating hCGbeta expression are conserved among the genes (Fig. 1C). These include cAMP-dependent transcription element mapped to -311 to -202 (Albanese et al., 1991), trophoblast-specific element (TSE) between -305 and -279 maintaining basal expression (Steger et al., 1993), as well as binding sites for AP-2 and Sp1 transcription factors required for the full activity of the promoter (Johnson et al., 1997; Johnson and Jameson, 1999; Knofler et al., 2004). Hollenberg et al. (1994) has suggested that the individual domains of the hCGbeta promoter act in an addictive or combinatory manner. Thus, the absence of hCGbeta MPR from CGB1/2 putative promoter region could possibly be compensated by the sequences within CGB1/2-specific insertion. However, whether this segment indeed has a regulatory function for CGB1/2 needs to be proven in wet-lab experiments.
###end p 34
###begin title 35
###xml 5 9 5 9 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1</underline>
###xml 15 19 15 19 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB2</underline>
3.5. CGB1- and CGB2-specific upstream region is predicted to harbor binding sites for transcription factors related to early placental development and implantation
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 48 52 48 52 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1</underline>
###xml 48 52 48 52 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1</underline></italic>
###xml 57 61 57 61 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB2</underline>
###xml 57 61 57 61 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB2</underline></italic>
###xml 73 79 73 79 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1/2</underline>
###xml 73 79 73 79 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1/2</underline></italic>
###xml 252 259 252 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Fig. 1B</xref>
###xml 335 344 335 344 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">In silico</italic>
###xml 395 401 395 401 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1/2</underline>
###xml 395 401 395 401 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1/2</underline></italic>
###xml 479 486 479 486 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Fig. 1D</xref>
###xml 707 727 700 720 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R36">Pestell et al., 1994</xref>
###xml 729 749 722 742 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R23">Johnson et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 829 848 818 837 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R42">Steger et al., 1994</xref>
In addition to alternative 5'UTR and exon 1 for CGB1 and CGB2 genes, the CGB1/2-specific insert (736 and 724 bp, respectively) provides a novel putative proximal promoter segment (481 bp and 469 bp, respectively) upstream the transcription start site (Fig. 1B). We evaluated this fragment as a potential additional promoter component. In silico analysis predicted several regulatory elements in CGB1/2 insert experimentally determined to be essential for gonadotrope expression (Fig. 1D): two copies of CRE sites binding cAMP-responsive element binding protein (CREB) and activating transcription factor (ATF), binding sites for CGalpha and CGbeta transcription inducer AP-2 as well as for repressor c-Jun (Pestell et al., 1994; Johnson et al., 1997), binding sites for GATA-2 regulating CG alpha-subunit expression in placenta (Steger et al., 1994).
###end p 36
###begin p 37
###xml 19 25 19 25 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1/2</underline>
###xml 19 25 19 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1/2</underline></italic>
###xml 177 184 177 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Fig. 1D</xref>
###xml 468 485 457 474 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R8">Chen et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 487 506 476 495 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R30">Muggia et al., 1999</xref>
###xml 525 542 514 531 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R34">Page et al., 2002</xref>
###xml 555 576 544 565 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R31">Nakamura et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 518 523 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 548 553 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Interestingly, the CGB1/2 specific upstream region is predicted to harbor interaction sites for several transcription factors regulating implantation and placental development (Fig. 1D): NF-kappaB, Cdx-2, ERR-beta, HIF1 and SF-1. Although NF-kappaB is a transcriptional factor involved mainly in inflammatory and immune responses, it regulates also several genes responsible for immunological adaptation at the feto-maternal interface and early embryonic development (Chen et al., 1999, Muggia et al., 1999). Both, in human (Page et al., 2002) and mouse (Nakamura et al., 2004) NF-kappaB is activated in the pregnant uterus during preimplantation period and is highly expressed during the implantation window.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 233 262 230 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R7">Chawengsaksophak et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 309 313 306 310 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Cdx2</underline>
###xml 309 313 306 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>Cdx2</underline></italic>
###xml 409 426 406 423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R18">Hall et al., 2005</xref>
###xml 446 465 443 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R17">Gruber et al., 2004</xref>
###xml 666 689 660 683 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R37">Pettersson et al., 1996</xref>
###xml 910 916 901 907 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Estrrb</underline>
###xml 910 916 901 907 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>Estrrb</underline></italic>
###xml 999 1015 990 1006 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R27">Luo et al., 1997</xref>
###xml 302 308 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">bovine</span>
###xml 703 707 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 856 861 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Cdx-2 and ERR-beta exhibit highly specific expression pattern during embryogenesis. Besides its main role in driving embryonic axial elongation and anterior-posterior patterning, Cdx2 is also essential for trophoblastic development (Chawengsaksophak et al., 2004). Consistently, aberrant expression of bovine Cdx2 in the preimplantation cloned embryo has been reported to lead to the failure of implantation (Hall et al., 2005). ERE (reviewed by Gruber et al., 2004) is a binding site (consensus sequence 5'-GGTCAnnnTGACC-3') not only for the estrogen receptor ligand complex, but also for ERR-beta, an orphan member of the superfamily of nuclear hormone receptors (Pettersson et al., 1996). Studies on mice have shown that ERR-beta is expressed during embryogenesis by ectodermally derived regions of the amniotic fold, forming chorion. Homozygous mutant mouse embryos generated by targeted disruption of the Estrrb gene have severely impaired placental development, and die 10.5 days post-coitum (Luo et al., 1997).
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 142 162 142 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R11">Daikoku et al., 2003</xref>
###xml 315 320 315 320 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GnRHR</underline>
###xml 315 320 315 320 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>GnRHR</underline></italic>
###xml 322 331 322 327 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;-GSU</underline>
###xml 322 331 322 327 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>&#945;-GSU</underline></italic>
###xml 333 337 329 333 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">FSHB</underline>
###xml 333 337 329 333 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>FSHB</underline></italic>
###xml 342 345 338 341 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">LHB</underline>
###xml 342 345 338 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>LHB</underline></italic>
###xml 359 384 355 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R35">Parker and Schimmer, 1997</xref>
Hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs) mediating oxygen homeostasis have been suggested to regulate uterine vascular permeability and angiogenesis (Daikoku et al., 2003). Transcription factor SF-1 is a key regulator of the transcription of many genes involved in sexual differentiation, steroidogenesis and reproduction (GnRHR, alpha-GSU, FSHB and LHB; reviewed by Parker and Schimmer, 1997).
###end p 39

###xml 253 257 253 257 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB2</underline>

###xml 370 389 367 386 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R38">Rull and Laan, 2005</xref>
In silico prediction of putative transcription factor binding sites allows to postulate the hypothesis of the involvement of CGB1 and CGB2 gene products in implantation and placental development. The hypothesis is supported by the detection of CGB1 and CGB2 transcripts in the placenta, although at much lower level compared to hCGbeta coding genes (Bo and Boime, 1992; Rull and Laan, 2005).

###begin title 41
4. Conclusions
###end title 41
###begin p 42
###xml 89 93 89 93 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1</underline>
###xml 89 93 89 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1</underline></italic>
###xml 98 102 98 102 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB2</underline>
###xml 98 102 98 102 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB2</underline></italic>
###xml 214 236 214 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="R47">Talmadge et al., 1984b</xref>
This report aimed to explore the evolution, variation and putative regulatory regions of CGB1 and CGB2 in order to seek indirect evidence for the functionality of these genes, originally considered as pseudogenes (Talmadge et al., 1984b).
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 255 259 255 256 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;</underline>
###xml 255 259 255 256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>&#946;</underline></italic>
###xml 52 57 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 66 76 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 81 88 <span type="species:ncbi:9593">gorilla</span>
###xml 100 109 <span type="species:ncbi:9600">orangutan</span>
###xml 187 191 <span type="species:ncbi:4456">apes</span>
As both of the genes were amplified additionally to human also in chimpanzee and gorilla but not in orangutan, we suggest that they have arisen among the common ancestor of African great apes. Gene duplication was accompanied by the replacement of the hCGbeta 5'UTR with a non-coding sequence providing novel putative promoter segment, 5'UTR and exon 1.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 10 14 10 14 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1</underline>

###xml 669 673 669 673 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB2</underline></italic>
###xml 3 8 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 182 187 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 192 202 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 361 366 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 371 381 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 448 455 <span type="species:ncbi:9593">gorilla</span>
###xml 694 699 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 700 710 <span type="species:ncbi:9598">chimpanzee</span>
###xml 715 722 <span type="species:ncbi:9593">gorilla</span>
In human, CGB1 and CGB2 exhibit three times lower diversity than for LHB and no ORF disturbing mutations for a sample representing three continents. Both genes are conserved between human and chimpanzee, exhibiting the same level of interspecific divergence as LHB. Especially CGB1 stands out with a strong exonic conservation with only 0.5% divergence between human and chimpanzee, whereas the respective number for LHB is 1.42%. In contrast, for gorilla both CGB1 and CGB2 harbor insertion/deletion changes, which disrupt the predicted protein and thus there is little support for the functionality of these genes. We hypothesize that the fate of duplicated CGB1 and CGB2 genes has split for human-chimpanzee and gorilla lineages evolving towards a novel functional gene for the former and pseudogenization for the latter.
###end p 44

###xml 9 13 9 13 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1</underline>
###xml 9 13 9 13 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1</underline></italic>
###xml 18 22 18 22 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB2</underline>
###xml 18 22 18 22 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB2</underline></italic>
###xml 147 153 147 153 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1/2</underline>
###xml 147 153 147 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1/2</underline></italic>
###xml 220 226 220 226 <underline xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CGB1/2</underline>
###xml 220 226 220 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><underline>CGB1/2</underline></italic>
###xml 272 281 272 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico</italic>
Upstream CGB1 and CGB2 is preserved almost full and well conserved (among genes) sequence of the promoter for hCG beta coding genes. Additionally, CGB1/2 possess a novel putative proximal promoter segment created by the CGB1/2-specific insertion. Analysis of this segment in silico for TFBSs highlighted several elements shown to regulate gene expression during implantation and placental development. However, as TFBS prediction programs can only infer the binding potential, and not the functionality of the site, only succeeding wet-lab experiments are able to uncover whether the predictions and postulated hypothesis hold true.
