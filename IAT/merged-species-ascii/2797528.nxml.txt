###begin article-title 0
###xml 33 38 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Signalling with retinoids in the human lung: validation of new tools for the expression study of retinoid receptors
###end article-title 0
###begin title 1
Background
###end title 1
###begin p 2
###xml 450 477 450 477 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Retinoic Acid Receptor beta</italic>
Retinoid Receptors are involved in development and cell homeostasis. Alterations of their expressions have been observed in lung cancer. However, retinoid chemoprevention trials in populations at risk to develop such tumors have failed. Therefore, the pertinence of new clinical trials using second generation retinoid requires prior better understanding of retinoid signalling. This is our aim when validating extensively research tools, focused on Retinoic Acid Receptor beta, whose major role in lung cancer is documented.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
Methods
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 60 68 60 68 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR beta</italic>
###xml 324 332 324 332 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo </italic>
###xml 339 344 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Biocomputing was used to assess the genomic organization of RAR beta. Its putative RAR-beta1' promoter features were investigated experimentally. Specific measures realized, with qRT-PCR Syber Green assays and a triplex of Taqman probes, were extensively validated to establish Retinoid Receptors mRNAs reference values for in vivo normal human bronchial cells, lung tumors and cell lines. Finally, a pan-RAR-beta antibody was generated and extensively validated by western-blot and immunoprecipitation.
###end p 4
###begin title 5
Results
###end title 5
###begin p 6
###xml 52 62 52 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR-beta2 </italic>
###xml 241 249 241 249 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RXR beta</italic>
###xml 117 122 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
No promoter-like activity was found for RAR-beta1'. RAR-beta2 mRNAs increase signs the normal differentiation of the human bronchial epithelium while a decrease is observed in most lung cancer cell lines. Accordingly, it is also, along with RXR beta, down-regulated in lung tumors. When using nuclear extracts of BEAS-2B and normal lung cells, only the RAR-beta2 long protein isoform was recognized by our antibody.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Conclusion
###end title 7
###begin p 8
Rigorous samples processing and extensive biocomputing, were the key factors for this study. mRNA reference values and validated tools can now be used to advance researches on retinoid signalling in the lung.
###end p 8
###begin title 9
Background
###end title 9
###begin p 10
###xml 172 173 172 173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 174 175 174 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>
###xml 371 372 371 372 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 424 448 424 448 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Retinoic Acid Receptors </italic>
###xml 449 452 449 452 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR</italic>
###xml 454 473 454 462 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;, &#946;, &#947; </italic>
###xml 477 486 466 475 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Retinoid </italic>
###xml 486 487 475 476 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">X</italic>
###xml 487 498 476 487 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"> Receptors </italic>
###xml 499 502 488 491 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RXR</italic>
###xml 504 522 493 500 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#945;, &#946;, &#947;</italic>
###xml 617 618 595 596 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 797 798 775 776 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 799 800 777 778 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 909 910 887 888 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 911 912 889 890 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
Vitamin A and its active derivatives referred to as retinoids are non-steroid hormones which play a critical role in the development and homeostasis of vertebrate tissues [1-3]. They exert their actions by regulating the expression of target genes, influencing likewise: cell proliferation, differentiation and apoptosis through nuclear Retinoid Receptors (RRs) binding [4]. Six different RRs are encoded by separate genes (Retinoic Acid Receptors (RAR) alpha, beta, gamma and Retinoid X Receptors (RXR) alpha, beta, gamma) with at least two isoforms for each, depending upon promoter usage and alternative splicing [5]. RAR/RXR heterodimers are the functional units transducing the retinoid signal when binding Retinoic Acid (RA), co-activators and DNA response elements (RAREs) of target genes [6,7]. RXRs form homodimers and/or heterodimers with RARs and most other nuclear receptors for extended effects [8,9].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 68 77 68 73 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#945; </italic>
###xml 127 129 123 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 224 226 220 222 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>
###xml 284 292 280 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 421 423 414 416 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>
###xml 424 426 417 419 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 448 457 441 446 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#947; </italic>
###xml 482 484 471 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 507 512 496 501 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RXRs </italic>
###xml 566 568 555 557 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 569 571 558 560 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 574 582 563 568 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 622 624 608 610 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 625 627 611 613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 992 994 978 980 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B22">22</xref>
###xml 995 997 981 983 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>
###xml 189 194 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 684 691 <span type="species:ncbi:4097">tobacco</span>
###xml 1084 1089 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Alterations in RRs expressions are observed in several cancers. The RARalpha gene is involved in acute promyelocytic leukemia [10] and is also silenced through promoter hypermethylation in human breast carcinoma cell lines [11]. In lung, breast and prostate tumors, the expression of RARbeta is found lost or down-regulated through unconstant promoter hypermethylation, but without any deletion or mutation of this gene [12-15]. The involvement of RARgamma in cancer is less known [16]. Down-expressions of RXRs have been reported in prostate and thyroid carcinoma [17,18]. RARbeta has been mostly studied in lung cancer [19-21]. For this serious disease, primary prevention, that is tobacco eviction, remains the best goal. However, considering the overall poor results obtained by the current treatments, it seems important to offer to "at risk" subjects a secondary chemoprevention. Retinoids have been used with such a goal but have given deceiving results for still unexplained reasons [22,23]. This underlines the need for more knowledge about retinoid signalling in the normal human lung.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 61 71 61 71 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico </italic>
###xml 85 93 85 90 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 79 84 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 269 274 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
In this context, we recapitulated the informations available in silico for the human RARbeta in order to update its genomic organization. The activity of a putative promoter RAR-beta1' was assessed experimentally. Reference values for RRs mRNAs were measured in Normal Human Bronchial Epithelial cells (NHBE) with qRT-PCR Syber Green assays and a triplex of Taqman probes. Conceived to save invaluable bronchial mucosa samples, the reliability of this last qRT-PCR tool was extensively validated. Hence, it was used to measure the relative RRs mRNAs levels in lung tumors and cell lines. Finally, contrary to commercial antibodies, our pan-RARbeta antibody immunoprecipitated a single band of protein in nuclear extracts of BEAS-2B and NHBE cells. Its molecular weight and further amino acid sequencing demonstrated that it corresponds to the RARbeta2 protein isoform. Rigorous samples processing, like extensive biocomputing analysis were the key factors for the realization of this study. Validated tools are now available for future investigations on Vitamin A/retinoid signalling in the lung.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Methods
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Samples
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 421 423 421 423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 890 892 889 891 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 284 292 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 643 651 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
This study was approved by the French Medical Ethical Committee for Bioresearch of Lorraine. All the samples were provided by the "Centre de Ressources Biologiques du Centre Hospitalier Universitaire de Nancy", an ISO 9001-2000 certified biobank. Informed consents were obtained from patients operated upon for lung cancer who donated their samples to the biobank. Samples collections have been extensively described in [24]. All the reagents were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (L'Isle d'Abeau Chesnes, Saint-Quentin Fallavier, France), unless mentioned. Twelve Squamous Cell epidermoid Lung Cancer (SqCLC) samples were obtained from different patients. Fourty mum frozen sections were cut and further dissected by JMV, our pathologist, after quick staining with RNAse-free azur blue solution. Contaminating normal tissues were removed and RNA was further prepared to give 90% tumoral mRNA [24].
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 1096 1098 1088 1090 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
###xml 258 262 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">Calf</span>
###xml 546 551 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 930 935 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Human</span>
###xml 1516 1524 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
Trachea and the beginning of the two main bronchi were obtained from ten donors. All subjects were kept under artificial respiration until the specimens were collected. Then, samples were bathed in cold RPMI-1640 complete medium supplemented with 10% Foetal Calf Serum, 50 units/mL penicillin, 50 mug/mL streptomycin and 5 mug/mL fungizone for express transport to the laboratory. There, they were cut open and the mucosa was washed with RPMI complete medium before being scratched with the back of a scalpel blade to give "Normal differentiated Human Bronchial Epithelial cells" or NHBE: our reference tissue sample. Pooled NHBE were kept frozen at -20degreesC until further processing. Histological examination demonstrated that such samples contained mainly differentiated bronchial cells. The remaining scratched mucosa was dissected out from the bronchi and cut into small explants to give rise to "Cultured undifferentiated Human Bronchial Epithelial cells" (CHBE) in LH9 medium (Cambrex Bio Sciences, Emerainville, France). Cells were grown for two weeks with regular medium change as in [25]. A week later, explants were detached from dishes and cultured again twice. At confluence, CHBE were scrapped and processed like NHBE for RNA purification. Histological examination revealed mostly undifferentiated basal bronchial cells. The smooth muscle sheaths from the back of the trachea were obtained by dissection and were cut frozen as 20 mum sections. Skin samples were collected from three different female patients undergoing fat reduction and were cut also as 20 mum frozen sections parallel to the basal membrane. Quick histological examination of every third section verified that collection was restricted to epidermis. All specimens were processed as NHBE to prepare RNA.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 475 477 469 471 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2 </sub>
###xml 35 40 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The BEAS-2B [CRL-9609] transformed human bronchial cell line, the pleural mesothelioma NCI-H513, the Small Cell Lung Cancer NCI-H69 [HTB-119], the Non-Small Cell Lung Cancers (NSCLC) NCI-H1648 [CRL-5882], NCI-H2087 [CRL-5922] and NCI-H2342 [CRL-5941], the lung A549 [CCL-185] and the breast MDA-MB-231 [HTB-26] adenocarcinoma were purchased from the American Type Culture Collection (Rockville, MD, USA). All were propagated at 37degreesC in a 96% humid atmosphere with 4% CO2 in RPMI-1640 complete medium to confluence to prepare RNA as for CHBE.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
RNA preparation
###end title 18
###begin p 19
In order to facilitate RNA solubilization, all frozen cut samples were first passed through a 23-Gauge needle. Total RNA was purified using the RNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen, Courtaboeuf, France) with DNA digestion and integrity verification steps according to the manufacturer's instructions and our internal quality controls. Then, RNAs were diluted to a final concentration of 125 ng/muL and kept frozen at -20degreesC.
###end p 19
###begin title 20
cDNA synthesis
###end title 20
###begin p 21
For each sample, 3 aliquots of 1 mug total RNA were reverse transcribed using the First-Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (GE Healthcare Life Sciences, Orsay, France) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Serial dilutions of each cDNA at 1/5, 1/10, 1/50, 1/100 and 1/500 were performed.
###end p 21

Genome databanks were mined to construct an extended RARbeta genomic organization (Figure 1). Relevant PCR primers were designed to evince the presence of non validated mRNA transcripts: [GenBank:], [GenBank:], [GenBank:] and [GenBank:] (Additional File 1). The PCR program was: 94degreesC for 2 min, followed by 30 cycles: 94degreesC for 20 s, 60-64degreesC for 20 s, 72degreesC for 45 s and then 10 min at 72degreesC. Amplicons were analyzed by agarose gel (2%) electrophoresis. The sequences of the 5' flanking regions (-1500/-50) of each upstream transcript were further screened for promoter features with: Proscan version 1.7 [26], Promoter 2.0 [27], and Promoter Inspector [28]. Additional analysis with MatInspector [28] and TFSEARCH [29] identified transcription factors DNA binding sites. In order to evince any RARE, these sequences were also analyzed by ModelInspector [28] using a RARE DR5 weight matrix generated from the following RARE DR5 model sequence: [pu]g [g/t]tca = [ag]g [gt]tca/tga [ac]c [ct]. Finally, the orthologous conservation of the mouse RAR-beta1 and the human RAR-beta1' promoters were investigated using the Eldorado comparative genomics module [28].

###begin title 25
RAR-beta1' promoter analysis
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 767 775 767 775 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Renilla </italic>
###xml 1260 1262 1242 1244 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3 </sup>
###xml 1404 1412 1374 1382 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Renilla </italic>
Using NHBE DNA, this 1434 bp RARE containing sequence [chr3:25,355,884-25,357,317] was amplified by PCR in both forward and reverse orientations. Amplicons were sequenced for verification and then cloned into pGL3 Basic Luciferase Reporter Vectors (Promega, Charbonnieres-les-Bains, France), using MluI and XhoI restriction sites. The forward PCR primers were: (F)5'-aatgcacgcgtccaattcaacgctttctgtacc-3' (R)5'-aacggctcgaggctctgtcaatggcttctcc-3', and the reverse primers were: (F)5'-aacggctcgagccaattcaacgctttctgtacc-3' (R)5'-aatgcacgcgtgctctgtcaatggcttctcc-3'. BEAS-2B and MDA-MB-231 cell lines were co-transfected in triplicate wells for 24 h and 48 h with the pGL3+RAR-beta1' plasmid constructs (firefly Luciferase) and the pRL-TK internal control reporter vector (Renilla Luciferase). Transfections were performed in 6-well plates using the jetPEItrade mark transfection reagent (Polyplus Transfection, Strasbourg, France) according to the manufacturer's instructions. The activity of the RAR-beta1' promoter was assessed by Luciferase reporter assays and carried out with the Dual-Glotrade mark Luciferase Assay reagent (Promega, Charbonnieres-les-Bains, France) following the manufacturer's instructions. Resulting fluorescence was measured using a Victor3 trade mark plate reader (PerkinElmer, Courtaboeuf, France) with fluorescence readings for firefly Luciferase (pGL3+P1'-RARbeta) normalized to Renilla Luciferase (pRL-TK).

The qRT-PCR primers and Taqman probes used in this study are presented in the Additional File 1. Primers for beta-Actin were chosen in the gene 3' untranslated region as a RNA integrity control. For Syber Green assays, a beta-Actin standard curve was first performed using the full cDNA cascade dilutions of each cDNA sample in triplicate and a RotorGene 6000 (Corbett Research, Labgene, Archamps, France). RRs were measured for the sole dilutions: 1/10, 1/50 and 1/100, in triplicates. Ten muL final volume contained: 4 muL of cDNA, 0.14 muL of 10 muM primers (140 nM each), 5 muL of Absolute QPCR SYBR Green Mix (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Courtaboeuf, France) and 0.72 muL of water. The PCR program was: 95degreesC for 15 min, followed by 50 cycles of 95degreesC: 10 s, 60degreesC: 20 s and 72degreesC: 20 s. ###begin p 30
###xml 129 131 129 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>
###xml 784 785 784 785 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="S2">2</xref>
###xml 855 857 855 857 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2 </sup>
Raw qRT-PCR data were collected with the RotorGene 6000 Series Software version 1.7.87. The comparative quantification approach [30] implemented within the built-in software was used to compute the second derivative of each amplification curve whose peak corresponds to the point at which the most efficient amplicon synthesis occurs. For each measure, the software determines a "Take-Off Point (TOP)" value, 80% before the peak and an "Amplification Efficiency (AE)" from the slope of the section of the curve between the TOP and the peak. For each gene and sample analyzed, all the TOP and AE values from the replicates were averaged to calculate the mean Take-Off Point (mTOP) and the mean Amplification Efficiency (mAE) values with their Standard Deviations (SD) (Additional File 2). The quality of each standard curve was assessed by its associated R2 value, computed with automatic fluorescence threshold settings by the RotorGene software.

###xml 649 665 635 651 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">(control-sample)</sup>
For statistical analysis, the qPCR data were first exported into the Bestkeeper(c) version 1 software [31] to determine the most stable and common reference genes across all the samples. Validated reference genes were then combined into a Bestkeeper(c) index for the normalization of RRs expression data. Then, the Rest-RG(c) software [32] was used to transform the raw qPCR data into normalized RR x-fold expression ratios (R). It uses the mathematical model described by Pfaffl et al. [33], that combines gene quantification and normalization, according to the following equation: Ratio (R) = (Etarget)DeltaCtarget(control-sample)/(Eref)DeltaCtref(control-sample).
###end p 31
###begin p 32
###xml 76 80 76 78 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#169; </sup>
###xml 300 303 284 287 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">vs </italic>
###xml 473 476 457 458 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#169;</sup>
Ratios were computed using a Pair Wise Fixed Reallocation Randomisation Test(c) and plotted with Standard Error (SE) estimation via a Taylor algorithm. Computations were based upon the sample-specific mAE (mAE<-->E) and the corresponding mTOP deviations (DeltamTOP<-->DeltaCt) for each sample tested vs the control sample: NHBE. In each sample tested, RRs genes expressions were also normalized by the optimal combination of reference genes previously defined by Bestkeeper(c).

###xml 1502 1504 1460 1462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 66 70 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 76 82 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">murine</span>
###xml 237 243 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 518 523 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 675 680 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 780 785 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 880 885 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1359 1364 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The different isoforms encoded by RARbeta were first described in mice. Two murine promoters have been characterized: RAR-beta1, encoding the RARbeta1 and RARbeta3 proteins, and RAR-beta2, encoding RARbeta2 and RARbeta4 [35,36]. Then in humans, the RARbeta2 protein [Swiss-Prot:], encoded by a CpG island and RARE containing RAR-beta2 promoter region, was reported [37]. The RARbeta4 protein isoform [Swiss-Prot:], generated from the alternate splicing of the RARbeta2 mRNA at the exon 3, was further described [5]. A human foetal protein isoform, RARbeta1 [Swiss-Prot:], also expressed in small cell lung cancers has been identified [38]. However, neither the corresponding mouse RAR-beta1-like promoter nor the RARbeta1 and RARbeta3 transcripts have been fully characterized in human cells. Another protein isoform, RARbeta5, has been characterized in both normal and malignant human breast cells [39]. It is supposed to contribute to the resistance of the cancerous cells to retinoids. This isoform is encoded by a cryptic distinct RAR-beta3 promoter, located in the first intron of the RARbeta2 transcript [39]. Finally, a RARbeta1' transcript isoform, with a specific first exon, has been reported [40]. However, the corresponding protein sequence is not described. This transcript is encoded by a distinct promoter RAR-beta1" and is expressed in normal human lung and normal RA-sensitive BEAS-2B cells but it is suppressed in RA-resistant BEAS-2B, in some lung cancer cell lines and lung tumors [40].

The RARgamma mRNA levels varied as follows: H513(2.27) > CHBE(1.63) > skin(1.29) > A549(0.74) > BEAS-2B(0.57) > SMC(0.53) > H2342(0.20) > H2087(-0.01) > H69(-2.32). In H1648, a very poor amplification signal was detected for RARgamma. RXRalpha transcripts levels were ranked as follows: H513(2.05) > skin(1.53) > A549(1.14) > H1648(1.05) > CHBE(0.15) > H2342(-0.07) > SMC(-0.14) > H2087(-0.71) > BEAS-2B(-0.96) > H69(-1.75). In Syber Green assays, RARalpha, RARgamma and RXRalpha were all found over-expressed in skin when compared to the other samples. Corresponding RRs protein levels were also previously reported as highly expressed in this tissue [46].
###end p 48

###xml 32 37 32 37 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RXRs </italic>
###xml 142 144 142 144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B47">47</xref>
###xml 150 158 150 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RXR&#946; </italic>
###xml 214 216 211 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B48">48</xref>
###xml 289 298 286 291 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RXR&#945; </italic>
###xml 302 310 295 300 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RXR&#946; </italic>
###xml 393 400 383 387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RXR&#946;</italic>
Significant down-expressions of RXRs mRNAs were previously reported in lung tumors by comparison to their respective matching normal tissues [47] and RXRbeta was reported as a biomarker of aggressivity for NSCLCs [48]. Nevertheless, in all the lung cancer cell lines tested in this study, RXRalpha and RXRbeta were not found differentially expressed from the control tissue NHBE. Furthermore, RXRbeta, was demonstrated as stably expressed across all the samples, and with both qRT-PCR methodologies. In this context, it was therefore considered as a relevant reference gene.

###begin title 51
Western-Blot and Immunoprecipitation of RARbeta
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 490 491 481 482 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 534 535 525 526 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>
###xml 981 982 960 961 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 1049 1051 1025 1027 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B49">49</xref>
###xml 1300 1302 1273 1275 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 1698 1700 1662 1664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B50">50</xref>
Commercial antibodies against RARbeta gave in our hands conflicting results in western-blots, with several protein bands that are either attributed to phosphorylation or degradation. Therefore, we validated extensively a new pan-RARbeta antibody directed toward a specific peptidic sequence common to all the RARbeta protein isoforms described (F2 region). Using nuclear extracts of BEAS-2B and NHBE cells, the antibody recognized a single band of about 52 kDa in both western-blot (Figure 3, panel A) and immunoprecipitation (Figure 3, panel B) experiments. The amino acid sequencing gave the following sequence: SSADHRVRLDLG (exon 5 of []) also common to all the RARbeta proteins described, attesting for the specificity of the antibody. The results obtained correspond to the molecular weight expected for the RARbeta2 protein, which is reported as a nuclear isoform of RARbeta in databanks. The RARbeta4 protein, whose molecular weight was previously reported at about 37 kDa [5] is reported as a cytoplasmic isoform of RARbeta in normal cells [49] and it was accordingly not detected by our antibody in BEAS-2B and NHBE nuclear extracts. The RARbeta1' protein isoform was previously characterized using whole cell extracts of BEAS-2B and it was reported with a molecular weight of about 47 KDa [40]. This isoform was also not detected by our antibody in the nuclear extracts analyzed. These results suggest that, if it is actually expressed in BEAS-2B and NHBE cells, the RARbeta1' protein is another short cytoplasmic isoform of RARbeta. That is particularly interesting as truncated isoforms of RARbeta with "tumorogenic activity" were previously reported over-expressed in malignant cells [50]. Indeed, these truncated isoforms are thought to compete with RARbeta2 in the nuclei of cancer cells; inhibiting its cellular functions, enhancing the proliferation process and contributing to the resistance of the cells to retinoids.

###begin title 54
Conclusion
###end title 54
###begin p 55
###xml 245 253 245 250 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 390 398 387 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 491 499 485 490 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 602 611 593 599 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946;2 </italic>
###xml 690 699 678 684 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946;2 </italic>
###xml 745 753 730 735 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 822 830 804 809 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 884 894 863 873 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in silico </italic>
###xml 908 915 887 891 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946;</italic>
###xml 971 979 947 952 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 1214 1222 1187 1192 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 1334 1342 1304 1309 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 1490 1501 1457 1465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#946;-Actin </italic>
###xml 1505 1514 1469 1474 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#945; </italic>
###xml 1567 1574 1527 1531 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RXR&#946;</italic>
###xml 1704 1712 1661 1666 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">RAR&#946; </italic>
###xml 545 550 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 902 907 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 2104 2109 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In this study, we used qRT-PCR analysis to measure RRs mRNAs expressions in several samples and compared the results obtained in Syber Green assays to those given by a triplex of Taqman probes that was conceived to save invaluable mRNA samples. RARbeta was the only RR gene whose mRNA levels were found significantly different when compared to NHBE. In these differentiated lung cells, the RARbeta transcripts were found at their highest levels of expression, indicating that an increase in RARbeta mRNAs signs the normal differentiation of the human bronchial epithelium. In all the samples analyzed, RARbeta2 was the major transcript isoform that was detected. In addition, the levels of RARbeta2 varied concomitantly with the whole levels of RARbeta mRNAs measured in each sample. To evince the existence of additional RARbeta isoforms, we recapitulated the informations available in silico for the human RARbeta. Then, we investigated for the presence of unvalidated RARbeta transcripts and for the activity of a putative RAR-beta1' promoter region. However, such transcripts were not detected in the samples tested and no activity was found for RAR-beta1'. This allowed to demonstrate that the whole level of RARbeta mRNAs observed in each sample is represented by the transcripts isoforms generated from the RAR-beta2 promoter. RARbeta transcripts were found highly down-expressed in NSCLC cell lines and SqCLC tumors, which is a reported event in the process of lung carcinogenesis. beta-Actin and RARalpha expressions were found stable across samples. As for RXRbeta, although its expression was found stable in normal lung cells and cancer lines, a concomitant down-regulation was observed with RARbeta in SqCLC samples. This suggests a possible role for this RR as an heterodimerization partner for RARbeta in the bronchial mucosa. Finally, RARbeta2 was the only protein isoform detected by our pan-RARbeta antibody in NHBE and BEAS-2B nuclear extracts, indicating that short protein isoforms of RARbeta were not present within the nuclei of these cells. In our view, the research for specific human micro-RNAs targeting each of the RRs, as well as RNA interference experiments, and the over-expressions of full or truncated versions of RRs, would be of great interest in order to complete our understanding of the molecular mechanisms involved in the process of lung carcinogenesis.
