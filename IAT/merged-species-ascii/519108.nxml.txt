Identification and cloning of two putative subunits of DNA polymerase epsilon in fission yeast
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 95 111 95 111 <email xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">gdurso@miami.edu</email>

###begin p 2
###xml 186 190 180 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb3</italic>
###xml 190 191 184 185 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 196 200 190 194 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 200 201 194 195 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 334 338 318 322 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 442 447 426 431 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc20</italic>
###xml 447 448 431 432 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 477 481 455 459 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cut5</italic>
###xml 481 482 459 460 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 513 518 491 496 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sna41</italic>
###xml 518 519 496 497 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 546 551 524 529 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc21</italic>
###xml 551 552 529 530 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 695 696 673 674 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S</italic>
###xml 1143 1150 1115 1122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vivo</italic>
###xml 158 171 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
DNA polymerase epsilon (Pol epsilon) is a multi-subunit enzyme required for the initiation of chromosomal DNA replication. Here, we report the cloning of two fission yeast genes, called dpb3+ and dpb4+ that encode proteins homologous to the two smallest subunits of Pol epsilon. Although Dpb4 is not required for cell viability, Deltadpb4 mutants are synthetically lethal with mutations in four genes required for DNA replication initiation, cdc20+ (encoding DNA Pol epsilon), cut5+ (homologous to DPB11/TopBP1), sna41+ (homologous to CDC45) and cdc21+ (encoding Mcm4, a component of the pre-replicative complex). In contrast to Dpb4, Dpb3 is essential for cell cycle progression. A glutathione S-transferase pull-down assay indicates that Dpb3 physically interacts with both Dpb2 and Dpb4, suggesting that Dpb3 associates with other members of the Pol epsilon complex. Depletion of Dpb3 leads to an accumulation of cells in S phase consistent with Dpb3 having a role in DNA replication. In addition, many of the cells have a bi-nucleate or multinucleate phenotype, indicating that cell separation is also inhibited. Finally, we have examined in vivo localization of green fluorescent protein (GFP)-tagged Dpb3 and Dpb4 and found that both proteins are localized to the nucleus consistent with their proposed role in DNA replication. However, in the absence of Dpb3, GFP-Dpb4 appears to be more dispersed throughout the cell, suggesting that Dpb3 may be important in establishing or maintaining normal localization of Dpb4.
###end p 2
###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 342 343 314 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c1">1</xref>
###xml 626 627 592 593 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c2">2</xref>
###xml 739 740 699 700 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c2">2</xref>
###xml 741 742 701 702 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c4">4</xref>
###xml 879 880 833 834 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c5">5</xref>
###xml 1201 1202 1143 1144 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c6">6</xref>
###xml 648 653 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Eukaryotic chromosomal DNA replication requires the activity of at least three DNA polymerases, alpha (Pol alpha), delta (Pol delta) and epsilon (Pol epsilon). It is generally accepted that Pol alpha is responsible for initiation through the synthesis of RNA/DNA primers, while both Pol delta and Pol epsilon participate in chain elongation (1). We have shown previously that Cdc20, the catalytic subunit of Pol epsilon, is composed of an N-terminal region that contains all the domains necessary for polymerase activity, while the C-terminal half of the enzyme contains essential elements with no known biochemical function (2). Interestingly, in yeast, the N-terminal catalytic domains of Pol epsilon are dispensable for cell viability (2-4). Based on our earlier observations that temperature-sensitive mutants in the catalytic subunit of Pol epsilon arrest in early S phase (5), we proposed that the primary role of Pol epsilon is to facilitate assembly of the DNA replication initiation complex. Consistent with this model, we have recently shown that Dpb2, the second largest subunit of Pol epsilon, is required for chromosomal DNA replication and binds to replication origins early in S phase (6).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 76 77 70 71 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c7">7</xref>
###xml 78 80 72 74 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c10">10</xref>
###xml 131 135 125 129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POL2</italic>
###xml 137 141 131 135 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB2</italic>
###xml 143 147 137 141 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB3</italic>
###xml 152 156 146 150 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 158 160 152 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c11">11</xref>
###xml 161 163 155 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c13">13</xref>
###xml 166 170 160 164 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POL2</italic>
###xml 175 179 169 173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB2</italic>
###xml 315 317 309 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c11">11</xref>
###xml 318 320 312 314 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c14">14</xref>
###xml 344 348 338 342 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB3</italic>
###xml 353 357 347 351 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 374 386 368 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 488 490 482 484 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c12">12</xref>
###xml 491 493 485 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c13">13</xref>
###xml 567 569 561 563 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c15">15</xref>
###xml 53 58 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 63 68 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 94 99 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 374 386 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 554 559 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Pol epsilon is composed of at least four subunits in yeast and human cells (7-10). In budding yeast, these subunits are encoded by POL2, DPB2, DPB3 and DPB4 (11-13). POL2 and DPB2 are essential for cell viability and mutants in either of these two genes lead to cell cycle arrest and inhibition of DNA replication (11,14). In contrast, neither DPB3 nor DPB4 is essential in S.cerevisiae. However, cells deleted for either of these genes do show subtle defects in nucleotide biosynthesis (12,13). Homologs of all of these proteins have been identified in human cells (15), yet their precise function in chromosomal DNA replication remains obscure.
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 49 50 49 50 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c9">9</xref>
###xml 51 53 51 53 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c12">12</xref>
###xml 54 56 54 56 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c13">13</xref>
###xml 196 198 196 198 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c16">16</xref>
###xml 199 201 199 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c17">17</xref>
###xml 209 219 209 219 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 380 382 380 382 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c18">18</xref>
###xml 383 385 383 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c20">20</xref>
###xml 537 539 537 539 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c21">21</xref>
###xml 224 229 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 405 410 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
Both Dpb3 and Dpb4 contain a histone-fold motif (9,12,13). The histone-fold motif, an extended helix-strand-helix, is believed to play a role in both protein-protein and protein-DNA interactions (16,17). Both Drosophila and human Dpb4 have been shown to be a component of the chromatin-accessibility complex (CHRAC), suggesting that Dpb4 may be involved in nucleosome remodeling (18-20). Also, in budding yeast, DPB4 has been identified as a component of ISW2, a CHRAC-like complex that is involved in epigenetic silencing at telomeres (21).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 117 125 117 125 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 127 129 127 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c22">22</xref>
###xml 425 427 409 411 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c14">14</xref>
###xml 428 430 412 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c23">23</xref>
###xml 15 20 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 108 112 <span type="species:ncbi:10633?0.9966199903428296">SV40</span>
Interestingly, human CHRAC has been reported to stimulate T-antigen-mediated replication of chromatin-bound SV40 DNA in vitro (22), suggesting that CHRAC might provide a remodeling activity that facilitates the initiation of DNA replication on chromatin. It has also been reported that the C-terminal region of Pol epsilon interacts with Pol sigma, suggesting that Pol epsilon might have a role in sister-chromatid cohesion (14,23). In conclusion, these observations suggest that Pol epsilon may have several functions independent of its polymerase activity.
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 174 186 174 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 241 248 241 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 65 78 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
###xml 120 125 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 138 143 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 174 186 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
###xml 241 248 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
In this paper, we report the cloning and characterization of two fission yeast genes that encode proteins homologous to human and budding yeast Dpb3 and Dpb4. In contrast to S.cerevisiae, we find that Dpb3 is essential for cell viability in S.pombe. Depletion of Dpb3 results in a strong cell cycle delay and accumulation of cells with slightly less than 2C DNA content, suggesting that cells have difficulty completing S phase. Also, a significant percentage of cells depleted for Dpb3 contain two or more nuclei, suggesting loss of Dpb3 results in a partial block to cell separation. GST-pull down assays show that Dpb3 can interact with both Dpb2 and Dpb4, confirming that Dpb3 interacts with the other putative subunits of the Pol epsilon complex.
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 76 80 76 80 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 80 81 80 81 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 181 186 175 180 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc20</italic>
###xml 186 187 180 181 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 198 207 192 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cut5-T401</italic>
###xml 209 218 203 212 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sna41-912</italic>
###xml 223 232 217 226 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc21-M68</italic>
Although Dpb4 was found to be non-essential for cell viability, deletion of dpb4+ is synthetically lethal with mutants defective in the catalytic subunit of Pol epsilon, encoded by cdc20+, and with cut5-T401, sna41-912 and cdc21-M68, three mutants defective in DNA replication initiation. These observations are consistent with Dpb4 being important for some aspect of DNA replication.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
Finally, we show that both Dpb3 and Dpb4 localize to the nucleus consistent with their proposed role in DNA replication and that normal localization of Dpb4 is dependent on expression of Dpb3.
###end p 10

The plasmids pKS(+)Deltadpb3::ura4+ and pKS(+)Deltadpb4::ura4+ used for deletion of dpb3+ and dpb4+ were constructed as follows: a 1.8 kb DNA fragment containing the ura4+ gene was isolated from pARC614 digested with HindIII and cloned into pKS(+)Bluescript. Both the upstream (1.6-2.0 kb) and the downstream (1.8-2.0 kb) flanking genomic sequences of each gene were amplified by PCR from cosmid DNA and cloned into pKS(+)Bluescript at the ApaI/XhoI and the XmaI/BamHI restriction sites, respectively, located upstream and downstream of the ura4+ gene. The resulting plasmids were digested with KpnI/NotI to obtain a DNA fragment containing ura4+ and either the dpb3+ or dpb4+ flanking sequences. Each DNA fragment was then transformed into the diploid strain (h+/h-leu1-32/leu1-32 ura4-D18/ura4-D18 ade6-M210/ade6-M216) and stable ura4+ integrants were selected. Replacement of either dpb3+ or dpb4+ with the ura4+ gene was confirmed by the Southern blot analysis. Diploids were induced to sporulate and meiotic segregants were analyzed by tetrad analysis.

We identified a gene in the S.pombe genome database (accession no. Q10315) that encodes a protein that is 34 and 26% identical to human p12 (human Dpb3) and to the N-terminal portion of S.cerevisiae DPB3, respectively. Consequently, the gene was termed dpb3+. Only the N-terminal half of the protein that contains the histone-fold motif (shown in Figure 1A and B) displays any significant homology to the other known orthologs. Human, mouse and Drosophila Dpb3 are considerably smaller (12-15 kD) than Dpb3 from budding or fission yeast. Although both S.pombe and S.cerevisiae Dpb3 contain an extended C-terminal region, a comparison between these two sequences reveals no significant homology within this region of the protein. This suggests that the histone-fold motif is critical for Dpb3 function. Also, although S.pombe Dpb3 shows limited identity to budding yeast DPB3 (<20% overall), the two proteins are nearly identical in size (both are estimated to be 22 kD). There is only one additional protein encoded by the S.pombe genome, called Php5 that is similar to human p12 or S.cerevisiae DPB3. Considering that the estimated mass of this protein (46 kD) is significantly larger than the other homologs, and that it has previously been reported to be a component of a CCAAT-binding factor essential for the expression of the cyc1+ gene, it is unlikely that Php5 represents the third subunit of DNA Pol epsilon (27). Therefore, we conclude that the gene we have identified as dpb3+ encodes an ortholog of S.cerevisiae DPB3 and human p12. To determine whether dpb3+ is essential for cell viability, we constructed a diploid strain where a single copy of dpb3+ was replaced by ura4+ (Figure 2A). Construction of the deletion strain was confirmed by Southern blot analysis (Figure 2B). Following sporulation, germinating spores were subjected to tetrad analysis. Most of the meiotic segregants examined gave rise to a 2:2 segregation pattern of viable/non-viable cells indicating that dpb3+ is an essential gene (Figure 2C). As expected, the viable colonies failed to grow in the absence of uracil. In the three tetrads that gave a 1:3 ratio of viable/non-viable cells, the viable colony was ura- suggesting that the remaining ura- spore failed to germinate. Failure of ura- spores to germinate during tetrad analysis is frequently observed. Microscopic analysis of Deltadpb3 germinating spores showed that cells underwent a few rounds of cell division before arresting with an elongated cell (cdc) phenotype, (Figure 2D) indicative of a cell cycle block. Therefore, we concluded that Dpb3 is essential for cell cycle progression.

To determine whether Dpb4 is involved in DNA synthesis, we have examined whether Deltadpb4 is synthetically lethal with temperature-sensitive DNA replication mutants, including mutants defective for DNA Pol epsilon (see Table 1). Cells deleted for dpb4+ were crossed to a variety of mutants and incubated at temperatures ranging from the permissive temperature of 25degreesC to the restrictive temperature of 36degreesC. As shown in Table 1, we were unable to recover Deltadpb4 cdc20-IA5 double mutants, suggesting that the mutations are synthetically lethal (confirmed by tetrad analysis). Similarly, when Deltadpb4 was crossed to two additional temperature-sensitive mutants of Pol epsilon, cdc20-M10 and cdc20-P7 (28), the restrictive temperature for the double mutants was lower than for the cdc20 mutants alone (30degreesC for Deltadpb4cdc20-P7 and 32degreesC for Deltadpb4cdc20-M10, respectively), indicating a synthetic-lethal interaction. As noted earlier, Deltadpb4 cells are not temperature sensitive. We also found that Deltadpb4 mutants were synthetically lethal with three additional replication mutants, sna41-912, cut5-T401 and cdc21-M68 (Table 1). Sna41 is homologous to budding yeast CDC45 and is thought to play a critical role in the loading of DNA polymerases to chromatin during early S phase (29-31). Cut5 was originally identified in fission yeast as a gene required for both DNA replication initiation and coupling of S phase to mitosis (32-34). When incubated at the restrictive temperature of 36degreesC, cut5 mutants arrest with a 1C DNA content, but fail to establish a checkpoint that blocks entry into mitosis and therefore arrest with a 'cut' (cell untimely torn) phenotype. The double mutant Deltadpb4cut5-T401 displayed a similar phenotype at an intermediate temperature of 30degreesC (data not shown). Cut5 is homologous to S.cerevisiae DPB11 and mammalian TopBP1. Both proteins have been implicated in DNA replication initiation and are thought to be required for the loading of CDC45 and DNA polymerases to chromatin in late G1 (35-37). Similar to Cut5, they have also been shown to be required for the checkpoint control in response to DNA damage (36-40). Cdc21 encodes a protein homologous to MCM4 that is required for the assembly of pre-replicative complexes at the conclusion of anaphase and is likely to be a component of the replicative helicase (41,42). Taken together, these results suggest that Pol epsilon might interact directly with proteins required during the early stages of DNA replication initiation.

###begin p 33
###xml 255 256 249 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh811f3">3</xref>
We also tested whether GST-Dpb3 or GST-Dpb4 could interact with Myc-tagged Dpb2, which encodes the second largest subunit of Pol epsilon. Our results indicate that GST-Dpb3, but not GST-Dpb4 (data not shown), can physically interact with Myc-Dpb2 (Figure 3B). In summary, these results suggest that Dpb3 is an essential component of the Pol epsilon complex, perhaps acting as a bridge between Dpb2 and Dpb4 subunits. However, it is also possible that GST fused to Dpb4 may interfere with its ability to bind Dpb2.
###end p 33
###begin p 34
We also tested whether any of the small subunits can interact with the large catalytic subunit of Pol epsilon. Unfortunately, due to the insolubility of Pol epsilon, we were unable to detect any interactions by co-immunoprecipitation experiments.
###end p 34
###begin title 35
Down-regulation of Dpb3 leads to a cell cycle delay
###end title 35
###begin p 36
###xml 109 113 109 113 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb3</italic>
###xml 113 114 113 114 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 242 246 238 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb3</italic>
###xml 296 300 292 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb3</italic>
###xml 300 301 296 297 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 348 353 344 349 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">nmt81</italic>
###xml 364 366 360 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c26">26</xref>
###xml 493 494 489 490 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh811f4">4</xref>
###xml 617 618 613 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh811f4">4</xref>
###xml 943 944 939 940 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh811f4">4</xref>
###xml 1090 1091 1086 1087 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh811f4">4</xref>
To further investigate the function of Dpb3, we attempted to isolate temperature-sensitive mutant alleles of dpb3+ by PCR mutagenesis, but unfortunately no conditional mutants were recovered. As an alternative approach, we constructed a Deltadpb3 deletion strain containing an integrated copy of dpb3+ under the control of the thiamine-repressible nmt81 promoter (26). Twenty-four hours after the addition of thiamine, cells appear elongated, suggesting that the cell cycle is delayed (Figure 4A). Over the next 48 h, there is a marginal increase in cell number consistent with a substantial cell cycle block (Figure 4B). These observations suggest that Dpb3 is essential for normal cell cycle progression, and that Dpb3 protein is relatively stable and can only be depleted after several generations. Flow cytometry analysis revealed that from 42 to 72 h, there is an increase in cells in S phase (cells with less than 2C DNA content; Figure 4C), suggesting that cells depleted for Dpb3 have difficulty progressing through S phase. There is also an increase in multinucleate cells (Figure 4A and D), which contributes to the peak at 4C. The increase in multinucleate cells suggests that loss of Dpb3 inhibits cytokinesis in addition to inhibiting DNA replication.
###end p 36
###begin title 37
Dpb3 is required for proper nuclear localization of Dpb4
###end title 37
###begin p 38
###xml 148 155 148 155 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in situ</italic>
###xml 422 423 422 423 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh811f5">5</xref>
###xml 531 532 531 532 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh811f5">5</xref>
###xml 76 81 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 204 217 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">Fission yeast</span>
To determine the sub-cellular localization of Dpb3 and Dpb4, we constructed yeast strains where GFP was fused to the C-terminal end of Dpb3 or Dpb4 in situ allowing their expression at endogenous levels. Fission yeast cells expressing either Dpb3-GFP or Dpb4-GFP were visualized using direct fluorescence microscopy. We observed that both proteins localized to the nucleus as expected for DNA replication proteins (Figure 5A and B). We also observed that in the absence of Dpb4, the localization of Dpb3-GFP did not change (Figure 5C).
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 81 96 77 92 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb3-nmt81-dpb3</italic>
###xml 96 97 92 93 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 277 278 273 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh811f5">5</xref>
###xml 530 531 526 527 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gkh811f5">5</xref>
To visualize the localization of Dpb4 in the absence of Dpb3, we isolated a Deltadpb3-nmt81-dpb3+ strain containing Dpb4-GFP. Addition of thiamine to this strain results in cell cycle arrest within 24-48 h. In the absence of thiamine, Dpb4-GFP localizes to the nucleus (Figure 5D). However, when Dpb3 expression is repressed by the addition of thiamine, Dpb4-GFP is not only found in the nucleus but also appears in the cytoplasm, suggesting that Dpb3 may be important to establish or maintain normal localization of Dpb4 (Figure 5E).
###end p 39
###begin title 40
DISCUSSION
###end title 40
###begin p 41
###xml 133 135 127 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c14">14</xref>
###xml 136 138 130 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c43">43</xref>
###xml 139 141 133 135 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c48">48</xref>
###xml 314 315 302 303 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c9">9</xref>
###xml 316 318 304 306 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c12">12</xref>
###xml 319 321 307 309 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c13">13</xref>
###xml 574 575 550 551 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">1</sub>
###xml 718 719 688 689 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c2">2</xref>
###xml 720 721 690 691 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c5">5</xref>
###xml 722 724 692 694 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c49">49</xref>
###xml 1075 1076 1033 1034 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c6">6</xref>
###xml 358 371 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
Pol epsilon has been implicated in chromosomal DNA replication, DNA repair, transcriptional silencing and sister-chromatid cohesion (14,43-48). Pol epsilon is composed of at least four subunits, two of which, Dpb3 and Dpb4, contain a histone-fold motif suggesting that they may be involved in modifying chromatin (9,12,13). Our own studies of Pol epsilon in fission yeast cells suggest that it is required for the assembly of the DNA replication initiation complex. This conclusion was based on observations that temperature-sensitive mutants of Pol epsilon arrest in late G1 or early S phase. Also, the fact that the catalytic domains of the large catalytic subunit of Pol epsilon are dispensable for cell viability (2,5,49) supports the notion that the C-terminal region of Pol epsilon has a unique function in DNA replication that is not dependent on its ability to synthesize DNA. We have also shown that Dpb2, the second largest subunit of Pol epsilon, binds to the origin-associated chromatin at the beginning of S phase and is required for DNA replication initiation (6).
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 13 25 13 25 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.cerevisiae</italic>
###xml 80 84 74 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB3</italic>
###xml 89 93 83 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 213 215 207 209 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c12">12</xref>
###xml 216 218 210 212 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c13">13</xref>
###xml 233 237 227 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB4</italic>
###xml 267 271 261 265 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">POL2</italic>
###xml 273 278 267 272 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB11</italic>
###xml 280 284 274 278 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB2</italic>
###xml 289 294 283 288 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">RAD53</italic>
###xml 473 475 461 463 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c13">13</xref>
###xml 490 494 478 482 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">DPB3</italic>
###xml 630 632 618 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c12">12</xref>
###xml 7 12 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 13 25 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">S.cerevisiae</span>
In the yeast S.cerevisiae, the two smallest subunits of Pol epsilon, encoded by DPB3 and DPB4, are not essential for viability but loss of these genes does appear to have a negative effect on the DNA replication (12,13). Deletion of DPB4 is synthetically lethal with POL2, DPB11, DPB2 and RAD53 mutants, suggesting that DPB4 has some role in chromosomal replication. It has been hypothesized that DPB4 may be important for maintaining stability of the Pol epsilon complex (13). Deletion of DPB3 results in an increase in the levels of spontaneous mutations, suggesting that DPB3 is required to maintain high replication fidelity (12).
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 355 359 349 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 359 360 353 354 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 496 501 490 495 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc20</italic>
###xml 501 502 495 496 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 530 534 518 522 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cut5</italic>
###xml 534 535 522 523 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 590 594 574 578 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">dpb4</italic>
###xml 637 642 621 626 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">sna41</italic>
###xml 642 643 626 627 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 692 697 676 681 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">cdc21</italic>
###xml 697 698 681 682 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 23 28 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 70 83 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">fission yeast</span>
In contrast to budding yeast, Dpb3 is essential for cell viability in fission yeast. Although we have been unable to detect a specific DNA replication defect in cells depleted of Dpb3, our GST pull-down assays indicate that Dpb3 can interact with both Dpb2 and Dpb4, consistent with it being a subunit of Pol epsilon. On the other hand, cells deleted for dpb4+ are viable, but are synthetically lethal with conditional mutants defective in DNA replication initiation. In addition to mutations in cdc20+ (encoding Pol epsilon) and cut5+ (homologous to DPB11/TopBP1), we also found that Deltadpb4 is synthetically lethal with mutations in sna41+ (encoding a protein homologous to CDC45) and in cdc21+ (encoding Mcm4) consistent with Pol epsilon having a function early during DNA replication initiation.
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 149 157 143 151 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">in vitro</italic>
###xml 240 242 234 236 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c50">50</xref>
###xml 462 464 444 446 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c50">50</xref>
###xml 720 722 692 694 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c51">51</xref>
###xml 723 725 695 697 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c53">53</xref>
###xml 3 9 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
###xml 132 136 <span type="species:ncbi:10633?0.9966199903428296">SV40</span>
###xml 707 711 <span type="species:ncbi:10633?0.9966199903428296">SV40</span>
In humans, it is not known whether Dpb3 or Dpb4 is essential for cell viability or DNA replication. Pol epsilon is not required for SV40 replication in vitro and is not found associated with the replicating viral template during infection (50). However, Pol epsilon is found associated with the replicating host chromosomal DNA, suggesting that Pol epsilon has a unique role in replication of a eukaryotic chromosome that is dispensable for viral DNA synthesis (50). One possibility is that Pol epsilon facilitates the loading of other replication proteins to the site of DNA replication initiation, similar to the role of T antigen in recruiting replication protein A, topoisomerase I and Pol alpha to the SV40 origin (51-53).
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 383 385 383 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gkh811c54">54</xref>
###xml 370 375 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Following depletion of Dpb3 protein, we observed a cell cycle delay that correlated with an increase in the percentage of cells in S phase. We also noticed that a substantial number of cells have two or more nuclei suggesting that cytokinesis might be inhibited when Dpb3 levels decrease. This observation is similar to what is observed following depletion of Orc6 from human cells (54). In the case of the Orc6 experiments, the authors suggested that cytokinesis might be more sensitive to changes in Orc6 protein levels than in DNA replication initiation. Therefore, it is possible that in our experiments, depletion of Dpb3 using the thiamine-repressible promoter may not be sufficient to arrest cells in early S phase but is capable of blocking the later stages of cell separation, resulting in an increase in multinucleate cells.
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 159 166 147 154 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">S.pombe</italic>
###xml 159 166 <span type="species:ncbi:4896">S.pombe</span>
The precise role of Pol epsilon in chromosomal replication still needs to be elucidated. With the cloning of all four subunits of the Pol epsilon complex from S.pombe, it should now be possible to begin a detailed analysis of protein function, and begin to address how this complex interacts with other replication proteins to promote initiation of DNA replication.
###end p 46
