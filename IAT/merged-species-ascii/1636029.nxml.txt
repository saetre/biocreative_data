DGAT1 and ABCG2 polymorphism in Indian cattle (Bos indicus) and buffalo (Bubalus bubalis) breeds

###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 15 26 15 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bos indicus</italic>
###xml 50 65 50 65 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bubalus bubalis</italic>
###xml 7 13 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 15 26 <span type="species:ncbi:9915">Bos indicus</span>
###xml 50 65 <span type="species:ncbi:89462">Bubalus bubalis</span>
###xml 158 164 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 558 564 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
Indian cattle (Bos indicus) and riverine buffalo (Bubalus bubalis) give a poor yield of milk but it has a high fat and protein percentage compared to taurine cattle. The identification of QTLs (Quantitative Trait Loci) on BTA14 and BTA6 and its subsequent fine mapping has led to identification of two non conservative mutations affecting milk production and composition. Our objective was to estimate the frequency of K232A (DGAT1 - diacylglycerol - acyltransferase 1) and Y581S (ABCG2 - ATP binding cassette sub family G member 2) polymorphisms in diverse cattle and buffalo breeds of India having large variation in terms of milk production.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Results
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 92 94 92 94 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K </sup>
###xml 103 105 103 105 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y </sup>
###xml 51 57 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 146 152 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
We screened the reported missense mutations in six cattle and five buffalo breeds. The DGAT1K and ABCG2Y alleles were found to be fixed in Indian cattle and buffalo breeds studied.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusion
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 61 67 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
This study provides an indirect evidence that all the Indian cattle and buffalo breeds have fixed alleles with respect to DGAT1 and ABCG2 genes reported to be responsible for higher milk fat yield, higher fat and protein percent.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Background
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 314 315 314 315 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>
###xml 286 292 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Significant development and refinement of analytical tools has resulted in identification of causal genes underlying QTLs (Quantitative Trait Loci) for various economic traits. is The detection of sequence variations of causal genes affecting the traits of interest are now possible in humans, animals and plants [1].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 27 28 27 28 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>
###xml 29 30 29 30 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>
###xml 440 441 440 441 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>
###xml 540 541 540 541 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 542 543 542 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 561 562 561 562 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B8">8</xref>
###xml 583 584 583 584 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>
###xml 643 653 643 653 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bos taurus</italic>
###xml 19 25 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 489 493 <span type="species:ncbi:10090|species:ncbi:10095">mice</span>
###xml 643 653 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">Bos taurus</span>
Several studies in cattle [2-4] identified a QTL for milk production traits especially the milk fat percentage on chromosome 14 (BTA 14) near the centromeric region. A comparative positional candidate gene approach led to identification of the candidate gene coding acylcoA: diacylglycerol - acyltransferase 1 (DGAT1). DGAT1 is considered to be the key enzyme in controlling the synthesis rate of triglycerides in adipocytes. Smith et al. [5] demonstrated absence of lactation in knockout mice lacking both copies of DGAT1. Grisart et al. [6,7], Winter et al. [8] and Weller et al. [9] identified a polymorphism in exon 8 of the DGAT1 gene in Bos taurus, AA --> GC exchange resulting in a non conservative substitution of amino acid 232 Lysine (K) --> Alanine (A). This polymorphism has been associated with increased fat yield, fat and protein percent as well as decrease in milk and protein.
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 111 122 111 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bos taurus </italic>
###xml 123 125 123 125 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>
###xml 126 128 126 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>
###xml 191 193 191 193 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 345 347 345 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B15">15</xref>
###xml 375 377 375 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 378 380 378 380 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>
###xml 437 439 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 519 521 519 521 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 111 121 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">Bos taurus</span>
Another segregating QTL for milk production trait on BTA 6 was found by many workers in various populations of Bos taurus [10-14]. The QTL centered around microsatellite BM143. Olsen et al. [15] used physical mapping and combined linkage and linkage disequilibrium mapping to fine map QTL region between ABCG2 and LAP3. Several genes like PKD2 [15], SPP1 (Osteopontin) gene [16,17] and ABCG2 (ATP binding cassette sub family G member 2)[18] have been proposed as candidate genes in the QTL region. Cohen-Zinder et al. [18] analysed the sequence variation of these genes and demonstrated that ABCG2 (Y581S) allele (SNP A --> C in exon 14) to be the only polymorphism which corresponded to the segregation status of the heterozygous and homozygous sires for the QTL based on the allele substitution effect.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 52 54 52 54 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K </sup>
###xml 63 65 63 65 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y </sup>
###xml 108 119 108 119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bos taurus </italic>
###xml 283 295 283 295 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bos indicus </italic>
###xml 299 314 299 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bubalus bubalis</italic>
###xml 108 118 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">Bos taurus</span>
###xml 283 294 <span type="species:ncbi:9915">Bos indicus</span>
###xml 299 314 <span type="species:ncbi:89462">Bubalus bubalis</span>
These two QTNs (Quantitative Trait Nucleotide) DGAT1K and ABCG2Y are proposed non conservative mutations in Bos taurus associated with increased fat yield, fat and protein percent as well as decrease in milk yield. A study was carried out to adjudge the status of these mutations in Bos indicus and Bubalus bubalis.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Results and discussion
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 189 200 189 200 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bos indicus</italic>
###xml 227 242 227 242 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bubalus bubalis</italic>
###xml 264 266 264 266 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K </sup>
###xml 279 280 279 280 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>
###xml 514 516 514 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 542 544 542 544 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">K </sup>
###xml 585 587 585 587 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 744 746 744 746 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">A </sup>
###xml 801 803 801 803 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B21">21</xref>
###xml 835 837 835 837 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>
###xml 46 52 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 181 187 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 189 200 <span type="species:ncbi:9915">Bos indicus</span>
###xml 227 242 <span type="species:ncbi:89462">Bubalus bubalis</span>
###xml 455 461 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 562 568 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 646 652 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 703 709 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 793 799 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
The sequence obtained for segment of DGAT1 in cattle (DQ228762) and buffalo (DQ228763); have been submitted to NCBI database. The sequence analysis of DGAT1 region in six breeds of cattle (Bos indicus) and 5 breeds of buffalo (Bubalus bubalis) revealed fixed DGAT1K allele (Fig. 1). The sequence of desired region of control individuals revealed heterozygous condition for reported mutation in 3 animals. No variation was observed in all the 11 breeds of cattle and buffalo undertaken in this study. Kaupe et al, [19] had reported fixed DGAT1K allele in Nellore cattle. Lacorta et al [20] also reported absence of A allele in Nellore and Guzerat cattle and very low frequency (<5%) in Gyr and Redsindhi cattle of Brazil. High frequency of DGAT1A has recently been reported in Uruguayan Creole cattle [21] and Holstein and its crosses [20]. This polymorphism has been associated with increased fat yield, fat and protein percent as well as decrease in milk and protein production.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 186 197 186 197 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bos indicus</italic>
###xml 212 227 212 227 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bubalus bubalis</italic>
###xml 249 251 249 251 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y </sup>
###xml 264 265 264 265 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>
###xml 357 359 357 359 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>
###xml 400 402 400 402 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Y </sup>
###xml 46 52 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 178 184 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 186 197 <span type="species:ncbi:9915">Bos indicus</span>
###xml 212 227 <span type="species:ncbi:89462">Bubalus bubalis</span>
The sequence obtained for ABCG2 (DQ205445 for cattle and DQ205444 for buffalo) have been submitted to NCBI database. The analysis of sequence of ABCG2 region in Indian breeds of cattle (Bos indicus) and buffalo (Bubalus bubalis) revealed fixed ABCG2Y allele (Fig. 2). Only one animal of control group was heterozygous for ABCG2 allele. Cohen-Zinder et al, [18] reported increase in frequency of ABCG2Y allele with selction for higher milk fat and protein percentage in the Israeli Holstein population.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Conclusion
###end title 16
###begin p 17
###xml 61 67 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
This study provides an indirect evidence that all the Indian cattle and buffalo breeds have fixed alleles with respect to DGAT1 and ABCG2 genes reported to be responsible for higher milk fat yield, higher fat and protein percent. There is considerable genetic variation in milk production in India which can be exploited to increase the milk yield without having an adverse effect on fat and protein percent.
###end p 17
###begin title 18
Methods
###end title 18
###begin p 19
###xml 428 438 428 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. taurus </italic>
###xml 440 451 440 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">B. indicus </italic>
###xml 672 674 672 674 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>
###xml 116 122 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">cattle</span>
###xml 428 437 <span type="species:ncbi:9913">B. taurus</span>
###xml 440 450 <span type="species:ncbi:9915">B. indicus</span>
The DNA samples of 20 unrelated animals from Sahiwal, Rathi, Deoni, Tharparkar, Red Kandhari and Punganur breeds of cattle; and Murrah, Jaffarabadi, Surti, Mehsana and Bhadawari breeds of buffalo were isolated. The blood samples were collected over a large area in the native tract of breeds where AI services were not in operation to avoid any relationship among the sampled individuals to the extent possible. Five samples of B. taurus x B. indicus crosses were taken as control. We sequenced the DGAT1 and ABCG2 regions for known mutations by designing primers. The primer sequence for DGAT1 were: forward 5'-GCACCATCCTCTTCCTCAAG-3' and reverse 5'-GGAAGCGCTTTCGGATG-3'[19] and for ABCG2 were: forward 5'-CAGGGCTGTTGGTAAATCTCA-3' (nt 62491-511) and reverse 5'-GCACGGTGACAGATAAGGAGA-3' (nt 62580-600) from NCBI database sequence AJ871176
###end p 19
###begin p 20
These PCR conditions were standardized using gradient PCR (iCycler, Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA, USA). For DGAT1 PCR conditions were 5 min at 95degreesC; 35 cycles 45 s at 95degreesC, 45 s at 57degreesC, 45 s at 72degreesC; and a final 10 min extension at 72degreesC. For ABCG2 touch down PCR profile included 5 min at 95degreesC; 5 cycles 45 s at 95degreesC, 45 s at 57degreesC, 45 s at 72degreesC; 15 cycles 45 s at 95degreesC, 45 s at 56degreesC, 45 s at 72degreesC; 15 cycles 45 s at 95degreesC, 45 s at 55degreesC, 45 s at 72degreesC; and a final 10 min extension at 72degreesC. The PCR products were purified with Exo-AP (New England Biolabs, Beverely, MA, USA) digestion and sequenced with Big Dye terminator chemistry (Applied Biosystems, California, CA, USA) on ABI 3100 Avant sequencer. The sequence analysis was performed using SeqScape software ver. 2.0 (Applied Biosystems, California, CA, USA).
###end p 20
