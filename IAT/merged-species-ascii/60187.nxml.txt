The Arabidopsis thaliana genome contains at least 29 active genes encoding SET domain proteins that can be assigned to four evolutionarily conserved classes
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
###xml 201 211 201 211 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 369 380 369 380 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 415 419 415 419 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E(z)</italic>
###xml 435 438 435 438 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trx</italic>
###xml 454 458 454 458 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ash1</italic>
###xml 492 502 492 502 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Su(var)3-9</italic>
###xml 533 536 533 536 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trx</italic>
###xml 558 562 558 562 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ash1</italic>
###xml 1070 1081 1070 1081 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 1147 1157 1147 1157 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SU(VAR)3-9</italic>
###xml 305 310 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
SET domains are conserved amino acid motifs present in chromosomal proteins that function in epigenetic control of gene expression. These proteins can be divided into four classes as typified by their Drosophila members E(Z), TRX, ASH1 and SU(VAR)3-9. Homologs of all four classes have been identified in yeast and mammals, but not in plants. A BLASTP screening of the Arabidopsis genome identified 37 genes: three E(z) homologs, five trx homologs, four ash1 homologs and 15 genes similar to Su(var)3-9. Seven genes were assigned as trx-related and three as ash1-related. Only four genes have been described previously. Our classification is based on the characteristics of the SET domains, cysteine-rich regions and additional conserved domains, including a novel YGD domain. RT-PCR analysis, cDNA cloning and matching ESTs show that at least 29 of the genes are active in diverse tissues. The high number of SET domain genes, possibly involved in epigenetic control of gene activity during plant development, can partly be explained by extensive genome duplication in Arabidopsis. Additionally, the lack of introns in the coding region of eight SU(VAR)3-9 class genes indicates evolution of new genes by retrotransposition. The identification of putative nuclear localization signals and AT-hooks in many of the proteins supports an anticipated nuclear localization, which was demonstrated for selected proteins.

###begin title 3
INTRODUCTION
###end title 3
###begin p 4
###xml 442 465 442 465 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila melanogaster</italic>
###xml 467 468 467 468 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c1">1</xref>
###xml 469 470 469 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c3">3</xref>
###xml 637 638 637 638 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c4">4</xref>
###xml 345 350 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 442 465 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">Drosophila melanogaster</span>
Gene expression in eukaryotes depends on both intrinsic regulatory mechanisms, including enhancer-promoter interactions, and chromosomal context, including chromatin structure. Chromatin silencing mechanisms are involved in X chromosome inactivation, genomic imprinting, developmental control of homeotic genes, silencing of mating type loci in yeast and heterochromatin-induced gene silencing, known as position-effect variegation (PEV), in Drosophila melanogaster (1-3). In addition, segregation of chromosomes during cell division and telomere and centromere function is dependent on the correct higher order chromatin structure (see 4).
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 175 185 175 185 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 207 210 207 210 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">PcG</italic>
###xml 278 282 278 282 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trxG</italic>
###xml 382 383 382 383 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c5">5</xref>
###xml 450 456 450 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E(var)</italic>
###xml 471 478 471 478 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Su(var)</italic>
###xml 485 486 485 486 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c6">6</xref>
 An understanding of the mechanisms governing modulation of chromatin structure is emerging from the identification of genes encoding proteins forming chromatin complexes. In Drosophila, the polycomb group (PcG) of genes maintains a repressive state, while the trithorax group (trxG) of genes preserves the activity of homeotic genes in appropriate segments throughout development (5). About 120 loci have been identified in which mutations enhance, E(var), or suppress, Su(var), PEV (6).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 102 103 102 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c7">7</xref>
###xml 199 200 199 200 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c5">5</xref>
###xml 201 202 201 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c8">8</xref>
###xml 203 204 203 204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c9">9</xref>
###xml 344 354 344 354 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Su(var)3-9</italic>
###xml 356 373 356 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Enhancer of zeste</italic>
###xml 375 379 375 379 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E(z)</italic>
###xml 385 394 385 394 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trithorax</italic>
###xml 396 399 396 399 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trx</italic>
 Chromatin-modulating proteins are thought to be involved in multimeric protein-protein interactions (7) and contain characteristic amino acid motifs, e.g. a chromo domain, PHD finger or SET domain (5,8,9). The SET domain, a 130-160 amino acid motif, is found in proteins that are members of PcG, trxG and SU(VAR) and was named after the genes Su(var)3-9, Enhancer of zeste [E(z)] and trithorax (trx).
###end p 6
###begin p 7
###xml 78 79 78 79 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c5">5</xref>
###xml 80 82 80 82 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c10">10</xref>
###xml 152 156 152 156 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E(Z)</italic>
###xml 171 181 171 181 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CURLY LEAF</italic>
###xml 183 186 183 186 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CLF</italic>
###xml 212 223 212 223 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 223 231 223 231 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">thaliana</italic>
###xml 309 311 309 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c11">11</xref>
###xml 314 319 314 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MEDEA</italic>
###xml 321 324 321 324 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MEA</italic>
###xml 348 392 348 392 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FERTILIZATION INDEPENDENT SEED DEVELOPMENT 1</italic>
###xml 394 398 394 398 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">FIS1</italic>
###xml 475 477 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c12">12</xref>
###xml 478 480 478 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c13">13</xref>
###xml 538 540 538 540 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c12">12</xref>
###xml 541 543 541 543 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c14">14</xref>
###xml 769 771 769 771 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c15">15</xref>
###xml 830 832 830 832 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c15">15</xref>
###xml 833 835 833 835 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c16">16</xref>
###xml 861 863 861 863 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c17">17</xref>
###xml 915 919 915 919 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E(z)</italic>
###xml 1026 1028 1026 1028 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c18">18</xref>
###xml 55 60 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 591 596 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 601 606 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
 Homologs of these three genes have been identified in yeast and mammals (see 5,10). The first plant genes identified encoding SET domain proteins were E(Z) homologs. The CURLY LEAF (CLF) gene of the model plant Arabidopsisthaliana is involved in the control of leaf and flower morphology and flowering time (11). MEDEA (MEA), alternatively called FERTILIZATION INDEPENDENT SEED DEVELOPMENT 1 (FIS1), is an inhibitor of endosperm development in the absence of fertilization (12,13) and is also implicated in imprinting of paternal genes (12,14). The CLF and MEA proteins, as well as the two mouse and human homologs of E(Z) share similar amino acid compositions over the length of the proteins and are particularly similar in a cysteine-rich region and the SET domain (15). The involvement in chromatin-dependent gene regulation (15,16), the influences on PEV (17) and decondensation of chromatin structure in some E(z) mutants, indicate that E(Z) class proteins play a major role in maintaining the integrity of chromosomes (18).
###end p 7
###begin p 8
###xml 24 34 24 34 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Su(var)3-9</italic>
###xml 164 165 164 165 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c4">4</xref>
###xml 166 167 166 167 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c8">8</xref>
###xml 168 170 168 170 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c19">19</xref>
###xml 306 308 306 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c20">20</xref>
###xml 309 311 309 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c22">22</xref>
###xml 533 534 533 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c4">4</xref>
###xml 43 48 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 50 54 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">CLR4</span>
###xml 57 62 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 77 82 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 177 182 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 187 192 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 205 209 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">CLR4</span>
###xml 218 223 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
 The protein encoded by Su(var)3-9 and its yeast (CLR4), human (SUV39H1) and mouse (Suv39h1) homologs, presumably have a key function in heterochromatin packaging (4,8,19). The human and mouse SUVAR39 and CLR4 and the human G9a proteins have been shown to selectively transfer a methyl group to histone 3 (20-22). Mutations in the SET domain abolish methyltransferase activity. In addition to the SET domain, these proteins have a conserved chromo domain in the N-terminal part and a cysteine-rich region adjacent to the SET domain (4).
###end p 8
###begin p 9
###xml 5 15 5 15 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 253 255 253 255 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c23">23</xref>
###xml 256 258 256 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c25">25</xref>
###xml 385 387 385 387 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c25">25</xref>
###xml 388 390 388 390 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c26">26</xref>
###xml 407 418 407 418 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 418 421 418 421 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trx</italic>
###xml 432 436 432 436 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ATX1</italic>
###xml 441 445 441 445 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ATX2</italic>
###xml 553 555 553 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c27">27</xref>
###xml 567 577 567 577 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 595 628 595 628 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">absent, small or homeotic discs 1</italic>
###xml 630 634 630 634 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ash1</italic>
###xml 665 669 665 669 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trxG</italic>
###xml 775 777 775 777 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c28">28</xref>
###xml 904 906 904 906 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c10">10</xref>
###xml 907 909 907 909 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c28">28</xref>
###xml 910 912 910 912 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c29">29</xref>
###xml 36 41 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 46 51 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 753 758 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
 The Drosophila TRX protein and its human and mouse homologs (ALL-1/All-1, also called MLL or HRX) share high similarity in the C-terminal SET domain and in the central part of the protein, where PHD fingers and an extended PHD finger (ePHD) are found (23-25). These fingers have unique Cys-His-Cys patterns similar to zinc fingers and may be involved in protein-protein interactions (25,26). Recently, two Arabidopsistrx homologs, ATX1 and ATX2, were identified and a new domain associated with SET in trithorax (DAST) class of proteins was described (27). A fourth Drosophila SET domain gene, absent, small or homeotic discs 1 (ash1), can also be classified as a trxG gene, and its encoded protein contains a PHD finger. This is also the case for the human homolog huASH1 (28). The SET domain of the ASH1 class proteins is not localized in the C-terminus, but rather in the middle part of the protein (10,28,29).
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 322 324 322 324 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c30">30</xref>
###xml 325 327 325 327 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c31">31</xref>
###xml 352 356 352 356 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E(z)</italic>
###xml 493 504 493 504 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 526 528 526 528 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c32">32</xref>
###xml 632 643 632 643 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
 The four classes of SET domain genes are evolutionarily conserved in the animal kingdom and they play important functions in epigenetic control of gene expression and chromatin packaging. Studies of (trans)gene silencing have given a clear indication of the importance of epigenetic control of gene expression in plants (30,31). Given the presence of E(z) class genes in plants, we expected that SET domain genes of the other classes would also be present. Since the complete sequence of the Arabidopsis genome is available (32), we chose to take a bioinformatics approach to identify such genes. In the present paper we show that Arabidopsis has more than 30 such genes and that they can be grouped into four distinct classes, based on the characteristics of the SET domains and cysteine-rich regions of E(Z), TRX, ASH1 and SU(VAR)3-9 and other associated domains. Our characterization of the expression patterns of these genes by RT-PCR indicates a wide, but spatially and temporally differential, distribution of their transcripts during plant development. At least 29 of the putative genes are expressed. Nuclear localization was demonstrated for selected proteins. The high number of genes and their diverse expression patterns may reflect a high complexity of epigenetic control of gene activity during plant development.
###end p 10
###begin title 11
MATERIALS AND METHODS
###end title 11
###begin title 12
Bioinformatics tools
###end title 12
###begin p 13
Database searches were performed using BLASTP and TBLASTX (). Multiple alignments of protein sequences were done with the ClustalX program () and manually adjusted with the GeneDoc program (). Proteins lacking vital conserved residues were excluded from the alignment. In cases where the annotations in the EMBL () and MIPS () databases deviated, gene predictions were controlled using GENSCAN (), GeneMark () and Gene finder. Protein domains were identified using the programs RPS-BLAST and PSI-BLAST (NCBI), ProfileScan () and PROSEARCH (MIPS) searching the Pfam-A, Prosite profiles and Smart databases. BAC clone positions were determined using MapViewer (). Gene duplications were investigated using the MIPS Interactive Redundancy Viewer ().
###end p 13
###begin title 14
RNA isolation and RT expression analyses
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 334 345 318 329 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
Total RNA was isolated using Trizol reagent (Gibco BRL) as described by the manufacturer. For the SUVH group, where most genes are intronless in the coding region, reverse transcription was carried out on total RNA using M-MLV reverse transcriptase (RT). First strand cDNA was made from approximately1 microg total RNA from different Arabidopsis tissues (seeds, roots, leaves, stem, floral buds, inflorescences and green siliques) which had been treated with DNase I (Boehringer Mannheim). The RNA was incubated at 37degreesC for 1 h in 10 mM each dNTP, 100 pmol random hexamers (Promega) and 200 U M-MLV RT (Gibco BRL), in a total volume of 20 microl, followed by incubation in 0.2 mM NaOH for 1 h. After precipitation and dilution in 20 microl, 1 microl of the reaction was used for each PCR. PCR was carried out under standard conditions using 8 pmol of each gene-specific primer and 35 cycles of 95degreesC for 30 s, 50-62degreesC for 30 s and 72degreesC for 30-60 s in a Robocycler Gradient 96 (Stratagene). Products were separated on 1.0% agarose gels and revealed by ethidium bromide staining.
###end p 15
###begin p 16
###xml 55 66 55 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 280 283 <span type="species:ncbi:11866?0.5863095238095238|species:ncbi:12321?0.21726190476190477">AMV</span>
 For genes of the other groups, mRNA was isolated from Arabidopsis tissues using magnetic oligo(dT) beads (GenoPrep mRNA beads; GenoVision, Norway) according to the manufacturer's instructions. The extracted mRNA, bound to the beads, was used for first strand cDNA synthesis with AMV RT. For each tissue a control reaction was run without RT. PCR using the control reaction as template would reveal DNA contamination in the mRNA. For RT-PCR, 5% of the generated first strand cDNA, and a corresponding amount from the control reaction, was used as template. Specific primers annealing to different exons were designed for each putative gene. RT-PCR products were sequenced using a MegaBACE 1000 sequencer.
###end p 16
###begin p 17
###xml 5 10 5 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH4</italic>
###xml 163 174 158 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
 For SUVH4 a gene-specific PCR primer pair fitting the SET domain region was used to clone a genomic gene fragment. This fragment was used to screen a lambdaZapII Arabidopsis cDNA library. Two nearly identical cDNA clones of approximately1 kb length were isolated. GENESCAN software and oligonucleotides for RT-PCR were used to lengthen the cDNA sequence up to a consensus start side immediately downstream of an in-frame stop.
###end p 17
###begin p 18
 Primer sequences used for the above purposes will be provided on request.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
5'-RACE and 3'-RACE
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 130 131 130 131 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
###xml 141 152 141 152 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
The 5'-RACE was done using 5'RACE Kit version 2 (Life Technologies) according the protocol of the manufacturer with 200 ng poly(A)+ RNA from Arabidopsis leaves, purified using a mRNA purification kit (Pharmacia). First strand cDNA synthesis was primed with a gene-specific primer. After second strand synthesis, the cDNA was amplified using an anchor oligo and gene-specific primers. 5'-RACE amplification was performed with a nested gene-specific primer.
###end p 20
###begin p 21
###xml 47 48 47 48 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">+</sup>
 The 3'-RACE was performed using 200 ng poly(A)+ RNA from leaves and 200 U M-MLV RT (Gibco BRL). First strand cDNA synthesis was primed with a poly(T) primer with anchor sequences. After second strand synthesis, the cDNA was amplified using an anchor oligo and gene-specific primers. 3'-RACE amplification was performed with a nested gene-specific primer. Lists of used primers are available on request.
###end p 21
###begin p 22
 The resultant PCR products from both 5'- and 3'-RACE analysis were gel-eluted and directly sequenced using a cycle sequencing protocol (Perkin Elmer) and analyzed using an ABI377 sequencer.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
Nuclear localization assay
###end title 23
###begin p 24
###xml 57 59 57 59 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c33">33</xref>
###xml 91 93 91 93 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c34">34</xref>
###xml 315 317 315 317 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c35">35</xref>
###xml 522 525 522 525 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bam</italic>
###xml 532 535 532 535 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sst</italic>
###xml 557 560 557 560 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Bam</italic>
###xml 567 570 567 570 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Sst</italic>
###xml 4 9 <span type="species:ncbi:4679">onion</span>
For onion epidermis assays we used the vector pKEx4tr-G (33) containing the 35S* promoter (34) and a fusion between the N-terminal ORF of GUS and the full-length ORF of one of the tested proteins at the C-terminus. For plant GFP fusions, we used plasmid CD3-327 (kindly provided by ABRC Stock Center) as described (35) to amplify smRSGFP (GenBank accession no. U70495) by PCR using primers 5'-GGA TCC CGC ATG AGT AAA GGA GAA G-3' and 5'-CTC GAG GAG CTC TTA TTT GTA TAG TTC ATC CAT GC-3'. The PCR product was digested with BamHI and SstI and cloned into the BamHI and SstI sites of the vector pKEx4tr-G to exchange the GUS ORF. The resulting vector was called pEKx-327.
###end p 24
###begin p 25
###xml 143 145 143 145 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c36">36</xref>
###xml 768 769 740 741 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 875 876 844 845 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">d</sc>
###xml 61 66 <span type="species:ncbi:4679">onion</span>
###xml 194 200 <span type="species:ncbi:4679">onions</span>
 GUS and GFP fusion constructs were transiently expressed in onion epidermal cells using a Vacuumbrand Heliumgun 461 essentially as described (36). Briefly, inner epidermal layers obtained from onions (purchased at a local market) were placed on MS basal medium with 30 g/l sucrose, 2% agar, 2.5 microg/ml amphotericin B (Sigma) and 5 microg/ml chloramphenicol. DNA-coated gold particles (1.6 microm gold; Bio-Rad) were briefly vortexed before bombardment. Purified plasmid DNA (0.8 microg) was bombarded onto each sample at a pressure of 700 kPa and a target distance of 9 cm. Petri dishes were sealed with parafilm and incubated for 4-24 h at 28degreesC in the dark. GUS activity was determined by histochemical staining at 37degreesC in X-glcUA solution (50 mM NaPO4 pH 7.0, 0.5 mM sodium ferro/ferricyanide, 0.05% Triton X-100 and 3 mg/ml 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indoxyl-beta-d-glucuronic acid, cyclohexylammonium salt; Duchefa). GFP fluorescence was determined by FITC-filtered visual inspection under a laser scanning microscope (Zeiss).

The SET domains from the proteins encoded by the Drosophila genes E(z), trx, Su(var)3-9 and ash1 were used for BLASTP and TBLASTX searches against the Arabidopsis non-redundant sequence databases. Proteins encoded by putative genes recognized by the hits in the BLAST searches were identified from the annotations in the databases. ###begin title 40
Distinct cysteine-rich domains are present in the four classes of AtSET proteins
###end title 40
###begin p 41
After assignment of the identified proteins to different classes based on their SET domains, characteristics of the other parts of the proteins were investigated by comparison to the homologs of other species and searches in the conserved domain databases Smart, Pfam-A and Profile prosite.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 152 154 152 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c10">10</xref>
###xml 161 162 161 162 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 527 528 527 528 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
 In addition to distinct differences in the amino acid sequence of the SET domain, the four classes of proteins have other significant characteristics (10; Fig. 3). Proteins of the E(Z) class have a region with 16-18 cysteine residues spaced in a given pattern in front of the C-terminal SET domain. Proteins of the SU(VAR)3-9 class have a SET domain-associated cysteine-rich region (SAC) with seven to eight cysteines in certain positions in front of the SET domain (N-SAC) and three C-terminal cysteines in the pattern CXC(X)4C (C-SAC) after the SET domain. The C-SAC is also found in TRX class proteins, which lack a cysteine-rich region N-terminal to the SET domain. ASH1 class proteins have, in contrast to the other three classes, the SET domain centrally placed. Their SET domains are preceded by a cysteine-rich region and followed by the C-SAC pattern. The number and spacing of the cysteine residues in the N-terminal cysteine region differ from that of the E(Z) C-rich region and also from the N-SAC.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 90 92 90 92 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c11">11</xref>
###xml 93 95 93 95 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c12">12</xref>
###xml 252 253 252 253 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f1">1</xref>
###xml 258 259 258 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 322 326 322 326 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">EZA1</italic>
###xml 333 334 333 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f1">1</xref>
###xml 339 340 339 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
 The similarities between the E(Z), MEA and CLF proteins have been recognized previously (11,12). In addition to the SET domain, these proteins have a C-rich stretch and the so-called domain II in common with the E(Z) proteins of other organisms (Figs 1 and 3). The C-rich region is also present in the protein encoded by EZA1 (Figs 1 and 3).
###end p 43
###begin p 44
###xml 127 128 127 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f1">1</xref>
###xml 133 134 133 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 250 251 250 251 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f1">1</xref>
###xml 256 257 256 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>

###xml 361 362 361 362 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
 All the ATX proteins have a complete C-SAC motif, while the ATXR proteins lack at least one of the C-terminal cysteines (Figs 1 and 3). All four ASHH proteins have the C-SAC motif and the cysteine-rich domain conforming to ASH1 class proteins (Figs 1 and 3). The ASHR proteins lack the C-rich regions with the exception of the C-SAC motif of ASHR3 (Figs 1 and 3).
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 69 70 69 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f1">1</xref>
###xml 75 76 75 76 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
 A complete SAC domain is present in 10 SUVH and SUVR proteins (Figs 1 and 3). Truncated N-SAC regions are present in SUVR3, SUVR5 and SUVH10, while SUVH2 and SUVH9 only have one C-terminal cysteine residue.

###begin title 46
A novel domain called the YDG domain is present in SUVH proteins
###end title 46
###begin p 47
###xml 230 241 230 241 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 285 296 285 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Deinococcus</italic>
###xml 296 307 296 307 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">radiodurans</italic>
###xml 315 316 315 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f4">4</xref>
###xml 563 564 563 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f4">4</xref>
Alignments of the N-terminal part of the SUVH proteins and the use of domain-finder programs revealed that these 10 proteins contain a conserved domain also found in non-SET proteins containing a RING finger motif (in mammals and Arabidopsis) or an HNH nuclease motif (in the bacteria Deinococcusradiodurans) (Fig. 4A). We have chosen to call the 150-170 amino acid long region the YDG domain because of a characteristic YDG motif. Further characteristics of the domain are the conservation of up to 13 evenly spaced glycine residues and a VRV(I/V)RG motif (Fig. 4A).

###end title 48
###begin p 49

###xml 336 338 336 338 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c38">38</xref>
###xml 624 625 612 613 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f4">4</xref>
###xml 64 69 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
In all the ATX proteins the PWWP motif, first identified in the human protein WHSC1 (38), was found (Fig. 4C). WHSC1 is most closely related to the ASH1 class of SET domain proteins, but we did not identify this domain in any of the Arabidopsis ASHH or ASHR proteins. The PWWP domain is present in a diverse groups of nuclear proteins (38) and typically has conserved PWWP residues. The first proline residue is present in three of the five ATX proteins, but none of them contain the first of the two tryptophans. The most conserved motifs are GDPhiPhiWXK (where Phi are hydrophobic residues), WPAPhiPhiPhiD and VXFFG (Fig. 4C).
###end p 49
###begin p 50
###xml 130 131 130 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f1">1</xref>
###xml 136 137 136 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f4">4</xref>
###xml 153 163 153 163 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 235 237 235 237 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c26">26</xref>
###xml 260 261 260 261 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">4</sub>
###xml 265 266 265 266 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">3</sub>
###xml 307 318 307 318 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
 In four of the five ATX proteins, as well as in ATXR5 and ATXR6, we identified amino acid motifs similar to the PHD finger (Figs 1 and 4B) found in the Drosophila and mammalian TRX/HXR proteins and a number of other nuclear proteins (26). The characteristic C4-H-C3 pattern is present once or twice in the Arabidopsis proteins. In the ATX proteins the PHD fingers are situated about midway between the PWWP motif and the SET domain.
###end p 50
###begin p 51
###xml 26 28 26 28 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c25">25</xref>
###xml 108 109 108 109 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f4">4</xref>
 Finally, the ePHD motif (25) was found in all the ATX proteins, positioned just after the PHD finger (Fig. 4D). The second half of this motif resembles a PHD finger (compare conserved cysteine and histidine residues).
###end p 51
###begin p 52
###xml 64 66 64 66 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c27">27</xref>
###xml 59 62 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">HRX</span>
 The DAST motif recently identified in ATX1, ATX2, TRX and HRX (27) was not found in the other ATX proteins.
###end p 52
###begin title 53
Putative nuclear localization signals and AT-hooks are found in many AtSET proteins
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 102 103 102 103 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f1">1</xref>
The domain-finder programs recognized putative bipartite nuclear localization signals (NLSs; see Fig. 1) in MEA and CLF, but not in EZA1. Among the ATX and ATXR proteins one or two such NLSs were identified in all proteins but four (ATX1, ATX4, ATXR2 and ATXR4). This signal was also found in three proteins of the ASH groups (ASHH2, ASHH4 and ASHR3) and three proteins in the SUV groups (SUVH6, SUVH4 and SUVR1). We cannot exclude the possibility that other types of NLSs are present in the other proteins.

 Putative AT-hooks, which mediate protein binding to the minor groove of AT-rich tracts in DNA (39), were identified in three of the SUVH proteins, in ASHH1 and in ATXR7 (Figs 1 and 4E). This motif has a characteristic GRP core.

###begin p 63

As mentioned above, the cDNAs from the genes in the E(z) class and recently also two ATX genes have been cloned by others (11,12,27). RT-PCR and cDNA cloning were used to verify expression of additional AtSET genes. cDNAs for SUVH1 and SUVH5 (Fig. 5A), and SUVH7 and SUVH9 (not shown) confirmed these genes as being intronless and showed that an annotated intron in the genomic region corresponding to SUVH7 (F2H15.1) is not spliced out. ###begin p 64
###xml 5 10 5 10 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH2</italic>
###xml 155 156 155 156 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f5">5</xref>

However, in the leader sequence of the SUVH3 gene there are two introns of 464 and of 111 bp (Fig. 5A).

###xml 902 907 <span type="species:ncbi:4679">onion</span>
To show that the gene products of the SET domain coding transcripts are nuclear proteins, as already shown for all functionally described SET domain proteins to date (see for example 4,10), transient expression assays were used (34) (see Materials and Methods). Constructs containing an in-frame fusion of the GUS gene, or a red-shifted GFP gene, and a cDNA encoding CLF, SUVH1, SUVH2 or SUVH3 in a transient expression vector, were shot into the inner epidermis of onions using a particle gun. Whereas the GUS protein alone is not localized to the nucleus, all of the fusion protein variants became concentrated in the nucleus (data not shown). The transiently expressed GFP fusions confirmed that nuclear transport was not an artifact of the test system: in contrast to GFP alone, all proteins became concentrated in the nucleus (Fig. 6A-E). Drosophila SU(VAR)3-9 also showed nuclear localization in onion cells (Fig. 6F).
###end p 70
###begin title 71
DISCUSSION
###end title 71
###begin title 72
###xml 0 11 0 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
Arabidopsis has at least 29 expressed SET domain genes
###end title 72
###begin p 73
###xml 51 62 51 62 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 147 148 147 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f1">1</xref>
###xml 333 334 333 334 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gke577tb1">1</xref>
###xml 401 406 401 406 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH8</italic>
###xml 408 413 408 413 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVR5</italic>
###xml 418 424 418 424 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH10</italic>
Our search has identified 30 putative genes in the Arabidopsis genome containing a conserved SET domain and seven with truncated SET domains (Fig. 1). At least 29 of these are expressed genes, as demonstrated by the cloning of cDNAs for 15 genes, RT-PCR products for 11 genes and/or the presence of matching ESTs for 18 genes (Table 1). We failed to amplify RT-PCR products for three potential genes, SUVH8, SUVR5 and SUVH10. Despite the long open reading frames, and for SUVH8 intactness of all conserved domains, these genes may represent pseudogenes. Alternatively, their expression may be very low or restricted to untested tissues and developmental stages.
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 0 6 0 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"> SUVR5</italic>
###xml 11 17 11 17 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH10</italic>
###xml 113 114 113 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 183 184 183 184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f5">5</xref>
###xml 195 196 195 196 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gke577tb2">2</xref>
###xml 199 204 199 204 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVR3</italic>
###xml 243 248 243 248 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVR4</italic>
###xml 314 319 314 319 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ATXR1</italic>
###xml 320 325 320 325 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ATXR4</italic>
###xml 330 335 330 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ASHR2</italic>
###xml 377 378 377 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gke577tb1">1</xref>
 SUVR5 and SUVH10 seem to have suffered deletions affecting the regions encoding the N-SAC and SET domains (Fig. 3). Other genes encoding divergent proteins are not pseudogenes (Fig. 5 and Table 2). SUVR3 mRNA is present in buds and seeds and SUVR4 is expressed in all tissues tested. The presence of ESTs for the ATXR1-ATXR4 and ASHR2 genes confirm their active status (Table 1).
###end p 74
###begin p 75
###xml 0 12 0 12 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"> Arabidopsis</italic>
 Arabidopsis has a remarkably high number of active genes encoding SET domain proteins compared to the number known in other organisms.
###end p 75
###begin title 76
###xml 4 9 4 9 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AtSET</italic>
The AtSET genes are expressed in a diversity of tissues
###end title 76
###begin p 77
###xml 0 7 0 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In situ</italic>
###xml 41 44 41 44 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CLF</italic>
###xml 130 132 130 132 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c11">11</xref>
###xml 135 142 135 142 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">In situ</italic>
###xml 188 191 188 191 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MEA</italic>
###xml 268 270 268 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c13">13</xref>
###xml 271 273 271 273 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c14">14</xref>
###xml 656 657 656 657 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f5">5</xref>
###xml 668 669 668 669 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gke577tb2">2</xref>
###xml 913 914 913 914 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gke577tb2">2</xref>
In situ hybridization studies have shown CLF to be expressed in leaves, vasculature and meristem, as well as in buds and flowers (11). In situ and promoter-GUS expression studies revealed MEA expression restricted to the female gametophyte and young developing seeds (13,14). We have used RT-PCR to gain a first overview of the expression patterns of the novel SET domain genes analyzed. This method does not allow a quantitative comparison of expression levels between tissues, but demonstrates that each of the 20 active genes tested is expressed in more than one tissue, that all are expressed in floral buds and the majority in flowers and seeds (Fig. 5 and Table 2). For only a few genes were RT-PCR products generated from roots. A confirmation of this general expression pattern is that the matching ESTs were derived from cDNA libraries made from inflorescences, developing seeds and aerial organs (Table 2).
###end p 77
###begin title 78
The AtSET proteins can be assigned to evolutionarily conserved classes
###end title 78
###begin p 79
###xml 153 154 153 154 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f2">2</xref>
###xml 246 256 246 256 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 261 272 261 272 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 288 293 288 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">E(z) </italic>
###xml 311 314 311 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trx</italic>
###xml 330 334 330 334 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ash1</italic>
###xml 368 378 368 378 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Su(var)3-9</italic>
###xml 427 430 427 430 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trx</italic>
###xml 452 456 452 456 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ash1</italic>
###xml 471 472 471 472 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f1">1</xref>
We have investigated the relationship between the AtSET proteins and their homologs in other organisms. The tree based on alignment of SET domains (Fig. 2) indicates that these genes fall into the four classes which were previously recognized in Drosophila. In Arabidopsis we found three E(z) class genes, five trx homologs, four ash1 homologs and 15 genes similar to Su(var)3-9. In addition, seven genes have been assigned as trx-related and three as ash1-related (Fig. 1).
###end p 79
###begin p 80
###xml 63 74 63 74 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 251 252 251 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 433 434 433 434 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f2">2</xref>
###xml 439 440 439 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 368 373 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
 The significant similarity between the SET domain proteins of Arabidopsis and their counterparts in other organisms is evident not only from the SET domains but also from the upstream cysteine-rich regions in the E(Z), ASH1 and SU(VAR) classes (Fig. 3). The C-SAC is found downstream of the SET domain in all ATX, ASHH, SUVH and SUVR proteins, as in their animal and yeast counterparts, with the exceptions of SUVH2 and SUVH9 (Figs 2 and 3).
###end p 80
###begin p 81
###xml 111 122 111 122 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 293 294 293 294 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f2">2</xref>
###xml 299 300 299 300 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f4">4</xref>
 The classification is further substantiated by the internal class similarities in the N-terminal parts of the Arabidopsis proteins, notably the common domain II in the E(Z) proteins, the PHD fingers and PWWP and ePHD motifs in the ATX proteins, and the YGD domain in the SUVH proteins, (Figs 2 and 4).
###end p 81
###begin title 82
###xml 88 99 88 99 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
Two different mechanisms may have contributed to the high number of SET domain genes in Arabidopsis
###end title 82
###begin p 83
###xml 46 57 46 57 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 142 153 142 153 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 211 213 211 213 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c32">32</xref>
###xml 235 246 235 246 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 393 395 393 395 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c32">32</xref>
Analyses of sequenced genomes have shown that Arabidopsis in general has more gene families with many members compared to other organisms. In Arabidopsis, 37.4% of the gene families have more than five members (32). It is assumed that Arabidopsis had a tetraploid ancestor and that segments have been lost gradually, resulting in the present day genome where duplicated regions encompass 60% (32).
###end p 83
###begin p 84
###xml 103 104 103 104 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gke577tb1">1</xref>
###xml 387 388 387 388 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f2">2</xref>
###xml 400 404 400 404 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">EZA1</italic>
###xml 409 412 409 412 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">MEA</italic>
###xml 537 540 537 540 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CLF</italic>
###xml 586 590 586 590 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">EZA1</italic>
###xml 602 607 602 607 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH3</italic>
###xml 612 617 612 617 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH7</italic>
###xml 801 802 801 802 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f2">2</xref>
###xml 882 887 882 887 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH7</italic>
###xml 892 897 892 897 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH8</italic>
###xml 1083 1088 1083 1088 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH8</italic>
###xml 1114 1119 1114 1119 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVR1</italic>
###xml 1124 1129 1124 1129 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVR2</italic>
###xml 1276 1281 1276 1281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AtSET</italic>
###xml 1376 1378 1376 1378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c40">40</xref>
###xml 1425 1427 1425 1427 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c41">41</xref>
 We found five pairs of SET domain genes localized in five different large genomic duplications (Table 1). The members of the duplicated pairs are highly similar along their whole lengths and the majority of the introns and their positions are conserved. The members of the two ATX pairs and the ASHH pair group closely together when aligning SET domains of all the AtSET proteins (Fig. 2). Although EZA1 and MEA reside in duplicated regions, judged from amino acid similarity EZA1 is more closely related to CLF than to MEA. Therefore, CLF is likely to be a more recent duplication of EZA1. Likewise, SUVH3 and SUVH7 may represent a pair of a large duplication from which new genes have been generated, since SUVH3 seems more closely related to SUVH1 and SUVH7 is more closely related to SUVH8 (Fig. 2). It is remarkable that the significant nucleotide sequence similarity between SUVH7 and SUVH8 continues upstream and downstream of the coding regions of both ORFs. This homology also exceeds the 23 nt long poly(A) stretch which is found 73 bp downstream of the stop codon of the SUVH8 ORF. The hypothesis that SUVR1 and SUVR2 have arisen from a duplication event is likewise substantiated by the presence of highly similar ORFs upstream of both genes. The multiplicity of AtSET genes is not necessarily surprising, since gene duplications occur at a relatively high rate (40), especially in angiosperms (see for instance 41).
###end p 84
###begin p 85
###xml 21 26 21 26 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AtSET</italic>
###xml 130 131 130 131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="table" rid="gke577tb1">1</xref>
###xml 161 165 161 165 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH</italic>
###xml 223 228 223 228 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH4</italic>
###xml 288 293 288 293 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH4</italic>
###xml 339 343 339 343 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH</italic>
###xml 532 537 532 537 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH2</italic>
###xml 542 547 542 547 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH3</italic>
###xml 666 667 666 667 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f5">5</xref>
###xml 701 706 701 706 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH2</italic>
###xml 711 716 711 716 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH3</italic>
 The majority of the AtSET genes contain a large number of introns, both in the SET domain itself and the rest of the gene (Table 1). In sharp contrast, all the SUVH genes are intronless in their coding regions, except for SUVH4. This suggests at least one retrotransposition event of an SUVH4-like gene transcript during evolution of the SUVH gene group. A promoter must accidentally have been recruited or evolved 5' of this retrotransposition(s). Interestingly, introns are found in the transcribed but non-coding regions of the SUVH2 and SUVH3 genes, as is evident from the different sizes of the RT-PCR and genomic fragments generated by the same primers (Fig. 5A). The intron-containing UTRs of SUVH2 and SUVH3 may stem from the insertion point rather that from the reverse transcribed mRNA. Another possibility could be that these introns have evolved or have been inserted after generation of the new genes.
###end p 85
###begin p 86
###xml 204 206 204 206 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c42">42</xref>
###xml 258 259 258 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c3">3</xref>
###xml 281 283 281 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c43">43</xref>
###xml 345 347 345 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c44">44</xref>
###xml 383 385 383 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c45">45</xref>
###xml 437 439 437 439 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c46">46</xref>
###xml 452 461 452 461 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">U2af1-rs1</italic>
###xml 468 470 468 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c47">47</xref>
###xml 536 538 532 534 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c48">48</xref>
###xml 539 541 535 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c49">49</xref>
###xml 559 563 551 555 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CP-1</italic>
###xml 631 635 619 623 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CP-2</italic>
###xml 744 748 728 732 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CP-1</italic>
###xml 814 816 798 800 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c49">49</xref>
###xml 896 898 880 882 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c50">50</xref>
###xml 1303 1305 1287 1289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c51">51</xref>
###xml 1454 1456 1438 1440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c52">52</xref>
###xml 87 92 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 430 435 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 446 451 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 548 553 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 905 910 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 1379 1384 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 1389 1392 <span type="species:ncbi:10116">rat</span>
 In mammals many cases of intronless processed genes have been reported, including the human splicing factor gene Srp46 (which was generated by retrotranspositon from the PR264/SC35 splicing factor gene; 42), several genes encoding proteins with RING and C (3) zinc finger motifs (43), the gene for the testes-specific poly(A) polymerase mPAPT (44), the HMGN4 protein encoding gene (45), two 1-Cys peroxiredoxin encoding genes of mouse (46), the mouse U2af1-rs1 gene (47) and the genes for the RBM12 and alphaCP-1 RNA-binding proteins (48,49). The human alphaCP-1 gene was generated by retrotransposition of a fully processed alphaCP-2 mRNA before mammalian radiation. Stringent structural conservation and ubiquitous tissue expression of alphaCP-1 indicates its rapid recruitment to a distinct cellular function (49). Retrotransposition has also been reported for glycerol kinase-related genes (50). The human glycerol kinase gene family consists of an X-linked locus and several X-linked and autosomal intronless genes. The intronless genes comprise both functional genes and pseudogenes. Similarly, the mammalian CDYL gene transcript has been reverse transcribed and inserted into the simian Y chromosome, resulting, after amplification, in the two testis-specifically expressed CDY1 and CDY2 genes (51). It could also be shown that some of the expressed 5S rRNA genes in the mouse and rat were derived from retrotransposition of 5S rRNA transcripts (52).
###end p 86
###begin p 87
###xml 277 281 277 281 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH</italic>
###xml 501 515 501 515 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Brassica napus</italic>
###xml 545 549 545 549 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">SUVH</italic>
###xml 501 515 <span type="species:ncbi:3708">Brassica napus</span>
 All these examples demonstrate that functional processed genes can occasionally be generated from retrotransposed fully processed mRNA transcripts and that these processed genes can take on a non-redundant essential functional role. The indications of genomic duplications of SUVH genes discussed above suggest a similar route of evolution: for these genes one or a few retrotransposition events were followed by conventional duplication. Comparison of gene number and order in related species, e.g. Brassica napus, may reveal how and when the SUVH genes evolved.
###end p 87
###begin title 88
AtSET proteins can be found in the nucleus
###end title 88
###begin p 89
###xml 66 67 66 67 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c4">4</xref>
###xml 68 70 68 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c10">10</xref>
###xml 173 175 173 175 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c28">28</xref>
###xml 376 377 376 377 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f1">1</xref>
###xml 476 478 476 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c53">53</xref>
###xml 77 82 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
SET domain proteins of other species have a nuclear localization (4,10). The human homolog to ASH1, huASH1, has in addition been localized to tight junctions between cells (28). The amino acid sequences of the AtSET proteins give some indications of nuclear localization. First, sequences with significant similarity to bipartite NLSs were found in 16 putative proteins (Fig. 1). Secondly, AT-hooks, which promote protein binding to the minor groove of AT-rich tracts of DNA (53), were found in five proteins.
###end p 89
###begin p 90
###xml 251 261 251 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 318 319 318 319 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f6">6</xref>
###xml 607 609 607 609 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c54">54</xref>
 Using protein-marker fusion constructs, we have investigated the localization of one protein (CLF) with three putative NLSs, two (SUVH1 and SUVH2) without recognizable bipartite NLSs and one with an AT-hook (SUVH3). For all four proteins, as well as Drosophila SU(VAR)3-9, nuclear localization was demonstrated (Fig. 6). Clearly, the absence of a bipartite NLS, as recognized by the protein domain databases, is not a strong prediction for non-nuclear localization. In addition to bipartite NLSs, single and multiple continuous as well as double and multiple bipartite NLSs have been recognized in plants (54).
###end p 90
###begin p 91
###xml 0 5 0 5 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink"> E(z)</italic>
###xml 100 110 100 110 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 112 114 112 114 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c55">55</xref>
###xml 248 250 248 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c13">13</xref>
###xml 268 271 268 271 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">CLF</italic>
###xml 314 321 314 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">AGAMOUS</italic>
###xml 392 394 392 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c11">11</xref>
 E(z) is required continuously through development in order to maintain homeotic gene repression in Drosophila (55). MEA is suggested to be involved in the repression of one or more seed development genes and in maintenance of the repressed state (13). Similarly, the CLF gene is required to repress expression of AGAMOUS in hypocotyls, cotyledons, leaves and inflorescence stems and petals (11). The demonstrated nuclear localization of CLF would be a prerequisite for direct involvement in repression of gene expression.
###end p 91
###begin title 92
Domains in ATX and ASHH proteins are possibly involved in protein-protein interactions
###end title 92
###begin p 93
###xml 4 7 4 7 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">trx</italic>
###xml 12 16 12 16 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">ash1</italic>
###xml 26 36 26 36 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 176 177 176 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c5">5</xref>
###xml 467 469 467 469 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c25">25</xref>
###xml 629 631 629 631 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c25">25</xref>
###xml 242 245 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">HRX</span>
###xml 429 432 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">HRX</span>
The trx and ash1 genes of Drosophila are involved in the transcriptional memory mechanisms that maintain homeotic gene activity in appropriate segments throughout development (5). The ATX proteins share a PHD finger and the ePHD with TRX and HRX. However, in the latter proteins the ePHD motif is separated from the classical PHD fingers. The position of the ePHD adjacent to a PHD finger is more similar to the situation in the HRX partner genes AF10, AF17 and CBP (25). The amino acid sequences of their ePHDs have extensive similarity to those of the ATX proteins. The ePHD of AF10 has the ability to mediate oligomerization (25).
###end p 93
###begin p 94
###xml 68 70 68 70 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c56">56</xref>
###xml 122 123 122 123 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 425 427 425 427 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c56">56</xref>
###xml 482 483 480 481 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 650 651 648 649 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 722 724 720 722 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c56">56</xref>
###xml 903 914 901 912 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 372 376 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">SET1</span>
###xml 380 385 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
 The SET domains of TRX and ASH1 have been shown to self-associate (56). In both cases mutation of the GXG residues (Fig. 3, positions 149-151) to VXV resulted in abolition of self-association. The two G residues are conserved in all ASHH proteins. The second of these G residues is conserved in all ATX but ATX3, while the first is not conserved in ATX3 and ATX5, nor in SET1 of yeast, which is known to not self-associate (56). The conserved arginine in the RPhiINHSC motif (Fig. 3, positions 229-235) is replaced by a histidine in ATX1 and ATX2. Other positions necessary for self-association are all conserved in the ATX and ASHH1 homologs (Fig. 3). In TRX these amino acids are also needed for interaction with SNR1 (56), a constituent of the SWI-SNF complex, which acts to remodel chromatin and thereby activate transcription. The conservation of these amino acids indicates the capability of the Arabidopsis proteins for similar interactions. The SNR1 interaction of TRX is not shared with ASH1, E(Z) and SU(VAR)3-9 class proteins.
###end p 94
###begin title 95
The SUVH and SUVR SET domains are similar to such domains conferring histone 3 methyltransferase activity
###end title 95
###begin p 96
###xml 148 150 148 150 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c20">20</xref>
###xml 151 153 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c22">22</xref>
###xml 373 374 373 374 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 501 502 497 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 597 599 593 595 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c20">20</xref>
###xml 691 692 687 688 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="fig" rid="gke577f3">3</xref>
###xml 1008 1010 1004 1006 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c20">20</xref>
###xml 1036 1047 1032 1043 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 18 23 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">yeast</span>
###xml 24 28 <span type="species:ncbi:4932">CLR4</span>
###xml 68 73 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The SET domain of yeast CLR4, the mammalian SU(VAR)3-9 homologs and human G9a protein have H3-specific histone methyltransferase (HMTase) activity (20-22). Such activity has not been demonstrated for E(Z) and TRX, which lack the C-SAC and N-SAC domain, respectively. There are marked differences between the conserved sequences of the SET domains of the four classes (Fig. 3, positions 198-337). Mutational analyses of the mammalian SU(VAR)3-9 homolog proteins demonstrated that the HPhiPhiNHSC (Fig. 3, positions 229-235) and GEELTFDY (positions 269-276) motifs were crucial for HMTase activity (20). The cysteine residue in the NHSC motif is not conserved in the E(Z) class proteins (Fig. 3). In this respect, two of the SUVH proteins (SUVH2 and SUVH9) also deviate. Furthermore, these two proteins lack two of three cysteines in the C-SAC, in contrast to the other SUVH and SUVR proteins. While mutation of H233 or C235 abolished HMTase activity, the activity increased when H229 was changed to arginine (20). Interestingly, all the Arabidopsis SUVH and SUVR proteins have an arginine in this position.
###end p 96
###begin p 97
###xml 162 173 162 173 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 231 241 231 241 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 475 477 475 477 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c20">20</xref>
###xml 509 511 509 511 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c57">57</xref>
###xml 562 564 562 564 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c58">58</xref>
###xml 710 712 710 712 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c59">59</xref>
###xml 778 780 778 780 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c60">60</xref>
 While the amino acid sequences of the SET domain and the SAC regions of the SUVH and SUVR proteins are similar to proteins with H3-specific HMTase activity, the Arabidopsis proteins lack an N-terminal chromo domain present in the Drosophila and mammalian homologs. In other organisms the YDG motif present in all the SUVH proteins is found in proteins in combination with different domains connected with an irreversible enzymatic activity [SET domain, histone methylation (20); RING domain, ubiquitination (57); HNH domain, non-specific endonuclease reaction (58)]. Thus, the YDG domain may be a target binding domain of functional similarity to the chromo domain (specific binding on methylated histone H3; 59) or the bromo domain (specific binding on acetylated histone H4; 60), which are alternatively found in SET domain or RING finger proteins, respectively.
###end p 97
###begin p 98
###xml 4 14 4 14 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 80 81 80 81 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c8">8</xref>
###xml 151 153 151 153 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c61">61</xref>
###xml 173 184 173 184 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 345 347 345 347 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c32">32</xref>
###xml 522 524 522 524 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ref-type="bibr" rid="gke577c62">62</xref>
###xml 574 585 574 585 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 121 130 <span type="species:ncbi:7227">fruit fly</span>
 In Drosophila, SU(VAR)3-9 is involved in the establishment of heterochromatin (8). About one third of the genome of the fruit fly is heterochromatin (61). In contrast, the Arabidopsis genome has only heterochromatic regions around the centromeres and two chromomeres, which are prerequisites for chromosome condensation on chromosomes 4 and 5 (32). In mammals, the SU(VAR) homologs are associated with the constitutive heterochromatin of the centromeres and play a role in chromosome segregation and mitotic progression (62). The YDG domain and the presence of AT-hooks in Arabidopsis SUVH proteins suggest that these proteins are not necessarily associated with heterochomatin.
###end p 98
###begin title 99
What are the biological functions of AtSET proteins?
###end title 99
###begin p 100
###xml 71 81 71 81 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Drosophila</italic>
###xml 95 106 95 106 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 205 216 205 216 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
###xml 291 302 291 302 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink">Arabidopsis</italic>
SET domain proteins have been implicated in developmental processes in Drosophila, mammals and Arabidopsis. It is therefore reasonable to postulate that many of the newly discovered SET domain proteins in Arabidopsis also serve functions in development. The high numbers of such proteins in Arabidopsis, compared to other organisms, seems to be a result of extensive duplications in the genome and occasional retrotransposition events. The high numbers may also reflect the differences between animal and plant development: during a plants life, meristems go through transitions to allow inflorescences to develop and, thereafter, flowers with reproductive organs to form. In animals, however, cells giving rise to somatic and reproductive organs segregate early in development. It is interesting to note that our expression analysis showed that all tested genes were expressed in buds and the majority in flowers and seeds, organs where important developmental processes take place. One possibility is that the AtSET proteins have different functions in the same cells. Alternatively, the different paralogs are committed to related functions in different cells and tissues.
###end p 100
###begin p 101
 We suggest that epigenetic control of gene programs is needed to maintain meristem and organ identity during the different stages of plant development and to aid developmental transitions in response to genetic programs and environmental influences. The four classes of SET domain genes may play crucial roles in such control.
###end p 101
