Identification of CD4-CD8- Double-Negative Natural Killer T Cell Precursors in the Thymus
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: ND HW MT. Performed the experiments: ND SS. Analyzed the data: ND MT. Wrote the paper: ND MT.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 119 120 115 116 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 123 124 119 120 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
It is well known that CD1d-restricted Valpha14 invariant natural killer T (NKT) cells are derived from cells in the CD4+CD8+ double-positive (DP) population in the thymus. However, the developmental progression of NKT cells in the earlier stages remains unclear, and the possible existence of NKT cell presursors in the earlier stages than DP stage remains to be tested.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 69 86 69 78 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;14-J&#945;18</italic>
###xml 194 195 182 183 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 198 199 186 187 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 288 295 276 283 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 300 308 288 296 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 517 525 505 513 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
###xml 631 638 619 626 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 390 394 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 474 478 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 730 734 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Here, we demonstrate that NKT cell precursors that express invariant Valpha14-Jalpha18 transcripts but devoid of surface expression of the invariant Valpha14 receptor are present in the late CD4-CD8- double-negative (DN)4 stage and have the potential to generate mature NKT cells in both in vivo and in vitro experimental conditions. Moreover, the DN4 population in CD1d knock-out (CD1dKO) mice was similar to those with an NKT cell potential in wild-type (WT) C57BL/6 (B6) mice, but failed to develop into NKT cells in vitro. However, these precursors could develop into NKT cells when co-cultured with normal thymocytes or in an in vivo experimental setting, indicating that functional NKT cell precursors are present in CD1dKO mice.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Together, these results demonstrate that thymic DN4 fraction contains NKT cell precursors. Our findings provide new insights into the early development of NKT cells prior to surface expression of the invariant Valpha14 antigen receptor and suggest the possible alternative developmental pathway of NKT cells.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 183 184 183 184 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 189 190 189 190 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 193 194 193 194 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 340 341 340 341 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 346 347 346 347 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 359 360 359 360 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 365 366 365 366 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 378 379 378 379 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 384 385 384 385 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 400 401 400 401 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 406 407 406 407 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 452 455 452 455 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Godfrey1">[1]</xref>
###xml 603 607 593 597 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Tcrb</italic>
###xml 760 761 747 748 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 764 765 751 752 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1074 1077 1050 1053 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-vonBoehmer1">[2]</xref>
The developmental progression of the T cell lineage in the thymus is precisely controlled, beginning with the early T cell precursors, which present in the population of lineage (Lin)-, CD4-CD8- double-negative (DN) thymocytes. The DN population in the thymus can be further divided based on their expression of CD25 and CD44 into DN1 (CD25- CD44+), DN2 (CD25+ CD44+), DN3 (CD25+ CD44-) and DN4 (CD25- CD44-), which are sequential developmental stages [1]. Major commitment events to the alphabeta T cell lineage occur at the DN3 stage, where rearrangement of the T cell receptor (TCR) beta chain gene (Tcrb) and subsequent beta-selection for a functional TCRbeta chain take place. The cells that survive beta-selection develop into the DN4 stage to become CD4+CD8+ double-positive (DP) thymocytes. In the case of conventional alphabeta T cells, rearrangement of the TCRalpha chain gene locus occurs at the DP stage, and those cells that make productive rearrangements undergo MHC-mediated positive and negative selection to generate single-positive (SP) CD4 or CD8 T cells [2].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 109 126 109 118 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;14-J&#945;18</italic>
###xml 139 156 131 140 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;24-J&#945;18</italic>
###xml 318 321 302 305 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Taniguchi1">[3]</xref>
###xml 439 442 423 426 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Brigl1">[4]</xref>
###xml 676 679 656 659 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Taniguchi2">[5]</xref>
###xml 681 684 661 664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Bendelac1">[6]</xref>
###xml 130 134 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 160 166 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">humans</span>
Natural killer T (NKT) cells are characterized by the expression of an invariant antigen receptor encoded by Valpha14-Jalpha18 in mice and Valpha24-Jalpha18 in humans. This receptor is used preferentially by NKT cells but not by conventional T cells, defining NKT cells as a distinct lineage from conventional T cells [3]. NKT cells recognize self- or non-self glycolipid ligands in conjunction with the monomorphic MHC-like molecule CD1d [4], and mediate intermediary functions that link the innate and acquired immune systems, regulating protective and regulatory responses by their rapid secretion of large amounts of cytokines such as IL-4, and IFN-gamma after activation [5], [6].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 241 244 241 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Gapin1">[7]</xref>
###xml 246 249 246 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Egawa1">[8]</xref>
###xml 498 499 483 484 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 511 514 496 499 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Bendelac1">[6]</xref>
###xml 515 519 500 504 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Bezbradica1">[10]</xref>
###xml 807 824 785 794 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;14-J&#945;18</italic>
###xml 1001 1005 971 975 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Makino1">[11]</xref>
###xml 1150 1154 1120 1124 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Sato1">[12]</xref>
###xml 1156 1160 1126 1130 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Bezbradica2">[13]</xref>
###xml 1495 1498 1465 1468 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Egawa1">[8]</xref>
###xml 886 890 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Since the discovery of this unique cell lineage, the developmental pathway of NKT cells has been one of the most intriguing topics. Based on previous findings that cells with NKT cell potential can be detected in the DP thymocyte population [7], [8], NKT cells have been thought to branch off from conventional alphabeta T cell precursors at the DP stage in the thymus, where the cells in the DP thymocyte pool expressing a rearranged invariant Valpha14-Jalpha18 TCR are positively selected by CD1d+ thymocytes [6]-[10]. However, currently available data do not rule out the possibility that NKT cells are derived from a precursor population distinct from that of conventional alphabeta T cells. Findings in support of independent origins for conventional T and NKT cells include the detection of invariant Valpha14-Jalpha18 transcripts before thymus formation in fetal (RAG-1-KOxWT)F1 mice, in which the possibility of contamination with maternal NKT cells in the samples tested is formally excluded [11], and the cell surface expression by NKT precursor cells and NKT cells of the granulocyte-macrophage colony stimulating factor (GM-CSF) receptor [12], [13], which is a unique marker for myeloid but not lymphoid lineage cells. Considering their unique characteristics compared with conventional T cells, it seemed possible to us that NKT cell precursors might differ from those of conventional T cells and exist at earlier stages than the DP stage previously defined by fate-mapping studies [8]. To test this possibility, we focused on cells with NKT cell potential at early stages of development, prior to the DP stage in the thymus.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 162 169 162 169 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 174 182 174 182 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vitro</italic>
In the present study, we demonstrate that NKT cell precursors are present in DN4 stage thymocytes and have the potential to give rise to mature NKT cells in both in vivo and in vitro experimental settings. Our findings provide new insights into the early development of NKT cells before expression of the invariant Valpha14-Jalpha18 antigen receptor.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Results and Discussion
###end title 13
###begin title 14
###xml 10 27 10 19 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;14-J&#945;18</italic>
Invariant Valpha14-Jalpha18 transcripts are present in the thymic DN4 fraction
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 116 117 116 117 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 699 700 682 683 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 744 745 727 728 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1037 1041 997 1001 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Matsuda1">[14]</xref>
###xml 1083 1084 1043 1044 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1238 1239 1198 1199 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1258 1259 1214 1215 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">&#8722;</sup>
###xml 1423 1446 1379 1402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003688-g001">Figure 1A (upper panel)</xref>
###xml 1534 1535 1486 1487 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1552 1553 1504 1505 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">+</sup>
###xml 1697 1714 1649 1658 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;14-J&#945;18</italic>
###xml 1772 1781 1716 1725 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003688-g001">Figure 1B</xref>
###xml 1470 1474 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1622 1626 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To investigate whether the DN population in the thymus contains cells with NKT cell potential, we first purified Lin- DN3 and DN4 thymocyte fractions devoid of Lin marker positive mature cells, including NKT cells, by a three-step cell purification method. In the first step, the DN fraction was enriched by depletion of DP and SP thymocytes by magnetic-activated cell sorting (MACS) using microbeads coated with anti-CD4 and anti-CD8 monoclonal antibodies (mAbs). Then the DN enriched fraction was stained with a biotin-labeled Lin mAb 'cocktail' (anti-B220, CD3epsilon, CD11b, CD11c, CD19, Gr-1, TER-119, TCRbeta, TCRgammadelta) and subsequently reacted with streptavidin microbeads to deplete Lin+ cells using MACS columns. The resulting Lin- cells in the eluate fraction were sorted by staining with fluorescent-conjugated streptavidin and also with directly labeled mAbs (anti-CD4, CD8alpha, TCRbeta, TCRgammadelta, NK1.1) together with alpha-galactosylceramide-loaded CD1d (alphaGC/CD1d) dimer which specifically detects NKT cells [14] to remove any unstained NKT cells and Lin+ cells left in the samples. This rigorous cell preparation method and staining protocol was used throughout this study unless otherwise indicated. The Lin- alphaGC/CD1d dimer- DN fraction was further analyzed by the CD25, CD44 bivariate staining pattern, which defines the well-characterized thymocyte differentiation pathway. As shown in Figure 1A (upper panel), the DN fraction of B6 mice contain neither DP or SP thymocytes, nor alphaGC/CD1d dimer+ NKT cells or Lin+ cells. When highly purified DN3 and DN4 fractions from C57BL/6 (B6) mice were analyzed by RT-PCR, the bands corresponding to NKT cell-specific Valpha14-Jalpha18 sequences were detected in the DN4 but not DN3 fraction (Figure 1B).
###end p 15

###xml 767 776 759 768 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003688-g001">Figure 1C</xref>
###xml 801 818 793 802 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;14-J&#945;18</italic>
###xml 272 276 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 522 526 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 654 658 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 703 707 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 878 882 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Because a fraction of NKT cells in the thymus is DN and CD44-, it is still possible that the DN fraction isolated by this method may contain mature or immature NKT cells. To exclude this possibility, we isolated the DN4 fraction from the thymus of CD1d knock-out (CD1dKO) mice, in which NKT cells do not exist because of the lack of expression of CD1d as a selection molecule [15]-[17]. The fluorescence-activated cell sorter (FACS) profiles of the CD1dKO DN fractions were essentially identical to those obtained from B6 mice (Figure 1A, lower panel). Notably, results from the RT-PCR analysis of the CD1dKO DN fractions were identical to those from B6 mice, where the DN4 but not DN3 fraction in both mice expressed NKT cell-specific Valpha14-Jalpha18 transcripts (Figure 1C). The identities of the Valpha14-Jalpha18 transcripts detected in the DN4 fractions of B6 and CD1dKO mice were confirmed by direct sequencing of the PCR products (data not shown).
###end p 18
###begin p 19
###xml 45 62 45 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;14-J&#945;18</italic>
###xml 152 169 141 158 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003688-g001">Figures 1B and 1C</xref>
###xml 203 220 192 201 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;14-J&#945;18</italic>
###xml 453 470 431 440 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;14-J&#945;18</italic>
###xml 514 517 484 487 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Gapin1">[7]</xref>
###xml 110 114 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In addition to their expression of invariant Valpha14-Jalpha18 transcripts, the DN4 fraction of B6 and CD1dKO mice contained TCRbeta chain transcripts (Figures 1B and 1C), suggesting that both invariant Valpha14-Jalpha18 and TCRbeta transcripts are already present in the DN4 stage before the cells reach the DP stage. It is important to note that our present findings do not necessarily contradict the previous report, demonstrating that the invariant Valpha14-Jalpha18 transcripts are detectable at the DP stage [7].
###end p 19
###begin p 20
###xml 45 62 45 54 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">V&#945;14-J&#945;18</italic>
###xml 316 320 304 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Aifantis1">[18]</xref>
Regarding the possibility of early invariant Valpha14-Jalpha18 gene rearrangement in the DN population, it has recently been shown that low level of TCRalpha chain gene rearrangement in the late DN stage, which was previously considered to represent a transgenic artifact, occurs even under physiological conditions [18]. The present findings support the possibility that the DN4 fraction in the thymus contains precursors for the NKT cell lineage.
###end p 20

###xml 810 814 778 782 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Bezbradica1">[10]</xref>
###xml 828 845 796 813 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003688-g001">Figures 1B and 1C</xref>

###xml 977 981 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
To further characterize the DN4 population in the thymus, we used RT-PCR to investigate the expression of genes relevant to the NKT cell development. As shown in Figures 1B and 1C, both B6 and CD1dKO DN4 cells, as well as DN3 cells used as positive controls, expressed transcripts encoding RAG-1/RAG-2, which are required for the rearrangement of TCR gene segments. They also expressed transcripts for pTalpha (Ptcra), which is required for the NKT cell development in a cell autonomous manner [19]. The thymus-specific isoform of retinoic acid receptor-related orphan receptor gamma (RORgammat) appears to be required for the rearrangement of the Valpha14-Jalpha18 gene segments as it was shown that RORgammat-deficient mice lack Valpha14-Jalpha18 transcripts and consequently are deficient in NKT cells [8], [10]. As shown in Figures 1B and 1C, RORgammat transcripts were clearly detected in the DN4 fraction, and to a lesser extent in the DN3 fraction in both B6 and CD1dKO mice.
###end p 22
###begin p 23
###xml 214 217 199 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Egawa1">[8]</xref>
###xml 229 246 214 231 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0003688-g001">Figures 1B and 1C</xref>
###xml 268 272 253 257 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Villey1">[20]</xref>
###xml 274 278 259 263 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-He1">[21]</xref>
###xml 483 487 457 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0003688-Aifantis1">[18]</xref>
Considering that DP thymocytes exclusively express RORgammat and using the RORgammat-Cre fate-mapping method, it has been concluded that all NKT cells and alphabeta T cells are derived from DP thymocyte precursors [8]. Our data (Figures 1B and 1C) and those of others [20], [21] clearly demonstrate that RORgammat expression begins at the DN3 stage, presumably as a result of pre-TCR signaling. Moreover, the recent report that not all alphabeta T cells develop through the DP stage [18] supports the possibility that some of NKT cells are not derived from DP precursors.
###end p 23

Next, we analyzed the surface phenotype of the DN4 thymic fraction from both B6 and CD1dKO mice. In contrast to alphaGC/CD1d dimer+ NKT cells in the thymus, which are CD24low CD62L- CD69+ and CD122+, both B6 and CD1dKO alphaGC/CD1d dimer- DN4 populations were uniformly CD24high, CD62L+, CD69-, and CD122- (Figure S1). Thus, the DN4 population is different from the alphaGC/CD1d dimer+ NKT cells in terms of cell surface phenotype.
###end p 24

###xml 454 458 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
It is well established that the earliest detectable NKT cell precursors just after positive-selection with CD1d in the thymus, which are alphaGC/CD1d tetramer+, have NK1.1-, CD4+, CD8+/-, CD24+, CD44low and CD69+ surface phenotype [22]. Given our findings that the DN4 fraction lacked expression of the cell surface invariant Valpha14 receptor (Figure 1A) and CD69 (Figure S1), but possessed the NKT cell-specific Valpha14-Jalpha18 transcripts in CD1dKO mice (Figure 1C), we considered that the DN4 population contains the bona fide precursors to NKT cells prior to positive-selection with CD1d in the thymus.

To determine whether the DN4 fraction, which expresses Valpha14-Jalpha18 transcripts but no cell surface TCR, has NKT cell precursor potential, we co-cultured the DN4 population with OP9 stromal cells engineered to express Notch ligand delta-like 1 (OP9/Dll-1) that support conventional T cell differentiation in vitro[23]. Although the developmental requirements of NKT cells might differ from those of conventional T cells, the OP9/Dll-1 stromal monolayer culture system was chosen because of its ability to promote the survival of immature T cells [24].

To formally exclude the possibility of contamination of our culture system with mature NKT cells and to confirm the presence of NKT cell precursors in the DN4 fraction of CD1dKO mice, we mixed the DN4 fraction from B6.CD45.1 congenic mice with the DN4 fraction from CD1dKO mice at 7:3 ratio, and then co-cultured with OP9/Dll-1. The CD1dKO DN4 cells, identified as CD45.2+, were able to generate alphaGC/CD1d dimer+ NKT cells in the presence of WT CD1d+/CD45.1+ thymocytes in the 8 day-culture (Figure 2B). These results, together with the data shown in Figure 1C, clearly demonstrated the presence of NKT precursors in the DN4 population. In addition, the results shown in Figure 2B confirmed the requirement for CD1d-expressing thymocytes for the development of NKT cells, in agreement with previous reports [9].

FACS analysis shown in Figure 3A revealed that the most alphaGC/CD1d dimer+ NKT cells generated in vitro from the B6 DN4 population were of the NK1.1-, DN, and CD44+ phenotype, while ex vivo B6 thymic NKT cells were of the NK1.1+, CD4+ or DN, and CD44+ phenotype. A standard cytokine secretion assay using alpha-galactosylceramide (alphaGalCer)-pulsed GM-CSF-induced bone marrow-derived dendritic cells (GM-DC) demonstrated that the in vitro derived NKT cells secreted both IFN-gamma and IL-4 in a CD1d-dependent manner, which is the hallmark of NKT cells (Figure 3B). Of note, the cytokine secretion profiles of the in vitro generated NKT cells were more of Th1-type, with very little IL-4, compared with their ex vivo counterparts (Figure 3B). These differences between in vitro generated NKT cells and ex vivo thymic NKT cells may be due to the limits of the OP9/Dll-1 culture system, which has been reported to support the preferential development of IFN-gamma producing CD8 T cells [23], and may also indicate a requirement for the intact thymic microenvironment for the full differentiation of NKT cells [26]. It was also reported that human DN NKT cells show Th1-type biased cytokine response upon stimulation [27]. Collectively, the data shown in Figures 3A and 3B suggest that the in vitro generated NKT cells resemble those found in vivo.
