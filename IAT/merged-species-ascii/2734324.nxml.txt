###begin article-title 0
LRP1 Regulates Architecture of the Vascular Wall by Controlling PDGFRbeta-Dependent Phosphatidylinositol 3-Kinase Activation
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Conceived and designed the experiments: LZ YT JH. Performed the experiments: LZ YT. Analyzed the data: LZ PB MDT JH. Contributed reagents/materials/analysis tools: LZ YT PB MDT JH. Wrote the paper: LZ JH.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
###xml 447 451 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Low density lipoprotein receptor-related protein 1 (LRP1) protects against atherosclerosis by regulating the activation of platelet-derived growth factor receptor beta (PDGFRbeta) in vascular smooth muscle cells (SMCs). Activated PDGFRbeta undergoes tyrosine phosphorylation and subsequently interacts with various signaling molecules, including phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K), which binds to the phosphorylated tyrosine 739/750 residues in mice, and thus regulates actin polymerization and cell movement.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Methods and Principal Findings
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 123 130 123 130 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">LRP1&#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 350 359 344 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">smLRP1&#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 481 490 472 481 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">smLRP1&#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 712 721 697 706 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">smLRP1&#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 361 365 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 491 495 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 695 699 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In this study, we found disorganized actin in the form of membrane ruffling and enhanced cell migration in LRP1-deficient (LRP1-/-) SMCs. Marfan syndrome-like phenotypes such as tortuous aortas, disrupted elastic layers and abnormally activated transforming growth factor beta (TGFbeta) signaling are present in smooth muscle-specific LRP1 knockout (smLRP1-/-) mice. To investigate the role of LRP1-regulated PI3K activation by PDGFRbeta in atherogenesis, we generated a strain of smLRP1-/- mice in which tyrosine 739/750 of the PDGFRbeta had been mutated to phenylalanines (PDGFRbeta F2/F2). Spontaneous atherosclerosis was significantly reduced in the absence of hypercholesterolemia in these mice compared to smLRP1-/- animals that express wild type PDGFR. Normal actin organization was restored and spontaneous SMC migration as well as PDGF-BB-induced chemotaxis was dramatically reduced, despite continued overactivation of TGFbeta signaling, as indicated by high levels of nuclear phospho-Smad2.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Conclusions and Significance
###end title 6
###begin p 7
Our data suggest that LRP1 regulates actin organization and cell migration by controlling PDGFRbeta-dependent activation of PI3K. TGFbeta activation alone is not sufficient for the expression of the Marfan-like vascular phenotype. Thus, regulation of PI3 Kinase by PDGFRbeta is essential for maintaining vascular integrity, and for the prevention of atherosclerosis as well as Marfan syndrome.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Introduction
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 184 187 184 187 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Herz1">[1]</xref>
###xml 386 389 386 389 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Herz1">[1]</xref>
###xml 391 394 391 394 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Lillis1">[2]</xref>
###xml 563 566 563 566 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Beisiegel1">[3]</xref>
###xml 568 571 568 571 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Kowal1">[4]</xref>
###xml 573 576 573 576 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Hussain1">[5]</xref>
###xml 742 745 742 745 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Swertfeger1">[6]</xref>
###xml 886 889 880 883 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Boucher1">[7]</xref>
Low density lipoprotein receptor related protein 1 (LRP1) is a multifunctional member of the LDL receptor (LDLR) gene family with a unique capacity of binding over 40 distinct ligands [1]. It plays diverse roles in a variety of biological processes including lipoprotein metabolism, protease degradation, activation of lysosomal enzymes, and endocytosis of bacterial toxins and viruses [1], [2]. Binding of apolipoprotein E (apoE) to the extracellular domain of LRP1 removes apoE-containing lipoprotein remnants from the circulation into the liver by endocytosis [3], [4], [5]. By contrast, in the smooth muscle cells (SMCs) of the arterial wall, apoE-lipoprotein binding inhibits platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF)-directed SMC migration [6]. Studies from our laboratory have shown that LRP1 suppresses PDGF receptor beta (PDGFRbeta) activation and protects against atherosclerosis [7].
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 280 283 269 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Tallquist1">[8]</xref>
###xml 455 458 441 444 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Okkenhaug1">[9]</xref>
###xml 520 524 506 510 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Arrieumerlou1">[10]</xref>
###xml 526 530 512 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Brachmann1">[11]</xref>
###xml 558 562 544 548 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Cantley1">[12]</xref>
###xml 400 405 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Activated PDGFRbeta undergoes tyrosine phosphorylation and subsequently interacts with a variety of SH2 domain-containing signaling molecules including phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K), phospholipase Cgamma (PLCgamma), Src family kinase, and phosphotyrosine phosphatase SHP-2 [8]. Among these interacting proteins, PI3K which binds to the phosphorylated tyrosine 740/751 residues (739/750 in the mouse) of PDGFRbeta through its p85 regulatory subunit [9], is particularly important for regulating actin organization [10], [11], cell growth and migration [12].
###end p 10
###begin p 11
###xml 229 233 214 218 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Huang1">[13]</xref>
###xml 235 239 220 224 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Tseng1">[14]</xref>
###xml 305 312 287 294 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 530 534 512 516 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Boucher2">[15]</xref>
###xml 371 375 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
LRP1 is also known as transforming growth factor beta (TGFbeta) receptor V (TbetaR-V) and appears to be required for mediating the growth inhibitory response of TGFbeta, in conjunction with Smad signaling through TbetaR-II and I [13], [14]. TGFbeta signaling is abnormally elevated in the absence of LRP1 in vivo, where analysis of SMC-specific LRP1 knockout (smLRP1-/-) mice revealed a Marfan syndrome-like phenotype with nuclear accumulation of phosphorylated Smad2 (p-Smad2) and disruption of elastic layers in the vessel wall [15].
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 93 97 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 468 472 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 637 641 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
For the present study we have generated a new, genetically complex strain of compound mutant mice that are LDL receptor-deficient (LDLR-/-), lack LRP1 only in their vascular smooth muscle cells, and express an endogenous, crippled form of the PDGFRbeta that is incapable of activating PI3K. Our goal was to test, whether increased PDGFRbeta signaling through PI3K is the primary cause for the increased susceptibility to atherosclerotic lesion development in LDLR -/- mice lacking LRP1 in their SMCs, and whether PDGFRbeta-dependent PI3K signaling is required for the expression of the Marfan syndrome-like phenotype in smLRP1-deficient mice.
###end p 12
###begin title 13
Results
###end title 13
###begin title 14
Lack of LRP1 expression in the SMCs results in cell hypertrophy and vessel elongation
###end title 14
###begin p 15
###xml 47 51 47 51 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">LRP1</italic>

Earlier data from our laboratory showed that smLRP1-/-; LDLR-/- mice are highly susceptible to atherosclerosis when fed a high-cholesterol diet [7]. To determine if this increased susceptibility to atherosclerosis is preserved in smLRP1-/- mice in the absence of hypercholesterolemia, smLRP1-/- mice either expressing or lacking LDLR were maintained on a standard low-fat rodent chow. Determination of plasma total cholesterol confirmed that smLRP1-/- mice did not develop hypercholesterolemia (129.9+/-5.3 mg/dl, Table 1). Whereas, smLRP1-/-; LDLR-/- mice had high total cholesterol levels of 246.5+/-61.0 mg/dl with a major increase in LDL (Figure S1).

Elongated aortas were present in the absence of hypercholesterolemia in smLRP1-/- mice (Figure 1c, h). However, atherosclerotic lesions were only visible in smLRP1-/-; LDLR-/- mice (Figure 1e, j). To study structural changes in the vascular wall of these elongated aortas, H&E, trichrome and elastin staining were performed. Thickened aortic walls with intima thickening, disarranged and hypertrophic SMCs, increased extracellular collagen accumulation, and elastic lamina disruption were observed in 11-month old smLRP1-/- mice (Figure 1B). Vascular wall thickening was also present in young mice at 7 weeks of age (Figure S2). Compared with wild type, the aortic wall of smLRP1-/- mice was significantly thicker (66.14+/-3.32 microm vs. 39.35+/-2.16 microm, Figure 1C). Our findings indicate that LRP1 expression in SMCs controls the architecture of the vascular wall in a plasma cholesterol-independent manner.

LRP1 controls PI3K binding and activation by PDGFRbeta in vivo
###end title 25
###begin p 26
###xml 238 242 232 236 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">LRP1</italic>
###xml 247 251 241 245 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">LDLR</italic>
###xml 287 290 281 284 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Boucher1">[7]</xref>
###xml 832 835 817 820 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Boucher1">[7]</xref>
###xml 905 909 887 891 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">LRP1</italic>
###xml 947 959 929 941 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006922-g002">Figure 2A, C</xref>
###xml 1030 1039 1012 1021 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006922-g002">Figure 2A</xref>
###xml 255 259 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 365 369 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 443 447 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 913 917 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
PDGFRbeta signaling is crucial for regulating SMC responses and is an important contributing factor to atherogenesis. Previous work from our laboratory showed dramatically increased expression and activation of PDGFRbeta in aortas from smLRP1-/-; LDLR-/- mice on a high-cholesterol diet [7]. To investigate the basal expression and activation level of PDGFRbeta in mice maintained on a low-cholesterol diet, aortic extracts were prepared from mice expressing or lacking LRP1 and LDLR and analyzed by Western blotting. Because PDGFRbeta activation, through transphosphorylation of tyrosine residues in its cytoplasmic domain, triggers a cascade of phosphorylation events which eventually lead to the activation of extracellular regulated-protein kinases (Erks), phosphorylated-Erk1/2 was used as an indicator of PDGFRbeta activation [7]. About a two-fold increase of PDGFRbeta expression was detected in smLRP1-/- mice regardless of LDLR genotype (Figure 2A, C). Increased Erk1/2 phosphorylation was also observed in these aortas (Figure 2A). These data suggest that the expression and activation of PDGFRbeta is only regulated by LRP1, not LDLR.

###begin title 43
PDGF-BB-induced chemotaxis is inhibited by blocking PI3K activation through PDGFRbeta
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 199 208 196 205 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0006922-g005">Figure 5E</xref>
To investigate whether PDGF-BB-induced SMC migration involves the PDGFRbeta-PI3K pathway, 10 ng/ml PDGF-BB was administrated as a chemo-attractant in the bottom well of a Boyden chamber. As shown in Figure 5E, PDGF-BB induced the transmigration of SMCs independent of LRP1 expression. However, when the Y739/750 phosphorylation sites of PDGFRbeta were mutated, this PDGF-BB-induced chemotaxis was completely abolished. This finding suggests that the increased migratory propensity of LRP1-deficient SMCs, which is further enhanced by PDGF-BB activation of PDGFRbeta, is in its entirety dependent upon the activation of PI3K by PDGFRbeta.

###begin title 45
Abnormal activation of TGFbeta signaling is present in the absence of LRP1
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 4 11 4 11 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">in vivo</italic>
###xml 466 470 466 470 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Robinson1">[21]</xref>
###xml 587 591 581 585 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Neptune1">[22]</xref>
###xml 593 597 587 591 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Ng1">[23]</xref>
###xml 712 716 703 707 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Neptune1">[22]</xref>
###xml 864 868 849 853 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Singh1">[24]</xref>
###xml 870 874 855 859 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Akutsu1">[25]</xref>
###xml 1022 1026 989 993 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Huang1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1140 1144 1095 1099 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Huang1">[13]</xref>
###xml 1146 1150 1101 1105 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Tseng1">[14]</xref>
###xml 1217 1221 1169 1173 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Boucher2">[15]</xref>
###xml 1204 1209 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
Our in vivo findings indicate that lack of LRP1 in SMCs results in elongation of the aorta, thickening of the vascular wall, and disruption of the elastic layers. Tortuous aorta and elastic laminar disruption are also two key cardiovascular manifestations of Marfan syndrome. As a connective tissue disorder with autosomal dominant inheritance, Marfan syndrome is caused by loss-of-function mutations in fibrillin-1, a matrix component of extracellular microfibrils [21]. Fibrillin-1 regulates activation of the cytokine TGFbeta, and its deficiency results in enhanced TGFbeta signaling [22], [23]. Recent studies have shown that abnormal activation of TGFbeta contributes to the pathogenesis of Marfan syndrome [22]. Paradoxically, loss of function mutations in TGFbeta receptor I or II also result in increased TGFbeta signaling and give rise to Marfan syndrome [24], [25]. LRP1 is identical to the type V TGFbeta receptor (TbetaR-V), which co-expresses with other TGFbeta receptors (TbetaR-I, TbetaR-II and TbetaR-III) [13]. LRP1/TbetaR-V mediates TGFbeta induced growth inhibition in concert with TbetaR-II/TbetaR-I/Smad2/3/4 signaling [13], [14] and TGFbeta signaling is increased in LRP1 deficient mouse aortas [15].

To evaluate the TGFbeta activation state in the aortas of the different genotypes, immunofluorescent staining and Western blotting of p-Smad2 were performed. Significantly increased Smad2 nuclear translocation was seen in smLRP1-/-; LDLR-/- aortas. ###begin p 50
Taken together, our findings suggest that aorta elongation and elastic layer disruption, both hallmarks of Marfan syndrome, require the PDGFRbeta dependent activation of PI3K. Activation of the TGFbeta signaling pathway alone is not sufficient. Thus, LRP1 acts as a molecular switch that integrates TGFbeta and PDGFRbeta/PI3K signals and this is essential for maintaining the integrity of the vascular wall architecture.
###end p 50
###begin title 51
Discussion
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 432 450 426 444 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">smLRP1&#8722;/&#8722;; LDLR&#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 451 455 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In this study we have investigated the role of LRP1 for PI3K activation by PDGFRbeta in SMCs, and the impact this LRP1 'checkpoint' has for preventing atherosclerotic lesion formation and progression, as well as for the maintenance of vascular wall integrity. We found that the selective genetic blockade of PI3K activation by PDGFRbeta substantially suppressed spontaneous atherosclerotic lesion development, which is prominent in smLRP1-/-; LDLR-/- mice. Furthermore, vascular wall elongation and medial thickening, due to SMC hyperproliferation, increased SMC migration and disruption of elastic layers are normalized throughout the entire aorta. Our findings suggest that PI3K is the main driving force that promotes SMC proliferation and migration, elastolysis, spontaneous atherosclerosis and lesion progression in the absence of LRP1.
###end p 52
###begin p 53
###xml 48 66 48 66 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">smLRP1&#8722;/&#8722;; LDLR&#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 149 158 149 158 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">smLRP1&#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 448 464 448 464 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">LRP1+/+; LDLR&#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 676 680 676 680 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Ishibashi1">[26]</xref>
###xml 67 71 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 465 469 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Prominent atherosclerotic lesions preexisted in smLRP1-/-; LDLR-/- mice maintained on standard, low-fat and cholesterol-free rodent chow, but not in smLRP1-/- animals of comparable age. These data suggest that in the presence of an intact endothelium and low plasma cholesterol levels, proliferative signals alone are not sufficient to initiate the pathogenic mechanisms that culminate in classic atherosclerotic plaques. By contrast, the aorta of LRP1+/+; LDLR-/- mice appears histoanatomically normal despite increased plasma cholesterol levels on the same chow, and extensive atherosclerotic lesions develop only after feeding of a high-cholesterol diet for several months [26]. Thus, LRP1 in SMCs functions cell autonomously in the maintenance of vascular wall integrity and protection from cholesterol-induced atherosclerosis.
###end p 53
###begin p 54
###xml 30 35 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mouse</span>
###xml 161 165 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 385 389 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
In the absence of smLRP1, the mouse aorta undergoes hyperplastic and hypertrophic changes that were apparent in young (7 weeks) as well as older (11 months old) mice indicating that they are not the result of aging, but the manifestation of an intrinsic change of smooth muscle phenotype. This is most likely caused by the increased expression and activation of PDGFRbeta in smLRP1-/- mice and an accompanying increase in PI3K association with PDGFRbeta. Disruption of an obligatory proatherogenic proliferative pathway, involving PI3K and PDGFRbeta, prevents or greatly reduces lesion development at sites of high shear stress, such as the aortic arch and the abdominal aorta, where endothelial integrity is easily compromised. Thus, by selectively controlling SMC proliferation and migration independent of endothelial integrity and plasma cholesterol levels in a novel genetically complex animal model, we have been able to isolate and demonstrate the pivotal and interdependent roles of two central mechanisms of atherosclerotic lesion development.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 109 113 106 110 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Brachmann1">[11]</xref>
###xml 115 119 112 116 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Arvidsson1">[27]</xref>
###xml 121 125 118 122 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Wennstrom1">[28]</xref>
###xml 127 131 124 128 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Wennstrom2">[29]</xref>
###xml 133 137 130 134 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-HooshmandRad1">[30]</xref>
###xml 472 476 463 467 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Heuchel1">[18]</xref>
###xml 478 482 469 473 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Tallquist2">[31]</xref>
###xml 637 641 628 632 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Wennstrom1">[28]</xref>
###xml 643 647 634 638 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Wennstrom2">[29]</xref>
###xml 1005 1013 993 1001 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">smLRP&#8722;/&#8722;</italic>
###xml 835 839 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 1014 1018 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Activation of the PDGFRbeta results in actin reorganization in the forms of membrane ruffling and chemotaxis [11], [27], [28], [29], [30] and thus provides an excellent functional assay for the physiological activation of PDGFRbeta through other genetic manipulations, such as the disruption of LRP1. PI3K binding to the cytoplasmic domain of activated PDGFRbeta receptors requires phosphorylation at residues 739 and 750 and this interaction in turn activates the kinase [18], [31]. Replacement of these tyrosines by non-phosphorylatable phenylalanines prevents binding of PI3K and fails to mediate membrane ruffling and cell migration [28], [29]. As a result, the pronounced edge ruffling and circular membrane ruffling as well as greatly enhanced SMC migration that were observed in the absence of LRP1 were virtually normalized in mice in which PDGFRbeta-dependent PI3K activation had been genetically disrupted. These findings show that the membrane ruffling and increased smooth muscle migration in smLRP-/- mice is critically dependent upon PI3K activation, which is mediated by PDGFRbeta. Nevertheless, a caveat to this interpretation is that, although PI3K is the only known cellular signal transducer that interacts with pY739 and 750 of PDGFRbeta, this do not exclude the possibility that another unknown signal modulator also interacts with this site and contributes to the pathogenic mechanism.
###end p 55
###begin p 56
###xml 191 195 191 195 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Robinson1">[21]</xref>
###xml 197 201 197 201 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Ramirez1">[32]</xref>
###xml 263 267 260 264 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Singh1">[24]</xref>
###xml 269 273 266 270 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Akutsu1">[25]</xref>
###xml 346 350 340 344 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Neptune1">[22]</xref>
###xml 352 356 346 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Ng1">[23]</xref>
###xml 358 362 352 356 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Habashi1">[33]</xref>
###xml 380 384 374 378 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Nataatmadja1">[34]</xref>
###xml 433 437 424 428 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Singh1">[24]</xref>
###xml 625 629 610 614 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="pone.0006922-Boucher2">[15]</xref>
###xml 959 985 944 967 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">smLRP1&#8722;/&#8722;; PDGFR&#946; F2/F2</italic>
###xml 341 345 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
###xml 367 372 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
###xml 986 990 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Marfan syndrome, a disorder of connective tissue architecture with prominent manifestations in the skeletal, ocular and cardiovascular systems, is caused by mutations in the fibrillin-1 gene [21], [32] or by loss of function mutations in TGFbeta receptor I or II [24], [25]. TGFbeta signaling is abnormally elevated in fibrillin-1-deficient mice [22], [23], [33] and human aortas [34] as well as TGFbeta receptor I and II deficiency [24]. Previous data from our laboratory have shown nuclear accumulation of phosphorylated Smad2, an indicator of activation of TGFbeta signaling, in the LRP1(TbetaR-V)-deficient vascular wall [15]. In the present study, we have reconfirmed these Marfan syndrome-like phenotypes, including elastic layer disruption, aorta elongation, and aneurysm formation in the presence of increased Smad2 phosphorylation when LRP1 is deficient in the SMCs. These phenotypic manifestations in the vascular wall were essentially abolished in smLRP1-/-; PDGFRbeta F2/F2 mice, however, the increased phosphorylation and nuclear translocation of Smad2 was not affected by the PDGFRbeta mutations. These findings indicate that TGFbeta activation through LRP1 precedes PDGFRbeta-dependent PI3K signaling, and that activation of TGFbeta signaling by itself is not sufficient to disrupt the vascular wall architecture. PDGFRbeta-dependent PI3K activation appears to be necessary for the expression of the Marfan-like phenotypes. Suppression of PI3K activation by PDGFRbeta prevents the Marfan-like phenotypic changes in the vascular wall in the presence of unabated TGFbeta signaling, suggesting a pivotal role of LRP1-controlled and PDGFRbeta-dependent PI3K activation in the pathogenesis of Marfan syndrome. Selective elimination of PDGFRbeta-dependent PI3K activation thus could be a potential therapeutic target for both atherosclerosis and Marfan syndrome.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
In conclusion, the current study reveals a novel PI3K-dependent mechanism by which LRP1 is essential for controlling the integrity of the vascular wall, and by which this multifunctional receptor potently protects against atherosclerosis and Marfan syndrome. The findings we have presented here shed new light on the molecular mechanisms that control cellular growth and migration, and which are thereby essential to the remodeling and repair of the vascular wall and for slowing or preventing degenerative disorders of the vascular wall.
###end p 57

SMC migration was measured using a 12-well modified Boyden chamber (AA12, Neuro Probe) hosting a polycarbonate filter with 8-microm pores (PFB8, Neuro Probe) as described [39]. 3x104 cells in 100 microl were loaded into the top chamber of each well while the lower chambers were filled with SMC medium. After incubating at 37degreesC in 5% CO2 for 6 hours, non-migrated cells were scraped from the upper surface of the filter. Cells on the lower surface were fixed with 95% ethanol and stained with Harris Modified Hematoxylin (HHS-16, Sigma). The number of SMCs on the lower surface of the filter was determined by counting five continuous high-power (200x) fields of constant area per well. Experiments were performed three times in duplicate wells.
###end p 77
###begin title 78
Two-dimensional migration (scratch) assay
###end title 78
###begin p 79
###xml 16 17 16 17 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">5</sup>
###xml 171 172 161 162 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
Subcultured 3x105 SMCs were seeded into 60 mm Petri dishes. 10 microg/ml mitomycin C was applied to inhibit cell proliferation. Cells were incubated at 37degreesC in 5% CO2 overnight. The next day, part of the dish was denuded by scratching along a straight line. The dishes were put back to the incubator for 24 hours. The cells which appeared on the denuded area were treated as migrated cells. To quantify the migrated cells, the cells in five continuous high-power (200x) fields were counted and statistical analysis was performed.
###end p 79
###begin title 80

###end title 80
###begin p 81
###xml 125 126 125 126 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">4</sup>
This experiment was performed as described above in the Boyden chamber transmigration assay with the exception that only 1x104 cells were seeded. In addition, 10 ng/ml PDGF-BB was administrated to the lower chambers as an attractant.
