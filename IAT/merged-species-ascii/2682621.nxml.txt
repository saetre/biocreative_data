###begin article-title 0
The Dynamics and Mechanisms of Interleukin-1alpha and beta Nuclear Import
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
Re-use of this article is permitted in accordance with the Creative Commons Deed, Attribution 2.5, which does not permit commercial exploitation.
###end p 1
###begin p 2
###xml 233 238 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
Pro-inflammatory members of the interleukin-1 (IL-1) family of cytokines (IL-1alpha and beta) are important mediators of host defense responses to infection but can also exacerbate the damaging inflammation that contributes to major human diseases. IL-1alpha and beta are produced by cells of the innate immune system, such as macrophages, and act largely after their secretion by binding to the type I IL-1 receptor on responsive cells. There is evidence that IL-1alpha is also a nuclear protein that can act intracellularly. In this study, we report that both IL-1alpha and IL-1beta produced by microglia (central nervous system macrophages) in response to an inflammatory challenge are distributed between the cytosol and the nucleus. Using IL-1-beta-galactosidase and IL-1-green fluorescent protein chimeras (analyzed by fluorescence recovery after photobleaching), we demonstrate that nuclear import of IL-1alpha is exclusively active, requiring a nuclear localization sequence and Ran, while IL-1beta nuclear import is entirely passive. These data provide valuable insights into the dynamic regulation of intracellular cytokine trafficking.
###end p 2
###begin p 3
###xml 306 309 292 295 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1">(1)</xref>
Inflammation is generally a beneficial response of an organism to an injury or an infection. However, dysregulated inflammation can worsen disease progression and outcome. Under both circumstances, the pro-inflammatory cytokines interleukin-1alpha and beta (IL-1alpha/beta) are key drivers of inflammation (1).
###end p 3
###begin p 4
###xml 352 353 331 332 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2">2</xref>
###xml 422 423 401 402 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3">3</xref>
###xml 564 565 543 544 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b4">4</xref>
###xml 666 667 641 642 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b5">5</xref>
###xml 668 670 643 645 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b11">11</xref>
IL-1alpha and beta are produced by cells of the innate immune system in response to an inflammatory challenge or stress, as precursor proteins termed pro-IL-1alpha and pro-IL-1beta. Pro-IL-1beta requires cleavage by the enzyme caspase-1 to produce an active molecule at the type I IL-1 receptor (IL-1R1). In contrast, pro-IL-1alpha is active at IL-1R1 2, although it also exists as a mature form after cleavage by calpain 3. Most reported effects of these proteins occur after secretion of the active IL-1 molecule through the 'classical' IL-1R1 signaling pathway 4. However, several groups report effects of IL-1alpha that are dependent on its nuclear localization 5-11. IL-1alpha can therefore be regarded as a dual function cytokine. In contrast, nothing is known about IL-1beta as a nuclear protein.
###end p 4
###begin p 5
###xml 175 179 175 179 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b12">(12)</xref>
###xml 239 243 239 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b13">(13)</xref>
###xml 288 292 284 288 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14">(14)</xref>
###xml 449 453 438 442 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b15">(15)</xref>
Nuclear import of proteins requires a nuclear localization sequence (NLS). The NLS is bound by cytosolic importins, which facilitate transport across the nuclear pore complex (12). This process is driven by the gradient of Ran-GTP:Ran-GDP (13). IL-1alpha contains a NLS in its pro-domain (14), thus suggesting an active import mechanism. However, both IL-1beta and IL-1alpha are small enough (each is less than 50 kD) to passively enter the nucleus (15).
###end p 5
###begin p 6
###xml 77 81 77 81 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b16">(16)</xref>
###xml 518 522 504 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b17">(17)</xref>
In an injured brain, IL-1 can influence inflammation independently of IL-1R1 (16). Thus, we tested the initial hypothesis that pro-IL-1alpha localized to the nuclei of microglia after an inflammatory challenge. Surprisingly, not only pro-IL-1alpha but also pro-IL-1beta was detected inside the nucleus. We therefore investigated whether the pro-IL-1beta nuclear trafficking was active or passive and established the dynamic nature of pro-IL-1 import mechanisms. Using fluorescence recovery after photobleaching (FRAP) (17), we discovered that pro-IL-1alpha undergoes NLS and Ran-dependent active transport, while pro-IL-1beta transport is passive.
###end p 6
###begin title 7
Results and Discussion
###end title 7
###begin title 8
Both pro-IL-1alpha and pro-IL-1beta are intranuclear cytokines in microglia
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 74 76 67 69 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b18">18</xref>
###xml 235 237 224 226 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19">19</xref>
###xml 238 240 227 229 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b22">22</xref>
###xml 631 640 598 607 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SD1">Figure S1</xref>
In the injured brain, microglia are the major source of IL-1alpha and beta18, which are thought to act exclusively post-secretion by IL-1R1. However, endogenous IL-1alpha is reported to localize to the nucleus of many other cell types 19-22, and the subcellular distribution of IL-1alpha and beta in microglia is unknown. We therefore induced endogenous pro-IL-1alpha and beta expression in BV-2 microglia by stimulation with bacterial endotoxin (lipopolysaccharide, LPS, 1 mug/mL, 6 h). Under these conditions, both IL-1 isoforms were present exclusively as the precursors pro-IL-1alpha and pro-IL-1beta (confirmed by immunoblot; Figure S1). Pro-IL-1 subcellular localization was then characterized by immunocytochemistry.
###end p 9
###begin p 10
###xml 207 218 200 211 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Figure 1A,B</xref>
###xml 362 364 348 350 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b23">23</xref>
###xml 492 501 471 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SD1">Figure S1</xref>
###xml 592 601 564 573 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SD2">Figure S2</xref>
###xml 736 745 694 703 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SD2">Figure S2</xref>
###xml 730 734 <span type="species:ncbi:10090">mice</span>
Using a confocal microscope to view z-sections at the level of the nucleus, we established that pro-IL-1alpha and beta were detected in both the cytosol and the nuclei of BV-2 microglia after LPS treatment (Figure 1A,B). In agreement with our previous data, we did not detect concentration of endogenous IL-1alpha or beta in any organelle other than the nucleus 23. Nuclear localization of pro-IL-1alpha and beta in BV-2 microglia was confirmed by cell fractionation and immunoblot analysis (Figure S1). We observed identical pro-IL-1alpha and beta nuclear localization in primary microglia (Figure S2). IL-1alpha and beta immunostaining was absent in untreated cells and in microglia isolated from IL-1alpha/beta double knockout mice (Figure S2), confirming the specificity of the intranuclear staining. While endogenously expressed IL-1alpha is reported widely to localize to cell nuclei, this detection of intranuclear IL-1beta was unexpected and raised the question of how IL-1beta enters the nucleus.

###begin title 13
NLS-dependent IL-1 trafficking
###end title 13
###begin p 14
###xml 127 131 123 127 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14">(14)</xref>
###xml 133 143 129 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Figure 1Ci</xref>
###xml 328 332 321 325 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b24">(24)</xref>
###xml 334 344 327 337 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig01">Figure 1Ci</xref>
Previous studies have validated the presence of a NLS in the pro-domain of pro-IL-1alpha that directs its nuclear localization (14) (Figure 1Ci). However, the mechanisms governing pro-IL-1beta nuclear localization remain completely unknown, although it is suggested to contain a putative NLS in the active domain of the protein (24) (Figure 1Cii). We therefore tested whether pro-IL-1beta nuclear localization was NLS driven.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 97 101 97 101 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b15">(15)</xref>
###xml 426 430 404 408 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b25">(25)</xref>
###xml 380 395 <span type="species:ncbi:10633">simian virus 40</span>
The nuclear pore complex is suggested to limit the passive entry of molecules greater than 50 kD (15). Because both pro-IL-1alpha and beta are 31 kD, we generated pro-IL-1-beta-galactosidase fusion proteins (147 kD in size) to limit passive entry into the nucleus. COS-7 and HeLa cells were transfected to express beta-galactosidase (betagal), betagal with an N-terminal NLS from simian virus 40 large T antigen (nls-betagal) (25), pro-IL-1alpha-betagal fusion (IL-1alpha-betagal), pro-IL-1beta-betagal (IL-1beta-betagal), NLS mutant K85E pro-IL-1alpha-betagal (mIL-1alpha-betagal) and a pro-IL-1beta-beta-galactosidase in which the putative NLS was mutated (K210E, mIL-1beta-betagal). Immunoblot of transfected cell lysates confirmed expression of the appropriately sized chimeric proteins (unpublished data).
###end p 15

To quantify the subcellular distribution of the chimeric proteins, wide-field fluorescence images of betagal immunostaining were taken blind and counted. Cells were scored as containing either nuclear, nuclear and cytosolic or cytosolic betagal immunostain (Table 1). These data show that only nls-betagal and IL-1alpha-betagal had exclusively nuclear localization, while the other chimeric betagal proteins had either a nuclear and cytosolic or exclusively cytosolic distribution (Table 1).

###begin p 23
###xml 308 315 292 299 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1</xref>

Similar results were obtained when fusion proteins were expressed in HeLa cells (Table 1). These effects on nuclear localization are further illustrated by representative confocal micrographs (Figure 2) of transfected COS-7 cells and suggest that the putative NLS of pro-IL-1beta is not an NLS and that its nuclear localization may not be regulated by an active process.

###begin title 26
Dynamic nuclear import of IL-1
###end title 26
###begin p 27
To further establish the mechanisms of nuclear IL-1 trafficking, we utilized live cell confocal microscopy to study the dynamic nature of IL-1 transport. The wild-type (WT) and mutant forms of pro-IL-1 used above were subcloned into a green fluorescent protein (GFP) expression vector and were used to transiently transfect COS-7 cells. Immunoblot of transfected cell lysates confirmed that the IL-1-GFP chimeras were the correct size (unpublished data).

###xml 121 122 113 114 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 122 125 114 117 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1/2</sub>
###xml 155 156 145 146 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 156 159 146 149 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1/2</sub>
###xml 282 283 262 263 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 283 286 263 266 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1/2</sub>
###xml 579 588 537 546 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig04">Figure 4A</xref>
###xml 681 682 633 634 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 682 685 634 637 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1/2</sub>
###xml 783 784 730 731 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 784 787 731 734 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1/2</sub>
IL-1alpha-GFP nuclear import occurred at a similar rate to that of GFP alone despite being twice the size (IL-1alpha-GFP t1/2 = 53.7 +/- 27.5 seconds, GFP t1/2 = 39.7 +/- 11.9 seconds). Nuclear import was significantly slowed by mutation of the NLS in pro-IL-1alpha (mIL-1alpha-GFP t1/2 = 198.7 +/- 108.8 seconds, p < 0.001 versus IL-1alpha-GFP), consistent with a reduced efficiency of active nuclear import. Mutation of the IL-1alpha NLS also led to a redistribution of IL-1alpha-GFP from the nucleus to the cytoplasm (compare IL-1alpha-GFP and mIL-1alpha-GFP localizations in Figure 4A). In contrast, IL-1beta-GFP import was significantly slower than that of GFP alone (IL-1betat1/2 = 105.1 +/- 43.3 seconds, p < 0.05) and was unaffected by mutation of the putative NLS (mIL-1betat1/2 = 84.3 +/- 48.4 seconds). This, combined with the IL-1-betagal study above, indicates that IL-1beta import occurs independently of this NLS and adds weight to the hypothesis that IL-1beta import, unlike IL-1alpha import, occurs by passive diffusion.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 207 211 200 204 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b30">(30)</xref>
###xml 319 323 312 316 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b13">(13)</xref>
###xml 617 627 610 620 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Figure 5Ai</xref>
###xml 703 713 692 702 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Figure 5Ai</xref>
To confirm that IL-1alpha and beta utilize different mechanisms for nuclear import, we coexpressed IL-1-GFP fusions with a dominant-negative isoform of Ran, RanQ69L, which lacks the ability to hydrolyse GTP (30). RanQ69L is known to inhibit active nuclear import but should leave import by passive diffusion unaffected (13). To identify Ran-expressing cells, we fused WT and Q69L Ran to mCherry. When expressed, mCherry-RanWT was predominantly cytosolic with some in the nucleus, whereas mCherry-RanQ69L was strongly nuclear. While overexpression of RanWT had no effect on the localization of any IL-1-GFP construct (Figure 5Ai-iv), RanQ69L expression reduced the nuclear localization of IL-1alpha-GFP (Figure 5Aii).
###end p 38
###begin title 39

###end title 39
###begin p 40
###xml 280 281 268 269 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>

###xml 492 497 478 483 <sc xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">anova</sc>

###begin p 41
###xml 198 207 198 207 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Figure 5B</xref>
###xml 313 314 309 310 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 314 317 310 313 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1/2</sub>
###xml 590 591 570 571 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
###xml 591 594 571 574 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">1/2</sub>
###xml 747 756 719 728 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig03">Figure 3C</xref>
We then carried out nuclear FRAP on IL-1-GFP-positive cells coexpressing mCherry-Ran. As expected, overexpression of RanWT or RanQ69L had no effect on the passive diffusion of GFP into the nucleus (Figure 5B). In contrast, IL-1alpha-GFP nuclear import was significantly impaired by the overexpression of RanQ69L (t1/2 increased from 58.1 +/- 29.8 to 323.3 +/- 145.1 seconds, p < 0.001 versus IL-1alpha-GFP expressed alone). Overexpression of RanWT did not significantly inhibit IL-1alpha-GFP import. mIL-1alpha-GFP nuclear import was also significantly inhibited by RanQ69L overexpression (t1/2 increased from 215.1 +/- 131.7 seconds to 587.1 +/- 340.9 seconds, p < 0.001). This suggests that the slow nuclear import of mIL-1alpha-GFP observed in Figure 3C represents a reduction in the efficiency of active import but that IL-1alpha does not enter the nucleus passively.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 65 74 62 71 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Figure 5B</xref>
IL-1beta-GFP import was unaffected by overexpression of RanQ69L (Figure 5B), confirming that IL-1alpha and beta do indeed use different mechanisms for nuclear import - active, NLS-driven and Ran-dependent active import for IL-1alpha and passive import for IL-1beta.
###end p 42
###begin title 43
IL-1beta: an intranuclear cytokine?
###end title 43
###begin p 44
###xml 447 467 420 440 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1 and Figure 2</xref>
###xml 591 606 564 579 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig04">Figures 4 and 5</xref>
###xml 693 694 662 663 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6">6</xref>
###xml 695 696 664 665 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9">9</xref>
###xml 697 699 666 668 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14">14</xref>
###xml 1174 1181 1124 1131 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl2">Table 2</xref>
We have shown in this study for the first time that both IL-1alpha and IL-1beta are intranuclear cytokines in microglia. Because IL-1beta nuclear localization had not previously been reported and the mechanism of IL-1beta import remained unknown, we investigated whether IL-1alpha and beta utilized similar nuclear import mechanisms. We have found that this is not the case. IL-1alpha can drive betagal nuclear import in an NLS-dependent fashion (Table 1 and Figure 2) and has an import rate that is dependent on the integrity of its NLS and upon an unperturbed nucleocytoplasmic Ran cycle (Figures 4 and 5). This fits well with previous reports that IL-1alpha nuclear import is NLS dependent 6,9,14 and confirms that IL-1alpha import uses the classical nuclear import machinery. The extremely slow rate of NLS mutant IL-1alpha import in comparison to the similarly sized IL-1beta, and the further slowing of this rate by RanQ69L overexpression, suggests that IL-1alpha only uses this active import mechanism and perhaps that the engagement by proteins of the active import machinery can inhibit their passive diffusion into the nucleus. The reduced IL-1alpha cytosolic Mf (Table 2) could be accounted for by this interaction with the import machinery.
###end p 44
###begin p 45
###xml 216 243 201 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="tbl1">Table 1 and Figures 2 and 4</xref>
###xml 521 529 493 501 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig05">Figure 5</xref>
In contrast, IL-1beta failed to drive betagal nuclear import, and mutation of the putative NLS in its mature domain had no effect either on the localization of IL-1beta-betagal or on the import rate of IL-1beta-GFP (Table 1 and Figures 2 and 4). This suggested that the IL-1beta putative NLS was not in fact capable of engaging with nuclear import machinery. RanQ69L overexpression had no effect on the import of IL-1beta, confirming that IL-1beta nuclear import occurs passively, in contrast to IL-1alpha active import (Figure 5).
###end p 45
###begin p 46
###xml 70 78 63 71 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="fig04">Figure 4</xref>
IL-1alpha-GFP and IL-1-beta-GFP import rates were remarkably similar (Figure 4), given that the two proteins enter the nucleus by different mechanisms. It appears that the active import of IL-1alpha serves to concentrate IL-1alpha inside the nucleus, against a concentration gradient, rather than to speed up its import.
###end p 46
###begin p 47
###xml 113 115 113 115 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b31">31</xref>
###xml 116 118 116 118 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b32">32</xref>
###xml 479 482 458 461 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1">(1)</xref>
###xml 580 581 551 552 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6">6</xref>
###xml 582 584 553 555 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10">10</xref>
###xml 585 587 556 558 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b33">33</xref>
Recent reports that the IL-1 family members IL-33 and IL-1F7b have intranuclear actions in IL-1-expressing cells 31,32 support the hypothesis that many IL-1 family members, and not just IL-1alpha, may be dual function cytokines. Our finding that IL-1beta, like IL-1alpha, is an intranuclear cytokine raises questions as to whether IL-1beta also has intranuclear actions. Although IL-1alpha and beta mature domains share structural similarity and both bind to and activate IL-1R1 (1), the IL-1alpha pro-domain is responsible for many of the reported IL-1alpha intranuclear actions 6-10,33, and this domain shares little sequence homology with the pro-domain of IL-1beta. Further investigation is now required to determine whether IL-1beta has a separate set of intranuclear actions.
###end p 47

###begin title 59
Fluorescence recovery after photobleaching
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 119 120 113 114 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
FRAP experiments were carried out 18-36 h after COS-7 cell transfection. Cells were maintained at 37degreesC with 5% CO2 throughout experiments. Confocal settings were as follows; argon laser power was set to 80%, scan speed was 700 Hz unidirectional, zoom was x1.7 and GFP fluorescence was imaged during the experiment utilizing 6-8% of the 488 laser line power.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
For cytoplasmic FRAP, a cytoplasmic ROI (9.6 x 9.1 mum) was defined. Twenty prebleach 256 x 256 pixel images were acquired followed by 10 ROI bleach cycles over 3.8 seconds using 100% of the 458, 476 and 488 laser lines. Recovery was followed over a further 50 post-bleach frames, all acquired at maximum speed (2.6 frames/second).
###end p 61
###begin p 62

Because of rapid intranuclear IL-1-GFP diffusion, this led to complete nuclear bleach. Recovery was then followed by taking images every 2-4 seconds until the intranuclear fluorescence asymptote was reached.

###xml 0 84 0 77 <bold xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">Figure S1: Detection of pro-IL-1&#945; and &#946; in BV-2 microglial nuclear fractions.</bold>
Figure S1: Detection of pro-IL-1alpha and beta in BV-2 microglial nuclear fractions. IL-1alpha and beta immunoblot (A), poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP, nuclear marker protein) immunoblot (B) and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH, cytosolic marker protein) assay (C) of nuclear and cytoplasmic fractions from BV-2 cells treated with LPS (1 mug/mL, 1-24 h). All data shown are from one representative experiment of three independent experiments. Cells were lysed (10 min, 4degreesC) in 1% Triton-X-100 (v/v) with 50 mM Tris-Hcl, pH 7.5, 50 mM beta glycerophosphate, 1 mM sodium orthovanadate, 5 mM sodium fluoride and protease inhibitors. Nuclei were isolated by centrifugation (5 min, 1500 x g, 4degreesC). Remaining debris was removed from supernatants by a further centrifugation (10 min, 10 000 x g, 4degreesC). Supernatants were then saved as cytoplasmic fractions. Nuclear pellets were washed twice prior to the extraction of nuclear proteins into lysis buffer with 0.5 M NaCl. Remaining nuclear debris was pelleted by centrifugation (10 min, 10 000 x g, 4degreesC), and the supernatant was saved as the nuclear fraction.
