###begin article-title 0
Black Carbon Exposure, Oxidative Stress Genes, and Blood Pressure in a Repeated-Measures Study
###end article-title 0
###begin p 1
The authors declare they have no competing financial interests.
###end p 1
###begin title 2
Background
###end title 2
###begin p 3
Particulate matter (PM) air pollution has been associated with cardiovascular morbidity and mortality, and elevated blood pressure (BP) is a known risk factor for cardiovascular disease. A small number of studies have investigated the relationship between PM and BP and found mixed results. Evidence suggests that traffic-related air pollution contributes significantly to PM-related cardiovascular effects.
###end p 3
###begin title 4
Objectives
###end title 4
###begin p 5
###xml 179 182 176 179 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 259 271 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
We hypothesized that black carbon (BC), a traffic-related combustion by-product, would be more strongly associated with BP than would fine PM [aerodynamic diameter </= 2.5 mum (PM2.5)], a heterogeneous PM mixture, and that these effects would be larger among participants with genetic variants associated with impaired antioxidative defense.
###end p 5
###begin title 6
Methods
###end title 6
###begin p 7
###xml 91 94 91 94 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 316 321 316 321 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 323 328 323 328 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 330 335 330 335 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTT1</italic>
###xml 337 341 337 341 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NQO1</italic>
###xml 357 363 357 363 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">HMOX-1</italic>
###xml 53 56 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">men</span>
We performed a repeated-measures analysis in elderly men to analyze associations between PM2.5 and BC exposure and BP using mixed-effects models with random intercepts, adjusting for potential confounders. We also examined statistical interaction between BC and genetic variants related to oxidative stress defense: GSTM1, GSTP1, GSTT1, NQO1, catalase, and HMOX-1.
###end p 7
###begin title 8
Results
###end title 8
###begin p 9
###xml 328 331 328 331 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
A 1-SD increase in BC concentration was associated with a 1.5-mmHg increase in systolic BP [95% confidence interval (CI), 0.1-2.8] and a 0.9-mmHg increase in diastolic BP (95% CI, 0.2-1.6). We observed no evidence of statistical interaction between BC and any of the genetic variants examined and found no association between PM2.5 and BP.
###end p 9
###begin title 10
Conclusions
###end title 10
###begin p 11
###xml 78 81 78 81 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
We observed positive associations between BP and BC, but not between BP and PM2.5, and found no evidence of effect modification of the association between BC and BP by gene variants related to antioxidative defense.
###end p 11
###begin p 12
###xml 138 148 138 148 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b4-ehp-117-1767">Brook 2008</xref>
###xml 150 168 150 168 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b25-ehp-117-1767">Miller et al. 2007</xref>
###xml 170 186 170 186 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b31-ehp-117-1767">Pope et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 319 340 319 340 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b15-ehp-117-1767">Gurgueira et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 342 357 342 357 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b20-ehp-117-1767">Kim et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 359 367 359 367 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b26-ehp-117-1767">Nel 2005</xref>
###xml 396 414 396 414 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b7-ehp-117-1767">Chuang et al. 2007</xref>
###xml 416 436 416 436 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b12-ehp-117-1767">Dubowsky et al. 2006</xref>
###xml 438 457 438 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b28-ehp-117-1767">O&#8217;Neill et al. 2007</xref>
###xml 459 478 459 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b33-ehp-117-1767">R&#252;ckerl et al. 2006</xref>
###xml 564 582 564 582 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b9-ehp-117-1767">Devlin et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 584 600 584 600 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14-ehp-117-1767">Gold et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 602 618 602 618 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b30-ehp-117-1767">Park et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 648 664 648 664 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b44-ehp-117-1767">Urch et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 666 687 666 687 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b46-ehp-117-1767">Zanobetti et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 891 914 891 914 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2-ehp-117-1767">Auchincloss et al. 2008</xref>
###xml 916 932 916 932 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6-ehp-117-1767">Choi et al. 2007</xref>
###xml 934 957 934 957 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b17-ehp-117-1767">Ibald-Mulli et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 959 980 959 980 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b46-ehp-117-1767">Zanobetti et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 996 1015 996 1015 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b16-ehp-117-1767">Harrabi et al. 2006</xref>
###xml 1017 1040 1017 1040 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b18-ehp-117-1767">Ibald-Mulli et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 1042 1060 1042 1060 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b23-ehp-117-1767">Madsen et al. 2006</xref>
Cardiovascular morbidity and mortality have been associated with particulate matter (PM) air pollution in numerous epidemiologic studies (Brook 2008; Miller et al. 2007; Pope et al. 2004). Several pathways have been proposed to explain these associations, including, at the molecular level, increased oxidative stress (Gurgueira et al. 2002; Kim et al. 2004; Nel 2005) and systemic inflammation (Chuang et al. 2007; Dubowsky et al. 2006; O'Neill et al. 2007; Ruckerl et al. 2006). At the functional level, potential pathways include changes in autonomic function (Devlin et al. 2003; Gold et al. 2005; Park et al. 2005) and in blood pressure (BP) (Urch et al. 2005; Zanobetti et al. 2004). Elevated BP is a major risk factor for coronary heart disease and stroke. To date, only a few studies have examined the role of PM air pollution in BP elevation, and these have revealed both positive (Auchincloss et al. 2008; Choi et al. 2007; Ibald-Mulli et al. 2001; Zanobetti et al. 2004) and negative (Harrabi et al. 2006; Ibald-Mulli et al. 2004; Madsen et al. 2006) findings.
###end p 12
###begin p 13
###xml 303 313 303 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b4-ehp-117-1767">Brook 2008</xref>
###xml 440 457 440 457 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b21-ehp-117-1767">Laden et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 459 479 459 479 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b34-ehp-117-1767">Schwartz et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 481 508 481 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b47-ehp-117-1767">Zanobetti and Schwartz 2006</xref>
The heterogeneous composition of PM, which includes a complex mixture of particles and liquid droplets consisting of organic chemicals, acids, metals, and dust particles, may explain some of the variation observed in these studies as a result of differences in PM composition across geographic regions (Brook 2008). Growing evidence suggests that traffic-related PM pollution contributes significantly to PM-related cardiovascular effects (Laden et al. 2000; Schwartz et al. 2005; Zanobetti and Schwartz 2006).
###end p 13
###begin p 14
###xml 176 179 173 176 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 226 246 223 243 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b10-ehp-117-1767">Dockery et al. 2005a</xref>
###xml 248 253 245 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b11-ehp-117-1767">2005b</xref>
###xml 255 271 252 268 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b14-ehp-117-1767">Gold et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 273 292 270 289 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b29-ehp-117-1767">O&#8217;Neill et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 294 314 291 311 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b34-ehp-117-1767">Schwartz et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 411 438 408 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b47-ehp-117-1767">Zanobetti and Schwartz 2006</xref>
###xml 476 483 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">persons</span>
Black carbon (BC) is a traffic-related particle produced as a combustion by-product and has been associated more strongly than has fine PM [aerodynamic diameter </= 2.5 mum (PM2.5)] with a number of cardiovascular end points (Dockery et al. 2005a, 2005b; Gold et al. 2005; O'Neill et al. 2005; Schwartz et al. 2005). BC is also associated with increased risk of emergency myocardial infarction hospitalization (Zanobetti and Schwartz 2006). No studies conducted among healthy persons have previously assessed the relation between BC and BP outcomes.
###end p 14
###begin p 15
###xml 165 168 165 168 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 242 252 242 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b4-ehp-117-1767">Brook 2008</xref>
###xml 254 274 254 274 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b35-ehp-117-1767">S&#248;rensen et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 454 457 454 457 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 351 363 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
We hypothesized that BC would elevate BP in our cohort and that this PM component may in part explain the mixed results observed in the association between BP and PM2.5. Because PM air pollution may elevate BP by increasing oxidative stress (Brook 2008; Sorensen et al. 2003), we also hypothesized that the effects of BC on BP would be stronger among participants with impaired anti-oxidative defense. Therefore, we examined the associations of BC and PM2.5 with BP in a repeated-measures analysis and investigated the possibility of effect modification of the association between BC and BP by gene variants related to oxidative stress defense.
###end p 15
###begin title 16
Materials and Methods
###end title 16
###begin title 17
Study population
###end title 17
###begin p 18
###xml 201 217 201 217 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b3-ehp-117-1767">Bell et al. 1972</xref>
###xml 1158 1161 1158 1161 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 10 22 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
###xml 500 512 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Participants</span>
###xml 634 646 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
###xml 722 734 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
###xml 780 792 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
###xml 877 889 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
###xml 1071 1083 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
###xml 1334 1346 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
Our study participants were from the Veterans Administration Normative Aging Study. This is an ongoing longitudinal study of aging established in 1963, details of which have been published previously (Bell et al. 1972). Briefly, the Normative Aging Study is a closed cohort of 2,280 male volunteers from the Greater Boston, Massachusetts, area who were 21-80 years of age at entry and who enrolled after an initial health screening determined that they were free of known chronic medical conditions. Participants were reevaluated every 3-5 years using detailed on-site physical examinations and questionnaires. BP was measured in all participants still presenting for examination between April 1999 and December 2007 (791 participants, 1,568 clinical examinations). Some of these participants presented only once during this time period, and our analysis was restricted to 461 participants (1,067 clinical examinations) with complete information regarding ambient BC concentrations and all covariates for at least two study visits, and a largely overlapping group of 457 participants (949 clinical examinations) with complete information regarding ambient PM2.5 concentrations and all covariates for at least two study visits. This study was reviewed and approved by the institutional review boards of participating institutions. All participants provided written informed consent.
###end p 18
###begin title 19
Physical parameters and medical history
###end title 19
###begin p 20
###xml 736 752 736 752 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b22-ehp-117-1767">Leon et al. 1977</xref>
###xml 252 264 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Participants</span>
Study center visits were conducted in the morning after an overnight fast and abstention from smoking. Physical examinations included measurement of height and weight, and body mass index was calculated as [weight (kilograms)/height (meters) squared]. Participants were classified as having diabetes based on a physician's diagnosis of type 2 diabetes and/or use of diabetes medications. Questionnaires collected details on smoking habits and medication use, with responses confirmed by an on-site physician. Alcohol intake was determined using a semiquantitative food frequency questionnaire. Serum fasting glucose concentrations were measured using the hexokinase method, with measurements performed in duplicate on an auto-analyzer (Leon et al. 1977).
###end p 20
###begin title 21
BP measurements
###end title 21
###begin p 22
###xml 244 255 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participant</span>
###xml 335 346 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participant</span>
###xml 500 507 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patient</span>
At each clinical visit, a physician measured BP using a standard mercury sphygmomanometer with a 14-cm cuff. Systolic blood pressure (SBP) and fifth-phase diastolic blood pressure (DBP) were measured in each arm to the nearest 2 mmHg while the participant was seated. The means of the right and left arm measurements were used as each participant's systolic and diastolic BP. Although there was no specific rest period before measurement of BP, SBP and DBP were measured immediately after a complete patient history was taken with the subject seated.
###end p 22
###begin title 23
Genotyping
###end title 23
###begin title 24
GSTM1
###end title 24
###begin p 25
###xml 31 32 31 32 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</italic>
###xml 51 56 51 56 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 100 113 100 113 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b37-ehp-117-1767">UniGene 2009a</xref>
###xml 256 261 256 261 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 463 469 463 469 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CYP1A1</italic>
###xml 490 503 490 503 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b38-ehp-117-1767">UniGene 2009b</xref>
###xml 562 568 562 568 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">CYP1A1</italic>
###xml 664 669 664 669 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
Genotyping for the glutathione S-transferase mu-1 (GSTM1) deletion polymorphism (UniGene Hs.301961; UniGene 2009a) was done at the Harvard School of Public Health. The assay consists of polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification of exons 4 and 5 of the GSTM1 allele. Because this polymorphism is a gene deletion, the PCR product indicates the presence of one or more copies of the gene. In each case, concomitant amplification of the cytochrome P450 1A1 gene (CYP1A1) (UniGene Hs.72912; UniGene 2009b) was done as a positive control. The PCR amplification of CYP1A1 results in a 312 base-pair product that is easily visualized in the presence or absence of the GSTM1 273 base-pair PCR product. The DNA template was prepared by mixing whole blood (0.5-1.0 muL) with reaction buffer and primers, followed by incubation. To this, 2.5 U Amplitaq was added; PCR was then performed, yielding the 273 base-pair product.
###end p 25
###begin title 26
GSTP1
###end title 26
###begin p 27
###xml 75 76 75 76 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</italic>
###xml 100 105 100 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 127 140 127 140 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b39-ehp-117-1767">UniGene 2009c</xref>
###xml 364 385 364 385 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b1-ehp-117-1767">Ali-Osman et al. 1997</xref>
###xml 619 634 619 634 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b36-ehp-117-1767">Sun et al. 2000</xref>
We genotyped two single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) on the glutathione S-transferase pi-1 gene (GSTP1) (UniGene Hs.523836; UniGene 2009c): Ile105Val (A313G, rs1695), caused by an A-to-G substitution that changes codon 105 from ATC (Ile) to GTC (Val); and Ala114Val (C2293T, rs1799811), a C-to-T substitution that changes codon 114 from GCG (Ala) to GTG (Val) (Ali-Osman et al. 1997). Both SNPs were genotyped using unlabeled mini-sequencing reactions and mass spectrometry via a Sequenom Mass Array MALDI-TOF (matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization time-of-flight) mass spectrometer (Sequenom, San Diego, CA) (Sun et al. 2000).
###end p 27
###begin title 28
###xml 0 6 0 6 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">HMOX-1</italic>
###xml 11 12 11 12 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</sub>
HMOX-1 (GT)n repeat polymorphism
###end title 28
###begin p 29
###xml 22 28 22 28 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">HMOX-1</italic>
###xml 49 62 49 62 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b40-ehp-117-1767">UniGene 2009d</xref>
###xml 119 139 119 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b45-ehp-117-1767">Yamada et al. (2000)</xref>
###xml 259 264 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">human</span>
The heme oxygenase-1 (HMOX-1; UniGene Hs.517581; UniGene 2009d) short-tandem-repeat assay was designed as described by Yamada et al. (2000). Resulting PCR products were analyzed with a laser-based automated DNA sequencer, and the sizes were compared with the human genome sequence () to determine the number of repeats.
###end p 29
###begin title 30
###xml 0 4 0 4 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NQO1</italic>
NQO1 and catalase
###end title 30
###begin p 31
###xml 50 63 50 63 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b41-ehp-117-1767">UniGene 2009e</xref>
###xml 204 208 204 208 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NQO1</italic>
###xml 229 242 229 242 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b42-ehp-117-1767">UniGene 2009f</xref>
###xml 173 180 <span type="species:ncbi:50278|species:ncbi:153742">quinine</span>
SNPs within the catalase gene (UniGene Hs.502302; UniGene 2009e) [C/T (rs480575); C1167T (rs769217); C(-262)T (rs1001179); A/G (rs2284367); and A/G (rs2300181)] and NAD(P)H quinine oxidoreductase 1 gene (NQO1; UniGene Hs.406515; UniGene 2009f) [C609T (rs1800566)] were genotyped using the Sequenom MassArray MALDI-TOF mass spectrometer (Sequenom) with semiautomated primer design (SpectroDESIGNER, Sequenom).
###end p 31
###begin title 32
GSTT1
###end title 32
###begin p 33
###xml 166 167 166 167 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">S</italic>
###xml 194 199 194 199 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTT1</italic>
###xml 231 244 231 244 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b43-ehp-117-1767">UniGene 2009g</xref>
###xml 309 314 309 314 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTT1</italic>
###xml 369 374 369 374 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTT1</italic>
###xml 428 433 428 433 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTT1</italic>
Analyses were performed at the Harvard School of Public Health using the TaqMan 5' nuclease real-time quantitative PCR assay. Subjects were genotyped for glutathione S-transferase theta-1 gene (GSTT1) deletions (UniGene Hs.268573; UniGene 2009g). For this analysis, the following primers and probe were used: GSTT1 exon 1 forward primer (5'-caggtcagcacttaagcgatgc-3'), GSTT1 reverse primer (5'-cagcagtcctaggg tcccagtta-3'), and GSTT1 probe (5'-Famccttcaaggctggcaccttcttgagg-Tamra-3').
###end p 33
###begin title 34
###xml 6 9 6 9 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
BC, PM2.5, and meteorologic measurements
###end title 34
###begin p 35
###xml 20 23 20 23 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 320 323 320 323 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 492 495 486 489 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
Continuous BC and PM2.5 concentrations were measured at a Harvard School of Public Health monitoring site located at the Countway Library (Boston, MA), 1 km from the clinical examination site, and were averaged by hour before BP measurement. BC was measured using an aethalometer (Magee Scientific, Berkeley, CA), and PM2.5 was measured using a tapered element oscillating microbalance (model 1400A; Rupprecht & Pataschnick Co., East Greenbush, NY), operated at 50degreesC with two 4-L/min PM2.5 impactors before the inlet. We obtained temperature and relative humidity measures from the Boston airport weather station.
###end p 35
###begin title 36
Statistical methods
###end title 36
###begin p 37
###xml 47 50 47 50 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
We analyzed associations between both BC and PM2.5 exposures and BP in a repeated-measures study using linear mixed-effects models with random intercepts and a block-diagonal (compound symmetry) covariance structure. In this approach, individual-level covariates are allowed to explain differences across subjects. In addition, a separate random intercept is fitted for each subject, so essentially all differences across subjects are controlled and the estimates of association reported are effectively from within-subject differences.
###end p 37
###begin p 38
###xml 116 137 116 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b46-ehp-117-1767">Zanobetti et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 193 196 193 196 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 259 270 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participant</span>
Previous studies have suggested that longer averaging times are more relevant to the association between PM and BP (Zanobetti et al. 2004), so we used 7-day moving averages of ambient BC and PM2.5 concentrations matched on the time of BP measurement for each participant. As a sensitivity analysis, we also evaluated the relation between PM and BP using averaging times ranging from 1 hr to 7 days before BP measurement. We evaluated SBP and DBP as dependent variables.
###end p 38
###begin p 39
###xml 37 45 37 45 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">a priori</italic>
###xml 807 815 805 813 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">a priori</italic>
The following covariates were chosen a priori and included in the regression models regardless of statistical significance: age (years), cigarette smoking (never, current, or former), pack-years of smoking, body mass index (kilograms per square meter), alcohol intake (< 2 drinks per day vs. >/= 2 drinks per day), fasting glucose (millimoles), current statin use (yes/no), diabetes status (yes/no), race/ethnicity, years of education, hour of clinical visit, year of clinical visit, weekday of clinical visit, and season of clinical visit (spring: March, April, May; summer: June, July, August; fall: September, October, November; winter: December, January, February). We also included temperature (degrees Celsius; 7-day moving average) and relative humidity (percent; 7-day moving average) in our models a priori, along with their quadratic terms. Both temperature and relative humidity variables were centered on their means.
###end p 39
###begin p 40
###xml 89 97 89 97 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">a priori</italic>
We identified current antihypertensive medication use (yes/no) as a potential confounder a priori. Because these medications may be protective for the association between PM air pollution and BP, we also tested for interactions with the pollutants before entering the medication variable into models to control for confounding. In addition, we conducted a sensitivity analysis to examine the effect of including antihypertensive medication use in our regression models because of concern about adjusting for a potential downstream consequence of the BP outcome. Exclusion of this variable did not appreciably alter results (data not shown). After weighing the evidence, we decided to include a term for current antihypertensive medication use in all regressions.
###end p 40
###begin p 41
###xml 239 249 239 249 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b4-ehp-117-1767">Brook 2008</xref>
###xml 251 271 251 271 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b35-ehp-117-1767">S&#248;rensen et al. 2003</xref>
###xml 354 359 354 359 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 361 366 361 366 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 368 373 368 373 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTT1</italic>
###xml 375 381 375 381 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">HMOX-1</italic>
###xml 383 387 383 387 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">NQO1</italic>
We also examined effect modification of the association between BC and BP by gene variants related to oxidative stress defense, because of prior research findings regarding mechanisms mediating the hypertensive effect of PM air pollution (Brook 2008; Sorensen et al. 2003). We examined polymorphisms in the following antioxidative-defense-related genes: GSTM1, GSTP1, GSTT1, HMOX-1, NQO1, and catalase.
###end p 41
###begin p 42
###xml 33 38 33 38 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 43 48 43 48 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTT1</italic>
###xml 87 93 87 93 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">HMOX-1</italic>
###xml 113 114 113 114 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">n</sub>
To assess effect modification by GSTM1 and GSTT1 genotypes (wild type vs. null) and by HMOX-1 microsatellite (GT)n repeat length (< 25 repeats vs. >/= 25 repeats), we modeled separate regressions stratified by genotype and also added interaction terms between BC concentration and each genotype to respective main effects models. To assess effect modification by SNPs, we first aggregated information regarding each SNP and classified the genotypes in two different manners: categorical (wild-type vs. any variant allele) and continuous (0, 1, or 2 variant alleles). For both of these classifications, we modeled separate regressions stratified by genotype and also added an interaction term between BC and the aforementioned variables to respective main effects models.
###end p 42
###begin p 43
###xml 781 782 781 782 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">t</italic>
We conducted a simulation study to analyze our power to detect gene-environment interactions. We generated repeated measures of exposure as multivariate normal with a correlation of 0.2 across multiple measurements, which were separated by approximately 4 years on average in our cohort. We then generated random binomial data to represent genotype, varying prevalence rates and the interaction between exposure and genotype, and generated the outcome to be multivariate normal with a higher correlation between repeated measures (0.5). We took the main effect of exposure to be the average of the effect we found for DBP and SBP, and the variance of the outcome to be the mean of the variances of the two BP measures. We then performed a regression on the data and calculated the t-statistic for the interaction. Finally, we repeated this analysis 1,000 times for relative effect modification of 2 and 3 and took the power to be the fraction of time a significant interaction was found.
###end p 43
###begin title 44
Results
###end title 44
###begin title 45
Study population
###end title 45
###begin p 46
###xml 174 181 174 181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t1-ehp-117-1767">Table 1</xref>
###xml 335 336 335 336 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
###xml 454 457 454 457 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 526 527 526 527 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sup>
###xml 735 736 734 735 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sup>
###xml 748 751 747 750 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 778 779 776 777 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sup>
###xml 213 225 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
###xml 401 413 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">Participants</span>
Our study population consisted entirely of males, most of whom were former cigarette smokers and current antihypertensive medication users at their first study center visit (Table 1). At this initial examination, participants evaluated for the association between BC and BP had a mean age of 71 years, a mean body mass index of 28 kg/m2, and average SBP and DBP of 132 mmHg and 78 mmHg, respectively. Participants evaluated for the association between PM2.5 and BP had a mean age of 72 years, a mean body mass index of 28 kg/m2, and average SBP and DBP of 131 mmHg and 76 mmHg, respectively. In both of these groups, we averaged pollutant concentrations across all study center visits and found that mean BC concentration was 1.1 mug/m3 and mean PM2.5 concentration was 12 mug/m3.
###end p 46
###begin title 47
###xml 25 28 25 28 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
Associations of BC and PM2.5 concentrations with BP
###end title 47
###begin p 48
###xml 72 75 72 75 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 177 178 176 177 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sup>
###xml 200 201 198 199 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">3</sup>
###xml 208 211 206 209 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 254 261 252 259 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t2-ehp-117-1767">Table 2</xref>
###xml 404 405 402 403 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 468 469 466 467 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">p</italic>
###xml 535 538 533 536 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 562 569 560 567 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t2-ehp-117-1767">Table 2</xref>
###xml 665 668 663 666 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
We evaluated the associations of SBP and DBP with both ambient BC and PM2.5 concentrations and express the results as the change associated with a 1-SD (equivalent to 0.43 mug/m3 for BC and 4.98 mug/m3 for PM2.5) increment in exposure to each pollutant (Table 2). We found that a 1-SD increase in BC concentration was associated with a 1.46-mmHg increase in SBP [95% confidence interval (CI), 0.10-2.82; p = 0.0356] and a 0.87-mmHg increase in DBP (95% CI, 0.15-1.59; p = 0.0180). In contrast, we did not find an association between PM2.5 and either SBP or DBP (Table 2). For BC, associations were strongest using a 7-day moving average, and associations between PM2.5 and BP remained close to the null for all averaging times examined (data not shown).
###end p 48
###begin title 49
Effect modification by SNPs in oxidative stress genes
###end title 49
###begin p 50
###xml 129 137 129 137 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t3-ehp-117-1767">Tables 3</xref>
###xml 142 143 142 143 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="t4-ehp-117-1767">4</xref>
We observed no evidence of statistically significant interaction between BC concentration and any of the gene variants assessed (Tables 3 and 4) using either categorical or continuous classifications of SNPs.
###end p 50
###begin title 51
Effect modification by antihypertensive medication use
###end title 51
###begin p 52
###xml 58 61 58 61 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
We found no evidence of statistical interaction between PM2.5 and antihypertensive medication use (data not shown). Similarly, stratified estimates for the effects of BC on BP differed very little by use of antihypertensive medications and did not show a consistent difference for SBP and DBP (data not shown).
###end p 52
###begin title 53
Power to detect gene-environment interactions
###end title 53
###begin p 54
Our simulation study showed that, for a relative effect modification of 2, we had 47% power for a gene prevalence of 20% rising to 67% power for a gene prevalence of 50%. However, for a relative effect modification of 3, we had 81% power even with a gene prevalence of 20%.
###end p 54
###begin title 55
Discussion
###end title 55
###begin p 56
###xml 231 234 231 234 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 156 159 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">men</span>
###xml 481 493 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
We found that BC concentration averaged over the 7 days preceding each study center visit was positively associated with SBP and DBP in a cohort of elderly men. We did not observe an association between a 7-day moving average of PM2.5 and either SBP or DBP and did not find evidence of effect modification of the association between BC and BP by genotypes related to oxidative stress defense. Ours is one of few studies to assess the relation between PM air pollution and BP among participants otherwise healthy at baseline. BC is a traffic-related particle, and our findings of a relation between BC and BP, if confirmed, may have important implications because of widespread exposure to traffic emissions across the U.S. population.
###end p 56
###begin p 57
###xml 111 129 111 129 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19-ehp-117-1767">Jansen et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 384 387 384 387 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 389 410 389 410 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b46-ehp-117-1767">Zanobetti et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 674 695 674 695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b24-ehp-117-1767">McCracken et al. 2007</xref>
###xml 212 220 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">patients</span>
###xml 475 480 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">women</span>
A panel study of 16 elderly subjects with respiratory disease did not reveal an association between BC and BP (Jansen et al. 2005). Furthermore, although a repeated-measures analysis of 62 cardiac rehabilitation patients showed that 120-hr averages of ambient BC were positively associated with resting DBP in single-pollutant models, this association was found to be confounded by PM2.5 (Zanobetti et al. 2004). A randomized trial of an improved cook stove among Guatemalan women showed that the use of chimney woodstoves, which resulted in lower personal PM exposures, was associated with a reduction in SBP and DBP relative to a traditional open-wood-fire control group (McCracken et al. 2007). Wood smoke is an important source of BC exposure.
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 116 139 116 139 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2-ehp-117-1767">Auchincloss et al. 2008</xref>
###xml 141 157 141 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b6-ehp-117-1767">Choi et al. 2007</xref>
###xml 159 177 159 177 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b13-ehp-117-1767">Dvonch et al. 2009</xref>
###xml 179 202 179 202 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b17-ehp-117-1767">Ibald-Mulli et al. 2001</xref>
###xml 204 225 204 225 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b46-ehp-117-1767">Zanobetti et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 234 252 234 252 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b19-ehp-117-1767">Jansen et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 254 272 254 272 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b23-ehp-117-1767">Madsen et al. 2006</xref>
###xml 301 320 301 320 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b16-ehp-117-1767">Harrabi et al. 2006</xref>
###xml 322 345 322 345 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b18-ehp-117-1767">Ibald-Mulli et al. 2004</xref>
###xml 445 462 445 462 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b5-ehp-117-1767">Brook et al. 2002</xref>
###xml 464 480 464 480 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b44-ehp-117-1767">Urch et al. 2005</xref>
###xml 483 508 483 508 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b2-ehp-117-1767">Auchincloss et al. (2008)</xref>
###xml 539 542 539 542 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 608 628 608 628 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b13-ehp-117-1767">Dvonch et al. (2009)</xref>
###xml 672 675 672 675 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
The literature regarding the relation between PM and BP is inconsistent. Epidemiologic studies have shown positive (Auchincloss et al. 2008; Choi et al. 2007; Dvonch et al. 2009; Ibald-Mulli et al. 2001; Zanobetti et al. 2004), null (Jansen et al. 2005; Madsen et al. 2006), and inverse associations (Harrabi et al. 2006; Ibald-Mulli et al. 2004) between PM and BP end points, and the results of chamber studies have been inconsistent, as well (Brook et al. 2002; Urch et al. 2005). Auchincloss et al. (2008) report associations between PM2.5 and BP only in areas with a high impact from traffic. Similarly, Dvonch et al. (2009) found much stronger associations between PM2.5 and BP in an area with high traffic density relative to other sections of the same city. Discrepant findings between investigations may be attributable to differences in study design and population susceptibility, as well as varying composition of PM across study sites. In particular, investigations include cross-sectional studies that are more susceptible to confounding by factors varying across subjects, panel studies with small numbers of subjects but many repeated measures, and those like the present study, with a smaller number of repeated measures in a larger cohort.
###end p 58
###begin p 59
###xml 209 228 209 228 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b8-ehp-117-1767">Delfino et al. 2008</xref>
###xml 230 250 230 250 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b35-ehp-117-1767">S&#248;rensen et al. 2003</xref>
Research regarding mechanisms mediating BC's hypertensive effects is quite limited. Plasma markers of systemic oxidative stress and inflammation have been associated with BC exposure in epidemiologic studies (Delfino et al. 2008; Sorensen et al. 2003). However, much of our discussion regarding potential mechanisms of BC cardiotoxicity is informed by broader PM research. Our results suggest that BC may be primarily responsible for the association of PM with BP.
###end p 59
###begin p 60
###xml 901 906 901 906 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTM1</italic>
###xml 908 913 908 913 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTT1</italic>
###xml 919 924 919 924 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">GSTP1</italic>
###xml 110 122 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
In addition to examining effects of BC on BP, we also hypothesized that these effects would be stronger among participants with genetic variants associated with impaired oxidative stress defense and therefore evaluated statistical interactions between BC exposure and relevant gene variants. We did not find statistically significant evidence of effect modification by antioxidative defense genes in this analysis. Although our findings may suggest that the effect of BC on BP is not mediated by the oxidative stress-related mechanisms associated with the examined genetic variants, this analysis may not have had sufficient power to assess lower levels of effect modification. Specifically, based on a simulation study, our investigation had limited power to detect a relative effect modification of 2 but reasonable power to detect a relative effect modification of 3. For some genotypes, including GSTM1, GSTT1, and GSTP1, we found a relative effect modification of approximately 2 in this analysis, so our lack of significance may be a result of power limitations.
###end p 60
###begin title 61
Study limitations
###end title 61
###begin p 62
###xml 264 281 264 281 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b48-ehp-117-1767">Zeger et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 488 507 488 507 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b27-ehp-117-1767">Nethery et al. 2008</xref>
###xml 509 533 509 533 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b32-ehp-117-1767">Rojas-Bracho et al. 2000</xref>
###xml 683 702 683 702 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="b48-ehp-117-1767">Zeger et al. (2000)</xref>
One potential limitation of our study is the use of stationary measures of air pollution to represent personal exposures. Prior research indicates that this exposure misclassification is likely to lead to an underestimation of the health effects of air pollution (Zeger et al. 2000). Several studies, including one conducted in the Greater Boston area, have found that longitudinal measures of ambient PM concentrations are representative of longitudinal variation in personal exposures (Nethery et al. 2008; Rojas-Bracho et al. 2000). Hence, although the use of ambient measurements clearly introduces measurement error into the study, it is not to an unacceptable level. Moreover, Zeger et al. (2000) have shown that most of the measurement error in such cases is Berkson error, which reduces power to find a significant effect but does not bias the estimated effect size. Another potential limitation of our study design was that BP was read only once at each clinical visit, because BP is dynamic and responsive to many stimuli. Furthermore, nonambient PM exposures within or on the way to the clinic were not measured in our study.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 619 622 619 622 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
###xml 68 71 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">men</span>
###xml 328 340 <span type="species:ncbi:9606">participants</span>
Our study population is homogeneous, consisting entirely of elderly men, 97% of whom are white. However, our findings are consistent with studies of PM air pollution conducted in more heterogeneous populations. The Normative Aging Study cohort was not originally recruited to study the effects of air pollution. A number of our participants had only two clinical visits. Although subjects with two measurements obviously contribute less power to the analysis than subjects with three or more measurements, we believe that the study is well powered, with 1,067 total observations for BC and 949 total observations for PM2.5 as well as substantial fluctuation in weekly measurements of air pollution over time. Last, we did not correct for multiple comparisons in this analysis. However, we have taken a targeted pathway approach and examined genetic variants known to be associated with oxidative stress.
###end p 63
###begin title 64
Conclusions
###end title 64
###begin p 65
More research is needed to address the relation between BC exposure and BP among diverse study populations and to clarify the mechanisms underlying the association between BC and BP. Potential effect modification of the association between BC and BP by genes affecting endothelial function, inflammation, and oxidative stress pathways should be explored in future studies.
###end p 65
###begin p 66
###xml 283 286 283 286 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2.5</sub>
Our analysis indicates a positive association between ambient BC concentration and BP. We found no evidence of effect modification of this relation by gene variants related to oxidative stress defense, possibly due to insufficient power, and did not observe an association between PM2.5 and BP. These results provide new information to guide future research regarding BC and PM exposures and cardiovascular outcomes.
