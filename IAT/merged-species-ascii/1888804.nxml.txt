The unstructured C-terminus of the tau subunit of Escherichia coli DNA polymerase III holoenzyme is the site of interaction with the alpha subunit

###xml 786 793 <span type="species:ncbi:562">E. coli</span>
The tau subunit of Escherichia coli DNA polymerase III holoenzyme interacts with the alpha subunit through its C-terminal Domain V, tauC16. We show that the extreme C-terminal region of tauC16 constitutes the site of interaction with alpha. The tauC16 domain, but not a derivative of it with a C-terminal deletion of seven residues (tauC16Delta7), forms an isolable complex with alpha. Surface plasmon resonance measurements were used to determine the dissociation constant (KD) of the alpha-tauC16 complex to be approximately260 pM. Competition with immobilized tauC16 by tauC16 derivatives for binding to alpha gave values of KD of 7 muM for the alpha-tauC16Delta7 complex. Low-level expression of the genes encoding tauC16 and tauC16triangle up7, but not tauC16Delta11, is lethal to E. coli. Suppression of this lethal phenotype enabled selection of mutations in the 3' end of the tauC16 gene, that led to defects in alpha binding. The data suggest that the unstructured C-terminus of tau becomes folded into a helix-loop-helix in its complex with alpha. An N-terminally extended construct, tauC24, was found to bind DNA in a salt-sensitive manner while no binding was observed for tauC16, suggesting that the processivity switch of the replisome functionally involves Domain IV of tau.

###xml 1044 1046 1002 1004 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B16">16</xref>
###xml 71 87 <span type="species:ncbi:562">Escherichia coli</span>
DNA polymerase III holoenzyme (Pol III HE), the enzyme responsible for Escherichia coli chromosomal DNA synthesis, is a complex of 10 different polypeptides (1-7). It is comprised of three functionally distinct but interconnected subassemblies: the core polymerase, the sliding clamp and the clamp loader complex (8). There are two core polymerases in the HE, each consisting of three different subunits (9): alpha, the DNA polymerase (10); epsilon, the proofreading exonuclease (11); and theta, which has a role in stabilizing epsilon (12). The core polymerase is neither highly processive nor rapid in DNA synthesis: it polymerizes nucleotides at a rate of approximately20/s (10) and dissociates from DNA after incorporating 10-20 nt (13). The beta sliding clamp subunit, needed for rapid and highly processive DNA synthesis (14), is a ring-shaped head-to-tail dimer (15). Once it is assembled onto DNA by the clamp loader complex, interaction of beta2 with the alpha subunit confers efficient synthesis on all core polymerase subassemblies (16).
###end p 4

###xml 481 483 445 447 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>
###xml 566 567 527 528 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 574 576 535 537 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>
The single clamp loader within Pol III HE contains seven subunits, with composition tau2gammadeltadelta'chipsi (17). It hydrolyzes ATP in a DNA-dependent manner to load beta2 clamps onto DNA for interaction with both core polymerases (18-21). The chi and psi subunits are involved in binding to ssDNA-binding protein (SSB) (22) and participate in the primase-to-polymerase switch on the lagging strand (23). In an interaction modulated by delta', the delta subunit binds to beta2 (24), inducing a conformational change in the clamp and subsequent opening of the beta2 ring (25).
###end p 5

###xml 294 299 282 287 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B29 B30 B31">29&#8211;31</xref>
###xml 459 461 433 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>
###xml 462 464 436 438 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>
The three ATP motor subunits of the clamp loader (gamma and the two tau subunits) are encoded by the same gene, dnaX (26,27). The 71-kDa tau subunit (28) is the full-length dnaX product whereas gamma (47 kDa) is a truncated form produced as the result of a programmed translational frameshift (29-31). The gamma subunit and the N-terminal portions of the two tau subunits bind delta and delta', forming a circular pentamer that functions as the clamp loader (32,33).
###end p 6

The holoenzyme contains two alphaepsilontheta core polymerases to enable simultaneous replication of both the leading and the lagging strands (34). These and the clamp loader are held together by the two tau subunits (35) via the strong alpha -tau interaction (34). Deletion of 48 residues from the C-terminus of alpha (residues 1113-1160) eliminates its binding to tau, while removal of 705 residues or more from the N-terminus also has a large effect on tau binding. (36). While this may indicate there are two regions of alpha that contact tau, the involvement of the N-terminal domains of alpha might be indirect through stabilization of the C-terminal region or through conformational changes that occur during function of the complex. Indeed, there appear to be two different binding modes for the alpha-tau interaction (37-39) depending on whether or not the holoenzyme is bound to a primer-template DNA (39).

As shown in Figure 1A, the tau subunit has a five-domain structure (40), the N-terminal Domains I-III being identical to gamma. The unique 24-kDa C-terminal fragment comprising most of Domain IV and all of Domain V (residues 430-643; referred to in this article as tauC24) is connected to Domain III by a proline-rich tether that may be flexible (38). The tauC24 protein can be isolated in monomeric form (41), and is reported to bind both to primed DNA (38) and to a 20-mer peptide from the C-terminus of alpha in an interaction modulated by DNA structure (39). The 8-kDa N-terminal region of tauC24 (termed Domain IVa, residues 430-498 of tau) is responsible for binding to DnaB helicase (42), and the 16-kDa C-terminal domain (Domain V; residues 499-643, here also referred to as tauC16) binds to alpha (40).

The solution structure of the folded core of Domain V of E. coli tau, a 14.3-kDa protein (called tauC14) derived from tauC16 by deletion of 18 C-terminal residues, has been determined by NMR spectroscopy (Figure 1B), and is reported in the accompanying article (43). Given that recent NMR studies (43,44) have also shown that all N- and/or C-terminal residues additional to the tauC14 core in the longer protein constructs tauC24, tauC22 (tauC24 lacking the C-terminal 18 residues), and tauC16 (see Figure 1A) are intrinsically unstructured, we can now develop a near-complete picture of the structure of the tau subunit in which the structured parts of Domains I-III and V are linked together by flexible segments that provide both docking sites for interactions with other proteins and DNA, as well as flexibility to enable structural transitions in the replisome as it cycles through its various functional states.

In this article, we describe the genesis of the tauC14 fragment and show that the additional residues in tauC16 relative to tauC14 are required for formation of an isolable complex with alpha. Alignment and secondary structure prediction combined with point mutagenesis and deletion studies suggest the induction of alpha-helices within the otherwise flexible (44) C-terminal region of tauC16 upon its interaction with alpha. We also show that although both tauC22 and tauC24 proteins bind weakly through predominantly electrostatic interactions to single-stranded (ss), double-stranded (ds) and primed DNA, tauC16 does not. This localizes the putative DNA-interaction site in tauC24 to Domain IVa. Implications for the role of this region of tau in switching processivity in Pol III HE are discussed.

Plasmid pSH1062 was used as a template for error-prone PCR amplification of the gene encoding tauC16. The PCR product was generated using a primer corresponding to a DNA sequence preceding the unique BssHII site within the gene (5'-CGGTTGAACTGACTATC GTTGAAGATG) and another complementary to a sequence following the stop codon and unique EcoRI site in the vector (5'-CCTTTCGGGCTTTGTTAGCAG). The plasmid pJC491 that directs overexpression of full-length tau under control of tandem temperature-inducible phage lambda pR and pL promoters was constructed by insertion of a fragment of the dnaX gene bearing silent A to G mutations in codons 428 and 429 (46) into the dnaX+ plasmid pJC490 (45). These mutations suppressed translational frameshifting, so that tau could be produced without significant production of gamma (not shown). Nevertheless, in early attempts to purify tau from an ompT + strain (AN1459) containing pJC490, proteolysis was observed to produce fragments of 47 kDa (N-terminal fragment similar in size to gamma), 24 and 16 kDa, and these products could be enriched by overnight storage of cell lysates at 4degreesC prior to purification. We used this strategy to generate the three fragments and purified both of the smaller fragments by chromatography (not shown). They were identified from their molecular weights determined by ESI-MS to correspond to the Domain V (16.4 kDa) and Domain IVa + V (23.9 kDa) fragments described earlier (40,41). Since we were subsequently able to purify tau, free of contaminating fragments, by overexpression directed by pJC491 in the ompT strain BL21(lambdaDE3)recA, these data extend earlier observations (57) by showing that tau is sensitive to cleavage by OmpT protease at or near both the Domain III-IVa and Domain IVa-V boundaries (i.e. after Lys429 and Lys498, respectively).

The part of dnaX that encodes tauC16 (codons 499-643) was amplified by PCR and inserted into a phage T7-promoter vector to generate plasmid pSH1062. However, repeated attempts to introduce pSH1062 into strain BL21(lambdaDE3) recA failed to give viable colonies, suggesting that expression of the gene that encodes tauC16 is, even in a small amounts, lethal to E. coli, presumably due to sequestration of alpha by tauC16 and consequent inhibition of its binding to native tau. Following overproduction in strain AN1459 containing pSJ1064, tauC16 was first purified in a yield of 13 mg/l of cell culture (Supplementary Data, method A). Its structure was initially examined by NMR measurements using a 0.45 mM solution at pH 7.0 and 30degreesC. Under these conditions, the protein underwent stepwise proteolysis to yield a final species with a molecular weight of approximately14 000, as assessed by SDS-PAGE. This fragment remained stable for longer than a day of NMR measurements, and the final TOCSY spectrum suggested the 14-kDa product to be a well-structured protein suitable for structure determination by NMR (43). A second sample of similarly purified tauC16 that had undergone partial proteolysis was chromatographed on a Mono-Q column, which separated a series of fragments (Supplementary Figure S6). The smallest of these had a molecular weight of 14 334.4 +/- 1.9, determined by ESI-MS, which indicated it most likely comprised residues 499-625 (14 334.2 Da, including the N-terminal Met introduced in cloning) or perhaps, 517-642 (14 348.1 Da) of tau. PCR-generated gene fragments encoding each of these fragments were inserted into the lambda-promoter vector pND706 (48). Although both of these plasmids directed overproduction of an approximately14-kDa protein (not shown), only the product corresponding to residues 499-625 of tau (encoded in plasmid pSJ1308) was in the soluble fraction following cell lysis. We concluded this corresponded to the stable proteolysis product of tauC16, and called the protein tauC14. It lacks the last 18 residues from the C-terminus of tauC16 (or tau). We found subsequently that more stable samples of tauC16 could be prepared by a modified procedure (method B, see Materials and methods section) which used strain BL21(lambdaDE3)recA/pSJ1064 for expression, and included treatment of the extract with protease inhibitors. The tauC14 domain was similarly purified in good yield using strain BL21(lambdaDE3)recA/pSJ1308.

We next used analytical gel filtration on a column of Sephacryl S100 HR to confirm that tauC16, but not tauC14, could form an isolable complex with alpha. The alpha subunit alone or in complex with tauC16 elutes in the void volume, while excess of the small tau fragments are well resolved in the included volume (Figure 2A and B). The results clearly showed that while tauC16 forms a stable isolable complex with alpha, tauC14 did not (Figure S7A), indicating that the last 18 amino acids of tau are required for the alpha-tau interaction. In published work, we have shown that this region of tauC16 is unstructured in solution (44); we presume it adopts an ordered structure when it interacts with alpha. Next we used SPR experiments with a BIACORE instrument to determine the interaction strength contributed by this 18-residue segment.

For comparison with previously reported experiments (40), we first prepared a N-terminally biotinylated version of tauC16 (bio-tauC16), bound it to streptavidin on the surface of a Biacore SA chip, and analyzed its interaction with alpha by SPR. Because of the slow diffusion of the 130-kDa alpha subunit through the dextran layer of the SPR chip, quantification of dissociation constants (KD) required low loadings of bio-tauC16, use of a high flow rate (30 mul/min) and correction of the data for mass transfer limitations as implemented in the Biacore software package. Sensorgrams recorded at a range of concentrations of alpha fit well globally to a 1:1 Langmuir binding model (with mass transfer) to yield a value of KD(alpha-tauC16) of (2.65 +/- 0.02) x 10-10 M (Figure 3A). This value was about two orders of magnitude higher than measured previously (40), largely because of a higher dissociation rate under our experimental conditions. Although the source of this difference has not yet been determined, the data clearly confirm that Domain V of tau interacts strongly with alpha.

We used additional approaches to further probe interactions of the C-terminus of tau with alpha. Guided by sequence alignments that show several amino acids in this region to be highly conserved (see Figure 1B and ref. 43), perhaps because they interact with a similarly conserved region of alpha, we next deleted residues from the C-terminus of tauC16, and measured their affinity for alpha both by gel filtration (Figures 2C and S7B) and in the competitive SPR assay (Figure S8C and Table 1). Deletion of either 7 (in tauC16Delta7) or 11 residues (tauC16Delta11) from the C-terminus of tauC16 resulted in soluble proteins that could be purified readily; removal of these amino acids does not appear to affect folding of the isolated domain, consistent with NMR studies that showed the lack of defined structure for this region (44). Since a T7-promoter vector that contained the gene encoding tauC16Delta11 could be easily maintained in strain BL21(lambdaDE3)recA, its expression was not toxic to E. coli. However, the corresponding T7 vector encoding the tauC16Delta7 mutant could not be stably maintained, so a lambda-promoter vector was again used to prepare this mutant protein. This suggested that deletion of seven residues may not be sufficient to completely prevent interaction of tau Domain V with alpha; this was examined further by gel filtration and competitive SPR experiments.

Neither tauC16Delta7 nor Delta11 formed a complex with alpha sufficiently stable to enable its isolation by gel filtration (Figures 2C and S7C). ###begin title 53
###xml 52 53 50 51 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
Targeted mutagenesis of the C-terminal region of tauC16
###end title 53
###begin p 54
###xml 133 134 125 126 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 522 524 514 516 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 527 529 519 521 <sup xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2+</sup>
###xml 660 664 647 651 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">recA</italic>
Finally, to further probe the alpha-tau interaction, we carried out a targeted mutagenesis experiment on the C-terminal region of tauC16. The gene in the T7-promoter plasmid pSH1062 contains a unique BssHII site situated 28 codons before the stop codon, and a single EcoRI site is placed immediately after the gene. A series of error-prone PCRs were used to target mutations to this region, and the product fragment was digested with BssHII and EcoRI. Separate libraries of mutant gene fragments obtained with different Mn2+:Mg2+ ratios in the PCRs were then inserted between the corresponding sites in pSH1062, and then transformed into strain BL21(lambdaDE3)recA. Nucleotide sequence determination revealed that plasmids in 24 randomly selected survivors that yielded interpretable sequences all carried mutations in the targeted region. The mutations included: (a) Nine single point mutations, resulting in mutant proteins L627P (three isolates), L635P (two isolates), S617P, I618T, F631I and D636G. The two mutants isolated more than once were derived from separate libraries. (b) Nine double point mutations, giving changes I618N/R628C, A620V/D632V, D621G/F630S, N622I/F631S, I624N/F630L, T626A/I640F, L627P/L635Q, D636N/R641S and P642S plus a TGA to AGA mutation at the stop codon. (c) Five frameshift mutations (three deletions and two insertions); and (d) A single alteration of the stop codon (TGA codon changed to CGA); stop-codon mutations result in extension of the protein by 17 additional residues at the C-terminus.
###end p 54
###begin p 55
###xml 88 89 86 87 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 149 157 147 155 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 259 260 255 256 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 354 361 346 353 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">E. coli</italic>
###xml 354 366 <span type="species:ncbi:511693">E. coli BL21</span>
Of the six single point mutations that suppressed the lethality of the gene encoding tauC16, four were in codons for conserved hydrophobic residues (Figure 1B) and two were in neighboring residues. To examine the roles, if any, of these amino acids in the tauC16-alpha interaction, these six proteins were isolated. All were soluble when overproduced in E. coli BL21(lambdaDE3)/pLysS, and could be isolated in good yield.
###end p 55
###begin title 56
Contributions of residues in the C-terminus of tau to alpha binding
###end title 56
###begin p 57
###xml 221 222 215 216 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 380 381 360 361 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 539 546 515 522 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T1">Table 1</xref>
Interactions between the mutant proteins and alpha were first assessed by gel filtration, as before. The results (Supplementary Figure S9) suggest that no single point mutation disturbed the interaction as much as the tauC16Delta7 and Delta11 truncations. Nevertheless, it is clear for all of them that interaction with alpha was impaired, and this was most obvious for mutant tauC16[L627P]. The relative strength of the interactions between the mutant proteins and alpha was then quantified more reliably using the SPR competition assay (Table 1 and Supplementary Figures S10 and S11).
###end p 57
###begin p 58
###xml 86 87 86 87 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">K</italic>
###xml 87 88 87 88 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">D</sub>
###xml 112 113 104 105 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">G</italic>
###xml 306 307 284 285 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">G</italic>
###xml 458 459 416 417 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 671 672 619 620 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
The I618T, L635P and D636G mutations had rather modest effects on binding (changes in KD of <30-fold, DeltaDeltaGdegrees <1.9 kcal/mol) and, as seen in the gel filtration data, the largest effect was with the L627P mutation (5.4 kcal/mol). The S617P and F631I mutations had intermediate effects (DeltaDeltaGdegrees of approximately4 kcal/mol). Thus, although the data cannot determine that the sole reason for the lethal phenotype of modest production of tauC16 is its interaction with alpha (as opposed to DNA or another replisomal component), all of the isolated mutant proteins had defects in alpha binding. This confirms that the unstructured C-terminal region of tauC16 carries many determinants for interaction with alpha, and provides clues to the structure of the complex, as discussed further below.
###end p 58
###begin title 59
###xml 17 18 15 16 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 29 30 25 26 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
Domain IVa of tauC24 (and tauC22) is responsible for its interaction with DNA
###end title 59
###begin p 60
###xml 7 8 5 6 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>

###xml 216 218 208 210 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 273 274 263 264 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 281 282 269 270 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 292 293 278 279 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 413 414 397 398 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 421 422 403 404 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 512 520 490 498 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 532 533 508 509 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 540 541 514 515 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 649 657 <span type="species:ncbi:10760">phage T7</span>
The tauC24 protein (Domains IVa + V) has previously been shown to bind to primed DNA (38), and interaction between the C-terminal (20-mer) peptide from alpha and tauC24 was proposed to be modulated by DNA structure (39). Accordingly, we used three different constructs, tauC24, tauC22 and tauC16 in SPR experiments with ss, ds and primer-template DNA to map the region responsible for DNA binding. Compared to tauC24, tauC22 lacks the 18 residues from the C-terminus that are involved in interaction with alpha (Figure 1A). Like tauC14, tauC22 could be overproduced in strain BL21(lambdaDE3)/pLysS/pKO1297, where its synthesis is under control of a phage T7-promoter.
###end p 60
###begin p 61
###xml 235 236 235 236 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
Surprisingly, we were unable to detect interaction between any of these protein constructs and any of the immobilized DNA structures in a buffer containing 150 mM NaCl. Further experiments using the same buffer containing 10 mM Mg(OAc)2 in the presence or absence of 1 mM ATP or AMP-PNP also yielded no evidence of interaction, and reduction of [NaCl] to 100 mM yielded similar negative results.
###end p 61
###begin p 62
###xml 96 97 94 95 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 108 109 104 105 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 254 262 250 258 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F3">Figure 3</xref>
###xml 431 432 425 426 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 442 443 434 435 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
However, reduction of the NaCl concentration to 75 mM yielded detectable interaction between tauC24 (and tauC22, not shown) and all DNA structures, while further progressive decrease in [NaCl] to 50 and 25 mM led to stronger, non-stoichiometric binding (Figure 3B). The data show that as more nucleotides are immobilized on the surface (more negative charges), the stronger the response, suggesting that the interaction between tauC24 (or tauC22) and DNA is largely electrostatic. Otherwise, there appeared to be no significant preference for binding to primer-template DNA than to ss or dsDNA.
###end p 62
###begin p 63
###xml 35 36 33 34 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 207 208 203 204 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>

In contrast, Domain V alone (in tauC16) was observed not to bind to any of the DNA structures even at the lowest salt concentrations. The data therefore show that the 69 residues comprising Domain IVa of tauC24 (and tauC22) contain all determinants for DNA binding, and the strong salt dependence and lack of apparent specificity is likely due to the high net positive charge of this region coupled with its lack of defined tertiary structure (43).

###begin title 64
DISCUSSION
###end title 64
###begin title 65
###xml 35 36 29 30 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
Structural aspects of the alpha-tauC16 interaction
###end title 65
###begin p 66
###xml 189 191 183 185 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 291 292 281 282 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 431 432 419 420 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 460 462 448 450 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 508 509 494 495 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 533 534 517 518 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 538 546 522 530 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 641 643 625 627 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>
###xml 723 724 703 704 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 825 826 799 800 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 830 837 804 811 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T1">Table 1</xref>
This report shows the important role the extreme C-terminal region of the tau subunit of Pol III HE plays in its interaction with the alpha polymerase subunit. In the accompanying article (43), we describe the solution structure of the folded core of the C-terminal Domain V of tau (i.e. tauC14) determined by NMR spectroscopy. The region between residues Pro507 and Ser617 of Domain V is structured; the last eight residues of tauC14 were found to be mobile (43) and the following 18 residues present in tauC16 beyond the end of tauC14 (Figure 1A) are also unstructured and show no evidence of association with the folded core of Domain V (44). Nevertheless, that a peptide comprising just the last 32 residues of tau (tauCpep32) and including all of this unstructured region does not associate as strongly with alpha as tauC16 (Table 1) suggests that some residues in the folded region of Domain V directly or indirectly contribute to the interaction with alpha.
###end p 66
###begin p 67
###xml 80 81 78 79 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 106 114 104 112 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 422 424 416 418 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B59">59</xref>
###xml 425 427 419 421 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B60">60</xref>
Sequence alignment reveals that certain residues in the C-terminal region of tauC16 are highly conserved (Figure 1B, and ref. 43), and our mutagenesis data suggest that these conserved residues are important for interaction with alpha. This interaction thus provides yet another example of how an intrinsically unstructured region of a protein can fold into a defined structure when it interacts with its binding partner (59,60). Although this region contains many polar and charged amino acids, most of the conserved residues are non-polar and are spaced in such a way that suggests the folded structure of this part of tau in the alpha-tau complex comprises two amphipathic alpha-helices.
###end p 67
###begin p 68
###xml 85 86 83 84 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 90 92 88 90 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 151 159 149 157 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 312 313 298 299 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">G</italic>
Secondary structure predictions suggest an extension of the C-terminal helix 6 of tauC14 (43) which includes the conserved residues Ala614 and Ile618 (Figure 1B). We studied two single point mutants from this region, S617P and I618T. While the I618T mutation had only a modest effect on alpha binding (DeltaDeltaGdegrees = 1.1 kcal/mol), the effect of the S617P mutation was larger (4.1 kcal/mol). Since the proline substitution at Ser617 would be expected to disrupt an alpha-helix, this is consistent with the interactions with alpha occurring via an incipient helix.
###end p 68
###begin p 69
###xml 400 408 393 401 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 469 470 450 451 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">G</italic>
###xml 778 779 747 748 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 915 917 870 872 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 977 978 930 931 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 1250 1252 1179 1181 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B62">62</xref>
###xml 1253 1255 1182 1184 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B63">63</xref>
###xml 1267 1268 1194 1195 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 1366 1367 1286 1287 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
Structure prediction further suggests a beta-turn involving Asp621-Asn623 followed by a helix from Ile624 to Phe631 with high propensity, or to Leu635 with lower propensity. This suggests that a second longer helix extending to Leu635 might be formed upon interaction with alpha. Consistent with this is the fact that the conserved residue mutant L635P located at the end of this hypothetical helix (Figure 1B) still interacts relatively strongly with alpha (DeltaDeltaGdegrees = 1.7 kcal/mol) while the L627P mutation within it has the largest effect of those examined (5.4 kcal/mol). Taken together, these data suggest that a helix-loop-helix structure is induced on interaction with alpha, with the extended helix 6 serving as a scaffold that links the globular domain of tauC14 and alpha. Whether the globular domain itself establishes contacts with alpha in either of the two proposed alpha-tau binding modes (38) remains to be shown. NMR studies of the interaction of tauC16Delta11 with alpha yielded a dissociation constant of approximately0.9 mM (61), indicating that additional binding residues are located within the structured portion near the end of helix 6. Two structures of alpha, from different bacteria, have recently been reported (62,63). Since tauC16 is expected to be bound close to the C-terminus of alpha due to its close proximity to the beta2 sliding clamp that binds in this region, one could speculate that the incipient additional helix 7 binds in a C-terminal cleft in alpha.
###end p 69
###begin p 70
###xml 100 101 88 89 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">G</italic>
Of the remaining mutants examined, the D636G change had a modest effect on alpha binding (DeltaDeltaGdegrees = 1.9 kcal/mol) while F631I resulted in a much weaker interaction (3.8 kcal/mol). Consistent with these data, Asp636 is not highly conserved while Phe631 is, and since isoleucine is a helix-breaking residue, the F631I mutation would be expected to disrupt the proposed helical structure in this region.
###end p 70
###begin p 71
###xml 35 42 35 42 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="table" rid="T1">Table 1</xref>
###xml 387 388 377 378 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 432 433 420 421 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 443 451 427 435 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 504 505 476 477 <italic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">G</italic>
###xml 733 735 693 695 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 751 753 711 713 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 801 802 759 760 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
Study of the single point mutants (Table 1) revealed that no single amino acid change is sufficient to completely disrupt the alpha-tau interaction, presumably because the interactions at the interface involve a series of residues located in an otherwise flexible segment of tau. Finally, we note that although no single point mutation was identified in the last seven amino acids of tauC16, complete removal of this segment (in tauC16Delta7; Figure 1B) had a large effect of binding of alpha (DeltaDeltaGdegrees = 6.3 kcal/mol). There must therefore be further contacts with this region in the complex with alpha. Indeed, extension of this region in full-length tau by fusion of a biotin-tag sequence also disrupts the interaction (40). NMR studies (43) similarly suggest that all determinants in tauC16 required for binding to alpha likely reside towards the end of helix 6 and in the following unstructured 26-residue segment that we propose to become structured on alpha binding.
###end p 71
###begin title 72
Structural aspects of the alpha-tau interaction
###end title 72
###begin p 73
###xml 117 118 113 114 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 122 130 118 126 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="fig" rid="F1">Figure 1</xref>
###xml 203 204 193 194 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 292 295 280 283 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B4 B5 B6 B7">4&#8211;7</xref>
The DnaB helicase interacts with Domain IV of tau, a region that immediately precedes helix 1 in the structure of tauC14 (Figure 1) and an alpha subunit interacts with the extension of helix 6 of the tauC14 domain in each of the two tau subunits in the clamp loader complex in the replisome (4-7). This places restraints on the alpha-DnaB and alpha-alpha distances that need to be considered once there is sufficient information to build models of the structure of the replisome in its various functional states.
###end p 73
###begin p 74
###xml 33 34 31 32 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 185 186 174 175 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
###xml 318 319 307 308 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>
###xml 320 321 309 310 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B7">7</xref>
###xml 322 324 311 313 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>
###xml 380 381 365 366 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 495 497 476 478 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B39">39</xref>
###xml 569 570 548 549 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">C</sub>
###xml 820 822 795 797 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B40">40</xref>
###xml 924 926 899 901 <xref xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>
###xml 1037 1038 999 1000 <sub xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">2</sub>
The DNA-binding properties of tauC24 place further structural restraints. The tau subunit is known to house a DNA-sensing function that leads to active detachment of alpha from the beta2 clamp when the last nucleotide of an Okazaki fragment on the lagging strand is incorporated, resulting in the processivity switch (6,7,38). Further, it was shown that Domain IVa + V of tau (tauC24) senses a primed template structure, resulting in lowering of its affinity for the C-terminal region of alpha (39). Although we have shown that the highly basic Domain IVa region of tauC24 interacts with DNA, we have been unable to confirm under our experimental conditions that it binds primer-template DNA with high affinity or significant specificity. Domain IV has previously been shown not to contribute directly to alpha binding (40). Considering the close proximity of the N- and C-terminal helices in the globular fold of Domain V (43), the DNA-binding Domain IV may readily interact with the alpha-binding site of tau to regulate the alpha-beta2 interaction when Domain IV senses changes in DNA structure. Possibly, the affinity with the lagging strand is enhanced through interaction between Domains IV and/or V in neighboring tau subunits in the clamp loader complex. This would place rather severe geometric constraints on the positions of the two Pol III cores and DnaB in the replisome.
